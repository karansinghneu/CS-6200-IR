style="text-align:center">&#160;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>Luna 1</b></i>, also known as <i><b>Mechta</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1052;&#1077;&#1095;&#1090;&#1072;</span> <small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Russian" title="Help:IPA/Russian">[m&#690;&#618;t&#865;&#597;&#712;ta]</a></span>, <i><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a></i>: <i>Dream</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i><b>E-1 No.4</b></i> and <i>First Lunar Rover </i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bharv_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bharv-3">[3]</a></sup>  was the first <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> to reach the vicinity of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and the first spacecraft to be placed in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>. Intended as an impactor, <i>Luna 1</i> was launched as part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna programme</a> in 1959, however due to an incorrectly timed upper stage burn during its launch, it missed the Moon, in the process becoming the first spacecraft to leave <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">geocentric orbit</a>.\n</p><p>While traveling through the outer <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>, the spacecraft\'s <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillator</a> made observations indicating that a small number of high-energy <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">particles</a> exist in the outer belt. The measurements obtained during this mission provided new data on the Earth\'s radiation belt and <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The Moon was found to have no detectable <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. The first-ever direct observations and measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bharv_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bharv-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ddar_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ddar-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> a strong flow of <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">ionized plasma</a> emanating from the Sun and streaming through interplanetary space, were performed. That ionized plasma concentration was measured to be some 700 particles per cm<sup>3</sup> at altitudes 20&#8211;25 thousand km and 300 to 400 particles per cm<sup>3</sup> at altitudes 100&#8211;150,000&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSE59_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSE59-6">[6]</a></sup> The spacecraft also marked the first instance of radio communication at the half-million-kilometer distance.\n</p><p>A malfunction in the ground-based control system caused an error in the rocket\'s burntime, and the spacecraft missed the target and flew by the Moon at a distance of 5,900&#160;km at the closest point. <i>Luna 1</i> then became the first man-made object to reach <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a> and was then dubbed a "new planet" and renamed <i>Mechta</i> (<i>Dream</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cormack2012_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cormack2012-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Luna 1</i> was also referred to as the "First Cosmic Rocket", in reference to its achievement of escape velocity.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" coat of arms</a> that were included into its package. This mission was eventually accomplished by <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_2" title="Luna 2">Luna 2</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Luna 1</i> was launched at 16:41 GMT (22:41 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC%2B6" title="UTC+6">local time</a>) on 2 January 1959  from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_1" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 1">Site 1/5</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luna_8K72" title="Luna 8K72">Luna 8K72</a> rocket. <i>Luna 1</i> became the first man-made object to reach the <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a> of the Earth, along with its carrier rocket\'s 1,472-kilogram (3,245&#160;lb)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> upper stage, which it separated from after achieving <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.\n</p><p>Due to a programming error, the duration of the upper stage\'s burn was incorrect, and consequently <i>Luna 1</i> failed to impact the Moon. The spacecraft passed within 5,995 kilometres (3,725&#160;mi) of the Moon\'s surface on 4 January after 34 hours of flight. It remains in orbit around the Sun, between the orbits of Earth and Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sodium_release_experiment">Sodium release experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sodium release experiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At 00:56:20 UTC on 3 January, at a distance of 119,500 kilometres (74,300&#160;mi) from Earth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSE59_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BSE59-6">[6]</a></sup> 1 kilogram (2.2&#160;lb) of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> gas was released by the spacecraft, forming a cloud behind it to serve as an artificial <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>. This glowing orange trail of gas, visible over the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> with the brightness of a sixth-magnitude star for a few minutes, was photographed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mstislav_Gnevyshev&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mstislav Gnevyshev (page does not exist)">Mstislav Gnevyshev</a> at the Mountain Station of the Main Astronomical Observatory of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_USSR" title="Academy of Sciences of the USSR">Academy of Sciences of the USSR</a> near <a href="/wiki/Kislovodsk" title="Kislovodsk">Kislovodsk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSE59_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-BSE59-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since May 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> It served as an experiment on the behavior of gas in outer space.\n</p><p>A wired press photograph entitled "Rockets / Russian rocket sent into outer space January 1959 (first rocket fired at moon)" describes how the sodium gas cloud was photographed by Morris Alan, the following text is taken verbatim from the reverse of a press photograph stamped Kemsley Newspapers 6 Jan 1959.\n</p><p>"The Russian rocket on its way to the moon.\nMr Morris Alan, 34 year old freelance photographer, who was the first man to photograph the original Russian Sputnik, early this morning took this photograph of the moon rocket. He and his three assistants saw the rocket from Kingscat Hill, near Dunfermline, Firthshire, just after 1 AM and held it in view for almost eight minutes. "It appeared like a cloud on the horizon near the constellation Virgo", he said. "We photographed it with three cameras and with a movie camera. It\'s emerged in the sky just over the horizon near Edinburgh, but it was a second or two before we realised what we had". The picture shows the lights of Edinburgh in the foreground. The rocket is seen as an illuminated cloud top centre."\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Malfunction">Malfunction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Malfunction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Luna 1</i> was meant to crash on the Moon; however, due to a malfunction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_control_system" title="Ground control system">ground control system</a>, the probe missed its target by 5,995 kilometers.  Despite this, <i>Luna 1</i> still managed to collect vital information to assist in the understanding of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Its goal to crash on the Moon was subsequently achieved by <a href="/wiki/Luna_2" title="Luna 2">Luna 2</a> on September 13, 1959.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
