href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">Mount Wilson Observatory</a> near Los Angeles, USA.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Telescopes</b> are <a href="/wiki/Optical_instrument" title="Optical instrument">optical instruments</a> that make distant objects appear magnified by using an arrangement of lenses or curved mirrors and lenses, or various devices used to observe distant objects by their emission, absorption, or reflection of electromagnetic radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The first known practical telescopes were <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescopes</a> invented in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/17th_century" title="17th century">17th century</a>, by using glass <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>. They found use in both terrestrial applications and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a>, which uses mirrors to collect and focus light, was invented within a few decades of the first refracting telescope. In the <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>, many new types of telescopes were invented, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_telescopes" title="Radio telescopes">radio telescopes</a> in the 1930s and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_telescopes" title="Infrared telescopes">infrared telescopes</a> in the 1960s. The word <i>telescope</i> now refers to a wide range of instruments capable of detecting different regions of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, and in some cases other types of detectors.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i>telescope</i> (from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%E1%BF%86%CE%BB%CE%B5" title="wikt:&#964;&#8134;&#955;&#949;">&#964;&#8134;&#955;&#949;</a>, <i>tele</i> "far" and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CE%BA%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%AD%CF%89" title="wikt:&#963;&#954;&#959;&#960;&#941;&#969;">&#963;&#954;&#959;&#960;&#949;&#8150;&#957;</a>, <i>skopein</i> "to look or see"; &#964;&#951;&#955;&#949;&#963;&#954;&#972;&#960;&#959;&#962;, <i>teleskopos</i> "far-seeing") was coined in 1611 by the Greek mathematician <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Demisiani" title="Giovanni Demisiani">Giovanni Demisiani</a> for one of <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>\'s instruments presented at a banquet at the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei" title="Accademia dei Lincei">Accademia dei Lincei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starry_Messenger" title="Starry Messenger">Starry Messenger</a></i>, Galileo had used the term <i>perspicillum</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote foreground.</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest existing record of a telescope was a 1608 <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> submitted to the government in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> by Middelburg spectacle maker <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Hans Lippershey</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The actual inventor is unknown but word of it spread through Europe. <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a> heard about it and, in 1609, built his own version, and made his telescopic observations of celestial objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The idea that the <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">objective</a>, or light-gathering element, could be a mirror instead of a lens was being investigated soon after the invention of the refracting telescope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The potential advantages of using <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic mirrors</a>&#8212;reduction of <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">spherical aberration</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">chromatic aberration</a>&#8212;led to many proposed designs and several attempts to build <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescopes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1668, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> built the first practical reflecting telescope, of a design which now bears his name, the <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_telescope" title="Newtonian telescope">Newtonian reflector</a>.\n</p><p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Achromatic_lens" title="Achromatic lens">achromatic lens</a> in 1733 partially corrected color aberrations present in the simple lens and enabled the construction of shorter, more functional refracting telescopes. Reflecting telescopes, though not limited by the color problems seen in refractors, were hampered by the use of fast tarnishing <a href="/wiki/Speculum_metal" title="Speculum metal">speculum metal</a> mirrors employed during the 18th and early 19th century&#8212;a problem alleviated by the introduction of silver coated glass mirrors in 1857,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and aluminized mirrors in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The maximum physical size limit for refracting telescopes is about 1 meter (40&#160;inches), dictating that the vast majority of large optical researching telescopes built since the turn of the 20th century have been reflectors. The largest reflecting telescopes currently have objectives larger than 10&#160;m (33&#160;feet), and work is underway on several 30-40m designs.\n</p><p>The 20th century also saw the development of telescopes that worked in a wide range of wavelengths from <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_telescope" title="Gamma-ray telescope">gamma-rays</a>. The first purpose built radio telescope went into operation in 1937. Since then, a large variety of complex astronomical instruments have been developed.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Refimprove_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Most detect <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, but there are major differences in how astronomers must go about collecting light (electromagnetic radiation) in different frequency bands.\n</p><p>Telescopes may be classified by the wavelengths of light they detect:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/X-ray_telescope" title="X-ray telescope">X-ray telescopes</a>, using shorter wavelengths than ultraviolet light</li>\n<li>Ultraviolet telescopes, using shorter wavelengths than visible light</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">Optical telescopes</a>, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Infrared_telescope" title="Infrared telescope">Infrared telescopes</a>, using longer wavelengths than visible light</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Submillimetre_astronomy" title="Submillimetre astronomy">Submillimetre telescopes</a>, using longer wavelengths than infrared light</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fresnel_Imager" title="Fresnel Imager">Fresnel Imager</a>, an optical lens technology</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/X-ray_optics" title="X-ray optics">X-ray optics</a>, optics for certain X-ray wavelengths</li></ul>\n<p>As wavelengths become longer, it becomes easier to use antenna technology to interact with electromagnetic radiation (although it is possible to make very tiny antenna). The near-infrared can be collected much like visible light, however in the far-infrared and submillimetre range,  telescopes can operate more like a radio telescope. For example, the <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell_Telescope" title="James Clerk Maxwell Telescope">James Clerk Maxwell Telescope</a> observes from wavelengths from 3&#160;&#956;m (0.003&#160;mm) to 2000&#160;&#956;m (2&#160;mm), but uses a parabolic aluminum antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a>, observing from about 3&#160;&#956;m (0.003&#160;mm) to 180&#160;&#956;m (0.18&#160;mm) uses a mirror (reflecting optics). Telescopes such as <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">TRACE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> use special mirrors to reflect <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">Extreme ultraviolet</a>, producing higher resolution and brighter images than are otherwise possible. A larger aperture does not just mean that more light is collected, it also enables a finer angular resolution.\n</p><p>Telescopes may also be classified by location: ground telescope, <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">space telescope</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_telescope" title="Flying telescope">flying telescope</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Optical telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Telescope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1707" data-file-width="1108" decoding="async" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Telescope.jpg/160px-Telescope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Telescope.jpg/240px-Telescope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Telescope.jpg/320px-Telescope.jpg 2x" href="/wiki/Nice_Observatory" title="Nice Observatory">Nice Observatory</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">Optical telescope</a></div>\n<p>An optical telescope gathers and <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)">focuses</a> light mainly from the visible part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> (although some work in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Optical telescopes increase the apparent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_size" title="Angular size">angular size</a> of distant objects as well as their apparent <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">brightness</a>. In order for the image to be observed, photographed, studied, and sent to a computer, telescopes work by employing one or more curved optical elements, usually made from <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, to gather light and other electromagnetic radiation to bring that light or radiation to a focal point. Optical telescopes are used for <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and in many non-astronomical instruments, including: <i><a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">theodolites</a></i> (including <i>transits</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Spotting_scope" title="Spotting scope">spotting scopes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Monocular" title="Monocular">monoculars</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>,</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Camera_lens" title="Camera lens">camera lenses</a></i>, and <i>spyglasses</i>. There are three main optical types:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a> which uses lenses to form an image.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a> which uses an arrangement of mirrors to form an image.</li>\n<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catadioptric#Catadioptric_telescopes" title="Catadioptric">catadioptric telescope</a> which uses mirrors combined with lenses to form an image.</li></ul>\n<p>Beyond these basic optical types there are many sub-types of varying optical design classified by the task they perform such as <a href="/wiki/Astrograph" title="Astrograph">astrographs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comet_seeker" title="Comet seeker">comet seekers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_telescope" title="Solar telescope">solar telescopes</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_telescopes">Radio United States.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">Radio telescope</a></div>\n<p>Radio telescopes are <a href="/wiki/Directional_antenna" title="Directional antenna">directional</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_antennas" title="Radio antennas">radio antennas</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>. The dishes are sometimes constructed of a conductive wire mesh whose openings are smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> being observed. Multi-element <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">Radio telescopes</a> are constructed from pairs or larger groups of these dishes to synthesize large \'virtual\' apertures that are similar in size to the separation between the telescopes; this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Aperture_synthesis" title="Aperture synthesis">aperture synthesis</a>. As of 2005, the current record array size is many times the width of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>&#8212;utilizing space-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Long_Baseline_Interferometry" title="Very Long Baseline Interferometry">Very Long Baseline Interferometry</a> (VLBI) telescopes such as the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/HALCA" title="HALCA">HALCA</a> (Highly Advanced Laboratory for Communications and Astronomy) <a class="external text" href="http://www.vsop.isas.ac.jp/" rel="nofollow">VSOP (VLBI Space Observatory Program) satellite</a>. Aperture synthesis is now also being applied to optical telescopes using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_interferometry#Astronomical_optical_interferometry" title="Optical interferometry">optical interferometers</a> (arrays of optical telescopes) and <a href="/wiki/Aperture_masking_interferometry" title="Aperture masking interferometry">aperture masking interferometry</a> at single reflecting telescopes. Radio telescopes are also used to collect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radiation" title="Microwave radiation">microwave radiation</a>, which is used to collect radiation when any visible light is obstructed or faint, such as from <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>. Some radio telescopes are used by programs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_for_Extraterrestrial_Intelligence" title="Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence">SETI</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> to search for extraterrestrial life.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X-ray_telescopes">X-ray href="/wiki/Wolter_telescope" title="Wolter telescope">Wolter telescopes</a> composed of ring-shaped \'glancing\' <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" In 1952, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Wolter" title="Hans Wolter">Hans Wolter</a> outlined 3 ways a telescope could be built using only this kind of mirror.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Examples of an observatory using this type of telescope are the <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Observatory" title="Einstein Observatory">Einstein Observatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/ROSAT" title="ROSAT">ROSAT</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandra_X-Ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-Ray Observatory">Chandra X-Ray Observatory</a>. By 2010, Wolter focusing X-ray telescopes are possible up to 79 keV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nustar1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nustar1-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma-ray_telescopes">Gamma-ray telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gamma-ray telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Higher energy X-ray and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">Gamma-ray</a> telescopes refrain from focusing completely and use <a href="/wiki/Coded_aperture" title="Coded aperture">coded aperture</a> masks: the patterns of the shadow the mask creates can be reconstructed to form an image.\n</p><p>X-ray and Gamma-ray telescopes are usually on Earth-orbiting <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> or high-flying balloons since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> is opaque to this part of the electromagnetic spectrum. However, high energy X-rays and gamma-rays do not form an image in the same way as telescopes at visible wavelengths. An example of this type of telescope is the <a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope</a>.\n</p><p>The detection of very high energy gamma rays, with shorter wavelength and higher frequency than regular gamma rays, requires further specialization. An example of this type of observatory is <a href="/wiki/VERITAS" title="VERITAS">VERITAS</a>. Very high energy gamma-rays are still photons, like visible light, whereas <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> includes particles like electrons, protons, and heavier nuclei.\n</p><p>A discovery in 2012 may allow focusing gamma-ray telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wogan_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wogan-17">[17]</a></sup> At photon energies greater than 700 keV, the index of refraction starts to increase again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wogan_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-wogan-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High-energy_particle_telescopes">High-energy particle telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High-energy particle telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/High-energy_astronomy" title="High-energy astronomy">High-energy astronomy</a> requires specialized telescopes to make observations since most of these particles go through most metals and glasses.\n</p><p>In other types of high energy particle telescopes there is no <a href="/wiki/Image-forming_optical_system" title="Image-forming optical system">image-forming optical system</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cosmic-ray_observatory" title="Cosmic-ray observatory">Cosmic-ray telescopes</a> usually consist of an array of different detector types spread out over a large area. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrino_telescope" title="Neutrino telescope">Neutrino telescope</a> consists of a large mass of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, surrounded by an array of sensitive light detectors known as <a href="/wiki/Photomultiplier" title="Photomultiplier">photomultiplier</a> tubes. Originating direction of the neutrinos is determined by reconstructing the path of secondary particles scattered by neutrino impacts, from their interaction with multiple detectors. <a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">Energetic neutral atom</a> observatories like <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">Interstellar Boundary Explorer</a> detect particles traveling at certain energies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_telescopes">Other types of telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other types of telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kepler_EQ.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1015" data-file-width="980" decoding="async" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kepler_EQ.svg/220px-Kepler_EQ.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kepler_EQ.svg/330px-Kepler_EQ.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kepler_EQ.svg/440px-Kepler_EQ.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kepler_EQ.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Equatorial-mounted Keplerian telescope</div></div></div>\n<p>Astronomy is not limited to using electromagnetic radiation. Additional information can be obtained using other media. The detectors used to observe the Universe are analogous to telescopes. Telescope mounts are designed to support the mass of the telescope and allow for accurate pointing of the instrument. Many sorts of mounts have been developed over the years, with the majority of effort being put into systems that can track the motion of the stars as the Earth rotates. The two main types of tracking mount are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Altazimuth_mount" title="Altazimuth mount">Altazimuth mount</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Equatorial_mount" title="Equatorial mount">Equatorial mount</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity">Atmospheric electromagnetic opacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Atmospheric electromagnetic opacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airmass" title="Airmass">Airmass</a></div>\n<p>Since the atmosphere is opaque for most of the electromagnetic spectrum, only a few bands can be observed from the Earth\'s surface. These bands are visible &#8211; near-infrared and a portion of the radio-wave part of the spectrum. For this reason there are no X-ray or far-infrared ground-based telescopes as these have to be observed from orbit. Even if a wavelength is observable from the ground, it might still be advantageous to place a telescope on a satellite due to <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">astronomical seeing</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="780" data-file-width="1650" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/700px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/1050px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/1400px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 2x" width="700"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A diagram of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> with the Earth\'s atmospheric transmittance (or opacity) and the types of telescopes used to image parts of the spectrum.</div></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Telescopic_image_from_different_telescope_types">Telescopic image from different telescope types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telescope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Telescopic image from different telescope types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Different types of telescope, operating in different wavelength bands, provide different information about the same object.
