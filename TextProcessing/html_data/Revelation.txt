links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some religions have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religious_texts" title="Religious texts">religious texts</a> which they view as divinely or supernaturally revealed or inspired. For instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Jews" title="Orthodox Jews">Orthodox Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> believe that the <i><a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a></i> was received from <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> on <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Mount_Sinai" title="Biblical Mount Sinai">biblical Mount Sinai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most Christians believe that both the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> were <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inspiration" title="Biblical inspiration">inspired</a> by God. Muslims believe the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> was revealed by God to <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> word by word through the angel <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">Gabriel</a> (<i>Jibril</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_&amp;_Drew_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_&amp;_Drew-4">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> are considered <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apaurusheyatva" title="Apaurusheyatva"><i lang="sa-Latn" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration">apauru&#7779;eya</i></a></i>, "not human compositions", and are supposed to have been directly revealed, and thus are called <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aruti" title="&#346;ruti">&#347;ruti</a></i>, "what is heard". The 15,000 handwritten pages produced by the mystic <a href="/wiki/Maria_Valtorta" title="Maria Valtorta">Maria Valtorta</a> were represented as direct dictations from <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, while she attributed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Azariah" title="The Book of Azariah">The Book of Azariah</a></i> to her <a href="/wiki/Guardian_angel" title="Guardian angel">guardian angel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aleister_Crowley" title="Aleister Crowley">Aleister Crowley</a> stated that <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_the_Law" title="The Book of the Law">The Book of the Law</a></i> had been revealed to him through a higher being that called itself <i><a href="/wiki/Aiwass" title="Aiwass">Aiwass</a></i>.\n</p><p>A revelation communicated by a supernatural entity reported as being present during the event is called a <a href="/wiki/Vision_(spirituality)" title="Vision (spirituality)">vision</a>. Direct conversations between the recipient and the supernatural entity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or physical marks such as <a href="/wiki/Stigmata" title="Stigmata">stigmata</a>, have been reported. In rare cases, such as that of Saint <a href="/wiki/Juan_Diego" title="Juan Diego">Juan Diego</a>, physical artifacts accompany the revelation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> concept of <a href="/wiki/Interior_locution" title="Interior locution">interior locution</a> includes just an inner voice heard by the recipient.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a>, the term is used to refer to the process by which <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> reveals knowledge of himself, his <a href="/wiki/Will_of_God" title="Will of God">will</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">divine providence</a> to the world of human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In secondary usage, revelation refers to the resulting human knowledge about God, <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divine" title="Divine">divine</a> things. Revelation from a supernatural source plays a less important role in some other religious traditions such as <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Revelation may be defined as the communication of some truth by God to a rational creature through means which are beyond the ordinary course of nature. George Joyce draws a distinction between revelation and inspiration. Inspiration &#8211; such as that bestowed by God on the author of a sacred book &#8211; involves a special illumination of the mind, in virtue of which the recipient conceives such thoughts as God desires him to commit to writing, and does not necessarily involve supernatural communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joyce_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-joyce-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a> in Europe, beginning about the mid-17th century, the development of <a href="/wiki/Rationalism" title="Rationalism">rationalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_atheism" title="History of atheism">atheism</a>, the concept of supernatural revelation itself faced skepticism. In  <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Reason" title="The Age of Reason">The Age of Reason</a></i> (1794&#8211;1809), <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a> develops the theology of <a href="/wiki/Deism" title="Deism">deism</a>, rejecting the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a> and arguing that a revelation can be considered valid only for the original recipient, with all else being <a href="/wiki/Hearsay" title="Hearsay">hearsay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span In <a href="/wiki/General_revelation" title="General revelation">general revelation</a>, God reveals himself through his creation, such that at least some truths about God can be learned by the <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empirical</a> study of nature, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>, etc., to an individual. Special revelation is the knowledge of God and spiritual matters which can be discovered through <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> means, such as scripture or miracles, by individuals. <a href="/wiki/Direct_revelation" title="Direct revelation">Direct revelation</a> refers to communication from God to someone in particular.\n</p><p>Though one may deduce the existence of God and some of God\'s attributes through general revelation, certain specifics may be known only through special revelation. Aquinas believed that special revelation is equivalent to the revelation of God in Jesus. The major theological components of Christianity, such as the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">Incarnation</a>, are revealed in the teachings of the church and the scriptures and may not otherwise be deduced. Special revelation and natural revelation are complementary rather than contradictory in nature.\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Continuous_revelation" title="Continuous revelation">Continuous revelation</a>" is a term for the theological position that God continues to reveal divine principles or commandments to humanity.\n</p><p>In the 20th century, religious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Existentialist" title="Existentialist">existentialists</a> proposed that revelation held no content in and of itself but rather that God inspired people with his presence by coming into contact with them. Revelation is a human response that records how we respond to God.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_revelation">Public revelation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Public revelation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Ten_Commandments_(Bible_Card).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="885" data-file-width="775" decoding="async" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg/100px-The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg/150px-The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg/200px-The_Ten_Commandments_%28Bible_Card%29.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Ten_Commandments_(Bible_Card).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The mass-revelation at the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Horeb" title="Mount Horeb">Mount Horeb</a> in an illustration from a Christian Bible card published by the Providence Lithograph Company, 1907</div></div></div>\n<p>Some religious groups believe a deity has been revealed or spoken to a large group of people or have legends to a similar effect. In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Book of Deuteronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> is said to have been revealed upon giving the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">Ten Commandments</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelites</a> at <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Mount_Sinai" title="Biblical Mount Sinai">Mount Sinai</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Acts" title="Book of Acts">Book of Acts</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Day of Pentecost</a> wherein a large group of the followers of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> experienced mass revelation. The <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota people</a> believe <a href="/wiki/White_Buffalo_Calf_Woman" title="White Buffalo Calf Woman">Ptes&#225;&#331;wi&#331;</a> spoke directly to the people in the establishment of Lakota religious traditions. Some versions of an <a href="/wiki/Aztec_mythology" title="Aztec mythology">Aztec legend</a> tell of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">Huitzilopochtli</a> speaking directly to the Aztec people upon their arrival at <a href="/wiki/Anahuac_(Aztec)" title="Anahuac (Aztec)">An&#229;huac</a>. Historically, some <a href="/wiki/List_of_people_who_have_been_considered_deities" title="List of people who have been considered deities">emperors, cult leaders, and other figures</a> have also been deified and treated as though their words are themselves revelations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span People were divinely inspired by God with a message, but not in a verbal-like fashion.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">Rabbi</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Joshua_Heschel" title="Abraham Joshua Heschel">Abraham Joshua Heschel</a> has written, "To convey what the prophets experienced, the Bible could either use terms of descriptions or terms of indication.  Any description of the act of revelation in empirical categories would have produced a caricature.  That is why all the Bible does is to state that revelation happened; how it happened is something they could only convey in words that are evocative and suggestive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a> writes that he received his message through visions, where he would see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YHWH" title="YHWH">YHWH</a>, the God of Israel, speaking to angelic beings that surrounded him. Isaiah would then write down the dialogue exchanged between YHWH and the angels. This form of revelation constitutes the major part of the text of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">Book of Isaiah</a>. The same formula of divine revelation is used by other prophets throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">Tanakh</a>, such as Micaiah in <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1%20kings%2022:19-22&amp;version=HCSB" rel="nofollow">1 Kings 22:19&#8211;22</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (November 2010)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemology">Epistemology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Epistemology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Members of <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a>, including Judaism, Christianity and Islam, believe that God exists and can in some way reveal his will to people. Members of those religions distinguish between true <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophets</a> and <a href="/wiki/False_prophet" title="False prophet">false prophets</a>, and there are documents offering criteria by which to distinguish true from false prophets. The question of <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> then arises: how to know?\n</p><p>Some believe that revelation can originate directly from a <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> or through an agent such as an <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angel</a>. One who has experienced such contact with, or communication from, the divine is often called a prophet. An article (p.&#160;555) under the heading "mysticism," and contributed by Ninian Smart, J.&#160;F. Rowny Professor of Comparative Religion, University of California, and President of the American Academy of Religion, writing in the 1999 edition of "The Norton Dictionary of Modern Thought," (W.&#160;W. Norton &amp; Co. Inc.), suggests that the more proper and wider term for such an encounter would be mystical, making such a person a <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mystic</a>. All prophets would be mystics, but not all mystics would be prophets.\n</p><p>Revelation from a supernatural source is of lesser importance in some other religious traditions, such as <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_various_religions">In various religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In various religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah&#225;\'&#237;">Bah&#225;\'&#237;</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bah&#225;\'&#237;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_literature" title="Bah&#225;\'&#237; literature">Bah&#225;\'&#237; literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progressive_revelation_(Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD)" title="Progressive revelation (Bah&#225;\'&#237;)">Progressive revelation (Bah&#225;\'&#237;)</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Revelation-writing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="305" decoding="async" height="356" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Revelation-writing.jpg/220px-Revelation-writing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Revelation-writing.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Revelation-writing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\'Revelation writing\': The first draft of a tablet of <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h">Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="B&#225;b">The B&#225;b</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h">Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h</a> and <a href="/wiki/%60Abdu%27l-Bah%C3%A1" title="`Abdu\'l-Bah&#225;">`Abdu\'l-Bah&#225;</a> received thousands of written enquiries, and wrote thousands of responses, hundreds of which amount to whole and proper books, while many are shorter texts, such as letters. In addition, the Bah&#225;\'&#237; faith has large works which were divinely revealed in a very short time, as in a night, or a few days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Additionally, because many of the works were first recorded by an <a href="/wiki/Amanuensis" title="Amanuensis">amanuensis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> most were submitted for approval and correction and the final text was personally approved by the revelator.\n</p><p>Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h would occasionally write the words of revelation down himself, but normally the revelation was dictated to his amanuensis, who sometimes recorded it in what has been called <i>revelation writing</i>, a shorthand script written with extreme speed owing to the rapidity of the utterance of the words. Afterwards, Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h revised and approved these drafts. These <i>revelation drafts</i> and many other transcriptions of Bah&#225;\'u\'ll&#225;h\'s writings, around 15,000 items, some of which are in his own handwriting, are kept in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Archives" title="International Archives">International Bah&#225;\'&#237; Archives</a> in <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inspiration" title="Biblical inspiration">Biblical inspiration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_mystics" title="Christian mystics">Christian mystics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Visions_of_Jesus_and_Mary" title="Visions of Jesus and Mary">Visions of Jesus and Mary</a></div>\n<p>Many Christians believe in the possibility and even reality of <a href="/wiki/Private_revelation" title="Private revelation">private revelations</a>, messages from God for individuals, which can come in a variety of ways. <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanism</a> is an example in <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early Christianity</a> and there are alleged cases today also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vatican1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-vatican1-18">[18]</a></sup> However, Christians see as of a much higher level the revelation recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">collection of books</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. They consider these books to be written by human authors under the inspiration of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a>. They regard Jesus as the supreme revelation of God, with the Bible being a revelation in the sense of a witness to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">Catechism of the Catholic Church</a></i> states that "the Christian faith is not a \'religion of the book.\' Christianity is the religion of the <a href="/wiki/Logos_(Christianity)" title="Logos (Christianity)">\'Word of God\'</a>, a word which is \'not a written and mute word, but the Word which is incarnate and living".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gregory and Nix speak of <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inerrancy" title="Biblical inerrancy">Biblical inerrancy</a> as meaning that, in its original form, the Bible is totally without error, and free from all contradiction, including the historical and scientific parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inerrancy_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-inerrancy-21">[21]</a></sup> Coleman speaks of <a href="/wiki/Biblical_infallibility" title="Biblical infallibility">Biblical infallibility</a> as meaning that the Bible is inerrant on issues of faith and practice but not history or science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infallible_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-infallible-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> speaks not about infallibility of Scripture but about its freedom from error, holding "the doctrine of the inerrancy of Scripture".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>, citing earlier declarations, stated: "Since everything asserted by the inspired authors or sacred writers must be held to be asserted by the Holy Spirit, it follows that the books of Scripture must be acknowledged as teaching solidly, faithfully and without error that truth which God wanted put into sacred writings for the sake of salvation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It added: "Since God speaks in Sacred Scripture through men in human fashion, the interpreter of Sacred Scripture, in order to see clearly what God wanted to communicate to us, should carefully investigate what meaning the sacred writers really intended, and what God wanted to manifest by means of their words."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Reformed Churches believe in the Bible is inerrant in the sense spoken of by Gregory and Nix and "deny that Biblical infallibility and inerrancy are limited to spiritual, religious, or redemptive themes, exclusive of assertions in the fields of history and science".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Confession_of_Faith" title="Westminster Confession of Faith">Westminster Confession of Faith</a> speaks of "the infallible truth and divine authority" of the Scriptures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> treats the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> as authoritative and says it "cannot be broken" (<a class="external text" href="https://www.esv.org/John+10:34" rel="nofollow">John 10:34&#8211;36</a>). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_Timothy" title="2 Timothy">2 Timothy</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.esv.org/2+Timothy+3:16" rel="nofollow">3:16</a> says: "All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness". The <a href="/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_Peter" title="Second Epistle of Peter">Second Epistle of Peter</a> claims that "no prophecy of Scripture comes from someone\'s own interpretation. For no prophecy was ever produced by the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a>" (<a class="external text" href="https://www.esv.org/2+Peter+1:20" rel="nofollow">2 Peter 1:20&#8211;21</a>). It also speaks of Paul\'s letters as containing some things "hard to understand, which the ignorant and unstable twist to their own destruction, as they do the other Scriptures".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This letter does not specify "the other Scriptures", nor does the term "all Scripture" in 2 Timothy indicate which writings were or would be breathed out by God and useful for teaching, since it does not preclude later works, such as the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epistles_of_John" title="Epistles of John">Epistles of John</a> may have been. The Catholic Church recognizes 73 books as inspired and forming the Bible (46 books of the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_Old_Testament_canon" title="Development of the Old Testament canon">Old Testament</a> and 27 books of the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_New_Testament_canon" title="Development of the New Testament canon">New Testament</a>). The most common versions of the Bible that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a> have today consist of 66 of these books. None of the 66 or 73 books gives a list of revealed books.\n</p><p>Theologian and Christian existentialist philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Johannes_Tillich" title="Paul Johannes Tillich">Paul Johannes Tillich</a> (1886&#8211;1965), who sought to correlate culture and faith so that "faith need not be unacceptable to contemporary culture and contemporary culture need not be unacceptable to faith", argued that revelation never runs counter to reason (affirming <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a> who said that faith is eminently rational), and that both poles of the subjective human experience are complementary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Barth" title="Karl Barth">Karl Barth</a> argued that God is the object of God&#8217;s own self-knowledge, and revelation in the Bible means the self-unveiling to humanity of the God who cannot be discovered by humanity simply through its own efforts. For him, the Bible is not <i>The Revelation</i>; rather, it points to revelation. Human concepts can never be considered as identical to God\'s revelation, and Scripture is written in human language, expressing human concepts. It cannot be considered identical with God\'s revelation. However, God does reveal himself through human language and concepts, and thus Christ is truly presented in scripture and the preaching of the church.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" denomination believes that the president of the church receives revelation directly from God for the direction of the church. <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) and some other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint" title="Latter Day Saint">Latter Day Saint denominations</a> claim to be led by revelation from God to a <a href="/wiki/Prophet,_seer,_and_revelator" title="Prophet, seer, and revelator">living prophet</a>, who receives God&#8217;s word, just as <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, other ancient <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophets</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_apostles" title="Twelve apostles">apostles</a> did.\n</p><p>Latter-day Saints believe in an open scriptural canon, and in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">Book of Mormon</a>, have books of scripture containing the revelations of modern-day prophets such as the <a href="/wiki/Doctrine_and_Covenants" title="Doctrine and Covenants">Doctrine and Covenants</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pearl_of_Great_Price_(Mormonism)" title="Pearl of Great Price (Mormonism)">Pearl of Great Price</a>. LDS Church leaders (from the <a href="/wiki/Quorum_of_the_Twelve_Apostles_(LDS_Church)" title="Quorum of the Twelve Apostles (LDS Church)">Quorum of the Twelve Apostles</a>) have taught during the church\'s <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_(LDS_Church)" title="General Conference (LDS Church)">General Conferences</a> that conference talks which are "&#8230;[spoken as] moved upon by the Holy Ghost shall be scripture&#8230;".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In addition, many Mormons believe that ancient prophets in other regions of the world received revelations that resulted in additional scriptures that have been lost and may, one day, be forthcoming. Hence, the belief in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuing_revelation" title="Continuing revelation">continuing revelation</a>. Latter Day Saints also believe that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> is a divinely inspired document.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mormons sustain the President of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints as prophet, seer, and revelator&#8212;the only person on earth who receives revelation to guide the entire church. They also sustain the two counselors in the First Presidency, as well as the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles, as prophets, seers, and revelators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> They believe that God has followed a pattern of continued revelation to prophets throughout the history of mankind (KJV Luke 1:70)--both to establish doctrine and maintain its integrity, as well as to guide the church under changing world conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> When this pattern of revelation was broken, it was because the receivers of revelation had been rejected and often killed (Matt 23:31&#8211;37, Luke 11:47&#8211;51). In the meridian of time, Paul described prophets and apostles in terms of a foundation, with Christ as the cornerstone, which was built to prevent doctrinal shift&#8212;"that we henceforth be no more children, tossed to and fro, and carried about by every wind of doctrine" (Eph 2:20 and 4:11&#8211;14, see also Matt 16:17&#8211;18). To maintain this foundation, new apostles were chosen and ordained to replace those lost to death or transgression, as when Matthias was called by revelation to replace Judas (Acts 1:15&#8211;26). However, as intensifying persecution led to the imprisonment and martyrdom of the apostles, it eventually became impossible to continue the apostolic succession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Once the foundation of apostles and prophets was lost, the integrity of Christian doctrine as established by Christ and the apostles began to be compromised by those who continued to develop doctrine despite not being called or authorized to receive revelation for the body of the church. In the absence of revelation, these post-apostolic theologians couldn&#8217;t help but introduce elements of human reasoning, speculation, and personal interpretation of scripture (2 Pet 1:19&#8211;20)&#8212;which over time led to the loss or corruption of various doctrinal truths, as well as the addition of new man-made doctrines. This naturally led to much disagreement and schism, which over the centuries culminated in the large number of Christian churches on the earth today. Mormons believe that God resumed his pattern of revelation when the world was again ready, by calling the Prophet Joseph Smith to restore the fullness of the gospel of Jesus Christ to the earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Since that time there has been a consistent succession of prophets and apostles, which God has promised will not be broken before the Second Coming of Christ (Dan 2:44).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Each member of the LDS Church is also confirmed a member of the church following baptism and given the "gift of the Holy Ghost" by which each member is encouraged to develop a personal relationship with that divine being and receive personal revelation for their own direction and that of their family. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint" title="Latter Day Saint">Latter Day Saint</a> concept of revelation includes the belief that revelation from God is available to all those who earnestly seek it with the intent of doing good. It also teaches that everyone is entitled to <i>personal</i> revelation with respect to his or her <a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a> (leadership responsibility). Thus, parents may receive inspiration from God in raising their families, individuals can receive divine inspiration to help them meet personal challenges, church officers may receive revelation for those whom they serve, and so forth.\n</p><p>The important consequence of this is that each person may receive confirmation that particular doctrines taught by a prophet are true, as well as gain divine insight in using those truths for their own benefit and eternal progress. In the church, personal revelation is expected and encouraged, and many converts believe that personal revelation from God was instrumental in their conversion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_F._Smith" title="Joseph F. Smith">Joseph F. Smith</a>, the sixth <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Church_(LDS_Church)" title="President of the Church (LDS Church)">president</a> of the LDS Church, summarized this church\'s belief concerning revelation by saying, "We believe&#8230; in the principle of direct revelation from God to man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> (Smith, 362)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apaurusheyatva" title="Apaurusheyatva">Apaurusheyatva</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aruti" title="&#346;ruti">&#346;ruti</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wahy" title="Wahy">Wahy</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="730" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="321" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg/220px-Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg/330px-Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg/440px-Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muhammad-Majmac-al-tawarikh-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Muhammad\'s Call to Prophecy and the First Revelation; leaf from a copy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majmac_al-tawarikh" title="Majmac al-tawarikh">Majmac al-tawarikh</a> (Compendium of Histories), ca. 1425; <a href="/wiki/Timurid_dynasty" title="Timurid dynasty">Timurid</a>. In The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> believe that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_concept_of_God" title="Islamic concept of God">God</a> (Arabic: &#1571;&#1604;&#1604;&#1607; <i><a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a></i>) revealed his final message to all of existence through <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> via the angel <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">Gabriel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Muhammad is considered to have been the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_of_the_Prophets" title="Seal of the Prophets">Seal of the Prophets</a> and the last revelation, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur\'an">Qur\'an</a>, is believed by Muslims to be the flawless final revelation of God to humanity, valid until the <a href="/wiki/End_time" title="End time">Last Day</a>. The Qur\'an claims to have been revealed word by word and letter by letter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Muslims hold that the message of Islam is the same as the message preached by all the messengers sent by God to humanity since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adam_(Bible)" title="Adam (Bible)">Adam</a>. Muslims believe that Islam is the oldest of the monotheistic religions because it represents both the original and the final revelation of God to <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, and Muhammad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Likewise, Muslims believe that every <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> received revelation in their lives, as each prophet was sent by God to guide mankind. <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> is significant in this aspect as he received revelation in a twofold aspect, as Muslims believe he preached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injil" title="Injil">Gospel</a> while also having been taught the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>.\n</p><p>According to Islamic traditions, Muhammad began receiving revelations from the age of 40, delivered through the angel Gabriel over the last 23 years of his life. The content of these revelations, known as the Qur\'an,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> was memorized and recorded by his followers and compiled from dozens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hafiz_(Qur%27an)" title="Hafiz (Qur\'an)">hafiz</a> as well as other various parchments or hides into a single volume shortly after his death. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_theology" title="Muslim theology">Muslim theology</a>, Muhammad is considered equal in importance to all other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophets_in_Islam" title="Prophets in Islam">prophets of God</a> and to make distinction among the prophets is a <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a>, as the Qur\'an itself promulgates equality between God\'s prophets.(Qur\'an 3:84)\n</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Scholar" title="Scholar">scholars</a> have made the distinction between revelation and <a href="/wiki/Biblical_inspiration" title="Biblical inspiration">inspiration</a>, which according to Muslim theology, all righteous people can receive. Inspiration refers to God inspiring a person to commit some action, as opposed to revelation, which only the prophets received. Moses\'s mother, <a href="/wiki/Jochebed" title="Jochebed">Jochebed</a>, being inspired to send the infant Moses in a cradle down the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_river" title="Nile river">Nile river</a> is a frequently cited example of inspiration, as is <a href="/wiki/Hagar" title="Hagar">Hagar</a> searching for water for the infant <a href="/wiki/Ishmael" title="Ishmael">Ishmael</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jewish_principles_of_faith" title="Jewish principles of faith">Jewish principles of faith</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Mattan Torah" redirects here. Mattan Torah is "the gift of Torah". For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z%27man_Mattan_Torah" title="Z\'man Mattan Torah">Z\'man Mattan Torah</a> ("the time of the giving of the Torah"), see <a href="/wiki/Shavuot" title="Shavuot">Shavuot</a>.</div>\n<p>The term "revelation" is used in two senses in Jewish theology; it either denotes (1) what in rabbinical language is called "Gilluy Shekinah," a manifestation of God by some wondrous act of His which overawes man and impresses him with what he sees, hears, or otherwise perceives of His glorious presence; or it denotes (2) a manifestation of His will through oracular words, signs, statutes, or laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-je_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-je-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, issues of epistemology have been addressed by <a href="/wiki/Jewish_philosophy" title="Jewish philosophy">Jewish philosophers</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saadiah_Gaon" title="Saadiah Gaon">Saadiah Gaon</a> (882&#8211;942) in his <a href="/wiki/Emunoth_ve-Deoth" title="Emunoth ve-Deoth">Book of Beliefs and Opinions</a>; <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> (1135&#8211;1204) in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guide_for_the_Perplexed" title="Guide for the Perplexed">Guide for the Perplexed</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Bergmann" title="Hugo Bergmann">Samuel Hugo Berman</a>, professor of philosophy at the Hebrew University; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Dov_Soloveitchik" title="Joseph Dov Soloveitchik">Joseph Dov Soloveitchik</a> (1903&#8211;1993), talmudic scholar and philosopher; <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gillman" title="Neil Gillman">Neil Gillman</a>, professor of philosophy at the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Theological_Seminary_of_America" title="Jewish Theological Seminary of America">Jewish Theological Seminary of America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elliot_N._Dorff" title="Elliot N. Dorff">Elliot N. Dorff</a>, professor of philosophy at the <a href="/wiki/American_Jewish_University" title="American Jewish University">American Jewish University</a>.\n</p><p>One of the major trends in modern Jewish philosophy was the attempt to develop a theory of Judaism through existentialism. One of the primary players in this field was <a href="/wiki/Franz_Rosenzweig" title="Franz Rosenzweig">Franz Rosenzweig</a>. His major work, <i>Star of Redemption</i>, expounds a philosophy in which he portrays the relationships between God, humanity and world as they are connected by creation, revelation and redemption.\nConservative Jewish philosophers <a href="/wiki/Elliot_N._Dorff" title="Elliot N. Dorff">Elliot N. Dorff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gillman" title="Neil Gillman">Neil Gillman</a> take the existentialist philosophy of Rosenzweig as one of their starting points for understanding <a href="/wiki/Jewish_philosophy" title="Jewish philosophy">Jewish philosophy</a>. (They come to different conclusions, however.)\n</p><p>Rabbinic Judaism, and contemporary <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Judaism" title="Orthodox Judaism">Orthodox Judaism</a>, hold that the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> (Pentateuch) extant today is essentially the same one that the whole of the Jewish people received on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai" title="Mount Sinai">Mount Sinai</a>, from God, upon their Exodus from Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Beliefs that God gave a "Torah of truth" to Moses (and the rest of the people), that Moses was the greatest of the prophets, and that the Law given to Moses will never be changed, are three of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirteen_Principles_of_Faith" title="Thirteen Principles of Faith">Thirteen Principles of Faith</a> of Orthodox Judaism according to <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>.\n</p><p>Orthodox Judaism believes that in addition to the written Torah, God also revealed to Moses a set of oral teachings, called the <a href="/wiki/Oral_Torah" title="Oral Torah">Oral Torah</a>. In addition to this revealed law, <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Jewish law</a> contains decrees and enactments made by prophets, rabbis, and sages over the course of Jewish history. <a href="/wiki/Haredi_Judaism" title="Haredi Judaism">Haredi Judaism</a> tends to regard even rabbinic decrees as being of divine origin or divinely inspired, while <a href="/wiki/Modern_Orthodox_Judaism" title="Modern Orthodox Judaism">Modern Orthodox Judaism</a> tends to regard them as being more potentially subject to human error, although due to the Biblical verse "Do not stray from their words" ("Deuteronomy 17:11) it is still accepted as binding law.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Conservative_Judaism" title="Conservative Judaism">Conservative Judaism</a> tends to regard both the Torah and the Oral law as not verbally revealed. The Conservative approach tends to regard the Torah as compiled by redactors in a manner similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary_Hypothesis" title="Documentary Hypothesis">Documentary Hypothesis</a>. However, Conservative Jews also regard the authors of the Torah as divinely inspired, and many regard at least portions of it as originating with Moses. Positions can vary from the position of <a href="/wiki/Joel_Roth" title="Joel Roth">Joel Roth</a>, following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Weiss_HaLivni" title="David Weiss HaLivni">David Weiss HaLivni</a>, that while the Torah originally given to Moses on Mount Sinai became corrupted or lost and had to be recompiled later by redactors, the recompiled Torah is nonetheless regarded as fully Divine and legally authoritative, to the position of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Tucker" title="Gordon Tucker">Gordon Tucker</a> that the Torah, while Divinely inspired, is a largely human document containing significant elements of human error, and should be regarded as the beginning of an ongoing process which is continuing today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Conservative Judaism regards the Oral Law as divinely inspired, but nonetheless subject to human error.\n</p><p>Reform and Reconstructionist Jews also accept the Documentary Hypothesis for the origin of the Torah, and tend to view all of the Oral law as an entirely human creation. <a href="/wiki/Reform_Judaism" title="Reform Judaism">Reform</a> believe that the Torah is not a <a href="/wiki/Direct_revelation" title="Direct revelation">direct revelation</a> from God, but is a document written by human ancestors, carrying human understanding and experience, and seeking to answer the question: \'What does God require of us?\'. They believe that, though it contains many \'core-truths\' about God and humanity, it is also time bound. They believe that God\'s will is revealed through the interaction of humanity and God throughout history, and so, in that sense, Torah is a product of an ongoing revelation. <a href="/wiki/Reconstructionist_Judaism" title="Reconstructionist Judaism">Reconstructionist Judaism</a> denies the notion of revelation entirely.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prophets">Prophets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Prophets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Nevi%27im" title="Nevi\'im">Nevi\'im</a> (the books of the Prophets) are considered divine and true, this does not imply that the books of the prophets are always read literally. Jewish tradition has always held that prophets used metaphors and analogies. There exists a wide range of commentaries explaining and elucidating those verses consisting of metaphor. <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_Judaism" title="Rabbinic Judaism">Rabbinic Judaism</a> regards Moses as the greatest of the prophets, and this view is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirteen_Principles_of_Faith" title="Thirteen Principles of Faith">Thirteen Principles of Faith</a> of traditional Judaism. Consistent with the view that revelation to Moses was generally clearer than revelation to other prophets, Orthodox views of revelation to prophets other than Moses have included a range of perspectives as to directness. For example, <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guide_for_the_Perplexed" title="The Guide for the Perplexed">The Guide for the Perplexed</a></i> said that accounts of revelation in the Nevi\'im were not always as literal as in the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> and that some prophetic accounts reflect allegories rather than literal commands or predictions.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Conservative_Judaism" title="Conservative Judaism">Conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">Rabbi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jewish_philosophy" title="Jewish philosophy">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Joshua_Heschel" title="Abraham Joshua Heschel">Abraham Joshua Heschel</a> (1907&#8211;1972), author of a number of works on prophecy, said that, "Prophetic inspiration must be understood <i>as an event</i>, not as <i>a process</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In his work <i><a href="/wiki/God_in_Search_of_Man" title="God in Search of Man">God in Search of Man</a></i>, he discussed the experience of being a prophet. In his book <i>Prophetic Inspiration After the Prophets: Maimonides and Others</i>, Heschel references to continued prophetic inspiration in Jewish <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_literature" title="Rabbinic literature">rabbinic literature</a> following the destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a> and into medieval and even Modern times. He wrote that\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"To convey what the prophets experienced, the Bible could either use terms of descriptions or terms of indication. Any description of the act of revelation in empirical categories would have produced a caricature. That is why all the Bible does is to state that revelation happened. How it happened is something they could only convey in words that are evocative and suggestive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_revelations">Recent revelations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revelation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Recent revelations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Miracle_of_the_Sun" title="Miracle of the Sun">The miracle of the Sun</a> is probably the best-known revelation of recent times, but while some still consider it to be a genuine miracle,
