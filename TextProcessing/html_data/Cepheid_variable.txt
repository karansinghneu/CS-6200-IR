class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress well-defined stable <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">period</a> and amplitude.\n</p><p>A strong direct relationship between a Cepheid variable\'s <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">pulsation period</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udalski99_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-udalski99-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sos08_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sos08-2">[2]</a></sup> established Cepheids as important <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder#Galactic_distance_indicators" title="Cosmic distance ladder">indicators of cosmic benchmarks</a> for scaling <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">galactic and extragalactic distances</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2001-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tammannsandage2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tammannsandage2008-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_Clouds" title="Magellanic Clouds">Magellanic Clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This discovery allows one to know the true luminosity of a Cepheid by simply observing its pulsation period. This in turn allows one to determine the distance to the star, by comparing its known luminosity to its observed brightness.\n</p><p>The term <i>Cepheid</i> originates from <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">Delta Cephei</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Cepheus_(constellation)" title="Cepheus (constellation)">Cepheus</a>, identified by <a Delta Scuti variables and RR Lyrae variables are not generally treated with Cepheid variables although their pulsations originate with the same helium ionisation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kappa_mechanism" title="Kappa mechanism">kappa mechanism</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Cepheids">Classical Cepheids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical Cepheids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_Cepheid_variable" title="Classical Cepheid variable">Classical Cepheid variable</a></div>\n<p>Classical Cepheids (also known as Population I Cepheids, type I Cepheids, or Delta Cepheid variables) undergo pulsations with very regular periods on the order of days to months.  Classical Cepheids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_I" title="Population I">Population I</a> <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable stars</a> which are 4&#8211;20 times more massive than the Sun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner96_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner96-9">[9]</a></sup> and up to 100,000 times more luminous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner10_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner10-10">[10]</a></sup> These Cepheids are yellow bright giants and supergiants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_classification" title="Spectral classification">spectral class</a> F6 &#8211; K2 and their radii change by (~25% for the longer-period <a href="/wiki/HD_84810" title="HD 84810">I Carinae</a>) millions of kilometers during a pulsation cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Classical Cepheids are used to determine distances to galaxies within the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a> and beyond, and are a means by which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a> can be established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2001_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2001-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tammannsandage2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tammannsandage2008-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2010_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2010-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngeow2006_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngeow2006-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macririess2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-macririess2009-13">[13]</a></sup>  Classical Cepheids have also been used to clarify many characteristics of our galaxy, such as the Sun\'s height above the galactic plane and the Galaxy\'s local spiral structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2009_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2009-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A group of classical Cepheids with small amplitudes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinusoidal" title="Sinusoidal">sinusoidal</a> light curves are often separated out as Small Amplitude Cepheids or s-Cepheids, many of them pulsating in the first overtone.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Type_II_Cepheids">Type II Cepheids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Type II Cepheids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Type_II_Cepheid" title="Type II Cepheid">Type II Cepheid</a></div>\n<p>Type II Cepheids (also termed Population II Cepheids) are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">population II</a> variable stars which pulsate with periods typically between 1 and 50 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wallerstein02_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-wallerstein02-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-so08_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-so08-15">[15]</a></sup> Type II Cepheids are typically <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metal</a>-poor, old (~10 Gyr), low mass objects (~half the mass of the Sun). Type II Cepheids are divided into several subgroups by period.  Stars with periods between 1 and 4 days are of the <a href="/wiki/BL_Herculis_variable" title="BL Herculis variable">BL Her subclass</a>, 10&#8211;20 days belong to the <a href="/wiki/W_Virginis_variable" title="W Virginis variable">W Virginis subclass</a>, and stars with periods greater than 20 days belong to the <a href="/wiki/RV_Tauri_variable" title="RV Tauri variable">RV Tauri subclass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wallerstein02_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-wallerstein02-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-so08_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-so08-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Type II Cepheids are used to establish the distance to the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2009_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ku02_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ku02-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mat06_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-mat06-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feast08_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-feast08-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2009b_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2009b-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess09c_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess09c-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mat09_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-mat09-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalous_Cepheids">Anomalous Cepheids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anomalous Cepheids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A group of pulsating stars on the instability strip have periods of less than 2 days, similar to <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">RR Lyrae variables</a> but with higher luminosities.  Anomalous Cepheid variables have masses higher than type II Cepheids, RR Lyrae variables, and our sun.  It is unclear whether they are young stars on a "turned-back" <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_branch" title="Horizontal branch">horizontal branch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_straggler" title="Blue straggler">blue stragglers</a> formed through <a href="/wiki/Mass_transfer" title="Mass transfer">mass transfer</a> in binary systems, or a mix of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caputo_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-caputo-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sosznytski_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-sosznytski-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double-mode_Cepheids">Double-mode Cepheids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Double-mode Cepheids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A small proportion of Cepheid variables have been observed to pulsate in two modes at the same time, usually the fundamental and first overtone, occasionally the second overtone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smolec_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-smolec-24">[24]</a></sup>  A very small number pulsate in three modes, or an unusual combination of modes including higher overtones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On September 10, 1784, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Pigott" title="Edward Pigott">Edward Pigott</a> detected the variability of <a href="/wiki/Eta_Aquilae" title="Eta Aquilae">Eta Aquilae</a>, the first known representative of the class of classical Cepheid variables.  However, the eponymous star for classical Cepheids is <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">Delta Cephei</a>, discovered to be variable by <a href="/wiki/John_Goodricke" title="John Goodricke">John Goodricke</a> a few months later.\n</p><p>A relationship between the period and luminosity for classical Cepheids was discovered in 1908 by <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt" title="Henrietta Swan Leavitt">Henrietta Swan Leavitt</a> in an investigation of thousands of variable stars in the <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_Clouds" title="Magellanic Clouds">Magellanic Clouds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> She published it in 1912<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> with further evidence.\n</p><p>In 1913, <a href="/wiki/Ejnar_Hertzsprung" title="Ejnar Hertzsprung">Ejnar Hertzsprung</a> attempted to find distances to 13 Cepheids using the motion through the sky. His research would later require revision, however. In 1915, <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a> used Cepheids to place initial constraints on the size and shape of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, and of the placement of our Sun within it. In 1924, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> established the distance to classical Cepheid variables in the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a>, until then known as the <i>Andromeda</i> <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy#Spiral_nebula" title="Spiral galaxy"><i>Nebula</i></a>, and showed that the variables were not members of the Milky Way.  Hubble\'s finding settled the question raised in the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy)" title="Great Debate (astronomy)">Great Debate</a>" of whether the Milky Way represented the entire Universe or was merely one of numerous <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> in the Universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubble1925_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-hubble1925-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1929, Hubble and <a href="/wiki/Milton_L._Humason" title="Milton L. Humason">Milton L. Humason</a> formulated what is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_Law" title="Hubble\'s Law">Hubble\'s Law</a> by combining Cepheid distances to several galaxies with <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a>\'s measurements of the speed at which those galaxies recede from us. They discovered that the Universe is expanding (see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expansion of the Universe</a>).  However, the expansion of the Universe was posited several years before by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema&#238;tre">Georges Lema&#238;tre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lemaitre1927_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-lemaitre1927-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the mid 20th century, significant problems with the astronomical distance scale were resolved by dividing the Cepheids into different classes with very different properties.  In the 1940s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Heinrich_Walter_Baade" title="Wilhelm Heinrich Walter Baade">Walter Baade</a> recognized two separate populations of Cepheids (classical and type II). Classical Cepheids are younger and more massive population I stars, whereas type II Cepheids are older fainter Population II stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wallerstein02_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-wallerstein02-14">[14]</a></sup> Classical Cepheids and type II Cepheids follow different period-luminosity relationships. The luminosity of type II Cepheids is, on average, less than classical Cepheids by about 1.5 <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">magnitudes</a> (but still brighter than <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">RR Lyrae</a> stars).   Baade\'s seminal discovery led to a twofold increase in the distance to <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">M31</a>, and the extragalactic distance scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baade_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-baade-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">RR Lyrae stars</a>, then known as Cluster Variables, were recognized fairly early as being a separate class of variable, due in part to their short periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncertainties_in_Cepheid_determined_distances">Uncertainties in Cepheid determined distances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uncertainties in Cepheid determined distances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Chief among the uncertainties tied to the classical and type II Cepheid distance scale are: the nature of the period-luminosity relation in various <a href="/wiki/Passband" title="Passband">passbands</a>, the impact of <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a> on both the zero-point and slope of those relations, and the effects of photometric contamination (blending) and a changing (typically unknown) extinction law on Cepheid distances.  All these topics are actively debated in the literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tammannsandage2008_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-tammannsandage2008-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner10_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-turner10-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngeow2006_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ngeow2006-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2009b_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2009b-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benedict2007_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-benedict2007-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanekudalski1999_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-stanekudalski1999-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udalski2001_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-udalski2001-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macri2006_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-macri2006-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bono2008_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-bono2008-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madore2009_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-madore2009-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scowcroft2009_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-scowcroft2009-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2010_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2010-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These unresolved matters have resulted in cited values for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a> (established from Classical Cepheids) ranging between 60&#160;km/s/Mpc and 80&#160;km/s/Mpc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2001_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2001-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tammannsandage2008_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-tammannsandage2008-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2010_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2010-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngeow2006_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ngeow2006-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macririess2009_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-macririess2009-13">[13]</a></sup> Resolving this discrepancy is one of the foremost problems in astronomy since the cosmological parameters of the Universe may be constrained by supplying a precise value of the Hubble constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freedman2010_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2010-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macririess2009_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-macririess2009-13">[13]</a></sup> Uncertainties have diminished over the years, due in part to discoveries such as <a href="/wiki/RS_Puppis" title="RS Puppis">RS Puppis</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">Delta Cephei</a> is also of particular importance as a calibrator of the Cepheid period-luminosity relation since its distance is among the most precisely established for a Cepheid, partly because it is a member of a <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star cluster</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dezeeuw1999_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-dezeeuw1999-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2012_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2012-42">[42]</a></sup> and the availability of precise <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> parallaxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benedict02_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-benedict02-43">[43]</a></sup>  The accuracy of the distance measurements to Cepheid variables and other bodies within 7,500 lightyears is vastly improved by combining images from Hubble taken six months apart when the Earth and Hubble are on opposite sides of the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics_of_the_pulsation">Dynamics of the pulsation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamics of the pulsation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The accepted explanation for the pulsation of Cepheids is called the Eddington valve,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9A-mechanism" title="&#922;-mechanism">&#954;-mechanism</a>, where the Greek letter &#954; (kappa) denotes gas opacity.\n<a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> is the gas thought to be most active in the process. Doubly <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionized</a> helium (helium whose atoms are missing both electrons) is more opaque than singly ionized helium.  The more helium is heated, the more ionized it becomes. At the dimmest part of a Cepheid\'s cycle, the ionized gas in the outer layers of the star is opaque, and so is heated by the star\'s radiation, and due to the increased temperature, begins to expand. As it expands, it cools, and so becomes less ionized and therefore more transparent, allowing the radiation to escape. Then the expansion stops, and reverses due to the star\'s gravitational attraction. The process then repeats.\n</p><p>The mechanics of the pulsation as a heat-engine was proposed in 1917 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington" title="Arthur Stanley Eddington">Arthur Stanley Eddington</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> (who wrote at length on the dynamics of Cepheids), but it was not until 1953 that <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Alexandrovich_Zhevakin" title="Sergei Alexandrovich Zhevakin">S. A. Zhevakin</a> identified ionized helium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> as a likely valve for the engine.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Classical Cepheids include: <a href="/wiki/Eta_Aquilae" title="Eta Aquilae">Eta Aquilae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Geminorum" title="Zeta Geminorum">Zeta Geminorum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beta_Doradus" title="Beta Doradus">Beta Doradus</a>, <a href="/wiki/RT_Aurigae" title="RT Aurigae">RT Aurigae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">Polaris</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">Delta Cephei</a>.</li>\n<li>Type II Cepheids include: <a href="/wiki/W_Virginis" title="W Virginis">W Virginis</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=BL_Herculis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="BL Herculis (page does not exist)">BL Herculis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Anomalous Cepheids include: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=XZ_Ceti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="XZ Ceti (page does not exist)">XZ Ceti</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-szabados_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-szabados-49">[49]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/BL_Bo%C3%B6tis" title="BL Bo&#246;tis">BL Bo&#246;tis</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cepheid_variable&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
