Warm fronts lie within broader troughs of low pressure than <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>, and move more slowly than the cold fronts which usually follow because cold air is denser and less easy to remove from the Earth\'s surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DR-1">[1]</a></sup>  This also forces temperature differences across warm fronts to be broader in scale.  \nClouds ahead of the warm front are mostly <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a>, and rainfall gradually increases as the front approaches.  <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fog</a> can also occur preceding a warm frontal passage. Clearing and warming is usually rapid after frontal passage.  If the warm air mass is unstable, thunderstorms may be embedded among the stratiform clouds ahead of the front, and after frontal passage thundershowers may continue.  On <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">weather maps</a>, the surface location of a warm front is marked with a red line of semicircles pointing in the direction of travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DR-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warm_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airmassesorigin.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="226" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Airmassesorigin.png/250px-Airmassesorigin.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Airmassesorigin.png 1.5x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airmassesorigin.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Different air masses that affect North America, as well as other continents, tend to be separated by frontal boundaries.</div></div></div>\n<p>Air masses are large bodies of air with similar properties of <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> that form over source regions.  The warm air mass behind a warm front is not only warmer, but often (but not always) also higher in humidity than the colder air preceding it. Because of a warm air mass&#8217;s higher temperature and thus lesser <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, mixing between the two air masses is unlikely. Being light, the warm air mass is unable to displace the cooler air mass and instead is forced upward along the upper boundary of the colder air in a process known as overrunning. The boundary between the two air masses has a gradual slope of 1:200 and lifting is slow but persistent.\n</p><p>As the air mass rises into regions of lower pressure, it expands and cools. As it cools, any <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> that is present will <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condense</a> and form extensive <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> cover.  The first clouds that indicate an approaching warm front tend to be mostly high <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus</a> at first, changing to <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus</a> as the front approaches. However, if <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus_cloud" title="Cirrocumulus cloud">cirrocumulus</a> also appears, there is greater airmass instability approaching ahead of the front. When these high clouds progressively invade the sky and the barometric pressure begins to fall, precipitation associated with the disturbance is likely about 6 to 8 hours away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Justice_Perrin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Justice_Perrin-2">[2]</a></sup>  A thickening and lowering of these high clouds into middle-&#233;tage <a href="/wiki/Altostratus_cloud" title="Altostratus cloud">altostratus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Altocumulus_cloud" title="Altocumulus cloud">altocumulus</a> is a good sign the warm front or low has moved closer and precipitation may begin within less than six hours.  Once the clouds have thickened to 2,500 metres (8,200&#160;ft) from the earth&#8217;s surface, precipitation can begin to fall from heavy <a href="/wiki/Nimbostratus_cloud" title="Nimbostratus cloud">nimbostratus</a>.  If unstable altocumulus  castellanus accompanies or takes the place of the main altostratus layer, cumulus congestus or cumulonimbus producing showers or thunderstorms may follow.  Low stratus and stratocumulus commonly form underneath the main precipitating clouds.\n</p><p>A warm front is also defined as the transition zone where a warmer air mass is replacing a cooler air mass. Warm fronts generally move from southwest to northeast.  If the warmer air originates over the ocean, it is not only warmer but also more moist than the air ahead of it.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warm_front&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>If the air mass is relatively stable, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> will increase until the front reaches the location, at which time the clouds can extend all the way to the earth&#8217;s surface as <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>.  Once the front passes, the location experiences some warming and clearing.  If the <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a> is unstable, <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> may precede and follow the front and temperature changes will be larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>, a warm front causes a shift of <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> blowing from southeast to southwest, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a> a shift from winds blowing from northeast to northwest. Temperatures are often warmer than they are before the warm front or after the cold front. Cloud types can be mixed, but usually consist of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a>, which can range to being broken to covering the entire sky depending on distance from the centre of low pressure. Temperature rises and the dew point remains steady.
