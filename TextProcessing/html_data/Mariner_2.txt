officials after the successful completion of the mission, 1963</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Mariner 2</b> (<b>Mariner-Venus 1962</b>), an American space probe to <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, was the first robotic <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> to conduct a successful planetary encounter.  The first successful <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> in the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Mariner_program" title="Mariner program">Mariner program</a>, it was a simplified version of the Block I spacecraft of the <a href="/wiki/Ranger_program" title="Ranger program">Ranger program</a> and an exact copy of <a href="/wiki/Mariner_1" title="Mariner 1">Mariner 1</a>. The missions of Mariner 1 and 2 spacecraft are together sometimes known as the Mariner R missions. Original plans called for the probes to be launched on the <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Centaur" title="Atlas-Centaur">Atlas-Centaur</a>, but serious developmental problems with that vehicle forced a switch to the much smaller Agena B stage. As such, the design of the Mariner R vehicles was greatly simplified. Far less instrumentation was carried than on the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a> probes of this period, including no TV camera as the Atlas-Agena B had only half as much lift capacity as the Soviet 8K78 booster. The Mariner 2 spacecraft was launched from Cape Canaveral on August 27, 1962 and passed as close as 34,773 kilometers (21,607&#160;mi) to Venus on December 14, 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Mariner probe consisted of a 100&#160;cm (39.4&#160;in) diameter hexagonal bus, to which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a>, instrument booms, and <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a> were attached. The scientific instruments on board the Mariner spacecraft were two <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometers</a> (one each for the <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> portions of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectrum</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Micrometeorite" title="Micrometeorite">micrometeorite</a> sensor, a solar <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> sensor, a <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particle</a> sensor, and a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>. These instruments were designed to measure the temperature distribution on the surface of Venus, as well as making basic measurements of Venus\' <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.\n</p><p>The primary mission was to receive communications from the spacecraft in the vicinity of Venus and to perform <a href="/wiki/Radiometry" title="Radiometry">radiometric</a> temperature measurements of the planet. A second objective was to measure the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a> and charged particle environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650072055_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650072055-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650023548_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650023548-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>En route to Venus, Mariner 2 measured the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, a constant stream of charged particles flowing outwards from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, confirming the measurements by <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a> in 1959. It also measured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust" title="Interplanetary dust">interplanetary dust</a>, which turned out to be scarcer than predicted. In addition, Mariner 2 detected high-energy charged particles coming from the Sun, including several brief <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> from outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. As it flew by Venus on December 14, 1962, Mariner 2 scanned the planet with its pair of radiometers, revealing that Venus has cool clouds and an extremely hot surface.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1008" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="277" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Mariner_2_inspection.jpg/440px-Mariner_2_inspection.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_inspection.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pre-flight inspection of the completed Mariner 2 spacecraft</div></div></div>\n<p>The Mariner 2 spacecraft was designed and built by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profile_of_Events_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Profile_of_Events-5">[5]</a></sup> It consisted of a hexagonal base, 1.04 meters across and 0.36 meters thick, which contained six <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> chassis housing the electronics for the science experiments, communications, data encoding, computing, timing, and attitude control, and the power control, battery, and <a href="/wiki/Battery_charger" title="Battery charger">battery charger</a>, as well as the attitude control gas bottles and the rocket engine. On top of the base was a tall pyramid-shaped mast on which the science experiments were mounted, which brought the total height of the spacecraft to 3.66 meters. Attached to either side of the base were rectangular solar panel wings with a total span of 5.05 meters and width of 0.76 meters. Attached by an arm to one side of the base and extending below the spacecraft was a large directional dish antenna.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\nThe power system of Mariner 2 consisted of two solar cell wings, one 183&#160;cm by 76&#160;cm and the other 152&#160;cm by 76&#160;cm (with a 31&#160;cm dacron extension (a <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">solar sail</a>) to balance the solar pressure on the panels), which powered the craft directly or recharged a 1000 watt-hour sealed <a href="/wiki/Silver-oxide_battery" title="Silver-oxide battery">silver-zinc cell battery</a>. This battery was used before the panels were deployed, when the panels were not illuminated by the Sun, and when loads were heavy. A power-switching and booster regulator device controlled the power flow. Communications consisted of a 3-watt transmitter capable of continuous telemetry operation, the large high gain directional dish antenna, a cylindrical omnidirectional antenna at the top of the instrument mast, and two command antennas, one on the end of either solar panel, which received instructions for midcourse maneuvers and other functions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Propulsion for midcourse maneuvers was supplied by a <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">monopropellant</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a>) 225 N retro-rocket. The hydrazine was ignited using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_oxide" title="Aluminum oxide">aluminum oxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Pelletizing" title="Pelletizing">pellets</a>, and thrust direction was controlled by four jet vanes situated below the thrust chamber. Attitude control with a 1 degree pointing error was maintained by a system of nitrogen gas jets. The Sun and Earth were used as references for attitude stabilization. Overall timing and control was performed by a digital Central Computer and Sequencer. Thermal control was achieved through the use of passive reflecting and absorbing surfaces, thermal shields, and movable louvers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_instruments">Scientific instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Only 40 pounds (18&#160;kg) of the spacecraft could be allocated to scientific experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Summary of instruments:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Microwave radiometer</li>\n<li>Infrared radiometer</li>\n<li>Three axis fluxgate magnetometer</li>\n<li>Cosmic ray detector</li>\n<li>Cosmic dust detector</li>\n<li>Solar plasma spectrometer</li>\n<li>Particle detector</li></ul>\n<p>The following scientific instruments were mounted on the instrument mast and base:\n</p>\n<ul><li>A two-channel <b><a href="/wiki/Microwave_radiometer" title="Microwave radiometer">microwave radiometer</a></b> of the crystal video type operating in the standard <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke" title="Robert H. Dicke">Dicke</a> mode of chopping between the main antenna, pointed at the target, and a reference horn pointed at cold space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660006862_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660006862-8">[8]</a></sup> It was used to determine the absolute temperature of Venus\' surface and details concerning its atmosphere through its microwave-radiation characteristics, including the daylight and dark hemispheres, and in the region of the terminator. Measurements were performed simultaneously in two frequency bands of 13.5&#160;mm and 19&#160;mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Feb64_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Feb64-9">[9]</a></sup> The total weight of the radiometer was 22 pounds (10&#160;kg). Its average power consumption was 4 watts and its peak power consumption 9 watts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="364" data-file-width="556" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png/220px-Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png/330px-Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png/440px-Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_infrared_radiometer.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Labeled diagram of the infrared radiometer design</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>A two-channel <b><a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometer</a></b> to measure the effective temperatures of small areas of Venus. The radiation that was received could originate from the planetary surface, clouds in the atmosphere, the atmosphere itself or a combination of these. The radiation was received in two spectral ranges: 8 to 9&#160;<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">&#956;m</a> (0.00031 to 0.00035 inches) (focused on 8.4&#160;&#956;m) and 10 to 10.8&#160;&#956;m (0.00039 to 0.00043 inches) (focused on 10.4&#160;&#956;m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup> The latter corresponding to the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Mar63_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Mar63-11">[11]</a></sup> The total weight of the infrared radiometer, which was housed in a magnesium casting, was 1.3&#160;kg, and it required 2.4 watts of power. It was designed to measure radiation temperatures between 200 and approximately 500 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Nov63_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Nov63-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<ul><li>A three-axis <b><a href="/wiki/Magnetometer#Fluxgate_magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">fluxgate magnetometer</a></b> to measure planetary and interplanetary magnetic fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup> Three probes were incorporated in its sensors, so it could obtain three mutually orthogonal components of the field vector. Readings of these components were separated by 1.9 seconds. It had three analog outputs that had each two sensitivity scales: &#177; 64 &#947; and &#177; 320 &#947; (1 &#947; = 1 nano<a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">tesla</a>). These scales were automatically switched by the instrument. The field that the magnetometer observed was the super-position of a nearly constant spacecraft field and the interplanetary field. Thus, it effectively measured only the changes in the interplanetary field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coleman_et_al._13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman_et_al.-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<ul><li>An <b><a href="/wiki/Ionization_chamber" title="Ionization chamber">ionization chamber</a></b> with matched <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geiger-M%C3%BCller_tube" title="Geiger-M&#252;ller tube">Geiger-M&#252;ller tubes</a></b> (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> detector) to measure high-energy cosmic radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<ul><li>A <b>particle detector</b> (implemented through use of an Anton type 213 Geiger-M&#252;ller tube) to measure lower radiation (especially near Venus),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank-15">[15]</a></sup> also known as the Iowa detector, as it was provided by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-14">[14]</a></sup> It was a miniature tube having a 1.2&#160;mg/cm<sup>2</sup> mica window about 0.3&#160;cm in diameter and weighing about 60 g. It detected soft <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a> efficiently and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> inefficiently, and was previously used in <a href="/wiki/Injun_(satellite)" title="Injun (satellite)">Injun 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Explorer_12" title="Explorer 12">Explorer 12</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explorer_14" title="Explorer 14">Explorer 14</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank-15">[15]</a></sup> It was able to detect protons above 500&#160;keV in energy and electrons above 35&#160;keV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650072055_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650072055-3">[3]</a></sup> The length of the basic telemetry frame was 887.04 seconds. During each frame, the counting rate of the detector was sampled twice at intervals separated by 37 seconds. The first sampling was the number of counts during an interval of 9.60 seconds (known as the \'long gate\'); the second was the number of counts during an interval of 0.827 seconds (known as the \'short gate\'). The long gate accumulator overflowed on the 256th count and the short gate accumulator overflowed on the 65,536th count. The maximum counting rate of the tube was 50,000 per second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen_&amp;_Frank-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<ul><li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> detector</b> to measure the flux of cosmic dust particles in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="322" data-file-width="431" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png/220px-Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png/330px-Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_plasma_probe.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Instrument for studying plasma</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>A <b>solar plasma <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a></b> to measure the spectrum of low-energy positively charged particles from the Sun, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The magnetometer was attached to the top of the mast below the <a href="/wiki/Omnidirectional_antenna" title="Omnidirectional antenna">omnidirectional antenna</a>. Particle detectors were mounted halfway up the mast, along with the cosmic ray detector. The cosmic dust detector and solar plasma spectrometer were attached to the top edges of the spacecraft base. The microwave radiometer, the infrared radiometer and the radiometer reference horns were rigidly mounted to a 48&#160;cm diameter parabolic radiometer antenna mounted near the bottom of the mast. All instruments were operated throughout the cruise and encounter modes except the radiometers, which were only used in the immediate vicinity of Venus.\n</p><p>In addition to these scientific instruments, Mariner 2 had a data conditioning system (DCS) and a scientific power switching (SPS) unit. The DCS was a solid-state electronic system designed to gather information from the scientific instruments on board the spacecraft. It had four basic functions: analog-to-digital conversion, digital-to-digital conversion, sampling and instrument-calibration timing, and planetary acquisition. The SPS unit was designed to perform the following three functions: control of the application of <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> power to appropriate portions of the science subsystem, application of power to the radiometers and removal of power from the cruise experiments during radiometer calibration periods, and control of the speed and direction of the radiometer scans. The DCS sent signals to the SPS unit to perform the latter two functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_objectives">Mission objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The scientific objectives were:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650072055_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650072055-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Radiometer experiment.</li>\n<li>Infrared experiment.</li>\n<li>Magnetometer experiment.</li>\n<li>Charged particles experiment.</li>\n<li>Plasma experiment.</li>\n<li>Micrometeorite experiment.</li></ul>\n<p>Besides the experiments with the scientific instruments, the objectives of both the Mariner 1 and 2 probes included also engineering objectives:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650072055_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650072055-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Evaluation of the attitude control system.</li>\n<li>Evaluation of the environmental control system.</li>\n<li>Evaluation of the entire power system.</li>\n<li>Evaluation of the communication system.</li></ul>\n<h2><span background-color: RoyalBlue; color:black; font-size:95%; line-height:1.25; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a></span></div></div></div>\n<p>Mariner 2 was launched from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a> Launch Complex 12 at 06:53:14 UTC on August 27, 1962 by a two-stage <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">Atlas-Agena</a> rocket. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neugebauer_&amp;_Snyder_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neugebauer_&amp;_Snyder-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The two-stage <a href="/wiki/Atlas-Agena" title="Atlas-Agena">Atlas-Agena</a> rocket carrying Mariner 1 had veered off-course during its launch on July 22, 1962 due to a defective signal from the Atlas and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_1#Program_error" title="Mariner 1">a bug</a> in the program equations of the ground-based guidance computer, and so the spacecraft was destroyed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_Safety_Officer" title="Range Safety Officer">Range Safety Officer</a>. \n</p><p>Two days after that launch, the backup probe and booster (Atlas vehicle 179D) were rolled out to LC-12. The Atlas proved troublesome to prepare for launch, and multiple serious problems with the autopilot occurred, including a complete replacement of the servoamplifier after it had suffered component damage due to shorted transistors. At 1:53 AM EST on August 27, Mariner 2 was launched. \n</p><p>The flight proceeded normally up to the point of booster engine cutoff, at which point the V-2 vernier engine lost pitch and yaw control. The vernier started oscillating and banging against its stops, resulting in a rapid roll of the launch vehicle that came close to threatening the integrity of the stack. At T+189 seconds, the rolling stopped and the launch continued without incident. The rolling motion of the Atlas resulted in ground guidance losing its lock on the booster and preventing any backup commands from being sent to counteract the roll. The incident was traced to a loose electrical connection in the vernier feedback transducer, which was pushed back into place by the centrifugal force of the roll, which also by fortunate coincidence left the Atlas only a few degrees off from where it started and within the range of the Agena\'s horizontal sensor. As a consequence of this episode, GD/A implemented improved fabrication of wiring harnesses and checkout procedures.\n</p><p>Five minutes after liftoff, the Atlas and Agena-Mariner separated, followed by the first Agena burn and second Agena burn. The Agena-Mariner separation injected the Mariner 2 spacecraft into a geocentric escape hyperbola at 26 minutes 3 seconds after liftoff. The NASA NDIF tracking station at Johannesburg, South Africa, acquired the spacecraft about 31 minutes after launch. Solar panel extension was completed approximately 44 minutes after launch. The Sun lock acquired the Sun about 18 minutes later. The high-gain antenna was extended to its acquisition angle of 72&#176;. The output of the solar panels was slightly above the predicted output. \n</p><p>As all subsystems were performing normally, the battery was fully charged, and the solar panels were providing adequate power, the decision was made on August 29 to turn on cruise science experiments. On September 3rd, the Earth acquisition sequence was initiated, and Earth lock was established 29 minutes later. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-course_maneuver">Mid-course maneuver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mid-course maneuver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The accuracy of the Atlas-Agena was such that a mid-course correction was required to satisfy the mission requirements. The mid-course correction consisted of a roll-turn sequence, followed by a pitch-turn sequence and finally a motor-burn sequence. Preparation commands were sent to the spacecraft at 21:30 UTC on September 4. Initiation of the mid-course maneuver sequence was sent at 22:49:42 UTC and the roll-turn sequence started one hour later. The entire maneuver took approximately 34 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the mid-course maneuver, the sensors lost their lock with the Sun and Earth. At 00:27:00 UTC the Sun re-acquisition began and at 00:34 UTC the Sun was reacquired. Earth re-acquisition started at 02:07:29 UTC and Earth was reacquired at 02:34 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_attitude_control">Loss of attitude control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Loss of attitude control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On September 8 at 12:50 UTC, the spacecraft experienced a problem with <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a>. It automatically turned on the gyros, and the cruise science experiments were automatically turned off. The exact cause is unknown as attitude sensors went back to normal before telemetry measurements could be sampled, but it may have been an Earth-sensor malfunction or a collision with a small unidentified object which temporarily caused the spacecraft to lose Sun lock. A similar experience happened on September 29 at 14:34 UTC. Again, all sensors went back to normal before it could be determined which axis had lost lock. By this date, the Earth sensor brightness indication had essentially gone to zero. This time, however, telemetry data indicated that the Earth-brightness measurement had increased to the nominal value for that point in the trajectory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_panel_output">Solar panel output</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solar panel output">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On October 31, the output from one solar panel (with solar sail attached) deteriorated abruptly. It was diagnosed as a partial short circuit in the panel. As a precaution, the cruise science instruments were turned off. A week later, the panel resumed normal function, and cruise science instruments were turned back on. The panel permanently failed on November 15, but Mariner 2 was close enough to the Sun that one panel could supply adequate power; thus, the cruise science experiments were left active.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-14"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Encounter_with_Venus">Encounter with Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Encounter with Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mariner 2 was the first spacecraft to successfully encounter another planet, passing as close as 34,773 kilometers (21,607&#160;mi) to Venus after 110 days of flight on December 14, 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_encounter">Post encounter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post encounter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After encounter, cruise mode resumed. Spacecraft perihelion occurred on December 27 at a distance of 105,464,560&#160;km. The last transmission from Mariner 2 was received on January 3, 1963 at 07:00 UTC, making the total time from launch to termination of the Mariner 2 mission 129 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparks_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sparks-17">[17]</a></sup>\nMariner 2 remains in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The data produced during the flight consisted of two categories, namely tracking data and telemetry data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparks_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sparks-17">[17]</a></sup> Mariner 2 was the first spacecraft to flyby Venus, and one standout piece of data was the high temperature of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-18">[18]</a></sup> The atmosphere was measured to be 500&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">&#176;C</a> (773&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>; 932&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">&#176;F</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-18">[18]</a></sup> Various properties of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> were also measured for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa.gov_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa.gov-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_observations">Scientific observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scientific observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="337" decoding="async" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png/220px-Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png/330px-Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_radiometric_scans.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Radiometric scanning of Venus</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3220" data-file-width="2520" decoding="async" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg/220px-Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg/330px-Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg/440px-Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_2_venus_data.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A print-out of data from the flyby</div></div></div>\n<p>The microwave radiometer made three scans of Venus in 35 minutes on December 14, 1962 starting at 18:59 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup> The first scan was made on the dark side, the second was near the terminator, and the third was located on the light side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup> The scans with the 19&#160;mm band revealed peak temperatures of 490&#160;&#177;&#160;11&#160;K (216.9&#160;&#177;&#160;11.0&#160;&#176;C; 422.3&#160;&#177;&#160;19.8&#160;&#176;F) on the dark side, 595 &#177; 12 K near the terminator, and 511 &#177; 14 K on the light side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan-20">[20]</a></sup> It was concluded that there is no significant difference in temperature across Venus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the results suggest a <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">limb darkening</a>, an effect which presents cooler temperatures near the edge of the planetary disk and higher temperatures near the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660006862_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660006862-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Feb64_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Feb64-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan-21">[21]</a></sup> This also supported the theory that the Venusian surface was extremely hot or the atmosphere optically thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barath_et_al._Mar63_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Barath_et_al._Mar63-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack_&amp;_Sagan-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The infrared radiometer showed that the 8.4 &#956;m and 10.4 &#956;m radiation temperatures were in agreement with radiation temperatures obtained from Earth-based measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Nov63_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Nov63-12">[12]</a></sup> There was no systematic difference between the temperatures measured on the light side and dark side of the planet, which was also in agreement with Earth-based measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Nov63_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Nov63-12">[12]</a></sup> The limb darkening effect that the microwave radiometer detected was also present in the measurements by both channels of the infrared radiometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Nov63_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Nov63-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan-21">[21]</a></sup> The effect was only slightly present in the 10.4 &#956;m channel but was more pronounced in the 8.4 &#956;m channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup> The 8.4 &#956;m channel also showed a slight phase effect. The phase effect indicated that if a greenhouse effect existed, heat was transported in an efficient manner from the light side to the dark side of the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup> The 8.4 &#956;m and 10.4 &#956;m showed equal radiation temperatures, indicating that the limb darkening effect would appear to come from a cloud structure rather than the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Nov63_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Nov63-12">[12]</a></sup> Thus, if the measured temperatures were actually cloud temperatures instead of surface temperatures, then these clouds would have to be quite thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chase_et_al._Mar63_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chase_et_al._Mar63-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The magnetometer detected a persistent interplanetary magnetic field varying between 2 &#947; and 10 &#947; (<a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">nanotesla</a>), which agrees with prior <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_5" title="Pioneer 5">Pioneer 5</a> observations from 1960. This also means that interplanetary space is rarely empty or field-free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coleman_et_al._13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman_et_al.-13">[13]</a></sup> The magnetometer could detect changes of about 4 &#947; on any of the axes, but no trends above 10 &#947; were detected near Venus, nor were fluctuations seen like those that appear at Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetospheric</a> termination. This means that Mariner 2 found no detectable magnetic field near Venus, although that didn\'t necessarily mean that Venus had none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonett_19-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sonett-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_et_al._22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_et_al.-22">[22]</a></sup> However, if Venus had a magnetic field, then it would have to be at least smaller than 1/10 the magnetic field of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_et_al._22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_et_al.-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650006314_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650006314-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1980, the <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus_project" title="Pioneer Venus project">Pioneer Venus Orbiter</a> indeed showed that Venus has a small weak magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kivelson_&amp;_Russell_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kivelson_&amp;_Russell-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Anton type 213 Geiger-M&#252;ller tube performed as expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19640016686_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19640016686-25">[25]</a></sup> The average rate was 0.6 counts per second. Increases in its counting rate were larger and more frequent than for the two larger tubes, since it was more sensitive to particles of lower energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-15"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup> It detected 7 small solar bursts of radiation during September and October and 2 during November and December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650076538_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650076538-26">[26]</a></sup> The absence of a detectable magnetosphere was also confirmed by the tube; it detected no radiation belt at Venus similar to that of Earth. The count rate would have increased by 10<sup>4</sup>, but no change was measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19660005413_6-16"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19660005413-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_et_al._27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank_et_al.-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was also shown that in interplanetary space, the solar wind streams continuously<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neugebauer_&amp;_Snyder_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neugebauer_&amp;_Snyder-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_&amp;_Wilcox_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ness_&amp;_Wilcox-28">[28]</a></sup>\nand the cosmic dust density is much lower than the near-Earth region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-29">[29]</a></sup>\nImproved estimates of Venus\' mass and the value of the Astronomical Unit were made. Also, research, which was later confirmed by other explorations, suggested that Venus rotates very slowly and in a direction opposite that of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_&amp;_Carpenter_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein_&amp;_Carpenter-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
