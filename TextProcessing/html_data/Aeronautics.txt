For the company, see <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics_Defense_Systems" title="Aeronautics Defense Systems">Aeronautics Defense Systems</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2360" data-file-width="3030" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg/220px-Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg/330px-Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg/440px-Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlantis_on_Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis">Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i></a> on a <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_Carrier_Aircraft" title="Shuttle Carrier Aircraft">Shuttle Carrier Aircraft</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Aeronautics</b> is the science or art involved with the study, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Design_process" title="Design process">design</a>, and manufacturing of air <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> capable machines, and the techniques of operating <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and rockets within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. The British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aeronautical_Society" title="Royal Aeronautical Society">Royal Aeronautical Society</a> identifies the aspects of "aeronautical Art, Science and Engineering" and "the profession of Aeronautics (which expression includes Astronautics)." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the term originally referred solely to <i>operating</i> the aircraft, it has since been expanded to include technology, business, and other aspects related to aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-americana-2">[2]</a></sup> \nThe term "<a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a>" is sometimes used interchangeably with aeronautics, although "aeronautics" includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lighter-than-air" title="Lighter-than-air">lighter-than-air</a> craft such as <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>, and includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_vehicle" title="Ballistic vehicle">ballistic vehicles</a> while "aviation" technically does not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-americana-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A significant part of <b>aeronautical science</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a> called <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, which deals with the motion of air and the way that it interacts with objects in motion, such as an aircraft.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_ideas">Early ideas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early ideas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="364" decoding="async" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg/220px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg/330px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_helicopter_and_lifting_wing.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Designs for flying machines by Leonardo da Vinci, circa 1490</div></div></div>\n<p>Attempts  to fly without any real aeronautical understanding have been made from the earliest times, typically by constructing wings and jumping from a tower with crippling or lethal results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWragg1974_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWragg1974-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Wiser investigators sought to gain some rational understanding through the study of bird flight. An early example appears in ancient Egyptian texts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Later medieval Islamic scientists also made such studies. The founders of modern aeronautics, <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> in the Renaissance and Cayley in 1799, both began their investigations with studies of bird flight.\n</p><p>Man-carrying kites are believed to have been used extensively in ancient China. In 1282 the European explorer <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> described the Chinese techniques then current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pelham_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pelham-4">[4]</a></sup> The Chinese also constructed small hot air balloons, or lanterns, and rotary-wing toys.\n</p><p>An early European to provide any scientific discussion of flight was <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>, who described principles of operation for the lighter-than-air <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">balloon</a> and the flapping-wing <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a>, which he envisaged would be constructed in the future. The lifting medium for his balloon would be an "aether" whose composition he did not know.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWragg197410&#8211;11_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWragg197410&#8211;11-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late fifteenth century, Leonardo da Vinci followed up his study of birds with designs for some of the earliest flying machines, including the flapping-wing <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a> and the rotating-wing <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a>. Although his designs were rational, they were not based on particularly good science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWragg197411_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWragg197411-6">[6]</a></sup> Many of his designs, such as a four-person screw-type helicopter, have severe flaws. He did at least understand that "An object offers as much resistance to the air as the air does to the object."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairlieCayley1965163_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairlieCayley1965163-7">[7]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a> would not publish the <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton.27s_third_law" title="Newton\'s laws of motion">Third law of motion</a> until 1687.) His analysis led to the realisation that manpower alone was not sufficient for sustained flight, and his later designs included a mechanical power source such as a spring. Da Vinci\'s work was lost after his death and did not reappear until it had been overtaken by the work of <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Balloon_flight">Balloon flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Balloon flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ballooning" title="History of ballooning">History of ballooning</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flying_boat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Flying_boat.png/170px-Flying_boat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Flying_boat.png/255px-Flying_boat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Flying_boat.png/340px-Flying_boat.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flying_boat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Francesco Lana de Terzi\'s flying boat concept c.1670</div></div></div>\n<p>The modern era of lighter-than-air flight began early in the 17th century with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>\'s experiments in which he showed that air has weight. Around 1650 <a href="/wiki/Cyrano_de_Bergerac" title="Cyrano de Bergerac">Cyrano de Bergerac</a> wrote some fantasy novels in which he described the principle of ascent using a substance (dew) he supposed to be lighter than air, and descending by releasing a controlled amount of the substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEge19736_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEge19736-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Lana_de_Terzi" title="Francesco Lana de Terzi">Francesco Lana de Terzi</a> measured the pressure of air at sea level and in 1670 proposed the first scientifically credible lifting medium in the form of hollow metal spheres from which all the air had been pumped out. These would be lighter than the displaced air and able to lift an <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airship</a>. His proposed methods of controlling height are still in use today; by carrying ballast which may be dropped overboard to gain height, and by venting the lifting containers to lose height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEge19737_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEge19737-9">[9]</a></sup> In practice de Terzi\'s spheres would have collapsed under air pressure, and further developments had to wait for more practicable lifting gases.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4912" data-file-width="5040" decoding="async" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg/220px-Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg/330px-Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg/440px-Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Montgolfier_brothers_flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Montgolfier brothers flight, 1784</div></div></div>\n<p>From the mid-18th century the <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier brothers</a> in France began experimenting with balloons. Their balloons were made of paper, and early experiments using steam as the lifting gas were short-lived due to its effect on the paper as it condensed. Mistaking smoke for a kind of steam, they began filling their balloons with hot smoky air which they called "electric smoke" and, despite not fully understanding the principles at work, made some successful launches and in 1783 were invited to give a demonstration to the French <i>Acad&#233;mie des Sciences</i>.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> led <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Black" title="Joseph Black">Joseph Black</a> in c. 1780 to propose its use as a lifting gas, though practical demonstration awaited a gas tight balloon material. On hearing of the Montgolfier Brothers\' invitation, the French Academy member <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Charles" title="Jacques Charles">Jacques Charles</a> offered a similar demonstration of a hydrogen balloon. Charles and two craftsmen, the Robert brothers, developed a gas tight material of rubberised silk for the envelope. The hydrogen gas was to be generated by chemical reaction during the filling process.\n</p><p>The Montgolfier designs had several shortcomings, not least the need for dry weather and a tendency for sparks from the fire to set light to the paper balloon. The manned design had a gallery around the base of the balloon rather than the hanging basket of the first, unmanned design, which brought the paper closer to the fire. On their free flight, De Rozier and d\'Arlandes took buckets of water and sponges to douse these fires as they arose. On the other hand, the manned design of Charles was essentially modern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEge197397&#8211;100_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEge197397&#8211;100-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result of these exploits, the hot-air balloon became known as the <i>Montgolfi&#232;re</i> type and the hydrogen balloon the <i>Charli&#232;re</i>.\n</p><p>Charles and the Robert brothers\' next balloon, <i><a href="/wiki/Robert_brothers#Further_ballooning_activities_&#8211;_La_Caroline" title="Robert brothers">La Caroline</a></i>, was a Charli&#232;re that followed <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Meusnier" title="Jean Baptiste Meusnier">Jean Baptiste Meusnier</a>\'s proposals for an elongated dirigible balloon, and was notable for having an outer envelope with the gas contained in a second, inner ballonet. On 19 September 1784, it completed the first flight of over 100&#160;km, between Paris and <a href="/wiki/Beuvry" title="Beuvry">Beuvry</a>, despite the man-powered propulsive devices proving useless.\n</p><p>In an attempt the next year to provide both endurance and controllability, de Rozier developed a balloon having both hot air and hydrogen gas bags, a design which was soon named after him as the <i>Rozi&#232;re.</i> The principle was to use the hydrogen section for constant lift and to navigate vertically by heating and allowing to cool the hot air section, in order to catch the most favourable wind at whatever altitude it was blowing. The balloon envelope was made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldbeaters_skin" title="Goldbeaters skin">goldbeater\'s skin</a>. The first flight ended in disaster and the approach has seldom been used since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEge1965105_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEge1965105-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cayley_and_the_foundation_of_modern_aeronautics">Cayley and the foundation of modern aeronautics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cayley and the foundation of modern aeronautics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_George_Cayley" title="Sir George Cayley">Sir George Cayley</a> (1773-1857) is widely acknowledged as the founder of modern aeronautics. He was first called the "father of the aeroplane" in 1846<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFairlieCayley1965_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFairlieCayley1965-12">[12]</a></sup> and Henson called him the "father of aerial navigation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWragg1974_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWragg1974-3">[3]</a></sup> He was the first true scientific aerial investigator to publish his work, which included for the first time the underlying principles and forces of flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1809 he began the publication of a landmark three-part treatise titled "On Aerial Navigation" (1809&#8211;1810).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AerNav123_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AerNav123-14">[14]</a></sup> In it he wrote the first scientific statement of the problem, "The whole problem is confined within these limits, viz. to make a surface support a given weight by the application of power to the resistance of air." He identified the four vector forces that influence an aircraft: <i><a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">drag</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a></i> and distinguished stability and control in his designs.\n</p><p>He developed the modern conventional form of the fixed-wing aeroplane having a stabilising tail with both horizontal and vertical surfaces, flying gliders both unmanned and manned.\n</p><p>He introduced the use of the whirling arm test rig to investigate the aerodynamics of flight, using it to discover the benefits of the curved or <a href="/wiki/Camber_(aerodynamics)" title="Camber (aerodynamics)">cambered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerofoil" title="Aerofoil">aerofoil</a> over the flat wing he had used for his first glider. He also identified and described the importance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">dihedral</a>, diagonal bracing and drag reduction, and contributed to the understanding and design of <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachutes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWragg1974_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWragg1974-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another significant invention was the tension-spoked wheel, which he devised in order to create a light, strong wheel for aircraft undercarriage.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_19th_century">The 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the 19th century Cayley\'s ideas were refined, proved and expanded on. Important investigators included <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lilienthal" title="Otto Lilienthal">Otto Lilienthal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horatio_Phillips" title="Horatio Phillips">Horatio Phillips</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_20th_century">The 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pedro Paulet, scientist born in the city of Arequipa, Peru in the year of 1874, was one of the first to experiment with propulsion rockets being considered the &#171;Father of Modern Rocket&#187; and by others as the &#171;Father of Aeronautics Modern&#160;&#187;. He developed plans for a "torpedo plane", which is why he is considered ahead of his time. When the internal explosion engines were invented, small enough to be able to propel a flying device with them, a race started between two flight possibilities: the lighter than the air (dirigibles) and the heavier than the air (aeroplanes) .\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">Branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">Aviation</a></div>\n<p>Aviation is the art or practice of aeronautics. Historically aviation meant only heavier-than-air flight, but nowadays it includes flying in balloons and airships.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeronautical_science">Aeronautical science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aeronautical science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Aeronautical science covers the practical theory of aeronautics and aviation, including operations, navigation, air safety and human factors.\n</p><p>A candidate pilot is likely to study for a qualification in aeronautical science.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeronautical_engineering">Aeronautical engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aeronautical engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">Aerospace engineering</a></div>\n<p>Aeronautical engineering covers the design and construction of aircraft, including how they are powered, how they are used and how they are controlled for safe operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A major part of aeronautical engineering is <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, the science of passing through the air.\n</p><p>With the increasing activity in space flight, nowadays aeronautics and astronautics are often combined as <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronautics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aerodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</a></div>\n<p>The science of aerodynamics deals with the motion of air and the way that it interacts with objects in motion, such as an aircraft.\n</p><p>The study of aerodynamics falls broadly into three areas:\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Incompressible_flow" title="Incompressible flow">Incompressible flow</a></i> occurs where the air simply moves to avoid objects, typically at subsonic speeds below that of sound (Mach 1).\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Compressible_flow" title="Compressible flow">Compressible flow</a></i> occurs where shock waves appear at points where the air becomes compressed, typically at speeds above Mach 1.\n</p><p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transonic_flow" title="Transonic flow">Transonic flow</a></i> occurs in the intermediate speed range around Mach 1, where the airflow over an object may be locally subsonic at one point and locally supersonic at another.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a>, spacecraft, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a> which obtains <a In all rockets, the exhaust is formed entirely from <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a> carried within the rocket before use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPE7_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-RPE7-16">[16]</a></sup> Rocket engines work by <a href="/wiki/Reaction_(physics)" title="Reaction (physics)">action and reaction</a>. Rocket engines push rockets forwards simply by throwing their exhaust backwards extremely fast.\n</p><p>Rockets for military and recreational uses date back to at least 13th-century <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_EARLY_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_EARLY-17">[17]</a></sup> Significant scientific, interplanetary and industrial use did not occur until the 20th century, when rocketry was the enabling technology of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">setting foot on the moon</a>.\n</p><p>Rockets are used for <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, weaponry, <a href="/wiki/Ejection_seat" title="Ejection seat">ejection seats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicles</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">exploration</a> of other planets. While comparatively inefficient for low speed use, they are very lightweight and powerful, capable of generating large accelerations and of attaining <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">extremely high speeds</a> with reasonable efficiency.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_rocket" title="Chemical rocket">Chemical rockets</a> are the most common type of rocket and they typically create their exhaust by the combustion of <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">rocket propellant</a>. Chemical rockets store a large amount of energy in an easily released form, and can be very dangerous. However, careful design, testing, construction and use minimizes risks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
