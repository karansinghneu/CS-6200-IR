Geographically they are located east of <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, and north and east of <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>.\n</p><p>The islands were known as the <b>Spice Islands</b> due to the <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mace_(spice)" title="Mace (spice)">mace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">cloves</a> that were originally exclusively found there, the presence of which sparked colonial interest from Europe in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Maluku Islands formed a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Province_of_Indonesia" title="Province of Indonesia">province</a> from Indonesian independence until 1999, when it was split into two provinces. A new province, <a href="/wiki/North_Maluku" title="North Maluku">North Maluku</a>, incorporates the area between <a href="/wiki/Morotai" title="Morotai">Morotai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sula_Islands_Regency" title="Sula Islands Regency">Sula</a>, with the arc of islands from <a href="/wiki/Buru" title="Buru">Buru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seram_Island" title="Seram Island">Seram</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wetar" title="Wetar">Wetar</a> remaining within the existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maluku_Province" title="Maluku Province">Maluku Province</a>. North Maluku is predominantly Muslim, and its capital is <a href="/wiki/Sofifi" title="Sofifi">Sofifi</a> on <a href="/wiki/Halmahera" title="Halmahera">Halmahera</a> island. Maluku province has a larger Christian population, and its capital is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambon_City" title="Ambon City">Ambon</a>. Though originally <a href="/wiki/Melanesians" title="Melanesians">Melanesian</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> many island populations, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda Islands</a>, were massacred in the 17th century during the <a href="/wiki/Dutch%E2%80%93Portuguese_War" title="Dutch&#8211;Portuguese War">spice wars</a>. A second influx of immigrants primarily from Java began in the early twentieth century under the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch</a> and continues in the Indonesian era.\n</p><p>Between 1999 and 2002, <a href="/wiki/Maluku_sectarian_conflict" title="Maluku sectarian conflict">conflict between Muslims and Christians</a> killed thousands and displaced half a million people.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" sources claim that the name comes from a local language with the meaning "the head of a bull" or "the head of something large".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="/wiki/Halmahera" title="Halmahera">Halmahera</a> (the largest of the Maluku Islands).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MONK_7_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MONK_7-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span title="Islam">Islam</a>. Peaceful <a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion" title="Religious conversion">conversion</a> to Islam occurred in many islands<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Inset shows Saint John Baptist Portuguese-built fort on the island</div></div></div>\n<p>The most significant lasting effects of the Portuguese presence was the disruption and reorganization of the Southeast Asian trade, and in eastern Indonesia&#8212;including Maluku&#8212;the introduction of Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp26_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp26-8">[8]</a></sup> The Portuguese had <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Malacca_(1511)" title="Capture of Malacca (1511)">conquered the city-state of Malacca</a> in the early 16th century and their influence was most strongly felt in Maluku and other parts of eastern Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp24_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp24-4">[4]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a> annexed <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> in August 1511, one Portuguese diary noted \'it is thirty years since they became <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> learned of the route to the <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda Islands</a> and other \'Spice Islands\', and sent an exploratory expedition of three vessels under the command of <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Abreu" title="Ant&#243;nio de Abreu">Ant&#243;nio de Abreu</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sim%C3%A3o_Afonso_Bisigudo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sim&#227;o Afonso Bisigudo (page does not exist)">Sim&#227;o Afonso Bisigudo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Serr%C3%A3o" title="Francisco Serr&#227;o">Francisco Serr&#227;o</a> was shipwrecked at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitu" title="Hitu">Hitu</a> island (northern <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Ambon</a>) in 1512. There he established ties with the local ruler who was impressed with his martial skills. The rulers of the competing island states of <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidore" title="Tidore">Tidore</a> also sought Portuguese assistance and the newcomers were welcomed in the area as buyers of supplies and spices during a lull in the regional trade due to the temporary disruption of <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malays_(ethnic_group)" title="Malays (ethnic group)">Malay</a> sailings to the area following the 1511 conflict in Malacca. The spice trade soon revived but the Portuguese would not be able to fully monopolize nor disrupt this trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp24_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp24-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Allying himself with Ternate\'s ruler, Serr&#227;o constructed a fortress on that tiny island and served as the head of a <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenary</a> band of Portuguese seamen under the service of one of the two local feuding sultans who controlled most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spices" title="Spices">spice</a> trade. Both Serr&#227;o and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>, however, perished before they could meet one another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp24_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp24-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Portuguese first landed in Ambon in 1513, but it only became the new centre for their activities in Maluku following the expulsion from Ternate. European power in the region was weak and Ternate became an expanding, fiercely Islamic and anti-European state under the rule of Sultan Baab Ullah (r. 1570&#8211;1583) and his son Sultan Said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp25_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp25-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following Portuguese missionary work, there have been large Christian communities in eastern Indonesia through to contemporary times, which has contributed to a sense of shared interest with Europeans, particularly among the Ambonese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp25_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp25-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fort_te_Saparoea.jpg"><img alt="" 1846</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch">Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: After Indonesian independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With the declaration of a single republic of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> in 1950 to replace the federal state, a <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_South_Maluku" title="Republic of South Maluku">Republic of South Maluku</a> (Republik Maluku Selatan, RMS) was declared and attempted to secede.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and led by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Soumokil" title="Chris Soumokil">Chris Soumokil</a> (former Supreme Prosecutor of the Eastern Indonesia state) and supported by the Moluccan members of the Netherlands special troops. This movement was defeated by the Indonesian army and by special agreement with the Netherlands the troops were transferred to the Netherlands.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Maluku is one of the first provinces of Indonesia, proclaimed in 1945 until 1999, when the Maluku Utara and Halmahera Tengah Regencies were split off as a separate province of <a href="/wiki/North_Maluku" title="North Maluku">North Maluku</a>. Its capital used to be <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a>, on a small island to the west of the large island of <a href="/wiki/Halmahera" title="Halmahera">Halmahera</a>, but has been moved to <a href="/wiki/Sofifi" title="Sofifi">Sofifi</a> on Halmahera itself. The capital of the remaining part of Maluku province remains at <a href="/wiki/Ambon,_Maluku" title="Ambon, Maluku">Ambon</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1999.E2.80.932003_inter-communal_conflict"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999&#8211;2003_inter-communal_conflict">1999&#8211;2003 inter-communal conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1999&#8211;2003 inter-communal conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maluku_sectarian_conflict" title="Maluku sectarian conflict">Maluku sectarian conflict</a></div>\n<p>Religious conflict erupted across the islands in January 1999. The subsequent 18 months were characterized by fighting between largely local groups of Muslims and Christians, the destruction of thousands of houses, the displacement of approximately 500,000 people, the loss of thousands of lives, and the segregation of Muslims and Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology_and_geography">Geology and geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geology and geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="728" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg/220px-L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg/330px-L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg/440px-L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:L%C3%ADnea_de_Wallace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of <a href="/wiki/Wallacea" title="Wallacea">Wallacea</a>; upper right corner facing North. The red line denotes the western border of Wallacea. The eastern border corresponds to the light Australia-New Guinea shelf.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Maluku Islands have a total area of 850,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>, 90% of which is sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MONK_9_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MONK_9-14">[14]</a></sup> There are an estimated 1027 islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup> The largest two islands, <a href="/wiki/Halmahera" title="Halmahera">Halmahera</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seram" title="Seram">Seram</a> are sparsely populated, while the most developed, <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Ambon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a> are small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The majority of the islands are forested and mountainous. The <a href="/wiki/Tanimbar_Islands" title="Tanimbar Islands">Tanimbar Islands</a> are dry and hilly, while the <a href="/wiki/Aru_Islands_Regency" title="Aru Islands Regency">Aru Islands</a> are flat and swampy. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Binaya" title="Mount Binaya">Mount Binaya</a> (3027 m) on Seram is the highest mountain. A number of islands, such as Ternate (1721 m) and the TNS islands, are volcanoes emerging from the sea with villages sited around their coasts. There have been over 70 serious volcanic eruptions in the last 500 years and earthquakes are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ternate_Island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Ternate_Island.jpg/220px-Ternate_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Ternate_Island.jpg/330px-Ternate_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Ternate_Island.jpg/440px-Ternate_Island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ternate_Island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ternate_Island" title="Ternate Island">Ternate Island</a>, as seen from Halmahera</div></div></div>\n<p>The geology of the Maluku Islands share much similar history, characteristics and processes with the neighbouring Nusa Tenggara region. There is a long history of geological study of these regions since <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Indonesian colonial times</a>; however, the geological formation and progression is not fully understood, and theories of the island\'s geological evolution have changed extensively in recent decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monk_1996,_page_9_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monk_1996,_page_9-16">[16]</a></sup> The Maluku Islands comprise some of the most geologically complex and active regions in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> resulting from their position at the meeting point of four geological plates and two continental blocks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biota_and_environment">Biota and environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Biota and environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">Biogeographically</a>, all of the islands apart from the Aru group lie in <a href="/wiki/Wallacea" title="Wallacea">Wallacea</a>, the region between the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Shelf" title="Sunda Shelf">Sunda Shelf</a> (part of the Asia block), and the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Shelf</a> (part of the Australian block). More specifically, they lie between <a href="/wiki/Max_Carl_Wilhelm_Weber" title="Max Carl Wilhelm Weber">Weber\'s Line</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lydekker%27s_Line" title="Lydekker\'s Line">Lydekker\'s Line</a>, and thus have a fauna that is rather more Australasian than Asian. Malukan biodiversity and its distribution are affected by various tectonic activities; most of the islands are geologically young, being from 1 million to 15 million years old, and have never been attached to the larger landmasses. The Maluku islands differ from other areas in Indonesia; they contain some of the country\'s smallest islands, coral island reefs scattered through some of the deepest seas in the world, and no large islands such as <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>. Flora and fauna immigration between islands is thus restricted, leading to a high rate of endemic biota evolving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monk_1996,_page_9_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Monk_1996,_page_9-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ecology of the Maluku Islands has fascinated naturalists for centuries; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Wallace" title="Alfred Wallace">Alfred Wallace</a>\'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Malay_Archipelago" title="The Malay Archipelago">The Malay Archipelago</a></i> was the first significant study of the area\'s natural history, and remains an important resource for studying Indonesian biodiversity. Maluku is the subject of two major historical works of natural history by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Eberhard_Rumphius" title="Georg Eberhard Rumphius">Georg Eberhard Rumphius</a>: the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Herbarium_Amboinense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Herbarium Amboinense (page does not exist)">Herbarium Amboinense</a></i> and the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Amboinsche_Rariteitkamer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Amboinsche Rariteitkamer (page does not exist)">Amboinsche Rariteitkamer</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rainforest covered most of northern and central Maluku, which, on the smaller islands has been replaced by plantations, including the region\'s endemic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloves" title="Cloves">cloves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Tanimbar_Islands" title="Tanimbar Islands">Tanimbar Islands</a> and other southeastern islands are arid and sparsely vegetated, much like nearby <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1997 the <a href="/wiki/Manusela_National_Park" title="Manusela National Park">Manusela National Park</a>, and in 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Aketajawe-Lolobata_National_Park" title="Aketajawe-Lolobata National Park">Aketajawe-Lolobata National Park</a>, were established, for the protection of endangered species.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <a href="/wiki/King_bird-of-paradise" title="King bird-of-paradise">king</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twelve-wired_bird-of-paradise" title="Twelve-wired bird-of-paradise">twelve-wired</a> birds-of paradise.</div></div></div>\n<p>Nocturnal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupials" title="Marsupials">marsupials</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cuscus" title="Cuscus">cuscus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandicoots" title="Bandicoots">bandicoots</a>, make up the majority of the mammal species, and introduced mammals include <a href="/wiki/Malayan_civet" title="Malayan civet">Malayan civets</a> and wild pigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup> Bird species include approximately 100 endemics with the greatest variety on the large islands of Halmahera and Seram. North Maluku has two species of endemic birds of paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup> Uniquely among the Maluku Islands, the <a href="/wiki/Aru_Islands_Regency" title="Aru Islands Regency">Aru Islands</a> have a purely Papuan fauna including kangaroos, cassowaries, and birds of paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While many ecological problems affect both small islands and large landmasses, small islands suffer their particular problems. Development pressures on small islands are increasing, although their effects are not always anticipated. Although Indonesia is richly endowed with natural resources, the resources of the small islands of Maluku are limited and specialised; furthermore, human resources in particular are limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monk,_1996_1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monk,_1996_1-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>General observations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> about small islands that can be applied to the Maluku Islands include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monk,_1996_1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Monk,_1996_1-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>a higher proportion of the landmass will be affected by <a href="/wiki/List_of_volcanoes_in_Indonesia" title="List of volcanoes in Indonesia">volcanic activity</a>, earthquakes, landslips, and cyclone damage;</li>\n<li>Climates are more likely to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_climate" title="Maritime climate">maritime</a> influenced;</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">Catchment areas</a> are smaller and degree of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> higher;</li>\n<li>A higher proportion of the landmass is made up of coastal areas;</li>\n<li>A higher degree of environmental specialisation, including a higher proportion of <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> species in an overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depauperate" title="Depauperate">depauperate</a> community;</li>\n<li>Societies may retain a strong sense of culture having developed in relative isolation;</li>\n<li>Small island populations are more likely to be affected by economic migration.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Central and southern Maluku Islands experience the dry monsoon between October to March and the wet monsoon from May to August, which is the reverse of the rest of Indonesia. The dry monsoon\'s average maximum temperature is 30&#160;&#176;C while the wet\'s average maximum is 23&#160;&#176;C. Northern Maluku has its wet monsoon from December to March in line with the rest of Indonesia. Each island group have their own climatic variations, and the larger islands tend to have drier coastal lowlands and their mountainous hinterlands are wetter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span (T-AH-19)</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Maluku\'s population is about 2 million, less than 1% of Indonesia\'s population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over 130 languages were once spoken across the islands; however many have now switched to the creoles of <a href="/wiki/North_Moluccan_Malay" title="North Moluccan Malay">Ternate Malay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambonese_Malay" title="Ambonese Malay">Ambonese Malay</a>, the lingua franca of northern and southern Maluku respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-9"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A long history of trade and seafaring has resulted in a high degree of mixed ancestry in Malukans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-10"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_peoples" title="Austronesian peoples">Austronesian peoples</a> added to the native <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesian population</a> around 2000&#160;BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Melanesian features are strongest in the islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kei_Islands" title="Kei Islands">Kei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aru_Islands_Regency" title="Aru Islands Regency">Aru</a> and amongst the interior people of the islands <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seram" title="Seram">Seram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buru" title="Buru">Buru</a>. Later added to this Austronesian-Melanesian mix were some Indian and Arab strain. More recent arrivals include <a href="/wiki/Bugis" title="Bugis">Bugis</a> trader settlers from Sulawesi and <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmigrants" title="Transmigrants">transmigrants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-11"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maluku_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cloves and nutmeg are still cultivated, as are cocoa, coffee and fruit. Fishing is a big industry across the islands but particularly around Halmahera and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacan" title="Bacan">Bacan</a>. The Aru Islands produce pearls, and Seram exports <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>. Logging is a significant industry on the larger islands with Seram producing ironwood and <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> and ebony are produced on Buru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LP818-819_15-12"><a href="#cite_note-LP818-819-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
