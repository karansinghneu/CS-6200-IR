were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_space_mission" title="Unmanned space mission">space probes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_program" title="Pioneer program">Pioneer program</a>. They were a series of solar-orbiting, spin-stabilized, solar-cell and battery-powered satellites designed to obtain measurements on a continuing basis of interplanetary phenomena from widely separated points in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup> They were also known as <b>Pioneer A</b>, <b>B</b>, <b>C</b>, and <b>D</b>. The fifth (<b>Pioneer E</b>) was lost in a launch accident.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Pioneers 6</i>, <i>7</i>, <i>8</i>, and <i>9</i> were created to make the first detailed, comprehensive measurements of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, solar <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. They were designed to measure large scale magnetic phenomena and particles and fields in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">interplanetary space</a>. Data from the vehicles has been used to better understand stellar processes and the structure and flow of the solar wind. The vehicles also acted as the world\'s first space-based solar weather network, providing practical data on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_storm_(disambiguation)" title="Solar storm (disambiguation)">solar storms</a> which impact communications and power on Earth.\n</p><p>The experiments studied the positive <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> (cations) and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in the solar wind, the interplanetary electron density (<a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a> experiment), solar and galactic cosmic rays, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Magnetic_Field" title="Interplanetary Magnetic Field">Interplanetary Magnetic Field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The spacecraft were important collectors <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">heliophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a> data. In conjunction with other spacecraft these, for the first time, enabled spaceborne observations to be combined with terrestrial observations on the ground and from sounding balloons. Pioneer 9 in early August 1972 recorded significant observations of one of the most potent <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_August_1972" title="Solar storm of August 1972">solar storms</a> ever recorded, and the most hazardous to <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knipp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knipp-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_description">Vehicle description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vehicle description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Each craft was identical, with an on-orbit dry mass of 146&#160;kg. They were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin-stabilized_satellite" title="Spin-stabilized satellite">spin-stabilized</a> 0.94 m diameter &#215; 0.81 m tall cylinders with a 1.8 m long magnetometer boom and solar panels mounted around the body.\n</p><p>The main <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antenna</a> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-gain_antenna" title="High-gain antenna">high-gain</a> directional antenna. The spacecraft were spin-stabilized at about 60 RPM, and the spin <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">axis</a> was <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">ecliptic plane</a> and pointed toward the south <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Instruments:\n</p>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 20em; -webkit-column-width: 20em; column-width: 20em;">\n<ul><li>Solar Wind Plasma Faraday Cup (6,7)</li>\n<li>Cosmic-Ray Telescope (6,7)</li>\n<li>Electrostatic Analyzer (6,7,8)</li>\n<li>Superior Conjunction Faraday Rotation (6,7)</li>\n<li>Spectral Broadening (6)</li>\n<li>Relativity Investigation (6)</li>\n<li>Uniaxial Fluxgate Magnetometer (6)</li>\n<li>Cosmic-Ray Anisotropy (6,7,8,9)</li>\n<li>Celestial Mechanics (6,7,8,9)</li>\n<li>Two-Frequency Beacon Receiver (6,7,8,9)</li>\n<li>Single-Axis Magnetometer (7,8)</li>\n<li>Cosmic Dust Detector (8,9)</li>\n<li>Cosmic Ray Gradient Detector (8,9)</li>\n<li>Plasma Wave Detector (8)</li>\n<li>Triaxial Magnetometer (9)</li>\n<li>Solar Plasma Detector (9)</li>\n<li>Electric Field Detector (9)</li></ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">Communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>By ground command, one of five <a href="/wiki/Bit_rate" title="Bit rate">bit rates</a>, one of four data formats, and one of four operating modes could be selected. The five bit rates were 512, 256, 64, 16, and 8&#160;bit/s. Three of the four data formats contained primarily scientific data and consisted of 32 seven-bit words per frame. One scientific data format was for use at the two highest bit rates. Another was for use at the three lowest bit rates. The third contained data from only the <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a> experiment. The fourth data format contained mainly engineering data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The four operating modes were: real time, telemetry store, duty cycle store, and memory readout. In the real-time mode, data were sampled and transmitted directly (without storage) as specified by the data format and bit rate selected. In the telemetry store mode, data were stored and transmitted simultaneously in the format and at the bit rate selected. In the duty-cycle store mode, a single frame of scientific data was collected and stored at a rate of 512&#160;bit/s. The time interval between the collection and storage of successive frames could be varied by ground command between 2 and 17 min to provide partial data coverage for periods up to 19 hours, as limited by the bit storage capacity. In the memory readout mode, data was read out at whatever bit rate was appropriate to the satellite distance from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_line_and_current_status">Time line and current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Time line and current status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As stated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JPL" title="JPL">JPL</a>, "The Pioneer 6&#8211;9 program has been touted as one of the least expensive of all NASA spacecraft programs in terms of scientific results per dollar spent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quicklook_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-quicklook-3">[3]</a></sup> Although the spacecraft have not been regularly tracked for science data return in recent years, a successful telemetry contact with Pioneer 6 was made on December 8, 2000 to celebrate 35 years of continuous operation since launch. Its original design life expectancy was only 6 months.\n</p><p>Although NASA described <i>Pioneer 6</i> as "extant" as of  26&#160;March&#160;2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> there has been no contact since December 8, 2000. At this time <i>Pioneer 6</i> had operated for 12,758 days, making it the oldest operating space probe until it was surpassed by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> on August 13, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also believed that contact is still possible with <i>Pioneer 7</i> and <i>8</i>;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> only <i>Pioneer 9</i> is definitely not working.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pioneer 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>December 16, 1965</b>\nLaunched at 07:31:00 UTC from Cape Canaveral to a circular solar orbit with a mean distance of 0.8 AU.\n</p><p><b>December 1995</b>\nThe prime Traveling-wave tube (<a href="/wiki/Traveling-wave_tube" title="Traveling-wave tube">TWT</a>) failed some time after December 1995.\n</p><p><b>July 1996</b>\nSpacecraft commanded to the backup TWT.\n</p><p><b>October 6, 1997</b>\nTracked with the <a href="/wiki/Canberra_Deep_Space_Communication_Complex" title="Canberra Deep Space Communication Complex">70 meter Deep Space Station 43 in Australia</a>.\nThe MIT and ARC Plasma Analyzers as well as the cosmic ray detector from University of Chicago were turned on and working.\n</p><p><b>December 8, 2000</b>\nSuccessful telemetry contact for about two hours.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_7"><i>Pioneer 7</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pioneer 7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>August 17, 1966</b>\nLaunched from Cape Canaveral into solar orbit with a mean distance of 1.1 AU.\n</p><p><b>March 20, 1986</b>  \nFlew within 12.3 million kilometers of <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">Halley\'s Comet</a> and monitored the interaction between the cometary hydrogen tail and the solar wind. It discovered He+ plasma produced by charge exchange of solar wind He++ with neutral cometary material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>March 31, 1995</b>\nTracked successfully. The spacecraft and one of the science instruments were still functioning.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_8"><i>Pioneer 8</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pioneer 8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>December 13, 1967</b>\nLaunched at 14:08:00 UTC from Cape Canaveral into solar orbit with a mean distance of 1.1 AU from the Sun.\n</p><p><b>August 22, 1996</b>\nThe spacecraft commanded to switch to the backup TWT. Downlink signal was re-acquired, one of the science instruments again functioning.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_9"><i>Pioneer 9</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pioneer 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>November 8, 1968</b>\nLaunched at 09:46:00 UTC from Cape Canaveral into solar orbit with a mean distance of 0.8 AU.\n</p><p><b>1983</b> Final contact.\n</p><p><b>1987</b> Contact was attempted, but failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_E">Pioneer E</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pioneer E">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>August 27, 1969</b>\nLaunched at 21:59:00 UTC from Cape Canaveral. The launch vehicle was destroyed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_Safety_Officer" title="Range Safety Officer">range safety</a> after hydraulics in the first stage failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quicklook_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-quicklook-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pioneer_6,_7,_8,_and_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-NASA-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-NASA_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NASA_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NASA_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NASA_1-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NASA_1-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="https://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/nmc/spacecraftDisplay.do?id=1965-105A" rel="nofollow">"Pioneer 6: NSSDCA/COSPAR ID: 1965-105A"</a>.
