pressure</b>, sometimes also called <b>barometric pressure</b> (after the <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">sensor</a>), is the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a> (or that of another planet). The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere</a> (symbol: atm) is a unit of pressure defined as 1013.25&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (101.325&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>), equivalent to 760<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a> (<a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a>), 29.9212<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inches<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Hg</a>, or 14.696<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icao_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-icao-1">[1]</a></sup> The atm unit is roughly equivalent to the mean sea-level atmospheric pressure on Earth, that is, the Earth\'s atmospheric pressure at sea level is approximately 1 atm.\n</p><p>In most circumstances atmospheric pressure is closely approximated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_pressure" title="Fluid pressure">hydrostatic pressure</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">air</a> above the measurement point. As <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> increases, there is less overlying atmospheric mass, so that atmospheric pressure decreases with increasing elevation. Pressure measures force per unit area, with <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI units</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pascals</a> (1 pascal = 1 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> per square <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>N/m<sup>2</sup>). On average, a column of air with a cross-sectional area of 1 square <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">centimetre</a> (cm<sup>2</sup>), measured from mean (average) <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> to the top of Earth\'s atmosphere, has a <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of about 1.03 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> and exerts a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> or "weight" of about 10.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtons" title="Newtons">newtons</a> or 2.37<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Pound_(force)" title="Pound (force)">lb<sub>f</sub></a>, resulting in a pressure at sea level of about 10.1 N/cm<sup>2</sup> or 101<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)#Commonly_seen_as_kilonewtons" title="Newton (unit)">kN</a>/m<sup>2</sup> (101 kilopascals, kPa). A column of air with a cross-sectional area of 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>in<sup>2</sup> (6.45<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>cm<sup>2</sup>) would have a mass of about 6.65<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>kg and a weight of about 65.4<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>N or 14.7<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>lb<sub>f</sub>, resulting in a pressure of 10.1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>N/cm<sup>2</sup> or 14.7<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>lb<sub>f</sub>/in<sup>2</sup>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Atmospheric pressure is caused by the gravitational attraction of the planet on the atmospheric gases above the surface, and is a function of the mass of the planet, the radius of the surface, and the amount and composition of the gases and their vertical distribution in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is modified by the planetary rotation and local effects such as wind velocity, density variations due to temperature and variations in composition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_sea-level_pressure">Mean sea-level pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> of 80&#160;ft (24&#160;m).</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>mean sea-level pressure</i> (MSLP) is the average atmospheric pressure at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>. This is the atmospheric pressure normally given in weather reports on radio, television, and newspapers or on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometers</a> in the home are set to match the local weather reports, they measure pressure adjusted to sea level, not the actual local atmospheric pressure.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Altimeter_setting" title="Altimeter setting">altimeter setting</a></i> in aviation is an atmospheric pressure adjustment.\n</p><p>Average <i>sea-level pressure</i> is 1013.25&#160;mbar (101.325&#160;kPa; 29.921&#160;inHg; 760.00&#160;mmHg). In aviation, weather reports (<a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a>), <a href="/wiki/QNH" title="QNH">QNH</a> is transmitted around the world in millibars or hectopascals (1 hectopascal = 1 millibar), except in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> where it is reported in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">inches of mercury</a> (to two decimal places). The United States and Canada also report <i>sea-level pressure</i> SLP, which is adjusted to sea level by a different method, in the remarks section, not in the internationally transmitted part of the code, in hectopascals or millibars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, in Canada\'s public weather reports, sea level pressure is instead reported in kilopascals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the US weather code remarks, three digits are all that are transmitted; decimal points and the one or two most significant digits are omitted: 1013.2&#160;mbar (101.32&#160;kPa)  is transmitted as 132; 1000.0&#160;mbar (100.00&#160;kPa) is transmitted as 000; 998.7<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>mbar is transmitted as 987; etc. The highest <i>sea-level pressure</i> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> occurs in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">Siberian High</a> often attains a <i>sea-level pressure</i> above 1050&#160;mbar (105&#160;kPa; 31&#160;inHg), with record highs close to 1085&#160;mbar (108.5&#160;kPa; 32.0&#160;inHg). The lowest measurable <i>sea-level pressure</i> is found at the centers of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, with a record low of 870&#160;mbar (87&#160;kPa; 26&#160;inHg) (see <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure#Records" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric pressure records</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_pressure">Surface pressure <span id="Surface"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surface pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Surface pressure" redirects here. For surface pressure in physical chemistry, see <a href="/wiki/Pressure#Surface_pressure_and_surface_tension" title="Pressure">Pressure &#167;&#160;Surface pressure and surface tension</a>.</div>\n<p><i>Surface pressure</i> is the atmospheric pressure at a location on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s surface (<a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>).  It is directly proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of air over that location.\n</p><p>For numerical reasons, atmospheric models such as <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">general circulation models</a> (GCMs) usually predict the nondimensional <i><a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a> of surface pressure</i>.\n</p><p>The average value of surface pressure on Earth is 985 hPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacobs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-jacobs-6">[6]</a></sup> This is in contrast to mean sea-level pressure, which involves the extrapolation of pressure to sea-level for locations above or below sea-level. The average pressure at mean sea-level (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Level" title="Sea Level">MSL</a>) in the International Standard Atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">ISA</a>) is 1013.25 hPa, or 1 atmosphere (Atm), or 29.92 inches of mercury. \n</p><p>Pressure (P), mass (m), and the acceleration due to gravity (g), are related by P = F/A = (m*g)/A, where A is surface area. Atmospheric pressure is thus proportional to the weight per unit area of the atmospheric mass above that location.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Altitude_variation">Altitude (12.79&#160;psi) in the storm\'s eye</div></div></div>\n<p>Atmospheric pressure varies widely on Earth, and these changes are important in studying <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">pressure system</a> for the effects of air pressure variations on weather.\n</p><p>Atmospheric pressure shows a diurnal or semidiurnal (twice-daily) cycle caused by global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_tides" title="Atmospheric tides">atmospheric tides</a>. This effect is strongest in tropical zones, with an amplitude of a few millibars, and almost zero in polar areas. These variations have two superimposed cycles, a circadian (24&#160;h) cycle and semi-circadian (12&#160;h) cycle.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The highest adjusted-to-sea level barometric pressure ever recorded on Earth (above 750 meters) was 1084.8&#160;hPa (32.03&#160;inHg) measured in <a href="/wiki/Tosontsengel,_Zavkhan" title="Tosontsengel, Zavkhan">Tosontsengel, Mongolia</a> on 19 December 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo.asu.edu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmo.asu.edu-8">[8]</a></sup> The highest adjusted-to-sea level barometric pressure ever recorded (below 750 meters) was at Agata in <a href="/wiki/Evenk_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Evenk Autonomous Okrug">Evenk Autonomous Okrug</a>, Russia (66&#176;53&#8217;<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>N, 93&#176;28&#8217;<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>E, elevation: 261&#160;m, 856&#160;ft) on 31 December 1968 of 1083.8&#160;hPa (32.005&#160;inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  The discrimination is due to the problematic assumptions (assuming a standard lapse rate) associated with reduction of sea level from high elevations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo.asu.edu_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmo.asu.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, the lowest place on Earth at 430 metres (1,410&#160;ft) below sea level, has a correspondingly high typical atmospheric pressure of 1065<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>hPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The lowest non-tornadic atmospheric pressure ever measured was 870&#160;hPa (0.858&#160;atm; 25.69&#160;inHg), set on 12 October 1979, during <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a> in the western Pacific Ocean. The measurement was based on an instrumental observation made from a reconnaissance aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ1-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_based_on_depth_of_water">Measurement based on depth of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Measurement based on depth of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere</a> (101.325&#160;kPa or 14.7&#160;psi) is also the pressure caused by the weight of a column of fresh water of approximately 10.3&#160;m (33.8&#160;ft). Thus, a diver 10.3&#160;m underwater experiences a pressure of about 2 atmospheres (1&#160;atm of air plus 1&#160;atm of water). Conversely, 10.3&#160;m is the maximum height to which water can be raised using <a href="/wiki/Suction" title="Suction">suction</a> under standard atmospheric conditions.\n</p><p>Low pressures such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> lines are sometimes specified in <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_water" title="Inch of water">inches of water</a>, typically written as <i>w.c.</i> (water column) gauge or <i>w.g.</i> (inches water gauge). A typical gas-using residential appliance in the US is rated for a maximum of 14&#160;w.g., which is approximately 1048.37<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HPa" title="HPa">hPa</a>. Similar metric units with a wide variety of names and notation based on <a href="/wiki/Millimeters,_water_gauge" title="Millimeters, water gauge">millimetres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centimetre_of_water" title="Centimetre of water">centimetres</a> or metres are now less commonly used.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiling_point_of_water">Boiling point of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Boiling point of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kochendes_wasser02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="644" data-file-width="674" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/220px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/330px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/440px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kochendes_wasser02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_water" title="Boiling water">Boiling water</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Pure water <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boils</a> at 100&#160;&#176;C (212&#160;&#176;F) at earth\'s standard atmospheric pressure. The boiling point is the temperature at which the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> is equal to the atmospheric pressure around the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Because of this, the boiling point of water is lower at lower pressure and higher at higher pressure. Cooking at high elevations, therefore, requires adjustments to recipes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Pressure_cooking" title="Pressure cooking">pressure cooking</a>. A rough approximation of elevation can be obtained by measuring the temperature at which water boils; in the mid-19th century, this method was used by explorers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_and_maps">Measurement and maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Measurement and maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An important application of the knowledge that atmospheric pressure varies directly with altitude was in determining the height of hills and mountains thanks to the availability of reliable pressure measurement devices. While in 1774, Maskelyne was confirming Newton\'s theory of gravitation at and on Schiehallion in Scotland (using plumb bob deviation to show the effect of gravity) and accurately measure elevation, William Roy using barometric pressure was able to confirm his height determinations, the agreement being to within one meter (3.28 feet).  This method became and continues to be useful for survey work and map making.  This early application of science gave people insight into how science could easily be put to practical use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
