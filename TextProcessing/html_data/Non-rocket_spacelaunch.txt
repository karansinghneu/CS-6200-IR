For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Sky_Tower_(disambiguation)" title="Sky Tower (disambiguation)">Sky Tower (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Non-rocket spacelaunch</b> refers to concepts for <a href="/wiki/Space_launch" title="Space launch">launch into space</a> where some or all of the needed speed and altitude are provided by something other than <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>, or by other than <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable rockets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A number of alternatives to expendable rockets have been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  In some systems such as a combination launch system, <a href="/wiki/Skyhook_(structure)" title="Skyhook (structure)">skyhook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled_launch" title="Rocket sled launch">rocket sled launch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockoon" title="Rockoon">rockoon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Air_launch_to_orbit" title="Air launch to orbit">air launch</a>, a rocket would be part, but only part of the system used to reach orbit.\n</p><p>Present-day launch costs are very high &#8211; $2,500 to $25,000 per kilogram from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO).  As a result, launch costs are a large percentage of the cost of all space endeavors.  If launch can be made cheaper, the total cost of space missions will be reduced.  \nDue to the exponential nature of the <a href="/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_rocket_equation" title="Tsiolkovsky rocket equation">rocket equation</a>, providing even a small amount of the velocity to LEO by other means has the potential of greatly reducing the cost of getting to orbit.\n</p><p>Launch costs in the hundreds of dollars per kilogram would make possible many proposed large-scale space projects such as <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">space colonization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space-based_solar_power" title="Space-based solar power">space-based solar power</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars#Orbiting_mirrors" title="Terraforming of Mars">terraforming Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Requirements_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Requirements-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div usage, the term "static" is intended to convey the understanding that the structural portion of the system has no internal moving parts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_tower">Space tower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Space tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A space tower is a tower that would reach <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. To avoid an immediate need for a vehicle launched at orbital velocity to raise its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a>, a tower would have to extend above the edge of space (above the 100&#160;km <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[20]</a></sup> but a far lower tower height could reduce atmospheric drag losses during ascent.  If the tower went all the way to <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous orbit</a> at approximately 36,000&#160;km, or 22,369 miles, objects released at such height could then drift away with minimal power and would be in a circular orbit. The concept of a structure reaching to geosynchronous orbit was first conceived by <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[21]</a></sup>\nThe original concept envisioned by Tsiolkovsky was a compression structure.  \nBuilding a compression structure from the ground up proved an unrealistic task as there was no material in existence with enough compressive strength to support its own weight under such conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBIS1999_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS1999-30">[22]</a></sup>\nOther ideas use very tall compressive towers to reduce the demands on launch vehicles.  The vehicle is "elevated" up the tower, which may extend above the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and is launched from the top.  Such a tall tower to access near-space altitudes of 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) has been proposed by various researchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1996_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-landis1996-31">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tensile_structures">Tensile structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tensile structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tether_propulsion" title="Tether propulsion">Tether propulsion</a></div>\n<p>Tensile structures for non-rocket spacelaunch are proposals to use long, very strong cables (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tether_propulsion" title="Tether propulsion">tethers</a>) to lift a payload into space. Tethers can also be used for changing orbit once in space.\n</p><p>Orbital tethers can be tidally locked (<a href="/wiki/Skyhook_(structure)" title="Skyhook (structure)">skyhook</a>) or rotating (rotovators).  They can be designed (in theory) to pick up the payload when the payload is stationary or when the payload is hypersonic (has a high but not orbital velocity).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Endo-atmospheric tethers can be used to transfer kinetics (energy and momentum) between large conventional aircraft (subsonic or low supersonic) or other motive force and smaller aerodynamic vehicles, propelling them to hypersonic velocities without exotic propulsion systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skyhook">Skyhook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">Space elevator</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" As the planet rotates, the centrifugal force at the upper end of the tether counteracts gravity, and keeps the cable taut. Vehicles can then climb the tether and reach orbit without the use of rocket propulsion.\n</p><p>Such a cable could be made out of any material able to support itself under tension by tapering the cable\'s diameter sufficiently quickly as it approached the Earth\'s surface.  On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, with its relatively strong gravity, current materials are not sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Specific_strength" title="Specific strength">strong and light</a>.  With conventional materials, the taper ratio would need to be very large, increasing the total launch mass to a fiscally infeasible degree.  However, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotube</a> or <a href="/wiki/Boron_nitride#Boron_nitride_nanotubes" title="Boron nitride">boron nitride nanotube</a> based materials have been proposed as the tensile element in the tether design.  Their measured strengths are high compared to their linear densities.  They hold promise as materials to make an Earth-based space elevator possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edwards_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards-37">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Landis and Cafarelli suggested that a tension structure ("space elevator") extending downward from geosynchronous orbit could be combined with the compression structure ("Tsiolkovski tower") extending upward from the surface, forming the combined structure reaching geosynchronous orbit from the surface, and having structural advantages over either one individually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBIS1999_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS1999-30">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The space elevator concept is also applicable to other planets and <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial bodies</a>. For locations in the Solar System with weaker gravity than Earth\'s (such as the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>), the strength-to-density requirements aren\'t as great for tether materials.  Currently available materials (such as <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a>) could serve as the tether material for elevators there.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endo-atmospheric_tethers">Endo-atmospheric tethers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Endo-atmospheric tethers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:114px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="554" data-file-width="176" decoding="async" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png/112px-Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png/168px-Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/7f/Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png 2x" width="112"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kite_Launcher_at_Max_Vtip.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>KITE Launcher&#160;&#8212; transferring momentum to the vehicle.</div></div></div>\n<p>An endo-atmospheric tether uses the long cable within the atmosphere to provide some or all of the velocity needed to reach orbit. The tether is used to transfer kinetics (energy and momentum) from a massive, slow end (typically a large subsonic or low supersonic aircraft) to a hypersonic end through aerodynamics or centripetal action. The <i>Kinetics Interchange TEther (KITE) Launcher</i> is one proposed endo-atmospheric tether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johansen_19-1"><a Yunitsky in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Paul_Birch_(writer)" title="Paul Birch (writer)">Paul Birch</a> JBIS design<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[32]</a></sup> of an orbital ring system, a rotating cable is placed in a low Earth orbit, rotating at slightly faster than orbital speed. Not in orbit, but riding on this ring, supported electromagnetically on superconducting magnets, are ring stations that stay in one place above some designated point on Earth. Hanging down from these ring stations are short space elevators made from cables with high tensile-strength-to-mass ratio.  Birch claimed that the ring stations, in addition to holding the tether, could accelerate the orbital ring eastwards, causing it to <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precess</a> around Earth. If it were possible to make the precession rate large enough &#8211; once per day, the rate of rotation of the Earth &#8211; the ring would be "geostationary" without having to be at the normal geostationary altitude or even in the equatorial plane.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_loop">Launch loop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Launch loop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Launch_loop" title="Launch loop">Launch loop</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaunchLoop.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="677" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/LaunchLoop.svg/480px-LaunchLoop.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/LaunchLoop.svg/640px-LaunchLoop.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaunchLoop.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch loop.</div></div></div>\n<p>A launch loop or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lofstrom_loop" title="Lofstrom loop">Lofstrom loop</a> is a design for a <a href="/wiki/Belt_(mechanical)" title="Belt (mechanical)">belt</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_(transport)" title="Maglev (transport)">maglev</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_launch" title="Orbital launch">orbital launch</a> system that would be around 2000&#160;km long and maintained at an altitude of up to 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi). <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">Vehicles</a> weighing 5 metric tons would be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_(transport)" title="Maglev (transport)">electromagnetically accelerated</a> on top of the cable which forms an acceleration track, from which they would be projected into Earth <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> or even beyond. The structure would constantly need around 200 MW of power to keep it in place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The system is designed to be suitable for launching humans for <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">space colonization</a> with a maximum of 3&#160;g acceleration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[33]</a></sup> Some other Launch Loops are developed in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pneumatic_freestanding_tower">Pneumatic freestanding tower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pneumatic freestanding tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One proposed design is a freestanding tower composed of high strength material (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a>) tubular columns inflated with a low density gas mix, and with dynamic stabilization systems including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyroscopes" title="Gyroscopes">gyroscopes</a> and "pressure balancing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hdl.handle.net/10315/2587_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-hdl.handle.net/10315/2587-43">[35]</a></sup> Suggested benefits in contrast to other space elevator designs include avoiding working with the great lengths of structure involved in some other designs, construction from the ground instead of orbit, and functional access to the entire range of altitudes within the design\'s practical reach. The design presented is "at 5 km altitude and extending to 20&#160;km above sea level", and the authors suggest that "the approach may be further scaled to provide direct access to altitudes above 200&#160;km".\n</p><p>A major difficulty of such a tower is buckling since it is a long slender construction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projectile_launchers">Projectile launchers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Projectile launchers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With any of these projectile launchers, the launcher gives a high velocity at, or near, ground level.  In order to achieve orbit, the projectile must be given enough extra velocity to punch through the atmosphere, unless it includes an additional propulsion system (such as a rocket). Also, the projectile needs either an internal or external means to perform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_insertion" title="Orbital insertion">orbital insertion</a>.  The designs below fall into three categories, electrically driven, chemically driven, and mechanically driven.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetic_acceleration">Electromagnetic acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Electromagnetic acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Electrical launch systems include mass drivers, <a href="/wiki/Railgun" title="Railgun">railguns</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coilgun" title="Coilgun">coilguns</a>. All of these systems use the concept of a stationary launch track which uses some form of linear electrical motor to accelerate a projectile.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_driver">Mass driver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mass driver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">Mass driver</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_base_concept_drawing_s78_23252.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="1023" decoding="async" height="190" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Lunar_base_concept_drawing_s78_23252.jpg/576px-Lunar_base_concept_drawing_s78_23252.jpg 2x" width="288"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_base_concept_drawing_s78_23252.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A mass driver for lunar launch (artist\'s conception).</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:226px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Railgun-1.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="321" data-file-width="480" railgun.</div></div></div>\n<p>A mass driver is basically a very long and mainly horizontally aligned <a href="/wiki/Launch_track" title="Launch track">launch track</a> or tunnel for space launch, curved upwards at the end. The concept was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a> in 1950,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[36]</a></sup> and was developed in more detail by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_K._O%27Neill" title="Gerard K. O\'Neill">Gerard K. O\'Neill</a>, working with the <a href="/wiki/Space_Studies_Institute" title="Space Studies Institute">Space Studies Institute</a>, focusing on the use of a mass driver for launching material from the Moon.\n</p><p>A mass driver uses some sort of repulsion to keep a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a> separated from the track or walls. Then it uses a <a href="/wiki/Linear_motor" title="Linear motor">linear motor</a> (an alternating-current motor such as in a coil gun, or a <a href="/wiki/Homopolar_motor" title="Homopolar motor">homopolar motor</a> as in a railgun) to accelerate the payload to high speeds. After leaving the launch track, the payload would be at its launch velocity.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="StarTram">StarTram</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: StarTram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/StarTram" title="StarTram">StarTram</a></div>\n<p>StarTram is a proposal to launch vehicles directly to space by accelerating them with a mass driver. Vehicles would float by <a href="/wiki/Maglev" title="Maglev">maglev</a> repulsion between superconductive magnets on the vehicle and the aluminum tunnel walls while they were accelerated by AC magnetic drive from aluminum coils. The power required would probably be provided by superconductive energy storage units distributed along the tunnel. Vehicles could coast up to low or even geosynchronous orbital height; then a small rocket motor burn would be required to circularize the orbit.\n</p><p>Cargo-only Generation 1 systems would accelerate at 10&#8211;20 Gs and exit from a mountain top. While not suitable for passengers, they could put cargo into orbit for $40 per kilogram, 100 times cheaper than rockets.\n</p><p>Passenger-capable Generation 2 systems would accelerate for a much longer distance at 2 Gs. The vehicles would enter the atmosphere at an altitude of 20&#160;km from an evacuated tunnel restrained by Kevlar tethers and supported by magnetic repulsion between superconducting cables in the tunnel and on the ground.  For both Gen 1&#8211;2 systems, the mouth of the tube would be open during vehicle acceleration, with air kept out by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic" title="Magnetohydrodynamic">magnetohydrodynamic</a> pumping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Startram_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Startram-20">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StarTramBook_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-StarTramBook-45">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_gun">Space gun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Space gun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_gun" title="Space gun">Space gun</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:210px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Project_Harp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="304" decoding="async" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Project_Harp.jpg/208px-Project_Harp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Project_Harp.jpg 1.5x" width="208"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Project_Harp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Project_HARP" title="Project HARP">Project HARP</a>, a prototype of a space gun.</div></div></div>\n<p>A space gun is a proposed method of launching an object into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> using a large <a href="/wiki/Gun" title="Gun">gun</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a>.  Science fiction writer <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> proposed such a launch method in <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>, and in 1902 a movie, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon" title="A Trip to the Moon">A Trip to the Moon</a></i>, was adapted.\n</p><p>However, even with a "<a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">gun barrel</a>" through both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth\'s crust">Earth\'s crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-forces" title="G-forces">g-forces</a> required to generate <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a> would still be more than what a human tolerates. Therefore, the space gun would be restricted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freight" title="Freight">freight</a> and ruggedized satellites. Also, the projectile needs either an internal or external means to stabilize on orbit.\n</p><p>Gun launch concepts do not always use combustion.  In pneumatic launch systems, a projectile is accelerated in a long tube by air pressure, produced by ground-based turbines or other means.  In a <a href="/wiki/Light-gas_gun" title="Light-gas gun">light-gas gun</a>, the pressurant is a gas of light molecular weight, to maximize the speed of sound in the gas.\n</p><p>In the 1990s, <a href="/wiki/John_Hunter_(scientist)" title="John Hunter (scientist)">John Hunter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quicklaunch" title="Quicklaunch">Quicklaunch</a> proposed use of a \'Hydrogen Gun\' to launch unmanned payloads to orbit for less than the regular launch costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ram_accelerator">Ram accelerator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ram accelerator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ram_accelerator" title="Ram accelerator">Ram accelerator</a></div>\n<p>A ram accelerator also uses chemical energy like the <i>space gun</i> but it is entirely different in that it relies on a jet-engine-like propulsion cycle utilizing <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a> combustion processes to accelerate the <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectile</a> to extremely high speeds.\n</p><p>It is a long tube filled with a mixture of combustible gases with a frangible diaphragm at either end to contain the gases. The projectile, which is shaped like a ram jet core, is fired by another means (e.g., a space gun, discussed above) supersonically through the first diaphragm into the end of the tube.  It then burns the gases as fuel, accelerating down the tube under jet propulsion. Other physics come into play at higher velocities.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Blast_wave_accelerator">Blast wave accelerator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Blast wave accelerator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A blast wave accelerator is similar to a <i>space gun</i> but it differs in that rings of explosive along the length of the barrel are detonated in sequence to keep the accelerations high. Also, rather than just relying on the pressure behind the projectile, the blast wave accelerator specifically times the explosions to squeeze on a tail cone on the projectile, as one might shoot a pumpkin seed by squeezing the tapered end.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical">Mechanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mechanical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Slingatron">Slingatron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Slingatron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In a slingatron,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tidman_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tidman-25">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sling_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sling-48">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> are accelerated along a rigid tube or track that typically has circular or spiral turns, or combinations of these geometries in two or three dimensions. A projectile is accelerated in the curved tube by propelling the entire tube in a small-amplitude circular motion of constant or increasing frequency without changing the orientation of the tube, i.e. the entire tube gyrates but does not spin. An everyday example of this motion is stirring a beverage by holding the container and moving it in small horizontal circles, causing the contents to spin, without spinning the container itself.\n</p><p>This gyration continually displaces the tube with a component along the direction of the centripetal force acting on the projectile, so that work is continually done on the projectile as it advances through the machine. The centripetal force experienced by the projectile is the accelerating force, and is proportional to the projectile mass.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_launch">Air launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Air launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_launch" title="Air launch">Air launch</a></div>\n<p>In air launch, a carrier aircraft carries the space vehicle to high altitude and speed before release.  This technique was used on the suborbital X-15 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceshipOne" title="SpaceshipOne">SpaceshipOne</a> vehicles, and for the <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(rocket)" title="Pegasus (rocket)">Pegasus</a> orbital launch vehicle.\n</p><p>The main disadvantages are that the carrier aircraft tends to be quite large, and separation within the airflow at supersonic speeds has never been demonstrated, thus the boost given is relatively modest.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceplanes">Spaceplanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Spaceplanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">Spaceplane</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:210px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg/208px-X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg/312px-X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg/416px-X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg 2x" width="208"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X43a2_nasa_scramjet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-43A" title="X-43A">X-43A</a> with scramjet attached to the underside.</div></div></div>\n<p>A spaceplane is an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> designed to pass the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_of_space" title="Edge of space">edge of space</a>. It combines some features of an aircraft with some of a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>. Typically, it takes the form of a spacecraft equipped with <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">aerodynamic surfaces</a>, one or more rocket engines, and sometimes additional <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">airbreathing propulsion</a> as well.\n</p><p>Early spaceplanes were used to explore hypersonic flight (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">X-15</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some air-breathing engine-based designs (cf <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-30" title="X-30">X-30</a>) such as aircraft based on <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pulse_detonation_engine" title="Pulse detonation engine">pulse detonation engines</a> could potentially achieve orbital velocity or go some useful way to doing so; however, these designs still must perform a final rocket burn at their apogee to circularize their trajectory to avoid returning to the atmosphere.  Other, reusable turbojet-like designs like <a href="/wiki/Skylon_(spacecraft)" title="Skylon (spacecraft)">Skylon</a> which uses <a href="/wiki/Precooled_jet_engine" title="Precooled jet engine">precooled jet engines</a> up to Mach&#160;5.5 before employing rockets to enter orbit appears to have a mass budget that permits a larger payload than pure rockets while achieving it in a single stage.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Balloon">Balloon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Balloon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloons" title="Balloons">Balloons</a> can raise the initial altitude of rockets. However, balloons have relatively low payload (although see the <a href="/wiki/SkyCat" title="SkyCat">Sky Cat</a> project for an example of a heavy-lift balloon intended for use in the lower atmosphere), and this decreases even more with increasing altitude.\n</p><p>The lifting gas could be <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.  Helium is not only expensive in large quantities but is also a <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">nonrenewable resource</a>. This makes balloons an expensive launch assist technique. Hydrogen could be used as it has the advantage of being cheaper and lighter than helium, but the disadvantage of also being highly flammable. Rockets launched from balloons, known as " <a href="/wiki/Rockoon" title="Rockoon">rockoons</a>", have been demonstrated but to date, only for suborbital ("sounding rocket") missions.  The size of balloon that would be required to lift an orbital launch vehicle would be extremely large.\n</p><p>One prototype of a balloon launch platform has been made by <a href="/wiki/JP_Aerospace" title="JP Aerospace">JP Aerospace</a> as "Project Tandem",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[42]</a></sup> although it has not been used as a rocket launch vehicle.  JP Aerospace further proposes a hypersonic, lighter than air upper stage.  A Spanish company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero2infinity" title="Zero2infinity">zero2infinity</a>, is officially developing a launcher system called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloostar" title="Bloostar">bloostar</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Rockoon" title="Rockoon">rockoon</a> concept, expected to be operational by 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gerard_K._O%27Neill" title="Gerard K. O\'Neill">Gerard K. O\'Neill</a> proposed that by using very large balloons it may be possible to construct a space port in the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Rockets could launch from it or a mass driver could accelerate payloads into the orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[44]</a></sup> This has the advantage that most (about 90%) of the atmosphere is below the space port.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lateral_aerial_view_of_a_SpaceShaf.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="356" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Lateral_aerial_view_of_a_SpaceShaf.png/270px-Lateral_aerial_view_of_a_SpaceShaf.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Lateral_aerial_view_of_a_SpaceShaf.png 1.5x" width="270"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lateral_aerial_view_of_a_SpaceShaf.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist depiction of an aerial view of a SpaceShaft.</div></div></div>\n<p>A SpaceShaft is a proposed version of an atmospherically buoyant structure that would serve as a system to lift cargo to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_space" title="Near space">near-space</a> <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a>, with platforms distributed at several <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a> that would provide <a href="/wiki/Habitation_Module" title="Habitation Module">habitation facilities</a> for long term human operations throughout the mid-<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a> and near-space altitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-k-53">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ss-54">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esw_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-esw-55">[47]</a></sup> For space launch, it would serve as a non-rocket first stage for rockets launched from the top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-ss-54">[46]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_launch_systems">Hybrid launch systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Non-rocket_spacelaunch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Hybrid launch systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:480914main_ELHVrailartistconcept.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/480914main_ELHVrailartistconcept.jpg/320px-480914main_ELHVrailartistconcept.jpg" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:480914main_ELHVrailartistconcept.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> art for a concept combining three technologies: electromagnetic <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled_launch" title="Rocket sled launch">launch assist</a> from a hypothetical 2-mile (3.2&#160;km) track at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a> aircraft, and a carried rocket for use after <a href="/wiki/Air_launch" title="Air launch">air launch</a> reaches orbit.</div></div></div>\n<p>Separate technologies may be combined. In 2010, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> suggested that a future scramjet aircraft might be accelerated to 300&#160;m/s (a solution to the problem of <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet engines</a> not being startable at zero airflow velocity) by electromagnetic or other <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled_launch" title="Rocket sled launch">sled launch</a> assist, in turn air-launching a second-stage rocket delivering a satellite to orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>All forms of projectile launchers are at least partially hybrid systems if launching to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, due to the requirement for <a href="/wiki/Space_gun#Getting_to_orbit" title="Space gun">orbit circularization</a>, at a minimum entailing several percent of total <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> to raise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> (e.g. a tiny rocket burn), or in some concepts much more from a rocket thruster to ease ground accelerator development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Startram_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Startram-20">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some technologies can have exponential scaling if used in isolation, making the effect of combinations be of counter-intuitive magnitude. For instance, 270&#160;m/s is under 4% of the velocity of <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, but a NASA study estimated that Maglifter <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled_launch" title="Rocket sled launch">sled launch</a> at that velocity could increase the payload of a conventional <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">ELV</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> by 80% when also having the track go up a 3000&#8209;meter mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-57">[49]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Forms of ground launch limited to a given maximum acceleration (such as due to human <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g-force</a> tolerances if intended to carry passengers) have the corresponding minimum launcher length scale not linearly but with velocity squared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physics_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-physics-58">[50]</a></sup> Tethers can have even more non-linear, exponential scaling. The tether-to-payload <a href="/wiki/Space_tether#Mass_ratio" title="Space tether">mass ratio</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Space_tether" title="Space tether">space tether</a> would be around <a href="/wiki/Space_tether#Mass_ratio" title="Space tether">1:1 at a tip velocity 60% of its characteristic velocity</a> but becomes <a href="/wiki/Space_tether#Mass_ratio" title="Space tether">more than 1000:1 at a tip velocity 240% of its characteristic velocity</a>. For instance, for anticipated practicality and a moderate mass ratio with current materials, the HASTOL concept would have the first half (4&#160;km/s) of velocity to orbit be provided by other means than the tether itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesSim_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-DesSim-15">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A proposal to use a hybrid system combining a mass driver for initial lofting followed by additive thrust by a series of ground-based lasers sequenced according to wavelength was proposed by Mashall Savage in the book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Millennial_Project:_Colonizing_the_Galaxy_in_Eight_Easy_Steps" title="The Millennial Project: Colonizing the Galaxy in Eight Easy Steps">The Millennial Project</a></i> as one of the core theses of the book, but the idea has not been pursued to any notable degree. Savage\'s specific proposals proved to be infeasible on both engineering and political grounds, and while the difficulties could be overcome, the group Savage founded, now called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_Universe_Foundation" title="Living Universe Foundation">Living Universe Foundation</a>, has been unable to raise significant funds for research.\n</p><p>Combining multiple technologies would in itself be an increase to complexity and development challenges, but reducing the performance requirements of a given subsystem may allow reduction in its individual complexity or cost. For instance, the number of parts in a liquid-fueled rocket engine may be <a href="/wiki/Rocket#Costs_and_economics" title="Rocket">two orders of magnitude less if pressure-fed rather than pump-fed</a> if its <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> requirements are limited enough to make the weight penalty of such be a practical option, or a high-velocity ground launcher may be able to use a relatively moderate performance and inexpensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid fuel</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_rocket" title="Hybrid rocket">hybrid</a> small motor on its projectile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CheapRockets_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-CheapRockets-59">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system#Launch_assistance" title="Reusable launch system">Assist</a> by non-rocket methods may compensate against the <a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system#Weight_penalty" title="Reusable launch system">weight penalty</a> of making an orbital <a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system" title="Reusable launch system">rocket reusable</a>. Though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suborbital" title="Suborbital">suborbital</a>, the first private manned spaceship, <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a> had reduced rocket performance requirements due to being a combined system with its <a href="/wiki/Air_launch" title="Air launch">air launch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[52]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
