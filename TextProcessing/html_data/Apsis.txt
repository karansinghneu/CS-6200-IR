+)</div></div></div>\n<p>The term <b>apsis</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">&#7937;&#968;&#943;&#962;</span>; plural  <b>apsides</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/&#230;/: \'a\' in \'bad\'">&#230;</span><span title="\'p\' in \'pie\'">p</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="\'d\' in \'dye\'">d</span><span title="/i&#720;/: \'ee\' in \'fleece\'">i&#720;</span><span title="\'z\' in \'zoom\'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>, Greek: &#7937;&#968;&#8150;&#948;&#949;&#962;; "orbit") refers to an extreme point in the <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">object</a>. It denotes either the points on the orbit, or the respective distance of the bodies. The word comes via Latin from Greek, there denoting a whole orbit, and is <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with <i><a href="/wiki/Apse" title="Apse">apse</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Except for the theoretical possibility of one common circular orbit for two bodies of equal mass at diametral positions (symmetric <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a>), there are two apsides for any <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a>, named with the prefixes <i>peri-</i> (from <i> </i>&#960;&#949;&#961;&#943;<i> (peri)</i>, meaning \'near\') and <i>ap-</i>/<i>apo-</i> (from <i> </i>&#7936;&#960;(&#972;)<i> (ap(&#243;))</i>, meaning \'away from\') added to a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Primary_(astronomy)" title="Primary (astronomy)">body being orbited</a>. All periodic orbits are, according to Newton\'s Laws of motion, ellipses: either the two individual ellipses of both bodies (see the two graphs in the second figure), with the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">barycenter</a>) of this two-body system at the one common <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">focus</a> of the ellipses, or the orbital ellipses, with one body taken as fixed at one focus, and the other body orbiting this focus (see top figure). All these ellipses share a straight line, the <i>line of apsides</i>, that contains their major axes (the greatest diameter), the foci, and the vertices, and thus also the periapsis and the apoapsis (see both figures). The major axis of the orbital ellipse (top figure) is the distance of the apsides, when taken as points on the orbit, or their sum, when taken as distances.\n</p><p>The major axes of the individual ellipses around the barycenter, respectively the contributions to the major axis of the orbital ellipses are inverse proportional to the masses of the bodies, i.e., a bigger mass implies a smaller axis/contribution. Only when one mass is sufficiently larger than the other, the individual ellipse of the smaller body around the barycenter comprises the individual ellipse of the larger body as shown in the second figure. For remarkable asymmetry, the barycenter of the two bodies may lie well within the bigger body, e.g., the Earth&#8211;Moon barycenter is about 75% of the way from Earth\'s center to its surface. If the smaller mass is negligible compared to the larger, then  the orbital parameters are independent of the smaller mass (e.g. for satellites).\n</p>\n<ul><li>For general orbits, the terms whereas periapsis and apoapsis may also refer to the smallest and largest distances of the orbiter and its host.</li>\n<li>For a body orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, the point of The \'-gee\', \'-helion\', \'-astron\' and \'-galacticon\' forms are frequently used in the astronomical literature when referring to the Earth, Sun, stars and the Galactic Center respectively.  The suffix \'-jove\' is occasionally used for Jupiter, while \'-saturnium\' has very rarely been used in the last 50 years for Saturn. The \'-gee\' form is commonly used as a generic \'closest approach to planet\' term instead of specifically applying to the Earth. During the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, the terms <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pericynthion" title="Pericynthion">pericynthion</a></i> and <i>apocynthion</i> (referencing Cynthia, an alternative name for the Greek Moon goddess <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>) were used when referring to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Regarding black holes, the term <i>peri/apomelasma</i> (from a Greek root) was used by physicist and science-fiction author <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_A._Landis" title="Geoffrey A. Landis">Geoffrey A. Landis</a> in a 1998 story,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asimov\'s_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Asimov\'s-5">[5]</a></sup> before <i>peri/aponigricon</i> (from Latin) appeared in the scientific literature in 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as well as <i>peri/apobothron</i> (from Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Bothros" title="Bothros">bothros</a></i>, meaning hole or pit).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_summary">Terminology summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The following suffixes are added to peri- and apo- to form the terms for the nearest and farthest orbital distances from these objects. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apsis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The words perihelion and aphelion were coined by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> to describe the orbital motion of the planets.\nThe words are formed from the prefixes <i>peri-</i> (Greek: <i>&#960;&#949;&#961;&#943;</i>, near)  and <i>apo-</i> (Greek: <i>&#7936;&#960;&#972;</i>, away from) affixed to the Greek word for the sun, &#7973;&#955;&#953;&#959;&#962;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span At perihelion, the Earth\'s center is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999983290000000000&#9824;</span>0.983<span style="margin-left:.25em;">29</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a> (AU) or 147,098,070&#160;km (91,402,500&#160;mi) from the Sun\'s center. In contrast, the Earth reaches aphelion currently in early July, approximately 14 days after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/June_Solstice" title="June Solstice">June Solstice</a>. The aphelion distance between the Earth\'s and Sun\'s centers is currently about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011152097651119397&#9824;</span>1.016<span style="margin-left:.25em;">71</span>&#160;AU</span> or 152,097,700&#160;km (94,509,100&#160;mi). Dates change over time due to precession and other orbital factors, which follow cyclical patterns known as <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>. In the short term, the dates of perihelion and aphelion can vary up to 2 days from one year to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This significant variation is due to the presence of the Moon: while the Earth&#8211;Moon barycenter is moving on a stable orbit around the Sun, the position of the Earth\'s center which is on average about 4,700 kilometres (2,900&#160;mi) from the barycenter, could be shifted in any direction from it &#8211; and this affects the timing of the actual closest approach between the Sun\'s and the Earth\'s centers (which in turn defines the timing of perihelion in a given year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of the increased distance at aphelion, only 93.55% of the solar radiation from the Sun falls on a given area of land as does at perihelion. However, this fluctuation does not account for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons" title="Seasons">seasons</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as it is <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> in the northern hemisphere when it is <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> in the southern hemisphere and <i>vice versa.</i> Instead, seasons result from the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">tilt of Earth\'s axis</a>, which is 23.4 degrees away from perpendicular to the plane of Earth\'s orbit around the sun. Winter falls on the hemisphere where sunlight strikes least directly, and summer falls where sunlight strikes most directly, regardless of the Earth\'s distance from the Sun. In the northern hemisphere, summer occurs at the same time as aphelion. Despite this, there are larger land masses in the northern hemisphere, which are easier to heat than the seas. Consequently, summers are 2.3&#160;&#176;C (4&#160;&#176;F) warmer in the northern hemisphere than in the southern hemisphere under similar conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earth_at_Aphelion_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earth_at_Aphelion-15">[15]</a></sup> Astronomers commonly express the timing of perihelion relative to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">vernal equinox</a> not in terms of days and hours, but rather as an angle of orbital displacement, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Longitude_of_the_periapsis" title="Longitude of the periapsis">longitude of the periapsis</a> (also called longitude of the pericenter). For the orbit of the Earth, this is called the <i>longitude of perihelion</i>, and in 2000 it was about 282.895&#176;; by the year 2010, this had advanced by a small fraction of a degree to about 283.067&#176;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the orbit of the Earth around the Sun, the time of apsis is often expressed in terms of a time relative to seasons, since this determines the contribution of the elliptical orbit to seasonal variations. The variation of the seasons is primarily controlled by the annual cycle of the elevation angle of the Sun, which is a result of the tilt of the axis of the Earth measured from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">plane of the ecliptic</a>. The Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> and other orbital elements are not constant, but vary slowly due to the perturbing effects of the planets and other objects in the solar system. See <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>. On a very long time scale, the dates of the perihelion and of the aphelion progress through the seasons, and they make one complete cycle in 22,000 to 26,000 years. There is a corresponding movement of the position of the stars as seen from Earth that is called the <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">apsidal precession</a>. (This is closely related to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">precession of the axis</a>.)
