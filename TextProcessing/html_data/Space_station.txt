For stations named "Space", see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Space_(disambiguation)" title="Space (disambiguation)">Space (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2832" data-file-width="4256" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg/220px-STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg/330px-STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg/440px-STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS-135_final_flyaround_of_ISS_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The modular <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, the largest human-made body in Earth orbit</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>space station</b>, also known as an <b>orbital station</b> or an <b>orbital space station</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">Space craft</a>  capable of supporting crewmembers, which is designed to remain in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> (most commonly as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>) for an extended period of time and for other spacecraft to dock. A space station is distinguished from other spacecraft used for <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> by lack of major <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a> systems. Instead, other vehicles transport people and cargo to and from the station. As of  2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, one fully functioning space station is in Earth orbit: the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (operational and permanently inhabited). Various other components of future space stations, such as Japan\'s <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">space elevator</a> and U.S. inflatable modules, are also being tested in orbit. Previous stations include the <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut_Program" title="Salyut Program">Salyut series</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-1" title="Tiangong-1">Tiangong-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-2" title="Tiangong-2">Tiangong-2</a> . China, Russia, the U.S., as well as a few private companies are all planning other stations for the coming decades.\n</p><p>Today\'s space stations are research platforms, used to study the effects of long-term space flight on the human body as well as to provide platforms for greater number and length of scientific studies than available on other space vehicles. Each crew member stays aboard the station for weeks or months, but rarely more than a year. Since the ill-fated flight of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> to <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a>, all human spaceflight duration records have been set aboard space stations. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_spaceflight_records#10_longest_human_space_flights" title="List of spaceflight records">duration record for a single spaceflight</a> is 437.7 days, set by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valeriy_Polyakov" title="Valeriy Polyakov">Valeriy Polyakov</a> aboard <i>Mir</i> from 1994 to 1995. As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, four cosmonauts have completed single missions of over a year, all aboard <i>Mir</i>. Space stations have also been used for both military and civilian purposes. The last military-use space station was <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a>, which was used by the <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> program of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in 1976 and 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input in <i>The Problem of Space Travel</i> (1929)</div></div></div>\n<p>Space stations have been envisaged since at least as early as 1869 when <a href="/wiki/Edward_Everett_Hale" title="Edward Everett Hale">Edward Everett Hale</a> wrote "<a href="/wiki/The_Brick_Moon" title="The Brick Moon">The Brick Moon</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-2">[2]</a></sup> The first to give serious consideration to space stations were <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a> in the early 20th century and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Oberth" title="Hermann Oberth">Hermann Oberth</a> about two decades later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boys_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-boys-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1929 <a href="/wiki/Herman_Poto%C4%8Dnik" title="Herman Poto&#269;nik">Herman Poto&#269;nik</a>\'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Problem_of_Space_Travel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Problem of Space Travel (page does not exist)">The Problem of Space Travel</a></i> was published, the first to envision a "rotating wheel" space station to create artificial gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, German scientists researched the theoretical concept of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_weapon" title="Orbital weapon">orbital weapon</a> based on a space station. Pursuing Oberth\'s idea of a space-based weapon, the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Sun_gun" title="Sun gun">sun gun</a>" was a concept of a space station orbiting Earth at a height of 8,200 kilometres (5,100&#160;mi), with a weapon that was to utilize the sun\'s energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1951, in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collier%27s_Weekly" title="Collier\'s Weekly">Collier\'s Weekly</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> published his design for a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_wheel_space_station" title="Rotating wheel space station">rotating wheel space station</a>, which referenced Poto&#269;nik\'s idea &#8211; however these concepts would never leave the concept stage during the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boys_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-boys-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the same time as von Braun pursued Poto&#269;nik\'s ideas, the Soviet design bureaus &#8211; chiefly <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Chelomey" title="Vladimir Chelomey">Vladimir Chelomey</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-52" title="OKB-52">OKB-52</a> &#8211; were pursuing Tsiolkovsky\'s ideas for space stations. The work by OKB-52 would lead to the Almaz programme and (together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">OKB-1</a>) to the first space station: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a>. The developed hardware laid the ground for the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut and Mir space stations</a>, and is even today a considerable part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS space station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyut_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salyut-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Salyut.2C_Almaz.2C_and_Skylab_.281971.E2.80.931986.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skylab_3_flyaround.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3900" data-file-width="3820" decoding="async" height="225" href="/wiki/File:Skylab_3_flyaround.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The U.S. Skylab station of the 1970s</div></div></div>\n<p>The first space station was <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a>, which was launched by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> on April 19, 1971. Like all the early space stations, it was "monolithic", intended to be constructed and launched in one piece, and then inhabited by a crew later. As such, monolithic stations generally contained all their supplies and experimental equipment when launched, and were considered "expended", and then abandoned, when these were used up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyut_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salyut-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earlier Soviet stations were all designated "Salyut", but among these there were two distinct types: civilian and military. The military stations, <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a>, were also known as <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outposts_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Outposts-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The civilian stations <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">Salyut 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_7" title="Salyut 7">Salyut 7</a> were built with two docking ports, which allowed a second crew to visit, bringing a new spacecraft with them; the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-T" title="Soyuz 7K-T">Soyuz ferry</a> could spend 90 days in space, after which point it needed to be replaced by a fresh Soyuz spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MHH_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MHH-7">[7]</a></sup> This allowed for a crew to man the station continually. <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a> was also equipped with two docking ports, like second-generation stations, but the extra port was never utilized. The presence of a second port on the new stations allowed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">Progress</a> supply vehicles to be docked to the station, meaning that fresh supplies could be brought to aid long-duration missions. This concept was expanded on Salyut 7, which "hard docked" with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_spacecraft" title="TKS spacecraft">TKS tug</a> shortly before it was abandoned; this served as a proof-of-concept for the use of modular space stations. The later Salyuts may reasonably be seen as a transition between the two groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Outposts_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Outposts-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Mir_.281986.E2.80.932001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mir_(1986&#8211;2001)"><i>Mir</i> (1986&#8211;2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mir (1986&#8211;2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mir_during_STS-74_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3830" data-file-width="4718" decoding="async" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Mir_during_STS-74_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mir_during_STS-74_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth and the <i>Mir</i> station</div></div></div>\n<p>Unlike previous stations, the Soviet space station <i><a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a></i> had a <a href="/wiki/Modular_design" title="Modular design">modular</a> design; a core unit was launched, and additional modules, generally with a specific role, were later added to that.  This method allows for greater flexibility in operation, as well as removing the need for a single immensely powerful <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a>. Modular stations are also designed from the outset to have their supplies provided by logistical support, which allows for a longer lifetime at the cost of requiring regular support launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Future modules are still based on initial design and capabilities.\n</p>\n<h3><span Connections are made manually for electrical power, data, propulsion and cooling fluids. This results in a single piece which is not designed for disassembly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Russian Orbital Segment\'s modules are able to launch, fly and dock themselves without human intervention using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton rockets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Connections are automatically made for power, data and propulsion fluids and gases. The Russian approach would hypothetically allow the assembly of space stations orbiting other worlds in preparation for human missions.\n</p><p>Russian modular or "second-generation" space stations differ from "monolithic" single-piece stations by allowing reconfiguration of the station to suit changing needs. <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>, on 2 April 2018 at 00:15 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space-track_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-space-track-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Canceled projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MOL_USAF.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="551" data-file-width="695" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/MOL_USAF.png/220px-MOL_USAF.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/MOL_USAF.png/330px-MOL_USAF.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/MOL_USAF.png/440px-MOL_USAF.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MOL_USAF.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1967 conceptual drawing of Gemini B reentry module separating from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_Orbital_Laboratory" title="Manned Orbital Laboratory">Manned Orbital Laboratory</a> (MOL) (USAF)</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Manned_Orbiting_Laboratory" title="Manned Orbiting Laboratory">Manned Orbiting Laboratory</a> (MOL)</i> project was to employ elements of existing <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a> craft. This was unusual in being an explicitly military project, as opposed to the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> program, which was heavily intertwined with, and concealed by, the contemporaneous Salyut program. It was canceled in 1969, about a year before the first planned test flight.</li>\n<li>A second Skylab unit (<i><a href="/wiki/Skylab_B" title="Skylab B">Skylab B</a></i>) was manufactured, as a backup article; due to the high costs of providing launch vehicles, and a desire by NASA to cease Saturn and Apollo operations in time to prepare for the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> coming into service, it was never flown. The hull can now be seen in the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>, in Washington DC.</li>\n<li>A number of additional <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut_Program" title="Salyut Program">Salyuts</a></i> were produced, as backups or as flight articles that were later canceled.</li>\n<li>The U.S. <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Station_Freedom" title="Space Station Freedom">Space Station Freedom</a></i> program, despite being under development for ten years, was never launched, instead evolving into the International Space Station.</li>\n<li>The Soviet/Russian <i><a href="/wiki/Mir-2" title="Mir-2">Mir-2</a></i> station, which was never constructed, had some of its elements incorporated into the International Space Station.</li>\n<li>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Space_Facility" title="Industrial Space Facility">Industrial Space Facility</a></i> was a station proposed in the 1980s that was to be privately funded. The project was canceled when the company created to build it, <a href="/wiki/Space_Industries_Incorporated" title="Space Industries Incorporated">Space Industries Incorporated</a>, was unable to secure funding from the United States government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The European <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbus_(spacecraft)" title="Columbus (spacecraft)">Columbus</a></i> project planned to create a small space station serviced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermes_(shuttle)" title="Hermes (shuttle)">Hermes</a> shuttle. It evolved into the ISS <a href="/wiki/Columbus_(ISS_module)" title="Columbus (ISS module)">Columbus</a> module.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Excalibur_Almaz" title="Excalibur Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</a>, a company based in the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, was developing a reusable space vehicle and a space station based on old Soviet "Almaz" technology for flight in the early 2010s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In March 2016, plans were announced to have the equipment converted into an educational exhibit, owing to lack of funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planned_projects">Planned projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Planned projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spacecolony3edit.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4515" data-file-width="5732" decoding="async" height="173" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Spacecolony3edit.jpeg/330px-Spacecolony3edit.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Spacecolony3edit.jpeg/440px-Spacecolony3edit.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spacecolony3edit.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior view of an <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_cylinder" title="O\'Neill cylinder">O\'Neill cylinder</a></div></div></div>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> plans to establish its first permanent space station, provisionally called the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_large_modular_space_station" title="Chinese large modular space station">Chinese large modular space station</a>, to receive crews by 2022, with construction currently scheduled to begin in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>\n<li>In April 2008, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roscosmos_State_Corporation" title="Roscosmos State Corporation">Roscosmos State Corporation</a> proposed the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Piloted_Assembly_and_Experiment_Complex" title="Orbital Piloted Assembly and Experiment Complex">Orbital Piloted Assembly and Experiment Complex</a> (OPSEK), a space station that would serve as an orbital assembly yard for spacecraft too heavy to launch from Earth directly. It would not begin construction or be finished until after the decommissioning of the International Space Station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This plan was described to ISS partners by <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Perminov" title="Anatoly Perminov">Anatoly Perminov</a> on 17 June 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>\n<li>American firm <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Aerospace" title="Bigelow Aerospace">Bigelow Aerospace</a> is developing the <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station" title="Bigelow Commercial Space Station">Bigelow Commercial Space Station</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private</a> orbital complex. Bigelow proposes to construct the space station using the <a href="/wiki/B330" title="B330">B330</a> expandable spacecraft module, as well other modules. On 8 April 2016, a smaller scale <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Expandable_Activity_Module" title="Bigelow Expandable Activity Module">Bigelow Expandable Activity Module</a> was launched to the International Space Station to test expandable habitat technology in situ.</li>\n<li>Russian corporation Orbital Technologies is developing the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station" title="Orbital Technologies Commercial Space Station">Orbital Technologies Commercial Space Station</a>, a private space station that would serve as a hotel for <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="1138" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg/220px-ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg/330px-ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg/440px-ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ExplorationGatewayPlatform_components.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Proposed Exploration Gateway Platform at EML-1</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>In December 2011 <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> proposed an <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Gateway_Platform" title="Exploration Gateway Platform">Exploration Gateway Platform</a> to be constructed at the ISS and relocated via space tug to an Earth-Moon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_point" title="Lagrange point">Lagrange point</a> (EML-1 or 2). The purpose of the platform would be to serve as a <a href="/wiki/Propellant_depot" title="Propellant depot">propellant depot</a> and to support lunar landing missions with a reusable lunar lander after the first two <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">SLS</a> flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> plans to upgrade its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRO_Orbital_Vehicle" title="ISRO Orbital Vehicle">ISRO Orbital Vehicle</a> to perform rendezvous and <a href="/wiki/Docking_and_berthing_of_spacecraft" title="Docking and berthing of spacecraft">docking</a> after the planned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_human_spaceflight_programme" title="Indian human spaceflight programme">Indian human spaceflight programme</a> by 2021.</li>\n<li>Starting in 2015 <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is developing <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Habitat" title="Deep Space Habitat">Deep Space Habitats</a> (DSH) under the <a href="/wiki/Next_Space_Technologies_for_Exploration_Partnerships" title="Next Space Technologies for Exploration Partnerships">Next Space Technologies for Exploration Partnerships</a> (NextSTEP) for Beyond Earth Orbit (BEO) spacestations and transfer vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/NanoRacks" title="NanoRacks">NanoRacks</a>, after finalizing its contract with NASA, and after winning NextSTEPs Phase II award, is now developing its concept Independence-1 (previously known as Ixion), which would turn spent rocket tanks into a <a href="/wiki/Wet_workshop" title="Wet workshop">habitable living area</a> to be tested out in space. In 2018, NanoRacks announced that Ixion is now Independence-1, the first \'outpost\' in NanoRacks\' Space Outpost Program.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbital_Station" title="Lunar Orbital Station">Lunar Orbital Station</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbital_Platform-Gateway" title="Lunar Orbital Platform-Gateway">Lunar Orbital Platform-Gateway</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span
