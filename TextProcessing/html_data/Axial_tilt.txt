For the book, see <a href="/wiki/Obliquity_(book)" title="Obliquity (book)">Obliquity (book)</a>.</div>\n<p>\nIn <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <b>axial tilt</b>, also known as <b>obliquity</b>, is the angle between an object\'s <a href="/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis" title="Rotation around a fixed axis">rotational axis</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital</a> axis, or, equivalently, the angle between its <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> plane and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">orbital plane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It differs from <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">orbital inclination</a>.\n</p><p>At an obliquity of 0 degrees, the two axes point in the same direction; i.e., the rotational axis is perpendicular to the orbital plane. Earth\'s obliquity oscillates between 22.1 and 24.5 degrees<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> on a 41,000-year cycle; Earth\'s mean obliquity is currently 23&#176;26&#8242;12.5&#8243;&#160;(or&#160;23.43681&#176;) and decreasing.\n</p><p>Over the course of an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a>, the obliquity usually does not change considerably, and the orientation of the axis remains the same relative to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">background</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">stars</a>. This causes one pole to be directed more toward the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> on one side of the <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, and the other pole on the other side&#8212;the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="infobox" style="width: 280px;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="white-space: nowrap;">\n<table cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" style="background-color: white;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="padding-top: 12px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Planet_axis_comparison.png" title="The axial tilt of Earth, Uranus, and Venus"><img alt="The axial tilt of Earth, Uranus, and Venus" data-file-height="402" data-file-width="706" decoding="async" height="159" <b>positive</b> pole of a planet is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">right-hand rule</a>: if the fingers of the right hand are curled in the direction of the rotation then the thumb points to the positive pole. The axial tilt is defined as the angle between the direction of the positive pole and the normal to the orbital plane. The angles for Earth, Uranus and Venus are approximately  23&#176;,  97&#176;, and 177&#176; respectively. \n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>There are two standard methods of specifying tilt. The <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) defines the <i>north pole</i> of a planet as that which lies on Earth\'s north side of the <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">invariable plane</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> under this system, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> is tilted 3&#176; and spins retrograde, opposite that of most of the other planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorreiaVenusI_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorreiaVenusI-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The IAU also uses the <a href="/wiki/Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation" title="Axis&#8211;angle representation">right-hand rule</a> to define a <i>positive pole</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> for the purpose of determining orientation. Using this convention, Venus is tilted 177&#176; ("upside down").\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Axial_tilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AxialTiltObliquity.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="590" data-file-width="760" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/220px-AxialTiltObliquity.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/330px-AxialTiltObliquity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/AxialTiltObliquity.png/440px-AxialTiltObliquity.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AxialTiltObliquity.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth\'s axial tilt (obliquity) is currently about 23.4&#176;.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital plane</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> plane, and <a href="/wiki/Earth#Axial_tilt_and_seasons" title="Earth">Earth\'s tilt</a> is known to astronomers as the obliquity of the ecliptic, being the angle between the ecliptic and the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is denoted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_letter" title="Greek letter">Greek letter</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Epsilon" title="Epsilon">&#949;</a></i>.\n</p><p>Earth currently has an axial tilt of about 23.4&#176;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> This value remains about the same relative to a stationary orbital plane throughout the cycles of <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">axial precession</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> But the ecliptic (<i>i.e</i>., Earth\'s orbit) moves due to planetary <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a>, and the obliquity of the ecliptic is not a fixed quantity. At present, it is decreasing at a rate of about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_of_arc" title="Second of arc">47&#8243;</a> per <a href="/wiki/Century" title="Century">century</a> <i>(see details in <a href="#Short_term">Short term</a> below)</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Axial_tilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Earth\'s obliquity may have been reasonably accurately measured as early as 1100 BC in India and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The ancient Greeks had good measurements of the obliquity since about 350 BC, when <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> of Marseilles measured the shadow of a <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">gnomon</a> at the summer solstice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> About 830 AD, the Caliph <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Mamun" title="Al-Mamun">Al-Mamun</a> of Baghdad directed his astronomers to measure the obliquity, and the result was used in the Arab world for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1437, <a href="/wiki/Ulugh_Beg" title="Ulugh Beg">Ulugh Beg</a> determined the Earth\'s axial tilt as 23&#176;30&#8242;17&#8243; (23.5047&#176;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was widely believed, during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, that both precession and Earth\'s obliquity oscillated around a mean value, with a period of 672 years, an idea known as <i>trepidation</i> of the equinoxes. Perhaps the first to realize this was incorrect (during historic time) was <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Shatir" title="Ibn al-Shatir">Ibn al-Shatir</a> in the fourteenth century<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\nand the first to realize that the obliquity is decreasing at a relatively constant rate was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracastoro" title="Fracastoro">Fracastoro</a> in 1538.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The first accurate, modern, western observations of the obliquity were probably those of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> from <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, about 1584,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> although observations by several others, including <a href="/wiki/Al-Ma%27mun" title="Al-Ma\'mun">al-Ma\'mun</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharaf_al-D%C4%ABn_al-T%C5%ABs%C4%AB" title="Sharaf al-D&#299;n al-T&#363;s&#299;">al-Tusi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_Purbach" title="Georg Purbach">Purbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus" title="Regiomontanus">Regiomontanus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Walther" title="Bernhard Walther">Walther</a>, could have provided similar information.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasons">Seasons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Axial_tilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seasons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">Season</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s axis remains tilted in the same direction with reference to the background stars throughout a year (regardless of where it is in its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>). This means that one pole (and the associated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemispheres_of_the_Earth" title="Hemispheres of the Earth">hemisphere of Earth</a>) will be directed away from the Sun at one side of the orbit, and half an orbit later (half a year later) this pole will be directed towards the Sun. This is the cause of Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">Summer</a> occurs in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">Northern hemisphere</a> when the north pole is directed toward the Sun. Variations in Earth\'s axial tilt can influence the seasons and is likely a factor in long-term <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> <i>(also see <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>)</i>.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a The red point represents the year 2000.</div></div></div>\n<p>The exact angular value of the obliquity is found by observation of the motions of Earth and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a> over many years. Astronomers produce new <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris" title="Fundamental ephemeris">fundamental ephemerides</a> as the accuracy of <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">observation</a> improves and as the understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">dynamics</a> increases, and from these ephemerides various astronomical values, including the obliquity, are derived.\n</p><p>Annual <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">almanacs</a> are published listing the derived values and methods of use. Until 1983, the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac" title="Astronomical Almanac">Astronomical Almanac</a>\'s angular value of the mean obliquity for any date was calculated based on the <a href="/wiki/Newcomb%27s_Tables_of_the_Sun" title="Newcomb\'s Tables of the Sun">work of Newcomb</a>, who analyzed positions of the planets until about 1895:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="texhtml"><i>&#949;</i> = 23&#176; 27&#8242; 8.26&#8243; &#8722; 46.845&#8243; <i>T</i> &#8722; 0.0059&#8243; <i>T</i><sup>2</sup> + <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997181000000000000&#9824;</span>0.001<span style="margin-left:.25em;">81</span></span>&#8243; <i>T</i><sup>3</sup></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where <span class="texhtml"><i>&#949;</i></span> is the obliquity and <span class="texhtml"><i>T</i></span> is <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">tropical centuries</a> from <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">B1900.0</a> to the date in question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1984, the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory_Development_Ephemeris" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory Development Ephemeris">Jet Propulsion Laboratory\'s DE series</a> of computer-generated ephemerides took over as the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris" title="Fundamental ephemeris">fundamental ephemeris</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac" title="Astronomical Almanac">Astronomical Almanac</a>. For instance, the <i>Astronomical Almanac</i> for 2010 specifies:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="texhtml"><i>&#949;</i> = 23&#176; 26&#8242; 21.406&#8243; &#8722; <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001468367689999999&#9824;</span>46.836<span style="margin-left:.25em;">769</span></span>&#8243; <i>T</i> &#8722; <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996183100000000000&#9824;</span>0.000<span style="margin-left:.25em;">1831</span></span>&#8243; <i>T</i><sup>2</sup> + <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997200339999999999&#9824;</span>0.002<span style="margin-left:.25em;">003</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">40</span></span>&#8243; <i>T</i><sup>3</sup> &#8722; 5.76&#8243; &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;7</sup> <i>T</i><sup>4</sup> &#8722; 4.34&#8243; &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;8</sup> <i>T</i><sup>5</sup></span></dd></dl>\n<p>These expressions for the obliquity are intended for high precision over a relatively short time span, perhaps <span class="texhtml">&#177;</span> several centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> J. Laskar computed an expression to order <span class="texhtml"><i>T</i><sup>10</sup></span> good to 0.02&#8243; over 1000 years and several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">arcseconds</a> over 10,000 years.\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="texhtml"><i>&#949;</i> = 23&#176; 26&#8242; 21.448&#8243; &#8722; 4680.93&#8243; <i>t</i> &#8722; 1.55&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>2</sup> + 1999.25&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>3</sup> &#8722; 51.38&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>4</sup> &#8722; 249.67&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>5</sup> &#8722; 39.05&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>6</sup> + 7.12&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>7</sup> + 27.87&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>8</sup> + 5.79&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>9</sup> + 2.45&#8243; <i>t</i><sup>10</sup></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where here <span class="texhtml"><i>t</i></span> is multiples of 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">Julian years</a> from <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">J2000.0</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laskar_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-laskar-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These expressions are for the so-called <i>mean</i> obliquity, that is, the obliquity free from short-term variations. Periodic motions of the Moon and of Earth in its orbit cause much smaller (9.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">arcseconds</a>) short-period (about 18.6 years) oscillations of the rotation axis of Earth, known as <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_nutation" title="Astronomical nutation">nutation</a>, which add a periodic component to Earth\'s obliquity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <i>true</i> or instantaneous obliquity includes this nutation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_term">Long term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Axial_tilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Long term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">Formation and evolution of the Solar System</a></div>\n<p>Using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_methods" title="Numerical methods">numerical methods</a> to simulate <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> behavior, long-term changes in Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, and hence its obliquity, have been investigated over a period of several million years. For the past 5 million years, Earth\'s obliquity has varied between <span class="nowrap">22&#176; 2&#8242; 33&#8243;</span> and <span class="nowrap">24&#176; 30&#8242; 16&#8243;</span>, with a mean period of 41,040 years. This cycle is a combination of precession and the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Term_(mathematics)" title="Term (mathematics)">term</a> in the motion of the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>. For the next 1 million years, the cycle will carry the obliquity between <span class="nowrap">22&#176; 13&#8242; 44&#8243;</span> and <span class="nowrap">24&#176; 20&#8242; 50&#8243;</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> has a stabilizing effect on Earth\'s obliquity. Frequency map analysis conducted in 1993 suggested that, in the absence of the Moon, the obliquity can change rapidly due to <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stability_of_the_Solar_System" title="Stability of the Solar System">chaotic behavior</a> of the Solar System, reaching as high as 90&#176; in as little as a few million years <i>(also see <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">Orbit of the Moon</a>)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LaskarRobutel_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LaskarRobutel-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\nHowever, more recent numerical simulations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> made in 2011 indicated that even in the absence of the Moon, Earth\'s obliquity might not be quite so unstable; varying only by about 20&#8211;25&#176;.\nTo resolve this contradiction, diffusion rate of obliquity has been calculated, and it was found that it takes more than billions of years for Earth\'s obliquity to reach near 90&#176;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Moon\'s stabilizing effect will continue for less than 2 billion years. As the Moon continues to recede from Earth due to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">tidal acceleration</a>, resonances may occur which will cause large oscillations of the obliquity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and In both graphs, the red point represents the year 1850. <i>(Source: Berger, 1976)</i>.</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_System_bodies">Solar System bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Axial_tilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solar System bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>All four of the innermost, rocky planets of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> may have had large variations of their obliquity in the past. Since obliquity is the angle between the axis of rotation and the direction perpendicular to the orbital plane, it changes as the orbital plane changes due to the influence of other planets. But the axis of rotation can also move (<a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">axial precession</a>), due to torque exerted by the sun on a planet\'s equatorial bulge. Like Earth, all of the rocky planets show axial precession. If the precession rate were very fast the obliquity would actually remain fairly constant even as the orbital plane changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ward-31">[31]</a></sup> The rate varies due to <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">tidal dissipation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_core" title="Planetary core">core</a>-<a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> interaction, among other things. When a planet\'s precession rate approaches certain values, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonances</a> may cause large changes in obliquity. The amplitude of the contribution having one of the resonant rates is divided by the difference between the resonant rate and the precession rate, so it becomes large when the two are similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ward_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ward-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> have most likely been stabilized by the tidal dissipation of the Sun. Earth was stabilized by the Moon, as mentioned above, but before its <a href="/wiki/Moon#Formation" title="Moon">capture</a>, Earth, too, could have passed through times of instability. <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>\'s obliquity is quite variable over millions of years and may be in a chaotic state; it varies as much as 0&#176; to 60&#176; over some millions of years, depending on <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a> of the planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LaskarRobutel_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-LaskarRobutel-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some authors dispute that Mars\'s obliquity is chaotic, and show that tidal dissipation and viscous core-mantle coupling are adequate for it to have reached a fully damped state, similar to Mercury and Venus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorreiaVenusI_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CorreiaVenusI-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Correia2009_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Correia2009-33">[33]</a></sup>\nThe occasional shifts in the axial tilt of Mars have been suggested as an explanation for the appearance and disappearance of rivers and lakes over the course of the existence of Mars. A shift could cause a burst of methane into the atmosphere, causing warming, but then the methane would be destroyed and the climate would become arid again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The obliquities of the outer planets are considered relatively class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet#Rotation_and_axial_tilt" title="Exoplanet">Exoplanet &#167;&#160;Rotation and axial tilt</a></div>\n<p>The stellar obliquity <span class="texhtml"><i>&#968;</i><sub>s</sub></span>, i.e. the axial tilt of a star with respect to the orbital plane of one of its planets, has been determined for only a few systems. But for 49 stars as of today, the sky-projected spin-orbit misalignment <span class="texhtml"><i>&#955;</i></span> has been observed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRM_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRM-38">[38]</a></sup> which serves as a lower limit to <span class="texhtml"><i>&#968;</i><sub>s</sub></span>. Most of these measurements rely on the <a href="/wiki/Rossiter%E2%80%93McLaughlin_effect" title="Rossiter&#8211;McLaughlin effect">Rossiter&#8211;McLaughlin effect</a>. So far, it has not been possible to constrain the obliquity of an extrasolar planet. But the rotational flattening of the planet and the entourage of moons and/or rings, which are traceable with high-precision photometry, e.g. by the space-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_spacecraft" title="Kepler spacecraft">Kepler spacecraft</a>, could provide access to <span class="texhtml"><i>&#968;</i><sub>p</sub></span> in the near future.\n</p><p>Astrophysicists have applied tidal theories to predict the obliquity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planets" title="Extrasolar planets">extrasolar planets</a>. It has been shown that the obliquities of exoplanets in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> around low-mass stars tend to be eroded in less than 10<sup>9</sup> years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heller_2011_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heller_2011-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heller_2011b_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heller_2011b-40">[40]</a></sup> which means that they would not have seasons as Earth has.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
