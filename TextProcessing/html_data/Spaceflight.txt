template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Spaceflight</b> (also written <b>space flight</b>) is <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> into or through <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. Spaceflight can occur with <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> with or without humans on board. Examples of <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> include the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo Moon landing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle programs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_programme" title="Soyuz programme">Soyuz program</a>, as well as the ongoing <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. Examples of unmanned spaceflight include <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probes</a> that leave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_orbit" title="Earth orbit">Earth orbit</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellites</a>. These operate either by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telerobotic" title="Telerobotic">telerobotic</a> control or are fully <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_robot" title="Autonomous robot">autonomous</a>.\n</p><p>Spaceflight is used in <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, and also in commercial activities like <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications_satellite" title="Telecommunications satellite">satellite telecommunications</a>. Additional non-commercial uses of spaceflight include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">space observatories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellites</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a>.\n</p><p>A spaceflight typically begins with a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket_launch" title="Rocket launch">launch</a>, which provides the initial thrust to overcome the force of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and propels the spacecraft from the surface of the Earth. Once in space, the motion of a spacecraft&#160;&#8211; both when unpropelled and when under propulsion&#160;&#8211; is covered by the area of study called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">astrodynamics</a>. Some spacecraft remain in space indefinitely, some disintegrate during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric reentry</a>, and others reach a planetary or lunar surface for landing or impact.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872693506">.mw-parser-output .toclimit-2 .toclevel-1 ul,.mw-parser-output .toclimit-3 .toclevel-2 class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_spaceflight" title="History of spaceflight">History of spaceflight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_spaceflight" title="Timeline of spaceflight">Timeline of spaceflight</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1135" data-file-width="851" decoding="async" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/130px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/195px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/260px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg 2x" width="130"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Tsiolkovsky</a>, early space theorist</div></div></div>\n<p>The first theoretical proposal of space travel using <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> was published by Scottish astronomer and mathematician <a href="/wiki/William_Leitch_(scientist)" title="William Leitch (scientist)">William Leitch</a>, in an 1861 essay "A Journey Through Space".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> More well-known (though not widely outside Russia) is <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a>\'s work, "<span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1048;&#1089;&#1089;&#1083;&#1077;&#1076;&#1086;&#1074;&#1072;&#1085;&#1080;&#1077; &#1084;&#1080;&#1088;&#1086;&#1074;&#1099;&#1093; &#1087;&#1088;&#1086;&#1089;&#1090;&#1088;&#1072;&#1085;&#1089;&#1090;&#1074; &#1088;&#1077;&#1072;&#1082;&#1090;&#1080;&#1074;&#1085;&#1099;&#1084;&#1080; &#1087;&#1088;&#1080;&#1073;&#1086;&#1088;&#1072;&#1084;&#1080;</span>" (<i>The Exploration of Cosmic Space by Means of Reaction Devices</i>), published in 1903.\n</p><p>Spaceflight became an engineering possibility with the work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a>\'s publication in 1919 of his paper <i><a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard#A_Method_of_Reaching_Extreme_Altitudes" title="Robert H. Goddard">A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes</a></i>. His application of the <a href="/wiki/De_Laval_nozzle" title="De Laval nozzle">de Laval nozzle</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel_rocket" title="Liquid fuel rocket">liquid fuel rockets</a> improved efficiency enough for interplanetary travel to become possible. He also proved in the laboratory that rockets would work in the vacuum of space;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (October 2015)">specify</span></a></i>]</sup> nonetheless, his work was not taken seriously by the public. His attempt to secure an Army contract for a rocket-propelled weapon in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">first World War</a> was defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Armistice_of_11_November_1918" title="Armistice of 11 November 1918">November 11, 1918 armistice with Germany</a>.\n</p><p>Nonetheless, Goddard\'s paper was highly influential on <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Oberth" title="Hermann Oberth">Hermann Oberth</a>, who in turn influenced <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>. Von Braun became the first to produce modern rockets as guided weapons, employed by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>. Von Braun\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-2" title="V-2">V-2</a> was the first rocket to reach space, at an altitude of 189 kilometers (102 nautical miles) on a June 1944 test flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky\'s rocketry work was not fully appreciated in his lifetime, but he influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Korolev" title="Sergey Korolev">Sergey Korolev</a>, who became the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>\'s chief rocket designer under <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, to develop <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a> to carry <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> as a counter measure to United States bomber planes. Derivatives of Korolev\'s <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a> missiles were used to launch the world\'s first artificial Earth <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, on October 4, 1957, and later the first human to orbit the Earth, <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a>, on April 12, 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, von Braun and most of his rocket team surrendered to the United States, and were expatriated to work on American missiles at what became the <a href="/wiki/Army_Ballistic_Missile_Agency" title="Army Ballistic Missile Agency">Army Ballistic Missile Agency</a>. This work on missiles such as <a href="/wiki/Juno_I" title="Juno I">Juno I</a> and <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">Atlas</a> enabled launch of the first US satellite <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a> on February 1, 1958, and the first American in orbit, <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friendship_7" title="Friendship 7">Friendship 7</a></i> on February 20, 1962. As director of the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">Marshall Space Flight Center</a>, Von Braun oversaw development of a larger class of rocket called <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">Saturn</a>, which allowed the US to send the first two humans, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a>, to the Moon and back on <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> in July 1969. Over the same period, the Soviet Union secretly tried but failed to develop the <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1 rocket</a> to give them the capability to land one person on the Moon.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phases">Phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rocket_launch" title="Rocket launch">Rocket launch</a></div><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_space_launch_system_designs" title="List of space launch system designs">List of space launch system designs</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_4_Saturn_V,_s67-50531.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2369" decoding="async" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg/170px-Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg/255px-Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg/340px-Apollo_4_Saturn_V%2C_s67-50531.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_4_Saturn_V,_s67-50531.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Saturn V on the launch pad before the launch of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_4" title="Apollo 4">Apollo 4</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Rockets are the only means currently capable of reaching orbit or beyond. Other <a href="/wiki/Non-rocket_spacelaunch" title="Non-rocket spacelaunch">non-rocket spacelaunch</a> technologies have yet to be built, or remain short of orbital speeds.\nA <a href="/wiki/Rocket_launch" title="Rocket launch">rocket launch</a> for a spaceflight usually starts from a <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceport</a> (cosmodrome), which may be equipped with launch complexes and <a href="/wiki/Launch_pad" title="Launch pad">launch pads</a> for vertical rocket launches, and runways for takeoff and landing of carrier airplanes and winged spacecraft. Spaceports are situated well away from human habitation for noise and safety reasons. <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBMs</a> have various special launching facilities.\n</p><p>A launch is often restricted to certain <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch windows</a>. These windows depend upon the position of celestial bodies and orbits relative to the launch site. The biggest influence is often the rotation of the Earth itself. Once launched, orbits are normally located within relatively constant flat planes at a fixed angle to the axis of the Earth, and the Earth rotates within this orbit.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Launch_pad" title="Launch pad">launch pad</a> is a fixed structure designed to dispatch airborne vehicles. It generally consists of a launch tower and flame trench. It is surrounded by equipment used to erect, fuel, and maintain launch vehicles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaching_space">Reaching space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaching space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The most commonly used definition of <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> is everything beyond the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a>, which is 100 kilometers (62&#160;mi) above the Earth\'s surface. The United States sometimes defines outer space as everything beyond 50 miles (80&#160;km) in altitude.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">Rockets</a> are the only currently practical means of reaching space. Conventional airplane engines cannot reach space due to the lack of oxygen. Rocket engines expel <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">propellant</a> to provide forward <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> that generates enough <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> (change in velocity) to reach orbit.\n</p><p>For manned launch systems <a href="/wiki/Launch_escape_system" title="Launch escape system">launch escape systems</a> are frequently fitted to allow astronauts to escape in the case of emergency.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-rocket_spacelaunch" title="Non-rocket spacelaunch">Non-rocket spacelaunch</a></div>\n<p>Many ways to reach space other than rockets have been proposed. Ideas such as the <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">space elevator</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Momentum_exchange_tether" title="Momentum exchange tether">momentum exchange tethers</a> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotovator_(tether_propulsion)" title="Rotovator (tether propulsion)">rotovators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Skyhook_(structure)" title="Skyhook (structure)">skyhooks</a> require new materials much stronger than any currently known. Electromagnetic launchers such as <a href="/wiki/Launch_loop" title="Launch loop">launch loops</a> might be feasible with current technology. Other ideas include rocket assisted aircraft/spaceplanes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Skylon" title="Reaction Engines Skylon">Reaction Engines Skylon</a> (currently in early stage development), <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a> powered spaceplanes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RBCC" title="RBCC">RBCC</a> powered spaceplanes. Gun launch has been proposed for cargo.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaving_orbit">Leaving orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leaving orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section  <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Spaceflight#Leaving_orbit:_original_research" title="Talk:Spaceflight">Talk:Spaceflight</a>. Early <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> space vehicles successfully achieved very high altitudes without going into orbit. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> considered launching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> missions directly into lunar trajectories but adopted the strategy of first entering a temporary <a href="/wiki/Parking_orbit" title="Parking orbit">parking orbit</a> and then performing a separate burn several orbits later onto a lunar trajectory. This costs additional propellant because the parking orbit perigee must be high enough to prevent reentry while direct injection can have an arbitrarily low perigee because it will never be reached.\n</p><p>However, the parking orbit approach greatly simplified Apollo mission planning in several important ways. It substantially widened the allowable <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch windows</a>, increasing the chance of a successful launch despite minor technical problems during the countdown. The parking orbit was a stable "mission plateau" that gave the crew and controllers several hours to thoroughly check out the spacecraft after the stresses of launch before committing it to a long lunar flight; the crew could quickly return to Earth, if necessary, or an alternate Earth-orbital mission could be conducted. The parking orbit also enabled translunar trajectories that avoided the densest parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belts" title="Van Allen radiation belts">Van Allen radiation belts</a>.\n</p><p>Apollo missions minimized the performance penalty of the parking orbit by keeping its altitude as low as possible. For example, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a> used an unusually low parking orbit (even for Apollo) of 92.5 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nmi</a> by 91.5 nmi (171&#160;km by 169&#160;km) where there was significant atmospheric drag. But it was partially overcome by continuous venting of hydrogen from the third stage of the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>, and was in any event tolerable for the short stay.\n</p><p>Robotic missions do not require an abort capability or radiation minimization, and because modern launchers routinely meet "instantaneous" launch windows, space probes to the Moon and other planets generally use direct injection to maximize performance. Although some might coast briefly during the launch sequence, they do not complete one or more full parking orbits before the burn that injects them onto an Earth escape trajectory.\n</p><p>Note that the escape velocity from a celestial body decreases with altitude above that body. However, it is more fuel-efficient for a craft to burn its fuel as close to the ground as possible; see <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a> and reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Escape_Velocity_of_Earth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Escape_Velocity_of_Earth-5">[5]</a></sup> This is another\nway to explain the performance penalty associated with establishing the safe perigee of a parking orbit.\n</p><p>Plans for future crewed interplanetary spaceflight missions often include final vehicle assembly in Earth orbit, such as NASA\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_Multi-Purpose_Crew_Vehicle" title="Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle">Project Orion</a> and Russia\'s <a href="/wiki/Kliper" title="Kliper">Kliper</a>/<a href="/wiki/Parom" title="Parom">Parom</a> tandem.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrodynamics">Astrodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Astrodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_mechanics" title="Orbital mechanics">Orbital mechanics</a></div>\n<p>Astrodynamics is the study of spacecraft trajectories, particularly as they relate to gravitational and propulsion effects. energy" means the total amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> imparted by a rocket stage to its payload.  This can be the energy imparted by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_stage_(rocketry)" title="First stage (rocketry)">first stage</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> to an upper stage plus payload, or by an upper stage or spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Apogee_kick_motor" title="Apogee kick motor">kick motor</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reentry">Reentry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reentry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">Atmospheric reentry</a></div>\n<p>Vehicles in orbit have large amounts of kinetic energy. This energy must be discarded if the vehicle is to land safely without vaporizing in the atmosphere. Typically this process requires special methods to protect against <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_heating" title="Aerodynamic heating">aerodynamic heating</a>. The theory behind reentry was developed by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Julian_Allen" title="Harry Julian Allen">Harry Julian Allen</a>. Based on this theory, reentry vehicles present blunt shapes to the atmosphere for reentry. Blunt shapes mean that less than 1% of the kinetic energy ends up as heat that reaches the vehicle, and the remainder heats up the atmosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landing">Landing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Landing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo capsules all <a href="/wiki/Splashdown" title="Splashdown">splashed down</a> in the sea. These capsules were designed to land at relatively low speeds with the help of a parachute.\nRussian capsules for Soyuz make use of a big parachute and braking rockets to touch down on land.\nThe Space Shuttle glided to a touchdown like a plane.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery">Recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After a successful landing the spacecraft, its occupants and cargo can be recovered. In some cases, recovery has occurred before landing: while a spacecraft is still descending on its parachute, it can be snagged by a specially designed aircraft. human presence in space. This includes all space probes, satellites and robotic spacecraft and missions. Uncrewed spaceflight is the opposite of manned spaceflight, which is usually called <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>. Subcategories of uncrewed spaceflight are "robotic spacecraft" (objects) and "robotic space missions" (activities). A <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> is an uncrewed spacecraft with no humans on board, that is usually under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telerobotic" title="Telerobotic">telerobotic</a> control. A robotic spacecraft designed to make scientific research measurements is often called a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a>.\n</p><p>Uncrewed space missions use remote-controlled <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>. The first uncrewed space mission was <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sputnik_I" title="Sputnik I">Sputnik I</a></i>, launched October 4, 1957 to orbit the Earth. Space missions where <a href="/wiki/Animals_in_space" title="Animals in space">animals</a> but no humans are on-board are considered uncrewed missions.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Many space missions are more suited to telerobotic rather than <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">crewed</a> operation, due to lower cost and lower risk factors. In addition, some planetary destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> or the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> are too hostile for human survival, given current technology. Outer planets such as <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> are too distant to reach with current crewed spaceflight technology, so telerobotic probes are the only way to explore them. Telerobotics also allows exploration of regions that are vulnerable to contamination by Earth micro-organisms since spacecraft can be sterilized. Humans can not be sterilized in the same way as a spaceship, as they coexist with numerous micro-organisms, and these micro-organisms are also hard to contain within a spaceship or spacesuit.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Telepresence">Telepresence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Telepresence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Telerobotics becomes telepresence when the time delay is short enough to permit control of the spacecraft in close to real time by humans. Even the two seconds light speed delay for the Moon is too far away for telepresence exploration from Earth. The L1 and L2 positions permit 400-millisecond round trip delays, which is just close enough for telepresence operation. Telepresence has also been suggested as a way to repair satellites in Earth orbit from Earth. The Exploration Telerobotics Symposium in 2012 explored this and other topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-119_Flyaround_(Low_Resolution).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="303" data-file-width="425" href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a> the spacecraft reaches space and then returns to the atmosphere after following a (primarily) ballistic trajectory. This is usually because of insufficient <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a>, in which case a suborbital flight will last only a few minutes, but it is also possible for an object with enough energy for an orbit to have a trajectory that intersects the Earth\'s atmosphere, sometimes after many hours. <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_1" title="Pioneer 1">Pioneer 1</a> was NASA\'s first <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> intended to reach the Moon. A partial failure caused it to instead follow a suborbital trajectory to an altitude of 113,854 kilometers (70,746&#160;mi) before reentering the Earth\'s atmosphere 43 hours after launch.\n</p><p>The most generally recognized boundary of space is the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a> 100&#160;km above sea level. (NASA alternatively defines an astronaut as someone who has flown more than 50 miles (80&#160;km) above sea level.) It is not generally recognized by the public that the increase in potential energy required to pass the K&#225;rm&#225;n line is only about 3% of the orbital energy (potential plus kinetic energy) required by the lowest possible Earth orbit (a circular orbit just above the K&#225;rm&#225;n line.) In other words, it is far easier to reach space than to stay there. On May 17, 2004, <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Space_eXploration_Team" title="Civilian Space eXploration Team">Civilian Space eXploration Team</a> launched the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GoFast_Rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GoFast Rocket (page does not exist)">GoFast Rocket</a> on a suborbital flight, the first amateur spaceflight. On June 21, 2004, <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a> was used for the first <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">privately funded</a> <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Point-to-point">Point-to-point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Point-to-point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Point-to-point is a category of <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a> in which a spacecraft provides rapid transport between two terrestrial locations. Consider a conventional airline route between <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, a flight that normally lasts <a href="/wiki/Non-stop_flight#Future_of_ultra_long-haul" title="Non-stop flight">over twenty hours</a>. With point-to-point suborbital travel the same route could be traversed in less than one hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While no company offers this type of transportation today, <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> has revealed plans to do so as early as the 2020s using its <a href="/wiki/BFR_(rocket)" title="BFR (rocket)">BFR</a> vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-musk20170929_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-musk20170929-11">[11]</a></sup> Suborbital spaceflight over an intercontinental distance requires a vehicle velocity that is only a little lower than the velocity required to reach low Earth orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> If rockets are used, the size of the rocket relative to the payload is similar to an Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM). Any intercontinental spaceflight has to surmount problems of heating during atmosphere re-entry that are nearly as large as those faced by orbital spaceflight.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital">Orbital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Orbital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">Orbital spaceflight</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S68-27366.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8266" data-file-width="6548" decoding="async" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/S68-27366.jpg/220px-S68-27366.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/S68-27366.jpg/330px-S68-27366.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/S68-27366.jpg/440px-S68-27366.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S68-27366.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Apollo 6 heads into orbit</div></div></div>\n<p>A minimal <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital spaceflight</a> requires much higher velocities than a minimal sub-orbital flight, and so it is technologically much more challenging to achieve. To achieve orbital spaceflight, the tangential velocity around the Earth is as important as altitude. In order to perform a stable and lasting flight in space, the spacecraft must reach the minimal <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">orbital speed</a> required for a <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">closed orbit</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interplanetary">Interplanetary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Interplanetary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_spaceflight" title="Interplanetary spaceflight">Interplanetary spaceflight</a></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_travel" title="Interplanetary travel">Interplanetary travel</a> is travel between planets within a single <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary system</a>. In practice, the use of the term is confined to travel between the planets of our <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstellar">Interstellar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Interstellar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">Interstellar travel</a></div>\n<p>Five spacecraft are currently leaving the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> on escape trajectories, <i>Voyager 1</i>, <i>Voyager 2</i>, <i>Pioneer 10</i>, <i>Pioneer 11</i>, and <i>New Horizons</i>. The one farthest from the Sun is <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i>, which is more than 100 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> distant and is moving at 3.6 AU per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In comparison, <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>, the closest star other than the Sun, is 267,000 AU distant. It will take <i>Voyager 1</i> over 74,000 years to reach this distance. Vehicle designs using other techniques, such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion" title="Nuclear pulse propulsion">nuclear pulse propulsion</a> are likely to be able to reach the nearest star significantly faster. Another possibility that could allow for human interstellar spaceflight is to make use of <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">time dilation</a>, as this would make it possible for passengers in a fast-moving vehicle to travel further into the future while aging very little, in that their great speed slows down the rate of passage of on-board time.  However, attaining such high speeds would still require the use of some new, advanced method of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intergalactic">Intergalactic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a There is therefore some risk that this energy can be released prematurely and suddenly, with significant effects. When a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> rocket exploded 13 seconds after launch on January 17, 1997, there were reports of store windows 10 miles (16&#160;km) away being broken Long-term exposure causes multiple health issues. The most significant is bone loss, some of which is permanent, but microgravity also leads to significant <a href="/wiki/Deconditioning" title="Deconditioning">deconditioning</a> of muscular and cardiovascular tissues.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation">Radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Once above the atmosphere, radiation due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_belts" title="Van Allen belts">Van Allen belts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" title="Cosmic radiation">cosmic radiation</a> issues occur and increase. Further away from the Earth, <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a> can give a fatal radiation dose in minutes, and the <a href="/wiki/Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays" title="Health threat from cosmic rays">health threat from cosmic radiation</a> significantly increases the chances of cancer over a decade exposure or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_support">Life support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Life support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Life_support_system" title="Life support system">Life support system</a></div>\n<p>In human spaceflight, the <a href="/wiki/Life_support_system" title="Life support system">life support system</a> is a group of devices that allow a human being to survive in outer space. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> often uses the phrase Environmental Control and Life Support System or the acronym ECLSS when describing these systems for its <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The life support system may supply: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>. It must also maintain the correct body temperature, an acceptable pressure on the body and deal with the body\'s waste products. Shielding against harmful external influences such as radiation and micro-meteorites may also be necessary. Components of the life support system are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life-critical_system" title="Life-critical system">life-critical</a>, and are designed and constructed using <a href="/wiki/Safety_engineering" title="Safety engineering">safety engineering</a> techniques.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_weather">Space weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Space weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">Space weather</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="1782" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/280px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/420px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg/560px-Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aurora-SpaceShuttle-EO.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">Aurora australis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a>, May 1991.</div></div></div>\n<p>Space weather is the concept of changing environmental conditions in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. It is distinct from the concept of <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">planetary atmosphere</a>, and deals with phenomena involving ambient <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>, magnetic fields, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> in space (generally close to Earth but also in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">interplanetary</a>, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>). "Space weather describes the conditions in space that affect Earth and its technological systems. Our space weather is a consequence of the behavior of the Sun, the nature of Earth\'s magnetic field, and our location in the Solar System."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Space weather exerts a profound influence in several areas related to space exploration and development. Changing geomagnetic conditions can induce changes in atmospheric density causing the rapid degradation of spacecraft altitude in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a>. Geomagnetic storms due to increased solar activity can potentially blind sensors aboard spacecraft, or interfere with on-board electronics. An understanding of space environmental conditions is also important in designing shielding and life support systems for manned spacecraft.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_considerations">Environmental considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Environmental considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rockets as a class are not inherently grossly polluting. However, some rockets use toxic propellants, and most vehicles use propellants that are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a>. Many solid rockets have chlorine in the form of <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a> or other chemicals, and this can cause temporary local holes in the ozone layer. Re-entering spacecraft generate nitrates which also can temporarily impact the ozone layer. Most rockets are made of metals that can have an environmental impact during their construction.\n</p><p>In addition to the atmospheric effects there are effects on the near-Earth space environment. There is the possibility that orbit could become inaccessible for generations due to exponentially increasing <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spalling" title="Spalling">spalling</a> of satellites and vehicles (<a href="/wiki/Kessler_syndrome" title="Kessler syndrome">Kessler syndrome</a>). Many launched vehicles today are therefore designed to be re-entered after use.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3349" data-file-width="4367" decoding="async" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg/400px-S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg/600px-S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg/800px-S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:S74-15583skylabsunview.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This shows an extreme ultraviolet view of the sun (the Apollo Telescope Mount SO82A Experiment) taken during <a href="/wiki/Skylab_3" title="Skylab 3">Skylab 3</a>, with the Earth added for scale. On the right an image of the Sun shows a helium emissions, and there is an image on the left showing emissions from iron. One application for spaceflight is to take observation hindered or made more difficult by being on Earth\'s surface. Skylab included a massive manned solar observatory that revolutionized solar science in the early 1970s using the Apollo-based space station in conjunction with manned spaceflights to it.</div></div></div>\n<p>Current and proposed applications for spaceflight include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">Spy satellites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">Space exploration</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">Communication satellites</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">Satellite television</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">Satellite navigation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">Space tourism</a></li>\n<li>Protecting Earth from <a href="/wiki/Potentially_hazardous_object" title="Potentially hazardous object">Potentially hazardous objects</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">Space colonization</a></li></ul>\n<p>Most early spaceflight development was paid for by governments. However, today major launch markets such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">Communication satellites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">Satellite television</a> are purely commercial, though many of the launchers were originally funded by governments.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">Private spaceflight</a> is a rapidly developing area: space flight that is not only paid for by corporations or even private individuals, but often provided by <a href="/wiki/List_of_private_spaceflight_companies" title="List of private spaceflight companies">private spaceflight companies</a>. These companies often assert that much of the previous high cost of access to space was caused by governmental inefficiencies they can avoid. This assertion can be supported by much lower published launch costs for private space launch vehicles such as <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> developed with private financing. Lower launch costs and excellent safety will be required for the applications such as <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">Space tourism</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">Space colonization</a> to become successful.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
