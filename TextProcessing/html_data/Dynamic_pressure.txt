Dynamic pressure is in fact one of the terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_equation#Bernoulli_equations" title="Bernoulli\'s equation">Bernoulli\'s equation</a>, which can be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> for a fluid in motion. In simplified cases, the dynamic pressure is equal to the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Stagnation_pressure" title="Stagnation pressure">stagnation pressure</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Static_pressure" title="Static pressure">static pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LJC3.5_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-LJC3.5-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another important aspect of dynamic pressure is that, as <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a> shows, the <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic</a> stress (i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> within a structure subject to aerodynamic forces) experienced by an aircraft travelling at speed <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>v</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.128ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span> is proportional to the air density and square of <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>v</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.128ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span>, i.e. proportional to <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle q}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>q</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle q}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="q" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.07ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span>.\nTherefore, by looking at the variation of <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle q}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>q</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle q}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="q" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.07ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span> during flight, it is possible to determine how the stress will vary and in particular when it will reach its maximum value. The point of maximum aerodynamic load is often referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Q" title="Max Q">max Q</a></i> and it is a critical parameter in many applications, such as launch vehicles.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamic_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VenturiFlow.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="220" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/78/VenturiFlow.png" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VenturiFlow.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flow of air through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venturi_meter" title="Venturi meter">venturi meter</a>, showing the columns connected in a U-shape (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manometer" title="Manometer">manometer</a>) and partially filled with water. The meter is "read" as a differential pressure head in cm or inches of water and is equivalent to the difference in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocity_head" title="Velocity head">velocity head</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The dynamic pressure, along with the <a href="/wiki/Static_pressure" title="Static pressure">static pressure</a> and the pressure due to elevation, is used in <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_principle" title="Bernoulli\'s principle">Bernoulli\'s principle</a> as an <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">energy balance</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Closed_system" title="Closed system">closed system</a>. The three terms are used to define the state of a closed system of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompressible" title="Incompressible">incompressible</a>, constant-density fluid.\n</p><p>When the dynamic pressure is divided by the product of fluid density and <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">acceleration due to gravity, g</a>, the result is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocity_head" title="Velocity head">velocity head</a>, which is used in head equations like the one used for <a href="/wiki/Pressure_head" title="Pressure head">pressure head</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">hydraulic head</a>. In a venturi flow meter, the <i>differential pressure head</i> can be used to calculate the <i>differential velocity head</i>, which are equivalent in the adjacent picture. An alternative to <i>velocity head</i> is <i>dynamic head</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressible_flow">Compressible flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamic_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compressible flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many authors define <i>dynamic pressure</i> only for incompressible flows.
