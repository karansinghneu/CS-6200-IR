For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Sky_(disambiguation)" title="Sky (disambiguation)">Sky (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sky-3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="675" decoding="async" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Sky-3.jpg/250px-Sky-3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Sky-3.jpg/375px-Sky-3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Sky-3.jpg/500px-Sky-3.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sky-3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sky above the <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>sky</b> (or <b>celestial dome</b>) is everything that lies above the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.\n</p><p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the sky is also called the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>. This is viewed from Earth\'s surface as an abstract dome on which the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> appear to be <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">traveling</a>. The celestial sphere is conventionally divided into designated areas called <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a>. Usually, the term <i>sky</i> is used informally as the point of view from the Earth\'s surface; however, the meaning and usage can vary. In some cases, such as in discussing the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, the sky refers to only the lower, more dense portions of the atmosphere.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a>, the sky appears to be <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a> because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">scatters</a> more blue <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> than red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluesky_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bluesky-4">[4]</a></sup> At night, the sky appears to be a mostly dark surface or region spangled with stars. During the day, the Sun can be seen in the sky unless obscured by <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a> (and to some extent during the day) the Moon, planets and stars are visible in the sky. Some of the natural phenomena seen in the sky are <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbows</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_Aurora" title="Polar Aurora">aurorae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> can also be seen in the sky during <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storms</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">Birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_flying" title="Kite flying">kites</a> are often considered to <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">fly</a> in the sky. Due to human activities, <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a> during the day and <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> during the night are often seen above large <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rayleigh_sunlight_scattering.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A greater proportion of blue light scattered by the atmosphere relative to red light.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twilight_subcategories.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="961" data-file-width="1435" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Twilight_subcategories.svg/220px-Twilight_subcategories.svg.png" Dusk is the end of evening <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Low_precision_formulae_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Low_precision_formulae-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twilight_description_full_day.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1097" data-file-width="1598" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Twilight_description_full_day.svg/220px-Twilight_description_full_day.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Twilight_description_full_day.svg/330px-Twilight_description_full_day.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Twilight_description_full_day.svg/440px-Twilight_description_full_day.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Twilight_description_full_day.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">Dawn</a> is the beginning of morning twilight.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_optics" title="Atmospheric optics">Atmospheric optics</a></div>\n<p>Except for <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">light</a> that comes directly from the sun, most of the light in the day sky is caused by <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">scattering</a>, which is dominated by a small-particle limit called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rayleigh_Scattering" title="Rayleigh Scattering">Rayleigh Scattering</a>. The scattering due to <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> sized particles (as in air) is greater in the forward and backward directions than it is in the lateral direction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=\'For the non-expert, the relationship between "forward and backward directions" and "the lateral direction" and the spectrum should be made clear. (October 2014)\'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Scattering is significant for light at all visible wavelengths but is stronger at the shorter (bluer) end of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>, meaning that the scattered light is bluer than its source, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. The remaining sunlight,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What\'s this? Where? And what is the point of mentioning it (October 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> having lost some of its short wavelength components, appears slightly less blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluesky_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-bluesky-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scattering also occurs even more strongly in <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>. Individual water droplets exposed to white light will create a set of colored rings.  If a cloud is thick enough, scattering from multiple water droplets will wash out the set of colored rings and create a washed-out white color.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=\'What in the world is "a washed-out white color"? (October 2014)\'>clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sky can turn a multitude of colors such as red, orange, purple and yellow (especially near <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>) when the light must pass through a much longer path (or <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a>) through the atmosphere.  Scattering effects also partially <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarize</a> light from the sky and are most pronounced at an angle 90&#176; from the sun.  Scattered light from the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> travels through as much as 38 times the atmosphere as does light from the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a>, causing a blue <a href="/wiki/Image_gradient" title="Image gradient">gradient</a>: vivid at the zenith, and pale near the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Because red light also scatters if there is enough air between the source and the observer causing parts of the sky to change color during a sunset. As the amount of atmosphere nears infinity, the scattered light appears whiter and whiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynch_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lynch-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sun is not the only object that may appear less blue in the atmosphere. Far away clouds or snowy mountaintops may appear yellowish. The effect is not very obvious on clear days but is very pronounced when clouds cover the line of sight, reducing the blue hue from scattered sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lynch_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lynch-9">[9]</a></sup> At higher altitudes, the sky tends toward darker colors since scattering is reduced due to lower air density; an extreme example is the moon, where there is no atmosphere and no scattering, making the sky on the moon black even when the sun is visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sky <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">luminance</a> distribution models have been recommended by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Illumination" title="International Commission on Illumination">International Commission on Illumination</a> (CIE) for the design of <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">daylighting</a> schemes. Recent developments relate to &#8220;all sky models&#8221; for modelling sky <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">luminance</a> under <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> conditions ranging from clear to <a href="/wiki/Overcast" title="Overcast">overcast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dusk_and_dawn">Dusk and dawn</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg/220px-Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg/330px-Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg/440px-Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_in_Sunrise_Sky_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The crescent <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a> remains visible just moments before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_shadow" title="Earth\'s shadow">Earth\'s shadow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_flash" title="Green flash">Green flash</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sky_brightness" title="Sky brightness">Sky brightness</a></div>\n<p>The intensity of the sky varies greatly over the day, and the primary cause of that intensity differs as well. When the sun is well above the horizon, direct scattering of sunlight (Rayleigh scattering) is the overwhelmingly dominant source of light. However, in twilight, the period of time between sunset and night and between night and sunrise, the situation is more complicated. <a href="/wiki/Green_flash" title="Green flash">Green flashes</a> and green rays are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_phenomenon" title="Optical phenomenon">optical phenomena</a> that occur shortly after <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> or before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>, when a <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a> spot is visible above the sun, usually for no more than a second or two, or it may resemble a green ray shooting up from the sunset point. Green flashes are a group of phenomena that stem from different causes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-young-12">[12]</a></sup> most of which occur when there is a temperature inversion (when the temperature increases with altitude rather than the normal decrease in temperature with altitude). Green flashes may be observed from any <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> (even from an aircraft). They are usually seen at an unobstructed <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, such as over the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, but are also seen over cloud tops and mountain tops. Green flashes may also be observed at the horizon in association with the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and bright planets, including <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_shadow" title="Earth\'s shadow">Earth\'s shadow</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">shadow</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> casts on its <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_phenomenon" title="Atmospheric phenomenon">atmospheric phenomenon</a> is sometimes seen twice a day, around the times of sunset and sunrise.  When the weather conditions and the observer\'s viewing point permit a clear sight of the horizon, the shadow can be seen as a dark blue or greyish-blue band. Assuming the sky is clear, the Earth\'s shadow is visible in the half of the sky opposite to the sunset or sunrise, and is seen as a dark blue band right above the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>. A related phenomenon is the "<a href="/wiki/Belt_of_Venus" title="Belt of Venus">Belt of Venus</a>" or "anti-twilight arch", a pink band that is visible above the dark blue band of the Earth\'s shadow in the same part of the sky. There is no clear dividing line between the Earth\'s shadow and the Belt of Venus: one colored band shades into the other in the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AO_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AO-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth-shadow_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-earth-shadow-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Twilight is divided into three segments according to how far the sun is below the horizon, measured in segments of 6&#176;. After sunset the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_twilight" title="Civil twilight">civil twilight</a> sets in; it ends when the sun drops more than 6&#176; below the horizon. This is followed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_twilight" title="Nautical twilight">nautical twilight</a>, when the sun is 6&#176; and 12&#176; below the horizon (heights of between &#8722;6&#176; and &#8722;12&#176;), after which comes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_twilight" title="Astronomical twilight">astronomical twilight</a>, defined as the period from &#8722;12&#176; to &#8722;18&#176;. When the sun drops more than 18&#176; below the horizon, the sky generally attains its minimum brightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several sources can be identified as the source of the intrinsic brightness of the sky, namely <a href="/wiki/Airglow" title="Airglow">airglow</a>, indirect scattering of sunlight, scattering of starlight, and artificial <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="During_the_night">During the night</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">Night sky</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polarlicht_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1960" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="143" href="/wiki/File:Polarlicht_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aurora borealis over Bear Lake, Alaska</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deathvalleysky_nps_big.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1288" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="97" href="/wiki/File:Deathvalleysky_nps_big.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> can be seen as a large band across the night sky, and is distorted into an arch in this 360&#176; <a href="/wiki/Panorama" title="Panorama">panorama</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The term night sky refers to the sky as seen at <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>. The term is usually associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skygazing" title="Skygazing">skygazing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, with reference to views of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_bodies" title="Celestial bodies">celestial bodies</a> such as stars, the Moon, and planets that become visible on a clear night after the Sun has set. Natural light sources in a night sky include <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">moonlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starlight" title="Starlight">starlight</a>, and airglow, depending on location and timing.  The fact that the sky is not completely dark at night can be easily observed. Were the sky (in the absence of moon and city lights) absolutely dark, one would not be able to see the silhouette of an object against the sky.\n</p><p>The night sky and studies of it have a historical place in both ancient and modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultures" title="Cultures">cultures</a>.  In the past, for instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmers" title="Farmers">farmers</a> have used the state of the night sky as a <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a> to determine when to plant <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/History_of_astrology" title="History of astrology">ancient belief</a> in <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a> is generally based on the belief that relationships between heavenly bodies influence or convey information about events on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.  The <i>scientific</i> study of the night sky and bodies observed within it, meanwhile, takes place in the science of astronomy.\n</p><p>Within <a href="/wiki/Visible-light_astronomy" title="Visible-light astronomy">visible-light astronomy</a>, the visibility of celestial objects in the night sky is affected by light pollution. The presence of the Moon in the night sky has historically hindered astronomical observation by increasing the amount of ambient lighting. With the advent of artificial light sources, however, light pollution has been a growing problem for viewing the night sky. Special filters and modifications to light fixtures can help to alleviate this problem, but for the best views, both professional and amateur optical astronomers seek viewing sites located far from major urban areas.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_weather_forecasting">Use in weather forecasting</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">Weather forecasting</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast,_Dhaka,_Bangladesh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1832" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG/220px-Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG/330px-Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG/440px-Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast%2C_Dhaka%2C_Bangladesh.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Appearance_of_sky_for_weather_forecast,_Dhaka,_Bangladesh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>White cumulus clouds appeared over Dhaka, Bangladesh, when significant flooding was underway in many parts of the country.</div></div></div>\n<p>Along with pressure tendency, the condition of the sky is one of the more important parameters used to forecast weather in mountainous areas. Thickening of cloud cover or the invasion of a higher cloud deck is indicative of rain in the near future. At night, high thin <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus clouds</a> can lead to <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, which indicate the approach of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> and its associated <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Morning <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> portends fair conditions and can be associated with a <a href="/wiki/Marine_layer" title="Marine layer">marine layer</a>, an indication of a stable atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Rainy conditions are preceded by wind or clouds which prevent fog formation. The approach of a line of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> could indicate the approach of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>. Cloud-free skies are indicative of fair weather for the near future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The use of sky cover in weather prediction has led to various <a href="/wiki/Weather_lore" title="Weather lore">weather lore</a> over the centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1168" data-file-width="1788" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/220px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/330px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/440px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Picture of the sky in the eye of a tropical cyclone</div></div></div>\n<p>Within 36&#160;hours of the passage of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>\'s center, the pressure begins to fall and a veil of white cirrus clouds approaches from the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>\'s direction.  Within 24&#160;hours of the closest approach to the center, low clouds begin to move in, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(tropical_cyclone)" title="Bar (tropical cyclone)">bar</a> of a tropical cyclone, as the barometric pressure begins to fall more rapidly and the winds begin to increase. Within 18&#160;hours of the center\'s approach, <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squally</a> weather is common, with sudden increases in wind accompanied by rain showers or thunderstorms. Within six hours of the center\'s arrival, rain becomes continuous. Within an hour of the center, the rain becomes very heavy and the highest winds within the tropical cyclone are experienced. When the center arrives with a strong tropical cyclone, weather conditions improve and the sun becomes visible as the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> moves overhead. Once the system departs, winds reverse and, along with the rain, suddenly increase. One day after the center\'s passage, the low overcast is replaced with a higher overcast, and the rain becomes intermittent. By 36&#160;hours after the center\'s passage, the high overcast breaks and the pressure begins to level off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div href="/wiki/Object_(physics)" title="Object (physics)">object</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">moves</a> through or beyond the sky (as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a>), whether by generating <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">aerodynamic lift</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_propulsion" title="Air propulsion">propulsive thrust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lighter_than_air" title="Lighter than air">aerostatically</a> using <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, or by <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistic</a> movement, without any direct mechanical support from the ground.  The engineering aspects of flight are studied in <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a> which is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>, which is the study of vehicles that travel through the air, and <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>, the study of vehicles that travel through space, and in <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a>, the study of the flight of projectiles.  While <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> beings have been capable of flight via <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a> since 1783,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> other species have used flight for significantly longer.  <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> are capable of flight.  <a href="/wiki/Spore" title="Spore">Spores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> from <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> use flight, via use of the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, as a method of propagating their <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_in_mythology">Significance in mythology</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">Astrology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sky_father" title="Sky father">Sky father</a></div>\n<p>Many mythologies have deities especially associated with the sky.  In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Egyptian religion</a>, the sky was deified as the goddess <a href="/wiki/Nut_(goddess)" title="Nut (goddess)">Nut</a> and as the god <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dyeus" title="Dyeus">Dyeus</a> is reconstructed as the god of the sky, or the sky personified, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">Proto-Indo-European religion</a>, whence <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, the god of the sky and <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman</a> god of sky and thunder <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>.\n</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_mythology" title="Australian Aboriginal mythology">Australian Aboriginal mythology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altjira" title="Altjira">Altjira</a> (or Arrernte) is the main sky god and also the creator god. In <a href="/wiki/Iroquois_mythology" title="Iroquois mythology">Iroquois mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atahensic" title="Atahensic">Atahensic</a> was a sky goddess who fell down to the ground during the creation of the Earth. Many cultures have drawn <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a> between <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in the sky, using them in association with <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a> about their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deities" title="Deities">deities</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r874315073">.mw-parser-output
