The <a href="/wiki/Mass_attenuation_coefficient" title="Mass attenuation coefficient">mass attenuation coefficient</a> can be looked up or calculated for any material and energy combination using the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) databases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST2-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiography" title="Radiography">radiography</a> the calculation of the <i>mean free path</i> is more complicated, because photons are not mono-energetic, but have some <a href="/wiki/Frequency_distribution" title="Frequency distribution">distribution</a> of energies called a <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a>. As photons move through the target material, they are <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuated</a> with probabilities depending on their energy, as a result their distribution changes in process called spectrum hardening. Because of spectrum hardening, the <i>mean free path</i> of the <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> spectrum changes with distance.\n</p><p>Sometimes one measures the thickness of a material in the <i>number of mean free paths</i>. Material with the thickness of one <i>mean free path</i> will attenuate 37% (1/<a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)"><i>e</i></a>) of photons. This concept is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Half-value_layer" title="Half-value layer">half-value layer</a> (HVL): a material with a thickness of one HVL will attenuate 50% of photons.
