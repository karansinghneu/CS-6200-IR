class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Onde_compression_impulsion_1d_30_petit.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of rarefaction is also as a <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> wave or <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">phonon</a>.  Half of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound wave</a> is made up of the compression of the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">medium</a>, and the other half is the decompression or <i>rarefaction</i> of the medium.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Rarefaction</b> is the reduction of an item\'s density, the opposite of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> Like compression, which can travel in waves (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_wave" title="Sound wave">sound waves</a>, for instance), rarefaction waves also exist in nature. A common rarefaction wave is the area of low relative pressure following a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> (see picture).\n</p><p>Rarefaction waves expand with time (much like sea waves spread out as they reach a beach); in most cases rarefaction waves keep the same overall profile (\'shape\') at all times throughout the wave\'s movement: it is a <i>self-similar expansion</i>. Each part of the wave travels at the local speed of sound, in the local medium. This expansion behaviour is in contrast to the behaviour of pressure increases, which gets narrower with time, until they steepen into <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Physical_examples"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Physical examples</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Rarefaction_in_manufacturing"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Rarefaction in manufacturing</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Citations"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Citations</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_examples">Physical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rarefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A natural example of rarefaction occurs in the layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>. Because the atmosphere has <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, most atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> is nearer to the Earth due to the Earth\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>. Therefore, air at higher layers of the atmosphere is less dense, or <i>rarefied</i>, relative to air at lower layers. Thus rarefaction can refer either to a reduction in density over space at a single point of time, or a reduction of density over time for one particular area.\n</p><p>Rarefaction can be easily  observed by compressing a <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">spring</a> and releasing it. Instead of seeing compressed loops seeming to move through the spring, spaced-out loops move through it: rarefaction waves....\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rarefaction_in_manufacturing">Rarefaction in manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rarefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rarefaction in manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Modern construction of guitars is an example of using rarefaction in manufacturing. By forcing the reduction of density (loss of oils and other impurities) in the cellular structure of the soundboard, a rarefied guitar top produces a tonal decompression affecting the sound of the instrument, mimicking aged wood.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
