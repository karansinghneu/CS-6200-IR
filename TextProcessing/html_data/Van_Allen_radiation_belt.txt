class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Van_Allen_radiation_belt.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cross section of Van Allen radiation belts</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>Van Allen radiation belt</b> is a  zone of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a>, most of which originate from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, that are captured by and held around a planet by that planet\'s <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> has two such belts and sometimes others may be temporarily created. The discovery of the belts is credited to <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a>, and as a result, Earth\'s belts are known as the <b>Van Allen belts</b>. Earth\'s two main belts extend from an <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> of about 640 to 58,000&#160;km (400 to 36,040&#160;mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> above the surface in which region <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> levels vary. Most of the particles that form the belts are thought to come from <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and other particles by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts-2">[2]</a></sup> By trapping the solar wind, the magnetic field deflects those energetic particles and protects <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">the atmosphere</a> from destruction.\n</p><p>The belts are located in the inner region of Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. The belts trap energetic <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>. Other nuclei, such as <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>, are less prevalent. The belts endanger <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, which must have their sensitive components protected with adequate shielding if they spend significant time near that zone. In 2013, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> reported that the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes" title="Van Allen Probes">Van Allen Probes</a> had discovered a transient, third radiation belt, which was observed for four weeks until it was destroyed by a powerful, interplanetary <a href="/wiki/Shock_waves_in_astrophysics" title="Shock waves in astrophysics">shock wave</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_St%C3%B8rmer" title="Carl St&#248;rmer">Carl St&#248;rmer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Christofilos" title="Nicholas Christofilos">Nicholas Christofilos</a> had investigated the possibility of trapped charged particles before the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explorer_3" href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>. The trapped radiation was first mapped by <a href="/wiki/Explorer_4" title="Explorer 4">Explorer 4</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_3" title="Pioneer 3">Pioneer 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a>.\n</p><p>The term <i>Van Allen belts</i> refers specifically to the radiation belts surrounding Earth; however, similar radiation belts have been discovered around other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>. The Sun does not support long-term radiation belts, as it lacks a stable, global, dipole field. The Earth\'s atmosphere limits the belts\' particles to regions above 200&#8211;1,000&#160;km,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-intro-5">[5]</a></sup> (124&#8211;620 miles) while the belts do not extend past 8 <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">Earth radii</a> <i>R<sub>E</sub></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-intro-5">[5]</a></sup> The belts are confined to a volume which extends about 65<a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">&#176;</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intro_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-intro-5">[5]</a></sup> on either side of the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial equator</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jupiter_radio.jpg/330px-Jupiter_radio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jupiter_radio.jpg/440px-Jupiter_radio.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter_radio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jupiter\'s variable radiation belts</div></div></div>\n<p>The NASA <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes" title="Van Allen Probes">Van Allen Probes</a> mission aims at understanding (to the point of predictability) how populations of <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_electron_beam" title="Relativistic electron beam">relativistic electrons</a> and ions in space form or change in response to changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_activity" title="Solar activity">solar activity</a> and the solar wind.\n<a href="/wiki/NASA_Institute_for_Advanced_Concepts" title="NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts">NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts</a>&#8211;funded studies have proposed magnetic scoops to collect <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> that naturally occurs in the Van Allen belts of Earth, although only about 10 micrograms of <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiprotons</a> are estimated to exist in the entire belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Van Allen Probes mission successfully launched on August 30, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The primary mission is scheduled to last two years with expendables expected to last four. NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> manages the <a href="/wiki/Living_With_a_Star" title="Living With a Star">Living With a Star</a> program of which the Van Allen Probes is a project, along with <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">Solar Dynamics Observatory</a> (SDO). The <a href="/wiki/Applied_Physics_Laboratory" title="Applied Physics Laboratory">Applied Physics Laboratory</a> is responsible for the implementation and instrument management for the Van Allen Probes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Radiation belts exist around other planets and moons in the solar system that have magnetic fields powerful enough to sustain them. To date, most of these radiation belts have been poorly mapped. The Voyager Program (namely <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a>) only nominally confirmed the existence of similar belts around <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inner_belt">Inner belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inner belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="580" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg/220px-Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg/330px-Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg/440px-Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rendering_of_Van_Allen_radiation_belts_of_Earth_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cutaway_drawing" title="Cutaway drawing">Cutaway drawing</a> of two radiation belts around Earth: the inner belt (red) dominated by protons and the outer one (blue) by electrons. Image Credit: NASA</div></div></div>\n<p>The inner Van Allen Belt extends typically from an altitude of 0.2 to 2 Earth radii (L values of 1 to 3) or 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi) to 6,000&#160;km (3,700&#160;mi) above the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In certain cases when solar activity is stronger or in geographical areas such as the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly" title="South Atlantic Anomaly">South Atlantic Anomaly</a>, the inner boundary may decline to roughly 200 kilometers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> above the Earth\'s surface. The inner belt contains high concentrations of electrons in the range of hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">keV</a> and energetic protons with energies exceeding 100&#160;MeV, trapped by the strong (relative to the outer belts) magnetic fields in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is believed that proton energies exceeding 50&#160;MeV in the lower belts at lower altitudes are the result of the <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> created by cosmic ray collisions with nuclei of the upper atmosphere. The source of lower energy protons is believed to be proton diffusion due to changes in the magnetic field during geomagnetic storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the slight offset of the belts from Earth\'s geometric center, the inner Van Allen belt makes its closest approach to the surface at the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly" title="South Atlantic Anomaly">South Atlantic Anomaly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goddard_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goddard-13">[13]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On March 2014, a pattern resembling \'zebra stripes\' was observed in the radiation belts by the Radiation Belt Storm Probes Ion Composition Experiment (RBSPICE) onboard <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes" title="Van Allen Probes">Van Allen Probes</a>. The reason reported was that due to the tilt in Earth\'s magnetic field axis, the planet&#8217;s rotation generated an oscillating, weak electric field that permeates through the entire inner radiation belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It was later demonstrated that the zebra stripes were in fact an imprint of <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_dynamo_region" title="Ionospheric dynamo region">ionospheric winds</a> on radiation belts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_belt">Outer belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outer belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="769" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg/300px-Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg/450px-Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg/600px-Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Laboratory simulation of the Van Allen belt\'s influence on the Solar Wind; these aurora-like <a href="/wiki/Birkeland_current" title="Birkeland current">Birkeland currents</a> were created by the scientist <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Terrella" title="Terrella">terrella</a>, a magnetized anode globe in an evacuated chamber</div></div></div>\n<p>The outer belt consists mainly of high energy (0.1&#8211;10&#160;<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a>) electrons trapped by the Earth\'s magnetosphere. It is more variable than the inner belt as it is more easily influenced by solar activity. It is almost <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">toroidal</a> in shape, beginning at an altitude of three and extending to ten Earth radii (<i>R<sub>E</sub></i>) 13,000 to 60,000 kilometres (8,100 to 37,300&#160;mi) above the Earth\'s surface. Its greatest intensity is usually around 4&#8211;5 <i>R<sub>E</sub></i>. The outer electron radiation belt is mostly produced by the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">inward radial diffusion</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and local acceleration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature437_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature437-19">[19]</a></sup> due to transfer of energy from whistler-mode <a href="/wiki/Waves_in_plasmas" title="Waves in plasmas">plasma waves</a> to radiation belt electrons. Radiation belt electrons are also constantly removed by collisions with Earth\'s atmosphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature437_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature437-19">[19]</a></sup> losses to the <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">magnetopause</a>, and their outward radial diffusion. The <a href="/wiki/Gyroradius" title="Gyroradius">gyroradii</a> of energetic protons would be large enough to bring them into contact with the Earth\'s atmosphere. Within this belt, the electrons have a high <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> and at the outer edge (close to the magnetopause), where <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">geomagnetic field</a> lines open into the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere#Magnetotail" title="Magnetosphere">geomagnetic "tail"</a>, the flux of energetic electrons can drop to the low interplanetary levels within about 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi), a decrease by a factor of 1,000.\n</p><p>In 2014 it was discovered that the inner edge of the outer belt is characterized by a very sharp transition, below which highly relativistic electrons (&gt; 5MeV) cannot penetrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The reason for this shield-like behavior is not well understood.\n</p><p>The trapped particle population of the outer belt is varied, containing electrons and various ions. Most of the ions are in the form of energetic protons, but a certain percentage are alpha particles and O<sup>+</sup> oxygen ions, similar to those in the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> but are much more energetic. This mixture of ions suggests that <a href="/wiki/Ring_current" title="Ring current">ring current</a> particles probably come from more than one source.\n</p><p>The outer belt is larger than the inner belt and its particle population fluctuates widely. Energetic (radiation) particle fluxes can increase and decrease dramatically in response to <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storms</a>, which are themselves triggered by magnetic field and plasma disturbances produced by the Sun. The increases are due to storm-related injections and acceleration of particles from the tail of the magnetosphere.\n</p><p>On February 28, 2013, a third radiation belt, consisting of high-energy <a href="/wiki/Ultrarelativistic_limit" title="Ultrarelativistic limit">ultrarelativistic</a> charged particles, was reported to be discovered. In a news conference by NASA\'s Van Allen Probe team, it was stated that this third belt is a product of <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejection</a> from the Sun. It has been represented as a separate creation which splits the Outer Belt, like a knife, on its outer side, and exists separately as a storage container of particles for a month\'s time, before merging once again with the Outer Belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The unusual stability of this third, transient belt has been explained as due to a \'trapping\' by the Earth\'s magnetic field of ultrarelativistic particles as they are lost from the second, traditional outer belt. While the outer zone, which forms and disappears over a day, is highly variable due to interactions with the atmosphere, the ultrarelativistic particles of the third belt are thought to not scatter into the atmosphere, as they are too energetic to interact with atmospheric waves at low latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nphys2760_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nphys2760-22">[22]</a></sup> This absence of scattering and the trapping allows them to persist for a long time, finally only being destroyed by an unusual event, such as the shock wave from the Sun.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flux_values">Flux values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flux values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the belts, at a given point, the flux of particles of a given energy decreases sharply with energy.\n</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dip" title="Magnetic dip">magnetic equator</a>, electrons of energies exceeding 500&#160;keV (resp. 5&#160;MeV) have omnidirectional fluxes ranging from 1.2&#215;10<sup>6</sup> (resp. 3.7&#215;10<sup>4</sup>) up to 9.4&#215;10<sup>9</sup> (resp. 2&#215;10<sup>7</sup>) particles per square centimeter per second.\n</p><p>The proton belts contain protons with kinetic energies ranging from about 100&#160;keV (which can penetrate 0.6&#160;&#181;m of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>) to over 400&#160;MeV (which can penetrate 143&#160;mm of lead).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most published flux values for the inner and outer belts may not show the maximum probable flux densities that are possible in the belts. There is a reason for this discrepancy: the flux density and the location of the peak flux is variable (depending primarily on solar activity), and the number of spacecraft with instruments observing the belt in real time has been limited. The Earth has not experienced a solar storm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrington_event" title="Carrington event">Carrington event</a> intensity and duration while spacecraft with the proper instruments have been available to observe the event.\n</p><p>Regardless of the differences of the flux levels in the Inner and Outer Van Allen belts, the beta radiation levels would be dangerous to humans if they were exposed for an extended period of time. The Apollo missions minimised hazards for astronauts by sending spacecraft at high speeds through the thinner areas of the upper belts, bypassing inner belts completely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goddard_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goddard-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-popsci.com-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional" style="max-width: 343px;_width: 343px;">\n<li class="gallerycaption">Flux values, normal solar conditions</li>\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 335px"><div style="width: 335px">\n<div class="thumb" style="width: 330px;"><div style="margin:23px auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png"><img alt="" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="904" decoding="async" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png/300px-Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png/450px-Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png/600px-Ap8-omni-0.100MeV.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div></div>\n<div class="gallerytext">\n<p>AP8 MIN omnidirectional proton flux &#8805; 400 MeV\n</p>\n</div>\n</div></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antimatter_confinement">Antimatter confinement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antimatter confinement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2011, a study confirmed earlier speculation that the Van Allen belt could confine antiparticles. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_for_Antimatter_Matter_Exploration_and_Light-nuclei_Astrophysics" title="Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics">PAMELA</a> experiment detected orders of magnitude higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiprotons</a> than are expected from normal <a href="/wiki/Particle_decay" title="Particle decay">particle decays</a> while passing through the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly" title="South Atlantic Anomaly">South Atlantic Anomaly</a>. This suggests the Van Allen belts confine a significant flux of antiprotons produced by the interaction of the Earth\'s upper atmosphere with cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The energy of the antiprotons has been measured in the range from 60&#8211;750&#160;MeV.\n</p><p>Research funded by the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Institute_for_Advanced_Concepts" title="NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts">NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts</a> concluded that harnessing these antiprotons for spacecraft propulsion would be feasible.  Researchers believed that this approach would have advantages over antiproton generation at CERN because collecting the particles in situ eliminates transportation losses and costs.  Jupiter and Saturn are also possible sources but the Earth belt is the most productive.  Jupiter is less productive than might be expected due to magnetic shielding from cosmic rays of much of its atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_space_travel">Implications for space travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span article.)</div></div></div>\n<p>Spacecraft travelling beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> enter the zone of radiation of the Van Allen belts. Beyond the belts, they face additional hazards from cosmic rays and <a href="/wiki/Solar_particle_event" title="Solar particle event">solar particle events</a>. A region between the inner and outer Van Allen belts lies at two to four Earth radii and is sometimes referred to as the "safe zone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">Solar cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a> can be damaged by radiation. Geomagnetic storms occasionally damage <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> components on spacecraft. Miniaturization and <a href="/wiki/Digital_electronics" title="Digital electronics">digitization</a> of electronics and <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">logic circuits</a> have made satellites more vulnerable to radiation, as the total <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> in these circuits is now small enough so as to be comparable with the charge of incoming ions. Electronics on satellites must be <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">hardened</a> against radiation to operate reliably. The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, among other satellites, often has its sensors turned off when passing through regions of intense radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> A satellite shielded by 3&#160;mm of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> in an elliptic orbit (200 by 20,000 miles (320 by 32,190&#160;km)) passing the radiation belts will receive about 2,500 <a href="/wiki/Roentgen_equivalent_man" title="Roentgen equivalent man">rem</a> (25&#160;<a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">Sv</a>) per year (for comparison, a full-body dose of 5 Sv is deadly). Almost all radiation will be received while passing the inner belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_missions" title="List of Apollo missions">Apollo missions</a> marked the first event where humans traveled through the Van Allen belts, which was one of several radiation hazards known by mission planners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vernon_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vernon-34">[32]</a></sup> The astronauts had low exposure in the Van Allen belts due to the short period of time spent flying through them. Apollo flight trajectories bypassed the inner belts completely, passing through the thinner areas of the outer belts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popsci.com_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-popsci.com-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-35">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Astronauts\' overall exposure was actually dominated by solar particles once outside Earth\'s magnetic field. The total radiation received by the astronauts varied from mission to mission but was measured to be between 0.16 and 1.14 <a href="/wiki/Rad_(unit)" title="Rad (unit)">rads</a> (1.6&#160;and&#160;11.4&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gray_(unit)" title="Gray (unit)">mGy</a>), much less than the standard of 5 rem (50&#160;mSv) per year set by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">United States Atomic Energy Commission</a> for people who work with radioactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vernon_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vernon-34">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is generally understood that the inner and outer Van Allen belts result from different processes. The inner belt, consisting mainly of energetic protons, is the product of the decay of so-called "<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>" neutrons which are themselves the result of cosmic ray collisions in the upper atmosphere. The outer belt consists mainly of electrons. They are injected from the geomagnetic tail following geomagnetic storms, and are subsequently energized through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_stream_instability#Wave-particle_interactions" title="Two stream instability">wave-particle interactions</a>.\n</p><p>In the inner belt, particles that originate from the Sun are trapped in the Earth\'s magnetic field. Particles spiral along the magnetic lines of flux as they move "longitudinally" along those lines. As particles move toward the poles, the magnetic field line density increases and their "longitudinal" velocity is slowed and can be reversed, reflecting the particle and causing them to bounce back and forth between the Earth\'s poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> In addition to the spiral about and motion along the flux lines, the electrons move slowly in an eastward direction, while the ions move westward.\n</p><p>A gap between the inner and outer Van Allen belts, sometimes called safe zone or safe slot, is caused by the <a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">Very Low Frequency</a> (VLF) waves which scatter particles in <a href="/wiki/Pitch_angle_(particle_motion)" title="Pitch angle (particle motion)">pitch angle</a> which results in the gain of particles to the atmosphere. Solar outbursts can pump particles into the gap but they drain again in a matter of days. The radio waves were originally thought to be generated by turbulence in the radiation belts, but recent work by <a href="/wiki/James_L._Green" title="James L. Green">James L. Green</a> of the Goddard Space Flight Center comparing maps of lightning activity collected by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Microlab_1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Microlab 1 (page does not exist)">Microlab 1</a> spacecraft with data on radio waves in the radiation-belt gap from the <a href="/wiki/IMAGE" title="IMAGE">IMAGE</a> spacecraft suggests that they are actually generated by lightning within Earth\'s atmosphere. The radio waves that generate strike the ionosphere at the correct angle to pass through only at high latitudes, where the lower ends of the gap approach the upper atmosphere. These results are still under scientific debate.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_removal">Proposed removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Proposed removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>High Voltage Orbiting Long Tether</b>, or HiVOLT, is a concept proposed by Russian physicist <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Danilov" title="Valentin Danilov">V. V. Danilov</a> and further refined by <a href="/wiki/Robert_P._Hoyt" title="Robert P. Hoyt">Robert P. Hoyt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Forward" title="Robert L. Forward">Robert L. Forward</a> for draining and removing the radiation fields of the Van Allen radiation belts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> that surround the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirnov1996_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-mirnov1996-38">[36]</a></sup> A proposed configuration consists of a system of five 100&#160;km long conducting <a href="/wiki/Electrodynamic_tether" title="Electrodynamic tether">tethers</a> deployed from satellites, and charged to a large voltage. This would cause charged particles that encounter the tethers to have their pitch angle changed; thus, over time, dissolving the inner belts. Hoyt and Forward\'s company, Tethers Unlimited, performed a preliminary analysis simulation in 2011, and produced a chart depicting a theoretical radiation flux reduction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup> to less than 1% of current levels within two months for the inner belts that threaten <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">LEO</a> objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
