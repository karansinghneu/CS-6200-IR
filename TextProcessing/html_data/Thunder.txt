title="Lightning">lightning</a>. Depending on the distance from and nature of the lightning, it can range from a sharp, loud crack to a long, low rumble (brontide). The sudden increase in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> from lightning produces rapid <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">expansion of the air</a> surrounding and within a bolt of lightning. In turn, this expansion of air creates a sonic shock wave, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a>, often referred to as a "thunderclap" or "peal of thunder".\n</p>\n<table class="mbox-small noprint" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><img alt="" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="160" decoding="async" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/50px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/75px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/100px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></div></div></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist" style="line-height:1.1em"><div class="haudio">\n<div style="padding:4px 0"><a href="/wiki/File:Thunder.ogg" title="File:Thunder.ogg">Thunder</a></div>\n<div><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="18.416326530612" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Thunder.ogg" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_0" preload="none" style="width:220px"><source data-bandwidth="62333" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-title="Original Ogg file (62 kbps)" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/Thunder.ogg" type=\'audio/ogg; The first recorded theory is attributed to the Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> in the fourth century BC, and an early speculation was that it was caused by the collision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>. Subsequently, numerous other theories were proposed. By the mid-19th century, the accepted theory was that lightning produced a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>.\n</p><p>In the 20th century a consensus evolved that thunder must begin with a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> in the air due to the sudden thermal expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> in the lightning channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007-1">[1]</a></sup>  The temperature inside the lightning channel, measured by <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectral analysis</a>, varies during its 50 <a href="/wiki/Microsecond" title="Microsecond">&#956;s</a> existence, rising sharply from an initial temperature of about 20,000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> to about 30,000&#160;K, then dropping away gradually to about 10,000&#160;K. The average is about 20,400&#160;K (20,100&#160;&#176;C; 36,300&#160;&#176;F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This heating causes a rapid outward expansion, impacting the surrounding cooler air at a speed faster than sound would otherwise travel. The resultant outward-moving pulse is a shock wave,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">[3]</a></sup>\nsimilar in principle to the shock wave formed by an <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a>, or at the front of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>.\n</p><p>Experimental studies of simulated lightning have produced results largely consistent with this model, though there is continued debate about the precise physical mechanisms of the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-UmanVladimir_A._Rakov2007-1">[1]</a></sup> Other causes have also been proposed, relying on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamic" title="Electrodynamic">electrodynamic</a> effects of the massive current acting on the plasma in the bolt of lightning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The shockwave in thunder is sufficient to cause injury, such as internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contusion" title="Contusion">contusion</a>, to individuals nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Inversion thunder results when lightning strikes between cloud and ground occur during a temperature inversion. In such an inversion, the air near the ground is cooler than the higher air. The sound energy is prevented from dispersing vertically as it would in a non-inversion and is thus concentrated in the near-ground layer. Inversions often occur when warm moist air passes above a cold front; the resulting thunder sound is significantly louder than it would be if heard at the same distance in a non-inversion condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i>d</i> in Modern English <i>thunder</i> (from earlier <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>&#254;unor</i>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epenthetic" title="Epenthetic">epenthetic</a>, and is now found as well in Modern Dutch <i>donder</i> (cp <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> <i>donre</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorr" title="&#222;orr">&#254;orr</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">Old Frisian</a> <i>&#254;uner</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>donar</i> descended from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>&#254;unraz</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> the term was <i>tonare</i> "to thunder". The name of the Nordic god <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a> comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> word for thunder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The shared <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_root" title="Proto-Indo-European root">root</a> is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:List_of_Proto-Indo-European_roots#t" title="wikt:Appendix:List of Proto-Indo-European roots">*<i>t&#243;n-r&#805;</i></a> or <i>*<span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space:nowrap;" title="Proto-Indo-European language">tar-</span></i>, also found Gaulish <i><a href="/wiki/Taranis" title="Taranis">Taranis</a></i> and Hittite <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarhunt" title="Tarhunt">Tarhunt</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_calculation">Distance calculation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thunder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distance calculation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A flash of lightning, followed after some time by a rumble of thunder, illustrates the fact that <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> travels significantly slower than <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. Using this difference, one can estimate how far away the bolt of lightning is by timing the interval between seeing the flash and hearing thunder. The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> in dry air is approximately 343&#160;m/s or 1,127&#160;ft/s or 768&#160;mph (1,236&#160;km/h) at 20&#160;&#176;C (68&#160;&#176;F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  This translates to approximately 3 seconds per kilometer (or 5 seconds per mile); saying "One-Mississippi... Two-Mississippi..." (or "One One-thousand... Two One-Thousand...)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-One_One-Thousand_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-One_One-Thousand-10">[10]</a></sup> is a useful method of counting the seconds from the perception of a given lightning flash to the perception of its thunder (which can be used to gauge the proximity of lightning for the sake of safety).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-One-Mississippi_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-One-Mississippi-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> is high enough that it can be taken as <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinite</a> in this calculation because of the relatively small distance involved. Therefore, the lightning is approximately one kilometer distant for every three seconds that elapse between the visible flash and the first sound of thunder (or one mile for every five seconds). In the same five seconds, the light could have traveled the Lunar distance four times. (In this calculation, the initial shock wave, which travels at a rate faster than the speed of sound, but only extends outward for the first 30 feet (9&#160;m), is ignored.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Thunder is seldom heard at distances over 20 kilometers (12&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> A very bright flash of lightning and an almost simultaneous sharp "crack" of thunder, a <i>thundercrack</i>, therefore indicates that the lightning strike was very near.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="0" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Very_close_thunder_cracks.flac" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_1" preload="none" style="width:220px"><source data-height="0" data-shorttitle="FLAC source" data-title="Original FLAC file (0 bps)" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/Very_close_thunder_cracks.flac" type="audio/flac"/><source data-bandwidth="0" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="Ogg Vorbis" data-title="Ogg Vorbis" data-transcodekey="ogg" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/f/f5/Very_close_thunder_cracks.flac/Very_close_thunder_cracks.flac.ogg" type=\'audio/ogg;
