Similarly, conventional, chemical, and biological weapons can also be delivered with varying effectiveness, but have never been deployed on ICBMs. Most modern designs support <a href="/wiki/Multiple_independently_targetable_reentry_vehicle" title="Multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle">multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles</a> (MIRVs), allowing a single missile to carry several warheads, each of which can strike a different target.\n</p><p>Early ICBMs had <a href="/wiki/Circular_error_probable" title="Circular error probable">limited precision</a>, which made them suitable for use only <a href="/wiki/Countervalue" title="Countervalue">against the largest targets</a>, such as cities. They were seen as a "safe" basing option, one that would keep the deterrent force close to home where it would be difficult to attack. <a href="/wiki/Counterforce" title="Counterforce">Attacks against military targets</a> (especially hardened ones) still demanded the use of a more precise, manned <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a>. Second- and third-generation designs (such as the <a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">LGM-118 Peacekeeper</a>) dramatically improved accuracy to the point where even the smallest point targets can be successfully attacked.\n</p><p>ICBMs are differentiated by having greater range and speed than other ballistic missiles: <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-range_ballistic_missile" title="Intermediate-range ballistic missile">intermediate-range ballistic missiles</a> (IRBMs), <a href="/wiki/Medium-range_ballistic_missile" title="Medium-range ballistic missile">medium-range ballistic missiles</a> (MRBMs), <a href="/wiki/Short-range_ballistic_missile" title="Short-range ballistic missile">short-range ballistic missiles</a> (SRBMs) and <a href="/wiki/Tactical_ballistic_missile" title="Tactical ballistic missile">tactical ballistic missiles</a> (TBMs). Short and medium-range ballistic missiles are known collectively as <a href="/wiki/Theatre_ballistic_missile" title="Theatre ballistic missile">theatre ballistic missiles</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:R-7_(7A)_misil.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1332" data-file-width="541" decoding="async" height="419" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg/170px-R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg/255px-R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg/340px-R-7_%287A%29_misil.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:R-7_(7A)_misil.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Primary views of an <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a>, the world\'s first ICBM and satellite launch vehicle</div></div></div>\n<p>The development of the world\'s first practical design for an ICBM, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_(rocket_family)#A9/A10" title="Aggregate (rocket family)">A9/10</a>, intended for use in bombing New York and other American cities, was undertaken in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> by the team of <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> under <i>Projekt Amerika</i>. The ICBM A9/A10 rocket initially was intended to be guided by radio, but was changed to be a piloted craft after the failure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Elster" title="Operation Elster">Operation Elster</a>. The second stage of the A9/A10 rocket was tested a few times in January and February 1945. The progenitor of the A9/A10 was the German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a>, also designed by von Braun and widely used at the end of World War II to bomb British and Belgian cities. All of these rockets used liquid propellants. Following the war, von Braun and other leading German scientists were relocated to the United States to work directly for the US Army through <a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">Operation Paperclip</a>, developing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRBM" title="IRBM">IRBMs</a>, ICBMs, and <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launchers</a>.\n</p><p>This technology was also predicted by US Army General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hap_Arnold" title="Hap Arnold">Hap Arnold</a>, who wrote in 1943:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p> Someday, not too distant, there can come streaking out of somewhere &#8211; we won\'t be able to hear it, it will come so fast &#8211; some kind of gadget with an explosive so powerful that one projectile will be able to wipe out completely this city of Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the immediate post-war era, the US and USSR both started rocket research programs based on the German wartime designs, especially the V-2. In the US, each branch of the military started its own programs, leading to considerable duplication of effort. In the USSR, rocket research was centrally organized, although several teams worked on different designs. Early designs from both countries were short-range missiles, like the V-2, but improvements quickly followed.\n</p><p>In the USSR, early development was focused on missiles able to attack European targets. This changed in 1953 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolyov" title="Sergei Korolyov">Sergei Korolyov</a> was directed to start development of a true ICBM able to deliver newly developed hydrogen bombs. Given steady funding throughout, the <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7</a> developed with some speed. The first launch took place on 15 May 1957 and led to an unintended crash 400&#160;km (250&#160;mi) from the site. The first successful test followed on 21 August 1957; the R-7 flew over 6,000&#160;km (3,700&#160;mi) and became the world\'s first ICBM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The first strategic-missile unit became operational on 9 February 1959 at <a href="/wiki/Plesetsk_Cosmodrome" title="Plesetsk Cosmodrome">Plesetsk</a> in north-west Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was the same <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7</a> <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> that placed the first artificial satellite in space, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik</a>, on 4 October 1957. The first <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> in history was accomplished on a derivative of R-7, <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(rocket_family)" title="Vostok (rocket family)">Vostok</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Cosmonautics_Day" title="Cosmonautics Day">12 April 1961</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>. A heavily modernized version of the R-7 is still used as the <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> for the Soviet/Russian <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz spacecraft</a>, marking more than 60 years of operational history of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolyov" title="Sergei Korolyov">Sergei Korolyov</a>\'s original rocket design.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="482" decoding="async" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg/170px-Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg/255px-Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg/340px-Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atlas-B_ICBM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">SM-65 Atlas</a>, the first US ICBM, first launched in 1957</div></div></div>\n<p>The U.S. initiated ICBM research in 1946 with the <a href="/wiki/RTV-A-2_Hiroc" title="RTV-A-2 Hiroc">RTV-A-2 Hiroc</a> project. This was a three-stage effort with the ICBM development not starting until the third stage. However, funding was cut after only three partially successful launches in 1948 of the second stage design, used to test variations on the V-2 design. With overwhelming air superiority and truly intercontinental bombers, the newly forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a> did not take the problem of ICBM development seriously. Things changed in 1953 with the Soviet testing of their first <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear weapon</a>, but it was not until 1954 that the <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">Atlas missile</a> program was given the highest national priority. The Atlas A first flew on 11 June 1957; the flight lasted only about 24 seconds before the rocket blew up. The first successful flight of an Atlas missile to full range occurred 28 November 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The first armed version of the Atlas, the Atlas D, was declared operational in January 1959 at Vandenberg, although it had not yet flown. The first test flight was carried out on 9 July 1959,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the missile was accepted for service on 1 September.\n</p><p>The R-7 and Atlas each required a large launch facility, making them vulnerable to attack, and could not be kept in a ready state. Failure rates were very high throughout the early years of ICBM technology. Human spaceflight programs (<a href="/wiki/Vostok_programme" title="Vostok programme">Vostok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_programme" title="Voskhod programme">Voskhod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a>, etc.) served as a highly visible means of demonstrating confidence in reliability, with successes translating directly to national defense implications. The US was well behind the Soviet Union in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>, so U.S. President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> increased the stakes with the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, which used <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(rocket_family)" title="Saturn (rocket family)">Saturn rocket</a> technology that had been funded by President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_(highlighted).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1295" data-file-width="2571" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png/330px-USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png/495px-USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png/660px-USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_%28highlighted%29.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USAF_ICBM_and_NASA_Launch_Vehicle_Flight_Test_Successes_and_Failures_(highlighted).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1965 graph of USAF <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">Atlas</a> and Titan ICBM launches, cumulative by month with failures highlighted (pink), showing how NASA\'s use of ICBM boosters for Projects Mercury and Gemini (blue) served as a visible demonstration of reliability at a time when failure rates had been substantial.</div></div></div>\n<p>These early ICBMs also formed the basis of many space launch systems. Examples include <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">R-7</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket_family)" title="Atlas (rocket family)">Atlas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Redstone_(rocket_family)" title="Redstone (rocket family)">Redstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton</a>, which was derived from the earlier ICBMs but never deployed as an ICBM. The Eisenhower administration supported the development of solid-fueled missiles such as the <a href="/wiki/LGM-30_Minuteman" title="LGM-30 Minuteman">LGM-30 Minuteman</a>, <a href="/wiki/UGM-27_Polaris" title="UGM-27 Polaris">Polaris</a> and <a href="/wiki/GAM-87_Skybolt" title="GAM-87 Skybolt">Skybolt</a>. Modern ICBMs tend to be smaller than their ancestors, due to increased accuracy and smaller and lighter warheads, and use solid fuels, making them less useful as orbital launch vehicles.\n</p><p>The Western view of the deployment of these systems was governed by the strategic theory of <a href="/wiki/Mutual_assured_destruction" title="Mutual assured destruction">Mutual Assured Destruction</a>. In the 1950s and 1960s, development began on <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">Anti-Ballistic Missile</a> systems by both the U.S. and USSR; these systems were restricted by the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Anti-Ballistic_Missile_Treaty" title="Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty">ABM treaty</a>. The first successful ABM test were conducted by the USSR in 1961, that later deployed a fully operating system defending Moscow in the 1970s (see <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile#Moscow_ABM_system" title="Anti-ballistic missile">Moscow ABM system</a>).\n</p><p>The 1972 <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Arms_Limitation_Talks" title="Strategic Arms Limitation Talks">SALT</a> treaty froze the number of ICBM launchers of both the U.S. and the USSR at existing levels, and allowed new submarine-based <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">SLBM</a> launchers only if an equal number of land-based ICBM launchers were dismantled. Subsequent talks, called SALT II, were held from 1972 to 1979 and actually reduced the number of nuclear warheads held by the U.S. and USSR. SALT II was never ratified by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>, but its terms were nevertheless honored by both sides until 1986, when the Reagan administration "withdrew" after accusing the USSR of violating the pact.\n</p><p>In the 1980s, President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/LGM-118_Peacekeeper" title="LGM-118 Peacekeeper">MX</a> and <a href="/wiki/MGM-134_Midgetman" title="MGM-134 Midgetman">Midgetman</a> ICBM programs.\n</p><p>China developed a minimal independent nuclear deterrent entering its own cold war after an <a href="/wiki/Sino-Soviet_split" title="Sino-Soviet split">ideological split</a> with the Soviet Union beginning in the early 1960s. After first testing a domestic built nuclear weapon in 1964, it went on to develop various warheads and missiles. Beginning in the early 1970s, the liquid fuelled <a href="/wiki/DF-5" title="DF-5">DF-5</a> ICBM was developed and used as a satellite launch vehicle in 1975. The DF-5, with range of 10,000 to 12,000&#160;km (6,200 to 7,500&#160;mi) long enough to strike the western US and the USSR, was silo deployed with the first pair in service by 1981 with possibly twenty missiles in service by the late 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas.org_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-fas.org-9">[9]</a></sup> China also deployed the <a href="/wiki/JL-1" title="JL-1">JL-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Medium-range_ballistic_missile" title="Medium-range ballistic missile">Medium-range ballistic missile</a> with a reach of 1,700 kilometres (1,100&#160;mi) aboard the ultimately unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Type_092_submarine" title="Type 092 submarine">type 92 submarine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Cold_War">Post-Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Icbm-hist-en.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4115" data-file-width="3847" decoding="async" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Icbm-hist-en.png/290px-Icbm-hist-en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Icbm-hist-en.png/435px-Icbm-hist-en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Icbm-hist-en.png/580px-Icbm-hist-en.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Icbm-hist-en.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Deployment history of land-based ICBM, 1959-2014</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1991, the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> agreed in the <a href="/wiki/START_I" title="START I">START I</a> treaty to reduce their deployed ICBMs and attributed warheads.\n</p><p>As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, all five of the nations with permanent seats on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> have operational long-range ballistic missile systems; Russia, the United States, and China also have land-based ICBMs (the US missiles are silo-based, while China and Russia have both silo and road-mobile (<a href="/wiki/DF-31" title="DF-31">DF-31</a>, <a href="/wiki/RT-2PM2_Topol-M" title="RT-2PM2 Topol-M">RT-2PM2 Topol-M</a> missiles).\n</p><p>Israel is believed to have deployed a road mobile nuclear ICBM, the <a href="/wiki/Jericho_(missile)#Jericho_III" title="Jericho (missile)">Jericho III</a>, which entered service in 2008; an upgraded version is in development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS-RL30427_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-RL30427-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pfeffer1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pfeffer1-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> successfully test fired <a href="/wiki/Agni-V" title="Agni-V">Agni V</a>, with a strike range of more than 5,000&#160;km (3,100&#160;mi) on 19 April 2012, claiming entry into the ICBM club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehindu.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehindu.com-13">[13]</a></sup> The missile\'s actual range is speculated by foreign researchers to be up to 8,000&#160;km (5,000&#160;mi) with India having downplayed its capabilities to avoid causing concern to other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 2012 there was speculation by some <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_agency" title="Intelligence agency">intelligence agencies</a> that <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> is developing an ICBM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> North Korea successfully put a <a href="/wiki/Kwangmy%C5%8Fngs%C5%8Fng-3_Unit_2" title="Kwangmy&#335;ngs&#335;ng-3 Unit 2">satellite</a> into space on 12 December 2012 using the 32-metre-tall (105&#160;ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unha-3" title="Unha-3">Unha-3</a> rocket. The United States claimed that the launch was in fact a way to test an ICBM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (See <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_first_orbital_launches_by_country" title="Timeline of first orbital launches by country">Timeline of first orbital launches by country</a>.) In early July 2017, North Korea claimed for the first time to have tested successfully an ICBM capable of carrying a large thermonuclear warhead.\n</p><p>In July 2014, China announced the development of its newest generation of ICBM, the Dongfeng-41 (<a href="/wiki/DF-41" title="DF-41">DF-41</a>), which has a range of 12,000 kilometres (7,500 miles), capable of reaching the United States, and which analysts believe is capable of being outfitted with <a href="/wiki/Multiple_independently_targetable_reentry_vehicle" title="Multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle">MIRV</a> technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most countries in the early stages of developing ICBMs have used liquid propellants, with the known exceptions being the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Agni-V" title="Agni-V">Agni-V</a>, the planned but cancelled<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> South African RSA-4 ICBM, and the now in service Israeli <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jericho_missile#Jericho_III" title="Jericho missile">Jericho III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/RS-28_Sarmat" title="RS-28 Sarmat">RS-28 Sarmat</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS28_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-RS28-20">[20]</a></sup> (Russian: &#1056;&#1057;-28 &#1057;&#1072;&#1088;&#1084;&#1072;&#1090;; <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a>: SATAN 2), is a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-fueled</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_independently_targetable_reentry_vehicle" title="Multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle">MIRV</a>-equipped, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_ICBM" title="Heavy ICBM">super-heavy</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear</a> armed intercontinental ballistic missile in development by the <a href="/wiki/Makeyev_Rocket_Design_Bureau" title="Makeyev Rocket Design Bureau">Makeyev Rocket Design Bureau</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS28_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-RS28-20">[20]</a></sup> from 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> intended to replace the previous <a href="/wiki/R-36_(missile)" title="R-36 (missile)">R-36 missile</a>. Its large payload would allow for up to 10 heavy <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warheads</a> or 15 lighter ones or up to 24 hypersonic glide vehicles <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yu-74" title="Yu-74">Yu-74</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> or a combination of warheads and massive amounts of <a href="/wiki/Countermeasure" title="Countermeasure">countermeasures</a> designed to defeat <a href="/wiki/Missile_defense" title="Missile defense">anti-missile systems</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> it was heralded by the Russian military as a response to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Prompt_Global_Strike" title="Prompt Global Strike">Prompt Global Strike</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-invuln_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-invuln-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_phases">Flight phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flight phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following flight phases can be distinguished:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boost_phase" title="Boost phase">boost phase</a>: 3 to 5 minutes; it is shorter for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid-fuel rocket</a> than for a <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-propellant rocket</a>; depending on the trajectory chosen, typical burnout speed is 4&#160;km/s (2.5&#160;mi/s), up to 7.8&#160;km/s (4.8&#160;mi/s); altitude at the end of this phase is typically 150 to 400&#160;km (93 to 249&#160;mi).</li>\n<li>midcourse phase: approx. 25 minutes&#8212;<a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a> with a flightpath being a part of an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a> with a vertical major axis; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">apogee</a> (halfway through the midcourse phase) is at an altitude of approximately 1,200&#160;km (750&#160;mi); the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> is between 3,186 and 6,372&#160;km (1,980 and 3,959&#160;mi); the projection of the flightpath on the Earth\'s surface is close to a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>, slightly displaced due to earth rotation during the time of flight; the missile may release several independent warheads and <a href="/wiki/Penetration_aid" title="Penetration aid">penetration aids</a>, such as metallic-coated balloons, aluminum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaff_(radar_countermeasure)" title="Chaff (radar countermeasure)">chaff</a>, and full-scale warhead <a href="/wiki/Decoy" title="Decoy">decoys</a>.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">reentry</a>/terminal phase (starting at an altitude of 100&#160;km, 62&#160;mi): 2 minutes &#8211; impact is at a speed of up to 7&#160;km/s (4.3&#160;mi/s) (for early ICBMs less than 1&#160;km/s (0.62&#160;mi/s)); see also <a href="/wiki/Maneuverable_reentry_vehicle" title="Maneuverable reentry vehicle">maneuverable reentry vehicle</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>ICBMs usually use the trajectory which optimizes range for a given amount of payload (the <i>minimum-energy trajectory</i>); an alternative is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depressed_trajectory" title="Depressed trajectory">depressed trajectory</a>, which allows less payload, shorter flight time, and has a much lower apogee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_ICBMs">Modern ICBMs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intercontinental_ballistic_missile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern ICBMs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TridentMissileSystem.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/TridentMissileSystem.png/220px-TridentMissileSystem.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/TridentMissileSystem.png/330px-TridentMissileSystem.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/TridentMissileSystem.png/440px-TridentMissileSystem.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TridentMissileSystem.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic view of a submarine-launched <a href="/wiki/UGM-133_Trident_II" title="UGM-133 Trident II">Trident II</a> D5 nuclear missile system, capable of carrying multiple nuclear warheads up to 8,000&#160;km (5,000&#160;mi)</div></div></div>\n<p>Modern ICBMs typically carry <a href="/wiki/Multiple_independently_targetable_reentry_vehicle" title="Multiple independently targetable reentry vehicle">multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles</a> (<i>MIRVs</i>), each of which carries a separate <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a> <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warhead</a>, allowing a single missile to hit multiple targets. MIRV was an outgrowth of the rapidly shrinking size and weight of modern warheads and the Strategic Arms Limitation Treaties which imposed limitations on the number of launch vehicles (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SALT_I" title="SALT I">SALT I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SALT_II" title="SALT II">SALT II</a>). It has also proved to be an "easy answer" to proposed deployments of <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">Anti-ballistic missile (ABM)</a> systems&#8212;it is far less expensive to add more warheads to an existing missile system than to build an ABM system capable of shooting down the additional warheads; hence, most ABM system proposals have been judged to be impractical. The first operational ABM systems were deployed in the U.S. during the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Safeguard_Program" title="Safeguard Program">Safeguard ABM</a> facility was located in North Dakota and was operational from 1975 to 1976. The USSR deployed its <a href="/wiki/ABM-1_Galosh" title="ABM-1 Galosh">ABM-1 Galosh</a> system around Moscow in the 1970s, which remains in service. Israel deployed a national ABM system based on the <a href="/wiki/Arrow_(Israeli_missile)" title="Arrow (Israeli missile)">Arrow missile</a> in 1998,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> but it is mainly designed to intercept shorter-ranged theater ballistic missiles, not ICBMs. The Alaska-based <a href="/wiki/United_States_national_missile_defense" title="United States national missile defense">United States national missile defense</a> system attained initial operational capability in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ICBMs can be deployed from <a href="/wiki/Transporter_erector_launcher" title="Transporter erector launcher">transporter erector launchers</a> (TEL), such as the Russian <a href="/wiki/RT-2PM2_Topol-M" title="RT-2PM2 Topol-M">RT-2PM2 Topol-M</a></div></div></div>\n<p>ICBMs can be deployed from multiple platforms:\n</p>\n<ul><li>in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">missile silos</a>, which offer some protection from military attack (including, the designers hope, some protection from a nuclear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_strike" title="First strike">first strike</a>)</li>\n<li>on <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>: <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missiles</a> (SLBMs); most or all SLBMs have the long range of ICBMs (as opposed to IRBMs)</li>\n<li>on heavy trucks; this applies to one version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RT-2UTTKh_Topol-M" title="RT-2UTTKh Topol-M">Topol</a> which may be deployed from a self-propelled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_launcher_vehicle" title="Mobile launcher vehicle">mobile launcher</a>, capable of moving through roadless terrain, and launching a missile from any point along its route</li>\n<li>mobile launchers on rails; this applies, for example, to &#1056;&#1058;-23&#1059;&#1058;&#1058;&#1061; "&#1052;&#1086;&#1083;&#1086;&#1076;&#1077;&#1094;" (<a href="/wiki/RT-23_Molodets" title="RT-23 Molodets">RT-23UTTH "Molodets"</a>&#8212;SS-24 "Scalpel")</li></ul>\n<p>The last three kinds are mobile and therefore hard to find.\nDuring storage, one of the most important features of the missile is its serviceability. One of the key features of the first <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">computer-controlled</a> ICBM, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minuteman_missile" title="Minuteman missile">Minuteman missile</a>, was that it could quickly and easily use its computer to test itself.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS-24-DIA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="889" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SS-24-DIA.jpg/220px-SS-24-DIA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SS-24-DIA.jpg/330px-SS-24-DIA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/SS-24-DIA.jpg/440px-SS-24-DIA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS-24-DIA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist\'s concept of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-24" title="SS-24">SS-24</a> deployed on railway</div></div></div>\n<p>After launch, a <a href="/wiki/Booster_(rocketry)" title="Booster (rocketry)">booster</a> pushes the missile and then falls away. Most modern boosters are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_rocket" title="Solid rocket">solid-fueled rocket motors</a>, which can be stored easily for long periods of time. Early missiles used <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-fueled rocket motors</a>. Many liquid-fueled ICBMs could not be kept fueled all the time as the <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic fuel</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> boiled off and caused ice formation, and therefore fueling the rocket was necessary before launch. This procedure was a source of significant operational delay, and might allow the missiles to be destroyed by enemy counterparts before they could be used. To resolve this problem the United Kingdom invented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">missile silo</a> that protected the missile from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_strike" title="First strike">first strike</a> and also hid fuelling operations underground.\n</p><p>Once the booster falls away, the remaining "bus" releases several warheads, each of which continues on its own unpowered ballistic <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a>, much like an artillery shell or cannonball. The warhead is encased in a cone-shaped reentry vehicle and is difficult to detect in this phase of flight as there is no rocket exhaust or other emissions to mark its position to defenders. The high speeds of the warheads make them difficult to intercept and allow for little warning, striking targets many thousands of kilometers away from the launch site (and due to the possible locations of the submarines: anywhere in the world) within approximately 30 minutes.\n</p><p>Many authorities say that missiles also release aluminized balloons, electronic noise-makers, and other <a href="/wiki/Penetration_aid" title="Penetration aid">items</a> intended to confuse interception devices and radars.\n</p><p>As the nuclear warhead reenters the Earth\'s atmosphere its high speed causes compression of the air, leading to a dramatic rise in temperature which would destroy it if it were not shielded in some way. As a result, warhead components are contained within an aluminium <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb_structure" title="Honeycomb structure">honeycomb substructure</a>, sheathed in a <a href="/wiki/Pyrolytic_carbon" title="Pyrolytic carbon">pyrolytic carbon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a> <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_resin" title="Synthetic resin">synthetic resin</a> <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> heat shield. Warheads are also often radiation-hardened (to protect against nuclear-tipped ABMs or the nearby detonation of friendly warheads), one neutron-resistant material developed for this purpose in the UK is <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_quartz_phenolic" title="Three-dimensional quartz phenolic">three-dimensional quartz phenolic</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Circular_error_probable" title="Circular error probable">Circular error probable</a> is crucial, because halving the circular error probable decreases the needed warhead energy by a <a href="/wiki/Inverse-square_law" title="Inverse-square law">factor of four</a>. Accuracy is limited by the accuracy of the navigation system and the available <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodetic</a> information.\n</p><p>Strategic missile systems are thought to use custom <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> designed to calculate <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigational</a> <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> thousands to millions of <a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a> in order to reduce navigational errors caused by calculation alone. These circuits are usually a network of binary addition circuits that continually recalculate the missile\'s position. The inputs to the navigation circuit are set by a general purpose computer according to a navigational input schedule loaded into the missile before launch.\n</p><p>One particular weapon developed by the Soviet Union&#8212;the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_Orbital_Bombardment_System" title="Fractional Orbital Bombardment System">Fractional Orbital Bombardment System</a>&#8212;had a partial <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital</a> trajectory, and unlike most ICBMs its target could not be deduced from its orbital flight path. It was decommissioned in compliance with arms control agreements, which address the maximum range of ICBMs and prohibit orbital or fractional-orbital weapons. However, according to reports, Russia is working on the new Sarmat ICBM which leverages Fractional Orbital Bombardment concepts to use a Southern polar approach instead of flying over the Northern polar regions. Using this approach, it is theorized, avoids the US missile defense batteries in California and Alaska.\n</p><p>New development of ICBM technology are ICBMs able to carry hypersonic glide vehicles as a <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">payload</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarmat" title="Sarmat">RS-28 Sarmat</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile" title="Anti-ballistic missile">Anti-ballistic missile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missile_defense" title="Missile defense">Missile defense</a></div>\n<p>An anti-ballistic missile is a missile which can be deployed to counter an incoming nuclear or non-nuclear ICBM. ICBMs can be intercepted in three regions of their trajectory: boost phase, mid-course phase or terminal phase. Currently <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">US</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> have developed anti-ballistic missile systems, of which the Russian <a href="/wiki/A-135_anti-ballistic_missile_system" title="A-135 anti-ballistic missile system">A-135 anti-ballistic missile system</a>, US <a href="/wiki/Ground-Based_Midcourse_Defense" title="Ground-Based Midcourse Defense">Ground-Based Midcourse Defense</a>, Systems have the capability to intercept ICBMs carrying <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological</a>, or conventional <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warheads</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
