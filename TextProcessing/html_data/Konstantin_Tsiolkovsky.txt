class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tsiolkovsky was born in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Izhevskoye&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Izhevskoye (page does not exist)">Izhevskoye</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Spassky_District,_Ryazan_Oblast" title="Spassky District, Ryazan Oblast">Spassky District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryazan_Oblast" title="Ryazan Oblast">Ryazan Oblast</a>), in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>, to a middle-class family. His father, Edward Tsiolkovsky was a <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a> forester of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox</a> faith who was exiled to Russia;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> his mother was of mixed <a href="/wiki/Volga_Tatars" title="Volga Tatars">Volga Tatar</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russian</a> origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His father was successively a forester, teacher, and minor government official.  At the age of 10, Konstantin caught <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_fever" title="Scarlet fever">scarlet fever</a> and became hard of hearing. When he was 13, his mother died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NotableScientists_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NotableScientists-8">[8]</a></sup> He was not admitted to elementary schools because of his hearing problem, so he was self-taught.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NotableScientists_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NotableScientists-8">[8]</a></sup> As a reclusive home-schooled child, he passed much of his time by reading books and became interested in mathematics and physics. As a teenager, he began to contemplate the possibility of space travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-brit-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky spent three years attending a Moscow library where <a href="/wiki/Russian_cosmism" title="Russian cosmism">Russian cosmism</a> proponent <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Fyodorovich_Fyodorov" title="Nikolai Fyodorovich Fyodorov">Nikolai Fyodorov</a> worked. He later came to believe that <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">colonizing space</a> would lead to the perfection of the human race, with immortality and a carefree existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Additionally, inspired by the fiction of <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>, Tsiolkovsky theorized many aspects of space travel and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">rocket propulsion</a>. He is considered the father of <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> and the first person to conceive the <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">space elevator</a>, becoming inspired in 1895 by the newly constructed <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a> in Paris.\n</p><p>Despite the youth\'s growing knowledge of physics, his father was concerned that he would not be able to provide for himself financially as an adult and brought him back home at the age of 19 after learning that he was overworking himself and going hungry. Afterwards, Tsiolkovsky passed the teacher\'s exam and went to work at a school in <a href="/wiki/Borovsk" title="Borovsk">Borovsk</a> near Moscow. He also met and married his wife Varvara Sokolova during this time. Despite being stuck in <a href="/wiki/Kaluga" title="Kaluga">Kaluga</a>, a small town far from major learning centers, Tsiolkovsky managed to make scientific discoveries on his own.\n</p><p>The first two decades of the 20th century were marred by personal tragedy. Tsiolkovsky\'s son Ignaty committed suicide in 1902, and in 1908 many of his accumulated papers were lost in a flood. In 1911, his daughter Lyubov was arrested for engaging in revolutionary activities.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_achievements">Scientific achievements</span><span only as a supplement to philosophical research on the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He wrote more than 400 works including approximately 90 published pieces on space travel and related subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Among his works are designs for rockets with steering thrusters, multistage boosters, <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space stations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airlock" title="Airlock">airlocks</a> for exiting a spaceship into the vacuum of space, and closed-cycle biological systems to provide food and oxygen for <a href="/wiki/Space_habitat" title="Space habitat">space colonies</a>.\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky\'s first scientific study dates back to 1880&#8211;1881. He wrote a paper called "Theory of Gases," in which he outlined the basis of the kinetic theory of gases, but after submitting it to the Russian Physico-Chemical Society (RPCS), he was informed that his discoveries had already been made 25 years earlier. Undaunted, he pressed ahead with his second work, "The Mechanics of the Animal Organism". It received favorable feedback, and Tsiolkovsky was made a member of the Society. Tsiolkovsky\'s main works after 1884 dealt with four major areas: the scientific rationale for the all-metal balloon (airship), streamlined airplanes and trains, hovercraft, and rockets for interplanetary travel.\n</p><p>In 1892, he was transferred to a new teaching post in Kaluga where he continued to experiment. During this period, Tsiolkovsky began working on a problem that would occupy much of his time during the coming years: an attempt to build an <a href="/wiki/Metal-clad_airship" title="Metal-clad airship">all-metal dirigible</a> that could be expanded or shrunk in size.\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky developed the first aerodynamics laboratory in Russia in his apartment. In 1897, he built the first Russian wind tunnel with an open test section and developed a method of experimentation using it. In 1900, with a grant from the Academy of Sciences, he made a survey using models of the simplest shapes and determined the drag coefficients of the sphere, flat plates, cylinders, cones, and other bodies. Tsiolkovsky\'s work in the field of aerodynamics was a source of ideas for Russian scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolay_Yegorovich_Zhukovsky" title="Nikolay Yegorovich Zhukovsky">Nikolay Zhukovsky</a>, the father of modern aerodynamics and hydrodynamics. Tsiolkovsky described the airflow around bodies of different geometric shapes, but because the RPCS did not provide any financial support for this project, he was forced to pay for it largely out of his own pocket.\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky studied the mechanics of powered flying machines, which were designated "dirigibles" (the word "airship" had not yet been invented). Tsiolkovsky first proposed the idea of an <a href="/wiki/Metal-clad_airship" title="Metal-clad airship">all-metal dirigible</a> and built a model of it. The first printed work on the airship was "A Controllable Metallic Balloon" (1892), in which he gave the scientific and technical rationale for the design of an airship with a metal sheath. Progressive for his time, Tsiolkovsky was not supported on the airship project, and the author was refused a grant to build the model. An appeal to the General Aviation Staff of the Russian army also had no success. In 1892, he turned to the new and unexplored field of heavier-than-air aircraft. Tsiolkovsky\'s idea was to build an airplane with a metal frame. In the article "An Airplane or a Birdlike (Aircraft) Flying Machine" (1894) are descriptions and drawings of a monoplane, which in its appearance and aerodynamics anticipated the design of aircraft that would be constructed 15 to 18 years later. In an Aviation Airplane, the wings have a thick profile with a rounded front edge and the fuselage is <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fairing" title="Aircraft fairing">faired</a>. But work on the airplane, as well as on the airship, did not receive recognition from the official representatives of Russian science, and Tsiolkovsky\'s further research had neither monetary nor moral support. In 1914, he displayed his models of all-metal dirigibles at the Aeronautics Congress in St. Petersburg but met with a lukewarm response.\n</p><p>Disappointed at this, Tsiolkovsky gave up on space and aeronautical problems with the onset of World War I and instead turned his attention to the problem of alleviating poverty. This occupied his time during the war years until the Russian Revolution in 1917.\n</p><p>Starting in 1896, Tsiolkovsky systematically studied the theory of motion of rocket apparatus. Thoughts on the use of the rocket principle in the cosmos were expressed by him as early as 1883, and a rigorous theory of rocket propulsion was developed in 1896. In the same year, the formula for the motion of a body of variable mass was published in the thesis of the Russian mathematician <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ivan_Meshchersky" title="Ivan Meshchersky">I. V. Meshchersky</a> ("Dynamics of a Point of Variable Mass," I. V. Meshchersky, St. Petersburg, 1897).\n</p><p>His most important work, published in May 1903, was <i>Exploration of Outer Space by Means of Rocket Devices</i> (Russian: <span lang="ru">&#1048;&#1089;&#1089;&#1083;&#1077;&#1076;&#1086;&#1074;&#1072;&#1085;&#1080;&#1077; &#1084;&#1080;&#1088;&#1086;&#1074;&#1099;&#1093; &#1087;&#1088;&#1086;&#1089;&#1090;&#1088;&#1072;&#1085;&#1089;&#1090;&#1074; &#1088;&#1077;&#1072;&#1082;&#1090;&#1080;&#1074;&#1085;&#1099;&#1084;&#1080; &#1087;&#1088;&#1080;&#1073;&#1086;&#1088;&#1072;&#1084;&#1080;</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Tsiolkovsky calculated, using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_equation" title="Tsiolkovsky equation">Tsiolkovsky equation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup> that the horizontal speed required for a minimal <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around the Earth is 8,000&#160;m/s (5 miles per second) and that this could be achieved by means of a <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">multistage rocket</a> fueled by <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>. In the article "Exploration of Outer Space by Means of Rocket Devices", it was proved for the first time that a rocket could perform space flight. In this article and its subsequent sequels (1911 and 1914), he developed some ideas of missiles and considered the use of liquid rocket engines.\n</p><p>The outward appearance of Tsiolkovsky\'s spacecraft design, published in 1903, was a basis for modern spaceship design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The design had a hull divided into three main sections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The pilot and copilot were in the first section, the second and third sections held the liquid oxygen and liquid hydrogen needed to fuel the spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the result of the first publication was not what Tsiolkovsky expected. No foreign scientists appreciated his research, which today is a major scientific discipline. In 1911 he published the second part of the work "Exploration of Outer Space by Means of Rocket Devices". Here Tsiolkovsky evaluated the work needed to overcome the force of gravity, determined the speed needed to propel the device into the solar system ("escape velocity"), and examined calculation of flight time. The publication of this article made a splash in the scientific world, Tsiolkovsky found many friends among his fellow scientists.\n</p><p>In 1926&#8211;1929, Tsiolkovsky solved the practical problem regarding the role played by rocket fuel in getting to escape velocity and leaving the Earth. He showed that the final speed of the rocket depends on the rate of gas flowing from it and on how the weight of the fuel relates to the weight of the empty rocket.\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky conceived a number of ideas that have been later used in rockets. They include: gas rudders (graphite) for controlling a rocket\'s flight and changing the trajectory of its center of mass, the use of components of the fuel to cool the outer shell of the spacecraft (during re-entry to Earth) and the walls of the combustion chamber and nozzle, a pump system for feeding the fuel components, the optimal descent trajectory of the spacecraft while returning from space, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the field of rocket propellants, Tsiolkovsky studied a large number of different oxidizers and combustible fuels and recommended specific pairings: liquid oxygen and hydrogen, and oxygen with hydrocarbons. Tsiolkovsky did much fruitful work on the creation of the theory of jet aircraft, and invented his chart Gas Turbine Engine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  In 1927 he published the theory and design of a train on an air cushion. He first proposed a "bottom of the retractable body" chassis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, space flight and the airship were the main problems to which he devoted his life. Tsiolkovsky had been developing the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Hovercraft" title="Hovercraft">hovercraft</a> since 1921, publishing a fundamental paper on it in 1927, entitled "Air Resistance and the Express Train" (Russian: <span lang="ru">&#1057;&#1086;&#1087;&#1088;&#1086;&#1090;&#1080;&#1074;&#1083;&#1077;&#1085;&#1080;&#1077; &#1074;&#1086;&#1079;&#1076;&#1091;&#1093;&#1072; &#1080; &#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1088;&#1099;&#1081; &#1087;&#1086;&#769;&#1077;&#1079;&#1076;</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1929, Tsiolkovsky proposed the construction of multistage rockets in his book <i>Space Rocket Trains</i> (Russian: <span lang="ru">&#1050;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1080;&#1095;&#1077;&#1089;&#1082;&#1080;&#1077; &#1088;&#1072;&#1082;&#1077;&#1090;&#1085;&#1099;&#1077; &#1087;&#1086;&#1077;&#1079;&#1076;&#1072;</span>).\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky championed the idea of the diversity of life in the universe and was the first theorist and advocate of <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>.\n</p><p>Tsiolkovsky never built a rocket; he apparently did not expect many of his theories to ever be implemented.\n</p><p>Hearing problems did not prevent the scientist from having a good understanding of music, as outlined in his work "The Origin of Music and Its Essence."\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1135" data-file-width="851" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/170px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/255px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg/340px-Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky_1934.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tsiolkovsky in 1934</div></div></div>\n<p>Although Tsiolkovsky supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik_Revolution" title="Bolshevik Revolution">Bolshevik Revolution</a>, he did not particularly flourish under a communist system. Eager to promote science and technology, the new Soviet government elected him a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Academy" title="Socialist Academy">Socialist Academy</a> in 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1&#8211;2,8</span></sup> He worked as a high school mathematics teacher until retiring in 1920 at the age of 63. In 1921 he received a lifetime pension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1&#8211;2,8</span></sup>\n</p><p>Only late in his lifetime was Tsiolkovsky honored for his pioneering work. In particular, his support of <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a> made him politically unpopular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, from the mid 1920s onwards the importance of his other work was acknowledged, and he was honoured for it and the Soviet state provided financial backing for his research. He was initially popularized in Soviet Russia in 1931&#8211;1932 mainly by two writers:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2010_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2010-20">[20]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iakov_Perel%27man" title="Iakov Perel\'man">Iakov Perel\'man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Rynin" title="Nikolai Rynin">Nikolai Rynin</a>. Tsiolkovsky died in Kaluga on 19 September 1935 after undergoing an operation for <a href="/wiki/Stomach_cancer" title="Stomach cancer">stomach cancer</a>. He bequeathed his life\'s work to the Soviet state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although many called his ideas impractical,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8,117</span></sup> Tsiolkovsky influenced later rocket scientists throughout Europe, like <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>. Russian search teams at <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenem&#252;nde">Peenem&#252;nde</a> found a German translation of a book by Tsiolkovsky of which "almost every page...was embellished by von Braun\'s comments and notes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>27</span></sup> Leading Soviet rocket-engine designer <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Glushko" title="Valentin Glushko">Valentin Glushko</a> and rocket designer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Korolev" title="Sergey Korolev">Sergey Korolev</a> studied Tsiolkovsky\'s works as youths,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6&#8211;7,333</span></sup> and both sought to turn Tsiolkovsky\'s theories into reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3,166,182,187,205&#8211;206,208</span></sup> In particular, Korolev saw traveling to Mars as the more important priority,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>208,333,337</span></sup> until in 1964 he decided to compete with the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Project Apollo</a> for the moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siddiqi2000_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-siddiqi2000-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>404</span></sup>\n</p><p>In 1989, Tsiolkovsky was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Air_%26_Space_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Air &amp; Space Hall of Fame">International Air &amp; Space Hall of Fame</a> at the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Air_%26_Space_Museum" title="San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum">San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_work">Philosophical work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Philosophical work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="755" data-file-width="568" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg/170px-Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg/255px-Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg/340px-Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volition-of-the-Cosmos.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The cover of the book <i>The Will of the Universe. The Unknown Intelligence</i> by Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, 1928, considered to be a work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmism_(Russian)" title="Cosmism (Russian)">Cosmist</a> philosophy.</div></div></div>\n<p>Tsiolkovsky wrote a book called <i>The Will of the Universe. The Unknown Intelligence</i> in 1928 in which he propounded a philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Panpsychism" title="Panpsychism">panpsychism</a>.  He believed humans would eventually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonization_of_space" title="Colonization of space">colonize the Milky Way galaxy</a>. His thought preceded the <a href="/wiki/Space_Age" title="Space Age">Space Age</a> by several decades, and some of what he foresaw in his imagination has come into being since his death. Tsiolkovsky also did not believe in traditional religious cosmology, but instead (and to the chagrin of the Soviet authorities) he believed in a cosmic being that governed humans as "marionettes, mechanical puppets, machines, movie characters",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> thereby adhering to a mechanical view of the universe, which he believed would be controlled in the millennia to come through the power of human science and industry.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="408" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg/170px-Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg/255px-Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg/340px-Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chertrg_Tsiolkovsky.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Draft first space ship by Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Monument_to_the_Conquerors_of_Space" title="Monument to the Conquerors of Space">The Monument to the Conquerors of Space</a> was erected to celebrate the achievements of the Soviet people in space exploration.
