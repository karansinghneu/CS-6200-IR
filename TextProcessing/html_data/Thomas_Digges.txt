title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="\'d\' in \'dye\'">d</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="/&#609;/: \'g\' in \'guy\'">&#609;</span><span title="\'z\' in \'zoom\'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; c. 1546 &#8211; 24 August 1595) was an English mathematician and <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>. He was the first to expound the <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">Copernican system</a> in English but discarded the notion of a fixed shell of immoveable stars to postulate infinitely many stars at varying distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was also first to postulate the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_night_sky_paradox" title="Dark night sky paradox">dark night sky paradox</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Digges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Thomas Digges, born about 1546, was the son of <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Digges_(scientist)" title="Leonard Digges (scientist)">Leonard Digges</a> (c. 1515 &#8211; c. 1559), the mathematician and surveyor, and Bridget Wilford, the daughter of Thomas Wilford, esquire, of Hartridge in <a href="/wiki/Cranbrook,_Kent" title="Cranbrook, Kent">Cranbrook, Kent</a>, by his first wife, Elizabeth Culpeper, the daughter of Walter Culpeper, esquire. Digges had two brothers, James and Daniel, and three sisters, Mary, who married a man with the surname of  Barber; Anne, who married William Digges; and Sarah, whose first husband was surnamed Martin, and whose second husband was John Weston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the death of his father, Digges grew up under the guardianship of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Dee_(mathematician)" title="John Dee (mathematician)">John Dee</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a typical <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_philosopher" title="Natural philosopher">natural philosopher</a>. In 1583, <a href="/wiki/William_Cecil,_1st_Baron_Burghley" title="William Cecil, 1st Baron Burghley">Lord Burghley</a> appointed Digges, with <a href="/wiki/John_Chamber_(academic)" title="John Chamber (academic)">John Chamber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Savile_(Bible_translator)" title="Henry Savile (Bible translator)">Henry Savile</a>, to sit on a commission to consider whether England should adopt the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>, as proposed by Dee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Digges served as a member of parliament for <a href="/wiki/Wallingford_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Wallingford (UK Parliament constituency)">Wallingford</a> and also had a military career as a Muster-Master General to the English forces from 1586 to 1594 during the war with the Spanish Netherlands.\n</p><p>Digges died on 24 August 1595. His last will, in which he specifically excluded both his brother, James Digges, and William Digges, was proved on 1 September. Digges was buried in the chancel of the church of <a href="/wiki/St_Mary_Aldermanbury" title="St Mary Aldermanbury">St Mary Aldermanbury</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_issue">Marriage and issue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Digges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and issue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Digges married Anne St Leger (1555&#8211;1636), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Warham_St_Leger" title="Warham St Leger">Sir Warham St Leger</a> and his first wife, Ursula Neville (d. 1575), the fifth daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Neville,_5th_Baron_Bergavenny" title="George Neville, 5th Baron Bergavenny">George Neville, 5th Baron Bergavenny</a>, by his third wife, Mary Stafford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In his will he named two surviving sons, Sir <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Digges" title="Dudley Digges">Dudley Digges</a> (1583&#8211;1639), politician and statesman, and <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Digges_(writer)" title="Leonard Digges (writer)">Leonard Digges</a> (1588&#8211;1635), poet, and two surviving daughters, Margaret and Ursula. After Digges\' death, his widow, Anne, married Thomas Russell of <a href="/wiki/Alderminster" title="Alderminster">Alderminster</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>, "whom in 1616 <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> named as an <a href="/wiki/Executor" title="Executor">overseer</a> of his will".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Digges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Digges attempted to determine the <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of the 1572 <a href="/wiki/SN_1572" title="SN 1572">supernova</a> observed by <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a>, and concluded it had to be beyond the orbit of the Moon. This contradicted Aristotle\'s view of the universe, according to which no change could take place among the fixed stars.\n</p><p>In 1576, he published a new edition of his father\'s perpetual almanac, <i>A Prognostication everlasting</i>. The text written by Leonard Digges for the third edition of 1556 was left unchanged, but Thomas added new material in several appendices. The most important of these was <i>A Perfit Description of the Caelestiall Orbes according to the most aunciente doctrine of the Pythagoreans, latelye revived by Copernicus and by Geometricall Demonstrations approved</i>. Contrary to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_system" title="Ptolemaic system">Ptolemaic</a> <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a> of the original book by his father, the appendix featured a detailed discussion of the controversial and still poorly known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copernicus" title="Copernicus">Copernican</a> heliocentric model of the Universe. This was the first publication of that model in English, and a milestone in the popularisation of science.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ThomasDiggesmap.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="627" decoding="async" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/ThomasDiggesmap.JPG/220px-ThomasDiggesmap.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/ThomasDiggesmap.JPG/330px-ThomasDiggesmap.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/ThomasDiggesmap.JPG/440px-ThomasDiggesmap.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ThomasDiggesmap.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of the Copernican universe from Thomas Digges\' book</div></div></div>\n<p>For the most part, the appendix was a loose translation into English of chapters from Copernicus\' book <i><a href="/wiki/De_revolutionibus_orbium_coelestium" title="De revolutionibus orbium coelestium">De revolutionibus orbium coelestium</a></i>. Thomas Digges went further than Copernicus, however, by proposing that the universe is infinite, containing infinitely many stars, and may have been the first person to do so. According to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Robert_Harrison" title="Edward Robert Harrison">Harrison</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Copernicus had said little or nothing about what lay beyond the sphere of fixed stars. Digges\'s original contribution to cosmology consisted of dismantling the starry sphere, and scattering the stars throughout endless space. ...</p><p>By grafting endless space onto the Copernican system and scattering the stars throughout this endless space, Digges pioneered... the idea of an unlimited universe filled with the mingling rays of countless stars.</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>An illustration of the Copernican universe can be seen at right. The outer inscription on the map reads (after spelling adjustments from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabethan_English" title="Elizabethan English">Elizabethan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">Modern English</a>):\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This orb of stars fixed infinitely up extends itself in altitude spherically, and therefore immovable the palace of felicity garnished with perpetual shining glorious lights innumerable, far excelling our sun both in quantity and quality the very court of celestial angels, devoid of grief and replenished with perfect endless joy, the habitacle for the elect.\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Digges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
