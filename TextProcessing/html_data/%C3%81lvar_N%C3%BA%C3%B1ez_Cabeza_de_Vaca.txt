International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[&#712;al&#946;a&#638; &#712;n&#361;&#626;e&#952; ka&#712;&#946;e&#952;a &#240;e &#712;&#946;aka]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Jerez_de_la_Frontera" title="Jerez de la Frontera">Jerez de la Frontera</a>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1488/1490/1492<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca,_Alvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_1600_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca,_Alvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_1600-1">[1]</a></sup>&#160;&#8211; <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1557/1558/1559<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca,_Alvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_1600_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca,_Alvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_1600-1">[1]</a></sup>/1560<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014-2">[2]</a></sup>) was a Spanish explorer of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, and one of four survivors of the 1527 <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narv&#225;ez expedition">Narv&#225;ez expedition</a>. During eight years of traveling across the US Southwest, he became a trader and <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">faith healer</a> to various <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> tribes before reconnecting with Spanish civilization in Mexico in 1536. After returning to Spain in 1537, he wrote an account, first published in 1542 as <i>La relaci&#243;n y comentarios</i> ("The Account and Commentaries"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>), which in later editions was retitled <i>Naufragios</i> ("Shipwrecks"). Cabeza de Vaca is sometimes considered a proto-<a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">anthropologist</a> for his detailed accounts of the many tribes of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> that he encountered.\n</p><p>In 1540, Cabeza de Vaca was appointed <i><a href="/wiki/Adelantado" title="Adelantado">adelantado</a></i> of what is now <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, where he was governor and <a href="/wiki/Captaincies_of_the_Spanish_Empire" title="Captaincies of the Spanish Empire">captain general</a> of <a href="/wiki/Governorate_of_New_Andalusia" title="Governorate of New Andalusia">New Andalusia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca_2004_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca_2004-4">[4]</a></sup> He worked to build up the population of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, where settlement had declined due to poor administration. Cabeza de Vaca was transported to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> for trial in 1545. Although his sentence was eventually commuted, he never returned to the Americas. He died in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" N&#250;&#241;ez de Vera and Teresa Cabeza de Vaca y de Zurita, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Jerez_de_la_Frontera" title="Jerez de la Frontera">Jerez de la Frontera</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" Despite the family\'s status as minor nobility, they possessed modest economic resources. In 16th-century documents, his name appears as "Alvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_Arms_of_Cabeza_de_Vaca.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="252" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Coat_of_Arms_of_Cabeza_de_Vaca.jpg/220px-Coat_of_Arms_of_Cabeza_de_Vaca.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/Coat_of_Arms_of_Cabeza_de_Vaca.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_Arms_of_Cabeza_de_Vaca.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coat of Arms of Cabeza de Vaca from the Archivo de Indias, Sevilla, Spain. Reprinted in <i>The Odyssey of Cabeza de Vaca</i> by Morris Bishop. New York: The Century Co., 1933.</div></div></div>\n<p>&#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez\'s maternal surname, Cabeza de Vaca (meaning &#8220;head of cow&#8221;) was said to be associated with a maternal ancestor, <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Alhaja" title="Mart&#237;n Alhaja">Mart&#237;n Alhaja</a>. He had shown the Spanish king a secret mountain pass, marked by a cow&#8217;s skull, enabling the king to win the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Navas_de_Tolosa" title="Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa">Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa</a> against the Muslim Moors in 1212.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca_2004_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca_2004-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some sources indicate that after his parents died when he was young, the boy &#193;lvar was taken in by relatives (most likely his aunt and uncle or his paternal grandfather, Pedro de Vera). Evidence suggests that he probably had a moderately comfortable early life. He was appointed chamberlain for the house of a noble family in his teen years then participated in the conquest of the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> where he was appointed a governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca_2004_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca_2004-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1511, he enlisted in the Spanish army, serving in Italy (with distinction), Spain and Navarre. He received several medals of honor and became more of a political figure in Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1527, N&#250;&#241;ez joined the <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narv&#225;ez expedition">Florida expedition</a> of conquistador <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nfilo_de_Narv%C3%A1ez" title="P&#225;nfilo de Narv&#225;ez">P&#225;nfilo de Narv&#225;ez</a> during which he served as treasurer and marshal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_2014-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Narv.C3.A1ez_Expedition_and_early_Native_American_relations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Narv&#225;ez_Expedition_and_early_Native_American_relations">Narv&#225;ez Expedition and early Native American relations</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="905" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png/260px-Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png/390px-Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png/520px-Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Expedition_Cabeza_de_Vaca_Karte.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Route of <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narv&#225;ez expedition">Narv&#225;ez expedition</a> (until November 1528 at Galveston Island), and a historical reconstruction of Cabeza de Vaca\'s later wanderings</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1527, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nfilo_de_Narv%C3%A1ez" title="P&#225;nfilo de Narv&#225;ez">P&#225;nfilo de Narv&#225;ez</a> was sent by Spain&#8217;s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Spain" title="Charles I of Spain">King Charles V</a> to explore the unknown territory which the Spanish called La Florida, including not only present-day <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> but a large, poorly-defined section of what today is the southeastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> Cabeza de Vaca was attached to this expedition as the expedition&#8217;s treasurer. Records indicate that he also had a military role as one of the chief officers on the <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narv&#225;ez expedition">Narv&#225;ez expedition</a>, noted as sheriff or marshal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Resendez_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Resendez-7">[7]</a></sup> On June 17, 1527, the fleet of five ships set sail towards the province of P&#225;nuco (which was on the western border of Florida). When they stopped in <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> for supplies, Narv&#225;ez lost approximately 150 of his men, who chose to stay on the island rather than continue with the expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The expedition continued to Cuba, where Cabeza de Vaca took two ships to recruit more men and buy supplies. Their fleet was battered by a hurricane, resulting in the destruction of both ships and loss of most of Cabeza de Vaca&#8217;s men. Narv&#225;ez arrived days later to pick up the survivors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs.org_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbs.org-8">[8]</a></sup> By February 1528, the remaining ships and men resumed their expedition, reaching Florida in April. They anchored near what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Prada_Site" title="Jungle Prada Site">Jungle Prada Site</a> in  <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg</a>, claiming this land as a possession of the Spanish crown.\n</p><p>After communicating with the Native Americans, the Spanish heard rumours that a city named <a href="/wiki/Apalachee#History" title="Apalachee">Apalachen</a> was full of food and gold. Against the advice of Cabeza de Vaca, Narv&#225;ez decided to split up his men. Some 300 were to go on foot to Apalachen and the other would sail to <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nuco,_Veracruz" title="P&#225;nuco, Veracruz">P&#225;nuco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> Apalachen had no gold but had only corn, but the explorers were told a village known as Aute, about 5 or 9 days away, was rich. They pushed on through the swamps, harassed by the Native Americans. A few Spanish men were killed and more wounded. When they arrived in Aute, they found that the inhabitants had burned down the village and left. But the fields had not been harvested, so at least the Spanish scavenged food there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> After several months of fighting native inhabitants through wilderness and swamp, the party decided to abandon the interior and try to reach P&#225;nuco.\n</p><p>Slaughtering and eating their remaining horses, they gathered the stirrups, spurs, horseshoes and other metal items. They fashioned a <a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> from deer hide to make a fire hot enough to forge tools and nails. They used these in making five primitive boats to use to get to Mexico. Cabeza de Vaca commanded one of these vessels, each of which held 50 men. Depleted of food and water, the men followed the coast westward. But when they reached the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, the powerful current swept them out into the Gulf, where the five rafts were separated by a hurricane. Some lives were lost forever, including that of Narv&#225;ez.\n</p><p>Two crafts with about 40 survivors each, including Cabeza de Vaca, wrecked on or near <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Island" title="Galveston Island">Galveston Island</a> (now part of Texas). Out of the 80 or so survivors, only 15 lived past that winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> The explorers called the island Malhado (&#8220;Ill fate&#8221; in Spanish), or the Island of Doom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They tried to repair the rafts, using what remained of their own clothes as <a href="/wiki/Oakum" title="Oakum">oakum</a> to plug holes, but they lost the rafts to a large wave.\n</p><p>As the number of survivors dwindled rapidly, they were enslaved for a few years by various American Indian tribes of the upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>. Because Cabeza de Vaca survived and prospered from time to time, some scholars argue that he was not enslaved but using a figure of speech. He and other noblemen were accustomed to better living. Their encounters with harsh conditions and weather, and being required to work like native women, must have seemed like slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup> The tribes to which Cabeza de Vaca was enslaved included the Hans and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Capoques&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Capoques (page does not exist)">Capoques</a>, and tribes later called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karankawa" title="Karankawa">Karankawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coahuiltecan" title="Coahuiltecan">Coahuiltecan</a>. After escaping, only four men, Cabeza de Vaca, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Dorantes_de_Carranza" title="Andr&#233;s Dorantes de Carranza">Andr&#233;s Dorantes de Carranza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alonso_del_Castillo_Maldonado" title="Alonso del Castillo Maldonado">Alonso del Castillo Maldonado</a>, and an enslaved Moroccan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">Berber</a> named Esteban (later called <a href="/wiki/Estevanico" title="Estevanico">Estevanico</a>), survived to reach <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>.\n</p><p>Traveling mostly with this small group, Cabeza de Vaca explored what is now the U.S. state of Texas, as well as the northeastern Mexican states of <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuevo_Le%C3%B3n" title="Nuevo Le&#243;n">Nuevo Le&#243;n</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, and possibly smaller portions of <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. He traveled on foot through the then-colonized territories of Texas and the coast<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2018)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup>. He continued through Coahuila and <a href="/wiki/Nueva_Vizcaya" title="Nueva Vizcaya">Nueva Vizcaya</a>; then down the Gulf of California coast to what is now Sinaloa, Mexico, over a period of roughly eight years. Throughout those years, Cabeza de Vaca and the other men adapted to the lives of the indigenous people they stayed with, whom he later described as Roots People, the Fish and Blackberry People, or the Fig People, depending on their principal foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During his wanderings, passing from tribe to tribe, Cabeza de Vaca later reported that he developed sympathies for the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous</a> peoples. He became a trader and a healer, which gave him some freedom to travel among the tribes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As a healer, Cabeza de Vaca used blowing (like the Native Americans) to heal, but claimed that God and the Christian cross led to his success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup> His healing of the sick gained him a reputation as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faith_healer" title="Faith healer">faith healer</a>. His group attracted numerous native followers, who regarded them as "children of the sun", endowed with the power to heal and destroy. As Cabeza de Vaca grew healthier, he decided that he would make his way to P&#225;nuco, supporting himself through trading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs.org_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-pbs.org-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup> He finally decided to try to reach the Spanish colony in Mexico. Many natives were said to accompany the explorers on their journey across what is now known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Southwest" title="American Southwest">American Southwest</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.\n</p><p>After finally reaching the colonized lands of <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>, where he first encountered fellow Spaniards near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Culiac%C3%A1n" title="Culiac&#225;n">Culiac&#225;n</a>, Cabeza de Vaca and the three other men reached <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. From there he sailed back to Europe in 1537.\n</p><p>Numerous researchers have tried to trace his route across the Southwest. As he did not begin writing his chronicle until back in Spain, he had to rely on memory. He did not have the instruments (<a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a>) to determine his location; he had to rely on <a href="/wiki/Dead_reckoning" title="Dead reckoning">dead reckoning</a>, and was uncertain of his route. Aware that his recollection has numerous errors in chronology and geography, historians have worked to put together pieces of the puzzle to discern his paths.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_America">Return to America</span></h2>\n<p>In 1540, Cabeza de Vaca was appointed <i><a href="/wiki/Adelantado" title="Adelantado">adelantado</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Governorate_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Governorate of the R&#237;o de la Plata">R&#237;o de la Plata</a> in South America. The colony comprised parts of what is now <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>. Cabeza de Vaca was assigned to find a usable route from this colony to the colony in Peru, on the other side of the Andes Mountains on the Pacific Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG/220px-CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG/330px-CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG/440px-CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CPonte_Placa_Alvar.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plaque commemorating Cabeza de Vaca as the first European to see the <a href="/wiki/Iguazu_Falls" title="Iguazu Falls">Iguazu Falls</a></div></div></div>\n<p>En route, he disembarked from his fleet at <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_Island" title="Santa Catarina Island">Santa Catarina Island</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. With an indigenous force, plus 250 musketeers and 26 horses, he followed native trails<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> discovered by <a href="/wiki/Aleixo_Garcia" title="Aleixo Garcia">Aleixo Garcia</a> overland to the district\'s Spanish capital, <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunci&#243;n">Asunci&#243;n</a>, far inland on the great <a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay River</a>. Cabeza de Vaca is thought to have been the first European to see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igua%C3%A7u_Falls" title="Igua&#231;u Falls">Igua&#231;u Falls</a>.\n</p><p>In March 1542 Cabeza de Vaca met with <a href="/wiki/Domingo_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_Irala" title="Domingo Mart&#237;nez de Irala">Domingo Mart&#237;nez de Irala</a> and relieved him of his position as governor.  The government of <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunci&#243;n">Asunci&#243;n</a> pledged loyalty to Cabeza de Vaca, and Irala was assigned to explore a possible route to Peru. Once Irala returned and reported, Cabeza de Vaca planned his own expedition. He hoped to reach Los Reyes (a base that Irala set up) and push forward into the jungle in search of a route to the gold and silver mines of Peru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> The expedition did not go well, and Cabeza de Vaca returned to <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunci&#243;n">Asunci&#243;n</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During his absence, Irala had stirred up resistance to Cabeza de Vaca&#8217;s rule and capitalized on political rivalries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup> Scholars widely agree that Cabeza de Vaca had an unusually sympathetic attitude towards the Native Americans for his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs.org_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-pbs.org-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup> The elite settlers in modern Argentina, known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encomenderos" title="Encomenderos">encomenderos</a></i>, generally did not agree with his enlightened conduct toward the Natives; they wanted to use them for labor. Because he lost elite support, and Buenos Aires was failing as a settlement, not attracting enough residents, Mart&#237;nez de Irala arrested Cabeza de Vaca in 1544 for poor administration. The former explorer was returned to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in 1545 for trial.\n</p><p>Although eventually exonerated, Cabeza de Vaca never returned to South America. He wrote an extensive report on the R&#237;o de la Plata colony in South America, strongly criticizing the conduct of Mart&#237;nez de Irala. The report was bound with his earlier <i>La Relaci&#243;n</i> and published under the title <i>Comentarios</i> (Commentary). He died poor in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> around the year 1560.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="La_relaci.C3.B3n_of_.C3.81lvar_N.C3.BA.C3.B1ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="La_relaci&#243;n_of_&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca"><i>La relaci&#243;n of &#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca</i></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title,_La_relacion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2197" data-file-width="1509" decoding="async" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg/220px-Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg/330px-Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg/440px-Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title%2C_La_relacion.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Houghton_US_2415.3_-_title,_La_relacion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Title page from a 1555 edition of <i>La relacion y comentarios del gobernador Aluar Nu&#241;ez Cabeca de Vaca</i></div></div></div>\n<p><i>La relaci&#243;n of &#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca</i> is the account of his experiences with the Narvaez expedition and after being wrecked on <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Island" title="Galveston Island">Galveston Island</a> in November 1528. Cabeza de Vaca and his last three men struggled to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cabeza_de_Vaca:EB_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cabeza_de_Vaca:EB-13">[13]</a></sup> They wandered along the Texas coast as prisoners of the Han and Capoque American Indians for two years, while Cabeza de Vaca observed the people, picking up their ways of life and customs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca-14">[14]</a></sup> They traveled through the American Southwest and ultimately reached Mexico City, nearly eight years after being wrecked on the island.\n</p><p>In 1537, Cabeza de Vaca returned to Spain, where he wrote his narratives of the Narvaez expedition. These narratives were collected and published in 1542 in Spain. They are now known as <i>The Relation of &#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca.</i> The narrative of Cabeza de Vaca is the &#8220;first European book devoted completely to North America.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Vaca:AJ_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Vaca:AJ-15">[15]</a></sup> His detailed account describes the lives of numerous tribes of American Indians of the time. Cabeza de Vaca showed compassion and respect for native peoples, which, together with the great detail he recorded, distinguishes his narrative from others of the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Vaca:AJ_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_Vaca:AJ-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_observer">Role of observer</span></h3>\n<p>Cabeza de Vaca reported on the customs and ways of American Indian life, aware of his status as an early European explorer. He spent eight years with various peoples, including the Capoque, Han, Avavare, and Arbadao. He describes details of the culture of the Malhado people, the Capoque, and Han American Indians, such as their treatment of offspring, their wedding rites, and their main sources of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca-14">[14]</a></sup> Cabeza de Vaca and his three fellow survivors at times served as slaves to the American Indians to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cabeza_de_Vaca:EB_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cabeza_de_Vaca:EB-13">[13]</a></sup> Through his observations, Cabeza de Vaca provides insights into 16th-century American Indian life near the present-day Mexico-Texas border.\n</p><p>For many peoples the accounts of Cabeza de Vaca and <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> are the only written records of their existence. By the time of the next European contact, many had vanished, possibly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbian_Exchange#Disease" title="Columbian Exchange">diseases carried by Cabeza de Vaca and his companions</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambassador_for_Christ">Ambassador for Christ</span></h3>\n<p>One of Cabeza de Vaca\'s greatest achievements of his journey, was that he played an important role as an ambassador to bring peace throughout the land. As the party of travellers passed from one tribe to the next, warring tribes would immediately make peace and become friendly, so that the natives could receive the party and give them gifts. Cabeza notes in his personal account of his journey that in this way; <i>"We left the whole country in peace."</i> Cabeza saw these events as part of his mission and purpose in America, acknowledging in his account that he believed that: <i>"God was guiding us to where we could serve Him."</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandelier_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bandelier-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cabeza\'s greatest challenge as an ambassador came when he attempted to bring peace between the conquering Spanish army and the natives. As Cabeza approached Spanish settlement, he and his companions were very grieved to see the destruction of the native villages and enslavement of the natives. The fertile land lay uncultivated and the natives were nearly starving, hiding in the forest, for fear of the Spanish army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandelier_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bandelier-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cabeza then encountered Diego de Alcaraz, commander of a slavery expedition of about 20 horsemen and attempted to negotiate peace between them and the natives. However, as soon as they departed, Diego went back on his word and plundered Cabeza\'s entourage of natives that he had sent back home. Not long after this, Cabeza encountered the chief <a href="/wiki/Alcalde" title="Alcalde">Alcalde</a> (Spanish captain of the province) named Melchor Diaz. Melchor Diaz ordered Cabeza to bring the natives back from the forests so that they would re-cultivate the land. Cabeza and Melchor invited the natives to convert to Christianity and the natives did so willingly. Cabeza instructed them to build a large wooden cross in each village, which would cause members of the Spanish army to pass through the village and not attack it. Soon afterward the Diego de Alcaraz expedition returned and explained to Melchor that they were shocked at how, on their return journey, not only did they find the land repopulated, but the natives coming to greet them with crosses in hand and also gave them provisions. Melchor then ordered Diego that no harm be done to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandelier_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bandelier-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_report">Personal report</span></h3>\n<p>Cabeza de Vaca wrote this narrative to <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> to &#8220;transmit what I saw and heard in the nine years I wandered lost and miserable over many remote lands&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca-14">[14]</a></sup>  He wanted to convey &#8220;not merely a report of positions and distances, flora and fauna, but of the customs of the numerous indigenous people I talked with and dwelt among, as well as any other matters I could hear of or observe&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca-14">[14]</a></sup> He took care to present facts, as a full account of what he observed. <i>The Relation</i> is the only account of many details concerning the indigenous people whom he encountered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-&#193;lvar_N&#250;&#241;ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca-14">[14]</a></sup> The accuracy of his account has been validated by later reports of others, as well as by the oral traditions of descendants of some of the tribes.\n</p><p>Cabeza&#8217;s account also served as a petition to the King of Spain to both establish a permanent Christian mission and eventually establish the native tribes as a nation under the governance of Spain. In his reflection Cabeza writes to the king of Spain:\n</p><p><i>May God in His infinite mercy grant that in the days of Your Majesty and under your power and sway, these people become willingly and sincerely subjects of the true Lord Who created and redeemed them. We believe they will be, and that Your Majesty is destined to bring it about, as it will not be at all difficult.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandelier_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bandelier-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cabeza continued to be a strong advocate for the rights of Native American Indians throughout his lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariners_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-mariners-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs.org_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-pbs.org-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texas_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-texas-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Indian_nations_noted_by_name">American Indian nations noted by name</span></h3>\n<p>Cabeza De Vaca identified the following peoples by name in his <i>La Relacion</i> (1542). The publication of <i>Comentarios</i> was appended to <i>La relaci&#243;n</i> as a joint publication in Valladolid, Spain entitled: <i>Naufragios.</i>  At that time, explorers often published their reports of travels in foreign lands.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_editions">Later editions</span></h3>\n<p>In 1906, <i>Naufragios</i> was published in a new edition in Madrid, Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The introduction says the intent of this edition was to publicize Cabeza de Vaca\'s observations and experiences to strengthen authentic representations. This has been described as having the objective of portraying Cabeza de Vaca as less aggressive , while trying to authenticate his role as a sympathetic observer of the natives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Place_in_Chicano_literature">Place in Chicano literature</span></h2>\n<p>Herrera (2011) classifies Cabeza de Vaca&#8217;s <i>La Relacion</i> as the first major contribution to <a href="/wiki/Chicano" title="Chicano">Chicano</a> literature. Scholars have identified five major periods of Chicano literature: Spanish Mexican, Mexican American, Annexation, Chicano Renaissance, and Modern. Cabeza de Vaca is classified as part of the Spanish Mexican period; he recounted eight years of travel and survival in the area of Chicano culture: present-day Texas, New Mexico, and northern Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herrera2011_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herrera2011-21">[21]</a></sup> His account is the first known written description of the American Southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vaca_2004_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vaca_2004-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptation">Film adaptation</span></h2>\n<ul><li>The drama feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Cabeza_de_Vaca_(film)" title="Cabeza de Vaca (film)">Cabeza de Vaca</a></i> (1991), a Mexican production, was directed by <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Echevarr%C3%ADa" title="Nicol&#225;s Echevarr&#237;a">Nicol&#225;s Echevarr&#237;a</a> and starred <a href="/wiki/Juan_Diego_(actor)" title="Juan Diego (actor)">Juan Diego</a>. Based on <i>Naufragios</i>, the film was entered into the <a href="/wiki/41st_Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="41st Berlin International Film Festival">41st Berlin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlinale_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlinale-22">[22]</a></sup> A DVD version was released in 2012.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representation_in_other_media">Representation in other media</span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Laila_Lalami" title="Laila Lalami">Laila Lalami</a>\'s novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moor%27s_Account" title="The Moor\'s Account">The Moor\'s Account</a></i> (2014), is a fictional memoir of <a href="/wiki/Estevanico" title="Estevanico">Estevanico</a>, the Moroccan slave who survived the journey and accompanied Cabeza de Vaca through the Southwest.
