href="/wiki/Table_of_nuclides" title="Table of nuclides">Complete table of nuclides</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Helium-3</b> (<sup>3</sup>He, see also <a href="/wiki/Helion_(chemistry)" title="Helion (chemistry)">helion</a>) is a light, non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> with two <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> (common helium having two protons and two neutrons). Its hypothetical existence was first proposed in 1934 by the Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_physicist" title="Nuclear physicist">nuclear physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Mark_Oliphant" title="Mark Oliphant">Mark Oliphant</a> while he was working at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a>. Oliphant had performed experiments in which fast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteron" title="Deuteron">deuterons</a> collided with deuteron targets (incidentally, the first demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Helium-3 was thought to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotope" title="Radioactive isotope">radioactive isotope</a> until it was also found in samples of natural helium,  which is mostly <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a>, taken both from the terrestrial atmosphere and from natural <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Other than <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_hydrogen#Hydrogen-1_(protium)" title="Isotopes of hydrogen">protium</a> (ordinary <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>), helium-3 is the only stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of any <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> with more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrons" title="Neutrons">neutrons</a>.\n</p><p>Helium-3 occurs as a <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclide</a>, escaping from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> over millions of years. Helium-3 is also thought to be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural" title="Natural">natural</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleogenic" title="Nucleogenic">nucleogenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmogenic_nuclide" title="Cosmogenic nuclide">cosmogenic nuclide</a>, one produced when <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> is bombarded by natural neutrons, which can be released by <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_fission" title="Spontaneous fission">spontaneous fission</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reactions</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. Some of the helium-3 found in the terrestrial atmosphere is also a relic of atmospheric and underwater <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">nuclear weapons testing</a>.\n</p><p>Much speculation has been made over the possibility of helium-3 as a future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_source" title="Energy source">energy source</a>.  Unlike most other nuclear fusion <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">reactions</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> of helium-3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> releases large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> without causing the surrounding material to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a>.  However, the temperatures required to achieve helium-3 fusion reactions are much higher than in traditional fusion reactions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the process may unavoidably create other reactions that themselves would cause the surrounding material to become radioactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The abundance of helium-3 is thought to be greater on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> than on Earth, having been embedded in the upper layer of <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> over billions of years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> though still lower in abundance than in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._H._Cocks_2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-F._H._Cocks_2010-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Because of its low atomic mass of 3.02 <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass units</a>, helium-3 has some <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical properties</a> different from those of helium-4, with a mass of 4.00 atomic mass units. Because of the weak, induced dipole&#8211;dipole <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interaction</a> between the helium atoms, their microscopic physical properties are mainly determined by their <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a>. Also, the microscopic properties of helium-3 cause it to have a higher zero-point energy than helium-4. This implies that helium-3 can overcome dipole&#8211;dipole interactions with less <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> than helium-4 can.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanical" title="Quantum mechanical">quantum mechanical</a> effects on helium-3 and helium-4 are significantly different because with two <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, and two <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, helium-4 has an overall <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> of zero, making it a <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">boson</a>, but with one fewer neutron, helium-3 has an overall spin of one half, making it a <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a>.\n</p><p>Helium-3 boils at 3.19 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> compared with helium-4 at 4.23 K, and its <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a> is also lower at 3.35 K, compared with helium-4 at 5.2 K. Helium-3 has less than half the density of Helium-4 when it is at its boiling point: 59 gram per liter compared to the 125 gram per liter of helium-4&#8212;at a pressure of one atmosphere. Its latent heat of vaporization is also considerably lower at 0.026 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilojoule_per_mole" title="Kilojoule per mole">kilojoules per mole</a> compared with the 0.0829 kilojoules per mole of helium-4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span If the fusion temperature is below that for the helium nuclei to fuse, the reaction produces a high energy alpha particle which quickly acquires an electron producing a stable light helium ion which can be utilized directly as a source of electricity without producing dangerous neutrons.  </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fusion_rxnrate.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="540" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Fusion_rxnrate.svg/300px-Fusion_rxnrate.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Fusion_rxnrate.svg/450px-Fusion_rxnrate.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Fusion_rxnrate.svg/600px-Fusion_rxnrate.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fusion_rxnrate.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The fusion <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">reaction rate</a> increases rapidly with temperature until it maximizes and then gradually drops off. The DT rate peaks at a lower temperature (about 70&#160;keV, or 800 million kelvins) and at a higher value than other reactions commonly considered for fusion energy.</div></div></div>\n<p><sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> can be used in fusion reactions by either of the reactions <sup>2</sup>H + <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> &#8594; <sup>4</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> + <sup>1</sup>p + 18.3 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">MeV</a>, or <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> + <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> &#8594; <sup>4</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> + 2 <sup>1</sup>p+ 12.86 MeV.\n</p><p>The conventional <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> + <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> ("D-T") fusion process produces energetic neutrons which render reactor components <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> with <a href="/wiki/Activation_product" title="Activation product">activation products</a>. The appeal of helium-3 fusion stems from the <a href="/wiki/Aneutronic_fusion" title="Aneutronic fusion">aneutronic</a> nature of its reaction products. Helium-3 itself is non-radioactive. The lone high-energy by-product, the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>, can be contained using electric and magnetic fields. The momentum energy of this proton (created in the fusion process) will interact with the containing electromagnetic field, resulting in direct net electricity generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of the higher <a href="/wiki/Coulomb_barrier" title="Coulomb barrier">Coulomb barrier</a>, the temperatures required for <sup>2</sup><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a> + <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> fusion are much higher than those of conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-T_fusion" title="D-T fusion">D-T fusion</a>. Moreover, since both reactants need to be mixed together to fuse, reactions between nuclei of the same reactant will occur, and the D-D reaction (<sup>2</sup><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a> + <sup>2</sup><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a>) does produce a <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>. Reaction rates vary with temperature, but the D-<sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> reaction rate is never greater than 3.56 times the D-D reaction rate (see graph). Therefore, fusion using D-<sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> fuel at the right temperature and a D-lean fuel mixture, can produce a much lower neutron flux than D-T fusion, but is not clean, negating some of its main attraction.\n</p><p>The second possibility, fusing <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> with itself (<sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> + <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a>), requires even higher temperatures (since now both reactants have a +2 charge), and thus is even more difficult than the D-<sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> reaction. However, it does offer a possible reaction that produces no neutrons; the charged protons produced can be contained using electric and magnetic fields, which in turn results in direct electricity generation. <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> + <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> fusion is feasible as demonstrated in the laboratory and has immense advantages, but commercial viability is many years in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The amounts of helium-3 needed as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">conventional fuels</a> are substantial by comparison to amounts currently available. The total amount of energy produced in the <sup>2</sup><a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">D</a>&#160;+&#160;<sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a> reaction is 18.4 M<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a>, which corresponds to some 493 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour" title="Watt-hour">megawatt-hours</a> (4.93&#215;10<sup>8</sup> W&#183;h) per three <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">grams</a> (one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mole_(chemistry)" title="Mole (chemistry)">mole</a>) of <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a>  If the total amount of energy could be converted to electrical power with 100% efficiency (a physical impossibility), it would correspond to about 30 minutes of output of a gigawatt electrical plant per mole of <sup>3</sup><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">He</a>.  Thus, a year\'s production (at 6 grams for each operation hour) would require 52.5 kilograms of helium-3. The amount of fuel needed for large-scale applications can also be put in terms of total consumption: electricity consumption by 107 million U.S. households in 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> totaled 1,140 billion kW&#183;h (1.14&#215;10<sup>15</sup> W&#183;h). Again assuming 100% conversion efficiency, 6.7 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> per year of helium-3 would be required for that segment of the energy demand of the United States, 15 to 20 tonnes per year given a more realistic end-to-end conversion efficiency.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutron_detection">Neutron detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neutron detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Helium-3 is a most important isotope in instrumentation for <a href="/wiki/Neutron_detection" title="Neutron detection">neutron detection</a>. It has a high absorption cross section for thermal <a href="/wiki/Neutron_radiation" title="Neutron radiation">neutron</a> beams and is used as a converter gas in neutron detectors. The neutron is converted through the nuclear reaction\n</p>\n<dl><dd>n + <sup>3</sup>He &#8594; <sup>3</sup>H + <sup>1</sup>H + 0.764 MeV</dd></dl>\n<p>into charged particles <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> ions (T, <sup>3</sup>H) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ions" title="Hydrogen ions">Hydrogen ions</a>, or protons (p, <sup>1</sup>H) which then are detected by creating a charge cloud in the stopping gas of a <a href="/wiki/Proportional_counter" title="Proportional counter">proportional counter</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geiger-M%C3%BCller_tube" title="Geiger-M&#252;ller tube">Geiger-M&#252;ller tube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Furthermore, the absorption process is strongly <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>-dependent, which allows a <a href="/wiki/Spin_polarization" title="Spin polarization">spin-polarized</a> helium-3 volume to transmit neutrons with one spin component while absorbing the other. This effect is employed in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Polarized_neutron_scattering&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polarized neutron scattering (page does not exist)">neutron polarization analysis</a>, a technique which probes for magnetic properties of matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> had hoped to deploy detectors to spot smuggled plutonium in shipping containers by their neutron emissions, but the worldwide shortage of helium-3 following the drawdown in nuclear weapons production since the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> has to some extent prevented this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  As of 2012, DHS determined the commercial supply of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron-10" title="Boron-10">boron-10</a> would support converting its neutron detection infrastructure to that technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryogenics">Cryogenics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cryogenics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Helium-3_refrigerator" title="Helium-3 refrigerator">helium-3 refrigerator</a> uses helium-3 to achieve temperatures of 0.2 to 0.3 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Dilution_refrigerator" title="Dilution refrigerator">dilution refrigerator</a> uses a mixture of helium-3 and helium-4 to reach <a href="/wiki/Cryogenics" title="Cryogenics">cryogenic</a> temperatures as low as a few thousandths of a <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>An important property of helium-3, which distinguishes it from the more common helium-4, is that its nucleus is a <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a> since it contains an odd number of spin &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> particles. Helium-4 nuclei are <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a>, containing an even number of spin &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> particles. This is a direct result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_quantum_number#Addition_of_quantized_angular_momenta" title="Angular momentum quantum number">addition rules</a> for quantized angular momentum. At low temperatures (about 2.17 K), helium-4 undergoes a <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>: A fraction of it enters a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfluid" title="Superfluid">superfluid</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> that can be roughly understood as a type of <a href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate" title="Bose&#8211;Einstein condensate">Bose&#8211;Einstein condensate</a>. Such a mechanism is not available for helium-3 atoms, which are fermions. However, it was widely speculated that helium-3 could also become a superfluid at much lower temperatures, if the atoms formed into <i>pairs</i> analogous to <a href="/wiki/Cooper_pair" title="Cooper pair">Cooper pairs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/BCS_theory" title="BCS theory">BCS theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a>. Each Cooper pair, having integer spin, can be thought of as a boson. During the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/David_Lee_(physicist)" title="David Lee (physicist)">David Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Osheroff" title="Douglas Osheroff">Douglas Osheroff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Coleman_Richardson" title="Robert Coleman Richardson">Robert Coleman Richardson</a> discovered two phase transitions along the melting curve, which were soon realized to be the two superfluid phases of helium-3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The transition to a superfluid occurs at 2.491 millikelvins on the melting curve. They were awarded the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> for their discovery. <a href="/wiki/Anthony_James_Leggett" title="Anthony James Leggett">Tony Leggett</a> won the 2003 Nobel Prize in Physics for his work on refining understanding of the superfluid phase of helium-3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In a zero magnetic field, there are two distinct superfluid phases of <sup>3</sup>He, the A-phase and the B-phase. The B-phase is the low-temperature, low-pressure phase which has an isotropic energy gap. The A-phase is the higher temperature, higher pressure phase that is further stabilized by a magnetic field and has two point nodes in its gap. The presence of two phases is a clear indication that <sup>3</sup>He is an unconventional superfluid (superconductor), since the presence of two phases requires an additional symmetry, other than gauge symmetry, to be broken. In fact, it is a <i>p</i>-wave superfluid, with spin one, <b>S</b>=1, and angular momentum one, <b>L</b>=1. The ground state corresponds to total angular momentum zero, <b>J</b>=<b>S</b>+<b>L</b>=0 (vector addition). Excited states are possible with non-zero total angular momentum, <b>J</b>&gt;0, which are excited pair collective modes. Because of the extreme purity of superfluid <sup>3</sup>He (since all materials except <sup>4</sup>He have solidified and\nsunk to the bottom of the liquid <sup>3</sup>He and any <sup>4</sup>He has phase separated entirely, this is the most pure condensed matter state), these collective modes have been studied with much greater precision than in any other unconventional pairing system.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_lung_imaging">Medical lung imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medical lung imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Helium-3 nuclei have an intrinsic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_spin" title="Nuclear spin">nuclear spin</a> of &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>, and a relatively high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetogyric_ratio" title="Magnetogyric ratio">magnetogyric ratio</a>. Helium-3 can be <a href="/wiki/Hyperpolarization_(physics)" title="Hyperpolarization (physics)">hyperpolarized</a> using non-equilibrium means such as spin-exchange optical pumping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> During this process, <a href="/wiki/Circular_polarization" title="Circular polarization">circularly polarized</a> infrared laser light, tuned to the appropriate wavelength, is used to excite electrons in an <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> inside a sealed glass vessel. The <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> is transferred from the alkali metal electrons to the noble gas nuclei through collisions. In essence, this process effectively aligns the nuclear spins with the magnetic field in order to enhance the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a> signal. The hyperpolarized gas may then be stored at pressures of 10 atm, for up to 100 hours. Following inhalation, gas mixtures containing the hyperpolarized helium-3 gas can be imaged with an MRI scanner to produce anatomical and functional images of lung ventilation. This technique is also able to produce images of the airway tree, locate unventilated defects, measure the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_gas_pressures" title="Pulmonary gas pressures">alveolar oxygen partial pressure</a>, and measure the <a href="/wiki/Ventilation/perfusion_ratio" title="Ventilation/perfusion ratio">ventilation/perfusion ratio</a>. This technique may be critical for the diagnosis and treatment management of chronic respiratory diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">cystic fibrosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_production">Industrial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Production, sales and distribution of helium-3 in the United States are managed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" title="US Department of Energy">US Department of Energy</a> (DOE) Isotope Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Virtually all helium-3 used in industry today is produced from the radioactive decay of <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a>. Tritium is a radioactive isotope of hydrogen and is typically produced by bombarding lithium-6 with neutrons in a nuclear reactor. The lithium nucleus absorbs a neutron and splits into helium-4 and tritium. Tritium decays into helium-3 with a half-life of 12.3 years, so helium-3 can be produced by simply storing the tritium until it undergoes radioactive decay.\n</p><p>Tritium is a critical component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> and historically it was produced and stockpiled primarily for this application. The decay of tritium into helium-3 reduces the explosive power of the fusion warhead, so periodically the accumulated helium-3 must be removed from warhead reservoirs and tritium in storage. Helium-3 removed during this process is marketed for other applications.\n</p><p>For decades this has been, and remains, the principal source of the world\'s helium-3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, since the signing of the <a href="/wiki/START_I" title="START I">START I</a> Treaty in 1991 the number of nuclear warheads that are kept ready for use has decreased<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This has reduced the quantity of helium-3 available from this source. Helium-3 stockpiles have been further diminished by increased demand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-35">[35]</a></sup> primarily for use in neutron radiation detectors and medical diagnostic procedures. US industrial demand for helium-3 reached a peak of 70,000 liters (approximately 8&#160;kg) per year in 2008. Price at auction, historically about $100/liter, reached as high as $2000/liter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  Since then, demand for helium-3 has declined to about 6000 liters per year due to the high cost and efforts by the DOE to recycle it and find substitutes.\n</p><p>The DOE recognized the developing shortage of both tritium and helium-3. and began producing tritium by lithium irradiation at the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watts_Bar_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Watts Bar Nuclear Generating Station">Watts Bar Nuclear Generating Station</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-35">[35]</a></sup> In this process tritium-producing burnable absorber rods (TPBARs) containing lithium in a ceramic form are inserted into the reactor in place of the normal boron control rods<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Periodically the TPBARs are replaced and the tritium extracted.\n</p><p>Currently only one reactor is used for tritium production but the process could, if necessary, be vastly scaled up to meet any conceivable demand simply by utilizing more of the nation\'s power reactors. Substantial quantities of tritium and helium-3 could also be extracted from the heavy water moderator in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CANDU" title="CANDU">CANDU</a> nuclear reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D2O_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-D2O-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_abundance">Natural abundance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural abundance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Solar_nebula_.28primordial.29_abundance"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_nebula_(primordial)_abundance">Solar nebula (primordial) abundance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solar nebula (primordial) abundance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One early estimate of the primordial ratio of <sup>3</sup>He to <sup>4</sup>He in the solar nebula has been the measurement of their ratio in the atmosphere of Jupiter, measured by the mass spectrometer of the Galileo atmospheric entry probe. This ratio is about 1:10,000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> or 100 parts of <sup>3</sup>He per million parts of <sup>4</sup>He. This is roughly the same ratio of the isotopes as in lunar regolith, which contains 28&#160;ppm helium-4 and 2.8&#160;ppb helium-3 (which is at the lower end of actual sample measurements, which vary from about 1.4 to 15&#160;ppb). However, terrestrial ratios of the isotopes are lower by a factor of 100, mainly due to enrichment of helium-4 stocks in the mantle by billions of years of <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> from <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_abundance">Terrestrial abundance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Terrestrial abundance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotope_geochemistry" title="Isotope geochemistry">isotope geochemistry</a></div>\n<p><sup>3</sup>He is a primordial substance in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, considered to have become entrapped within the Earth during planetary formation. The ratio of <sup>3</sup>He to <sup>4</sup>He within the Earth\'s crust and mantle is less than that for assumptions of solar disk composition as obtained from meteorite and lunar samples, with terrestrial materials generally containing lower <sup>3</sup>He/<sup>4</sup>He ratios due to ingrowth of <sup>4</sup>He from radioactive decay.\n</p><p><sup>3</sup>He has a cosmological ratio of 300 atoms per million atoms of <sup>4</sup>He (at. ppm),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Witt_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Witt-40">[40]</a></sup> leading to the assumption that the original ratio of these primordial gases in the mantle was around 200-300 ppm when Earth was formed. A lot of <sup>4</sup>He was generated by alpha-particle decay of uranium and thorium, and now the mantle has only around 7% primordial helium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Witt_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Witt-40">[40]</a></sup> lowering the total <sup>3</sup>He/<sup>4</sup>He ratio to around 20 ppm. Ratios of <sup>3</sup>He/<sup>4</sup>He in excess of atmospheric are indicative of a contribution of <sup>3</sup>He from the mantle. Crustal sources are dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4"><sup>4</sup>He</a> which is produced by the decay of radioactive elements in the crust and mantle.\n</p><p>The ratio of helium-3 to helium-4 in natural Earth-bound sources varies greatly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aldrich_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aldrich-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holden_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holden-42">[42]</a></sup> Samples of the <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> ore <a href="/wiki/Spodumene" title="Spodumene">spodumene</a> from Edison Mine, South Dakota were found to contain 12 parts of helium-3 to a million parts of helium-4. Samples from other mines showed 2 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aldrich_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aldrich-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Helium is also present as up to 7% of some natural gas sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and large sources have over 0.5% (above 0.2% makes it viable to extract).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithDM_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithDM-44">[44]</a></sup> The fraction of <sup>3</sup>He in helium separated from natural gas in the U.S. was found to range from 70 to 242 parts per billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS_35-3"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM/DoI_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoM/DoI-45">[45]</a></sup> Hence the US 2002 stockpile of 1 billion normal m<sup>3</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithDM_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-SmithDM-44">[44]</a></sup> would have contained about 12 to 43 kilograms of helium-3.  According to one expert, about 26 m<sup>3</sup> or almost 5&#160;kg of <sup>3</sup>He is available annually for separation from the US natural gas stream.  If the process of separating out the <sup>3</sup>He could employ as feedstock the liquefied helium typically used to transport and store bulk quantities, estimates for the incremental energy cost range from US$34 to $300 per liter NTP, excluding the cost of infrastructure and equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRS_35-4"><a href="#cite_note-CRS-35">[35]</a></sup> Algeria\'s annual gas production is assumed to contain 100 million normal cubic metres<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithDM_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-SmithDM-44">[44]</a></sup> and this would contain between 7 and 24 m<sup>3</sup> of helium-3 (about 1 to 4 kilograms) assuming a similar <sup>3</sup>He fraction.\n</p><p><sup>3</sup>He is also present in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>. The natural abundance of <sup>3</sup>He in naturally occurring helium gas is 1.38<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;<span style="white-space:nowrap">6</span></sup> (1.38 parts per million). The partial pressure of helium in the Earth\'s atmosphere is about 0.52 Pa, and thus helium accounts for 5.2 parts per million of the total pressure (101325 Pa) in the Earth\'s atmosphere, and <sup>3</sup>He thus accounts for 7.2 parts per trillion of the atmosphere. Since the atmosphere of the Earth has a mass of about 5.14<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup> tonnes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> the mass of <sup>3</sup>He in the Earth\'s atmosphere is the product of these numbers, or about 37,000 tonnes of <sup>3</sup>He.\n</p><p><sup>3</sup>He is produced on Earth from three sources: lithium <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spallation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, and beta decay of tritium (<sup>3</sup>H). The contribution from cosmic rays is negligible within all except the oldest regolith materials, and lithium spallation reactions are a lesser contributor than the production of <sup>4</sup>He by <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particle</a> emissions.\n</p><p>The total amount of helium-3 in the mantle may be in the range of 0.1&#8211;1 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. However, most of the mantle is not directly accessible. Some helium-3 leaks up through deep-sourced <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspot</a> volcanoes such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, but only 300&#160;grams per year is emitted to the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">Mid-ocean ridges</a> emit another 3 kilogram per year. Around <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction zones</a>, various sources produce helium-3 in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> deposits which possibly contain a thousand tonnes of helium-3 (although there may be 25 thousand tonnes if all ancient subduction zones have such deposits). Wittenberg estimated that United States crustal natural gas sources may have only half a tonne total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Wittenberg cited Anderson\'s estimate of another 1200 metric tonnes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust" title="Interplanetary dust">interplanetary dust</a> particles on the ocean floors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In the 1994 study, extracting helium-3 from these sources consumes more energy than fusion would release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction_from_extraterrestrial_sources">Extraction from extraterrestrial sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extraction from extraterrestrial sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Materials on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>\'s surface contain helium-3 at concentrations between 1.4 and 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a> in sunlit areas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and may contain concentrations as much as 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a> in permanently shadowed regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-F._H._Cocks_2010_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-F._H._Cocks_2010-7">[7]</a></sup> A number of people, starting with Gerald Kulcinski in 1986,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> have proposed to <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">explore the moon</a>, mine lunar <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> and use the helium-3 for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a>. Because of the low concentrations of helium-3, any mining equipment would need to process extremely large amounts of regolith (over 150  tonnes of regolith to obtain one gram of helium 3),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and some proposals have suggested that helium-3 extraction be piggybacked onto a larger mining and development operation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The primary objective of <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">Indian Space Research Organisation</a>\'s first lunar probe called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-I" title="Chandrayaan-I">Chandrayaan-I</a>, launched on October 22, 2008, was reported in some sources to be mapping the Moon\'s surface for helium-3-containing minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> However, no such objective is mentioned in the project\'s official list of goals, although many of its scientific payloads have noted helium-3-related applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">Cosmochemist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geochemist" title="Geochemist">geochemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ouyang_Ziyuan" title="Ouyang Ziyuan">Ouyang Ziyuan</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Chinese Academy of Sciences">Chinese Academy of Sciences</a> who is now in charge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chang%27e_program" title="Chang\'e program">Chinese Lunar Exploration Program</a> has already stated on many occasions that one of the main goals of the program would be the mining of helium-3, from which operation "each year, three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a> missions could bring enough fuel for all human beings across the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> \nIn January 2006, the Russian space company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RKK_Energiya" title="RKK Energiya">RKK Energiya</a> announced that it considers lunar helium-3 a potential economic resource to be mined by 2020,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> if funding can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mining <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a> for helium-3 has also been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Interplanetary_Society" title="British Interplanetary Society">British Interplanetary Society</a>\'s hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Project_Daedalus" title="Project Daedalus">Project Daedalus</a> interstellar probe design was fueled by helium-3 mines in the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, for example. Jupiter\'s high gravity makes this a less energetically favorable operation than extracting helium-3 from the other gas giants of the solar system, however.\n</p><p>Not all authors feel the extraterrestrial extraction of helium-3 is feasible. Dwayne Day, writing in The Space Review, identifies some major obstacles to helium-3 extraction from extraterrestrial sources for use in fusion, and questions the feasibility of extraterrestrial extraction when compared to production on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_generation">Power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Weasel plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Weasel" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" decoding="async" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">weasel words</a>: vague phrasing that often accompanies <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">biased</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">unverifiable</a> information</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Such statements should be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Improving_weasel-worded_statements" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">clarified or removed</a>.</span> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">March 2013</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A second-generation approach to controlled <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> power involves combining helium-3 (<sup>3</sup><sub>2</sub>He) and <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium"><sup>2</sup><sub>1</sub>H</a>). This reaction produces a <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a> ion (<a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4"><sup>4</sup><sub>2</sub>He</a>) (like an <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particle</a>, but of different origin) and a high-energy <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> (positively charged hydrogen ion) (<sup>1</sup><sub>1</sub>p). The most important potential advantage of this fusion reaction for power production as well as other applications lies in its compatibility with the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatic</a> fields to control fuel <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> and the fusion protons. High speed protons, as positively charged particles, can have their kinetic energy converted directly into <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, through use of <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_chemistry" title="Solid-state chemistry">solid-state</a> conversion materials as well as other techniques. Potential conversion efficiencies of 70% may be possible, as there is no need to convert proton energy to heat in order to drive a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electrical generator</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.\n</p><p>There have been many claims about the capabilities of helium-3 power plants. According to proponents, fusion power plants operating on <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> and helium-3 would offer lower capital and <a href="/wiki/Operating_cost" title="Operating cost">operating costs</a> than their competitors due to less technical complexity, higher conversion efficiency, smaller size, the absence of radioactive fuel, no air or water <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and only low-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> waste disposal requirements. Recent estimates suggest that about $6 billion in <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> <a href="/wiki/Capital_(economics)" title="Capital (economics)">capital</a> will be required to develop and construct the first helium-3 fusion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plant</a>. Financial breakeven at today\'s wholesale <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> prices (5 US cents per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" title="Kilowatt-hour">kilowatt-hour</a>) would occur after five 1-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">gigawatt</a> plants were on line, replacing old conventional plants or meeting new demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The reality is not so clear-cut. The most advanced fusion programs in the world are <a href="/wiki/Inertial_confinement_fusion" title="Inertial confinement fusion">inertial confinement fusion</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/National_Ignition_Facility" title="National Ignition Facility">National Ignition Facility</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_confinement_fusion" title="Magnetic confinement fusion">magnetic confinement fusion</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wendelstein_7-X" title="Wendelstein 7-X">Wendelstein 7-X</a>). In the case of the former, there is no solid roadmap to power generation. In the case of the latter, commercial power generation is not expected until around 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In both cases, the type of fusion discussed is the simplest: D-T fusion. The reason for this is the very low <a href="/wiki/Coulomb_barrier" title="Coulomb barrier">Coulomb barrier</a> for this reaction; for D+<sup>3</sup>He, the barrier is much higher, and it is even higher for <sup>3</sup>He&#8211;<sup>3</sup>He. The immense cost of reactors like <a href="/wiki/ITER" title="ITER">ITER</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Ignition_Facility" title="National Ignition Facility">National Ignition Facility</a> are largely due to their immense size, yet to scale up to higher plasma temperatures would require reactors far larger still. The 14.7 MeV proton and 3.6 MeV alpha particle from D&#8211;<sup>3</sup>He fusion, plus the higher conversion efficiency, means that more electricity is obtained per kilogram than with D-T fusion (17.6 MeV), but not that much more. As a further downside, the rates of reaction for <a href="/wiki/Aneutronic_fusion#Candidate_aneutronic_reactions" title="Aneutronic fusion">helium-3 fusion reactions</a> are not particularly high, requiring a reactor that is larger still or more reactors to produce the same amount of electricity.\n</p><p>To attempt to work around this problem of massively large power plants that may not even be economical with D-T fusion, let alone the far more challenging D&#8211;<sup>3</sup>He fusion, a number of other reactors have been proposed&#160;&#8211; the <a href="/wiki/Fusor" title="Fusor">Fusor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polywell" title="Polywell">Polywell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Focus_fusion" title="Focus fusion">Focus fusion</a>, and many more, though many of these concepts have fundamental problems with achieving a net energy gain, and generally attempt to achieve fusion in thermal disequilibrium, something that could potentially prove impossible,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> and consequently, these long-shot programs tend to have trouble garnering funding despite their low budgets. Unlike the "big", "hot" fusion systems, however, if such systems were to work, they could scale to the higher barrier "<a href="/wiki/Aneutronic_fusion" title="Aneutronic fusion">aneutronic</a>" fuels, and therefore their proponents tend to promote <a href="/wiki/Aneutronic_fusion#Proton&#8211;boron_fusion" title="Aneutronic fusion">p-B fusion</a>, which requires no exotic fuels like helium-3.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_science_fiction">References in science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Helium-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: References in science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> works have featured helium-3 extraction on the moon, including the films <i><a href="/wiki/Moon_(film)" title="Moon (film)">Moon</a></i> (2009) and <i><a href="/wiki/Iron_Sky" title="Iron Sky">Iron Sky</a></i> (2012), the manga and corresponding anime <a href="/wiki/Planetes" title="Planetes">Planetes</a>, the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Anno_2205" title="Anno 2205">Anno 2205</a></i> (2015) and the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Luna:_New_Moon" title="Luna: New Moon">Luna: New Moon</a></i> (2015). The novel <i><a href="/wiki/Morning_Star_(Brown_novel)" title="Morning Star (Brown novel)">Morning Star</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Pierce_Brown" title="Pierce Brown">Pierce Brown</a>, 2016) features helium-3 mining on <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> (a moon of Mars), while his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Rising" title="Red Rising">Red Rising</a></i> (2014) features helium-3 extraction from Mars itself.
