Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Academic discipline" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/School_discipline" title="School discipline">School discipline</a>.</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a topical guide to this subject, see <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_academic_disciplines" title="Outline of academic disciplines">Outline of academic disciplines</a>.</div>\n<p>\nAn <b>academic discipline</b> or <b>academic field</b>, also known as a <b>field of study</b> and <b>branch of knowledge</b>, is a subdivision of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teaching" title="Teaching">taught</a> and <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">researched</a> at the college or university level. Disciplines are defined (in part), and recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journals</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> is published, and the <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">learned societies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academic</a> departments or faculties to which their practitioners belong.\nIt includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_discipline" title="Scientific discipline">scientific disciplines</a>.\n</p><p>It incorporates expertise, people, projects, communities, challenges, studies, inquiry, and research areas that are strongly associated with a given scholastic subject area or college department. For example, the branches of science are commonly referred to as the scientific disciplines, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>.\n</p><p>Individuals associated with academic disciplines are commonly referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">experts</a></i> or <i>specialists</i>. Others, who may have studied <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_education" title="Liberal arts education">liberal arts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">systems theory</a> rather than concentrating in a specific academic discipline, are classified as <i>generalists</i>.\n</p><p>While academic disciplines in and of themselves are more or less focused practices, scholarly approaches such as <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinarity" title="Interdisciplinarity">multidisciplinarity/interdisciplinarity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinarity" title="Transdisciplinarity">transdisciplinarity</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-disciplinarity" title="Cross-disciplinarity">cross-disciplinarity</a> integrate aspects from multiple academic disciplines, therefore addressing any problems that may arise from narrow concentration within specialized fields of study. For example, professionals may encounter trouble communicating across academic disciplines because of differences in language or specified concepts.\n</p><p>Some researchers believe that academic disciplines may be replaced by what is known as <a href="/wiki/Mode_2" title="Mode 2">Mode 2</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or "post-academic science",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which involves the acquisition of cross-disciplinary knowledge through collaboration of specialists from various academic disciplines.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-11"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> in 1231 consisted of four <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(division)" title="Faculty (division)">faculties</a>: <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">Theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">Medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_Law" title="Canon Law">Canon Law</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Arts" title="Liberal Arts">Arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Educational institutions originally used the term "discipline" to catalog and archive the new and expanding body of information produced by the scholarly community. Disciplinary designations originated in German Universities during the beginning of the nineteenth century.\n</p><p>Most academic disciplines have their roots in the mid-to-late-nineteenth century <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a> of universities, when the traditional <a href="/wiki/Curriculum" title="Curriculum">curricula</a> were supplemented with non-classical languages and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literatures" title="Literatures">literatures</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>.\n</p><p>In the early twentieth century, new academic disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> were added. In the 1970s and 1980s, there was an explosion of new academic disciplines focusing on specific themes, such as <a href="/wiki/Media_studies" title="Media studies">media studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_studies" title="Women\'s studies">women\'s studies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africana_studies" title="Africana studies">Africana studies</a>. Many academic disciplines designed as preparation for careers and professions, such as <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">nursing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hospitality_management_studies" title="Hospitality management studies">hospitality management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corrections" title="Corrections">corrections</a>, also emerged in the universities. Finally, interdisciplinary scientific fields of study such as <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a> gained prominence as their contribution to knowledge became widely recognized.\n</p><p>As the twentieth century approached, these designations were gradually adopted by other countries and became the accepted conventional subjects. However, these designations differed between various countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the twentieth century, the natural science disciplines included: <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. The social science disciplines included: <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>.\n</p><p>Prior to the twentieth century, categories were broad and general, which was expected due to the lack of interest in science at the time. With rare exceptions, practitioners of science tended to be amateurs and were referred to as "natural historians" and "natural philosophers"&#8212;labels that date back to Aristotle&#8212;instead of "scientists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Natural history referred to what we now call life sciences and natural philosophy referred to the current physical sciences.\n</p><p>Few opportunities existed for science as an occupation outside the educational system. Higher education provided the institutional structure for scientific investigation, as well as economic support. Soon, the volume of scientific information rapidly increased and people realized the importance of concentrating on smaller fields of scientific activity. Because of this, scientific specializations emerged. As these specializations developed, modern scientific disciplines in universities also improved. Eventually, academia\'s identified disciplines became the foundations for people of specific specialized interests and expertise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions_and_criticism">Functions and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Functions and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A very influential critique of the concept of academic disciplines came from <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> in his 1975 book, <i>Discipline and Punish</i>. Foucault asserts that academic disciplines originate from the same social movements and mechanisms of control that established the modern prison and penal system in eighteenth-century <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and that this fact reveals essential aspects they continue to have in common: "The disciplines characterize, classify, specialize; they distribute along a scale, around a norm, hierarchize individuals in relation to one another and, if necessary, disqualify and invalidate." (Foucault, 1975/1979, p. 223)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities_of_academic_disciplines">Communities of academic disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Communities of academic disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Communities of academic disciplines can be found outside academia within corporations, government agencies, and independent organizations, where they take the form of associations of professionals with common interests and specific knowledge. Such communities include corporate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Think_tanks" title="Think tanks">think tanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a>. Communities such as these exist to benefit the organizations affiliated with them by providing specialized new ideas, research, and findings.\n</p><p>Nations at various developmental stages will find need for different academic disciplines during different times of growth. A newly developing nation will likely prioritize government and political matters over those of the arts and sciences. On the other hand, a well-developed nation may be capable of investing more into the arts and sciences. Communities of academic disciplines would contribute at varying levels of importance during different stages of development.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions">Interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>These categories explain how the different academic disciplines interact with one another.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multidisciplinary">Multidisciplinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multidisciplinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_approach" title="Multidisciplinary approach">Multidisciplinary approach</a></div>\n<p>Multidisciplinary knowledge is associated with more than one existing academic discipline or profession.\n</p><p>A multidisciplinary community or project is made up of people from different academic disciplines and professions. These people are engaged in working together as equal stakeholders in addressing a common challenge. A multidisciplinary person is one with degrees from two or more academic disciplines. This one person can take the place of two or more people in a multidisciplinary community. Over time, multidisciplinary work does not typically lead to an increase or a decrease in the number of academic disciplines. One key question is how well the challenge can be decomposed into subparts, and then addressed via the distributed knowledge in the community. The lack of shared vocabulary between people and communication overhead can sometimes be an issue in these communities and projects. If challenges of a particular type need to be repeatedly addressed so that each one can be properly decomposed, a multidisciplinary community can be exceptionally efficient and effective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>There are many examples of a particular idea appearing in different academic disciplines, all of which came about around the same time. One example of this scenario is the shift from the approach of focusing on sensory awareness of the whole, "an attention to the \'total field<span style="padding-right:.15em;">\'</span>", a "sense of the whole pattern, of form and function as a unity", an "integral idea of structure and configuration". This has happened in art (in the form of cubism), physics, poetry, communication and educational theory. According to <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a>, this paradigm shift was due to the passage from the era of mechanization, which brought sequentiality, to the era of the instant speed of electricity, which brought simultaneity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Multidisciplinary approaches also encourage people to help shape the innovation of the future. The political dimensions of forming new multidisciplinary partnerships to solve the so-called societal Grand Challenges were presented in the Innovation Union and in the European Framework Programme, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizon_2020" title="Horizon 2020">Horizon 2020</a> operational overlay. Innovation across academic disciplines is considered the pivotal foresight of the creation of new products, systems, and processes for the benefit of all societies\' growth and wellbeing. Regional examples such as Biopeople and industry-academia initiatives in translational medicine such as SHARE.ku.dk in Denmark provides the evidence of the successful endeavour of multidisciplinary innovation and facilitation of the paradigm shift.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transdisciplinary">Transdisciplinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transdisciplinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transdisciplinarity" title="Transdisciplinarity">Transdisciplinarity</a></div>\n<p>In practice, transdisciplinary can be thought of as the union of all interdisciplinary efforts. While interdisciplinary teams may be creating new knowledge that lies between several existing disciplines, a transdisciplinary team is more holistic and seeks to relate all disciplines into a coherent whole.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-disciplinary">Cross-disciplinary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cross-disciplinary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Cross-disciplinary</b> knowledge is that which explains aspects of one discipline in terms of another. Common examples of cross-disciplinary approaches are studies of the <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibliometric_studies_of_disciplines">Bibliometric studies of disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bibliometric studies of disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Bibliometrics" title="Bibliometrics">Bibliometrics</a> can be used to map several issues in relation to disciplines, for example the flow of ideas within and among disciplines (Lindholm-Romantschuk, 1998)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or the existence of specific national traditions within disciplines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Scholarly impact and influence of one discipline on another may be understood by analyzing the flow of citations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Bibliometrics approach is described as straightforward because it is based on simple counting. The method is also objective but the quantitative method may not be compatible with a qualitative assessment and therefore manipulated. The number of citations is dependent on the number of persons working in the same domain instead of inherent quality or published result&#8217;s originality<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discipline_(academia)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Gibbons, Michael; Camille Limoges, Helga Nowotny, Simon Schwartzman, Peter Scott, &amp; Martin Trow (1994). <i>The New Production of Knowledge: The Dynamics of Science and Research in Contemporary Societies</i>.
