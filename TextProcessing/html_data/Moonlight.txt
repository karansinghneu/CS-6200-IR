class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Illumination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The intensity of moonlight varies greatly depending on <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">its phase</a>, but even the <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full Moon</a> typically provides only about 0.05&#8211;0.1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Lux" title="Lux">lux</a> illumination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supermoon_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-supermoon-2">[2]</a></sup> When the full Moon is at <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">perigee</a> and viewed around upper <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">culmination</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> can reach up to 0.32&#160;lux.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supermoon_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-supermoon-2">[2]</a></sup> From Earth, the <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of the full Moon is only about &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>380,000</sub></span> that of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The color of moonlight, particularly around full moon, appears bluish to the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a> compared to most artificial light sources due to the <a href="/wiki/Purkinje_effect" title="Purkinje effect">Purkinje effect</a>. Moonlight is not actually tinted blue, and although moonlight is often referred to as "silvery", it has no inherent silvery quality.\n</p><p>The Moon\'s <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> is 0.136,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saari_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saari-3">[3]</a></sup> meaning only 13.6% of incident sunlight is reflected from the lunar surface. Moonlight generally hampers astronomical viewing, so astronomers usually avoid observing sessions around full Moon. It takes approximately 1.26 seconds for moonlight to reach Earth\'s surface. \n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px"><div style="width: class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In folklore, moonlight sometimes has a harmful influence. For example, sleeping in the light of a full Moon on certain nights was said to transform a person into a <a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">werewolf</a>. The light of the Moon was thought to worsen the symptoms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunatics" title="Lunatics">lunatics</a>, and to sleep in moonlight could make one blind, or mad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nyctalopia" title="Nyctalopia">Nyctalopia</a> (night blindness caused by a lack of vitamin A) was thought to be caused by sleeping in moonlight in the tropics.\n</p><p>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_blindness" title="Moon blindness">Moon blindness</a>" is a name for equine recurrent uveitis. Moonlight is no longer thought of as the cause.\n</p><p>In the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Moonmilk" title="Moonmilk">moonmilk</a>, a soft white limestone precipitate found in caves, was thought to be caused by the rays of the moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gessner_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gessner-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Moonlight_in_art">Moonlight in
