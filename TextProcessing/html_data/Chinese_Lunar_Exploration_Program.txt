Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">&#20013;&#22269;&#25506;&#26376;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zh&#333;nggu&#243; T&#224;nyu&#232;</span></i>), also known as the <b>Chang\'e Project</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">&#23270;&#23077;&#24037;&#31243;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Ch&#225;ng\'&#233; G&#333;ngch&#233;ng</span></i>) after the Chinese moon goddess <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e" title="Chang\'e">Chang\'e</a>, is an ongoing series of <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">Moon missions</a> by the <a href="/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration" title="China National Space Administration">China National Space Administration</a> (CNSA). The program incorporates <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">landers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">rovers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">sample return spacecraft</a>, launched using <a href="/wiki/Long_March_(rocket_family)" title="Long March (rocket family)">Long March rockets</a>. Launches and flights are monitored by a Telemetry, Tracking, and Command (TT&amp;C) system, which uses 50-metre (160-foot) radio antennas in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and 40-metre (130-foot) antennas in <a href="/wiki/Kunming" title="Kunming">Kunming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi" title="&#220;r&#252;mqi">&#220;r&#252;mqi</a> to form a 3,000-kilometre (1,900-mile) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLBI" title="VLBI">VLBI</a> antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A proprietary ground application system is responsible for downlink data reception.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ouyang_Ziyuan" title="Ouyang Ziyuan">Ouyang Ziyuan</a>, a geologist and <a href="/wiki/Cosmochemistry" title="Cosmochemistry">chemical cosmologist</a>, was among the first to advocate the exploitation not only of known lunar reserves of metals such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>, but also of <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">helium-3</a>, an ideal fuel for future <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> power plants. He currently serves as the chief scientist of the Chinese Lunar Exploration Program. Another scientist, <a href="/wiki/Sun_Jiadong" title="Sun Jiadong">Sun Jiadong</a>, was assigned as the general designer, while scientist Sun Zezhou was assigned as the deputy general designer. The leading program manager is Luan Enjie.\n</p><p>The first spacecraft of the program, the <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_1" title="Chang\'e 1">Chang\'e 1</a> lunar orbiter, was launched from <a href="/wiki/Xichang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Xichang Satellite Launch Center">Xichang Satellite Launch Center</a> on 24 October 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> having been delayed from the initial planned date of 17&#8211;19 April 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A second orbiter, <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_2" title="Chang\'e 2">Chang\'e 2</a>, was launched on 1 October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfnow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfnow-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bare_url_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang\'e 3">Chang\'e 3</a>, which includes a lander and rover, was launched on 1 December 2013 and successfully soft-landed on the Moon on 14 December 2013. <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_4" title="Chang\'e 4">Chang\'e 4</a>, which includes a lander and rover, was launched on 7 December 2018. A sample return mission, <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5" title="Chang\'e 5">Chang\'e 5</a>, is scheduled for December 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As indicated by the official insignia, the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calligraphy" title="Chinese calligraphy">calligraphic</a> nascent lunar crescent with two human footprints at its center reminiscent of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">Chinese character</a> <span lang="zh-CN" title="Chinese language text">&#26376;</span>, the Chinese character for "Month" which is shorthand for "Moon" (<a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%88%E7%90%83" title="zh:&#26376;&#29699;">&#26376;&#29699;</a>), the ultimate objective of the program is to pave the way for a <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">manned mission</a> to the Moon. Such a mission may occur in 2025&#8211;2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manned_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manned-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Program structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Chinese Lunar Exploration Program is divided into four main operational phases, with each mission serving as a technology demonstrator in preparation for future missions. International cooperation in the form of various payloads and a robotic station is invited by China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jan_2019_press_conference_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jan_2019_press_conference-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg/300px-Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg/450px-Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg/600px-Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_and_the_Moon_from_Chang%27e_5_T1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5-T1" title="Chang\'e 5-T1">Chang\'e 5-T1</a> test vehicle service module took this photo of Earth and the Moon together on November 9, 2014, the service module carrying the camera in 540,000 km from Earth, at a distance of 920,000 kilometers moon shot clear of the Earth-Moon photo images. a week after successfully returning the sample capsule. The Moon\'s surface is quite dark compared to Earth\'s bright clouds.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_I:_Orbital_missions">Phase I: Orbital missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phase I: Orbital missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first phase entailed the launch of two <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbiters</a>, and is now effectively complete.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_1" title="Chang\'e 1">Chang\'e 1</a>, launched on 24 October 2007 aboard a <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3A" title="Long March 3A">Long March 3A</a> rocket, scanned the entire Moon in unprecedented detail, generating a high definition 3D map that would provide a reference for future soft landings. The probe also mapped the abundance and distribution of various chemical elements on the lunar surface as part of an evaluation of potentially useful resources.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_2" title="Chang\'e 2">Chang\'e 2</a>, launched on 1 October 2010 aboard a <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3C" title="Long March 3C">Long March 3C</a> rocket, reached the Moon in under 5&#160;days, compared to 12&#160;days for Chang\'e 1, and mapped the Moon in even greater detail. It then left lunar orbit and headed for the Earth&#8211;Sun L2 <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a> in order to test the TT&amp;C network. Having done that it completed a flyby of asteroid <a href="/wiki/4179_Toutatis" title="4179 Toutatis">4179 Toutatis</a> on 13 December 2012, before heading into deep space to further test the TT&amp;C network.</li></ul>\n<h3><span id="Phase_II:_Soft_landers.2Frovers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_II:_Soft_landers/rovers">Phase II: Soft landers/rovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phase II: Soft landers/rovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The second phase is ongoing, and incorporates spacecraft capable of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft-landing</a> on the Moon and deploying <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">lunar rovers</a>.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang\'e 3">Chang\'e 3</a>, launched on 2 December 2013 aboard a <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3B" title="Long March 3B">Long March 3B</a> rocket, landed on the Moon on 14 December 2013. It carried with it a 140&#160;kg (310&#160;lb) lunar rover named <a href="/wiki/Yutu_(rover)" title="Yutu (rover)"><i>Yutu</i></a>, which was designed to explore an area of 3 square kilometres (1.2&#160;sq&#160;mi) during a 3-month mission. In addition, the Chang\'e probes had to carry out many attitude maneuvers during their flights to the Moon and during operations in lunar orbit. The distance across China from east to west is 5,000&#160;km (3,100&#160;mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geography_statistics_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-geography_statistics-23">[23]</a></sup> forming another challenge to TT&amp;C continuity. At present, the combination of the TT&amp;C system and the Chinese astronomical observation network has met the needs of the Chang\'e program,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> but only by a small margin.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_adaptability">Environmental adaptability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental adaptability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The complexity of the <a href="/wiki/Space_environment" title="Space environment">space environment</a> encountered during the Chang\'e missions imposed strict requirements for environmental adaptability and reliability of the probes and their instruments. The high-radiation environment in Earth-Moon space required <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">hardened</a> electronics to prevent electromagnetic damage to spacecraft instruments. The extreme temperature range, from 130&#160;&#176;C (266&#160;&#176;F) on the side of the spacecraft facing the Sun to &#8722;170&#160;&#176;C (&#8722;274&#160;&#176;F) on the side facing away from the Sun, imposed strict requirements for temperature control in the design of the detectors.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_design_and_flight_sequence_control">Orbit design and flight sequence control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Orbit design and flight sequence control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Given the conditions of the three-body system of the Earth, Moon and a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a>, the orbit design of lunar orbiters is more complicated than that of Earth-orbiting satellites, which only deal with a two-body system. The Chang\'e 1 and Chang\'e 2 probes were first sent into highly elliptical Earth orbits. After separating from their launch vehicles, they entered an Earth-Moon transfer orbit through three accelerations in the phase-modulated orbit. These accelerations were conducted 16, 24, and 48 hours into the missions, during which several orbit adjustments and attitude maneuvers were carried out so as to ensure the probes\' capture by lunar gravity. After operating in the Earth-Moon orbit for 4&#8211;5 days, each probe entered a lunar acquisition orbit. After entering their target orbits, conducting three braking maneuvers and experiencing three different orbit phases, Chang\'e 1 and Chang\'e 2 carried out their missions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitude_control">Attitude control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Attitude control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lunar orbiters have to remain properly oriented with respect to the Earth, Moon and Sun. All onboard detectors must be kept facing the lunar surface in order to complete their scientific missions, communication antennas have to face the Earth in order to receive commands and transfer scientific data, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> must be oriented toward the Sun in order to acquire power. During lunar orbit, the Earth, the Moon and the Sun also move, so <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a> is a complex three-vector control process. The Chang\'e satellites need to adjust their attitude very carefully to maintain an optimal angle towards all three bodies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazard_avoidance">Hazard avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hazard avoidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the second phase of the program, in which the spacecraft were required to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft-land</a> on the lunar surface, it was necessary to devise a system of automatic hazard avoidance in order that the landers would not attempt to touch down on unsuitable terrain. Chang\'e 3 utilized a <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">computer vision</a> system in which the data from a down-facing camera, as well as 2 ranging devices, were processed using specialized software. The software controlled the final stages of descent, adjusting the attitude of the spacecraft and the throttle of its main engine. The spacecraft hovered first at 100 metres (330&#160;ft), then at 30 metres (98&#160;ft), as it searched for a suitable spot to set down. The <i>Yutu</i> rover is also equipped with front-facing stereo cameras and hazard avoidance technology.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooperation_with_Russia">Cooperation with Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit
