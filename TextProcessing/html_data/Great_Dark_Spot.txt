tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neptune%27s_Great_Dark_Spot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="852" data-file-width="700" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neptune%27s_Great_Dark_Spot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Great Dark Spot as seen from <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Great Dark Spot</b> (also known as <b>GDS-89</b>, for <b>G</b>reat <b>D</b>ark <b>S</b>pot - 19<b>89</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammel_1995_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammel_1995-1">[1]</a></sup> was one of a series of dark spots on <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> similar in appearance to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a>. GDS-89  was the first Great Dark Spot on Neptune to be observed in 1989 by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> spaceprobe. Like Jupiter\'s spot, Great Dark Spots are <a href="/wiki/Anticyclonic_storm" title="Anticyclonic storm">anticyclonic storms</a>. However, their interiors are relatively cloud-free, and unlike Jupiter\'s spot, which has lasted for hundreds of years, their lifetimes appear to be shorter, forming and dissipating once every few years or so. Based on observations taken with <i>Voyager 2</i> and since then with the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, Neptune appears to spend somewhat more than half its time with a Great Dark Spot.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Characteristics"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Characteristics</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Disappearance"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Disappearance</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Dark_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The dark, <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptically</a>-shaped spot (with initial dimensions of 13,000 &#215; 6,600&#160;km, or 8,100 &#215; 4,100&#160;mi) of GDS-89 was about the same size as <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and was similar in general appearance to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a>. Around it, winds were measured blowing up to 2,400 kilometers (1,500&#160;mi) an hour, the fastest in the Solar System.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Great Dark Spot is thought to represent a hole in the <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> cloud deck of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. The spot was observed at different times with different sizes and shapes.\n</p><p>The Great Dark Spot generated large white clouds at or just below the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> layer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stratman_2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stratman_2001-2">[2]</a></sup> similar to high-altitude <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cirrus_clouds" title="Cirrus clouds">cirrus clouds</a> found on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Unlike the clouds on Earth, however, which are composed of crystals of ice, Neptune\'s cirrus clouds are made up of crystals of frozen <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. And while cirrus clouds usually form and then disperse within a period of a few hours, the clouds in the Great Dark Spot were still present after 36 hours, or two rotations of the planet.\n</p><p>Neptune\'s dark spots are thought to occur in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> at lower altitudes than the brighter upper cloud deck features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbard_2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbard_2003-3">[3]</a></sup> As they are stable features that can persist for several months, they are thought to be vortex structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj125_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj125-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">Disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Dark_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dark_spot_on_Neptune.jpg"><img alt="" href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>When the spot was to be photographed again in November 1994 by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>, it had disappeared completely, leaving astronomers to believe that it has either been covered up or vanished. The persistence of companion clouds shows that some former dark spots may continue to exist as cyclones even though they are no longer visible as a dark feature. Dark spots may dissipate when they migrate too close to the equator, or possibly through some other unknown mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sromovsky_2000_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sromovsky_2000-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, in 2016 an almost identical spot emerged in Neptune\'s northern hemisphere. This new spot, called the <b>Northern Great Dark Spot</b> (<b>NGDS</b>), has remained visible for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarviews_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-solarviews-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubblesite_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hubblesite-8">[8]</a></sup> It is unknown whether this spot is still present on the planet, as observations using the Hubble telescope are limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Dark_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Storm_(astronomy)" title="Dragon Storm (astronomy)">Dragon Storm (astronomy)</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_cyclone" title="Extraterrestrial cyclone">Extraterrestrial cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_BA" title="Oval BA">Oval BA</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Small_Dark_Spot" title="Small Dark Spot">Small Dark Spot</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Dark_Spot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Hammel_1995-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Hammel_1995_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">\n<cite class="citation journal">Hammel, H.
