onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">its atmosphere</a> and into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>, toward the <a During <a href="/wiki/Twilight#Civil_twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> (both early <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a> and late <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a>), the shadow\'s visible fringe (sometimes called the <b>dark segment</b> or <b>twilight wedge</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) appears in a clear <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> as a dark and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse" title="Diffuse">diffused</a> band low above the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>.\n</p>\n<div tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golden_Gate_Bridge,_the_Belt_of_Venus_and_the_Earth%27s_shadow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" href="/wiki/Marin_Headlands" title="Marin Headlands">Marin Headlands</a> just north of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in October 2010.<br/><span style="font-size:85%;">(Note: A thin layer of greyish cloud partially obscures the horizon in this image.)</span></div></div></div>\n<p>Earth\'s shadow cast onto the atmosphere can be viewed during the "civil" stage of <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>, assuming the sky is clear and the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> is relatively unobstructed. The shadow\'s fringe appears as a dark bluish to purplish band that stretches over <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">180&#176;</a> of the horizon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth\'s_shadow_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-earth\'s_shadow-3">[3]</a></sup> opposite the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, i.e. in the eastern sky at <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a> and in the western sky at <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a>. Before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>, Earth\'s shadow appears to recede as the Sun rises; after <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>, the shadow appears to rise as the Sun sets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AO_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AO-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Earth\'s shadow is best seen when the horizon is low, such as over the sea, and when the sky conditions are clear. In addition, the higher the observer\'s <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> is to view the horizon, the sharper the shadow appears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AO_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth\'s_shadow_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-earth\'s_shadow-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Belt_of_Venus">Belt of Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Belt of Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Belt_of_Venus" title="Belt of Venus">Belt of Venus</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg/250px-Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg/375px-Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg/500px-Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_and_red_blue_haze.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">Full moon</a> rising, as seen through the Belt of Venus. A very small part of the Earth\'s shadow (dark blue) is also visible in this image, but the horizon here is too high for more of the Earth\'s shadow to be seen.</div></div></div>\n<p>A related phenomenon in the same part of the sky is the <a href="/wiki/Belt_of_Venus" title="Belt of Venus">Belt of Venus</a>, or anti-twilight arch, a pinkish band visible above the bluish shade of Earth\'s shadow, named after the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> which, when visible, is typically located in this region of the sky.\nNo <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">defined line</a> divides the Earth\'s shadow and the Belt of Venus; one colored band blends into the other in the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AO_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-AO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth\'s_shadow_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-earth\'s_shadow-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Belt of Venus is quite a different phenomenon from the <a href="/wiki/Afterglow" title="Afterglow">afterglow</a>, which appears in the geometrically opposite part of the sky.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the Sun is near the horizon around <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>, the sunlight appears reddish. This is because the light rays are penetrating an especially <a href="/wiki/Air_mass_(astronomy)" title="Air mass (astronomy)">thick layer</a> of the atmosphere, which works as a filter, <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles" title="Light scattering by particles">scattering</a> all but the longer (redder) wavelengths.\n</p><p>From the observer\'s perspective, the red sunlight directly illuminates <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">small particles</a> in the lower atmosphere in the sky opposite of the Sun. The red light is <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">backscattered</a> to the observer, which is the reason why the <a href="/wiki/Belt_of_Venus" title="Belt of Venus">Belt of Venus</a> appears pink.\n</p><p>The lower the setting Sun descends, the less defined the boundary between Earth\'s shadow and the Belt of Venus appears. This is because the setting Sun now illuminates a thinner part of the upper atmosphere. There the red light is not scattered because fewer particles are present, and the eye only sees the "normal" (usual) blue sky, which is due to <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> from air molecules. Eventually, both Earth\'s shadow and the Belt of Venus dissolve into the darkness of the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth\'s_shadow_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-earth\'s_shadow-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Color_of_lunar_eclipses">Color of lunar eclipses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth%27s_shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Color of lunar eclipse">Lunar eclipse</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Near_Greatest_Eclipse_20101221_0011-crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="881" data-file-width="881" decoding="async" height="200" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Near_Greatest_Eclipse_20101221_0011-crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A total <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar surface.</div></div></div>\n<p>Earth\'s shadow is as curved as the planet is, and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">umbra</a> extends 1,400,000&#160;km (870,000&#160;mi) into outer space. (The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antumbra" title="Antumbra">antumbra</a>, however, extends indefinitely.) When the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, Earth, and the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> are <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">aligned perfectly</a> (or nearly so), with Earth between the Sun and the Moon, Earth\'s shadow falls onto the lunar surface facing the night side of the planet, such that the shadow gradually darkens the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_Moon" title="Full Moon">full Moon</a>, causing a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a>.\n</p><p>Even during a total lunar eclipse, a small amount of sunlight however still reaches the Moon. This indirect sunlight has been <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">refracted</a> as it passed through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>. The air molecules and <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a> in Earth\'s atmosphere <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raleigh_scattering" title="Raleigh scattering">scatter</a> the shorter wavelengths of this sunlight; thus, the longer wavelengths of reddish light reaches the Moon, in the same way that light at <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> appears reddish.
