href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">&#24373;&#39467;</span>; d. 113&#160;BC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELoewe2000688_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELoewe2000688-1">[1]</a></sup> was a Chinese official and diplomat who served as an imperial envoy to the world outside of China in the late 2nd century BC during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>. He was the first official diplomat to bring back reliable information about <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> to the Chinese imperial court, then under <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu of Han</a>, and played an important pioneering role in the Chinese colonization and conquest of the region now known as <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>.\n</p><p>Today Zhang Qian\'s travels are associated with the major route of transcontinental trade, the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>. In essence, his missions opened up to China the many kingdoms and products of a part of the world then unknown to the Chinese. Zhang Qian\'s accounts of his explorations of Central Asia are detailed in the Early Han historical chronicles, <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i>, compiled by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> in the 1st century BC. The <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asian</a> sections of the Silk Road routes were expanded around 114 BC largely through the missions and explorations of Zhang Qian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boulnois_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-boulnois-2">[2]</a></sup> Today Qian is considered a national hero and revered for the key role he played in opening China to the world href="/wiki/File:Statue_of_Zhang_Qian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Zhang Qian in <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi_History_Museum" title="Shaanxi History Museum">Shaanxi History Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi\'an">Xi\'an</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Zhang Qian was born in Chenggu district just east of <a href="/wiki/Hanzhong" title="Hanzhong">Hanzhong</a> in the north central province of <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He entered the capital, Chang\'an, today\'s <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi\'an">Xi\'an</a>, between 140 BC and 134 BC as a Gentleman (&#37070;), serving <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a>. At the time the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> tribes controlled what is now <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> and dominated the <a href="/wiki/Western_Regions" title="Western Regions">Western Regions</a>, Xiyu (&#35199;&#22495;), the areas neighbouring the territory of the Han Dynasty. The Han emperor was interested in establishing commercial ties with distant lands but outside contact was prevented by the hostile Xiongnu.\n</p><p>The Han court dispatched Zhang Qian, a military officer who was familiar with the Xiongnu, to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Regions" title="Western Regions">Western Regions</a> in 138 BC with a group of ninety-nine members to make contact and build an alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> against the Xiongnu. He was accompanied by a guide named Ganfu (&#29976;&#29238;), a Xiongnu who had been captured in war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The objective of Zhang Qian\'s first mission was to seek a military alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in modern <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>. However to get to the territory of the Yuezhi he was forced to pass through land controlled by the Xiongnu who captured him (as well as Ganfu) and enslaved him for ten years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> During this time he married a Xiongnu wife, who bore him a son, and gained the trust of the Xiongnu leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mayor2014_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mayor2014-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Zhang and Ganfu (as well as Zhang\'s Xiongnu wife and son) were eventually able to escape and, passing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lop_Nor" title="Lop Nor">Lop Nor</a> and following the northern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>, around the <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">Kunlun Mountains</a> and through small fortified areas in the middle of oases in what is now <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> until they made their way to <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> and eventually to the land of the Yuezhi. The Yuezhi were agricultural people who produced strong horses and many unknown crops including <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> for animal <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a>. However, the Yuezhi were too settled to desire war against the Xiongnu. Zhang spent a year in Yuezhi and the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactrian</a> territory, documenting their cultures, lifestyles and economy, before beginning his return trip to China, this time following the southern edge of the Tarim Basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\nOn his return trip he was again captured by the Xiongnu who again spared his life because they valued his sense of duty and composure in the face of death. Two years later the Xiongnu leader died and in the midst of chaos and infighting Zhang Qian escaped. Of the original mission of just over a hundred men, only Zhang Qian and Ganfu managed to return to China.\n</p><p>Zhang Qian returned in 125 BC with detailed news for the Emperor, showing that sophisticated civilizations existed to the West, with which China could advantageously develop relations. The Shiji relates that "the Emperor learned of the Dayuan (&#22823;&#23451;), Daxia (&#22823;&#22799;), Anxi (&#23433;&#24687;), and the others, all great states rich in unusual products whose people cultivated the land and made their living in much the same way as the Chinese. All these states, he was told, were militarily weak and prized Han goods and wealth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Upon Zhang Qian\'s return to China he was honoured with a position of palace counselor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although he was unable to develop commercial ties between China and these far-off lands, his efforts did eventually result in trade mission to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wu-sun" title="Wu-sun">Wu-sun</a> people in 119 BC which led to trade between China and <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persia</a>.\n</p><p>On his mission Zhang Qian had noticed products from an area now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_India" title="Northern India">northern India</a>. However, the task remained to find a trade route not obstructed by the Xiongnu to India. Zhang Qian set out on a second mission to forge a route from China to India via <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>, but after many attempts this effort proved unsuccessful. In 119&#8211;115 BC Zhang Qian was sent on a third mission by the emperor, to develop ties with the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a> (&#28879;&#23403;) people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Zhang_Qian.27s_reports"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Zhang_Qian\'s_reports">Zhang Qian\'s reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Zhang Qian\'s reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Han_Expansion.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="902" data-file-width="1275" decoding="async" height="354" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Han_Expansion.png/500px-Han_Expansion.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Han_Expansion.png/750px-Han_Expansion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Han_Expansion.png/1000px-Han_Expansion.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Han_Expansion.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zhang Qian\'s travels to the west</div></div></div>\n<p>The reports of Zhang Qian\'s travels are quoted extensively in the 1st century BC Chinese historic chronicles "Records of the Great Historian" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>. Zhang Qian visited directly the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> (&#22823;&#23451;) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>, the territories of the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> (&#26376;&#27663;) in <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactrian</a> country of <a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a> (&#22823;&#22799;) with its remnants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> rule, and <a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a> (&#24247;&#23621;). He also made reports on neighbouring countries that he did not visit, such as Anxi (&#23433;&#24687;) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsacid_Empire" title="Arsacid Empire">Arsacid</a> territories), Tiaozhi (&#26781;&#25903;/&#26465;&#25903;) (<a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>), Shendu (&#36523;&#27602;) (<a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a> (&#28879;&#23403;).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Dayuan_.28.E5.A4.A7.E5.AE.9B.29_.28Ferghana.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dayuan_(&#22823;&#23451;)_(Ferghana)">Dayuan (&#22823;&#23451;) (Ferghana)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dayuan (&#22823;&#23451;) (Ferghana)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:351px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZhangQianTravel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="672" decoding="async" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/ZhangQianTravel.jpg/349px-ZhangQianTravel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/ZhangQianTravel.jpg/524px-ZhangQianTravel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/ZhangQianTravel.jpg 2x" width="349"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZhangQianTravel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Countries described in Zhang Qian\'s report. Visited countries are highlighted in <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>After being released from captivity by Xiongnu, Zhang Qian visited <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Fergana" title="Fergana">Fergana</a> region west of the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>. The people of Dayuan were being portrayed as sophisticated urban dwellers similar to the Parthians and the Bactrians. The name Dayuan is thought to be a transliteration of the word <i><a href="/wiki/Yona" title="Yona">Yona</a></i>, the Greek descendants that occupied the region from the 4th to the 2nd century BCE. It was during this stay that Zhang reported the famous tall and powerful "blood-sweating" <a href="/wiki/Ferghana_horse" title="Ferghana horse">Ferghana horse</a>. The refusal by Dayuan to offer these horses to Emperor Wu of Han resulted in two <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Heavenly_Horses" title="War of the Heavenly Horses">punitive campaigns</a> launched by the Han Dynasty to acquire these horses by force.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Dayuan lies southwest of the territory of the Xiongnu, some 10,000 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Chinese_unit)" title="Li (Chinese unit)">li</a></i> (5,000 kilometers) directly west of China. The people are settled on the land, plowing the fields and growing rice and wheat. They also make wine out of grapes. The people live in houses in fortified cities, there being some seventy or more cities of various sizes in the region. The population numbers several hundred thousand" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Rouzhi_.28.E6.9C.88.E6.B0.8F.29_.28Yuezhi.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rouzhi_(&#26376;&#27663;)_(Yuezhi)">Rouzhi (&#26376;&#27663;) (Yuezhi)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rouzhi (&#26376;&#27663;) (Yuezhi)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After obtaining the help of the king of Dayuan, Zhang Qian went southwest to the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a>, with whom he was supposed to obtain a military alliance against the Xiongnu.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"The Great Yuezhi live some 2,000 or 3,000 li (1,000 or 1,500 kilometers) west of Dayuan, north of the Gui (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>) river. They are bordered to the south by Daxia (Bactria), on the west by <a href="#Anxi">Anxi</a>, and on the north by <a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju (&#24247;&#23621;)</a>. They are a nation of nomads, moving place to place with their herds and their customs are like those of the Xiongnu. They have some 100,000 or 200,000 archer warriors." (adapted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<p>Zhang Qian also describes the origins of the Yuezhi, explaining they came from the eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>.  This has encouraged some historians to connect them to the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_mummies" title="Tarim mummies">Caucasoid mummies of the Tarim</a>. (The question of links between the Yuezhi and the <a href="/wiki/Tocharians" title="Tocharians">Tocharians</a>  of the Tarim is more controversial.)\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"The Yuezhi originally lived in the area between the Qilian or Heavenly Mountains (<a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a>, but after they were defeated by the Xiongnu they moved far away to the west, beyond Dayuan (Ferghana), where they attacked the people of Daxia (Bactria) and set up the court of their king on the northern bank of the Gui (Oxus) river." (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<p>A smaller group of Yuezhi, the "Little Yuezhi", were not able to follow the exodus and reportedly found refuge among the "<a href="/wiki/Qiang_people" title="Qiang people">Qiang</a> barbarians".\n</p><p>Zhang was the first Chinese to write about one humped dromedary camels which he saw in this region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Daxia_.28.E5.A4.A7.E5.A4.8F.29_.28Bactria.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Daxia_(&#22823;&#22799;)_(Bactria)">Daxia (&#22823;&#22799;) (Bactria)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Daxia (&#22823;&#22799;) (Bactria)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Zhang Qian probably witnessed the last period of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>, as it was being subjugated by the nomadic Yuezhi. Only small powerless chiefs remained, who were apparently vassals to the Yuezhi horde. Their civilization was urban, almost identical to the civilizations of <a href="#Anxi">Anxi</a> and Dayuan, and the population was numerous.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Daxia is situated over 2,000 <i>li</i> (1,000 kilometers) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> (Ferghana), south of the Gui (Oxus) river. Its people cultivate the land, and have cities and houses. Their customs are like those of Dayuan. It has no great ruler but only a number of petty chiefs ruling the various cities. The people are poor in the use of arms and afraid of battle, but they are clever at commerce. After the Great Yuezhi moved west and attacked and conquered Daxia, the entire country came under their sway. The population of the country is large, numbering some 1,000,000 or more persons. The capital is Lanshi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactra" title="Bactra">Bactra</a>) where all sorts of goods are bought and sold." (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, translation Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<p>Cloth from Shu (<a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>) was found there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hargett2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hargett2006-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Shendu_.28.E8.BA.AB.E6.AF.92.29_.28India.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shendu_(&#36523;&#27602;)_(India)">Shendu (&#36523;&#27602;) (India)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shendu (&#36523;&#27602;) (India)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Zhang Qian also reports about the existence of <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">India</a> southeast of Bactria. The name <i>Shendu</i> (&#36523;&#27602;) comes from the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word "Sindhu", meaning the Indus river of Pakistan. Sindh was one of the richest regions of India at the time, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">Indo-Greek Kingdoms</a>, which explains the reported cultural similarity between Bactria and India:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Southeast of Daxia is the kingdom of Shendu (Sindh)... Shendu, they told me, lies several thousand <i>li</i> southeast of Daxia (Bactria). The people cultivate the land and live much like the people of Daxia. The region is said to be hot and damp. The inhabitants ride elephants when they go in battle. The kingdom is situated on a great river (<a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus</a>)" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Anxi_.28.E5.AE.89.E6.81.AF.29_.28Parthia.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Anxi_(&#23433;&#24687;)_(Parthia)">Anxi <span id="Anxi"></span> (&#23433;&#24687;) (Parthia)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anxi (&#23433;&#24687;) (Parthia)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Zhang Qian identifies "Anxi" (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant"><a class="extiw" href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%AE%89%E6%81%AF%E5%B8%9D%E5%9C%8B" title="zh:&#23433;&#24687;&#24093;&#22283;">&#23433;&#24687;</a></span>) as an advanced urban civilization, like Dayuan (Ferghana) and Daxia (Bactria). The name "Anxi" is a transcription of "<a href="/wiki/Arsaces_I_of_Parthia" title="Arsaces I of Parthia">Arshak</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Arsaces" title="Arsaces">Arsaces</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the name of the founder of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsacid_Empire" title="Arsacid Empire">Arsacid Empire</a> that ruled the regions along the silk road between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tedzhen_river" title="Tedzhen river">Tedzhen river</a> in the east and the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> in the west, and running through <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">Aria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> proper, and <a href="/wiki/Medes" title="Medes">Media</a> proper.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Anxi is situated several thousand <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Chinese_unit)" title="Li (Chinese unit)">li</a></i> west of the region of the Great Yuezhi. The people are settled on the land, cultivating the fields and growing rice and wheat. They also make wine out of grapes. They have walled cities like the people of Dayuan (Ferghana), the region contains several hundred cities of various sizes. The coins of the country are made of silver and bear the face of the king. When the king dies, the currency is immediately changed and new coins issued with the face of his successor. The people keep records by writing on horizontal strips of leather. To the west lies Tiaozhi (Mesopotamia) and to the north Yancai and Lixuan (<a href="/wiki/Hyrcania" title="Hyrcania">Hyrcania</a>)." (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Tiaozhi_.28.E6.9D.A1.E6.94.AF.29_.28Seleucid_Empire_in_Mesopotamia.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tiaozhi_(&#26465;&#25903;)_(Seleucid_Empire_in_Mesopotamia)">Tiaozhi (&#26465;&#25903;) (Seleucid Empire in Mesopotamia)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tiaozhi (&#26465;&#25903;) (Seleucid Empire in Mesopotamia)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Zhang Qian\'s reports on <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>, or Tiaozhi (&#26465;&#25903;), are in tenuous terms. He did not himself visit the region, and was only able to report what others told him.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Tiaozhi (Mesopotamia) is situated several thousand <i>li</i> west of Anxi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsacid_dynasty_of_Parthia" title="Arsacid dynasty of Parthia">Arsacid</a> territory) and borders the Western Sea (<a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>?). It is hot and damp, and the people live by cultivating the fields and planting rice... The people are very numerous and are ruled by many petty chiefs. The ruler of Anxi (the Arsacids) give orders to these chiefs and regards them as vassals. (adapted from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Kangju_.28.E5.BA.B7.E5.B1.85.29_northwest_of_Sogdiana_.28.E7.B2.9F.E7.89.B9.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kangju_(&#24247;&#23621;)_northwest_of_Sogdiana_(&#31903;&#29305;)">Kangju (&#24247;&#23621;) northwest of Sogdiana (&#31903;&#29305;)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kangju (&#24247;&#23621;) northwest of Sogdiana (&#31903;&#29305;)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Zhang Qian also visited directly the area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdiana" title="Sogdiana">Sogdiana</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a>), home to the Sogdian nomads:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Kangju is situated some 2,000 li (1,000 kilometers) northwest of Dayuan (Bactria). Its people are nomads and resemble the Yuezhi in their customs. They have 80,000 or 90,000 skilled archer fighters. The country is small, and borders Dayuan. It acknowledges sovereignty to the Yuezhi people in the South and the Xiongnu in the East." (Shiji, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h3><span id="Yancai_.E5.A5.84.E8.94.A1_.28Vast_Steppe.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Yancai_&#22852;&#34081;_(Vast_Steppe)">Yancai &#22852;&#34081; (Vast Steppe)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Yancai &#22852;&#34081; (Vast Steppe)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd>"Yancai lies some 2,000 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Chinese_unit)" title="Li (Chinese unit)">li</a></i> (832 km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a> (centered on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazrat-e_Turkestan" title="Hazrat-e Turkestan">Turkestan</a> at Beitian). The people are nomads and their customs are generally similar to those of the people of <a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a>. The country has over 100,000 archer warriors, and borders a great shoreless lake, perhaps what is now known as the Northern Sea (<a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, distance between <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aralsk" title="Aralsk">Aralsk</a> is about 866 km)" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123, Zhang Qian quote, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_East-West_contacts">Development of East-West contacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development of East-West contacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following Zhang Qian\'s embassy and report, commercial relations between China and Central as well as Western Asia flourished, as many Chinese missions were sent throughout the end of the 2nd century BC and the 1st century BC, initiating the development of the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"The largest of these embassies to foreign states numbered several hundred persons, while even the smaller parties included over 100 members... In the course of one year anywhere from five to six to over ten parties would be sent out." (Shiji, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<p>Many objects were soon exchanged, and travelled as far as <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a> in the East, as suggested by the discovery of a <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> box and various artifacts from Central Asia in the 122 BC tomb of King <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Mo" title="Zhao Mo">Zhao Mo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nanyue" title="Nanyue">Nanyue</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HanWudiBuddhas.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1996" data-file-width="1543" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/HanWudiBuddhas.jpg/170px-HanWudiBuddhas.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/HanWudiBuddhas.jpg/255px-HanWudiBuddhas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/HanWudiBuddhas.jpg/340px-HanWudiBuddhas.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HanWudiBuddhas.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">Mogao Caves</a> 8th-century mural depicting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudohistorical" title="Pseudohistorical">pseudohistorical</a> legend of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han" title="Emperor Wu of Han">Emperor Wu of Han</a> worshipping "golden man" Buddha statues captured in 121 BC.</div></div></div>\n<p>Murals in <a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">Mogao Caves</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> describe the Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Wudi" title="Han Wudi">Han Wudi</a> (156&#8211;87 BC) worshipping Buddhist statues, explaining them as "golden men brought in 120 BC by a great Han general in his campaigns against the nomads", although there is no other mention of Han Wudi worshipping the Buddha in Chinese historical literature.\n</p><p>China also sent a mission to <a href="#Anxi">Anxi</a>, which were followed up by reciprocal missions from Parthian envoys around 100 BC:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"When the Han envoy first visited the kingdom of <a href="#Anxi">Anxi</a>, the king of Anxi (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arsacid_Empire" title="Arsacid Empire">Arsacid</a> ruler) dispatched a party of 20,000 horsemen to meet them on the eastern border of the kingdom... When the Han envoys set out again to return to China, the king of Anxi dispatched envoys of his own to accompany them... The emperor was delighted at this." (adapted from Shiji, 123, trans. Burton Watson).</dd></dl>\n<p>The Roman historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florus" title="Florus">Florus</a> describes the visit of numerous envoys, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">Seres</a></i> (Chinese or central Asians), to the first Roman Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar_Augustus" title="Caesar Augustus">Augustus</a>, who reigned between 27 BC and 14:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"Even the rest of the nations of the world which were not subject to the imperial sway were sensible of its grandeur, and looked with reverence to the Roman people, the great conqueror of nations. Thus even <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a> sent envoys to seek the friendship of Rome. Nay, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">Seres</a> came likewise, and the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indians</a> who dwelt beneath the vertical sun, bringing presents of precious stones and pearls and elephants, but thinking all of less moment than the vastness of the journey which they had undertaken, and which they said had occupied four years. In truth it needed but to look at their complexion to see that they were people of another world than ours." ("Cathay and the way thither", <a href="/wiki/Henry_Yule" title="Henry Yule">Henry Yule</a>).</dd></dl>\n<p>In 97 the Chinese general <a href="/wiki/Ban_Chao" title="Ban Chao">Ban Chao</a> dispatched an envoy to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in the person of <a href="/wiki/Gan_Ying" title="Gan Ying">Gan Ying</a>.\n</p><p>Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_embassies_to_China" title="Roman embassies to China">Roman embassies to China</a> followed from 166, and are officially recorded in Chinese historical chronicles.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i> reports that Zhang Qian returned from his final expedition to the Wusun in 115 BC. After his return he "was honoured with the post of grand messenger, making him among the nine highest ministers of the government. A year or so later he died."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"The indications regarding the year of his death differ, but Shih Chih-mien (1961), p. 268 shows beyond doubt that he died in 113 B.C. His tomb is situated in Chang-chia ts\'un &#24373;&#23478;&#26449; near Ch\'eng-ku . . .&#160;; during repairs carried out in 1945 a clay mold with the inscription &#21338;&#26395;&#23478;&#36896; [Home of the Bowang (Marquis)] was found, as reported by Ch\'en Chih (1959), p. 162." Hulsew&#233; and Loewe (1979), p. 218, note 819.</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_achievements">Other achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhang_Qian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From his missions he brought back many important products, the most important being <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> seeds (for growing horse <a href="/wiki/Fodder" title="Fodder">fodder</a>), strong horses with hard hooves, and knowledge of the extensive existence of new products, peoples and technologies of the outside world. He died <i>c</i>. 114 BC after spending twenty-five years travelling on these dangerous and strategic missions. Although at a time in his life he was regarded with disgrace for being defeated by the Xiongnu, by the time of his death he had been bestowed with great honours by the emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Zhang Qian\'s journeys had promoted a great variety of economic and cultural exchanges between the Han Dynasty and the Western Regions. Because silk became the dominant product traded from China, this great trade route later became known as the Silk Road or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silk_Route" title="Silk Route">Silk Route</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
