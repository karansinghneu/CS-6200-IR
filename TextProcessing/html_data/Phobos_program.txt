title="Enlarge"></a></div>Phobos probe</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Phobos </b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1060;&#1086;&#1073;&#1086;&#1089;, Fobos</span>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">&#934;&#972;&#946;&#959;&#962;</span>)<b> program</b> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_space_mission" title="Unmanned space mission">unmanned space mission</a> consisting of two <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">probes</a> launched by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> to study <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moons</a> <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">Deimos</a>. <i>Phobos 1</i> was launched on 7 July 1988, and <i>Phobos 2</i> on 12 July 1988, each aboard a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_rocket" title="Proton rocket">Proton-K rocket</a>.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Phobos_1" title="Phobos 1">Phobos 1</a></i> suffered a terminal failure en route to Mars. <i><a href="/wiki/Phobos_2" title="Phobos 2">Phobos 2</a></i> attained Mars orbit, but contact was lost before the final phase, prior to deployment of a planned Phobos lander. <i>Phobos 1</i> and <i>2</i> were of a new spacecraft design, succeeding the type used in the <i><a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planetary</a> missions of 1975&#8211;1985, last used during the <i><a href="/wiki/Vega_1" title="Vega 1">Vega 1</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vega_2" title="Vega 2">Vega 2</a></i> missions to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Halley" title="Comet Halley">Comet Halley</a>. They each had a mass of 2600 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a> (6220&#160;kg with orbital insertion hardware attached).\n</p><p>The program featured cooperation from 14 other nations, including Sweden, Switzerland, Austria, France, West Germany, and the United States (which contributed the use of its <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">NASA Deep Space Network</a> for tracking the twin spacecraft).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The objectives of the Phobos missions were to:\n</p>\n<ul><li>conduct studies of the interplanetary environment;</li>\n<li>perform observations of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>;</li>\n<li>characterize the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> environment in the Martian vicinity;</li>\n<li>conduct surface and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmospheric</a> studies of Mars; and,</li>\n<li>study the surface composition of the Martian satellite Phobos.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_design">Spacecraft design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spacecraft design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The main section of the spacecraft consisted of a pressurized toroidal <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> section, surrounding a modular cylindrical experiment section. Below these were mounted four spherical tanks (the <a href="/wiki/Fregat" title="Fregat">Fregat</a> stage) containing <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> for <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a> and, after the main propulsion module was to be jettisoned, <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> adjustment. A total of 28 <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">thrusters</a> (twenty-four 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton_(units)" title="Newton (units)">N</a> thrusters and four 10 N thrusters) were mounted on the spherical tanks, with additional thrusters mounted on the spacecraft body and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a>. Attitude was maintained through the use of a three-axis control system, with pointing maintained with Sun and <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> sensors.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phobos_1">Phobos 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phobos 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Phobos_1" title="Phobos 1">Phobos 1</a></div>\n<p><i>Phobos 1</i> operated nominally until an expected communications session on September 2, 1988 failed to occur. The failure of controllers to regain contact with the spacecraft was traced to an error in the software uploaded on August 29/August 30, which had deactivated the attitude thrusters. By losing its lock on the Sun, the spacecraft could no longer properly orient its solar arrays, thus depleting its batteries.\n</p><p>Software instructions to turn off the probe\'s attitude control, normally a fatal operation, were part of a routine used when testing the spacecraft on the ground.  Normally this routine would be removed before launch.  However, the software was coded in <a href="/wiki/Programmable_read-only_memory" title="Programmable read-only memory">PROMs</a>, and so removing the test code would have required removing and replacing the entire computer.  Because of time pressure from the impending launch, engineers decided to leave the command sequence in, though it should never be used.  However, a single-character error in constructing an upload sequence resulted in the command executing, with subsequent loss of the spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceMag_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceMag-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phobos_2">Phobos 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phobos 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Phobos_2" title="Phobos 2">Phobos 2</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3_channels_VSK_image_Phobos_mission.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="256" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ef/3_channels_VSK_image_Phobos_mission.gif/220px-3_channels_VSK_image_Phobos_mission.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ef/3_channels_VSK_image_Phobos_mission.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:3_channels_VSK_image_Phobos_mission.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample image taken by the Phobos 2 probe. Enhanced image released by the IKI. Taken 430 km away with a resolution of 80/420 m.</div></div></div>\n<p><i>Phobos 2</i> was launched atop a <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">Blok D</a> upper stage from Baikonur cosmodrome on July 12, 1988, and entered orbit on January 29, 1989. <i>Phobos 2</i> operated nominally throughout its cruise and Mars orbital insertion phases on January 29, 1989, gathering data on the Sun, the interplanetary medium, Mars, and Phobos. Phobos 2 investigated Mars\'s surface and atmosphere and returned 37 images of Phobos<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceMag-2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceMag-2-2">[2]</a></sup> with a resolution of up to 40 meters.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_and_sensors">Systems and sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Systems and sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Phobos probes carried several instruments:  solar <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> telescopes, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neutron_spectrometer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neutron spectrometer (page does not exist)">neutron spectrometer</a> and the Grunt radar experiment designed to study the surface relief of Phobos.\nThe lander had an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/APXS" title="APXS">x-ray/alpha spectrometer</a> to provide information on the chemical element composition of the surface of Phobos, a <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometer</a> to determine the internal structure of Phobos, and the "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Razrez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Razrez (page does not exist)">Razrez</a>" penetrator with temperature sensors and an <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">accelerometer</a> for testing the physical and mechanical properties of the surface.\n</p><p>The Phobos 2 <a href="/wiki/Thermal_infrared_spectroscopy" title="Thermal infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectrometer</a> (ISM) obtained 45000 spectra in the near infrared (from 0.75 to 3.2&#160;&#181;m) in the equatorial areas of Mars, with a spatial resolution ranging from 7 to 25&#160;km, and 400 spectra of Phobos at 700 m resolution. These observations made it possible to retrieve the first mineralogical maps of the planet and its satellite, and to study the atmosphere of Mars. ISM was developed at <a href="/wiki/Institut_a%C3%A9ronautique_et_spatial" title="Institut a&#233;ronautique et spatial">IAS</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DESPA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DESPA (page does not exist)">DESPA</a> (<a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">Paris Observatory</a>) with support from <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">CNES</a>.\n</p><p>List of instruments:\n</p>\n<ul><li>"VSK" TV imaging system</li>\n<li>PROP-F "hopping" lander. designed to vaporise material from the Phobos surface for chemical analysis by a mass spectrometer</li>\n<li>Automatic Space Plasma Experiment with Rotating Analyzer (ASPERA), an electron spectrometer and ion mass analyser from the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Institute_of_Space_Physics" title="Swedish Institute of Space Physics">Swedish Institute of Space Physics</a>.</li>\n<li>"Grunt" imaging radar - Only carried by Phobos 1</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">Exploration of Mars</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_missions_to_Mars" title="List of missions to Mars">List of missions to Mars</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">Space exploration</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phobos_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-ScienceMag-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-ScienceMag_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://catless.ncl.ac.uk/Risks/9.24.html#subj3" rel="nofollow"><i>Risks digest</i> summary of the malfunctions, from SCIENCE magazine, Vol 245, 8 September 1989</a></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-ScienceMag-2-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-ScienceMag-2_2-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v341/n6243/abs/341585a0.html" rel="nofollow">Nature Publishing Group, Television observations of Phobos</a></span>\n</li>\n</ol></div></div>\n<ul><li><cite class="citation journal">R.
