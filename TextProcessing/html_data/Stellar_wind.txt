ejected from the atmosphere of stars. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Stellar_wind_(disambiguation)" title="Stellar wind (disambiguation)">Stellar wind (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="895" data-file-width="1074" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg/220px-52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg/330px-52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg/440px-52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:52706main_hstorion_lg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This image shows the wind from the star LL Orionis generating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a> (the bright arc) as it collides with material in the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>stellar wind</b> is a flow of gas ejected from the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere" title="Stellar atmosphere">upper atmosphere</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>.  It is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_outflow" title="Bipolar outflow">bipolar outflows</a> characteristic of young stars by being less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collimated" title="Collimated">collimated</a>, although stellar winds are not generally spherically symmetric.\n</p><p>Different <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">types</a> of stars have different types of stellar winds. \n</p><p>Post-<a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main-sequence</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> nearing the ends of their lives often eject large quantities of mass in massive (<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\scriptstyle {\\dot {M}}&gt;10^{-3}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mstyle displaystyle="false" scriptlevel="1">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mover>\n<mi>M</mi>\n<mo>&#729;<!-- &#729; --></mo>\n</mover>\n</mrow>\n</mrow>\n<mo>&gt;</mo>\n<msup>\n<mn>10</mn>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mn>3</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msup>\n</mstyle>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\scriptstyle {\\dot {M}}&gt;10^{-3}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\scriptstyle {\\dot  {M}}&gt;10^{{-3}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb44c7d8825f3deb94c97d74699f440277815a39" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:6.519ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span> solar masses per year), slow (v = 10&#160;km/s) winds.  These include <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_supergiant" title="Red supergiant">supergiants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a> stars.  These winds are understood to be driven by <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> on <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> condensing in the upper atmosphere of the stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Massive stars of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">types</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O_star" title="O star">O</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_V_star" title="B V star">B</a> have stellar winds with lower mass loss rates (<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\scriptstyle {\\dot {M}}&lt;10^{-6}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mstyle displaystyle="false" scriptlevel="1">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mover>\n<mi>M</mi>\n<mo>&#729;<!-- &#729; --></mo>\n</mover>\n</mrow>\n</mrow>\n<mo>&lt;</mo>\n<msup>\n<mn>10</mn>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mn>6</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msup>\n</mstyle>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\scriptstyle {\\dot {M}}&lt;10^{-6}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\scriptstyle {\\dot  {M}}&lt;10^{{-6}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9a81b800e837aff677b33cfa7591789e67d0052" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:6.519ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span> solar masses per year) but very high velocities (v &gt; 1&#8211;2000&#160;km/s).  Such winds are driven by radiation pressure on the resonance absorption lines of heavy elements such as carbon and nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  These high-energy stellar winds blow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_wind_bubble" title="Stellar wind bubble">stellar wind bubbles</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_long_goodbye.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="936" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_long_goodbye.jpg/220px-The_long_goodbye.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_long_goodbye.jpg/330px-The_long_goodbye.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/The_long_goodbye.jpg/440px-The_long_goodbye.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_long_goodbye.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> <a href="/wiki/NGC_6565" title="NGC 6565">NGC 6565</a>, a cloud of gas was ejected from the star after strong stellar winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/G-type_main-sequence_star" title="G-type main-sequence star">G-type stars</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> have a wind driven by their hot, magnetized <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>. The Sun\'s wind is called the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.  These winds consist mostly of high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> (about 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">keV</a>) that are able to escape the star\'s <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> because of the high <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>.\n</p><p>Stellar winds from main-sequence stars do not strongly influence the evolution of lower mass stars such as the Sun. However, for more massive stars such as O stars, the mass loss can result in a star shedding as much as 50% of its mass whilst on the main sequence: this clearly has a significant impact on the later stages of evolution. The influence can even be seen for intermediate mass stars, which will become <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarfs</a> at the ends of their lives rather than exploding as <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> only because they lost enough mass in their winds.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
