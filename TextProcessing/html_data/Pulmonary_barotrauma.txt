2x" style="vertical-align: text-top" width="10"/></a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Barotrauma</b> is physical damage to body tissues caused by a difference in <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> between a gas space inside, or in contact with the body, and the surrounding gas or fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-usn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk-2">[2]</a></sup> The initial damage is usually due to over-stretching the tissues in tension or shear, either directly by expansion of the gas in the closed space, or by pressure difference hydrostatically transmitted through the tissue. Tissue rupture may be complicated by the introduction of gas into the local tissue or circulation through the initial trauma site, which can cause blockage of circulation at distant sites, or interfere with normal function of an organ by its presence.\n</p><p>Barotrauma generally manifests as sinus or middle ear effects, <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> (DCS), lung overpressure injuries, and injuries resulting from external squeezes.\n</p><p>Barotrauma typically occurs when the organism is exposed to a significant change in <a href="/wiki/Ambient_pressure" title="Ambient pressure">ambient pressure</a>, such as when a <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diver</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">free-diver</a> or an airplane passenger ascends or descends, or during <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression" title="Uncontrolled decompression">uncontrolled decompression</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessel</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Diving_chamber" title="Diving chamber">diving chamber</a> or pressurised aircraft, but can also be caused by a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>.  Ventilator induced lung injury (VILI) is a condition caused by over-expansion of the lungs by mechanical ventilation used when the body is unable to breathe for itself, and is associated with relatively large tidal volumes and relatively high peak pressures. Barotrauma due to overexpansion of an internal gas-filled space may also be termed <b>volutrauma</b>. Bats can be killed by lung barotrauma when flying in low-pressure regions close to operating wind-turbine blades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baerwald_et_al._3-0"><a diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When diving, the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> differences which cause the barotrauma are changes in hydrostatic pressure:\nThere are two components to the surrounding pressure acting on the diver: the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> and the water pressure. A descent of 10 metres (33&#160;feet) in water increases the ambient pressure by an amount approximately equal to the pressure of the atmosphere at sea level. So, a descent from the surface to 10 metres (33&#160;feet) underwater results in a doubling of the pressure on the diver. This pressure change will reduce the volume of a gas filled space by half. <a href="/wiki/Boyle%27s_law" title="Boyle\'s law">Boyle\'s law</a> describes the relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of the gas space and the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> in the gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-usn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAN_FAQ_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAN_FAQ-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barotraumas of descent are caused by preventing the free change of volume of the gas in a closed space in contact with the diver, resulting in a pressure difference between the tissues and the gas space, and the unbalanced force due to this pressure difference causes deformation of the tissues resulting in cell rupture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barotraumas of ascent are also caused when the free change of volume of the gas in a closed space in contact with the diver is prevented. In this case the pressure difference causes a resultant tension in the surrounding tissues which exceeds their tensile strength. Besides tissue rupture, the overpressure may cause ingress of gases into the tissues and further afield through the circulatory system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk-2">[2]</a></sup> This pulmonary barotrauma (PBt) of ascent is also known as pulmonary over-inflation syndrome (POIS), lung over-pressure injury (LOP) and burst lung.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAN_FAQ_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAN_FAQ-21">[21]</a></sup> Consequent injuries may include arterial gas embolism, pneumothorax, mediastinal, interstitial and subcutaneous emphysemas, not usually all at the same time.\n</p><p>Breathing gas at depth from <a href="/wiki/Underwater_breathing_apparatus" title="Underwater breathing apparatus">underwater breathing apparatus</a> results in the lungs containing gas at a higher pressure than atmospheric pressure. So a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">free-diver</a> can dive to 10 metres (33&#160;feet) and safely ascend without exhaling, because the gas in the lungs had been inhaled at atmospheric pressure, whereas a <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">diver</a> who inhales at 10 metres and ascends without exhaling has lungs containing twice the amount of gas at atmospheric pressure and is very likely to suffer life-threatening lung damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAN_FAQ_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-DAN_FAQ-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_decompression" title="Explosive decompression">Explosive decompression</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Diving_chamber#Saturation_diving_life_support_systems" title="Diving chamber">hyperbaric environment</a> can produce severe barotrauma, followed by severe decompression bubble formation and other related injury. The <i><a href="/wiki/Byford_Dolphin" title="Byford Dolphin">Byford Dolphin</a></i> incident is an example.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blast-induced_barotrauma">Blast-induced barotrauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blast-induced barotrauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosive blast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression" title="Uncontrolled decompression">explosive decompression</a> create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_wave" title="Pressure wave">pressure wave</a> that can induce barotrauma.  The difference in pressure between internal organs and the outer surface of the body causes injuries to internal organs that contain gas, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_lung" title="Human lung">lungs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torkki_et_al_2006_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Torkki_et_al_2006-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lung injuries can also occur during <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression" title="Uncontrolled decompression">rapid decompression</a>, although the risk of injury is lower than with explosive decompression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_1959_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_1959-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1599378_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1599378-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilator-induced_barotrauma">Ventilator-induced barotrauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ventilator-induced barotrauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biotrauma" title="Biotrauma">Biotrauma</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mechanical_ventilation" title="Mechanical ventilation">Mechanical ventilation</a> can lead to barotrauma of the lungs. This can be due to either:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker_Hernandez_and_Perry_1993_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker_Hernandez_and_Perry_1993-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>absolute pressures used in order to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventilation_(physiology)" title="Ventilation (physiology)">ventilate</a> <a href="/wiki/Compliance_(physiology)" title="Compliance (physiology)">non-compliant</a> lungs.</li>\n<li>shearing forces, particularly associated with rapid changes in gas <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The resultant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolus" title="Alveolus">alveolar</a> rupture can lead to <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_interstitial_emphysema" title="Pulmonary interstitial emphysema">pulmonary interstitial emphysema</a> (PIE) and <a href="/wiki/Pneumomediastinum" title="Pneumomediastinum">pneumomediastinum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soo_Hoo_2015_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soo_Hoo_2015-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Barotrauma is a recognised complication of mechanical ventilation that can occur in any patient receiving mechanical ventilation, but is most commonly associated with acute respiratory distress syndrome. It used to be the most common complication of mechanical ventilation but can usually be avoided by limiting tidal volume and plateau pressure to less than 30 to 50&#160;cm water column (30 to 50&#160;mb). As an indicator of transalveolar pressure, which predicts alveolar distention, plateau pressure or peak airway pressure (PAP) may be the most effective predictor of risk, but there is no generally accepted safe pressure at which there is no risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soo_Hoo_2015_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Soo_Hoo_2015-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haake_et_al_1987_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haake_et_al_1987-27">[27]</a></sup> Risk also appears to be increased by <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration" title="Pulmonary aspiration">aspiration</a> of stomach contents and pre-existing disease such as <a href="/wiki/Necrosis" title="Necrosis">necrotising</a> <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> and chronic lung disease. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Status_asthmaticus" title="Status asthmaticus">Status asthmaticus</a> is a particular problem as it requires relatively high pressures to overcome bronchial obstruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haake_et_al_1987_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haake_et_al_1987-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When lung tissues are damaged by alveolar over-distension, the injury may be termed volutrauma, but volume and transpulmonary pressure are closely related. Ventilator induced lung injury is often associated with high tidal volumes (V<sub>t</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21489320_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21489320-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_a_hyperbaric_chamber.">Use of a hyperbaric chamber.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use of a hyperbaric chamber.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Patients undergoing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygen_therapy" title="Hyperbaric oxygen therapy">hyperbaric oxygen therapy</a> must equalize their ears to avoid barotrauma. High risk of otic barotrauma is associated with unconscious patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPUMS2003barotrauma_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPUMS2003barotrauma-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a of <i>barotrauma</i> the diagnostic workup for the affected individual would include the following:\n</p><p>Laboratory:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_2015_30-0"><a associated with decompression sickness.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Complete_blood_count" title="Complete blood count">Complete blood count</a> (CBC)</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" discomfort or breathing difficulty</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" title="Computed tomography">Computed tomography</a> (CT) scans and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) It can be used where barotrauma-related pneumothorax is suspected and chest radiograph findings are negative.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">Echocardiography</a> can be used to detect the number and size of gas bubbles in the right side of the heart.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ear_barotrauma">Ear barotrauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ear barotrauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Barotrauma can affect the external, middle, or inner ear.  Middle ear barotrauma (MEBT) is the most common being experienced by between 10% and 30% of divers and is due to insufficient <a href="/wiki/Ear_clearing" title="Ear clearing">equilibration of the middle ear</a>.  External ear barotrauma may occur on ascent if high pressure air is trapped in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_auditory_canal" title="External auditory canal">external auditory canal</a> either by tight fitting diving equipment or ear wax.  Inner ear barotrauma (IEBT), though much less common than MEBT, shares a similar mechanism.  Mechanical trauma to the inner ear can lead to varying degrees of <a href="/wiki/Conductive_hearing_loss" title="Conductive hearing loss">conductive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sensorineural_hearing_loss" title="Sensorineural hearing loss">sensorineural hearing loss</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertigo_(medical)" title="Vertigo (medical)">vertigo</a>. It is also common for conditions affecting the inner ear to result in auditory hypersensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marx_2010_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marx_2010-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barosinusitis">Barosinusitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Barosinusitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinuses" title="Sinuses">sinuses</a> similar to other air-filled cavities are susceptible to barotrauma if their openings become obstructed.  This can result in pain as well as epistaxis (<a href="/wiki/Nosebleed" title="Nosebleed">nosebleed</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marx_2010_1907_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marx_2010_1907-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mask_squeeze">Mask squeeze</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mask squeeze">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>If a diver\'s mask is not equalized during descent the relative negative pressure can produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petechial_hemorrhage" title="Petechial hemorrhage">petechial hemorrhages</a> in the area covered by the mask along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subconjunctival_hemorrhage" title="Subconjunctival hemorrhage">subconjunctival hemorrhages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marx_2010_1907_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marx_2010_1907-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helmet_squeeze">Helmet squeeze</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Helmet squeeze">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A problem mostly of historical interest, but still relevant to surface supplied divers who dive with the helmet sealed to the dry suit. If the air supply hose is ruptured near or above the surface, the pressure difference between the water around the diver and the air in the hose can be several bar. The non-return valve at the connection to the helmet will prevent backflow if it is working correctly, but if absent, as in the early days of helmet diving, or if it fails, the pressure difference will tend to squeeze the diver into the rigid helmet, which can result in severe trauma. The same effect can result from a large and rapid increase in depth if the air supply is insufficient to keep up with the increase in ambient pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Neuman_2003_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Neuman_2003-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulmonary_barotrauma">Pulmonary barotrauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pulmonary barotrauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_signs_and_symptoms_of_diving_disorders#Arterial_gas_embolism_and_pulmonary_barotrauma" title="List of signs and symptoms of diving disorders">List of signs and symptoms of diving disorders &#167;&#160;Arterial gas embolism and pulmonary barotrauma</a></div>\n<p>Lung over-pressure injury in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambient_pressure_diving" title="Ambient pressure diving">ambient pressure divers</a> using <a href="/wiki/Underwater_breathing_apparatus" title="Underwater breathing apparatus">underwater breathing apparatus</a> is usually caused by breath-holding on ascent. The compressed gas in the lungs expands as the ambient pressure decreases causing the lungs to over-expand and rupture unless the diver allows the gas to escape by maintaining an open <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airway" title="Airway">airway</a>, as in normal breathing. The lungs do not sense pain when over-expanded giving the diver little warning to prevent the injury.  This does not affect breath-hold divers as they bring a lungful of air with them from the surface, which merely re-expands safely to near its original volume on ascent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brubakk_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Brubakk-2">[2]</a></sup> The problem only arises if a breath of ambient pressure gas is taken at depth, which may then expand on ascent to more than the lung volume.  Pulmonary barotrauma may also be caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_decompression" title="Explosive decompression">explosive decompression</a> of a pressurised aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AC61-107B_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-AC61-107B-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_divers">In divers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In divers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Barotrauma may be caused when diving, either from being crushed, or squeezed, on descent or by stretching and bursting on ascent; both can be avoided by <i>equalising</i> the pressures. A negative, unbalanced pressure is known as a squeeze, crushing eardrums, dry suit, lungs or mask inwards and can be equalised by putting air into the squeezed space. A positive unbalanced pressure expands internal spaces rupturing tissue and can be equalised by letting air out, for example by exhaling.  Both may cause barotrauma. There are a variety of techniques depending on the affected area and whether the pressure inequality is a squeeze or an expansion:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">Ears</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paranasal_sinus" title="Paranasal sinus">sinuses</a>: There is a risk of stretched or burst <a href="/wiki/Eardrum" title="Eardrum">eardrums</a>, usually crushed inwards during descent but sometimes stretched outwards on ascent. The diver can use a <a href="/wiki/Ear_clearing" title="Ear clearing">variety of methods</a> to let air into or out of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_ear" title="Middle ear">middle ears</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Eustachian_tube" title="Eustachian tube">Eustachian tubes</a>. Sometimes swallowing will open the Eustachian tubes and equalise the ears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kay_2000_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kay_2000-35">[35]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_lung" title="Human lung">Lungs</a>: There is a risk of <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_gas_embolism" title="Arterial gas embolism">arterial gas embolism</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediastinal_emphysema" title="Mediastinal emphysema">mediastinal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_emphysema" title="Subcutaneous emphysema">subcutanous emphysemas</a> during ascent, which are commonly called burst lung or lung overpressure injury by divers. To equalise the lungs, all that is necessary is not to hold the breath during ascent. This risk does not occur when breath-hold diving from the surface, unless the diver breathes from an ambient pressure gas source underwater; breath-hold divers do suffer squeezed lungs on descent, crushing in the chest cavity, but, while uncomfortable, this rarely causes lung injury and returns to normal at the surface. Some people have pathologies of the lung which prevent rapid flow of excess air through the passages, which can lead to lung barotrauma even if the breath is not held during rapid depressurisation. These people should not dive as the risk is unacceptably high. Most commercial or military diving medical examinations will look specifically for signs of this pathology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fitness_to_dive_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fitness_to_dive-36">[36]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Diving_mask" title="Diving mask">Diving mask</a> squeeze enclosing the eyes and nose: The main risk is rupture of the capillaries of the eyes and facial skin because of the negative pressure difference between the gas space and blood pressure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mask_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-mask-10">[10]</a></sup> or orbital emphysema from higher pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18619111_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18619111-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This can be avoided by breathing air into the mask through the nose. <a href="/wiki/Goggles" title="Goggles">Goggles</a> covering only the eyes are not suitable for deep diving as they cannot be equalised.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dry_suit" title="Dry suit">Dry suit</a> squeeze. The main risk is skin getting pinched and bruised by folds of the dry suit when squeezed on descent. Most dry suits can be equalised against squeeze via a manually operated <a href="/wiki/Dry_suit#Valves" title="Dry suit">valve</a> fed from a low pressure gas supply. Air must be manually injected during the descent to avoid squeeze and is manually or automatically vented on the ascent to maintain buoyancy control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_Long_and_Stinton_2006_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_Long_and_Stinton_2006-38">[38]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">Diving helmet</a> squeeze: Helmet squeeze will occur if the gas supply hose is severed above the diver and the non-return valve at the helmet gas inlet fails or is not fitted. Severity will depend on the hydrostatic pressure difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DA_incidents_22_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-DA_incidents_22-39">[39]</a></sup> A very rapid descent, usually by accident, may exceed the rate at which the breathing gas supply can equalise the pressure causing a temporary squeeze. The introduction of the non-return valve and high maximum gas supply flow rates have all but eliminated both these risks. In helmets fitted with a neck dam, the dam will admit water into the helmet if the internal pressure gets too low; this is less of a problem than helmet squeeze but the diver may drown if the gas supply is not reinstated quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barsky_and_Neuman_2003_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barsky_and_Neuman_2003-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>90</span></sup> This form of barotrauma is avoidable by controlled descent rate, which is standard practice for commercial divers, who will use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shotline" title="Shotline">shotlines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diving_stage" title="Diving stage">diving stages</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_bell" title="Wet bell">wet bells</a> to control descent and ascent rates.</li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_screening">Medical screening</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fitness_to_dive" title="Fitness to dive">Fitness to dive</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Professional_diving" title="Professional diving">Professional divers</a> are screened for risk factors during initial and periodical medical examination for <a href="/wiki/Fitness_to_dive" title="Fitness to dive">fitness to dive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDTC_fitness_standard_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-EDTC_fitness_standard-40">[40]</a></sup> In most cases <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diving" title="Recreational diving">recreational divers</a> are not medically screened, but are required to provide a medical statement before acceptance for <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diver_training" title="Recreational diver training">training</a> in which the most common and easy to identify risk factors must be declared. If these factors are declared, the diver may be required to be examined by a medical practitioner, and may be disqualified from diving if the conditions indicate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson_2000_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson_2000-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">Asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COPD" title="COPD">COPD</a> pose a very high risk of pneumothorax.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="what counts as very high? (May 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some countries these may be considered absolute contraindications, while in others the <a href="/wiki/Fitness_to_dive#Conditions_which_may_disqualify_or_require_restrictions_depending_on_severity_and_management" title="Fitness to dive">severity may be taken into consideration</a>. Asthmatics with a mild and well controlled condition may be permitted to dive under restricted circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adir_and_Bove_2016_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adir_and_Bove_2016-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_diver_training" title="Recreational diver training">Recreational diver training</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scuba_skills" title="Scuba skills">Scuba skills</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_supplied_diving_skills" title="Surface supplied diving skills">Surface supplied diving skills</a></div>\n<p>A significant part of entry level diver training is focused on understanding the risks and <a href="/wiki/Scuba_skills" title="Scuba skills">procedural</a> avoidance of barotrauma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRSTC_OWTS_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-WRSTC_OWTS-43">[43]</a></sup> Professional divers and recreational divers with rescue training are trained in the basic skills of recognizing and first aid management of diving barotrauma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_Diving_Regulations_2009_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA_Diving_Regulations_2009-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDSA_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-IDSA-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mechanical_ventilation">In mechanical ventilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In mechanical ventilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Isolated mechanical forces may not adequately explain ventilator induced lung injury (VILI). The damage is affected by the interaction of these forces and the pre-existing state of the lung tissues, and dynamic changes in alveolar structure may be involved. Factors such as plateau pressure and positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) alone do not adequately predict injury. Cyclic deformation of lung tissue may play a large part in the cause of VILI, and contributory factors probably include tidal volume, positive end-expiratory pressure and respiratory rate. There is no protocol guaranteed to avoid all risk in all applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid21489320_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid21489320-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Treatment of diving barotrauma depends on the symptoms. Lung over-pressure injury may require a chest drain to remove air from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleura" title="Pleura">pleura</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mediastinum" title="Mediastinum">mediastinum</a>. Recompression with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygen_therapy" title="Hyperbaric oxygen therapy">hyperbaric oxygen therapy</a> is the definitive treatment for arterial gas embolism, as the raised pressure reduces bubble size, low inert gas <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> accelerates inert gas solution and high oxygen partial pressure helps oxygenate tissues compromised by the emboli. Care must be taken when recompressing to avoid a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tension_pneumothorax" title="Tension pneumothorax">tension pneumothorax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stephenson_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stephenson-46">[46]</a></sup> Barotraumas that do not involve gas in the tissues are generally treated according to severity and symptoms for similar trauma from other causes.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_aid">First aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: First aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pre-hospital care for lung barotrauma includes basic life support of maintaining adequate oxygenation and perfusion, assessment of airway, breathing and circulation, neurological assessment, and managing any immediate life-threatening conditions. High-flow oxygen up to 100% is considered appropriate for diving accidents. Large-bore venous access with isotonic fluid infusion is recommended to maintain blood pressure and pulse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_2015b_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan_2015b-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_treatment">Emergency treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Emergency treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pulmonary barotrauma:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_2015c_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan_2015c-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endotracheal_intubation" title="Endotracheal intubation">Endotracheal intubation</a> may be required if the airway is unstable or hypoxia persists when breathing 100% oxygen.</li>\n<li>Needle decompression or tube <a href="/wiki/Thoracostomy" title="Thoracostomy">thoracostomy</a> may be necessary to drain a pneumothorax or haemothorax</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Foley_catheter" title="Foley catheter">Foley catheterization</a> may be necessary for spinal cord AGE if the person is unable to urinate.</li>\n<li>Intravenous hydration may be required to maintain adequate blood pressure.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therapeutic_recompression" title="Therapeutic recompression">Therapeutic recompression</a> is indicated for severe AGE. The diving medical practitioner will need to know the vital signs and relevant symptoms, along with the recent pressure exposure and breathing gas history of the patient. Air transport should be below 1,000 feet (300&#160;m) if possible, or in a pressurized aircraft which should be pressurised to as low an altitude as reasonably possible.</li></ul>\n<p>Sinus squeeze and middle ear squeeze are generally treated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decongestants" title="Decongestants">decongestants</a> to reduce the pressure differential, with <a href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory" title="Anti-inflammatory">anti-inflammatory</a> medications to treat the pain. For severe pain, narcotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analgesics" title="Analgesics">analgesics</a> may be appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_2015c_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan_2015c-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Suit, helmet and mask squeeze are treated as trauma according to symptoms and severity.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">Medication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Medication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The primary medications for lung barotrauma are <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heliox" title="Heliox">oxygen-helium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonicity#Isotonicity" title="Tonicity">isotonic</a> fluids, <a href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory" title="Anti-inflammatory">anti-inflammatory</a> medications, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decongestants" title="Decongestants">decongestants</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analgesics" title="Analgesics">analgesics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan_2015d_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan_2015d-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outcomes">Outcomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Outcomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following barotrauma of the ears or lungs from diving the diver should not dive again until cleared by a diving doctor. After ear injury examination will include a hearing test and a demonstration that the middle ear can be autoinflated. Recovery can take weeks to months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHRF_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHRF-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Barotrauma_in_animals">Barotrauma in animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barotrauma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Barotrauma in animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Whales and dolphins suffer severely disabling barotrauma when exposed to excessive pressure changes induced by navy sonar, oil industry airguns, explosives, undersea earthquakes and volcanic eruptions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Injury and mortality of fish, marine mammals, including sea otters, seals, dolphins and whales, and birds by underwater explosions has been recorded in several studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Danil_and_St_Leger_2011_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Danil_and_St_Leger_2011-51">[51]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotrauma_and_Wind_turbines" title="Barotrauma and Wind turbines">Bats can suffer fatal barotrauma</a> in the low pressure zones behind the blades of wind turbines due to their more fragile mammalian lung structure in comparison with the more robust <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avian_lungs" title="Avian lungs">Avian lungs</a>, which are less affected by pressure change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_2008_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times_2008-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_Daily_2008_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science_Daily_2008-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swim_bladder_overexpansion">Swim bladder overexpansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span If the fish is brought upwards in the water column faster than the gas can be resorbed, the gas will expand until the bladder is stretched to its elastic limit, and may rupture.\nBarotrauma can be directly fatal or disable the fish rendering it vulnerable to predation, but <a href="/wiki/Sebastes" title="Sebastes">rockfish</a> are able to recover if they are returned to depths similar to those they were pulled up from, shortly after surfacing. Scientists at NOAA developed the Seaqualizer to quickly return rockfish to depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tripp_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tripp-54">[54]</a></sup> The device could increase
