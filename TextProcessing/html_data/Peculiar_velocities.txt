class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peculiar_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Galactic astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Galactic_astronomy" title="Galactic astronomy">galactic astronomy</a>, <b>peculiar motion</b> refers to the motion of an object (usually a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>) relative to a Galactic rest frame.\n</p><p>Local objects are often related in terms of <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a>, which can be combined through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(spatial)" title="Vector (spatial)">vector addition</a> to produce the object\'s motion relative to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Velocities for local objects are sometimes reported with respect to the <a href="/wiki/Local_standard_of_rest" title="Local standard of rest">local standard of rest</a> (LSR) &#8212; the average local motion of material in the galaxy &#8212; instead of the Sun\'s rest frame. Translating between the LSR and heliocentric rest frames requires the calculation of the Sun\'s peculiar velocity in the LSR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnras403_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mnras403-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmology">Cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peculiar_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cosmology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>, <b>peculiar velocity</b> refers to the components of a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>\'s velocity that deviate from the <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble flow</a>. According to Hubble\'s Law, galaxies recede from us at speeds proportional to their distance from us.\n</p><p>Galaxies are not distributed evenly throughout observable space, but are typically found in groups or <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">clusters</a>, where they have a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitational</a> effect on each other. <a href="/wiki/Velocity_dispersion" title="Velocity dispersion">Velocity dispersions</a> of galaxies arising from this gravitational attraction are usually in the hundreds of kilometers per second, but they can rise to over 1000 km/s in rich clusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Girardi93_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Girardi93-2">[2]</a></sup> This velocity can alter the <a href="/wiki/Recessional_velocity" title="Recessional velocity">recessional velocity</a> that  would be expected from the Hubble flow and effect the observed <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of objects via the <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Doppler_effect" title="Relativistic Doppler effect">relativistic Doppler effect</a>.
