href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">prediction</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>.\n</p><p>It has been a human desire for <a href="/wiki/Millennium" title="Millennium">millennia</a> to make accurate weather predictions. Oral and <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">written history</a> is full of <a href="/wiki/Rhyme" title="Rhyme">rhymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anecdote" title="Anecdote">anecdotes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adage" title="Adage">adages</a> meant to guide the uncertain in determining whether the next day will bring fair or foul weather. For the <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmer</a> wanting to plant crops, for the <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a> about to send ships on trade, foreknowledge of tomorrow\'s circumstances might mean the difference between success and failure. Prior to the invention of the mercury <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a>, it was very difficult to gather numerical data of any predictive value. Even though there were devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Weather_stick" title="Weather stick">weather stick</a> which gave some indication of moisture changes, the only instrument of any reliability was human experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-probably_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-probably-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Where weather happens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marestail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="134" data-file-width="200" decoding="async" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Marestail.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">Marestail</a> shows moisture at high altitude, signalling the later arrival of wet weather.</div></div></div>\n<p>It is in <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s middle latitudes, between roughly 30&#176; to 60&#176; North and South, that a significant portion of "<a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>" can be said to happen, that is, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_phenomenon" title="Meteorological phenomenon">meteorological phenomena</a> do not persist over the long term, and where it may be warm, sunny, and calm one day, and cold, overcast and stormy the next.\n</p><p>A great percentage of the world\'s population lives in the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> regions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but for the most part, these regions do not experience weather as it is understood by this definition. The <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> in northern Africa, for instance, is almost uniformly hot, sunny and dry all year long especially due to the non-stop presence of high atmospheric pressure aloft, whereas weather trends on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and in the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> belt, occur gradually over the very long term, and the diurnal weather patterns remain constant.\n</p><p>Weather <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>, therefore, refers to this mid-latitude region of daily variability. While most of it applies equally to the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, the Southern Hemisphere resident may need to take into account the fact that weather systems rotate opposite to those in the North. For instance, the "crossed winds"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crossed_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-crossed-4">[4]</a></sup> rule (see below) must be reversed for the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> reader.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sayings_which_may_be_locally_accurate">Sayings which may be locally accurate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sayings which may be locally accurate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Folkloric content usually has some locally based observational validity which allows these sayings to stand the test of time. Some of these observations may even have scientific explanations. Below are some of such examples.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="When_Clouds_Look_Like_Black_Smoke">When Clouds Look Like Black Smoke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: When Clouds Look Like Black Smoke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>When Clouds Look Like Black Smoke</i></dd>\n<dd><i>A Wise Man Will Put On His Cloak</i></dd></dl></dd></dl>\n<p>Thick, moisture laden storm clouds absorb sunlight. It gives them an appearance that somewhat resembles black smoke.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_sky_at_night">Red sky at night</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Red sky at night">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redskyatnight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="401" data-file-width="288" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Redskyatnight.jpg/150px-Redskyatnight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Redskyatnight.jpg/225px-Redskyatnight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Redskyatnight.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Redskyatnight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A red <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> probably<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-probably_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-probably-1">[1]</a></sup> means dry weather the next day.</div></div></div>\n<p>(See also <a href="/wiki/Red_sky_at_morning" title="Red sky at morning">Red sky at morning</a>)\n</p>\n<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>Red sky at night, shepherd\'s delight.</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Red sky in the morning, shepherd\'s warning.</i></dd></dl></dd></dl>\n<p>(In some variations, \'shepherd\' is replaced by \'sailor\')\n</p><p>A red sky &#8211; in the morning or evening, is a result of high pressure air in the atmosphere trapping particles of dust or soot. Air molecules scatter the shorter blue wavelengths of sunlight, but particles of dust, soot and other aerosols scatter the longer red wavelength of sunlight in a process called <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>. At sunrise and sunset, the sun is lower in the sky causing the sunlight to travel through more of the atmosphere so scattering more light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This effect is further enhanced when there are at least some high level clouds to reflect this light back to the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When weather systems predominantly move from <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">west</a> to <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">east</a>, a red sky at night indicates that the high pressure air (and better weather) is westwards. In the morning the light is eastwards, and so a red sky then indicates the high pressure (and better weather) has already passed, and an area of low pressure is following behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_pressure_regions">Low pressure regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Low pressure regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northwindbig.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="288" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Northwindbig.jpg/150px-Northwindbig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Northwindbig.jpg/225px-Northwindbig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Northwindbig.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northwindbig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cold, blustery northerly winds typically accompany a low pressure system.</div></div></div>\n<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>When the wind is blowing in the North</i></dd>\n<dd><i>No fisherman should set forth,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>When the wind is blowing in the East,</i></dd>\n<dd>\'<i>Tis not fit for man nor beast,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>When the wind is blowing in the South</i></dd>\n<dd><i>It brings the food over the fish\'s mouth,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>When the wind is blowing in the West,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>That is when the fishing\'s best!</i></dd></dl></dd></dl>\n<p>In western European seas, this description of wind direction is an excellent illustration of how the weather events of an active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> present themselves. With the approach of a low, easterly winds typically pick up. These gusty winds can be unpleasant for a number of reasons; they are often uncomfortably warm, dry, and dusty in the summer and bitterly cold in the winter. Northerly winds, which follow around a low, are cold and blustery. <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">Sailing</a> in conditions of northerly winds requires expertise and a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> capable of handling heavy waves. Southerly winds usually bring warm temperatures, and though they may not necessarily feed the fish, they do provide pleasant fishing weather. The best circumstance, however, is to have a westerly wind blowing; the wind condition is likely to persist for some time, the weather should remain fair and clear, and the wind should be relatively constant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Wind and weather observations will be different for a low passing to the north of the observer than for one passing to the south. When a low passes to the north, the winds typically pick up from the east, swing to southerly (possibly accompanied by light precipitation, usually not) with the passage of the low\'s warm front, and then switch to northwesterly or westerly as the cold front passes. Typically, if there is any heavy precipitation, it will accompany the passage of the cold front. When a low passes to the south, on the other hand, winds will initially pick up from the east, but will gradually shift to northerly. Overcast skies and steady precipitation often occur as the center of the low passes due south, but skies will clear and winds will gradually become westerly as the low moves off to the east. No observer will experience all the weather elements of a low in a single passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watts_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-watts-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Calm_conditions">Calm conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Calm conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>No weather is ill, if the wind be still.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Calm conditions, especially with clear skies, indicate the dominance of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">high pressure area</a>. Because highs are broad regions of descending air, they discourage the formation of phenomena typically associated with weather, such as clouds, wind, and precipitation. Calm conditions, though, may also result from a circumstance known as "the calm before the storm," in which a large thunderstorm cell to the west may be updrafting the westerly surface wind before it can arrive locally. This situation is readily identifiable by looking to the west &#8211; such an approaching storm will be close enough to be unmistakable. In winter, though, calm air and clear skies may signal the presence of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_high" title="Arctic high">Arctic high</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> typically accompanied by very cold air, and it is difficult to imagine describing a temperature of &#8211;35&#160;&#176;C (&#8211;31&#160;&#176;F) as pleasant.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="A_ring_around_the_Moon">A ring around the Moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Halos typically evolve into what is known as "milk sky", when the sky appears clear, but the typical blue is either washed-out or barely noticeable. This high, thick cirrostratus cloud is a clear indicator of an approaching low. In the coldest days of winter, a halo around the Sun is evidence of very cold and typically clear air at and above the surface. But <a href="/wiki/Sun_dog" title="Sun dog">sun dogs</a> are indicators that weather conditions are likely to change in the next 18 to 36 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity_indicators">Humidity indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Humidity indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>When <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">windows</a> won\'t open, and the <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> clogs the shaker,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>The weather will favour the <a href="/wiki/Umbrella" title="Umbrella">umbrella</a> maker!</i></dd></dl>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">Moisture</a> in the air causes <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> to swell, making doors and windows sticky, and salt is a very effective absorber of <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a>. With a high level of moisture in the air, the likelihood of precipitation is increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a> in iodized salt acts as an anti-clumping agent in humid conditions, leading to <a href="/wiki/Morton_Salt" title="Morton Salt">Morton Salt</a>\'s umbrella girl logo and slogan "When it rains, it pours".\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fog">Fog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>A summer <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> for fair,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>A winter fog for rain.</i></dd>\n<dd><i>A fact most everywhere,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>In <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valley</a> or on <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plain</a>.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Fog is formed when the air cools enough that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapour_pressure" title="Vapour pressure">vapour pressure</a> encourages <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> over <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. In order for the air to be cool on a summer night, the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> must be clear, so excess <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> can be radiated into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>. Cloudy skies act like a blanket, absorbing and reradiating the heat, keeping it in. So if it is cool enough (and clear enough) for fog to form, it will probably be clear the next day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Winter fog is the result of two entirely different circumstances. Above the ocean or a large lake, air is typically more humid than above land. When the humid air moves over cold land, it will form fog and precipitation. (To the east of the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_(North_America)" title="Great Lakes (North America)">Great Lakes</a>, this is a common phenomenon, and is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake effect</a>.")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In northerly climates, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">ice fog</a> may form when the temperature drops substantially below freezing. It is almost exclusively an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> phenomenon, when the air is so cold that any vapor pressure results in condensation, and additional vapour emitted by automobiles, household furnaces, and industrial plants simply accumulates as fog.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound_transmission">Sound transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sound transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>When <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sounds</a> travel far and wide,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>A stormy day will betide.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>This piece of lore is true in summer but conditionally false in winter. Moisture-laden air is a better conductor of sound than dry air, so moist air carries sounds farther. In winter, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> also becomes an important factor. If the air is warm and moist, the rule holds. If the air is very cold, it is also very dense and a better sound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conductor_(material)" title="Conductor (material)">conductor</a> than warm air, and also likely to be much drier. When sounds carry over a long distance, the cold, clear weather is likely to linger.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_movement">Cloud movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cloud movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>If clouds move against the wind, rain will follow.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>This rule may be true under a few special circumstances, otherwise it is false. By standing with one\'s back to the ground-level wind and observing the movement of the clouds, it is possible to determine whether the weather will improve or deteriorate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watts_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-watts-9">[9]</a></sup> For the Northern Hemisphere, it works like this: If the upper-level clouds are moving from the right, a low-pressure area has passed and the weather will improve; if from the left, a low pressure area is arriving and the weather will deteriorate. (Reverse for the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>.) This is known as the "crossed-winds" rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crossed_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-crossed-4">[4]</a></sup> Clouds traveling parallel to but against the wind may indicate a thunderstorm approaching. Outflow winds typically blow opposite to the updraft zone, and clouds carried in the upper level wind will appear to be moving against the surface wind. However, if such a storm is in the offing, it is not necessary to observe the cloud motions to know rain is a good possibility. The nature of airflows <i>directly at</i> a frontal boundary can also create conditions in which lower winds contradict the motions of upper clouds, and the passage of a frontal boundary is often marked by precipitation. Most often, however, this situation occurs in the lee of a low pressure area, to the north of the frontal zones and convergence region, and does not indicate a change in weather, but rather, that the weather, fair or showery, will remain so for a period of hours at least.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watts_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-watts-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aches_and_pains">Aches and pains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aches and pains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>A coming storm your shooting corns presage,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>And aches will throb, your hollow tooth will rage.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>There have been medical studies done which indicate some people experience this effect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The most likely reason is that with a fall in atmospheric pressure, <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a> dilate slightly in reaction. This has the effect of aggravating already-irritated nerves near corns, cavities, or <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritic joints</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Studies are inconclusive, however, with some researchers attributing this effect to selective memory.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fallibility_of_lore"><span id="False_lore,_and_why"></span> Fallibility of lore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Fallibility of lore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One of the problems in testing the veracity of traditions about the weather is the wide variety to be found in the details of sayings and traditions. Some variations are regional, while others exhibit less of a pattern.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_studies">Empirical studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Empirical studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>One case where weather lore has been studied for reliability against actual weather observations is the <a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">Groundhog Day</a> lore. It predicts that if the <a href="/wiki/Groundhog" title="Groundhog">groundhog</a> sees its shadow on this day (February 2), six weeks of winter remain. One analysis concluded the creature demonstrated no ability to predict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Other studies gave accuracy percentages, but differing figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[a]</a></sup> and some of the numbers were slightly better than hasarded guess (33% accurate), according to one source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radio-canada_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-radio-canada-18">[18]</a></sup> In other words, there is no appreciable correlation between cloud cover on that day, and the imminence of springlike weather.\n</p><p>There are some meteorological basis suggested, but is a fuzzy mechanism, and fixing a precise date compromises the effectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radio-canada_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-radio-canada-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calendrical_lore"><span id="Special_weather-forecasting_days"></span> Calendrical lore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Calendrical lore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are weather lore marked by dates on the year\'s calendar.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="February">February</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: February">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are weather lore around February 2, known as <a href="/wiki/Candlemas" title="Candlemas">Candlemas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigid%27s_Day" title="Brigid\'s Day">Brigid\'s Day</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Blaise" title="St. Blaise">St. Blaise</a>\'s Day (St. Blaze\'s Day). One French lore says that if it rains on Candlemas (<i>Chandeleur</i>) there will be forty more days of rainy day: <i>Quand il pleut pour la Chandeleur, il pleut pendant quarante jours</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">Groundhog Day</a> also falls on this day.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Candlemas_and_animal">Candlemas and animal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Candlemas and animal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">Groundhog Day</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Groundhog_Day" title="Groundhog Day">Groundhog Day</a>, observed in the U. S. and Canada, also falls on February 2 and is thought to derive from the Candlemas weather lore in Europe, particularly the German which features the badger as the predictor. An example of such German weather rhyme translates as:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>If the badger is in the sun at Candlemas,</i></dd>\n<dd>he will have to go back into his hole for another four weeks<i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_traditions_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-German_traditions-23">[21]</a></sup></i></dd></dl>\n<p>There are also French counterparts. One for <a href="/wiki/Saint-Vallier,_Vosges" title="Saint-Vallier, Vosges">Saint-Vallier</a> in Lorraine states:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>If it is fair weather on Candlemas, </i></dd>\n<dd>the bear returns to its cave for six weeks<i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[c]</a></sup></i></dd></dl>\n<p>And another from <a href="/wiki/Courbesseaux" title="Courbesseaux">Courbesseaux</a> says that if it is sunny on Candlemas the wolf returns to its cave for six weeks, and if not, for forty days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In French Canada, it may be a marmot or groundhog (<i>siffleux</i>), bear, skunk, otter etc. which if it sees its shadow on Candlemas, causes winter to prolong for 40 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>English traditional weather lore recites, \n"If Candlemas Day be fair and bright\nWinter will have another fight\nIf Candlemas Day brings cloud and rain\nWinter won\'t come again" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="March">March</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: March">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lion_and_Lamb">Lion and Lamb</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span early March weather:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>If March comes in like a <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>, it will go out like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep">lamb</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[26]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="March_thunderstorms">March thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: March thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>When March blows its <a href="/wiki/Horn_(acoustic)" title="Horn (acoustic)">horn</a>,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>your <a class="mw-redirect" its horn" refers to thunderstorms. While March thunderstorms indicate that the weather is unusually warm for that time of year (thunderstorms can occur only with a sufficiently large <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> difference between ground and sky and sufficient amounts of moisture to produce charge differential within a cloud).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="July">July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Swithun%27s_day" title="St Swithun\'s day">Saint Swithun\'s day</a> (July 15) is said to forecast the weather for the rest of the summer. If St Swithun\'s day is dry, then the legend says that the next forty days will also be dry: "St.Swithin\'s Day if thou be fair, \'Twill rain for forty days no mair; St. Swithin\'s Day if thou dost rain, For forty days it will remain.". If however it rains, the rain will continue for forty days.\n</p><p>There is a scientific basis to the legend of St Swithun\'s day. Around the middle of July, the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> settles into a pattern which, in the majority of years, holds reasonably steady until the end of August. When the jet stream lies north of the British Isles then continental high pressure is able to move in; when it lies across or south of the British Isles, Arctic air and Atlantic weather systems predominate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_feast_days">Other feast days</span><span Sleepers Day</a> (June 27).\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the weather on the feast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Protection_of_the_Mother_of_God" title="The Protection of the Mother of God">Protecting Veil</a> is popularly believed to indicate the severity of the forthcoming winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was an old <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">proverb</a> from <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> that ran: "Par San Paternian e\' trema la coda a e\' can."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> ("On <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Paternian" title="St. Paternian">St. Paternian</a>\'s day, the dog\'s tail wags"). This Cervian proverb refers to the fact that the cold began to be felt around the saint\'s feast day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A farmers\' saying associated with <a href="/wiki/Quirinus_of_Neuss" title="Quirinus of Neuss">Quirinus\'</a> feast day of March 30 was "Wie der Quirin, so der Sommer" ("As St. Quirinus\' Day goes, so will the summer").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ice_Saints" class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast days</a> of St. <a href="/wiki/Mamertus" title="Mamertus">Mamertus</a>, St. <a href="/wiki/Pancras_of_Rome" title="Pancras of id="Biological_signs">Biological signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Biological signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_signs">Animal signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Animal signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seagulls">Seagulls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Seagulls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Sometimes the lore is concurrent with existing conditions, more than prediction, as in:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seagull" title="Seagull">Seagull</a>, seagull sit on the <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>.</i></dd>\n<dd><i>It\'s never good weather when you\'re on land.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Seagulls tend to sleep on the water. However, seagulls, like people, find gusty, <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent</a> wind difficult to contend with, and under such circumstances, the water is also choppy and unpleasant. Seagulls huddled on the ground may be a sign that the weather is already bad.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cows_in_pasture">Cows in pasture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Cows in pasture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg/200px-Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg/300px-Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg/400px-Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Do_cows_know_if_it%27s_going_to_rain%5E_-_geograph.org.uk_-_993288.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"When cows are lying down in a field, rain is on its way" may not be a scientific prediction, and yet animal behavior at times of changing pressure systems makes for interesting theories. Are cool updrafts or the hovering of flies on their bellies a factor?</div></div></div>\n<dl><dd><i>A cow with its tail to the West makes the weather best,</i></dd>\n<dd><i>A cow with its tail to the East makes the weather least</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Cows prefer not to have the wind blowing in their faces, and so typically stand with their backs to the wind. Since westerly winds typically mean arriving or continuing fair weather and easterly winds usually indicate arriving or continuing unsettled weather, a cowvane is as good a way as any of knowing what the weather will be up to for the next few hours.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pets_eating_grass">Pets eating grass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Pets eating grass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i>Cats and dogs eat grass before a rain.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>While it is true that cats and dogs eat grass, it has nothing to do with the weather and is because cats and dogs are not exclusively carnivorous. Some researchers believe that dogs eat grass as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emetic" title="Emetic">emetic</a> when feeling ill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[31]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_signs">Plant signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Plant signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Onion_skins">Onion skins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Onion skins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd><i><a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">Onion</a> skins very thin</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Mild winter coming in;</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Onion skins thick and tough</i></dd>\n<dd><i>Coming winter cold and rough.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[32]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>This verse, and so many others like it, attempts to predict long-range conditions. These predictions have stood the test of time only because they rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selective_memory" title="Selective memory">selective memory</a>: people remember when they have predicted correctly and forget when predictions don\'t hold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[33]</a></sup> One possible factor which could provide these predictions with a thin edge of credibility is that there is some degree of consistency in weather from year to year. <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">Drought</a> cycles or <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni&#241;o">El Ni&#241;o</a> winters are a perfect example of such circumstances. A pattern of cool summers and warm winters, for instance, can produce patterns in other natural events sensitive enough to be affected by changes in temperature or precipitation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_signs">Meteorological signs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Meteorological signs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early-morning_rain">Early-morning rain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_lore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Early-morning rain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>Rain before seven, clear by eleven.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Late-night rains and early morning rains may simply be the last precipitation of a passing weather front. However, since fronts pass at night as often as they do in the day, morning rain is no predictor of a dry afternoon. However, this lore can describe non-frontal weather. Given sufficient surface heating, a late-day rainstorm may continue to develop into the night, produce early precipitation, then dissipate by late morning. This, though, is the exception rather than the rule.
