title="Sunset">sunset</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, USA</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>horizon</b> or <b>skyline</b> is the apparent line that separates <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a>, the line that divides all visible directions into two categories: those that intersect the Earth\'s surface, and those that do not.\nThe <i>true horizon</i> is actually a theoretical line, which can only be observed when it lies on the sea surface. At many locations, this line is obscured by land, trees, buildings, mountains, etc., and the resulting intersection of earth and sky is called the <i>visible horizon</i>. When looking at a sea from a shore, the part of the sea closest to the horizon is called the <b>offing</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WebstersThird_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WebstersThird-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The true horizon is horizontal. It surrounds the observer and it is typically assumed to be a circle, drawn on the surface of a perfectly spherical model of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Its center is below the observer and below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. Its distance from the observer varies from day to day due to <a href="#Effect_of_atmospheric_refraction">atmospheric refraction</a>, which is greatly affected by <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> conditions. Also, the higher the observer\'s eyes are from sea level, the farther away is the horizon from the observer. For instance, in <a href="/wiki/Reference_atmospheric_model" title="Reference atmospheric model">standard athmosferic conditions</a>, for an observer with eye level above sea level by 1.70 metres (5&#160;ft 7&#160;in), the horizon is at a distance of about 5 kilometres (3.1&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATYoungDistToHoriz_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATYoungDistToHoriz-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When observed from very high standpoints, such as a <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a>, the horizon is much farther away and it encompasses a much larger area of Earth\'s surface. In this case, it becomes evident that the horizon more closely resembles an ellipse than a perfect circle, especially when the observer is above the equator, and that the Earth\'s surface can be better modeled as an <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a> than as a sphere.\n</p><p>The word <i>horizon</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> "&#8001;&#961;&#943;&#950;&#969;&#957; &#954;&#973;&#954;&#955;&#959;&#962;" <i>horiz&#333;n kyklos</i>, "separating circle",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, where "&#8001;&#961;&#943;&#950;&#969;&#957;" is from the verb <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%81%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B6%CF%89" title="wikt:&#8001;&#961;&#943;&#950;&#969;">&#8001;&#961;&#943;&#950;&#969;</a> <i>horiz&#333;</i>, "to divide", "to separate",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which in turn derives from "&#8005;&#961;&#959;&#962;" (<i>oros</i>), "boundary, landmark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" id="Appearance_and_usage">Appearance and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sailing_Boat_Horizon.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2160" data-file-width="3840" decoding="async" height="124" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Sailing_Boat_Horizon.JPG/440px-Sailing_Boat_Horizon.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sailing_Boat_Horizon.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of the ocean with a ship near to the horizon</div></div></div>\n<p>Historically, the distance to the visible horizon has long been vital to survival and successful navigation, especially at sea, because it determined an observer\'s maximum range of vision and thus of <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>, with all the obvious consequences for safety and the transmission of information that this range implied.  This importance lessened with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraph</a>, but even today, when flying an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> under <a href="/wiki/Visual_flight_rules" title="Visual flight rules">visual flight rules</a>, a technique called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attitude_flying" title="Attitude flying">attitude flying</a> is used to control the aircraft, where the pilot uses the visual relationship between the aircraft\'s nose and the horizon to control the aircraft. A pilot can also retain his or her <a href="/wiki/Spatial_disorientation" title="Spatial disorientation">spatial orientation</a> by referring to the horizon.\n</p><p>In many contexts, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_projection" title="Perspective projection">perspective</a> drawing, the curvature of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is disregarded and the horizon is considered the theoretical line to which points on any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" title="Horizontal plane">horizontal plane</a> converge (when projected onto the picture plane) as their distance from the observer increases. For observers near <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> the difference between this <i>geometrical horizon</i> (which assumes a perfectly flat, infinite ground plane) and the <i>true horizon</i> (which assumes a <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">spherical Earth</a> surface) is imperceptible to the naked eye<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2011)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Horizon#Dubious" title="Talk:Horizon">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> (but for someone on a 1000-meter hill looking out to sea the true horizon will be about a degree below a horizontal line).\n</p><p>In astronomy the horizon is the horizontal plane through the eyes of the observer. It is the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_plane_(spherical_coordinates)" title="Fundamental plane (spherical coordinates)">fundamental plane</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system" title="Horizontal coordinate system">horizontal coordinate system</a>, the locus of points that have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)" title="Altitude (astronomy)">altitude</a> of zero degrees. While similar in ways to the geometrical horizon, in this context a horizon may be considered to be a plane in space, rather than a line on a picture plane.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_to_the_horizon">Distance to the horizon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distance to the horizon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One typically sees further along the Earth\'s curved surface than a simple geometric calculation allows for because of <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> error. If the ground, or water, surface is colder than the air above it, a cold, dense layer of air forms close to the surface, causing light to be refracted downward as it travels, and therefore, to some extent, to go around the curvature of the Earth. The reverse happens if the ground is hotter than the air above it, as often happens in deserts, producing <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">mirages</a>. As an approximate compensation for refraction, surveyors measuring distances longer than 100 meters subtract 14% from the calculated curvature error and ensure lines of sight are at least 1.5 meters from the ground, to reduce random errors created by refraction.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pale_Horizon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2280" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Pale_Horizon.jpg/220px-Pale_Horizon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Pale_Horizon.jpg/330px-Pale_Horizon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Pale_Horizon.jpg/440px-Pale_Horizon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pale_Horizon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical desert horizon</div></div></div>\n<p>However, ignoring the <a href="#Effect_of_atmospheric_refraction">effect of atmospheric refraction</a>, distance to the true horizon from an observer close to the Earth\'s surface is about<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATYoungDistToHoriz_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATYoungDistToHoriz-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle d\\approx 3.57{\\sqrt {h}}\\,,}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>d</mi>\n<mo>&#8776;<!-- &#8776; However, Earth has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere of air</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> vary considerably depending on the temperature and pressure. This makes the air <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">refract light</a> to varying extents, affecting the appearance of the horizon. Usually, the density of the air just above the surface of the Earth is greater than its density at greater altitudes. This makes its <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> greater near the surface than at higher altitudes, which causes light that is travelling roughly horizontally to be refracted downward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProctorRanyard1892_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ProctorRanyard1892-7">[7]</a></sup> This makes the actual distance to the horizon greater than the distance calculated with geometrical formulas. With standard atmospheric conditions, the difference is about 8%. This changes the factor of 3.57, in the metric formulas used above, to about 3.86.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATYoungDistToHoriz_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ATYoungDistToHoriz-2">[2]</a></sup> For instance, if an observer is standing on seashore, with eyes 1,70 m above sea level, according to the simple geometrical formulas given above the horizon should be 4,7 km away. Actually, atmospheric refraction allows the observer to see 300 metres farther, moving the true horizon 5 km away from the observer.\n</p><p>This correction can be, and often is, applied as a fairly good approximation when atmospheric conditions are close to <a href="/wiki/Reference_atmospheric_model" title="Reference atmospheric model">standard</a>. When conditions are unusual, this approximation fails. Refraction is strongly affected by temperature gradients, which can vary considerably from day to day, especially over water. In extreme cases, usually in springtime, when warm air overlies cold water, refraction can allow light to follow the Earth\'s surface for hundreds of kilometres. Opposite conditions occur, for example, in deserts, where the surface is very hot, so hot, low-density air is below cooler air. This causes light to be refracted upward, causing <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">mirage</a> effects that make the concept of the horizon somewhat meaningless. Calculated values for the effects of refraction under unusual conditions are therefore only approximate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATYoungDistToHoriz_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ATYoungDistToHoriz-2">[2]</a></sup> Nevertheless, attempts have been made to calculate them more accurately than the simple approximation described above.\n</p><p>Outside the visual wavelength range, refraction will be different. For <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> (e.g. for wavelengths 300 to 3&#160;mm i.e. frequencies between 1 and 100&#160;GHz) the radius of the Earth may be multiplied by 4/3 to obtain an effective radius giving a factor of 4.12 in the metric formula i.e. the radar horizon will be 15% beyond the geometrical horizon or 7% beyond the visual. A curvature of 1.0 appears as a circle of an angular radius of 57.3&#176; corresponding to an altitude of approximately 2,640&#160;km (1,640&#160;mi) above Earth\'s surface. At an altitude of 10&#160;km (6.2&#160;mi; 33,000&#160;ft), the <a href="/wiki/Cruise_(aeronautics)" title="Cruise (aeronautics)">cruising</a> altitude of a typical airliner, the mathematical curvature of the horizon is about 0.056, the same curvature of the rim of circle with a radius of 10&#160;m that is viewed from 56&#160;cm directly above the center of the circle. However, the apparent curvature is less than that due to <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">refraction</a> of light by the atmosphere and the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">obscuration</a> of the horizon by high cloud layers that reduce the altitude above the visual surface.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vanishing_points">Vanishing points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horizon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vanishing points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TwoPointPerspective.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="723" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/TwoPointPerspective.png/210px-TwoPointPerspective.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/TwoPointPerspective.png/315px-TwoPointPerspective.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/TwoPointPerspective.png/420px-TwoPointPerspective.png 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TwoPointPerspective.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two points on the horizon are at the intersections of the lines extending the segments representing the edges of the building in the foreground. The horizon line coincides here with the line at the top of the doors and windows.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">Vanishing point</a></div>\n<p>The horizon is a key feature of the <a href="/wiki/Picture_plane" title="Picture plane">picture plane</a> in the science of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphical_perspective" title="Graphical perspective">graphical perspective</a>. Assuming the picture plane stands vertical to ground, and <i>P</i> is the perpendicular projection of the eye point <i>O</i> on the picture plane, the horizon is defined as the horizontal line through <i>P</i>. The point <i>P</i> is the vanishing point of lines perpendicular to the picture. If <i>S</i> is another point on the horizon, then it is the vanishing point for all lines <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_lines" title="Parallel lines">parallel</a> to <i>OS</i>. But <a href="/wiki/Brook_Taylor" title="Brook Taylor">Brook Taylor</a> (1719) indicated that the horizon plane determined by <i>O</i> and the horizon was like any other <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>The term of Horizontal Line, for instance, is apt to confine the Notions of a Learner to the Plane of the Horizon, and to make him imagine, that that Plane enjoys some particular Privileges, which make the Figures in it more easy and more convenient to be described, by the means of that Horizontal Line, than the Figures in any other plane;&#8230;But in this Book I make no difference between the Plane of the Horizon, and any other Plane whatsoever...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>The peculiar geometry of perspective where parallel lines converge in the distance, stimulated the development of <a href="/wiki/Projective_geometry" title="Projective geometry">projective geometry</a> which posits a <a href="/wiki/Point_at_infinity" title="Point at infinity">point at infinity</a> where parallel lines meet. In her book <i>Geometry of an Art</i> (2007), <a href="/wiki/Kirsti_Andersen" title="Kirsti Andersen">Kirsti Andersen</a> described the evolution of perspective drawing and science up to 1800, noting that vanishing points need not be on the horizon. In a chapter titled "Horizon", <a href="/wiki/John_Stillwell" title="John Stillwell">John Stillwell</a> recounted how projective geometry has led to <a href="/wiki/Incidence_geometry" title="Incidence geometry">incidence geometry</a>, the modern abstract study of line intersection. Stillwell also ventured into <a href="/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics#Projective_geometry" title="Foundations of mathematics">foundations of mathematics</a> in a section titled "What are the Laws of Algebra&#160;?" The "algebra of points", originally given by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_von_Staudt" title="Karl von Staudt">Karl von Staudt</a> deriving the axioms of a <a href="/wiki/Field_(mathematics)" title="Field (mathematics)">field</a> was deconstructed in the twentieth century, yielding a wide variety of mathematical possibilities. Stillwell states\n</p>\n<dl><dd>This discovery from 100 years ago seems capable of turning mathematics upside down, though it has not yet been fully absorbed by the mathematical community. Not only does it defy the trend of turning geometry into algebra, it suggests that both geometry and algebra have a simpler foundation than previously thought.<sup class="reference"
