S<sub>g</sub>)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>geomagnetic poles</b> are <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">antipodal points</a> where the axis of a best-fitting <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a> <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(Euclidean_geometry)" title="Intersection (Euclidean geometry)">intersects</a> the surface of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. This theoretical dipole is equivalent to a powerful bar <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Inner_core" title="Inner core">center of Earth</a> and comes closer than any other model to accounting for the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> observed at Earth\'s surface. In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">magnetic poles</a> of the actual Earth are not <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">antipodal</a>; that is, the line on which they lie does not pass through Earth\'s center.\n</p><p>Owing to motion of fluid in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer core</a>, the actual magnetic poles are constantly moving. However, over thousands of years their direction averages to the Earth\'s rotation axis. On the order of once every half a million years, the <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">poles reverse</a> (north switches place with south).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orders_of_approximation" title="Orders of approximation">first-order approximation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">Earth\'s magnetic field</a> can be modeled as a simple <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a> (like a bar magnet), tilted about 9.6&#176; with respect to the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">Earth\'s rotation</a> axis (which defines the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">Geographic North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">Geographic South Poles</a>) and centered at the Earth\'s center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGDC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGDC-1">[1]</a></sup> The North and South Geomagnetic Poles are the <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">antipodal points</a> where the axis of this theoretical dipole intersects the Earth\'s surface, thus unlike the magnetic poles they always have an equal degree of latitude and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplementary_angles" title="Supplementary angles">supplementary</a> degrees of longitude respectively (2017: Lat. 80.5&#176;N, 80.5&#176;S; Long. 72.8&#176;W, 107.2&#176;E).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> If the Earth\'s magnetic field were a perfect dipole then the <a href="/wiki/Field_line" title="Field line">field lines</a> would be vertical to the surface at the Geomagnetic Poles, and they would coincide with the <a href="/wiki/North_Magnetic_Pole" title="North Magnetic Pole">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Magnetic_Pole" title="South Magnetic Pole">South</a> magnetic poles. However, the approximation is imperfect, and so the Magnetic and Geomagnetic Poles lie some distance apart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill1996ch2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merrill1996ch2-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Like the <a href="/wiki/North_Magnetic_Pole" title="North Magnetic Pole">North Magnetic Pole</a>, the North Geomagnetic Pole attracts the north pole of a bar <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnet</a> and so is in a physical sense actually a magnetic <i>south</i> pole. It is the center of the \'open\' magnetic field lines which connect to the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a> and provide a direct route for the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> to reach the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. As of  2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> it was located at approximately <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;params=80.37_N_72.62_W_&amp;title=Geomagnetic+North+Pole+2015+est"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">80&#176;22&#8242;N</span> <span class="longitude">72&#176;37&#8242;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">80.37&#176;N 72.62&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">80.37; -72.62</span></span><span style="display:none">&#65279; (<span class="fn org">Geomagnetic North Pole 2015 est</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, on <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Island" title="Ellesmere Island">Ellesmere Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGDC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NGDC-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The South Geomagnetic Pole is the point where the axis of this best-fitting tilted dipole intersects the Earth\'s surface in the southern hemisphere. As of 2005 it was calculated to be located at 79.74&#176;S 108.22&#176;E, near the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok Station</a>. \n</p><p>Because the Earth\'s actual magnetic field is not an exact dipole, the (calculated) North and South Geomagnetic Poles do not coincide with the North and South Magnetic Poles. If the Earth\'s magnetic fields were exactly dipolar, the north pole of a magnetic <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> needle would point directly at the North Geomagnetic Pole. In practice it does not because the geomagnetic field that originates in the core has a more complex non-dipolar part, and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_anomaly" title="Magnetic anomaly">magnetic anomalies</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth\'s crust">Earth\'s crust</a> also contribute to the local field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGDC_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NGDC-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The locations of geomagnetic poles are calculated by the <a href="/wiki/International_Geomagnetic_Reference_Field" title="International Geomagnetic Reference Field">International Geomagnetic Reference Field</a>, a statistical fit to measurements of the Earth\'s field by satellites and in geomagnetic observatories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAGA2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAGA2010-4">[4]</a></sup> The Geomagnetic Poles are wandering for the same reason the Magnetic Poles wander.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement">Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The geomagnetic poles move over time because the geomagnetic field is produced by motion of the molten iron alloys in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer core</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodynamo" title="Geodynamo">geodynamo</a>). Over the past 150 years the poles have moved westward at a rate of 0.05&#176; to 0.1&#176; per year, with little net north or south motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill1996ch2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merrill1996ch2-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over several thousand years, the average location of the geomagnetic poles coincides with the geographical poles. <a href="/wiki/Paleomagnetism" title="Paleomagnetism">Paleomagnetists</a> have long relied on the <i>Geocentric axial dipole (GAD) hypothesis</i>, which states that, aside from during geomagnetic reversals, the time-averaged position of the geomagnetic poles has always coincided with the geographic poles. There is considerable paleomagnetic evidence supporting this hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merrill1996ch6_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merrill1996ch6-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetic_reversal">Geomagnetic reversal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geomagnetic reversal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">Geomagnetic reversal</a></div>\n<p>Over the life of the Earth, the orientation of Earth\'s magnetic field has reversed many times, with geomagnetic north becoming geomagnetic south and vice versa &#8211; an event known as a <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">geomagnetic reversal</a>. Evidence of geomagnetic reversals can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a> where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a> move apart. As <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> seeps out of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and solidifies to become new ocean floor, the magnetic minerals in it are magnetized in the direction of the magnetic field. Thus, starting at the most recently formed ocean floor, one can read out the direction of the magnetic field in previous times as one moves farther away to older ocean floor.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dipole_model_of_the_Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Dipole model of the Earth\'s magnetic field">Dipole model of the Earth\'s magnetic field</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-NGDC-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-NGDC_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NGDC_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-NGDC_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/faqgeom.shtml" rel="nofollow">"Geomagnetism Frequently Asked Questions"</a>.
