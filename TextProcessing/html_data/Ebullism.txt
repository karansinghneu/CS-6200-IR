class="mw-indicators mw-body-content">\n</div>\n<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ebullism</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The formation of gas bubbles in bodily fluids due to reduced environmental pressure</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Embolism" title="Embolism">Embolism</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Ebullism</b> is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_bubble" title="Liquid bubble">bubbles</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodily_fluids" title="Bodily fluids">bodily fluids</a> due to reduced environmental pressure, for example at high <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>.  It occurs because a system of liquid and gas at equilibrium will see a net conversion of liquid to gas as pressure lowers, for example, liquids reach their boiling point at lower temperatures when the pressure on them is lowered.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Symptoms"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Symptoms</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Mechanism"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Mechanism</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Prevention"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Prevention</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Etymology"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Etymology</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms">Symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebullism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Symptoms of ebullism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> include bubbles in the membranes of the mouth and eyes, swelling of the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, and bubbles in the blood. Blood circulation and breathing may be impaired or stopped. The <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> tissue may be <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">starved</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> because of blockage of <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arteries</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a> may swell and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhage" title="Hemorrhage">hemorrhage</a>. Death results unless recompression is rapid enough to reduce the bubbles before tissue damage occurs.\n</p><p>In 1960, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kittinger" title="Joseph Kittinger">Joseph Kittinger</a> experienced localised ebullism during a 31 kilometres (19&#160;mi) ascent in a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>-driven gondola.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pilmanis2003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pilmanis2003-2">[2]</a></sup> His right-hand glove failed to pressurise and his hand expanded to roughly twice its normal volume<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_May_24,_2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_May_24,_2008-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  accompanied by disabling pain. His hand took about 3 hours to recover after his return to the ground.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebullism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the atmospheric pressure present at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>, water boils at 100&#160;&#176;C (212&#160;&#176;F). At an altitude of 63,000 feet (19,000&#160;m), it boils at only 37&#160;&#176;C (99&#160;&#176;F), the normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_temperature" title="Body temperature">body temperature</a> of humans.  This altitude is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Armstrong_limit" title="Armstrong limit">Armstrong&#8217;s Line</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fundamentals_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fundamentals-5">[5]</a></sup> In practice bodily fluids do not boil off at this altitude. This is because the skin and outer organs have enough strength to withstand this pressure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> thus pressure inside the body would be significantly higher&#8212;however, bubbles starting to form is still an issue.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebullism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>To prevent ebullism, a pure oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) atmosphere was used in early space flights to eliminate nitrogen in the blood. There are major fire hazards associated with using pure O<sub>2</sub> as a breathing gas, which was central to the death of three astronauts in a fire during a ground test with <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apollo1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-apollo1-7">[7]</a></sup> Nonetheless NASA continued to use a [nominally] pure oxygen atmosphere throughout the Apollo Program but switched to air for the follow-on Space Transport System "Space Shuttle". Russian cosmonauts used pure oxygen before changing to a higher-pressure <a href="/wiki/Nitrox" title="Nitrox">nitrox</a> mixture, leading to incompatibility problems in 1975 on the Apollo-Soyuz Test Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceline_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-spaceline-8">[8]</a></sup> Space suits are often pressurized to several <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> lower than stations\' capsules or shuttles and since they still use pure O<sub>2</sub>, an acclimation period is common in the airlock to remove nitrogen and other gases from the bloodstream.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ebullism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "space ebullism" was introduced by Captain Julian E. Ward in his paper "The True Nature of the Boiling of Body Fluids in Space", published in Aviation Medicine in October of 1956<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.  It was suggested "because the word ebullism does not connote the addition of heat to produce vapor." It comes from Latin ebullire, meaning "to bubble out, or to boil up."\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
