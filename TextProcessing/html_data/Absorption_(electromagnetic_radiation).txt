This example discusses the general principle using <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible light</a> as specific example. A white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_source" title="Light source">light source</a> &#8212; emitting light of multiple <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> &#8212; is focused on a sample (the pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_color" title="Complementary color">complementary colors</a> are indicated by the yellow dotted lines). Upon striking the sample, <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> that match the energy gap of the <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> present (green light in this example) are <i>absorbed</i>, exciting the molecules. Other photons are transmitted unaffected and, if the radiation is in the visible region (400&#8211;700&#160;nm), the transmitted light appears as the complementary color (here red). By recording the <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a> of light for various wavelengths, an <a href="/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy" title="Absorption spectroscopy">absorption spectrum</a> can be obtained.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>absorption</b> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> is the way in which the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is taken up by matter, typically the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>. Thus, the <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">electromagnetic energy</a> is transformed into <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> of the absorber, for example <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The reduction in intensity of a light wave <a href="/wiki/Wave_propagation" title="Wave propagation">propagating</a> through a medium by absorption of a part of its photons is often called <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a>. Usually, the absorption of waves does not depend on their intensity (linear absorption), although in certain conditions (usually, in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>), the medium changes its transparency dependently on the intensity of waves going through, and <a href="/wiki/Saturable_absorption" title="Saturable absorption">saturable absorption</a> (or nonlinear absorption) occurs.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" id="Quantifying_absorption">Quantifying absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Quantifying absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_descriptions_of_opacity" title="Mathematical descriptions of opacity">Mathematical descriptions of opacity</a></div>\n<p>There are a number of ways to quantify how quickly and effectively radiation is absorbed in a certain medium, for example the absorption coefficient, and some closely related derived quantities.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Attenuation_coefficient" title="Attenuation coefficient">attenuation coefficient</a>, which is sometimes but not always synonymous with the absorption coefficient.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molar_absorptivity" title="Molar absorptivity">Molar absorptivity</a> is called "molar extinction coefficient", which is the absorption coefficient divided by molarity (see also <a href="/wiki/Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law" title="Beer&#8211;Lambert law">Beer&#8211;Lambert law</a>).\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mass_attenuation_coefficient" title="Mass attenuation coefficient">mass attenuation coefficient</a> is also called "mass extinction coefficient", which is the absorption coefficient divided by density.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Absorption_cross_section" title="Absorption cross section">absorption cross section</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scattering_cross-section" title="Scattering cross-section">scattering cross-section</a> are closely related to the absorption and attenuation coefficients, respectively.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">"Extinction" in astronomy</a> is equivalent to the attenuation coefficient.\n</p><p>Other measures of radiation absorption are <a href="/wiki/Penetration_depth" title="Penetration depth">penetration depth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skin_effect" title="Skin effect">skin effect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propagation_constant" title="Propagation constant">propagation constant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propagation_constant#Attenuation_constant" title="Propagation constant">attenuation constant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propagation_constant#Phase_constant" title="Propagation constant">phase constant</a>, and complex <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a>,\n<a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">complex refractive index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index#Dispersion_and_absorption" title="Refractive index">extinction coefficient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Relative_permittivity" title="Relative permittivity">complex dielectric constant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity" title="Electrical resistivity and conductivity">electrical resistivity and conductivity</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">Absorbance</a> (also called "optical density") and <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a> (also called "optical thickness") are two related measures.\n</p><p>All these quantities measure, at least to some extent, how well a medium absorbs radiation. However, practitioners of different fields and techniques tend to conventionally use different quantities drawn from the list above.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_absorption">Measuring absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measuring absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorbance</a> of an object quantifies how much of the incident light is absorbed by it (instead of being <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected</a> or <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a>). This may be related to other properties of the object through the <a href="/wiki/Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law" title="Beer&#8211;Lambert law">Beer&#8211;Lambert law</a>.\n</p><p>Precise measurements of the absorbance at many wavelengths allow the identification of a substance via <a href="/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy" title="Absorption spectroscopy">absorption spectroscopy</a>, where a sample is illuminated from one side, and the intensity of the light that exits from the sample in every direction is measured. A few examples of absorption are <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet%E2%80%93visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet&#8211;visible spectroscopy">ultraviolet&#8211;visible spectroscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/X-ray_absorption_spectroscopy" title="X-ray absorption spectroscopy">X-ray absorption spectroscopy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span light</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Understanding and measuring the absorption of electromagnetic radiation has a variety of applications.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">Radio propagation</a> it is represented in <a href="/wiki/Non-line-of-sight_propagation" title="Non-line-of-sight propagation">Non-line-of-sight propagation</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, global and local temperatures depend in part on the absorption of radiation by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_gases" title="Atmospheric gases">atmospheric gases</a> (such as in the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>) and land and ocean surfaces (see <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>).\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> are absorbed to different extents by different tissues (<a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a> in particular), which is the basis for <a href="/wiki/Projectional_radiography" title="Projectional radiography">X-ray imaging</a>. For example, see <a href="/wiki/Computation_of_radiowave_attenuation_in_the_atmosphere" title="Computation of radiowave attenuation in the atmosphere">computation of radiowave attenuation in the atmosphere</a> used in satellite link design.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, because different materials and molecules will absorb radiation to different extents at different frequencies, which allows for material identification.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, sunglasses, colored filters, dyes, and other such materials are designed specifically with respect to which visible wavelengths they absorb, and in what proportions.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, photosynthetic organisms require that light of the appropriate wavelengths be absorbed within the active area of <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>, so that the <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> energy can be converted into <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> within sugars and other molecules.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, the D-region of Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> is known to significantly absorb radio signals that fall within the high-frequency electromagnetic spectrum.\n</p><p>In nuclear physics, absorption of nuclear radiations can be used for measuring the fluid levels, densitometry or thickness measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
