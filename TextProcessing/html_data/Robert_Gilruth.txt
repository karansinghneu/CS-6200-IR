Johnson Space Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He worked for the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> from 1937 to 1958 and its successor NASA, until his retirement in 1973. He was involved with early research into supersonic flight and rocket-powered aircraft, and then with the United States manned space program, including the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> programs.\n</p>\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_R._Gilruth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_R._Gilruth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gilruth was born October 8, 1913, in <a href="/wiki/Nashwauk,_Minnesota" title="Nashwauk, Minnesota">Nashwauk, Minnesota</a>, and moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duluth" title="Duluth">Duluth</a> when he was nine years old. He graduated  in 1931 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duluth_Central_High_School" title="Duluth Central High School">Duluth Central High School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As a teenager, Gilruth was fascinated by aeronautics and spent time building model airplanes. He was inspired to pursue a career in the field after reading about NACA&#8217;s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langley_Memorial_Aeronautical_Laboratory" title="Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory">Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory</a> in Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin-3">[3]</a></sup> Gilruth received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeronautical_Engineering" title="Aeronautical Engineering">Aeronautical Engineering</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> in 1935, and received his <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in 1936.  While there he was a member of the Professional Engineering Fraternity <a href="/wiki/Theta_Tau" title="Theta Tau">Theta Tau</a>, of which he was later inducted as a Hall of Fame Alumnus.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_test_career">Flight test career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_R._Gilruth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flight test career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In January 1937 Gilruth was hired at NACA&#8217;s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langley_Memorial_Aeronautical_Laboratory" title="Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory">Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory</a>, where he performed flight research. His research led to the NACA Report R755, <i>Requirements for Satisfactory Flying Qualities of an Airplane</i>, published in 1941, in which he defined a set of requirements for the handling characteristics of an aircraft. Until this point, no set of guidelines for pilots and aircraft designers existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gilruth also pioneered the recording of data from instruments during flight test, to be later correlated with the pilot\'s experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This would go on to become the standard operating procedure.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_career">NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_R._Gilruth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gilruth had been working on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersonic" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> missile rockets as the assistant director of the Pilotless Aircraft Research Division of NACA. He and his team pushed their superiors to pursue a program to launch satellites into space, but he was rebuffed by administrators. The dynamic quickly changed after the Soviets succeeded in launching Sputnik, and Gilruth became involved in the transition of NACA into NASA.\nWhen NASA was created, Gilruth became head of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Task_Group" title="Space Task Group">Space Task Group</a>, tasked with putting a man in space before the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.\n</p><p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_John_F._Kennedy" title="President John F. Kennedy">President John F. Kennedy</a> announced that America would put a man on the Moon before the end of the decade in 1961, Gilruth was "aghast" and unsure that such a goal could be accomplished. He was integral to the creation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemini_program" title="Gemini program">Gemini program</a>, which he advocated as a means for NASA to learn more about operating in space before attempting a lunar landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Soon the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> was born, and Gilruth was made head of the NASA center which ran it, the new Manned Spacecraft Center (MSC) (now the <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a>). Gilruth was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Space_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Space Hall of Fame">International Space Hall of Fame</a> in 1969 and served as director of the MSC until his retirement in 1972.  He oversaw a total of 25 manned space flights, from <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_3" title="Mercury-Redstone 3">Mercury-Redstone 3</a> to <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a>.\n</p><p>Gilruth died in Charlottesville, Virginia, at the age of 86.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytobit_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1992, Gilruth was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Air_%26_Space_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Air &amp; Space Hall of Fame">International Air &amp; Space Hall of Fame</a> at the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Air_%26_Space_Museum" title="San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum">San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portrayals_in_dramas">Portrayals in dramas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Carroll Lynch">John Carroll Lynch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li>In the 2016 film <i><a href="/wiki/Hidden_Figures" title="Hidden
