Williams (STScI), the Hubble Deep Field Team and NASA.</div></div></div><p><b>Space law</b> is the body of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> governing space-related activities, encompassing both international and domestic agreements, rules, and principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Parameters of space law include <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, liability for damage, weapons use, rescue efforts, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental preservation</a>, information sharing, new technologies, and <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Other fields of law, such as <a href="/wiki/Administrative_law" title="Administrative law">administrative law</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intellectual_property_law" title="Intellectual property law">intellectual property law</a>, arms control law, <a href="/wiki/Insurance_law" title="Insurance law">insurance law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_law" title="Commercial law">commercial law</a>, are also integrated within space law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:13-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The origins of space law date back to 1919, with international law recognizing each country\'s sovereignty over the airspace directly above their territory, later reinforced at the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Convention_on_International_Civil_Aviation" title="Chicago Convention on International Civil Aviation">Chicago Convention</a> in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> The onset of domestic space programs during the Cold War propelled the official creation of international space policy (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>) initiated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Council_of_Scientific_Unions" title="International Council of Scientific Unions">International Council of Scientific Unions</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>\'s 1957 launch of the world\'s first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, directly spurred the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> to pass the <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Act" title="National Aeronautics and Space Act">Space Act</a>, thus creating the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:13-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Because space exploration required crossing transnational boundaries, it was during this era where space law became a field independent from traditional aerospace law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the Cold War, the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies</a> (the "Outer Space Treaty") and the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunications Union</a> have served as the foundational legal framework and set of principles and procedures constituting space law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> Further, the United Nations Committee on Peaceful Uses of Outer Space, along with its The Office for Outer Space Affairs subcommittee, aid in governing international space law and policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Challenges that space law will continue to face in the future are fourfold&#8212;spanning across dimensions of domestic compliance, international cooperation, ethics, and the advent of scientific innovations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:13-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:22-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:02-11">[11]</a></sup> Furthermore, specific guidelines on the definition of airspace have yet to be universally determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:13-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_developments">Early developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caltech" title="Caltech">Caltech</a> in 1942 <a href="/wiki/Theodore_von_K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n" title="Theodore von K&#225;rm&#225;n">Theodore von K&#225;rm&#225;n</a> and other rocket scientists banded together to form <a href="/wiki/Aerojet" title="Aerojet">Aerojet</a> rocket company with the help of lawyer <a href="/wiki/Andrew_G._Haley" title="Andrew G. Haley">Andrew G. Haley</a>. To toast the new corporation, K&#225;rm&#225;n said, "Now, Andy, we will make the rockets &#8211; you must make the corporation and obtain the money. Later on you will have to see that we behave well in outer space...After all, we are the scientists but you are the lawyer, and you must tell us how to behave ourselves according to law and to safeguard our innocence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Indeed, twenty years later Haley published the fundamental textbook, <i>Space Law and Government</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="citation needed both for the book, but also of a secondary source by a 3rd party declaring this">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Beginning in 1957 with the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>, nations began discussing systems to ensure the peaceful use of outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inesap_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-inesap-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Bilateral discussions between the United States and USSR in 1958 resulted in the presentation of issues to the UN for debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inesap_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-inesap-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SinghMacWhinney1989_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SinghMacWhinney1989-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1959, the UN created the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Committee_on_the_Peaceful_Uses_of_Outer_Space" title="United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space">Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space</a> (COPUOS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> COPUOS in turn created two subcommittees, the Scientific and Technical Subcommittee and the Legal Subcommittee.  The COPUOS Legal Subcommittee has been a primary forum for discussion and negotiation of international agreements relating to outer space.\n</p><p>In 1960 the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronautical_Congress" title="International Astronautical Congress">International Astronautical Congress</a> met in Stockholm and heard several submissions including a survey of legal opinion on extraterrestrial jurisdiction by Andrew G. Haley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1963 <a href="/wiki/Yale_University_Press" title="Yale University Press">Yale University Press</a> published <i>Law and Public Order in Space</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myres_McDougal" title="Myres McDougal">Myres McDougal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lasswell" title="Harold Lasswell">Harold Lasswell</a> and Ivan Vlasic.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_treaties">International treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Five international treaties have been negotiated and drafted in the COPUOS:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The 1967 Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies (the "<a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a>").</li>\n<li>The 1968 Agreement on the Rescue of Astronauts, the Return of Astronauts and the Return of Objects Launched into Outer Space (the "<a href="/wiki/Rescue_Agreement" title="Rescue Agreement">Rescue Agreement</a>").</li>\n<li>The 1972 Convention on International Liability for Damage Caused by Space Objects (the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liability_Convention" title="Liability Convention">Liability Convention</a>").</li>\n<li>The 1975 Convention on Registration of Objects Launched into Outer Space (the "<a href="/wiki/Registration_Convention" title="Registration Convention">Registration Convention</a>").</li>\n<li>The 1979 Agreement Governing the Activities of States on the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies (the "<a href="/wiki/Moon_Treaty" title="Moon Treaty">Moon Treaty</a>").</li></ul>\n<p>The Outer Space Treaty is the most widely adopted treaty, with 104 parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unoosa.org_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-unoosa.org-19">[19]</a></sup> The rescue agreement, the liability convention and the registration convention all elaborate on provisions of the outer space treaty.  UN delegates apparently intended<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2011)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the moon treaty serve as a new comprehensive treaty which would supersede or supplement the outer space treaty, most notably by elaborating upon the outer space treaty\'s provisions regarding resource appropriation and prohibition of territorial sovereignty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The moon treaty has only 17 parties <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unoosa.org_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-unoosa.org-19">[19]</a></sup> however, and many consider it to be a failed treaty due to its limited acceptance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADL_Legal_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADL_Legal-21">[21]</a></sup> India is the only nation that has both signed the moon treaty and declared itself interested in going to the moon. Objects launched into space are subject to their nation of belonging, including people. Objects, parts, and components discovered outside the jurisdiction of a nation will be returned upon identification. One reason that the U.N. space treaties lack definitions and are unclear in other respects, is that it is easier to achieve consensus when language and terms are vague.  In recent years, the Legal Subcommittee has been unable to achieve consensus on discussion of a new comprehensive space agreement (the idea of which, though, was proposed just by a few member States). It is also unlikely that the Subcommittee will be able to agree to amend the Outer Space Treaty in the foreseeable future.  Many space faring nations seem to believe that discussing a new space agreement or amendment of the Outer Space Treaty would be futile and time-consuming, because entrenched differences regarding resource appropriation, property rights and other issues relating to commercial activity make consensus unlikely.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1998_ISS_agreement">1998 ISS agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1998 ISS agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In addition to the international treaties that have been negotiated at the United Nations, the nations participating in the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> have entered into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_and_financial_aspects_of_the_ISS" title="Political and financial aspects of the ISS">1998 Agreement</a> among the governments of Canada, Member States of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>, Japan, Russian Federation, and the United States concerning cooperation on the Civil International Space Station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This agreement provides, among other things, that <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is the lead agency in coordinating the member states\' contributions to and activities on the space station, and that each nation has jurisdiction over its own module(s).  The agreement also provides for protection of intellectual property and procedures for criminal prosecution.  This agreement may very well serve as a model for future agreements regarding international cooperation in facilities on the Moon and Mars, where the first off-world colonies and scientific/industrial bases are likely to be established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_law">National law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Space law also encompasses national laws, and many countries have passed national space legislation in recent years.  The Outer Space Treaty gives responsibility for regulating space activities, including both government and private sector, to the individual countries where the activity is taking place.  If a national of, or an organization incorporated in one country launches a spacecraft in a different country, interpretations differ as to whether the home country or the launching country has jurisdiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Outer Space Treaty also incorporates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">UN Charter</a> by reference, and requires parties to ensure that activities are conducted in accordance with other forms of international law such as <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a> (the custom and practice of states).\n</p><p>The advent of commercial activities like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_mining" title="Space mining">space mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a>, private exploration, and the development of many commercial spaceports, is leading many countries<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>, and why some oppose applying the same principles to outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several nations have recently updated their <a href="/wiki/Statutory_law" (No changes made.)</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allocative_Limitations">Allocative Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Allocative Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Objects in <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbits</a> remain stationary over a point on the earth due to <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. There are numerous advantages in being able to use these orbits, mostly due to the unique ability to send <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequencies</a> to and from <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> to collect data and send signals to various locations. The United Nations Committee on Peaceful Uses of Outer Space has approved seven nonmilitary uses for these orbits: <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, earth resources and environment, <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_flight_control_system" title="Aircraft flight control system">aircraft control</a>, testing of new systems, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_relay_satellite" title="Data relay satellite">data relay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-38">[38]</a></sup> The requirement to space these satellites apart means that there is a limited number of orbital "slots" available, thus only a limited number of satellites can be placed in geostationary orbit. This has led to conflict between different countries wishing access to the same orbital slots (countries at the same <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> but differing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>). These disputes are addressed through the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">ITU</a> allocation mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Countries located at the Earth\'s equator have also asserted their legal claim to control the use of space above their territory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> notably in 1976, when many countries located at the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bogota_Declaration" title="Bogota Declaration">Bogota Declaration</a>, in which they asserted their legal claim to control the use of space above their territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Controversy">Political Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Political Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Future developments using geostationary orbits may include an expansion of services in telecommunication, broadcasting, and meteorology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:02-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, uses for geostationary orbits may stir political controversy. For example, broadcasting and telecommunication services of satellites orbiting above Earth from certain nations may accidentally "spill over" into other nations\' territory. This may prompt conflict with nations that wish to restrict access to information and communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-38">[38]</a></sup> Current and future political and legal concerns allocation may pose may be addressed by international <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislatures</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Committee_on_the_Peaceful_Uses_of_Outer_Space" title="United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space">United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:02_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-:02-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">Ethics</a> can be defined as "the task of being human." In space law, ethics extend to topics regarding <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a>, space ownership, the <a href="/wiki/Militarisation_of_space" title="Militarisation of space">militarization of space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, and distinguishing the boundaries of space itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:22-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_Use">Commercial Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commercial Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Early discussions regarding space ethics revolved around whether or not the space frontier should be available for use, gaining prominence at the time of the Soviet Union and United States\' <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In 1967, the "<a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a>" dictated that all nations in compliance with <a href="/wiki/International_regulation" title="International regulation">international regulation</a> is permitted to exploit space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-8">[8]</a></sup> As a result, the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_use_of_space" title="Commercial use of space">commercial use</a> of space is open to exploitation by public and private entities, especially in relation to mining and space tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This principle has been the subject of controversy, particularly by those in favor of environmental protection, sustainability, and conservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-:6-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Protection">Environmental Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>More recent discussions focus on the need for the international community to draft and institute a code of space ethics to prevent the destruction of the space environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Furthermore, the advancement of life in space pertain to questions related to the ethics of <a href="/wiki/Biocentrism_(ethics)" title="Biocentrism (ethics)">biocentrism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a>, or in other words, determining how much value we place in all living things versus human beings specifically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Currently, researchers in the <a href="/wiki/Biological_engineering" title="Biological engineering">bioengineering</a> field are working towards contamination control measures integrated into <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecrafts</a> to protect both space and earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-:6-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Defining_.22space.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'Defining_"space"\'>Defining "space"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title=\'Edit section: Defining "space"\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many ethical questions arise from the difficulty of defining the term "<a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>." Scholars not only debate its <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical</a> definition (i.e. upper and lower limits), but also whether or not it also encompasses various objects within it (i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_objects_in_astrology" title="Celestial objects in astrology">celestial objects</a>, human beings, man-made devices). Lower limits are generally estimated to be about 50 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometers</a>. More difficulties arise trying to define the upper bounds of "space," as it would require more inquiry into the nature of the universe and the role of earth (along with within it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:22_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:22-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploitation">Exploitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a rapid change and development.  Arguably the resources of space are infinite.  If commercial space transportation becomes widely available, with substantially lower launch costs, then all countries will be able to directly reap the benefits of space resources.  In that situation, it seems likely that consensus will be much easier to achieve with respect to commercial development and human settlement of outer space. High costs are not the only factor preventing the economic exploitation of space: it is argued that space should be considered as a pristine environment worthy of protection and conservation, and that the legal regime for space should further protect it from being used as a resource for Earth\'s needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  Debate is also focused on whether space should continue to be legally defined as part of the &#8220;common heritage of man,&#8221; and therefore unavailable for national claims, or whether its legal definition should be changed to allow private property in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vpop1_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-vpop1-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of 2013, NASA\'s plans to capture an asteroid by 2021 has raised questions about how space law would be applied in practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, the nation of <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> has set out a formal legal framework which ensures that private companies engaged in mining resources in space have rights to those resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_profession">Legal profession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Legal profession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Michael Dodge, of <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_Mississippi" title="Long Beach, Mississippi">Long Beach, Mississippi</a>, was the first law school graduate to receive a space law certificate in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Dodge graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mississippi" title="University of Mississippi">University of Mississippi</a> School of Law in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a of North Dakota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mississippi_School_of_Law" title="University of Mississippi School of Law">University of Mississippi School of Law</a> continues to be the only law school in the United States to offer a Master of Laws (LL.M.) Program in Air and Space Law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sunderland" title="University of Sunderland">University of Sunderland</a> is the first UK University to offer a space law module as part of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LLB" title="LLB">LLB</a> programme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska" title="University of Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a> College of Law offers the U.S.&#8217;s first and only LL.M.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and Doctor of the Science of Law (J.S.D.) in space, cyber, and telecommunications law. Professor Frans von der Dunk, former Director of space law research at Leiden University joined the program in 2007.  In addition to the LL.M., students can earn a J.D. at Nebraska Law with an emphasis in space and telecommunications law. The program also hosts three space and telecommunications conferences each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (November 2012)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>For more than 10 years, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris-Sud" title="University of Paris-Sud">University of Paris-Sud</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Space_and_Telecommunications_Law" title="Institute of Space and Telecommunications Law">Institute of Space and Telecommunications Law</a> have offered a Master\'s degree in Space Activities and Telecommunications Law. This Master is supported by numerous companies of space and telecommunications sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2012, students at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_the_Pacific,_McGeorge_School_of_Law" title="University of the Pacific, McGeorge School of Law">University of the Pacific, McGeorge School of Law</a> in Sacramento, California created the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=McGeorge_Society_for_Space_Law_and_Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="McGeorge Society for Space Law and Policy (page does not exist)">McGeorge Society for Space Law and Policy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In September 2012, the Space Law Society (SLS) at the University of Maryland Francis King Carey School of Law was established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> A legal resources team united in Maryland, a "Space Science State," with Jorge Rodriguez, Lee Sampson, Patrick Gardiner, Lyra Correa and Juliana Neelbauer as SLS founding members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2014, students at Northeastern University School of Law founded the school\'s Space Law Society.\n</p><p>Since 1951, the McGill Faculty of Law in Montreal, Canada hosts the Institute of Air and Space Law, and offers an LL.M. in Air and Space law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">Leiden University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> hosts an International Institute of Air and Space Law and offers an LL.M. degree.\n</p><p>In 2014, students at American University Washington College of Law founded the school\'s Space Law Society, with the help of Pamela L. Meredith, space lawyer and adjunct professor of Satellite Communications and Space Law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference"
