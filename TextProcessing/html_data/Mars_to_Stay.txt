(2009)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Mars to Stay</b> missions propose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronauts" title="Astronauts">astronauts</a> sent to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> for the first time should intend to stay. Unused emergency return vehicles would be recycled into <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Mars" title="Colonization of Mars">settlement</a> construction as soon as the habitability of Mars becomes evident to the initial pioneers. Mars to Stay missions are advocated both to reduce cost and to ensure permanent <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Mars" title="Colonization of Mars">settlement of Mars</a>. Among many notable Mars to Stay advocates, former <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> has been particularly outspoken, suggesting in numerous forums "Forget the Moon, Let&#8217;s Head to Mars!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and, in June 2013, Aldrin promoted a manned mission "to homestead Mars and become a two-planet species".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20130613_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20130613-2">[2]</a></sup> In August 2015, Aldrin, in association with the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Institute_of_Technology" title="Florida Institute of Technology">Florida Institute of Technology</a>, presented a "master plan", for NASA consideration, for astronauts, with a "tour of duty of ten years", to colonize Mars before the year 2040.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20150827_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20150827-3">[3]</a></sup> The Mars Underground, Mars Homestead Foundation, <a href="/wiki/Mars_One" title="Mars One">Mars One</a>, and Mars Artists Community advocacy groups and business organizations have also adopted Mars to Stay policy initiatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earliest formal outline of a Mars to Stay mission architecture was given at the Case for Mars VI Workshop in 1996, during a presentation by George Herbert titled "One Way to Mars".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposals">Proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_for_settlement_missions">Arguments for settlement missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arguments for settlement missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since returning the astronauts from the surface of Mars is one of the most difficult parts of a Mars mission, the idea of a one-way trip to Mars has been proposed several times. Space activist Bruce Mackenzie, for example, proposed a one-way trip to Mars in a presentation "One Way to Mars &#8211; a Permanent Settlement on the First Mission" at the 1998 <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Development_Conference" title="International Space Development Conference">International Space Development Conference</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> arguing that since the mission could be done with less difficulty and expense if the astronauts were not required to return to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, the first mission to Mars should be a settlement, not a visit.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Davies" title="Paul Davies">Paul Davies</a>, writing in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> in 2004, made similar arguments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davies_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-davies-7">[7]</a></sup> Under Davies\' plan, an initial colony of four astronauts equipped with a small <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> and a couple of <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">rover vehicles</a> would make their own <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">grow food</a>, and even initiate building projects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">using local raw materials</a>. Supplemented by food shipments, medical supplies, and replacement gadgets from Earth, the colony would be indefinitely sustained.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_Aldrin_plan">Original Aldrin plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Original Aldrin plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Under Mars to Stay mission architectures, the first humans to travel to Mars would typically be in six-member teams. After this initial landing, subsequent missions would raise the number of persons on Mars to 30, thereby beginning a Martian settlement. Since the Martian surface offers some of the  natural resources and elements necessary to sustain a robust, mature, industrialized human settlement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>&#8212;unlike, for example the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>&#8212;a permanent Martian settlement is thought to be the most effective way to ensure that humanity becomes a space-faring, multi-planet species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Through the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_fabricator" title="Digital fabricator">digital fabricators</a> and <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilisation" title="In vitro fertilisation">in vitro fertilisation</a> it is assumed a permanent human settlement on Mars can grow organically from an original thirty to forty pioneers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorg-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A Mars to Stay mission following Aldrin\'s proposal would enlist astronauts in the following timeline:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Age 30: an offer to help settle Mars is extended to select pioneers</li>\n<li>Age 30&#8211;35: training and social conditioning for long-duration isolation and time-delay communications</li>\n<li>Age 35: launch three married couples to Mars; followed in subsequent years by a dozen or more couples</li>\n<li>Age 35&#8211;65: development of sheltered <a href="/wiki/Underground_living" title="Underground living">underground living</a> spaces; <a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a> ensures <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a></li>\n<li>Age 65: an offer to return to Earth or retire on Mars is given to first-generation settlers</li></ul>\n<p>As Aldrin has said, "who knows what advances will have taken place. The first generation can retire there, or maybe we can bring them back."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-physorg-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22To_Boldly_Go:_A_One-Way_Human_Mission_to_Mars.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'"To_Boldly_Go:_A_One-Way_Human_Mission_to_Mars"\'>"To Boldly Go: A One-Way Human Mission to Mars"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title=\'Edit section: "To Boldly Go: A One-Way Human Mission to Mars"\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An article by Dirk Schulze-Makuch (<a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Davies" title="Paul Davies">Paul Davies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">Arizona State University</a>) from the book <i>The Human Mission to Mars: Colonizing the Red Planet</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToBoldlyGo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ToBoldlyGo-12">[12]</a></sup> highlights their mission plans as:\n</p>\n<ul><li>No <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">base on the Moon</a> is needed. Given the broad variety of resources available on Mars, the long-term survival of Martian settlers is much more feasible than Lunar settlers.</li>\n<li>Since Mars affords neither an <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone shield</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetospheric</a> protection, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robots" title="Robots">robots</a> would prepare a basic modular base inside near-surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_tubes" title="Lava tubes">lava tubes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_caves" title="Ice caves">ice caves</a> for the human settlers.</li>\n<li>A volunteer signing up for a one-way mission to Mars would do so with the full understanding that they will not return to Earth; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_exploration" title="Mars exploration">Mars exploration</a> would proceed for a long time on the basis of outbound journeys only.</li>\n<li>The first human contingent would consist of a crew of four, ideally (if budget permits) distributed between two two-man spacecraft for mission redundancy.</li>\n<li>Over time humans on Mars will increase with follow-up missions. Several subsurface <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biospheres</a> would be created until there were 150+ individuals in a viable <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a>. <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">Genetic engineering</a> would further contribute to the health and <a href="/wiki/Longevity" title="Longevity">longevity</a> of settlers.</li></ul>\n<p>The astronauts would be sent supplies from Earth regularly. This proposal was picked up for discussion in a number of public sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_One">Mars One</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mars One">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_One" title="Mars One">Mars One</a></div>\n<p>A proposal for a one-way human settlement mission to Mars was put forward in 2012 by the <a href="/wiki/Mars_One" title="Mars One">Mars One</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private spaceflight</a> project led by <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Bas_Lansdorp" title="Bas Lansdorp">Bas Lansdorp</a> to establish a permanent <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Mars" title="Colonization of Mars">human colony on Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dj20120601_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dj20120601-14">[14]</a></sup>\nMars One is a Dutch not-for-profit foundation, a <i><a href="/wiki/Private_foundation#Netherlands" title="Private foundation">Stichting</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FvdL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FvdL-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stichting_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stichting-16">[16]</a></sup>\nThe proposal is to send a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">communication satellite</a> and path finder lander to the planet by 2018 and, after several stages, land four humans on Mars for permanent settlement in 2027.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-satellite_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-satellite-17">[17]</a></sup> \nA new set of four <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> would then arrive every two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reality_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reality-18">[18]</a></sup> 200,000 applications were started; about 2,500 were complete enough for consideration, from which one hundred applicants have been chosen so far. Further selections are planned to narrow this down to six groups of four before training begins in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (March 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> It is hoped their reality television show, participant fees, and donations will generate the funding for the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The project has been criticized by experts as a \'scam\'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day_2015_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Day_2015-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and as \'delusional\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delusional_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-delusional-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MIT_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strive_to_Stay:_Emergency_Return_Only">Strive to Stay: Emergency Return Only</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Strive to Stay: Emergency Return Only">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In response to feedback following the EarthLight Institute\'s "Mars Colony 2030" project at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspace" title="Newspace">NewSpace 2012</a> and the announcement of <a href="/wiki/Mars_One" title="Mars One">Mars One</a>, Eric Machmer proposed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction-class_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Conjunction-class mission (page does not exist)">conjunction-class missions</a> be planned with a bias to stay (if low gravity, radiation, and other factors present no pressing health issues),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> so that, if at the end of each 550-day period during a conjunction-class <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch window</a> no adverse health effects were observed, settlers would continue research and construction through another 550-day period. In the meantime, additional crews and supplies would continue to arrive, starting their own 550-day evaluation periods. Health tests would be repeated during subsequent 550-day periods until the viability of human life on Mars was proven. Once settlers determine that humans can live on Mars without negative health effects, emergency return vehicles would be recycled into permanent research bases.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_and_permanent_settlement">Initial and permanent settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Initial and permanent settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Initial explorers leave equipment in orbit and at landing zones scattered considerable distances from the main settlement. Subsequent missions therefore are assumed to become easier and safer to undertake, with the likelihood of back-up equipment being present if accidents in transit or landing occur.\n</p><p>Large subsurface, pressurized habitats would be the first step toward human settlement; as Dr. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Zubrin" title="Robert Zubrin">Robert Zubrin</a> suggests in the first chapter of his book <i><a href="/wiki/Mars_Direct" title="Mars Direct">Mars Direct</a></i>, these structures can be built as Roman-style <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(architecture)" title="Atrium (architecture)">atria</a> in mountainsides or underground with easily produced Martian brick. During and after this initial phase of habitat construction, hard-plastic <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and abrasion-resistant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodesic_domes" title="Geodesic domes">geodesic domes</a> could be deployed on the surface for eventual habitation and crop growth. Nascent industry would begin using indigenous resources: the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastics" title="Plastics">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> could be easily achieved.\n</p><p>The longer-term work of <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars" title="Terraforming of Mars">terraforming Mars</a> requires an initial phase of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to release <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> from the Martian <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> and to create a <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water-cycle</a>. Three methods of global warming are described by Zubrin, who suggests they are best deployed in tandem: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_mirror_(geoengineering)" title="Space mirror (geoengineering)">orbital mirrors</a> to heat the surface; factories on the ground to pump <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halocarbons" title="Halocarbons">halocarbons</a> into the atmosphere; and the seeding of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that can <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolize</a> water, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (these gases would aid in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>). While the work of terraforming Mars is on-going, robust settlement of Mars would continue.\n</p><p>Zubrin, in his 1996 book (revised 2011) <i><a href="/wiki/The_Case_for_Mars" title="The Case for Mars">The Case for Mars</a></i>, acknowledges any Martian colony will be partially Earth-dependent for centuries. However, Zubrin suggests Mars may be profitable for two reasons. First, it may contain concentrated supplies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">metals</a> equal to or of greater value than <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, which have not been subjected to millennia of human scavenging; it is suggested such ores may be sold on Earth for profit. Secondly, the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>&#8212;an extremely expensive but essential fuel for the as-yet non-existent <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> power industry&#8212;is five times greater on Mars. Humans emigrating to Mars, under this paradigm, are presumed to have an industry; it is assumed the planet will be a magnet for settlers as wage costs will be high. Because of the labor shortage on Mars and its subsequent high pay-scale, Martian civilization and the value placed upon each individual\'s productivity is proposed as a future engine of both technological and social advancement.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks">Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_to_Stay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars_mission.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1393" data-file-width="2032" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Mars_mission.jpg/300px-Mars_mission.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Mars_mission.jpg/450px-Mars_mission.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Mars_mission.jpg/600px-Mars_mission.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars_mission.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist\'s conception of a human mission on Mars <br/> 1989 painting by Les Bossinas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_Research_Center" title="Lewis Research Center">Lewis Research Center</a> for NASA</div></div></div>\n<p>In the fifth chapter of "Mars Direct", Zubrin addresses the idea that <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-gravity" title="Zero-gravity">zero-gravity</a> are unduly hazardous. He claims <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> rates <i>do</i> increase for astronauts who have spent extensive time in space, but only marginally. Similarly, while zero-gravity presents challenges, near total recovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musculature" title="Musculature">musculature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> vitality is presumed by all Mars to Stay mission plans once settlers are on the Martian surface. Several experiments, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Gravity_Biosatellite" title="Mars Gravity Biosatellite">Mars Gravity Biosatellite</a>, have been proposed to test this hypothetical assumption, but until humans have lived in Martian gravity conditions (38% of Earth\'s), human long-term viability in such low gravity will remain only a working assumption. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back-contamination" title="Back-contamination">Back-contamination</a>&#8212;humans acquiring and spreading hypothetical Martian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viruses" title="Viruses">viruses</a>&#8212;is described as "just plain nuts", because there are no host organisms on Mars for disease organisms to have evolved.\n</p><p>In the same chapter, Zubrin rejects suggestions the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> should be used as waypoint to Mars or as a preliminary training area. "It is ultimately much easier to journey to Mars from low Earth orbit than from the Moon and using the latter as a staging point is a pointless diversion of resources." While the Moon may superficially appear a good place to perfect Mars exploration and habitation techniques, the two bodies are radically different. The Moon has no <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, no analogous <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and a much greater temperature range and rotational period of illumination. It is argued <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">deserts</a> of Earth, and precisely controlled chilled <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_chamber" title="Vacuum chamber">vacuum chambers</a> on easily accessible NASA centers on Earth provide much better training grounds at lesser cost.\n</p>\n<h2><span For example, colonists and pilgrims seldom set off to the New World without the expectation of a return trip. As Lawrence Krauss writes, "To boldly go where no one has gone before does not require coming home again."\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>If it sounds unrealistic to suggest that astronauts would be willing to leave home never to return ... consider the results of several informal surveys I and several colleagues have conducted recently. One of my peers in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> recently accompanied a group of scientists and engineers from the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> on a geological survey. He asked how many would be willing to go on a one-way mission into space. Every member of the group raised their hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krauss_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-krauss-43">[43]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Additional immediate and pragmatic reasons to consider one-way human space exploration missions are explored by Krauss. Since much of the cost of a voyage to Mars will be spent on returning to Earth, if the fuel for the return is carried on board, this greatly increases the mission mass requirement &#8211; that in turn requires even more fuel. According to Krauss, "Human space travel is so expensive and so dangerous ... we are going to need novel, even extreme solutions if we really want to expand the range of human civilization beyond our own planet." Delivering food and supplies to pioneers via unmanned spacecraft is less expensive than designing an immediate return trip.\n</p><p>In an earlier 2004 op-ed for the <i>New York Times</i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Davies" title="Paul Davies">Paul Davies</a> says motivation for the less expensive, permanent "one-way to stay option" arises from a theme common in "Mars to Stay" advocacy: "Mars is one of the few accessible places beyond Earth that could have sustained life [...&#160;and] alone among our sister planets, it is able to support a permanent human presence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davies_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-davies-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Why is going to Mars so expensive? ... It takes a lot of fuel to blast off Mars and get back home. If the propellant has to be transported there from Earth, costs of a launching soar.\n</p><p>Without some radical improvements in technology, the prospects for sending astronauts on a round-trip to Mars any time soon are slim, whatever the presidential rhetoric. What\'s more, the president\'s suggestion of using the Moon as a base &#8212; a place to assemble equipment and produce fuel for a Mars mission less expensively &#8212; has the potential to turn into a costly sideshow. There is, however, an obvious way to slash the costs and bring Mars within reach of early manned exploration. The answer lies with a one-way mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davies_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-davies-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Davies argues that since "some people gleefully dice with death in the name of sport or adventure [and since] dangerous occupations that reduce life expectancy through exposure to hazardous conditions or substances are commonplace", we ought to not find the risks involved in a Mars to Stay architecture unusual. "A century ago, explorers set out to trek across Antarctica in the full knowledge that they could die in the process, and that even if they succeeded their health<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-29">[29]</a></sup> might be irreversibly harmed. Yet governments and scientific societies were willing sponsors of these enterprises." Davies then asks, "Why should it be different today?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davies_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-davies-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
