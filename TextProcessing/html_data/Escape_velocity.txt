<b>escape velocity</b> is the minimum speed needed for a free object to escape from the gravitational influence of a massive body. It is slower the further away from the body an object is, and slower for less massive bodies.\n</p><p>The escape velocity from Earth is about 11.186&#160;km/s (6.951&#160;mi/s; 40,270&#160;km/h; 36,700&#160;ft/s; 25,020&#160;mph; 21,744&#160;kn)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> at the surface. More generally, escape velocity is the speed at which the sum of an object\'s <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential#Potential_energy" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential energy</a> is equal to zero;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> an object which has achieved escape velocity is neither on the surface, nor in a closed orbit (of any radius). With escape velocity in a direction pointing away from the ground of a massive body, the object will move away from the body, slowing forever and approaching, but never reaching, zero speed.  Once escape velocity is achieved, no further impulse need be applied for it to continue in its escape. In other words, if given escape velocity, the object will move away from the other body, continually slowing, and will <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymptotically" title="Asymptotically">asymptotically</a> approach zero speed as the object\'s distance approaches <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinity</a>, never to come back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> Speeds higher than escape velocity have a positive speed at infinity. Note that the minimum escape velocity assumes that there is no friction (e.g., atmospheric drag), which would increase the required instantaneous velocity to escape the gravitational influence, and that there will be no future acceleration or deceleration (for example from <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> or gravity from other objects), which would change the required instantaneous velocity.\n</p><p>For a spherically symmetric, massive body such as a star, or planet, the escape velocity for that body, at a given distance, is calculated by the formula<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v_{e}={\\sqrt {\\frac {2GM}{r}}},}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>e</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<msqrt>\n<mfrac>\n<mrow>\n<mn>2</mn>\n<mi>G</mi>\n<mi>M</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mi>r</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</msqrt>\n</mrow>\n<mo>,</mo>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v_{e}={\\sqrt {\\frac {2GM}{r}}},}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v_e = \\sqrt{\\frac{2GM}{r}}," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f74d7bf2a53c4a4d6960295523b030d6755cd11e" style="vertical-align: -2.338ex; href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The existence of escape velocity is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> and an energy field of finite depth. For an object with a given total energy, which is moving subject to <a href="/wiki/Conservative_force" title="Conservative force">conservative forces</a> (such as a static gravity field) it is only possible for the object to reach combinations of locations and speeds which have that total energy; and places which have a higher potential energy than this cannot be reached at all. By adding speed (kinetic energy) to the object it expands the possible locations that can be reached, until, with enough energy, they become infinite.\n</p><p>For a given <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential</a> energy at a given position, the escape velocity is the minimum <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> an object without <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion</a> needs to be able to "escape" from the gravity (i.e. so that gravity will never manage to pull it back). Escape velocity is actually a speed (not a velocity) because it does not specify a direction: no matter what the direction of travel is, the object can escape the gravitational field (provided its path does not intersect the planet).\n</p><p>The simplest way of deriving the formula for escape velocity is to use conservation of energy.  For the sake of simplicity, unless stated otherwise, we assume that an object is attempting to escape from a uniform spherical planet by moving away from it and that the only significant force acting on the moving object is the planet\'s gravity. In its initial state, <i>i</i>, imagine that a spaceship of mass <i>m</i> is at a distance <i>r</i> from the center of mass of the planet, whose mass is <i>M</i>. Its initial speed is equal to its escape velocity, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v_{e}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>e</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v_{e}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v_{e}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3271a14c35fc1b6cb549d4d462e8d3255185cc9d" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.126ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span>. Additionally, the escape velocity at a point in space is equal to the speed that an object would have if it started at rest from an infinite distance and was pulled by gravity to that point.\n</p><p>In common usage, the initial point is on the surface of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moon</a>. On the surface of the Earth, the escape velocity is about 11.2&#160;km/s, which is approximately 33 times the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> (Mach 33) and several times the <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">muzzle velocity</a> of a rifle bullet (up to 1.7&#160;km/s). However, at 9,000&#160;km altitude in "space", it is slightly less than 7.1&#160;km/s.\n</p><p>The escape velocity is independent of the mass of the escaping object. It does not matter if the mass is 1&#160;kg or 1,000&#160;kg; what differs is the amount of energy required. For an object of mass <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle m}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>m</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle m}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="m" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:2.04ex; height:1.676ex;"/></span> the energy required to escape the Earth\'s gravitational field is <i>GMm&#160;/&#160;r</i>, a function of the object\'s mass (where <i>r</i> is the radius of the Earth, <i>G</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a>, and <i>M</i> is the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, <span class="nowrap"><i>M</i> = 5.9736 &#215; 10<sup>24</sup> kg</span>). A related quantity is the <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a> which is essentially the sum of the kinetic and potential energy divided by the mass. For example, as the Earth\'s rotational velocity is 465&#160;m/s at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, a rocket launched tangentially from the Earth\'s equator to the east requires an initial velocity of about 10.735&#160;km/s <i>relative to Earth</i> to escape whereas a rocket launched tangentially from the Earth\'s equator to the west requires an initial velocity of about 11.665&#160;km/s <i>relative to Earth</i>. The surface velocity decreases with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" title="Trigonometric function">cosine</a> of the geographic latitude, so space launch facilities are often located as close to the equator as feasible, e.g. the American <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral</a> (latitude 28&#176;28\'&#160;N) and the French <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a> (latitude 5&#176;14\'&#160;N).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_considerations">Practical considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Escape_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Practical considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In most situations it is impractical to achieve escape velocity almost instantly, because of the acceleration implied, and also because if there is an atmosphere, the hypersonic speeds involved (on Earth a speed of 11.2&#160;km/s, or 40,320&#160;km/h) would cause most objects to burn up due to <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_heating" title="Aerodynamic heating">aerodynamic heating</a> or be torn apart by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">atmospheric drag</a>. For an actual escape orbit, a spacecraft will accelerate steadily out of the atmosphere until it reaches the escape velocity appropriate for its altitude (which will be less than on the surface). In many cases, the spacecraft may be first placed in a <a href="/wiki/Parking_orbit" title="Parking orbit">parking orbit</a> (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> at 160&#8211;2,000&#160;km) and then accelerated to the escape velocity at that altitude, which will be slightly lower (about 11.0&#160;km/s at a low Earth orbit of 200&#160;km). The required additional <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">change in speed</a>, however, is far less because the spacecraft already has significant orbital velocity (in low Earth orbit speed is approximately 7.8&#160;km/s, or 28,080&#160;km/h).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_an_orbiting_body">From an orbiting body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Escape_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: From an orbiting body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The escape velocity at a given height is <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\sqrt {2}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<msqrt>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</msqrt>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle {\\sqrt {2}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\sqrt {2}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4afc1e27d418021bf10898eb44a7f5f315735ff" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:3.098ex; height:3.009ex;"/></span> times the speed in a circular orbit at the same height, (compare this with the velocity equation in <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">circular orbit</a>). This corresponds to the fact that the potential energy with respect to infinity of an object in such an orbit is minus two times its kinetic energy, while to escape the sum of potential and kinetic energy needs to be at least zero. The velocity corresponding to the circular orbit is sometimes called the <b>first cosmic velocity</b>, whereas in this context the escape velocity is referred to as the <b>second cosmic velocity</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For a body in an elliptical orbit wishing to accelerate to an escape orbit the required speed will vary, and will be greatest at periapsis when the body is closest to the central body. However, the orbital speed of the body will also be at its highest at this point, and the change in velocity required will be at its lowest, as explained by the <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barycentric_escape_velocity">Barycentric escape velocity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Escape_velocity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Barycentric escape velocity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Technically escape velocity can either be measured as a relative to the other, central body or relative to <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass or barycenter</a> of the system of bodies. Thus for systems of two bodies, the term <i>escape velocity</i> can be ambiguous, but it is usually intended to mean the barycentric escape velocity of the less massive body. Although this trajectory does not form a closed shape, it can be referred to as an orbit. Assuming that gravity is the only significant force in the system, this object\'s speed at any point in the trajectory will be equal to the escape velocity <i>at that point</i> due to the conservation of energy, its total energy must always be 0, which implies that it always has escape velocity; see the derivation above. The shape of the trajectory will be a <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a> whose focus is located at the center of mass of the planet. An actual escape requires a course with a trajectory that does not intersect with the planet, or its atmosphere, since this would cause the object to crash. When moving away from the source, this path is called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escape_orbit" title="Escape orbit">escape orbit</a>. Escape orbits are known as <i>C3</i> = 0 orbits. <i>C3</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Characteristic_energy" title="Characteristic energy">characteristic energy</a>, = &#8722;<i>GM/2a</i>, where <i>a</i> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a>, which is infinite for parabolic trajectories.\n</p><p>If the body has a velocity greater than escape velocity then its path will form a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_trajectory" title="Hyperbolic trajectory">hyperbolic trajectory</a> and it will have an excess hyperbolic velocity, equivalent to the extra energy the body has. A relatively small extra <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-<i>v</i></a> above that needed to accelerate to the escape speed can result in a relatively large speed at infinity.
