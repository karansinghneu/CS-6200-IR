class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Absence of gravity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A "stationary" micro-g environment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> would require travelling far enough into deep space so as to reduce the effect of gravity by <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuation</a> to almost zero. This is the simplest in conception, but requires traveling an enormous distance, rendering it most impractical. For example, to reduce the gravity of the Earth by a factor of one million, one needs to be at a distance of 6 million kilometers from the Earth, but to reduce the gravity of the Sun to this amount one has to be at a distance of 3.7 billion kilometers. (On Earth the gravity due to the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> is already attenuated by a factor greater than one million, so we do not need to move away further from its <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">center</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>). Thus it is not impossible, but it has only been achieved so far by four <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_probe" title="Interstellar probe">interstellar probes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">2</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager program</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">11</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_program" title="Pioneer program">Pioneer program</a>) and they did not return to Earth. pm/s<sup>2</sup>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>At a distance relatively close to Earth (less than 3000&#160;km), gravity is only slightly reduced. As an object orbits a body such as the Earth, gravity is still attracting objects towards the Earth and the object is accelerated downward at almost 1g. Because the objects are typically moving laterally with respect to the surface at such immense speeds, the object will not lose altitude because of the curvature of the Earth. When viewed from an orbiting observer, other close objects in space appear to be floating because everything is being pulled towards Earth at the same speed, but also moving forward as the Earth\'s surface "falls" away below. All these objects are in free fall, not zero gravity.\n</p><p>Compare the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential#Numerical_values" title="Gravitational potential">gravitational potential at some of these locations</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_fall">Free fall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Free fall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>What remains is a micro-g environment moving in <a href="/wiki/Free_fall" title="Free fall">free fall</a>, i.e. there are no forces other than gravity acting on the people or objects in this environment. To prevent air drag making the free fall less perfect, objects and people can free-fall in a capsule that itself, while not necessarily in free fall, is accelerated as in free fall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This can be done by applying a force to compensate for air drag. Alternatively free fall can be carried out in space, or in a vacuum tower or shaft.\n</p><p>Two cases can be distinguished: Temporary micro-g, where after some time the Earth\'s surface is or would be reached, and indefinite micro-g.\n</p><p>A temporary micro-g environment exists in a <a href="/wiki/Drop_tube" title="Drop tube">drop tube</a> (in a tower or shaft), a <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a>, e.g. with a <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rocket</a>, and in an airplane such as used by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s Reduced Gravity Research Program, aka the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vomit_Comet" title="Vomit Comet">Vomit Comet</a>, and by the <a href="/wiki/Zero_Gravity_Corporation" title="Zero Gravity Corporation">Zero Gravity Corporation</a>. A temporary micro-g environment is applied for training of astronauts, for some experiments, for filming movies, and for recreational purposes.\n</p><p>A micro-g environment for an indefinite time, while also possible in a spaceship going to infinity in a parabolic or hyperbolic orbit, is most practical in an Earth orbit. This is the environment commonly experienced in the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, etc. While this scenario is the most suitable for scientific experimentation and commercial exploitation, it is still quite expensive to operate in, mostly due to launch costs.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_and_inertial_acceleration">Tidal and inertial acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tidal and inertial acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Objects in orbit are not perfectly weightless due to several effects:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Effects depending on relative position in the spacecraft:\n<ul><li>Because the force of gravity decreases with distance, objects with non-zero size will be subjected to a <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal force</a>, or a differential pull, between the ends of the object nearest and furthest from the Earth. (An extreme version of this effect is <a href="/wiki/Spaghettification" title="Spaghettification">spaghettification</a>.) In a spacecraft in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO), the <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">centrifugal force</a> is also greater on the side of the spacecraft furthest from the Earth. At a 400&#160;km LEO altitude, the overall differential in g-force is approximately 0.384 &#956;<i>g</i>/m. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conceptual_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-conceptual-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>"Floating" objects in a spacecraft in LEO are actually in independent orbits around the Earth. If two objects are placed side-by-side (relative to their direction of motion), they will be orbiting the Earth in different orbital planes. Since all orbital planes pass through the center of the Earth, any two orbital planes intersect along a line. Therefore, two objects placed side-by-side (at any distance apart) will come together after one quarter of a revolution. If they are placed so they miss each other, they will oscillate past each other, with the same period as the orbit. This corresponds to an inward acceleration of 0.128 &#956;<i>g</i> per meter horizontal distance from the center at 400&#160;km altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conceptual_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-conceptual-3">[3]</a></sup> If they are placed one ahead of the other in the same orbital plane, they will maintain their separation. If they are placed one above the other (at different radii from the center of the Earth), they will have different potential energies, so the size, eccentricity, and period of their orbits will be different, causing them to move in a complex looping pattern relative to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Gravity between the spacecraft and an object within it may make the object slowly "fall" toward a more massive part of it. The acceleration is 0.007 &#956;<i>g</i> for 1000&#160;kg at 1&#160;m distance.</li></ul></li>\n<li>Uniform effects (which could be compensated):\n<ul><li>Though very thin, there is some air at orbital altitudes of 185 to 1,000&#160;km. This atmosphere causes deceleration due to friction. This could be compensated by a small continuous thrust, but in practice the deceleration is only compensated from time to time, so the small g-force of this effect is not eliminated.</li>\n<li>The effects of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> are similar, but directed away from the Sun. Unlike the effect of the atmosphere, it does not reduce with altitude.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_applications">Commercial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commercial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_spheres">Metal spheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Metal spheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Shot_tower" title="Shot tower">shot tower</a> (now obsolete), molten metal (such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>) was dripped through a sieve into free fall. href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Health effects of the micro-g environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_motion_sickness">Space motion sickness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Space motion sickness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2091" data-file-width="2807" decoding="async" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg/288px-Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg/432px-Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg/576px-Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg 2x" width="288"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Astronauts_in_weightlessness.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Six astronauts who had been in training at the Johnson Space Center for almost a year are getting a sample of a micro-g environment</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Space_adaptation_syndrome" title="Space adaptation syndrome">Space motion sickness</a> (SMS) is thought to be a subtype of <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a> that plagues nearly half of all astronauts who venture into space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> SMS, along with facial stuffiness from headward shifts of fluids, headaches, and back pain, is part of a broader complex of symptoms that comprise <i>space adaptation syndrome</i> (SAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> SMS was first described in 1961 during the second orbit of the fourth manned spaceflight when the cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Gherman_Titov" title="Gherman Titov">Gherman Titov</a> aboard the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_2" title="Vostok 2">Vostok 2</a>, described feeling disoriented with physical complaints mostly consistent with motion sickness. It is one of the most studied physiological problems of spaceflight but continues to pose a significant difficulty for many astronauts. In some instances, it can be so debilitating that astronauts must sit out from their scheduled occupational duties in space &#8211; including missing out on a spacewalk they have spent months training to perform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In most cases, however, astronauts will work through the symptoms even with degradation in their performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:03-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite their experiences in some of the most rigorous and demanding physical maneuvers on earth, even the most seasoned astronauts may be affected by SMS, resulting in symptoms of severe <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, projectile <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">fatigue</a>, malaise (feeling sick), and <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headache</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:03-10">[10]</a></sup> These symptoms may occur so abruptly and without any warning that space travelers may vomit suddenly without time to contain the emesis, resulting in strong odors and liquid within the cabin which may affect other astronauts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:03-10">[10]</a></sup> Symptoms typically last anywhere from one to three days upon entering weightlessness, but may recur upon reentry to Earth&#8217;s gravity or even shortly after landing. SMS differs from terrestrial motion sickness in that sweating and pallor are typically minimal or absent and gastrointestinal findings usually demonstrate absent bowel sounds indicating reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_motility" title="Gastrointestinal motility">gastrointestinal motility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:13-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even when the nausea and vomiting resolve, some central nervous system symptoms may persist which may degrade the astronaut&#8217;s performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:13-11">[11]</a></sup> Graybiel and Knepton proposed the term &#8220;<a href="/wiki/Sopite_syndrome" title="Sopite syndrome">sopite syndrome</a>&#8221; to describe symptoms of lethargy and drowsiness associated with motion sickness in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Since then, their definition has been revised to include &#8220;&#8230;a symptom complex that develops as a result of exposure to real or apparent motion and is characterized by excessive drowsiness, lassitude, lethargy, mild depression, and reduced ability to focus on an assigned task.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Together, these symptoms may pose a substantial threat (albeit temporary) to the astronaut who must remain attentive to life and death issues at all times.\n</p><p>SMS is most commonly thought to be a disorder of the vestibular system that occurs when sensory information from the visual system (sight) and the proprioceptive system (posture, position of the body) conflicts with misperceived information from the semicircular canals and the otoliths within the inner ear. This is known as the &#8216;neural mismatch theory&#8217; and was first suggested in 1975 by Reason and Brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Alternatively, the fluid shift hypothesis suggests that weightlessness reduces the hydrostatic pressure on the lower body causing fluids to shift toward the head from the rest of the body. These fluid shifts are thought to increase cerebrospinal fluid pressure (causing back aches), intracranial pressure (causing headaches), and inner ear fluid pressure (causing vestibular dysfunction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heer_77&#8211;79_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heer_77&#8211;79-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite a multitude of studies searching for a solution to the problem of SMS, it remains an ongoing problem for space travel. Most non-pharmacological countermeasures such as training and other physical maneuvers have offered minimal benefit. Thornton and Bonato noted, &#8220;Pre- and inflight adaptive efforts, some of them mandatory and most of them onerous, have been, for the most part, operational failures.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To date, the most common intervention is <a href="/wiki/Promethazine" title="Promethazine">promethazine</a>, an injectable antihistamine with antiemetic properties, but sedation can be a problematic side effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Other common pharmacological options include <a href="/wiki/Metoclopramide" title="Metoclopramide">metaclopromide</a>, as well as oral and transdermal application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scopolamine" title="Scopolamine">scopolamine</a>, but drowsiness and sedation are common side effects for these medications as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heer_77&#8211;79_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heer_77&#8211;79-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musculoskeletal_effects">Musculoskeletal effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Musculoskeletal effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the space (or microgravity) environment the effects of unloading varies significantly among individuals, with sex differences compounding the variability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Differences in mission duration, and the small sample size of astronauts participating in the same mission also adds to the variability to the <a href="/wiki/Musculoskeletal_disorder" title="Musculoskeletal disorder">musculoskeletal disorders</a> that are seen in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> In addition to muscle loss, microgravity leads to increased <a href="/wiki/Bone_resorption" title="Bone resorption">bone resorption</a>, decreased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_mineral_density" title="Bone mineral density">bone mineral density</a>, and increased fracture risks. Bone resorption leads to increased urinary levels of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_in_biology" title="Calcium in biology">calcium</a>, which can subsequently lead to an increased risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nephrolithiasis" title="Nephrolithiasis">nephrolithiasis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:33_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-:33-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the first two weeks that the muscles are unloaded from carrying the weight of the human frame during space flight, whole muscle atrophy begins. Postural muscles contain more slow fibers, and are more prone to atrophy than non-postural muscle groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> The loss of muscle mass occurs because of imbalances in protein synthesis and breakdown. The loss of muscle mass is also accompanied by a loss of muscle strength, which was observed after only 2&#8211;5 days of spaceflight during the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_3" title="Soyuz 3">Soyuz-3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_8" title="Soyuz 8">Soyuz-8</a> missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> Decreases in the generation of contractile forces and whole muscle power have also been found in response to microgravity.\n</p><p>To counter the effects of microgravity on the musculoskeletal system, aerobic exercise is recommended. This often takes the form of in-flight cycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> A more effective regimen includes resistive exercises or the use of a penguin suit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> (contains sewn-in elastic bands to maintain a stretch load on antigravity muscles), centrifugation, and vibration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:33_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-:33-20">[20]</a></sup> Centrifugation recreates Earth&#8217;s gravitational force on the space station, in order to prevent <a href="/wiki/Muscle_atrophy" title="Muscle atrophy">muscle atrophy</a>. Centrifugation can be performed with centrifuges or by cycling along the inner wall of the space station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup> Whole body vibration has been found to reduce bone resorption through mechanisms that are unclear. Vibration can be delivered using exercise devices that use vertical displacements juxtaposed to a fulcrum, or by using a plate that oscillates on a vertical axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The use of <a href="/wiki/Beta2-adrenergic_agonist" title="Beta2-adrenergic agonist">beta-2 adrenergic agonists</a> to increase muscle mass, and the use of essential amino acids in conjunction with resistive exercises have been proposed as pharmacologic means of combating muscle atrophy in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:23_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-:23-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Microgravity can also lead to height increases; in 2018 a Japanese astronout reported growing 2 centimetres in just three weeks of micro-gravity on board the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_effects">Cardiovascular effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:571px;"><div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_2" style="width:569px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:854px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_3" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/StationLIFE_-_Cardiovascular_%28January_2016%29.webm/854px--StationLIFE_-_Cardiovascular_%28January_2016%29.webm.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:854px;height:480px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="1391.841" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="StationLIFE_-_Cardiovascular_(January_2016).webm" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/03/StationLIFE_-_Cardiovascular_%28January_2016%29.webm/StationLIFE_-_Cardiovascular_%28January_2016%29.webm.480p.vp9.webm" type="video/webm; When standing, some \'excess\' fluid resides in vessels and tissues of the legs. In a micro-g environment, with the loss of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_pressure" title="Hydrostatic pressure">hydrostatic gradient</a>, some fluid quickly redistributes toward the chest and upper body; sensed as \'overload\' of circulating blood volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:42-24">[24]</a></sup> In the micro-g environment, the newly sensed excess blood volume is adjusted by expelling excess fluid into tissues and cells (12-15% volume reduction) and <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a> are adjusted downward to maintain a normal concentration (relative <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:42_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:42-24">[24]</a></sup> In the absence of gravity, venous blood will rush to the <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(heart)" title="Atrium (heart)">right atrium</a> because the force of gravity is no longer pulling the blood down into the vessels of the legs and abdomen, resulting in increased <a href="/wiki/Stroke_volume" title="Stroke volume">stroke volume</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> These fluid shifts become more dangerous upon returning to a regular gravity environment as the body will attempt to adapt to the reintroduction of gravity. The reintroduction of gravity again will pull the fluid downward, but now there would be a deficit in both circulating fluid and red blood cells. The decrease in cardiac filling pressure and stroke volume during the orthostatic stress due to a decreased blood volume is what causes <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_intolerance" title="Orthostatic intolerance">orthostatic intolerance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Orthostatic intolerance can result in temporary loss of consciousness and posture, due to the lack of pressure and stroke volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> More chronic orthostatic intolerance can result in additional symptoms such as <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sleep_disorder" title="Sleep disorder">sleep problems</a>, and other vasomotor symptoms as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many studies on the physiological effects of weightlessness on the cardiovascular system are done in parabolic flights. It is one of the only feasible options to combine with human experiments, making parabolic flights the only way to investigate the true effects of the micro-g environment on a body without traveling into space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup> Parabolic flight studies have provided a broad range of results regarding changes in the cardiovascular system in a micro-g environment. Parabolic flight studies have increased the understanding of orthostatic intolerance and decreased peripheral blood flow suffered by Astronauts returning to Earth. Due to the loss of blood to pump, the heart can atrophy in a micro-g environment. A weakened heart can result in low blood volume, low blood pressure and affect the body\'s ability to send oxygen to the brain without the individual becoming dizzy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Heart_arrhythmia" title="Heart arrhythmia">Heart rhythm disturbances</a> have also been seen among astronauts, but it is not clear whether this was due to pre-existing conditions of effects of a micro-g environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup> One current countermeasure includes drinking a salt solution, which increases the viscosity of blood and would subsequently increase blood pressure which would mitigate post micro-g environment orthostatic intolerance. Another countermeasure includes administration of <a href="/wiki/Midodrine" title="Midodrine">midodrine</a>, which is a selective alpha-1 adrenergic agonist. Midodrine produces arterial and venous constriction resulting in an increase in blood pressure by baroreceptor reflexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_to_worker_safety">Impacts to worker safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micro-g_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impacts to worker safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Space motion sickness can lead to degraded astronaut performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:03-10">[10]</a></sup> SMS threatens operational requirements, reduces situational awareness, and threatens the safety of those exposed to micro-g environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Lost muscle mass leads to difficulty with movement, especially when astronauts return to earth. This can pose a safety issue if the need for emergency egress were to arise. Loss of muscle power makes it extremely difficult, if not impossible, for astronauts to climb through emergency egress hatches or create unconventional exit spaces in the case of a crash upon landing. Additionally, bone resorption and inadequate hydration in space can lead to the formation of kidney stones, and subsequent sudden incapacitation due to pain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:33_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-:33-20">[20]</a></sup> If this were to occur during critical phases of flight, a capsule crash leading to worker injury and/or death could result. Short-term and long-term health effects have been seen in the cardiovascular system from exposure to the micro-g environment that would limit those exposed after they return to Earth or a regular gravity environment. Steps need to be taken to ensure proper precautions are taken into consideration when dealing a micro-g environment for worker safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup> Orthostatic intolerance can lead to temporary loss of consciousness due to the lack of pressure and stroke volume. This loss of consciousness inhibits and endangers those affected and can lead to deadly consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
