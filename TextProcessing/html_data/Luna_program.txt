<b>Luna programme</b> (from the Russian word &#1051;&#1091;&#1085;&#1072; "Luna" meaning "Lunar" or "Moon"), occasionally called <i>Lunik</i> or <i>Lunnik</i> by western media, was a series of <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> missions sent to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> between 1959 and 1976. Fifteen were successful, each designed as either an <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lander</a>, and accomplished many firsts in space exploration. They also performed many experiments, studying the Moon\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> composition, <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>.\n</p><p>Twenty-four spacecraft were formally given the Luna designation, although more were launched. Those that failed to reach orbit were not publicly acknowledged at the time, and not assigned a Luna number. Those that failed in low Earth orbit were usually given <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_(satellite)" title="Kosmos (satellite)">Cosmos</a> designations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The estimated cost of the Luna programme was about $4.5 billion.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a></i> (January 1959) missed its intended impact with the Moon and became the first spacecraft to fall into orbit around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_2" title="Luna 2">Luna 2</a></i> (September 1959) mission successfully hit the Moon\'s surface, becoming the first man-made object to reach the Moon.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">Luna 3</a></i> (October 1959) rounded the Moon later that year, and returned the first photographs of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">far side</a>, which can never be seen from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">Luna 9</a></i> (February 1966) became the first probe to achieve a soft landing on another planetary body. It returned five black and white stereoscopic circular panoramas, which were the first close-up shots of the Lunar surface.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_10" title="Luna 10">Luna 10</a></i> (March 1966) became the first artificial satellite of the Moon.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_17" title="Luna 17">Luna 17</a></i> (November 1970) and <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_21" title="Luna 21">Luna 21</a></i> (January 1973) carried the <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_programme" title="Lunokhod programme">Lunokhod</a> vehicles, which roamed around on the Moon\'s terrain.\n</p><p>Another major achievement of the Luna programme, with <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_16" title="Luna 16">Luna 16</a></i> (September 1970), <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_20" title="Luna 20">Luna 20</a></i> (February 1972) and <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_24" title="Luna 24">Luna 24</a></i> (August 1976), was the ability to collect samples of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">lunar soil</a> and return them to Earth. The programme returned 0.326&#160;kg of <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">lunar samples</a>. The Luna missions were the first space-exploration <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">sample return missions</a> to rely solely on advanced <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_notable_missions">Other notable missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other notable missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Luna_15" title="Luna 15">Luna 15</a></i> (July 1969), also designed to return soil samples from the lunar surface, underwent its mission at the same time as the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> mission. <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> were already on the lunar surface when <i>Luna 15</i> began its descent, and the spacecraft crashed into a mountain minutes later.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_missions">Failed missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Failed missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While the programme was active, it was Soviet practice not to release any details of missions which had failed to achieve orbit. This resulted in Western observers assigning their own designations to the missions, for example <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-1_No.1" title="Luna E-1 No.1">Luna E-1 No.1</a>, the first failure of 1958 which NASA believed was associated with the Luna programme was known as <i>Luna 1958A</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tent_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tent-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>NASA identified a spacecraft which it referred to as Luna 1966A as having launched on 30 April 1966, a spacecraft referred to as Luna 1969B as having launched on 15 April 1969, and a spacecraft referred to as Luna 1970B as having launched on 19 February 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tent_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tent-3">[3]</a></sup> When details of Soviet launches were later disclosed, no launches of Luna spacecraft were found to have occurred on those dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
