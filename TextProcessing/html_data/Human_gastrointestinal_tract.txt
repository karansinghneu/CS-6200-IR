<b>digestional tract</b>, <b>GI tract</b>, <b>GIT</b>, <b>gut</b>, or <b>alimentary canal</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> system within humans and other <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> which takes in food, <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digests</a> it to extract and absorb energy and nutrients, and expels the remaining waste as <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, stomach and intestines are part of the gastrointestinal tract. <i>Gastrointestinal</i> is an adjective meaning of or pertaining to the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">intestines</a>. A tract is a collection of related anatomic structures or a series of connected body organs.\n</p><p>All <a href="/wiki/Bilateria" title="Bilateria">bilaterians</a> have a gastrointestinal tract, also called a gut or an alimentary canal. This is a tube that transfers food to the <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RuppertBarnes2004EumetazoaTissues_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RuppertBarnes2004EumetazoaTissues-1">[1]</a></sup> In large bilaterians, the gastrointestinal tract also has an exit, the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>, by which the animal disposes of <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> (solid wastes). Some small bilaterians have no anus and dispose of solid wastes by other means (for example, through the mouth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RuppertBarnes2004BilateriaGut_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RuppertBarnes2004BilateriaGut-2">[2]</a></sup> The human gastrointestinal tract consists of the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, and intestines, and is divided into the upper and lower gastrointestinal tracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The GI tract includes all structures between the <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">mouth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Human_anus" title="Human anus">anus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> forming a continuous passageway that includes the main organs of digestion, namely, the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a>. However, the complete <a href="/wiki/Human_digestive_system" title="Human digestive system">human digestive system</a> is made up of the gastrointestinal tract plus the accessory organs of digestion (the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salivary_gland" title="Salivary gland">salivary glands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallbladder" title="Gallbladder">gallbladder</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The tract may also be divided into <a href="/wiki/Foregut" title="Foregut">foregut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midgut" title="Midgut">midgut</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hindgut" title="Hindgut">hindgut</a>, reflecting the <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryological</a> origin of each segment. The whole human GI tract is about nine metres (30 feet) long at <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a>. It is considerably shorter in the living body because the intestines, which are tubes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle_tissue" title="Smooth muscle tissue">smooth muscle tissue</a>, maintain constant <a href="/wiki/Muscle_tone" title="Muscle tone">muscle tone</a> in a halfway-tense state but can relax in spots to allow for local distention and <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G._C._J._P._pp._290&#8211;294_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-G._C._J._P._pp._290&#8211;294-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raines_Arbour_Thompson_Figueroa-Bodine_pp._67&#8211;72_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raines_Arbour_Thompson_Figueroa-Bodine_pp._67&#8211;72-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The gastrointestinal tract contains trillions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbes</a>, with some 4,000 different <a href="/wiki/Strain_(biology)" title="Strain (biology)">strains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> having diverse roles in maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora#Metabolism" title="Gut flora">metabolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clarke2014rev_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke2014rev-10">[10]</a></sup> Cells of the GI tract release <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a> to help regulate the digestive process. These <a href="/wiki/Digestion#Digestive_hormones" title="Digestion">digestive hormones</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Gastrin" title="Gastrin">gastrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">secretin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholecystokinin" title="Cholecystokinin">cholecystokinin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ghrelin" title="Ghrelin">ghrelin</a>, are mediated through either <a href="/wiki/Intracrine" title="Intracrine">intracrine</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autocrine" title="Autocrine">autocrine</a> mechanisms, indicating that the cells releasing these hormones are conserved structures throughout <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div The tract itself is divided into upper and lower tracts, and the intestines <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small</a> and <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large</a> parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_gastrointestinal_tract">Upper gastrointestinal tract</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Upper gastrointestinal tract">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">Esophagus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">Stomach</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a></div>\n<p>The upper gastrointestinal tract consists of the <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\nThe exact demarcation between the upper and lower tracts is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suspensory_muscle_of_the_duodenum" title="Suspensory muscle of the duodenum">suspensory muscle of the duodenum</a>. This differentiates the embryonic borders between the foregut and midgut, and is also the division commonly used by clinicians to describe gastrointestinal bleeding as being of either "upper" or "lower" origin. Upon <a href="/wiki/Dissection" title="Dissection">dissection</a>, the duodenum may appear to be a unified organ, but it is divided into four segments based upon function, location, and internal anatomy. The four segments of the duodenum are as follows (starting at the stomach, and moving toward the jejunum): <a href="/wiki/Duodenal_bulb" title="Duodenal bulb">bulb</a>, descending, horizontal, and ascending. The suspensory muscle attaches the superior border of the ascending duodenum to the <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">diaphragm</a>.\n</p><p>The suspensory muscle is an important anatomical landmark which shows the formal division between the duodenum and the jejunum, the first and second parts of the small intestine, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Warrell2005_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Warrell2005-14">[14]</a></sup> This is a thin muscle which is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mesoderm" title="Mesoderm">mesoderm</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lower_gastrointestinal_tract">Lower gastrointestinal tract</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lower gastrointestinal tract">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The lower gastrointestinal tract includes most of the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> and all of the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_anatomy" title="Human anatomy">human anatomy</a>, the <b>intestine</b> (<b>bowel</b>, or <b>gut</b>. Greek: <b>&#233;ntera</b>) is the segment of the gastrointestinal tract extending from the pyloric sphincter of the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a> and, as in other mammals, consists of two segments, the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a>. In humans, the small intestine is further subdivided into the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jejunum" title="Jejunum">jejunum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ileum" title="Ileum">ileum</a> while the large intestine is subdivided into the, <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a>, ascending, transverse, descending and sigmoid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kapoor2011_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kapoor2011-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayLargeIntestine_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrayLargeIntestine-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_intestine">Small intestine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Small intestine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">Small intestine</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> begins at the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> and is a tubular structure, usually between 6 and 7 m long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRAYS2005_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRAYS2005-18">[18]</a></sup> Its mucosal area in an adult human is about 30 m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-19">[19]</a></sup> Its main function is to absorb the products of digestion (including carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and vitamins) into the bloodstream. There are three major divisions:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">Duodenum</a>: A short structure (about 20&#8211;25&#160;cm long<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRAYS2005_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRAYS2005-18">[18]</a></sup>) which receives <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a> from the stomach, together with <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_juice" title="Pancreatic juice">pancreatic juice</a> containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_enzymes" title="Digestive enzymes">digestive enzymes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_bladder" title="Gall bladder">gall bladder</a>. The digestive enzymes break down proteins, and bile <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsifies</a> fats into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micelles" title="Micelles">micelles</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> contains <a href="/wiki/Brunner%27s_glands" title="Brunner\'s glands">Brunner\'s glands</a> which produce a mucus-rich alkaline secretion containing <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a>. These secretions, in combination with bicarbonate from the pancreas, neutralize the stomach acids contained in the chyme.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jejunum" title="Jejunum">Jejunum</a>: This is the midsection of the small intestine, connecting the duodenum to the ileum. It is about 2.5 m long, and contains the <a href="/wiki/Circular_folds" title="Circular folds">circular folds</a> also known as plicae circulares, and <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_villus" title="Intestinal villus">villi</a> that increase its surface area. Products of digestion (sugars, amino acids, and fatty acids) are absorbed into the bloodstream here.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ileum" title="Ileum">Ileum</a>: The final section of the small intestine. It is about 3 m long, and contains <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_villus" title="Intestinal villus">villi</a> similar to the jejunum. It absorbs mainly <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B12</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bile_acids" title="Bile acids">bile acids</a>, as well as any other remaining nutrients.</li></ol>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Large_intestine">Large intestine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Large intestine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">Large intestine</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a> also called the colon, consists of the <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>. It also includes the <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a>, which is attached to the <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a>. The colon is further divided into:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">Cecum</a> (first portion of the colon) and <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">Ascending colon</a> (ascending in the back wall of the abdomen)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Colic_flexures" title="Colic flexures">Right colic flexure</a> (flexed portion of the ascending and transverse colon apparent to the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">Transverse colon</a> (passing below the diaphragm)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Colic_flexures" title="Colic flexures">Left colic flexure</a> (flexed portion of the transverse and descending colon apparent to the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">Descending colon</a> (descending down the left side of the abdomen)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">Sigmoid colon</a> (a loop of the colon closest to the rectum)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">Rectum</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">Anus</a></li></ol>\n<p>The main function of the large intestine is to absorb water.\nThe area of the large intestinal mucosa of an adult human is about 2 m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_digestive_system" title="Development of the digestive system">Development of the digestive system</a></div>\n<p>The gut is an <a href="/wiki/Endoderm" title="Endoderm">endoderm</a>-derived structure. At approximately the sixteenth day of human development, the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a> begins to fold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventrally</a> (with the embryo\'s ventral surface becoming <a href="/wiki/Concave_polygon" title="Concave polygon">concave</a>) in two directions: the sides of the embryo fold in on each other and the head and tail fold toward one another. The result is that a piece of the <a href="/wiki/Yolk_sac" title="Yolk sac">yolk sac</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Endoderm" title="Endoderm">endoderm</a>-lined structure in contact with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral" title="Ventral">ventral</a> aspect of the embryo, begins to be pinched off to become the primitive gut. The yolk sac remains connected to the gut tube via the <a href="/wiki/Vitelline_duct" title="Vitelline duct">vitelline duct</a>. Usually this structure regresses during development; in cases where it does not, it is known as <a href="/wiki/Meckel%27s_diverticulum" title="Meckel\'s diverticulum">Meckel\'s diverticulum</a>.\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetal</a> life, the primitive gut is gradually patterned into three segments: <a href="/wiki/Foregut" title="Foregut">foregut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midgut" title="Midgut">midgut</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hindgut" title="Hindgut">hindgut</a>. Although these terms are often used in reference to segments of the primitive gut, they are also used regularly to describe regions of the definitive gut as well.\n</p><p>Each segment of the gut is further specified and gives rise to specific gut and gut-related structures in later development. Components derived from the gut proper, including the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a>, develop as swellings or dilatations  in the cells of the primitive gut. In contrast, gut-related derivatives &#8212; that is, those structures that derive from the primitive gut but are not part of the gut proper, in general develop as out-pouchings of the primitive gut. The mucosa surrounds the <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">lumen</a>, or open space within the tube. This layer comes in direct contact with digested food (<a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a>). The mucosa is made up of:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Intestinal_epithelium" title="Intestinal epithelium">Epithelium</a> &#8211; innermost layer. Responsible for most digestive, absorptive and secretory processes.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lamina_propria" title="Lamina propria">Lamina propria</a> &#8211; a layer of connective tissue. Unusually cellular compared to most connective tissue</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Muscularis_mucosae" title="Muscularis mucosae">Muscularis mucosae</a> &#8211; a thin layer of smooth muscle that aids the passing of material and enhances the interaction between the epithelial layer and the contents of the lumen by agitation and <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The mucosae are highly specialized in each organ of the gastrointestinal tract to deal with the different conditions. The most variation is seen in the epithelium.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Submucosa">Submucosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Submucosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Submucosa" title="Submucosa">Submucosa</a></div>\n<p>The submucosa consists of a dense irregular layer of connective tissue with large blood vessels, lymphatics, and nerves branching into the mucosa and muscularis externa. It contains the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submucosal_plexus" title="Submucosal plexus">submucosal plexus</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Enteric_nervous_system" title="Enteric nervous system">enteric nervous plexus</a>, situated on the inner surface of the <i>muscularis externa</i>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscular_layer">Muscular layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Muscular layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Muscular_layer" title="Muscular layer">muscular layer</a> consists of an inner circular layer and a <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location" title="Anatomical terms of location">longitudinal</a> outer layer. The circular layer prevents food from traveling backward and the longitudinal layer shortens the tract. The layers are not truly longitudinal or circular, rather the layers of muscle are helical with different pitches. The inner circular is helical with a steep pitch and the outer longitudinal is helical with a much shallower pitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarna_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarna-22">[22]</a></sup> Whilst the muscularis externa is similar throughout the entire gastrointestinal tract, an exception is the stomach which has an additional inner oblique muscular layer to aid with grinding and mixing of food. The muscularis externa of the stomach is composed of the inner oblique layer, middle circular layer and outer longitudinal layer .\n</p><p>Between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers is the <a href="/wiki/Myenteric_plexus" title="Myenteric plexus">myenteric plexus</a>. This controls peristalsis. Activity is initiated by the pacemaker cells, (myenteric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_cells_of_Cajal" title="Interstitial cells of Cajal">interstitial cells of Cajal</a>). The gut has intrinsic peristaltic activity (<a href="/wiki/Basal_electrical_rhythm" title="Basal electrical rhythm">basal electrical rhythm</a>) due to its self-contained enteric nervous system. The rate can be modulated by the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic nervous system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarna_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sarna-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The coordinated contractions of these layers is called <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a> and propels the food through the tract. Food in the GI tract is called a bolus (ball of food) from the mouth down to the stomach. After the stomach, the food is partially digested and semi-liquid, and is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a>. In the large intestine the remaining semi-solid substance is referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faeces" title="Faeces">faeces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarna_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sarna-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Adventitia_and_serosa">Adventitia and serosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Adventitia and serosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Serous_membrane" title="Serous membrane">Serous membrane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adventitia" title="Adventitia">Adventitia</a></div>\n<p>The outermost layer of the gastrointestinal tract consists of several layers of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peritoneum#Classification_of_abdominal_structures" title="Peritoneum">Intraperitoneal</a> parts of the GI tract are covered with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serosa" title="Serosa">serosa</a>. These include most of the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, first part of the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>, all of the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caecum" title="Caecum">caecum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermiform_appendix" title="Vermiform appendix">appendix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse colon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid colon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>. In these sections of the gut there is clear boundary between the gut and the surrounding tissue. These parts of the tract have a <a href="/wiki/Mesentery" title="Mesentery">mesentery</a>.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroperitoneal" title="Retroperitoneal">Retroperitoneal</a> parts are covered with <a href="/wiki/Adventitia" title="Adventitia">adventitia</a>. They blend into the surrounding tissue and are fixed in position. For example, the retroperitoneal section of the duodenum usually passes through the <a href="/wiki/Transpyloric_plane" title="Transpyloric plane">transpyloric plane</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pylorus" title="Pylorus">pylorus</a> of the stomach, distal <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending colon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anal_canal" title="Anal canal">anal canal</a>. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Human_mouth" title="Human mouth">oral cavity</a> has adventitia.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gene_and_protein_expression">Gene and protein expression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gene and protein expression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Approximately 20,000 protein coding genes are expressed in human cells and 75% of these genes are expressed in at least one of the different parts of the digestive organ system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Over 600 of these genes are more specifically expressed in one or more parts of the GI tract and the corresponding proteins have functions related to digestion of food and uptake of nutrients. Examples of specific proteins with such functions are <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsinogen PGC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lipase" title="Lipase">lipase LIPF</a>, expressed in <a href="/wiki/Gastric_chief_cell" title="Gastric chief cell">chief cells</a>, and gastric <a href="/wiki/ATPase" title="ATPase">ATPase ATP4A</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastric_intrinsic_factor" title="Gastric intrinsic factor">gastric intrinsic factor GIF</a>, expressed in <a href="/wiki/Parietal_cell" title="Parietal cell">parietal cells</a> of the stomach mucosa. Specific proteins expressed in the stomach and duodenum involved in defence include <a href="/wiki/Mucin" title="Mucin">mucin</a> proteins, such as <a href="/wiki/Mucin_6" title="Mucin 6">mucin 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intelectin-1" title="Intelectin-1">intelectin-1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The time taken for food or other ingested objects to transit through the gastrointestinal tract varies depending on many factors, but roughly, it takes less than an hour after a meal for 50% of stomach contents to empty into the intestines while total emptying takes around 2 hours. Subsequently, 50% emptying of the small intestine takes between 1 and 2 hours. Finally, transit through the colon takes 12 to 50 hours with wide variation between individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Immune_function">Immune function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Immune function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Immune_barrier">Immune barrier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Immune barrier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The gastrointestinal tract forms an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The surface area of the digestive tract is estimated to be about 32 square meters, or about half a badminton court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> With such a large exposure (more than three times larger than the <a href="/wiki/Human_skin" title="Human skin">exposed surface of the skin</a>), these immune components function to prevent pathogens from entering the blood and lymph circulatory systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Fundamental components of this protection are provided by the <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_mucosal_barrier" title="Intestinal mucosal barrier">intestinal mucosal barrier</a> which is composed of physical, biochemical, and immune elements elaborated by the intestinal mucosa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Microorganisms also are kept at bay by an extensive immune system comprising the <a href="/wiki/Gut-associated_lymphoid_tissue" title="Gut-associated lymphoid tissue">gut-associated lymphoid tissue</a> (GALT)\n</p><p>There are additional factors contributing to protection from pathogen invasion. For example, low <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> (ranging from 1 to 4) of the stomach is fatal for many <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> that enter it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> (containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IgA" title="IgA">IgA</a> <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibodies</a>) neutralizes many pathogenic microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Other factors in the GI tract contribution to immune function include <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> secreted in the <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bile" title="Bile">bile</a>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Immune_system_homeostasis">Immune system homeostasis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Immune system homeostasis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>Beneficial bacteria also can contribute to the homeostasis of the gastrointestinal immune system. For example, <a href="/wiki/Clostridia" title="Clostridia">Clostridia</a>, one of the most predominant bacterial groups in the GI tract, play an important role in influencing the dynamics of the gut\'s immune system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It has been demonstrated that the intake of a high fiber diet could be the responsible for the induction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-regulatory_cell" title="T-regulatory cell">T-regulatory cells</a> (Tregs). This is due to the production of <a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">short-chain fatty acids</a> during the fermentation of plant-derived nutrients  such as <a href="/wiki/Butyrate" title="Butyrate">butyrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propionate" title="Propionate">propionate</a>.  Basically, the butyrate induces the differentiation of Treg cells by enhancing <a href="/wiki/Histone_H3" title="Histone H3">histone H3</a> <a href="/wiki/Acetylation#Protein_acetylation" title="Acetylation">acetylation</a> in the promoter and conserved non-coding sequence regions of the <a href="/wiki/FOXP3" title="FOXP3">FOXP3</a> locus, thus regulating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_cells" title="T cells">T cells</a>, resulting in the reduction of the inflammatory response and allergies.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Intestinal_microbiota">Intestinal microbiota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Intestinal microbiota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The large intestine hosts several kinds of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that can deal with molecules that the human body cannot otherwise break down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight2002_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knight2002-35">[35]</a></sup> This is an example of <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiosis</a>. These bacteria also account for the production of gases at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Host-pathogen_interface" title="Host-pathogen interface">host-pathogen interface</a>, inside our intestine(this gas is released as <i><a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a></i> when eliminated through the anus). However the large intestine is mainly concerned with the absorption of water from digested material (which is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>) and the re absorption of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, as well as any nutrients that may have escaped primary digestion in the ileum.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Health-enhancing <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">intestinal bacteria</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a> serve to prevent the overgrowth of potentially harmful <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> in the gut. These two types of bacteria compete for space and "food," as there are limited resources within the intestinal tract. A ratio of 80-85% beneficial to 15&#8211;20% potentially harmful bacteria generally is considered normal within the intestines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Detoxification_and_drug_metabolism">Detoxification and drug metabolism</span><span metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzymes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/CYP3A4" title="CYP3A4">CYP3A4</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Antiporter" title="Antiporter">antiporter</a> activities, are also instrumental in the intestine\'s role of <a href="/wiki/Drug_metabolism" title="Drug metabolism">drug metabolism</a> in the detoxification of <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xenobiotic" title="Xenobiotic">xenobiotics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This section discusses related diseases, medical associations with the gastrointestinal tract, and use in surgery.</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_disease" title="Gastrointestinal disease">Gastrointestinal disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gastroenterology" title="Gastroenterology">Gastroenterology</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Clinical_significance" title="Clinical significance">Clinical significance</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are many diseases and conditions that can affect the gastrointestinal system, including <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>.\n</p><p>Various <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> that cause <a href="/wiki/Foodborne_illness" title="Foodborne illness">foodborne illnesses</a>, can induce <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a> which results from <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of the stomach and small intestine. <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">Antibiotics</a> to treat such bacterial infections can decrease the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbiome_of_humans" title="Microbiome of humans">microbiome</a> diversity of the gastrointestinal tract, and further enable inflammatory mediators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Gastroenteritis is the most common disease of the GI tract.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_cancer" title="Gastrointestinal cancer">Gastrointestinal cancer</a> may occur at any point in the gastrointestinal tract, and includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mouth_cancer" title="Mouth cancer">mouth cancer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongue_cancer" title="Tongue cancer">tongue cancer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oesophageal_cancer" title="Oesophageal cancer">oesophageal cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stomach_cancer" title="Stomach cancer">stomach cancer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer" title="Colorectal cancer">colorectal cancer</a>.</li>\n<li>Inflammatory conditions. <a href="/wiki/Ileitis" title="Ileitis">Ileitis</a> is an inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Ileum" title="Ileum">ileum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colitis" title="Colitis">colitis</a> is an inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Appendicitis" title="Appendicitis">Appendicitis</a> is inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Appendix_(anatomy)" title="Appendix (anatomy)">appendix</a> located at the caecum. This is a potentially fatal condition if left untreated; most cases of appendicitis require surgical intervention.</li></ul>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Diverticular_disease" title="Diverticular disease">Diverticular disease</a> is a condition that is very common in older people in industrialized countries.  It usually affects the large intestine but has been known to affect the small intestine as well.  <a href="/wiki/Diverticulosis" title="Diverticulosis">Diverticulosis</a> occurs when pouches form on the intestinal wall.  Once the pouches become inflamed it is known as <a href="/wiki/Diverticulitis" title="Diverticulitis">diverticulitis</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Inflammatory_bowel_disease" title="Inflammatory bowel disease">Inflammatory bowel disease</a> is an inflammatory condition affecting the bowel walls, and includes the subtypes <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn\'s disease">Crohn\'s disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis" title="Ulcerative colitis">ulcerative colitis</a>. While Crohn\'s can affect the entire gastrointestinal tract, ulcerative colitis is limited to the large intestine. Crohn\'s disease is widely regarded as an <a href="/wiki/Autoimmunity" title="Autoimmunity">autoimmune disease</a>. Although ulcerative colitis is often treated as though it were an autoimmune disease, there is no consensus that it actually is such.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Functional_gastrointestinal_disorder" title="Functional gastrointestinal disorder">Functional gastrointestinal disorders</a> the most common of which is <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a>. Functional constipation and <a href="/wiki/Chronic_functional_abdominal_pain" title="Chronic functional abdominal pain">chronic functional abdominal pain</a> are other functional disorders of the intestine that have physiological causes, but do not have identifiable structural, chemical, or infectious pathologies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symptoms">Symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Several symptoms are used to indicate problems with the gastrointestinal tract:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Vomiting" stool</a>, which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haematochezia" title="Haematochezia">fresh red blood</a>, maroon-coloured blood, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melaena" class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_surgery" title="Gastrointestinal surgery">Gastrointestinal surgery</a> can often be performed in the outpatient setting.  In the United States in 2012, operations on the digestive system accounted for 3 of the 25 most common ambulatory surgery procedures and constituted 9.1 percent of all outpatient ambulatory surgeries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imaging">Imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Various methods of imaging the gastrointestinal tract include the <a href="/wiki/Upper_gastrointestinal_series" title="Upper gastrointestinal series">upper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_gastrointestinal_series" title="Lower gastrointestinal series">lower gastrointestinal series</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioopaque" title="Radioopaque">Radioopaque</a> dyes may be swallowed to produce a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barium_swallow" title="Barium swallow">barium swallow</a></li>\n<li>Parts of the tract may be visualised by camera. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Endoscopy" title="Endoscopy">endoscopy</a> if examining the upper gastrointestinal tract, and <a href="/wiki/Colonoscopy" title="Colonoscopy">colonoscopy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sigmoidoscopy" title="Sigmoidoscopy">sigmoidoscopy</a> if examining the lower gastrointestinal tract. <a href="/wiki/Capsule_endoscopy" title="Capsule endoscopy">Capsule endoscopy</a> is where a capsule containing a camera is swallowed in order to examine the tract. <a href="/wiki/Biopsy" title="Biopsy">Biopsies</a> may also be taken when examined.</li>\n<li>An <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_x-ray" title="Abdominal x-ray">abdominal x-ray</a> may be used to examine the lower gastrointestinal tract.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_related_diseases">Other related diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other related diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">Cholera</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Enteric_duplication_cyst" title="Enteric duplication cyst">Enteric duplication cyst</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Giardiasis" title="Giardiasis">Giardiasis</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pancreatitis" title="Pancreatitis">Pancreatitis</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer_disease" title="Peptic ulcer disease">Peptic ulcer disease</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">Yellow fever</a></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Helicobacter_pylori" title="Helicobacter pylori">Helicobacter pylori</a></i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-negative" title="Gram-negative">gram-negative</a> spiral bacterium. Over half the world\'s population is infected with it, mainly during childhood; it is not certain how the disease is transmitted. It colonizes the gastrointestinal system, predominantly the stomach. The bacterium has specific survival conditions that our gastric <a href="/wiki/Microenvironment_(biology)" title="Microenvironment (biology)">microenvironment</a>: it is both <a href="/wiki/Capnophile" title="Capnophile">capnophilic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microaerophile" title="Microaerophile">microaerophilic</a>. <i>Helicobacter</i> also exhibits a <a href="/wiki/Tropism" title="Tropism">tropism</a> for gastric epithelial lining and the gastric mucosal layer about it. Gastric colonization of this bacterium triggers a robust immune response leading to moderate to severe <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>. Signs and symptoms of infection are gastritis, burning abdominal pain, weight loss, loss of appetite, bloating, burping, nausea, bloody vomit, and black tarry stools. Infection can be detected in a number of ways: GI X-rays, endoscopy, blood tests for anti-<i>Helicobacter</i> antibodies, a stool test, and a urease breath test (which is a by-product of the bacteria). If caught soon enough, it can be treated with three doses of different proton pump inhibitors as well as two antibiotics, taking about a week to cure. If not caught soon enough, surgery may be required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iagcj_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-iagcj-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-my_grad_advisor_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-my_grad_advisor-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-immu-sys_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-immu-sys-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mims-med-microb_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-mims-med-microb-42">[42]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestinal_pseudoobstruction" title="Intestinal pseudoobstruction">Intestinal pseudo-obstruction</a> is a syndrome caused by a malformation of the digestive system, characterized by a severe impairment in the ability of the intestines to push and assimilate. Symptoms include daily abdominal and stomach pain, nausea, severe distension, vomiting, heartburn, dysphagia, diarrhea, constipation, dehydration and malnutrition. There is no cure for intestinal pseudo-obstruction. Different types of surgery and treatment managing life-threatening complications such as ileus and volvulus, intestinal stasis which lead to bacterial overgrowth, and resection of affected or dead parts of the gut may be needed. Many patients require parenteral nutrition.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ileus" title="Ileus">Ileus</a> is a blockage of the intestines.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Coeliac_disease" title="Coeliac disease">Coeliac disease</a> is a common form of <a href="/wiki/Malabsorption" title="Malabsorption">malabsorption</a>, affecting up to 1% of people of northern European descent.  An autoimmune response is triggered in intestinal cells by digestion of gluten proteins. Ingestion of proteins found in wheat, barley and rye, causes villous atrophy in the small intestine.  Lifelong dietary avoidance of these foodstuffs in a gluten-free diet is the only treatment.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enteroviruses" title="Enteroviruses">Enteroviruses</a> are named by their transmission-route through the intestine (<i>enteric</i> meaning intestinal), but their symptoms aren\'t mainly associated with the intestine.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Endometriosis" title="Endometriosis">Endometriosis</a> can affect the intestines, with similar symptoms to IBS.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bowel_twist" title="Bowel twist">Bowel twist</a> (or similarly, bowel strangulation) is a comparatively rare event (usually developing sometime after major bowel surgery). It is, however, hard to diagnose correctly, and if left uncorrected can lead to bowel <a href="/wiki/Infarction" title="Infarction">infarction</a> and death. (The singer <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Gibb" title="Maurice Gibb">Maurice Gibb</a> is understood to have died from this.)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Angiodysplasia" title="Angiodysplasia">Angiodysplasia</a> of the colon</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">Constipation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">Diarrhea</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hirschsprung%27s_disease" title="Hirschsprung\'s disease">Hirschsprung\'s disease</a> (aganglionosis)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Intussusception_(medical_disorder)" title="Intussusception (medical disorder)">Intussusception</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Polyp_(medicine)" title="Polyp (medicine)">Polyp (medicine)</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_polyp" title="Colorectal polyp">colorectal polyp</a>)</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudomembranous_colitis" title="Pseudomembranous colitis">Pseudomembranous colitis</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Toxic_megacolon" title="Toxic megacolon">Toxic megacolon</a> usually a complication of ulcerative colitis</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_animal_guts">Uses of animal guts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Uses of animal guts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Intestines from animals other than humans are used in a number of ways.  From each species of <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> that is a source of <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, a corresponding <a href="/wiki/Rennet" title="Rennet">rennet</a> is obtained from the intestines of milk-fed <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/calves" title="wikt:calves">calves</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">Pig</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calf_(animal)" title="Calf (animal)">calf</a> intestines are eaten, and pig intestines are used as <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausage</a> casings. Calf intestines supply <a href="/wiki/Calf-intestinal_alkaline_phosphatase" title="Calf-intestinal alkaline phosphatase">calf-intestinal alkaline phosphatase</a> (CIP), and are used to make <a href="/wiki/Goldbeater%27s_skin" title="Goldbeater\'s skin">goldbeater\'s skin</a>.\nOther uses are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The use of animal gut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strings_(music)" title="Strings (music)">strings</a> by musicians can be traced back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Third dynasty of Egypt">third dynasty of Egypt</a>.  In the recent past, strings were made out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" title="Domestic sheep">lamb</a> gut.  With the advent of the modern era, musicians have tended to use strings made of <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, or synthetic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.  Some instrumentalists, however, still use gut strings in order to evoke the older tone quality.  Although such strings were commonly referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">catgut</a>" strings, <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a> were never used as a source for gut strings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Sheep gut was the original source for natural gut string used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racquet" title="Racquet">racquets</a>, such as for <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>.  Today, synthetic strings are much more common, but the best gut strings are now made out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a> gut.</li>\n<li>Gut cord has also been used to produce strings for the snares that provide a <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">snare drum</a>\'s characteristic buzzing timbre.  While the modern snare drum almost always uses metal wire rather than gut cord, the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a> <a href="/wiki/Bendir" title="Bendir">bendir</a> frame drum still uses gut for this purpose.</li>\n<li>"Natural" <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausage</a> hulls, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casing_(sausage)" title="Casing (sausage)">casings</a>, are made of animal gut, especially hog, beef, and lamb.</li>\n<li>The wrapping of <a href="/wiki/Kokoretsi" title="Kokoretsi">kokoretsi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gardoubakia" title="Gardoubakia">gardoubakia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Torcinello" title="Torcinello">torcinello</a> is made of lamb (or goat) gut.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Haggis" title="Haggis">Haggis</a> is traditionally boiled in, and served in, a sheep stomach.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chitterlings" title="Chitterlings">Chitterlings</a>, a kind of food, consist of thoroughly washed <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pig</a>\'s gut.</li>\n<li>Animal gut was used to make the cord lines in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longcase_clock" title="Longcase clock">longcase clocks</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Fusee_(horology)" title="Fusee (horology)">fusee</a> movements in <a href="/wiki/Bracket_clock" title="Bracket clock">bracket clocks</a>, but may be replaced by metal wire.</li>\n<li>The oldest known <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condoms</a>, from 1640 AD, were made from animal intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_animals">Other animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Other animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">Ruminant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanogens_in_digestive_tract_of_ruminants" title="Methanogens in digestive tract of ruminants">Methanogens in digestive tract of ruminants</a></div>\n<p>Many birds and other animals have a specialised stomach in the digestive tract called a <a href="/wiki/Gizzard" title="Gizzard">gizzard</a> used for grinding up food.\n</p><p>Another feature not found in the human but found in a range of other animals is the <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>. In birds this is found as a pouch alongside the esophagus.\n</p><p>Other animals including <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">egg-laying mammals</a> have a major difference in their GI tract in that it ends in a <a href="/wiki/Cloaca" title="Cloaca">cloaca</a> and not an <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gastrointestinal_tract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article uses anatomical terminology;
