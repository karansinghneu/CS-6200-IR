The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription reads: <i>&#914;&#913;&#931;&#921;&#923;&#917;&#937;&#931; &#916;&#921;&#927;&#916;&#927;&#932;&#927;&#933;</i> &#8211; "(of) King Diodotus".</div></div></div>\n<p>Diodotus, the <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrap</a> of Bactria (and probably the surrounding provinces) founded the Greco-Bactrian Kingdom when he seceded from the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> around 250 BC and became King <a href="/wiki/Diodotus_I" title="Diodotus I">Diodotus I</a> of Bactria. The preserved ancient sources (see below) are somewhat contradictory, and the exact date of Bactrian independence has not been settled. Somewhat simplified, there is a high chronology (c. 255 BC) and a low chronology (c. 246 BC) for Diodotos&#8217; secession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\nThe high chronology has the advantage of explaining why the Seleucid king <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II_Theos" title="Antiochus II Theos">Antiochus II</a> issued very few coins in Bactria, as Diodotos would have become independent there early in Antiochus\' reign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On the other hand, the low chronology, from the mid-240s BC, has the advantage of connecting the secession of Diodotus I with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Syrian_War" title="Third Syrian War">Third Syrian War</a>, a catastrophic conflict for the Seleucid Empire.\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Diodotus, the governor of the thousand cities of Bactria (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Theodotus, mille urbium Bactrianarum praefectus</i>), defected and proclaimed himself king; all the other people of the Orient followed his example and seceded from the Macedonians. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junianus_Justinus" title="Junianus Justinus">Justin</a>, XLI,4)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The new kingdom, highly urbanized and considered as one of the richest of the Orient (<i>opulentissimum illud mille urbium Bactrianum imperium</i> "The extremely prosperous Bactrian empire of the thousand cities" Justin, XLI,1 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>), was to further grow in power and engage in territorial expansion to the east and the west:\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BhalkCapital.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="923" data-file-width="761" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/BhalkCapital.jpg/170px-BhalkCapital.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/BhalkCapital.jpg/255px-BhalkCapital.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/BhalkCapital.jpg/340px-BhalkCapital.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BhalkCapital.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Remains of a Hellenistic <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capital</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>, ancient Bactra.</div></div></div>\n<blockquote><p>The Greeks who caused Bactria to revolt grew so powerful on account of the fertility of the country that they became masters, not only of <a href="/wiki/Ariana" title="Ariana">Ariana</a>, but also of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Apollodorus_of_Artemita" title="Apollodorus of Artemita">Apollodorus of Artemita</a> says: and more tribes were subdued by them than by Alexander... Their cities were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactra" title="Bactra">Bactra</a> (also called Zariaspa, through which flows a river bearing the same name and emptying into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>), and Darapsa, and several others. Among these was <a href="/wiki/Eucratideia" title="Eucratideia">Eucratidia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eucratidia_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eucratidia-9">[9]</a></sup> which was named after its ruler. (Strabo, XI.XI.I)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo_XI.XI.I_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo_XI.XI.I-10">[10]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>In 247 BC, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_empire" title="Ptolemaic empire">Ptolemaic empire</a> (the Greek rulers of Egypt following the death of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>) captured the Seleucid capital, <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>. In the resulting power vacuum, <a href="/wiki/Andragoras_(Seleucid_satrap)" title="Andragoras (Seleucid satrap)">Andragoras</a>, the Seleucid <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrap</a> of Parthia, proclaimed independence from the Seleucids, declaring himself king. A decade later, he was <a href="/wiki/Parni_conquest_of_Parthia" title="Parni conquest of Parthia">defeated and killed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Arsaces" title="Arsaces">Arsaces</a> of Parthia, leading to the rise of a <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian Empire</a>. This cut Bactria off from contact with the Greek world. Overland trade continued at a reduced rate, while sea trade between <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">Greek Egypt</a> and Bactria developed.\n</p><p>Diodotus was succeeded by his son <a href="/wiki/Diodotus_II" title="Diodotus II">Diodotus II</a>, who allied himself with the Parthian <a href="/wiki/Arsaces_I_of_Parthia" title="Arsaces I of Parthia">Arsaces</a> in his fight against <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_II_Callinicus" title="Seleucus II Callinicus">Seleucus II</a>:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Soon after, relieved by the death of Diodotus, Arsaces made peace and concluded an alliance with his son, also by the name of Diodotus; some time later he fought against Seleucos who came to punish the rebels, and he prevailed: the Parthians celebrated this day as the one that marked the beginning of their freedom. (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Junianus_Justinus" title="Junianus Justinus">Justin</a>, XLI,4)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span id="Overthrow_of_Diodotus_II_.28230_BC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Overthrow_of_Diodotus_II_(230_BC)">Overthrow of Diodotus title="Polybius">Polybius</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_11.34_13-0"><a href="/wiki/Diodotus_I" title="Diodotus I">Diodotus I</a> around 230-220 BC and started his own dynasty. Euthydemus\'s control extended to Sogdiana, going beyond the city of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a> founded by Alexander the Great in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>And they also held Sogdiana, situated above Bactriana towards the east between the Oxus River, which forms the boundary between the Bactrians and the Sogdians, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iaxartes" title="Iaxartes">Iaxartes</a> River. And the Iaxartes forms also the boundary between the Sogdians and the nomads. (Strabo XI.11.2)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo_11.11.2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo_11.11.2-14">[14]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seleucid_invasion">Seleucid invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seleucid invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EuthydemusMedailles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="620" data-file-width="1272" decoding="async" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/EuthydemusMedailles.jpg/220px-EuthydemusMedailles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/EuthydemusMedailles.jpg/330px-EuthydemusMedailles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/EuthydemusMedailles.jpg/440px-EuthydemusMedailles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EuthydemusMedailles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coin depicting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_I" title="Euthydemus I">Euthydemus</a> 230&#8211;200 BC. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription reads: <i>&#914;&#913;&#931;&#921;&#923;&#917;&#937;&#931; &#917;&#933;&#920;&#933;&#916;&#919;&#924;&#927;&#933;</i> &#8211; "(of) King Euthydemus".</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_I" title="Euthydemus I">Euthydemus</a> was attacked by the Seleucid ruler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochus_III" title="Antiochus III">Antiochus III</a> around 210 BC. Although he commanded 10,000 horsemen, Euthydemus initially lost a <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Arius" title="Battle of the Arius">battle</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hari_River,_Afghanistan" title="Hari River, Afghanistan">Arius</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_10.49,_Battle_of_the_Arius_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_10.49,_Battle_of_the_Arius-15">[15]</a></sup> and had to retreat. He then successfully <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Bactra" title="Siege of Bactra">resisted a three-year siege</a> in the fortified city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactra" title="Bactra">Bactra</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>), before Antiochus finally decided to recognize the new ruler, and to offer one of his daughters to Euthydemus\'s son <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria" title="Demetrius I of Bactria">Demetrius</a> around 206 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Classical accounts also relate that Euthydemus negotiated peace with Antiochus III by suggesting that he deserved credit for overthrowing the original rebel Diodotus, and that he was protecting Central Asia from nomadic invasions thanks to his defensive efforts:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p> ...for if he did not yield to this demand, neither of them would be safe: seeing that great hordes of Nomads were close at hand, who were a danger to both; and that if they admitted them into the country, it would certainly be utterly barbarised. (<a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a>, 11.34)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_11.34_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_11.34-13">[13]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_expansion">Geographic expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographic expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Following the departure of the Seleucid army, the Bactrian kingdom seems to have expanded. In the west, areas in north-eastern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> may have been absorbed, possibly as far as into <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>, whose ruler had been defeated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochus_III" title="Antiochus III">Antiochus the Great</a>. These territories possibly are identical with the Bactrian satrapies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapuria" title="Tapuria">Tapuria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khorasan" title="Greater Khorasan">Traxiane</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_Warrior_Statue_without_shade.jpg"><img alt="" href="/wiki/Fergana_Valley" title="Fergana Valley">Ferghana</a>, and there are indications that from <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a> the Greco-Bactrians may have led expeditions as far as <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cr%C3%BCmqi" title="&#220;r&#252;mqi">&#220;r&#252;mqi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, leading to the first known contacts between China and the West around 220 BC. The Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> too writes that: "they extended their empire even as far as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">Seres</a> (Chinese) and the <a href="/wiki/Phryni" title="Phryni">Phryni</a>". (<a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, XI.XI.I).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo_XI.XI.I_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo_XI.XI.I-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several statuettes and representations of Greek soldiers have been found north of the <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a>, on the doorstep to China, and are today on display in the <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> museum at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urumqi" title="Urumqi">Urumqi</a> (Boardman).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Middle Eastern or Greek influences on Chinese art have also been suggested (<a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hirth" title="Friedrich Hirth">Hirth</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rostovtzeff" title="Rostovtzeff">Rostovtzeff</a>). Designs with <a href="/wiki/Rosette_(design)" title="Rosette (design)">rosette</a> flowers, geometric lines, and glass inlays, suggestive of Egyptian, Persian, and/or Hellenistic influences,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> can be found on some early <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> bronze mirrors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Some speculate that Greek influence is found in the artworks of the burial site of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>\'s first Emperor <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>, dating back to the 3rd century BC, including in the manufacture of the famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terracotta_army" title="Terracotta army">Terracotta army</a>. This idea suggested that Greek artists may have come to China at that time to train local artisans in making sculptures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> However, this idea is disputed by Chinese scholars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Numismatics" title="Numismatics">Numismatics</a> also suggest that some technology exchanges may have occurred on these occasions: the Greco-Bactrians were the first in the world to issue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cupro-nickel" title="Cupro-nickel">cupro-nickel</a> (75/25 ratio) coins,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> an alloy technology only known by the Chinese at the time under the name "White copper" (some weapons from the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a> were in copper-nickel alloy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The practice of exporting Chinese metals, in particular iron, for trade is attested around that period. Kings Euthydemus, Euthydemus II, <a href="/wiki/Agathocles_of_Bactria" title="Agathocles of Bactria">Agathocles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pantaleon" title="Pantaleon">Pantaleon</a> made these coin issues around 170 BC and it has alternatively been suggested that a nickeliferous copper ore was the source from mines at <a href="/wiki/Anarak" title="Anarak">Anarak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Copper-nickel would not be used again in coinage until the 19th century.\n</p><p>The presence of Chinese people in India from ancient times is also suggested by the accounts of the "<a href="/wiki/Chinas" title="Chinas">Ci&#241;as</a>" in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manu_Smriti" title="Manu Smriti">Manu Smriti</a></i>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> explorer and ambassador <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> visited Bactria in 126 BC, and reported the presence of Chinese products in the Bactrian markets:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"When I was in Bactria (<a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a>)", Zhang Qian reported, "I saw bamboo canes from Qiong and cloth made in the province of Shu (territories of southwestern China). When I asked the people how they had gotten such articles, they replied, "Our merchants go buy them in the markets of Shendu (India)." (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i> 123, <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>, trans. Burton Watson).</p></blockquote>\n<p>Upon his return, Zhang Qian informed the Chinese emperor Han <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han_China" title="Emperor Wu of Han China">Wudi</a> of the level of sophistication of the urban civilizations of Ferghana, Bactria and Parthia, who became interested in developing commercial relationships with them:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The Son of Heaven on hearing all this reasoned thus: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a>) and the possessions of <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> (Anxi) are large countries, full of rare things, with a population living in fixed abodes and given to occupations somewhat identical with those of the Chinese people, and placing great value on the rich produce of China. (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanshu" title="Hanshu">Hanshu</a></i>, Former Han History).</p></blockquote>\n<p>A number of Chinese envoys were then sent to Central Asia, triggering the development of the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> from the end of the 2nd century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Contacts_with_the_Indian_Subcontinent_.28250.E2.80.93180.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Contacts_with_the_Indian_Subcontinent_(250&#8211;180)">Contacts with the Indian Subcontinent (250&#8211;180)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contacts with the Indian Subcontinent (250&#8211;180)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Indian emperor <a href="/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya" title="Chandragupta Maurya">Chandragupta</a>, founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan_dynasty" title="Mauryan dynasty">Mauryan dynasty</a>, had re-conquered the northwestern subcontinent upon the death of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> around 323 BC. However, contacts were kept with his Greek neighbours in the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>, a dynastic alliance or the recognition of intermarriage between Greeks and Indians were established (described as an agreement on <a href="/wiki/Epigamia" title="Epigamia">Epigamia</a> in Ancient sources), and several Greeks, such as the historian <a href="/wiki/Megasthenes" title="Megasthenes">Megasthenes</a>, resided at the Mauryan court. Subsequently, each Mauryan emperor had a Greek ambassador at his court.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AsokaKandahar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="717" data-file-width="683" decoding="async" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/AsokaKandahar.jpg/220px-AsokaKandahar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/AsokaKandahar.jpg/330px-AsokaKandahar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/AsokaKandahar.jpg/440px-AsokaKandahar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AsokaKandahar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kandahar_Bilingual_Rock_Inscription" title="Kandahar Bilingual Rock Inscription">Kandahar Bilingual Rock Inscription</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a>), found in <a href="/wiki/Kandahar" title="Kandahar">Kandahar</a>. Circa 250&#160;BC, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a> Museum.</div></div></div>\n<p>Chandragupta\'s grandson <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> converted to the Buddhist faith and became a great proselytizer in the line of the traditional Pali canon of <a href="/wiki/Theravada" title="Theravada">Theravada</a> Buddhism, directing his efforts towards the Indo-Iranic and the Hellenistic worlds from around 250 BC. According to the <a href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Ashoka" title="Edicts of Ashoka">Edicts of Ashoka</a>, set in stone, some of them written in Greek, he sent Buddhist emissaries to the Greek lands in Asia and as far as the Mediterranean. The edicts name each of the rulers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic</a> world at the time.\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The conquest by <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a> has been won here, on the borders, and even six hundred <a href="/wiki/Yojana" title="Yojana">yojanas</a> (4,000 miles) away, where the Greek king <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_II_Theos" title="Antiochus II Theos">Antiochos</a> rules, beyond there where the four kings named <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_II_Philadelphus" title="Ptolemy II Philadelphus">Ptolemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_II_Gonatas" title="Antigonus II Gonatas">Antigonos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magas_of_Cyrene" title="Magas of Cyrene">Magas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_II_of_Epirus" title="Alexander II of Epirus">Alexander</a> rule, likewise in the south among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chola" title="Chola">Cholas</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandya" title="Pandya">Pandyas</a>, and as far as <a href="/wiki/Tamraparni" title="Tamraparni">Tamraparni</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Ashoka" title="Edicts of Ashoka">Edicts of Ashoka</a>, 13th Rock Edict, S. Dhammika).</p></blockquote>\n<p>Some of the Greek populations that had remained in northwestern India apparently converted to Buddhism:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Here in the king\'s domain among the Greeks, the <a href="/wiki/Kambojas" title="Kambojas">Kambojas</a>, the Nabhakas, the Nabhapamkits, the Bhojas, the Pitinikas, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satavahanas" title="Satavahanas">Andhras</a> and the Palidas, everywhere people are following Beloved-of-the-Gods\' instructions in <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Ashoka" title="Edicts of Ashoka">Edicts of Ashoka</a>, 13th Rock Edict, S. Dhammika).</p></blockquote>\n<p>Furthermore, according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C4%81li" title="P&#257;li">Pali</a> sources, some of Ashoka\'s emissaries were Greek Buddhist monks, indicating close religious exchanges between the two cultures:\n</p><p><br/>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>When the thera (elder) Moggaliputta, the illuminator of the religion of the Conqueror (Ashoka), had brought the (third) council to an end&#8230; he sent forth theras, one here and one there: &#8230;and to Aparantaka (the "Western countries" corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a>) he sent the Greek (<a href="/wiki/Yona" title="Yona">Yona</a>) named <a href="/wiki/Dharmaraksita" title="Dharmaraksita">Dhammarakkhita</a>... and the thera Maharakkhita he sent into the country of the Yona. (<a href="/wiki/Mahavamsa" title="Mahavamsa">Mahavamsa</a>, XII).</p></blockquote>\n<p>Greco-Bactrians probably received these Buddhist emissaries (at least Maharakkhita, lit. "The Great Saved One", who was "sent to the country of the Yona") and somehow tolerated the Buddhist faith, although little proof remains.  In the 2nd century AD, the Christian dogmatist <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> recognized the existence of Buddhist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sramana" title="Sramana">Sramanas</a> among the Bactrians ("Bactrians" meaning "Oriental Greeks" in that period), and even their influence on Greek thought:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Thus philosophy, a thing of the highest utility, flourished in antiquity among the barbarians, shedding its light over the nations. And afterwards it came to <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. First in its ranks were the prophets of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptians</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Chaldea" title="Chaldea">Chaldeans</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a>; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Druids" title="Druids">Druids</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gauls</a>; and the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sramana" title="Sramana">Sramanas</a></b> among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactrians" title="Bactrians">Bactrians</a> ("&#931;&#945;&#961;&#956;&#945;&#957;&#945;&#943;&#959;&#953; &#914;&#940;&#954;&#964;&#961;&#969;&#957;"); and the philosophers of the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Magi" title="Magi">Magi</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persians</a>, who foretold the Saviour\'s birth, and came into the land of <a href="/wiki/Judea" title="Judea">Judea</a> guided by a star. The Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymnosophist" title="Gymnosophist">gymnosophists</a> are also in the number, and the other barbarian philosophers. And of these there are two classes, some of them called <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sramana" title="Sramana">Sramanas</a></b> ("&#931;&#945;&#961;&#956;&#940;&#957;&#945;&#953;"), and others <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmins" title="Brahmins">Brahmins</a> ("&#914;&#961;&#945;&#966;&#956;&#945;&#957;&#945;&#953;"). (Clement of Alexandria "The Stromata, or Miscellanies" Book I, Chapter XV)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Expansion into the Indian subcontinent (after 180 BC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:203px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Demetrius_I_of_Bactria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="191" data-file-width="201" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria.jpg" width="201"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Demetrius_I_of_Bactria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silver coin depicting <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria" title="Demetrius I of Bactria">Demetrius I of Bactria</a> (reigned c. 200&#8211;180 BC), wearing an elephant scalp, symbol of his conquests in northwest <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">Indo-Greek Kingdom</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Demetrius_I_of_Bactria" title="Demetrius I of Bactria">Demetrius</a>, the son of Euthydemus, started an invasion of the subcontinent from 180 BC, a few years after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan_empire" title="Mauryan empire">Mauryan empire</a> had been overthrown by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shunga_dynasty" title="Shunga dynasty">Shunga dynasty</a>. Historians differ on the motivations behind the invasion. Some historians suggest that the invasion of the subcontinent was intended to show their support for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan_empire" title="Mauryan empire">Mauryan empire</a>, and to protect the Buddhist faith from the religious persecutions of the <a href="/wiki/Shunga_Empire" title="Shunga Empire">Shungas</a> as alleged by Buddhist scriptures (Tarn). Other historians have argued however that the accounts of these persecutions have been exaggerated (<a href="/wiki/Romila_Thapar" title="Romila Thapar">Thapar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etienne_Lamotte" title="Etienne Lamotte">Lamotte</a>).\n</p><p>Demetrius may have been as far as the imperial capital <a href="/wiki/Pataliputra" title="Pataliputra">Pataliputra</a> in today\'s eastern India (today <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patna,_India" title="Patna, India">Patna</a>). However, these campaigns are typically attributed to Menander. The invasion was completed by 175 BC. This established in the northwestern Indian Subcontinent what is called the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_Kingdom" title="Indo-Greek Kingdom">Indo-Greek Kingdom</a>, which lasted for almost two centuries until around AD 10. The Buddhist faith flourished under the Indo-Greek kings, foremost among them <a href="/wiki/Menander_I" title="Menander I">Menander I</a>.\nIt was also a period of great cultural syncretism, exemplified by the development of <a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhism" title="Greco-Buddhism">Greco-Buddhism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usurpation_of_Eucratides">Usurpation of Eucratides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Usurpation of Eucratides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Back in Bactria, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucratides" title="Eucratides">Eucratides</a>, either a general of Demetrius or an ally of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucids" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a>, managed to overthrow the Euthydemid dynasty and establish his own rule around 170 BC, probably dethroning <a href="/wiki/Antimachus_I" title="Antimachus I">Antimachus I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antimachus_II" title="Antimachus II">Antimachus II</a>. The Indian branch of the Euthydemids tried to strike back. An Indian king called Demetrius (very likely <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_II_of_India" title="Demetrius II of India">Demetrius II</a>) is said to have returned to Bactria with 60,000 men to oust the usurper, but he apparently was defeated and killed in the encounter:\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="2259" decoding="async" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg/220px-Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg/330px-Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg/440px-Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tetradrachm_Eukratides.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silver <a href="/wiki/Tetradrachm" title="Tetradrachm">tetradrachm</a> of King <a href="/wiki/Eucratides_I" title="Eucratides I">Eucratides I</a> 171&#8211;145 BC. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription reads: <i>&#914;&#913;&#931;&#921;&#923;&#917;&#937;&#931; &#924;&#917;&#915;&#913;&#923;&#927;&#933; &#917;&#933;&#922;&#929;&#913;&#932;&#921;&#916;&#927;&#933;</i> &#8211; "(of) King Great Eucratides".</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="1389" decoding="async" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg/220px-BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg/330px-BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg/440px-BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BilingualCoinOfEucratides.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bilingual coin of Eucratides in the Indian standard, on the obverse <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription reads: <i>&#914;&#913;&#931;&#921;&#923;&#917;&#937;&#931; &#924;&#917;&#915;&#913;&#923;&#927;&#933; &#917;&#933;&#922;&#929;&#913;&#932;&#921;&#916;&#927;&#933;</i>-"(of) King Great Eucratides", <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kharoshthi" title="Kharoshthi">Kharoshthi</a> script on the reverse.</div></div></div>\n<blockquote><p>Eucratides led many wars with great courage, and, while weakened by them, was put under siege by Demetrius, king of the Indians. He made numerous sorties, and managed to vanquish 60,000 enemies with 300 soldiers, and thus liberated after four months, he put India under his rule. (Justin, XLI,6)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Justin_XLI,6_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Justin_XLI,6-33">[33]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Eucratides campaigned extensively in present-day northwestern India, and ruled a vast territory, as indicated by his minting of coins in many Indian mints, possibly as far as the <a href="/wiki/Jhelum_River" title="Jhelum River">Jhelum River</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_region" title="Punjab region">Punjab</a>. In the end, however, he was repulsed by the Indo-Greek king <a href="/wiki/Menander_I" title="Menander I">Menander I</a>, who managed to create a huge unified territory.\n</p><p>In a rather confused account, Justin explains that Eucratides was killed on the field by "his son and joint king", who would be his own son, either <a href="/wiki/Eucratides_II" title="Eucratides II">Eucratides II</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heliocles_I" title="Heliocles I">Heliocles I</a> (although there are speculations that it could have been his enemy\'s son <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_II_of_India" title="Demetrius II of India">Demetrius II</a>). The son drove over Eucratides\' bloodied body with his chariot and left him dismembered without a sepulchre:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>As Eucratides returned from India, he was killed on the way back by his son, whom he had associated to his rule, and who, without hiding his parricide, as if he didn\'t kill a father but an enemy, ran with his chariot over the blood of his father, and ordered the corpse to be left without a sepulture. (Justin XLI,6)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Justin_XLI,6_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Justin_XLI,6-33">[33]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defeats_by_Parthia">Defeats by Parthia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Defeats by Parthia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During or after his Indian campaigns, Eucratides was attacked and defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Mithridates_I_of_Parthia" title="Mithridates I of Parthia">Mithridates I</a>, possibly in alliance with partisans of the Euthydemids:\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monnaie_de_Bactriane,_Eucratide_I,_2_faces.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3224" data-file-width="6740" decoding="async" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg/220px-Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg/330px-Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg/440px-Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monnaie_de_Bactriane,_Eucratide_I,_2_faces.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gold 20-<a href="/wiki/Stater" title="Stater">stater</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eucratides_I" title="Eucratides I">Eucratides</a>, the largest gold coin of Antiquity. The coin weighs 169.2 grams, and has a diameter of 58 millimeters.</div></div></div>\n<blockquote><p>The Bactrians, involved in various wars, lost not only their rule but also their freedom, as, exhausted by their wars against the Sogdians, the Arachotes, the Dranges, the Arians and the Indians, they were finally crushed, as if drawn of all their blood, by an enemy weaker than them, the Parthians. (Justin, XLI,6)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Justin_XLI,6_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Justin_XLI,6-33">[33]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Following his victory, Mithridates I gained Bactria\'s territory west of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hari_River,_Afghanistan" title="Hari River, Afghanistan">Arius</a>, the regions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tapuria" title="Tapuria">Tapuria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Khorasan" title="Greater Khorasan">Traxiane</a>: "The satrapy Turiva and that of Aspionus were taken away from Eucratides by the Parthians." (Strabo XI.11.20)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strabo_11.11.2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strabo_11.11.2-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the year 141 BC, the Greco-Bactrians seem to have entered in an alliance with the Seleucid king <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_II_Nicator" title="Demetrius II Nicator">Demetrius II</a> to fight again against Parthia:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The people of the Orient welcomed his (Demetrius II\'s) arrival, partly because of the cruelty of the Arsacid king of the Parthians, partly because, used to the rule of the Macedonians, they disliked the arrogance of this new people. Thus, Demetrius, supported by the Persians, Elymes and Bactrians, routed the Parthians in numerous battles. At the end, deceived by a false peace treaty, he was taken prisoner. (Justin XXXVI, 1,1)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The 5th-century historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paulus_Orosius" title="Paulus Orosius">Orosius</a> reports that Mithridates I managed to occupy territory between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus" title="Indus">Indus</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydaspes" title="Hydaspes">Hydaspes</a> towards the end of his reign (c. 138 BC, before his kingdom was weakened by his death in 136 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Heliocles_I" title="Heliocles I">Heliocles I</a> ended up ruling what territory remained. The defeat, both in the west and the east, may have left Bactria very weakened and open to nomadic invasions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomadic_invasions">Nomadic invasions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nomadic invasions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Yuezhi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="672" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg/220px-Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg/330px-Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg/440px-Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yueh-ChihMigrations.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The migrations of the <b>Yuezhi</b> through Central Asia, from around 176 BC to AD 30.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 162BC the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> were driven west to the <a href="/wiki/Ili_River" title="Ili River">Ili River</a> valley by the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>. In 132 they were driven out of the Ili valley by the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a>. They fled southwest and two years later took northern Bactria from the Saka who had recently defeated the Greco-Bactrians.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Scythians_.28c._140_BC-.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Scythians_(c._140_BC-)">Scythians (c. 140 BC-)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Scythians (c. 140 BC-)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MenWithDragons.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="904" data-file-width="1190" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MenWithDragons.jpg/220px-MenWithDragons.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MenWithDragons.jpg/330px-MenWithDragons.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MenWithDragons.jpg/440px-MenWithDragons.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MenWithDragons.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gold artefacts of the Scythians in Bactria, at the site of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tillia_tepe" title="Tillia tepe">Tillia tepe</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Around 140 BC, eastern <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Saka</a>, or Sacaraucae of Greek sources), apparently being pushed forward by the southward migration of the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> started to invade various parts of Parthia and Bactria. Their invasion of Parthia is well documented: they attacked in the direction of the cities of <a href="/wiki/Merv" title="Merv">Merv</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hecatompolis" title="Hecatompolis">Hecatompolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecbatana" title="Ecbatana">Ecbatana</a>. They managed to defeat and kill the Parthian king <a href="/wiki/Phraates_II" title="Phraates II">Phraates II</a>, son of Mithridates I, routing the Greek mercenary troops under his command (troops he had acquired during his victory over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochus_VII" title="Antiochus VII">Antiochus VII</a>). Again in 123 BC, Phraates\'s successor, his uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artabanus_I" title="Artabanus I">Artabanus I</a>, was killed by the Scythians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Second_Yuezhi_expansion_.28120_BC-.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Yuezhi_expansion_(120_BC-)">Second Yuezhi expansion (120 BC-)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Second Yuezhi expansion (120 BC-)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Han_dynasty" title="History of the Han dynasty">Han Chinese</a> diplomat <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> visited the Yuezhi in 126 BC, trying to obtain their alliance to fight the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>, he explained that the Yuezhi were settled north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a> but also held under their sway the territory south of Oxus, which makes up the remainder of Bactria.\n</p><p>According to Zhang Qian, the Yuezhi represented a considerable force of between 100,000 and 200,000 mounted archer warriors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> with customs identical to those of the Xiongnu, which would probably have easily defeated Greco-Bactrian forces (in 208 BC when the Greco-Bactrian king <a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_I" title="Euthydemus I">Euthydemus I</a> confronted the invasion of the Seleucid king <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_III_the_Great" title="Antiochus III the Great">Antiochus III the Great</a>, he commanded 10,000 horsemen).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polybius_10.49,_Battle_of_the_Arius_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Polybius_10.49,_Battle_of_the_Arius-15">[15]</a></sup> Zhang Qian actually visited Bactria (named <a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Chinese" title="History of Chinese">Chinese</a>) in 126 BC, and portrays a country which was totally demoralized and whose political system had vanished, although its urban infrastructure remained:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Daxia (Bactria) is located over 2,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Chinese_unit)" title="Li (Chinese unit)">li</a> southwest of Dayuan, south of the Gui (Oxus) river. Its people cultivate the land and have cities and houses. Their customs are like those of Dayuan. It has no great ruler but only a number of petty chiefs ruling the various cities. The people are poor in the use of arms and afraid of battle, but they are clever at commerce. After the Great Yuezhi moved west and attacked Daxia, the entire country came under their sway. The population of the country is large, numbering some 1,000,000 or more persons. The capital is called the city of Lanshi (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bactra" title="Bactra">Bactra</a>) and has a market where all sorts of goods are bought and sold. (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Great_Historian" title="Records of the Great Historian">Records of the Great Historian</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>, quoting Zhang Qian, trans. Burton Watson)</p></blockquote>\n<p>The Yuezhi further expanded southward into Bactria around 120 BC, apparently further pushed out by invasions from the northern <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a>. It seems they also pushed Scythian tribes before them, which continued to India, where they came to be identified as <a href="/wiki/Indo-Scythians" title="Indo-Scythians">Indo-Scythians</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HelioclesCoin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="423" decoding="async" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/HelioclesCoin.jpg/220px-HelioclesCoin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/HelioclesCoin.jpg/330px-HelioclesCoin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/HelioclesCoin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HelioclesCoin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silver coin of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocles" title="Heliocles">Heliocles</a> (r. 150&#8211;125 BC), the last Greco-Bactrian king. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> inscription reads: <i>&#914;&#913;&#931;&#921;&#923;&#917;&#937;&#931; &#916;&#921;&#922;&#913;&#921;&#927;&#933; &#919;&#923;&#921;&#927;&#922;&#923;&#917;&#927;&#933;&#931;</i> &#8211; "(of) King Heliocles the Just".</div></div></div>\n<p>The invasion is also described in western Classical sources from the 1st century BC:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The best known tribes are those who deprived the Greeks of Bactriana, the Asii, Pasiani, <a href="/wiki/Tocharians" title="Tocharians">Tochari</a>, and Sacarauli, who came from the country on the other side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaxartes" title="Jaxartes">Jaxartes</a>, opposite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakas" title="Sakas">Sacae</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdians" title="Sogdians">Sogdiani</a>.<br/>(<a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, XI.8.1 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>)</p></blockquote>\n<p>Around that time the king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocles" title="Heliocles">Heliocles</a> abandoned Bactria and moved his capital to the <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a> valley, from where he ruled his Indian holdings. Having left the Bactrian territory, he is technically the last Greco-Bactrian king, although several of his descendants, moving beyond the Hindu Kush, would form the western part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Greek_kingdom" title="Indo-Greek kingdom">Indo-Greek kingdom</a>. The last of these "western" Indo-Greek kings, <a href="/wiki/Hermaeus" title="Hermaeus">Hermaeus</a>, would rule until around 70 BC, when the Yuezhi again invaded his territory in the <a href="/wiki/Paropamisadae" title="Paropamisadae">Paropamisadae</a> (while the "eastern" Indo-Greek kings would continue to rule until around AD 10 in the area of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_region" title="Punjab region">Punjab region</a>).\n</p><p>Overall, the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> remained in Bactria for more than a century. They became Hellenized to some degree, as suggested by their adoption of the Greek alphabet to write their Iranian language, and by numerous remaining coins, minted in the style of the Greco-Bactrian kings, with the text in Greek.\n</p><p>Around 12 BC the Yuezhi then moved further to northern India where they established the <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a>.\n</p><p><span id="Rulers"></span>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_forces">Military forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Military forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indian_war_elephant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="322" decoding="async" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Indian_war_elephant.jpg/170px-Indian_war_elephant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Indian_war_elephant.jpg/255px-Indian_war_elephant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/Indian_war_elephant.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indian_war_elephant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Indian War Elephant with wooden tower.</div></div></div>\n<p>Before the arrival of the Greek settlers, the armies of Bactria were overwhelmingly composed of cavalry and were well known as effective soldiers, making up large portions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achaemenid" title="Achaemenid">Achaemenid</a> cavalry contingents. 2,000 Bactrian horsemen fought at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Granicus" title="Battle of the Granicus">Granicus</a> against Alexander and 9,000 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gaugamela" title="Battle of Gaugamela">Battle of Gaugamela</a> on the left flank of Darius\' army. <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> also mentions the widespread use of chariots among the Bactrians.  After Alexander\'s conquest of Bactria, Bactrian cavalry units served in his army during the <a href="/wiki/Indian_campaign_of_Alexander_the_Great" title="Indian campaign of Alexander the Great">invasion of India</a> and after the Indian campaign, Alexander enlarged his elite <a href="/wiki/Companion_cavalry" title="Companion cavalry">companion cavalry</a> by adding Bactrians, Sogdians and other east Iranian cavalrymen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-39">[39]</a></sup> Both <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> (The Persians, v. 318) and <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Curtius_Rufus" title="Quintus Curtius Rufus">Curtius</a> mention that Bactria was able to field a force of 30,000 horse.  Most of these horsemen were lightly armed, using bows and javelins before closing with sword and spear. Herodotus describes the Persian cavalry of Mardonius at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Plataea" title="Battle of Plataea">Battle of Plataea</a> (which included Bactrians) as horse archers (<i>hippotoxotai</i>). Bactrian infantry is described by Herodotus as wearing caps in the Median style, short spears and reed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scythian" title="Scythian">Scythian</a> style bows.\n</p><p>Alexander and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucus_I" title="Seleucus I">Seleucus I</a> both settled Greeks in Bactria, while preferring to keep their Macedonian settlers farther west. Greek garrisons in the satrapy of Bactria were housed in fortresses called <i>phrouria</i> and at major cities. Military colonists were settled in the countryside and were each given an allotment of land called a <i>kleros</i>. These colonists numbered in the tens of thousands, and were trained in the fashion of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonian_army" title="Ancient Macedonian army">Macedonian army</a>. A Greek army in Bactria during the anti-Macedonian revolt of 323 numbered 23,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The army of the Greco-Bactrian kingdom was then a multi-ethnic force with Greek colonists making up large portions of the infantry as pike phalanxes, supported by light infantry units of local Bactrians and mercenary javelin-wielding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thureophoroi" title="Thureophoroi">Thureophoroi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The cavalry arm was very large for a Hellenistic army and composed  mostly of native Bactrian, <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdian</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranians" title="Indo-Iranians">Indo-Iranian</a> light horsemen. Polybius mentions 10,000 horse at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Arius" title="Battle of the Arius">Battle of the Arius</a> river in 208 BC. Greco-Bactrian armies also included units of heavily armored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataphracts" title="Cataphracts">cataphracts</a> and small elite units of <a href="/wiki/Companion_cavalry" title="Companion cavalry">companion cavalry</a>. The third arm of the Greco-Bactrian army was the Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_elephants" title="War elephants">war elephants</a>, which are depicted in some coins with a tower (<i>thorakion</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Howdah" title="Howdah">howdah</a> housing men armed with bows and javelins. This force grew as the Greco-Bactrian kingdom expanded into India and was widely depicted in Greco-Bactrian coinage. Other units in the Bactrian military included mercenaries or levies from various surrounding peoples such as the <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dahae" title="Dahae">Dahae</a>, Indians and <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthians</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_culture_in_Bactria">Greek culture in Bactria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Greek culture in Bactria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CapitalSharp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="597" decoding="async" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/CapitalSharp.jpg/220px-CapitalSharp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/CapitalSharp.jpg/330px-CapitalSharp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/CapitalSharp.jpg/440px-CapitalSharp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CapitalSharp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a> capital, found at <a href="/wiki/Ai-Khanoum" title="Ai-Khanoum">Ai-Khanoum</a>, 2nd century BC</div></div></div>\n<p>The Greco-Bactrians were known for their high level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> sophistication, and kept regular contact with both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and neighbouring <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. They were on friendly terms with India and exchanged ambassadors.\n</p><p>Their cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Ai-Khanoum" title="Ai-Khanoum">Ai-Khanoum</a> in northeastern <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> (probably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandria_on_the_Oxus" title="Alexandria on the Oxus">Alexandria on the Oxus</a>), and Bactra (modern <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>) where Hellenistic remains have been found, demonstrate a sophisticated Hellenistic urban culture. This site gives a snapshot of Greco-Bactrian culture around 145 BC, as the city was burnt to the ground around that date during nomadic invasions and never re-settled. Ai-Khanoum "has all the hallmarks of a Hellenistic city, with a Greek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater" title="Theater">theater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(ancient_Greece)" title="Gymnasium (ancient Greece)">gymnasium</a> and some Greek houses with colonnaded courtyards" (Boardman). Remains of Classical <a href="/wiki/Corinthian_order" title="Corinthian order">Corinthian</a> columns were found in excavations of the site, as well as various sculptural fragments.
