href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="/&#609;/: \'g\' in \'guy\'">&#609;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span><span title="/i/: \'y\' in \'happy\'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system in the <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation)" title="Cygnus (constellation)">Cygnus</a>, consisting of a pair of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">K-type</a> <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_star" title="Dwarf star">dwarf stars</a> that orbit each other in a period of about 659&#160;years. Of <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> 5.20 and 6.05, respectively, they can be seen with binoculars in city skies or with the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">naked eye</a> in rural areas without <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>.\n</p><p>61 Cygni first attracted the attention of astronomers when its large <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> was first demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Piazzi" title="Giuseppe Piazzi">Giuseppe Piazzi</a> in 1804. In 1838, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">Friedrich Bessel</a> measured its distance from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> at about 10.4&#160;<a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>, very close to the actual value of about 11.4&#160;light-years; this was the first distance estimate for any star other than the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, and first star to have its <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">stellar parallax</a> measured. Among all stars or stellar systems listed in the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hipparcos_Catalogue" title="Hipparcos Catalogue">Hipparcos Catalogue</a>, 61 Cygni has the seventh-highest proper motion, and the highest among all visible stars or systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highestpropermotion_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-highestpropermotion-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over the course of the twentieth century, several different astronomers reported evidence of a massive <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> orbiting one of the two stars, but recent high-precision <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a> observations have shown that all such claims were unfounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wittenmyer2006_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wittenmyer2006-17">[17]</a></sup> No planets have been confirmed in this stellar system to date.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>61 Cygni is relatively dim, so it does not appear on ancient star maps, nor is it given a name in <a href="/wiki/List_of_Arabic_star_names" title="List of Arabic star names">western</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acamar_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-acamar-18">[18]</a></sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_star_names" title="Traditional Chinese star names">Chinese systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name "61 Cygni" is part of the <a href="/wiki/Flamsteed_designation" title="Flamsteed designation">Flamsteed designation</a> assigned to stars. According to this designation scheme, devised by <a href="/wiki/John_Flamsteed" title="John Flamsteed">John Flamsteed</a> to catalog his observations, stars of a particular constellation are numbered in the order of their <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a>, not in Greek letters as the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_designation" title="Bayer designation">Bayer designation</a> does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-iau-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astriillinois_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-astriillinois-21">[21]</a></sup> The star does not appear under that name in Flamsteed\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historia_Coelestis_Britannica" title="Historia Coelestis Britannica">Historia Coelestis Britannica</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> although it has been stated by him that 61 Cygni actually corresponds to what he referred to as 85 Cygni in the 1712 edition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It has also been called "Bessel\'s Star" or "Piazzi\'s Flying Star".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsky_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsky-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">Observation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_observations">Early observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first well recorded observation of the star system using optical instruments was made by <a href="/wiki/James_Bradley" title="James Bradley">James Bradley</a> on 25 September 1753, when he noticed that it was a double star. <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> began systematic observations of 61 Cygni as part of a wider study of binary stars. His observations led to the conclusion that binary stars were separated enough that they would show different movements in <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> over the year, and hoped to use this as a way to measure the distance to the stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif"><img alt="GIF showing the proper motion of the stellar system, taken about in an interval of an year." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="1173" decoding="async" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif/220px-61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif/330px-61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif/440px-61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:61_Cygni_Proper_Motion.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>61 Cygni showing proper motion at one year intervals.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1792, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Piazzi" title="Giuseppe Piazzi">Giuseppe Piazzi</a> noticed the high proper motion when he compared his own observations of 61 Cygni with those of Bradley, made 40 years earlier. This led to considerable interest in 61 Cygni by contemporary astronomers, and its continual observation since that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup> Piazzi\'s repeated measurements led to a definitive value of its motion, which he published in 1804.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fodera-Serio_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fodera-Serio-28">[28]</a></sup> It was in this record he christened the system as the "Flying Star".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hirshfeld_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hirshfeld-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Piazzi noted that this motion meant that it was probably one of the closest stars, and suggested it would be a prime candidate for an attempt to determine its distance through parallax measurements, along with two other possibilities, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Eridani" title="Delta Eridani">Delta Eridani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mu_Cassiopeiae" title="Mu Cassiopeiae">Mu Cassiopeiae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fodera-Serio_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fodera-Serio-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parallax_measurement">Parallax measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parallax measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of astronomers soon took up the task, including attempts by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago" title="Fran&#231;ois Arago">Fran&#231;ois Arago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude-Louis_Mathieu" title="Claude-Louis Mathieu">Claude-Louis Mathieu</a> in 1812, who recorded the parallax at 500&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">milliarcseconds</a> (mas), and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Heinrich_Friedrich_Peters" title="Christian Heinrich Friedrich Peters">Christian Heinrich Friedrich Peters</a> used Arago\'s data to calculate a value of 550&#160;mas. Peters calculated a better value based on observations made by <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_von_Lindenau" title="Bernhard von Lindenau">Bernhard von Lindenau</a> at Seeburg between 1812 and 1814; he calculated it to be 470 &#177;510&#160;mas. Von Lindenau had already noted that he had seen no parallax, and as <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_von_Struve" title="Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve">Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve</a> pointed out after his own test series between 1818 and 1821, all of these numbers are more accurate than the accuracy of the instrument used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Bessel" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel">Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel</a> made a notable contribution in 1812 when he used a different method to measure distance. Assuming the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> of the two stars in the binary to be 400 years, he estimated the distance between the two this would require, and then measured the <a href="/wiki/Angular_distance" title="Angular distance">angular distance</a> between the stars. This led to a value of 460&#160;mas. He then followed this up with direct parallax measurements in a series of observations between 1815 and 1816, comparing it with six other stars. The two sets of measurements produced values of 760 and 1320&#160;mas. All of these estimates, like earlier attempts by others, retained inaccuracies greater than the measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">Joseph von Fraunhofer</a> invented a new type of <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">heliometer</a>, Bessel carried out another set of measurements using this device in 1837 and 1838 at <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="K&#246;nigsberg">K&#246;nigsberg</a>. He published his findings in 1838 with a value of 369.0&#177;19.1&#160;mas to <i>A</i> and 260.5&#177;18.8 to <i>B</i>, and estimated the center point to be at 313.6&#177;13.6. This corresponds to a distance of about 600,000 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a>, or about 10.4 light-years. This was the first direct and reliable measurement of the distance to a star other than the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bessel_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bessel-30">[30]</a></sup> His measurement was published only shortly before similar parallax measurements of <a href="/wiki/Vega" title="Vega">Vega</a> by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_von_Struve" title="Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve">Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henderson_(astronomer)" title="Thomas Henderson (astronomer)">Thomas Henderson</a> that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Bessel continued to make additional measurements at K&#246;nigsberg, publishing a total of four complete observational runs, the last in 1868. The best of these placed the center point at 360.2 &#177;12.1&#160;mas, made during observations in 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hopkins_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-hopkins-26">[26]</a></sup> This is close to the currently accepted value of 287.18&#160;mas (yielding 11.36&#160;light-years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bessel1839_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bessel1839-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Only a few years after Bessel\'s measurement, in 1842 <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Argelander" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander">Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander</a> noted that <a href="/wiki/Groombridge_1830" title="Groombridge 1830">Groombridge 1830</a> had an even larger proper motion, and 61 Cygni became the second highest known. It was later moved further down the list by <a href="/wiki/Kapteyn%27s_Star" title="Kapteyn\'s Star">Kapteyn\'s Star</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnard%27s_Star" title="Barnard\'s Star">Barnard\'s Star</a>. 61 Cygni has the seventh highest proper motion of all stellar systems listed in the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hipparcos_Catalogue" title="Hipparcos Catalogue">Hipparcos Catalogue</a>, but retains the title of highest proper motion among visible stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highestpropermotion_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-highestpropermotion-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Binary_observations">Binary observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Binary observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to the wide angular separation between 61 Cygni A and B, and the correspondingly slow orbital motion, it was initially unclear whether the two stars in the 61 Cygni system were a  <a href="/wiki/Star_system" title="Star system">gravitationally bound system</a> or simply a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_binary" title="Optical binary">juxtaposition of stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_1898_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis_1898-33">[33]</a></sup> von Struve first argued for its status as a binary in 1830, but the matter remained open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_1898_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davis_1898-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, by 1917 refined measured parallax differences demonstrated that the separation was significantly less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The binary nature of this system was clear by 1934, and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">orbital elements</a> were published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1911, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Boss" title="Benjamin Boss">Benjamin Boss</a> published data indicating that the 61 Cygni system was a member of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">comoving</a> group of stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boss_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-boss-36">[36]</a></sup> This group containing 61 Cygni was later expanded to include 26 potential members. Possible members include <a href="/wiki/Beta_Columbae" title="Beta Columbae">Beta Columbae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pi_Mensae" title="Pi Mensae">Pi Mensae</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=14_Tauri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="14 Tauri (page does not exist)">14 Tauri</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=68_Virginis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="68 Virginis (page does not exist)">68 Virginis</a>. The space velocities of this group of stars range from 105 to 114&#160;km/s relative to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Observations taken by planet search programs show that both components have strong linear trends in the <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocity</a> measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard2016_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howard2016-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_observation">Amateur observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Amateur observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An observer using 7&#215;50 <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a> can find 61 Cygni two binocular fields southeast of the bright star <a href="/wiki/Deneb" title="Deneb">Deneb</a>. The angular separation of the two stars is slightly greater than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_size" title="Angular size">angular size</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> (16&#8211;20&#8243;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> So, under ideal viewing conditions, the binary system can be resolved by a telescope with a 7&#160;mm aperture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[note 1]</a></sup> This is\nwell within the capability for aperture of typical binoculars, though to resolve the binary these need a steady mount and some 10x magnification. With a separation of 28 arc-seconds between the component stars, 10&#215; magnification would give an apparent separation of 280 arc-seconds, above the generally regarded eye resolution limit of 4 arc-minutes or 240 arc-seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skyandtelescope_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-skyandtelescope-42">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although it appears to be a single star to the naked eye, 61 Cygni is a widely separated binary star system, composed of two <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">K class (orange)</a> <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> stars, the brighter 61 Cygni A and fainter 61 Cygni B, which have <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitudes</a> of 5.2 and 6.1, respectively. Both appear to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old-disk_star" title="Old-disk star">old-disk stars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pasp81_482_553_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-pasp81_482_553-44">[43]</a></sup> with an estimated age that is older than the Sun. The system has a net <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_velocity_(astronomy)" title="Space velocity (astronomy)">space velocity</a> of 108&#160;km/s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronexus_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-astronexus-45">[44]</a></sup> relative to the Sun, which results in the high proper motion across the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa433_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa433-46">[45]</a></sup> At a distance of just over 11 light-years, it is the 15th-nearest-known star system to the Earth (not including the Sun). 61 Cygni A is the fourth-nearest star that is visible to the naked eye for mid-latitude northern observers, after <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Eridani" title="Epsilon Eridani">Epsilon Eridani</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procyon_A" title="Procyon A">Procyon A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RECONS_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-RECONS-8">[8]</a></sup> This system will make its closest approach at about 20,000 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>, when the separation from the Sun will be about 9 light-years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronexus_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-astronexus-45">[44]</a></sup> Smaller and dimmer than the Sun, 61 Cygni A has about 70 percent of a <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a>, 72 percent of its diameter and about 8.5 percent of its luminosity and 61 Cygni B has about 63 percent of a solar mass, 67 percent of its diameter, and 3.9 percent of its luminosity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-size_compare_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-size_compare-47">[46]</a></sup>\n61 Cygni A\'s long-term stability led to it being selected as an "anchor star" in the Morgan&#8211;Keenan (MK) classification system in 1943, serving as the  <a href="/wiki/K-type_main-sequence_star" title="K-type main-sequence star">K5 V</a> "anchor point" since that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baas25_1319_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-baas25_1319-48">[47]</a></sup> Starting in 1953, 61 Cygni B has been considered a <a href="/wiki/K-type_main-sequence_star" title="K-type main-sequence star">K7 V</a> standard star (Johnson &amp; Morgan 1953,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> Keenan &amp; McNeil 1989<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keenan89_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keenan89-50">[49]</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compare_61_cygni.png"><img alt="Diagram showing the size comparison between the two stars of the 61 Cygni binary system and A (bottom) and 61 Cygni B (upper right).</div></div></div>\n<p>61 Cygni A is a typical <a href="/wiki/BY_Draconis_variable" title="BY Draconis variable">BY Draconis</a> <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable star</a> designated as V1803 Cyg while 61 Cygni B is a <a href="/wiki/Flare_star" title="Flare star">flare type</a> variable star named HD 201092 with their magnitudes varying 5.21 V and 6.03, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simbad_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-simbad-51">[50]</a></sup> The two stars orbit their common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy)" title="Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)">barycenter</a> in a period of 659 years, with a mean separation of about 84&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>&#8212;84 times the separation between the Earth and the Sun. The relatively large <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> of 0.48 means that the two stars are separated by about 44&#160;AU at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a> and 124 AU at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">apoapsis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[note 2]</a></sup> The leisurely orbit of the pair has made it difficult to pin down their respective masses, and the accuracy of these values remain somewhat controversial. In the future this issue may be resolved through the use of <a href="/wiki/Asteroseismology" title="Asteroseismology">asteroseismology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa488_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-aaa488-9">[9]</a></sup> 61 Cygni A has about 11% more mass than 61 Cygni B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RECONS_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-RECONS-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The system has an <a href="/wiki/Stellar_magnetic_field" title="Stellar magnetic field">activity cycle</a> that is much more pronounced than the solar <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspot</a> cycle. This is a complex activity cycle that varies with a period of about 7.5&#177;1.7&#160;years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa406_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa406-54">[52]</a></sup> The starspot activity combined with rotation and chromospheric activity is a characteristic of a BY Draconis variable. Because of differential rotation, this star\'s surface rotation period varies by latitude from 27 to 45 days, with an average period of 35 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj657_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-apj657-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png"><img alt="Diagram showing the trajectory of 61 Cygni B relative to A as seen from Earth and from above." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png/220px-Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png/330px-Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png/440px-Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orbit_61_Cygni_arcsec.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The orbital motion of component B relative to component A as seen from Earth as well as the true appearance from face-on view. The time steps are approximately 10 years.</div></div></div>\n<p>The outflow of the stellar wind from component A produces a bubble within the local interstellar cloud. Along the direction of the star\'s motion within the Milky Way, this extends out to a distance of 30&#160;AU, or roughly the orbital distance of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> from the Sun. This is lower than the separation between the two components of 61 Cygni, and so the two most likely do not share a common atmosphere. The compactness of the <a href="/wiki/Stellar-wind_bubble" title="Stellar-wind bubble">astrosphere</a> is likely due to the low mass outflow and the relatively high velocity through the local interstellar medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[53]</a></sup>\n</p><p>61 Cygni B displays a more chaotic pattern of variability than A, with significant short-term flares. There is an 11.7-year periodicity to the overall activity cycle of B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa406_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaa406-54">[52]</a></sup> Both stars exhibit stellar flare activity, but the <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromosphere</a> of B is 25% more active than for 61 Cygni A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[54]</a></sup> As a result of differential rotation, the period of rotation varies by latitude from 32 to 47 days, with an average period of 38 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj657_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-apj657-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is some disagreement over the evolutionary age of this system. <a href="/wiki/Stellar_kinematics" title="Stellar kinematics">Kinematic</a> data gives an age estimate of about 10&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byr" title="Byr">Gyr</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa433_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-aaa433-46">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stellar_rotation" title="Stellar rotation">Gyrochronology</a>, or the age determination of a star based on its rotation and color, results in an average age of <span class="nowrap">2.0 &#177;0.2 Gyr</span>. The ages based on <a href="/wiki/Stellar_magnetic_field" title="Stellar magnetic field">chromospheric activity</a> for A and B are 2.36&#160;Gyr and 3.75&#160;Gyr, respectively. Finally the age estimates using the isochrone method, which involve fitting the stars to evolutionary models, yield upper limits of 0.44&#160;Gyr and 0.68&#160;Gyr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[55]</a></sup> However, a 2008 evolutionary model using the CESAM2k code from the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur_Observatory" title="C&#244;te d\'Azur Observatory">C&#244;te d\'Azur Observatory</a> gives an age estimate of <span class="nowrap">6.0 &#177;1.0 Gyr</span> for the pair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa488_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-aaa488-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Claims_of_a_planetary_system">Claims of a planetary system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Claims of a planetary system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On different occasions, it has been claimed that 61 Cygni might have unseen low-mass companions, planets or a <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kaj_Aage_Gunnar_Strand" title="Kaj Aage Gunnar Strand">Kaj Strand</a> of the Sproul Observatory, under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Peter_van_de_Kamp" title="Peter van de Kamp">Peter van de Kamp</a>, made the first such claim in 1942 using observations to detect tiny but systematic variations in the orbital motions of 61 Cygni A and B. These perturbations suggested that a third body of about 16 Jupiter masses must be orbiting 61 Cygni A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup> Reports of this third body served as inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Hal_Clement" title="Hal Clement">Hal Clement</a>\'s 1953 science fiction novel <i><a href="/wiki/Mission_of_Gravity" title="Mission of Gravity">Mission of Gravity</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[57]</a></sup> In 1957, van de Kamp narrowed his uncertainties, claiming that the object had a mass of eight times that of Jupiter, a calculated orbital period of 4.8&#160;years, and a semi-major axis of 2.4&#160;AU, where 1&#160;AU is the average distance from the Earth to the Sun. .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[58]</a></sup> In 1977, Soviet astronomers at the <a href="/wiki/Pulkovo_Observatory" title="Pulkovo Observatory">Pulkovo Observatory</a> near <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> suggested that the system included three planets: two giant planets with six and twelve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_masses" title="Jupiter masses">Jupiter masses</a> around 61 Cyg A,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cumming1999_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cumming1999-61">[59]</a></sup> and one giant planet with seven Jupiter masses around 61 Cygni B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[60]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1978, <a href="/wiki/Wulff-Dieter_Heintz" title="Wulff-Dieter Heintz">Wulff-Dieter Heintz</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sproul_Observatory" title="Sproul Observatory">Sproul Observatory</a> proved that these claims were spurious, as they were unable to detect any evidence of such motion down to six percent of the Sun\'s mass&#8212;equivalent to about 60 times the mass of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker1995_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker1995-64">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> for 61 Cygni A, defined as the locations where liquid water could be present on an Earth-like planet, is 0.26&#8211;0.58&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>. For 61 Cygni B, the habitable zone is 0.24&#8211;0.50 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa555_A104_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa555_A104-65">[63]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refining_planetary_boundaries">Refining planetary boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Refining planetary boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since no certain planetary object has been detected around either star so far, <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">McDonald Observatory</a> team has set limits to the presence of one or more planets around 61 Cygni A and 61 Cygni B with masses between 0.07 and 2.1&#160;Jupiter masses and average separations spanning between 0.05 and 5.2&#160;AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-search_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-search-66">[64]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of the proximity of this system to the Sun, it is a frequent target of interest for astronomers. Both stars were selected by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> as "Tier 1" targets for the proposed optical <a href="/wiki/Space_Interferometry_Mission" title="Space Interferometry Mission">Space Interferometry Mission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup> This mission is potentially capable of detecting planets with as little as 3&#160;times the mass of the Earth at an orbital distance of 2&#160;AU from the star.\n</p><p>Measurements of this system appeared to have detected an <a href="/wiki/Infrared_excess" title="Infrared excess">excess of far infrared radiation</a>, beyond what is emitted by the stars. Such an excess is sometimes associated with a <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">disk of dust</a>, but in this case it lies sufficiently close to one or both of the stars that it has not been resolved with a telescope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[66]</a></sup> A 2011 study using the <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">Keck Interferometer Nuller</a> failed to detect any <a href="/wiki/Exozodiacal_dust" title="Exozodiacal dust">exozodiacal dust</a> around 61 Cygni A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj734_1_67_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj734_1_67-69">[67]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=61_Cygni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars" title="List of nearest stars">List of nearest stars</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_planetary_systems_in_fiction#61_Cygni" title="Stars and planetary systems in fiction">61 Cygni in fiction</a> &#8212;
