id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump front.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Cirrostratus</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#716;/: secondary stress follows">&#716;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span><span title="/&#618;r/: \'irr\' in \'mirror\'">&#618;r</span><span title="/o&#650;/: \'o\' in \'code\'">o&#650;</span><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/&#593;&#720;/: \'a\' in \'father\'">&#593;&#720;</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a high-level, very thin, generally uniform <i>stratiform</i> genus-type of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>-crystals. It is difficult to detect and is capable of forming <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a> when the cloud takes the form of thin <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_nebulosus" title="Cirrostratus nebulosus">cirrostratus nebulosus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cirrostratus_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cirrostratus-1">[1]</a></sup> The cloud has a fibrous texture with no halos if it is thicker <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_fibratus" title="Cirrostratus fibratus">cirrostratus fibratus</a>. On the approach of a frontal system, the cirrostratus often begins as nebulosus and turns to fibratus. If the cirrostratus begins as fragmented fibratus it often means the front is weak. Cirrostratus is usually located above 5.5&#160;km (18,000&#160;ft). Its presence indicates a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="696" data-file-width="864" decoding="async" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg/300px-MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg/450px-MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg/600px-MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MoonHaloDonnellyMillsWA_2005_SeanMcClean.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cirrostratus at night causing a moon halo</div></div></div>\n<p>Cirrostratus clouds sometimes signal the approach of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> if they form after cirrus and spread from one area across the sky, and thus may be signs that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> might follow in the next 12 to 24&#160;hours<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or as soon as 6&#8211;8&#160;hours if the front is fast moving. If the cirrostratus is broken fibratus, it can mean that the front is weak and that stratus rather than nimbostratus will be the precipitating cloud (meaning drizzle or snow grains instead of moderate rain or snow). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_humilis" title="Cumulus humilis">Cumulus humilis</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a> clouds are often found below cirrostratus formations, due to the stable air associated with cirrostratus creating an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> and restricting convection, causing cumuliform clouds to become flattened. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contrails" title="Contrails">Contrails</a> also tend to spread out and can be visible for up to an hour in cirrostratus.\n</p><p>The phrase "milky sunshine" is often, as well as referring to <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> or light <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a>, used to refer to the milky look of the sky when cirrostratus is present.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Highcloudsymbols.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="454" decoding="async" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif/300px-Highcloudsymbols.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif/450px-Highcloudsymbols.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Highcloudsymbols.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High cloud weather map symbols.</div></div></div>\n<dl><dd><ul><li>Species: Cirrostratus <i>fibratus</i> (Cs fib) is a high fibrous sheet similar to cirrus but with less detached semi-merged filaments.  It is reported in the SYNOP code as C<sub>H</sub>8 or as C<sub>H</sub>5 or 6 (depending on the amount of sky covered) if increasing in amount.  If the high cloud covers the entire sky and takes on the form of a featureless veil, it is classified as cirrostratus of the species <i>nebulosus</i> (Cs neb)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Species_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Species-4">[4]</a></sup> and is coded C<sub>H</sub>7.</li></ul>\n<dl><dd><ul><li>Varieties:  Cirrostratus species have no opacity-based varieties as they are always translucent.  Two pattern-based varieties are sometimes seen with the species fibratus.  These are the closely spaced <i>duplicatus</i> and wavy <i>undulatus</i> types similar to those seen with cirrus fibratus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Varieties_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Varieties-5">[5]</a></sup>  Pattern-based varieties are not commonly associated with the species nebulosus due to its lack of features.</li></ul>\n<dl><dd><ul><li>Supplementary features: Cirrostratus produces no precipitation or virga, and is not accompanied by any accessory clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Features_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Features-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Genitus mother clouds: Cirrostratus fibratus <i>cirrocumulogenitus</i> sometimes appears as the latter cloud flattens and loses some of its stratocumuliform structure.  Cirrostratus fibratus <i>cumulonimbogenitus</i> may form if the cirriform top of a mature thundercloud spreads and flattens sufficiently to become a high stratiform cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo-cloud_classifications_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-cloud_classifications-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Mutatus mother clouds: Cirrostratus fibratus <i>cirromutatus</i> or <i>cirrocumulomutatus</i> are the result of a complete transformation from cirrus and cirrocumulus genus types.  Cirrostratus nebulosis <i>altostratomutatus</i> results when a high grey nebulous altostratus layer thins out into a whitish layer of featureless high cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo-cloud_classifications_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-cloud_classifications-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrostratus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">Stratus cloud</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Altostratus_cloud" title="Altostratus cloud">Altostratus cloud</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Nimbostratus_cloud" title="Nimbostratus cloud">Nimbostratus cloud</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_nebulosus" title="Cirrostratus nebulosus">Cirrostratus nebulosus</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Altostratus_undulatus_cloud" title="Altostratus undulatus cloud">Altostratus undulatus cloud</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fractus_cloud" title="Fractus cloud">Fractus cloud</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrostratus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Cirrostratus-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Cirrostratus_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">World Meteorological Organization, ed.
