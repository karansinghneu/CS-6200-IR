<small>German: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[max]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantity</a> representing the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a> past a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_(thermodynamic)" title="Boundary (thermodynamic)">boundary</a> to the local <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_et_al_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young_et_al-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graebel_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graebel-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}},}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi mathvariant="normal">M</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mi>u</mi>\n<mi>c</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo>,</mo>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}},}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\displaystyle \\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}},}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd63075d7bda376600d551f9d75fa3e7a6b95442" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:8.042ex; height:4.676ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span style="font-family: \'Georgia Pro\', Georgia, \'DejaVu Serif\', Times, \'Times New Roman\', serif;">M</span> is the Mach number,</dd>\n<dd><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">u</span> is the local flow velocity with respect to the boundaries (either internal, such as an object immersed in the flow, or external, like a channel), and</dd>\n<dd><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span> is the speed of sound in the medium.</dd></dl>\n<p>By definition, at Mach<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1, the local flow velocity <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">u</span> is equal to the speed of sound. At Mach<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>0.65, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">u</span> is 65% of the speed of sound (subsonic), and, at Mach<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1.35, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">u</span> is 35% faster than the speed of sound (supersonic).\n</p><p>The local speed of sound, and thereby the Mach number, depends on the condition of the surrounding medium, in particular the temperature. The Mach number is primarily used to determine the approximation with which a flow can be treated as an <a href="/wiki/Incompressible_flow" title="Incompressible flow">incompressible flow</a>. The medium can be a gas or a liquid. The boundary can be traveling in the medium, or it can be stationary while the medium flows along it, or they can both be moving, with different <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocities</a>: what matters is their relative velocity with respect to each other. The boundary can be the boundary of an object immersed in the medium, or of a channel such as a <a href="/wiki/Nozzle" title="Nozzle">nozzle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine#Air_intake" title="Jet engine">diffusers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnels</a> channeling the medium. As the Mach number is defined as the ratio of two speeds, it is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless_number" title="Dimensionless number">dimensionless number</a>. If <span style="font-family: \'Georgia Pro\', Georgia, \'DejaVu Serif\', Times, \'Times New Roman\', serif;">M</span>&#160;&lt;&#160;0.2&#8211;0.3 and the flow is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_flow" title="Steady flow">quasi-steady</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isothermal_flow" title="Isothermal flow">isothermal</a>, compressibility effects will be small and simplified incompressible flow equations can be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_et_al_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young_et_al-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graebel_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graebel-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Mach number is named after <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> physicist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">Ernst Mach</a>, and is a designation proposed by aeronautical engineer <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Ackeret" title="Jakob Ackeret">Jakob Ackeret</a>. As the Mach number is a dimensionless quantity rather than a unit of measure, with Mach, the number comes <i>after</i> the unit; the second Mach number is <i>Mach<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2</i> instead of <i>2<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Mach</i> (or Machs). This is somewhat reminiscent of the early modern ocean sounding unit <i>mark</i> (a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathom</a>), which was also unit-first, and may have influenced the use of the term Mach. In the decade preceding <a href="/wiki/Sound_barrier#Attempts_to_break_the_sound_barrier" title="Sound barrier">faster-than-sound human flight</a>, aeronautical engineers referred to the speed of sound as <i>Mach\'s number</i>, never <i>Mach 1</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_US_standard_atmosphere_1962.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The speed of sound (blue) depends only on the temperature variation at The speed of sound increases with height in two regions of the stratosphere and thermosphere, due to heating effects in these regions.</div></div></div>\n<p>Mach number is useful because the fluid behaves in a similar manner at a given Mach number, regardless of other variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">[4]</a></sup> As modeled in the <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a>, dry air at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>, standard temperature of 15&#160;&#176;C (59&#160;&#176;F), the speed of sound is 340.3 meters per second (1,116.5&#160;ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The speed of sound is not a constant; in a gas, it increases as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperature</a> increases, and since atmospheric temperature generally decreases with increasing altitude between sea level and 11,000 meters (36,089&#160;ft), the speed of sound also decreases. For example, the standard atmosphere model lapses temperature to &#8722;56.5&#160;&#176;C (&#8722;69.7&#160;&#176;F) at 11,000 meters (36,089&#160;ft) altitude, with a corresponding speed of sound (Mach<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>1) of 295.0 meters per second (967.8&#160;ft/s), 86.7% of the sea level value.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_Mach_regimes">Classification of Mach regimes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mach_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification of Mach regimes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While the terms <i>subsonic</i> and <i>supersonic</i>, in the purest sense, refer to speeds below and above the local speed of sound respectively, aerodynamicists often use the same terms to talk about particular ranges of Mach values. This occurs because of the presence of a <i>transonic regime</i> around M = 1 where approximations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navier-Stokes_equations" title="Navier-Stokes equations">Navier-Stokes equations</a> used for subsonic design no longer apply; the simplest explanation is that the flow locally begins to exceed M = 1 even though the freestream Mach number is below this value.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, the <i>supersonic regime</i> is usually used to talk about the set of Mach numbers for which linearised theory may be used, where for example the (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>) flow is not chemically reacting, and where heat-transfer between air and vehicle may be reasonably neglected in calculations.\n</p><p>In the following table, the <i>regimes</i> or <i>ranges of Mach values</i> are referred to, and not the <i>pure</i> meanings of the words <i>subsonic</i> and <i>supersonic</i>.\n</p><p>Generally, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> defines <i>high</i> hypersonic as any Mach number from 10 to 25, and re-entry speeds as anything greater than Mach 25. Aircraft operating in this regime include the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> and various space planes in development.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th rowspan="2">Regime\n</th>\n<th colspan="5">Speed\n</th>\n<th rowspan="2">General plane characteristics\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>(Mach)\n</th>\n<th>(knots)\n</th>\n<th>(mph)\n</th>\n<th>(km/h)\n</th>\n<th>(m/s)\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background-color: #FFFFFF;"><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">Subsonic</a>\n</th>\n<td>&lt;0.8\n</td>\n<td>&lt;530\n</td>\n<td>&lt;609\n</td>\n<td>&lt;980\n</td>\n<td>&lt;273\n</td>\n<td>Most often propeller-driven and commercial <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> aircraft with high aspect-ratio (slender) wings, and rounded features like the nose and leading edges.\n<p>The subsonic speed range is that range of speeds within all of the airflow over an aircraft is less than Mach 1.  The critical Mach number (Mcrit) is lowest free stream mach number at which airflow over any part of the aircraft first reaches Mach 1.  So the subsonic speed range includes all speeds that are less than Mcrit.\n</p>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background-color: #FFC0C0;"><a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">Transonic</a>\n</th>\n<td>0.8&#8211;1.3\n</td>\n<td>530&#8211;794\n</td>\n<td>609&#8211;914\n</td>\n<td>980&#8211;1,470\n</td>\n<td>273&#8211;409\n</td>\n<td>Transonic aircraft nearly always have <a href="/wiki/Swept_wing" title="Swept wing">swept wings</a>, causing the delay of drag-divergence, and often feature a design that adheres to the principles of the Whitcomb <a href="/wiki/Area_rule" title="Area rule">Area rule</a>.\n<p>The transonic speed range is that range of speeds within which the airflow over different parts of an aircraft is between subsonic and supersonic. The transonic period begins when first zones of M &gt; 1 flow appear around the object. In case of an airfoil (such as an aircraft\'s wing), this typically happens above the wing. Supersonic flow can decelerate back to subsonic only in a normal shock; this typically happens before the trailing edge. (Fig.1a)\n</p><p>As the speed increases, the zone of M &gt; 1 flow increases towards both leading and trailing edges. As M = 1 is reached and passed, the normal shock reaches the trailing edge and becomes a weak oblique shock: the flow decelerates over the shock, but remains supersonic. A normal shock is created ahead of the object, and the only subsonic zone in the flow field is a small area around the object\'s leading edge. (Fig.1b)\n</p>\n<table border="0">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg"><img alt="Transsonic flow over airfoil 1.svg" data-file-height="87" data-file-width="206" decoding="async" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg/300px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg/450px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg/600px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_1.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>\n</td>\n<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg"><img alt="Transsonic flow over airfoil 2.svg" data-file-height="136" data-file-width="229" decoding="async" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg/300px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg/450px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg/600px-Transsonic_flow_over_airfoil_2.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>(a)\n</td>\n<td>(b)\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Fig. 1.</b> <i>Mach number in transonic airflow around an airfoil; M &lt; 1 (a) and M &gt; 1 (b).</i>\n</p><p>When an aircraft exceeds Mach 1 (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Sound_barrier" title="Sound barrier">sound barrier</a>), a large pressure difference is created just in front of the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>. This abrupt pressure difference, called a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>, spreads backward and outward from the aircraft in a cone shape (a so-called <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom#Mach_cone" title="Sonic boom">Mach cone</a>). It is this shock wave that causes the <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a> heard as a fast moving aircraft travels overhead. A person inside the aircraft will not hear this. The higher the speed, the more narrow the cone; at just over M = 1 it is hardly a cone at all, but closer to a slightly concave plane.\n</p><p>At fully supersonic speed, the shock wave starts to take its cone shape and flow is either completely supersonic, or (in case of a blunt object), only a very small subsonic flow area remains between the object\'s nose and the shock wave it creates ahead of itself. (In the case of a sharp object, there is no air between the nose and the shock wave: the shock wave starts from the nose.)\n</p><p>As the Mach number increases, so does the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> and the Mach cone becomes increasingly narrow. As the fluid flow crosses the shock wave, its speed is reduced and temperature, pressure, and density increase. The stronger the shock, the greater the changes. At high enough Mach numbers the temperature increases so much over the shock that ionization and dissociation of gas molecules behind the shock wave begin. Such flows are called hypersonic.\n</p><p>It is clear that any object traveling at hypersonic speeds will likewise be exposed to the same extreme temperatures as the gas behind the nose shock wave, and hence choice of heat-resistant materials becomes important.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High-speed_flow_in_a_channel">High-speed flow in a channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mach_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High-speed flow in a channel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As a flow in a channel becomes supersonic, one significant change takes place. The conservation of <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">mass flow rate</a> leads one to expect that contracting the flow channel would increase the flow speed (i.e. making the channel narrower results in faster air flow) and at subsonic speeds this holds true. However, once the flow becomes supersonic, the relationship of flow area and speed is reversed: expanding the channel actually increases the speed.\n</p><p>The obvious result is that in order to accelerate a flow to supersonic, one needs a convergent-divergent nozzle, where the converging section accelerates the flow to sonic speeds, and the diverging section continues the acceleration. Such nozzles are called <a href="/wiki/De_Laval_nozzle" title="De Laval nozzle">de Laval nozzles</a> and in extreme cases they are able to reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersonic" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> speeds (Mach&#160;13 (15,926&#160;km/h; 9,896&#160;mph) at 20&#160;&#176;C).\n</p><p>An aircraft <a href="/wiki/Machmeter" title="Machmeter">Machmeter</a> or electronic flight information system (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EFIS" title="EFIS">EFIS</a>) can display Mach number derived from stagnation pressure (<a href="/wiki/Pitot_tube" title="Pitot tube">pitot tube</a>) and static pressure.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation">Calculation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mach_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calculation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Mach number at which an aircraft is flying can be calculated by\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi mathvariant="normal">M</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mi>u</mi>\n<mi>c</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\mathrm {M} ={\\frac {u}{c}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75bb73be1c50bf1f229747f63aaca0434479d517" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex;
