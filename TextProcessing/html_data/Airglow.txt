Telescope">VLT</a> platform<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Airglow</b> (also called <b>nightglow</b>) is a faint emission of light by a planetary <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. In the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>, this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_phenomenon" title="Optical phenomenon">optical phenomenon</a> causes the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a> to never be completely dark, even after the effects of <a href="/wiki/Starlight" title="Starlight">starlight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">diffused sunlight</a> from the far side are removed.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airglow in Allier, France, on the night of 13 August 2015</div></div></div>\n<p>The airglow phenomenon was first identified in 1868 by Swedish physicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anders_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders &#197;ngstr&#246;m">Anders &#197;ngstr&#246;m</a>. Since then, it has been studied in the laboratory, and various chemical reactions have been observed to emit electromagnetic energy as part of the process.  Scientists have identified some of those processes that would be present in Earth\'s atmosphere, and astronomers have verified that such emissions are present.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airglow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3857" data-file-width="2832" decoding="async" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg/220px-Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg/440px-Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iss030e015472_Edit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C/2011_W3" title="C/2011 W3">Comet Lovejoy</a> passing behind Earth\'s airglow on December 22, 2011, captured from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">ISS</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Airglow is caused by various processes in the upper <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a>, such as the recombination of atoms which were <a href="/wiki/Photoionization" title="Photoionization">photoionized</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> during the day, luminescence caused by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> striking the upper atmosphere, and <a href="/wiki/Chemiluminescence" title="Chemiluminescence">chemiluminescence</a> caused mainly by <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> reacting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> free radicals at heights of a few hundred kilometres. It is not noticeable during the daytime due to the <a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">glare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">scattering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>.\n</p><p>Even at the best ground-based observatories, airglow limits the <a href="/wiki/Photosensitivity" title="Photosensitivity">photosensitivity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescopes</a>. Partly for this reason, <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">space telescopes</a> like <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble</a> can observe much fainter objects than current ground-based telescopes at <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible wavelengths</a>.\n</p><p>Airglow at night may be bright enough for a ground observer to notice and appears generally bluish. Although airglow emission is fairly uniform across the atmosphere, it appears brightest at about 10&#176; above the observer\'s <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, since the lower one looks, the greater the <a href="/wiki/Air_mass_(astronomy)" title="Air mass (astronomy)">depth of atmosphere</a> one is looking through. Very low down, however, atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">extinction</a> reduces the apparent brightness of the airglow.\n</p><p>One airglow mechanism is when an atom of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> combines with an atom of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to form a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO). In the process, a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is emitted. This photon may have any of several different wavelengths characteristic of nitric oxide molecules. The free atoms are available for this process, because molecules of nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) and oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) are dissociated by solar energy in the upper reaches of the atmosphere and may encounter each other to form NO. Other species that can create air glow in the atmosphere are hydroxyl (OH),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meinel50a_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-meinel50a-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meinel50b_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-meinel50b-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-high10_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-high10-4">[4]</a></sup> atomic oxygen (O), sodium (Na), and lithium (Li).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sodium_layer" title="Sodium layer">Sodium layer</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sky_brightness" title="Sky brightness">sky brightness</a> is typically measured in units of <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> per square <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">arcsecond</a> of sky.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation_of_the_effects_of_airglow">Calculation of the effects of airglow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
