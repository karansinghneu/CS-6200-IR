In living beings, observation employs the <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a>. In science, observation can also involve the recording of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_(information)" title="Data (information)">data</a> via the use of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_instrument" title="Scientific instrument">scientific instruments</a>. The term may also refer to any data collected during the scientific activity. Observations can be <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_property" title="Qualitative property">qualitative</a>, that is, only the absence or presence of a property is noted, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> requires observations of <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_phenomena" title="List of natural phenomena">natural phenomena</a> to formulate and test <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypotheses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosso_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kosso-1">[1]</a></sup> It consists of the following steps:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sadava_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sadava-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shipman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shipman-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>Ask a <a href="/wiki/Question" title="Question">question</a> about a natural <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a></li>\n<li>Make observations of the phenomenon</li>\n<li>Formulate a <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> that tentatively answers the question</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">Predict</a> logical, observable <a href="/wiki/Consequent" title="Consequent">consequences</a> of the hypothesis that have not yet been investigated</li>\n<li>Test the hypothesis\' predictions by an <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Observational_study" title="Observational study">observational study</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_research" title="Field research">field study</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a></li>\n<li>Draw a <a href="/wiki/Logical_consequence" title="Logical consequence">conclusion</a> from <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> gathered in the experiment, or revise the hypothesis or form a new one and <a href="/wiki/Reproducibility" title="Reproducibility">repeat</a> the process</li>\n<li>Write a <a href="/wiki/Protocol_(science)" title="Protocol (science)">descriptive method</a> of observation and the <a href="/wiki/Result" title="Result">results</a> or conclusions reached</li>\n<li>Have peers with experience researching the same phenomenon <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">evaluate</a> the results</li></ol>\n<p>Observations play a role in the second and fifth steps of the scientific method. However, the need for <a href="/wiki/Reproducibility" title="Reproducibility">reproducibility</a> requires that observations by different observers can be comparable. Human <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sense</a> impressions are <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjective</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qualitative_data" title="Qualitative data">qualitative</a>, making them difficult to record or compare. The use of <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> developed to allow recording and comparison of observations made at different times and places, by different people. Measurement consists of using observation to compare the phenomenon being observed to a <a href="/wiki/Standard_(metrology)" title="Standard (metrology)">standard unit</a>. The standard unit can be an artifact, process, or definition which can be duplicated or shared by all observers.  In measurement the number of standard units which is equal to the observation is counted.  Measurement reduces an observation to a number which can be recorded, and two observations which result in the same number are equal within the <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">resolution</a> of the process.\n</p><p>Human senses are limited and subject to errors in perception, such as <a href="/wiki/Optical_illusion" title="Optical illusion">optical illusions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Scientific_instrument" title="Scientific instrument">Scientific instruments</a> were developed to aid human abilities of observation, such as <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">weighing scales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camera" title="Camera">cameras</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape recorders</a>, and also translate into perceptible form events that are unobservable by the senses, such as <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">indicator dyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voltmeter" title="Voltmeter">voltmeters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_camera" title="Infrared camera">infrared cameras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscopes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interferometer" title="Interferometer">interferometers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">geiger counters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">radio receivers</a>.\n</p><p>One problem encountered throughout scientific fields is that the observation may affect the process being observed, resulting in a different outcome than if the process was unobserved. This is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Observer_effect_(physics)" title="Observer effect (physics)">observer effect</a></i>. For example, it is not normally possible to check the air pressure in an automobile tire without letting out some of the air, thereby changing the pressure. However, in most fields of science it is possible to reduce the effects of observation to insignificance by using better instruments.\n</p><p>Considered as a physical process itself, all forms of observation (human or instrumental) involve <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplification</a> and are thus thermodynamically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irreversibility" title="Irreversibility">irreversible processes</a>, increasing <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paradoxes">Paradoxes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Paradoxes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In some specific fields of science the results of observation differ depending on factors which are not important in everyday observation.  These are usually illustrated with "<a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradoxes</a>" in which an event appears different when observed from two different points of view, seeming to violate "common sense".\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Relativity:</b> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_physics" title="Relativistic physics">relativistic physics</a> which deals with velocities close to the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, it is found that different observers may observe different values for the length, time rates, mass, and many other properties of an object, depending on the observer\'s velocity relative to the object.   For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Twin_paradox" title="Twin paradox">twin paradox</a> one twin goes on a trip near the speed of light and comes home younger than the twin who stayed at home.  This is not a paradox: time passes at a slower rate when measured from a frame moving with respect to the object.  In relativistic physics, an observation must always be qualified by specifying the state of motion of the observer, its <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">reference frame</a>.</li>\n<li><b>Quantum mechanics:</b> In <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, which deals with the behavior of very small objects, it is not possible to <a href="/wiki/Observer_(quantum_physics)" title="Observer (quantum physics)">observe a system</a> without changing the system, and the "observer" must be considered part of the <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> being observed. In isolation, quantum objects are represented by a <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> which often exists in a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">superposition</a> or mixture of different <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">states</a>. However, when an observation is made to determine the actual location or state of the object, it always finds the object in a single state, not a "mixture". The interaction of the observation process appears to "<a href="/wiki/Wave_function_collapse" title="Wave function collapse">collapse</a>" the wave function into a single state. So any interaction between an isolated wave function and the external world that results in this wave function collapse is called an <i>observation</i> or <i>measurement</i>, whether or not it is part of a deliberate observation process.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biases">Biases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The human senses do not function like a video <a href="/wiki/Camcorder" title="Camcorder">camcorder</a>, impartially recording all observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw-4">[4]</a></sup>  Human perception occurs by a complex, unconscious process of <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">abstraction</a>, in which certain details of the incoming sense data are noticed and remembered, and the rest forgotten.    What is kept and what is thrown away depends on an internal model or representation of the world, called by psychologists a <i><a href="/wiki/Schema_(psychology)" title="Schema (psychology)">schema</a></i>, that is built up over our entire lives.  The data is fitted into this schema.  Later when events are remembered, memory gaps may even be filled by "plausible" data the mind makes up to fit the model; this is called <i><a href="/wiki/Reconstructive_memory" title="Reconstructive memory">reconstructive memory</a></i>. How much attention the various perceived data are given depends on an internal value system, which judges how important it is to the individual. Thus two people can view the same event and come away with entirely different perceptions of it, even disagreeing about simple facts.  This is why <a href="/wiki/Eyewitness_testimony" title="Eyewitness testimony">eyewitness testimony</a> is notoriously unreliable.\n</p><p>Several of the more important ways observations can be affected by human psychology are given below.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confirmation_bias">Confirmation bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Confirmation bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a></div>\n<p>Human observations are biased toward confirming the observer\'s conscious and unconscious expectations and view of the world; we "<i>see what we expect to see</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shermer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shermer-5">[5]</a></sup> In psychology, this is called <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shermer_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shermer-5">[5]</a></sup>   Since the object of scientific research is the discovery of new phenomena, this bias can and has caused new discoveries to be overlooked; one example is the discovery of <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a>. It can also result in erroneous scientific support for widely held cultural myths, on the other hand, as in the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_racism" title="Scientific racism">scientific racism</a> that supported ideas of racial superiority in the early 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-6">[6]</a></sup> Correct scientific technique emphasizes careful recording of observations, separating experimental observations from the conclusions drawn from them, and techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blind_experiment" title="Blind experiment">blind</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_blind_experiment" title="Double blind experiment">double blind experiments</a>, to minimize observational bias.\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22Cargo_cult.22_science"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'"Cargo_cult"_science\'>"Cargo cult" science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title=\'Edit section: "Cargo cult" science\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cargo_cult_science" title="Cargo cult science">Cargo cult science</a></div>\n<p>Another bias, which has become more prevalent with the advent of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_science" title="Big science">big science</a>" and the large rewards of new discoveries, is bias in favor of the researcher\'s desired hypothesis or outcome; we "<i>see what we want to see</i>". Called <a href="/wiki/Pathological_science" title="Pathological science">pathological science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cargo_cult_science" title="Cargo cult science">cargo cult science</a>, this is different from deliberate falsification of results, and can happen to good-faith researchers.  Researchers with a great incentive or desire for a given outcome can misinterpret or misjudge results, or even persuade themselves they have seen something they haven\'t.  Possible examples of mistaken discoveries caused by this bias are <a href="/wiki/Mars#Martian_\'canals\'" title="Mars">Martian "canals"</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N_rays" title="N rays">N rays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polywater" title="Polywater">polywater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cold_fusion" title="Cold fusion">cold fusion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">perpetual motion</a> machines. Recent decades have seen scientific scandals caused by researchers playing "fast and loose" with observational methods in order to get their pet theories published. This type of bias is rampant in <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudoscience</a>, where correct scientific techniques are not followed. The main defense against this bias, besides correct research techniques, is <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a> and repetition of the experiment, or the observation, by other researchers with no incentive to bias. For example, an emerging practice in the competitive field of <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> is to require the physical results of experiments, such as serums and <a href="/wiki/Tissue_culture" title="Tissue culture">tissue cultures</a>, be made available to competing laboratories for independent testing.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing_bias">Processing bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Processing bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Modern scientific instruments can extensively process "observations" before they are presented to the human senses, and particularly with computerized instruments, there is sometimes a question as to where in the data processing chain "observing" ends and "drawing conclusions" begins. This has recently become an issue with <a href="/wiki/Digital_image_processing" title="Digital image processing">digitally enhanced</a> images published as experimental data in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_paper" title="Scientific paper">papers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journals</a>. The images are enhanced to bring out features that the researcher wants to emphasize, but this also has the effect of supporting the researcher\'s conclusions. This is a form of bias that is difficult to quantify. Some <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journals</a> have begun to set detailed standards for what types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_processing" title="Image processing">image processing</a> are allowed in research results. Computerized instruments often keep a copy of the "raw data" from sensors before processing, which is the ultimate defense against processing bias, and similarly scientific standards require preservation of the original unenhanced "raw" versions of images used as research data.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_bias">Observational bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Observational bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An observational bias occurs when researchers only look where they think they will find positive results, or where it is easy to record observations. This is called the "<a href="/wiki/Streetlight_effect" title="Streetlight effect">streetlight effect</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Observe always that everything is the result of a change, and get used to thinking that there is nothing <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a> loves so well as to change existing forms and to make new ones like them."</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Meditations. iv. 36. &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Observation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophical" title="Philosophical">philosophical</a> terms is the process of filtering <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sensory</a> <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> through the thought process. Input is received via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">hearing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">sight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Haptic_perception" title="Haptic perception">touch</a> and then analyzed through either rational or irrational thought.\n</p><p><br/>\nFor example, let us suppose that an observer <i>sees</i> a parent beat their child; and consequently <i>observes</i> that such an action is either good or bad.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="What is the source of the distinction">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Deductions about what behaviors are good or bad may be based on preferences about building relationships, or study of the consequences resulting from the observed behavior. With the passage of time, impressions stored in the consciousness about many, together with the resulting relationships and consequences, permit the individual to build a construct about the moral implications of behavior.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
