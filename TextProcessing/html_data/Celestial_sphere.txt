For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Celestial_(disambiguation)" title="Celestial (disambiguation)">Celestial (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_within_celestial_sphere.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="298" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Earth_within_celestial_sphere.gif/220px-Earth_within_celestial_sphere.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/Earth_within_celestial_sphere.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_within_celestial_sphere.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">Earth rotating</a> within a relatively small-radius <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">geocentric</a> <b>celestial sphere</b>. Shown here are stars (white), the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> (red, the circumscription of the Sun\'s apparent annual track), and the lines of <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> and circles of <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> (cyan) of the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">equatorial coordinate system</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, the <b>celestial sphere</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">abstract</a> <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> that has an arbitrarily large <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a> and is <a href="/wiki/Concentric_objects" title="Concentric objects">concentric</a> to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. All objects in the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> can be conceived as being <a href="/wiki/Projective_geometry" title="Projective geometry">projected</a> upon the inner surface of the celestial sphere, which may be <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">centered on Earth</a> or the observer. If centered on the observer, half of the sphere would resemble a <a href="/wiki/Sphere#Hemisphere" title="Sphere">hemispherical</a> <a href="/wiki/Projection_screen" title="Projection screen">screen</a> over the observing location.\n</p><p>The celestial sphere is a practical tool for <a href="/wiki/Spherical_astronomy" title="Spherical astronomy">spherical astronomy</a>, allowing <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a> to <a href="/wiki/Celestial_coordinate_system" title="Celestial coordinate system">specify</a> the <a href="/wiki/Apparent_place" title="Apparent place">apparent positions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">objects</a> in the sky if their distances are unknown or irrelevant. In the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">equatorial coordinate system</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a> divides the celestial sphere into two halves: the <a href="/wiki/Northern_celestial_hemisphere" title="Northern celestial hemisphere">northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_celestial_hemisphere" title="Southern celestial hemisphere">southern celestial hemispheres</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celestial_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Celestial_Sphere,_18th_century.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1238" decoding="async" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Celestial_Sphere%2C_18th_century.jpg/220px-Celestial_Sphere%2C_18th_century.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Celestial_Sphere%2C_18th_century.jpg/330px-Celestial_Sphere%2C_18th_century.jpg 1.5x, class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Celestial_Sphere,_18th_century.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Celestial Sphere, 18th century. <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Because <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a> are at such remote distances, casual observation of the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> offers no information on their actual distances. All celestial objects seem <a href="/wiki/Equidistant" title="Equidistant">equally far away</a>, as if <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">fixed</a> onto the inside of a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> with a large but unknown radius,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> \nwhich <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_motion" title="Diurnal motion">appears to rotate</a> westward overhead; meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> underfoot seems to remain still. For purposes of <a href="/wiki/Spherical_astronomy" title="Spherical astronomy">spherical astronomy</a>, which is concerned only with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry)" title="Direction (geometry)">directions</a> to celestial objects, it makes no difference if this is actually the case or if it is Earth that is <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">rotating</a> while the celestial sphere is stationary.\n</p><p>The celestial sphere can be considered to be <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a>. This means any <a href="/wiki/Point_(geometry)" title="Point (geometry)">point</a> within it, including that occupied by the observer, can be considered the <a href="/wiki/Centre_(geometry)" title="Centre (geometry)">center</a>. It also means that all <a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)" title="Parallel (geometry)">parallel</a> <a href="/wiki/Line_(geometry)" title="Line (geometry)">lines</a>, be they <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">millimetres</a> apart or across the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> from each other, will seem to intersect the sphere at a single point, analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_point" title="Vanishing point">vanishing point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">graphical perspective</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> \nAll parallel <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">planes</a> will seem to intersect the sphere in a coincident <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> \n(a "vanishing circle").\n</p><p>Conversely, observers looking toward the same point on an infinite-radius celestial sphere will be looking along parallel lines, and observers looking toward the same great circle, along parallel planes. On an infinite-radius celestial sphere, all observers see the same things in the same direction.\n</p><p>For some objects, this is over-simplified. Objects which are relatively near to the observer (for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>) will seem to change position against the distant celestial sphere if the observer moves far enough, say, from one side of planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to the other. This effect, known as <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a>, can be represented as a small offset from a mean position. The celestial sphere can be considered to be centered at the <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">Earth\'s center</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">Sun\'s center</a>, or any other convenient location, and offsets from positions referred to these centers can be calculated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In this way, <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a> can predict <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geocentric_coordinates" title="Geocentric coordinates">geocentric</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism#Modern_use_of_geocentric_and_heliocentric" title="Heliocentrism">heliocentric</a> positions of objects on the celestial sphere, without the need to calculate the individual <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> of any particular observer, and the utility of the celestial sphere is maintained. Individual observers can work out their own small offsets from the mean positions, if necessary. In many cases in astronomy, the offsets are insignificant.\n</p><p>The celestial sphere can thus be thought of as a kind of astronomical <a href="/wiki/Shorthand" title="Shorthand">shorthand</a>, and is applied very frequently by astronomers. For instance, the <i><a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac" title="Astronomical Almanac">Astronomical Almanac</a></i> for 2010 lists the apparent geocentric position of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> on January 1 at 00:00:00.00 <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">Terrestrial Time</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">equatorial coordinates</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> 6<sup>h</sup> 57<sup>m</sup> 48.86<sup>s</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> +23&#176; 30\' 05.5". Implied in this position is that it is as projected onto the celestial sphere; any observer at any location looking in that direction would see the "geocentric Moon" in the same place against the stars. For many rough uses (e.g. calculating an approximate phase of the Moon), this position, as seen from the Earth\'s center, is adequate.\n</p><p>For applications requiring precision (e.g. calculating the shadow path of an <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">eclipse</a>), the <i>Almanac</i> gives formulae and methods for calculating the <i>topocentric</i> coordinates, that is, as seen from a particular place on the Earth\'s surface, based on the geocentric position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This greatly abbreviates the amount of detail necessary in such almanacs, as each observer can handle their own specific circumstances.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celestial_coordinate_systems">Celestial coordinate systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celestial_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celestial coordinate systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>These concepts are important for understanding <a href="/wiki/Celestial_coordinate_system" title="Celestial coordinate system">celestial coordinate systems</a>, frameworks for measuring the positions of <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">objects in the sky</a>. Certain reference lines and <a href="/wiki/Plane_of_reference" title="Plane of reference">planes</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, when projected onto the celestial sphere, form the bases of the reference systems. These include the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">axis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth\'s orbit">orbit</a>. At their intersections with the celestial sphere, these form the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>, the north and south <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">celestial poles</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As the celestial sphere is considered arbitrary or infinite in radius, all observers see the celestial equator, celestial poles, and ecliptic at the same place against the <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">background stars</a>.\n</p><p>From these bases, directions toward objects in the sky can be quantified by constructing celestial coordinate systems. Similar to geographic <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">equatorial coordinate system</a> specifies positions relative to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">celestial poles</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic coordinate system</a> specifies positions relative to the ecliptic (Earth\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_plane" title="Orbital plane">orbit</a>), using <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system#Spherical_coordinates" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic longitude and latitude</a>. Besides the equatorial and ecliptic systems, some other celestial coordinate systems, like the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_coordinate_system" title="Galactic coordinate system">galactic coordinate system</a>, are more appropriate for particular purposes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celestial_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_astronomy" title="History of astronomy">History of astronomy</a></div>\n<p>The ancients assumed the literal truth of stars attached to a celestial sphere, revolving about the Earth in one day, and a fixed Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> \nThe <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus#Eudoxan_planetary_models" title="Eudoxus of Cnidus">Eudoxan planetary model</a>, on which the <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotelian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemaic</a> models were based, was the first geometric explanation for the "wandering" of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_planets" title="Classical planets">classical planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The outer most of these <a href="/wiki/Celestial_spheres" title="Celestial spheres">"crystal spheres"</a> was thought to carry the <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">fixed stars</a>. Eudoxus used 27 concentric spherical solids to answer <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato\'s</a> challenge: "By the assumption of what uniform and orderly motions can the apparent motions of the planets be accounted for?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_globe">Star globe</span><span Earth. The oldest surviving example of such an artifact is the globe of the <a href="/wiki/Farnese_Atlas" title="Farnese Atlas">Farnese Atlas</a> sculpture, a 2nd-century copy of an older (<a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, ca. 120 BCE) work.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bodies_other_than_Earth">Bodies other than Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celestial_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bodies other than Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/International_Celestial_Reference_System" title="International Celestial Reference System">International Celestial Reference System</a></div>\n<p>Observers on other worlds would, of course, see objects in that sky under much the same conditions &#8211; as if projected onto a dome. Coordinate systems based on the sky of that world could be constructed.
