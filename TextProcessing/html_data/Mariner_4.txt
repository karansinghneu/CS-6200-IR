style="text-align:center">&#160;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Mariner 4</b> (together with <a href="/wiki/Mariner_3" title="Mariner 3">Mariner 3</a> known as <b>Mariner&#8211;Mars 1964</b>) was the <a href="/wiki/Mariner_program" title="Mariner program">fourth in a series of spacecraft</a> intended for planetary exploration in a flyby mode. It was designed to conduct closeup scientific observations of Mars and to transmit these observations to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Launched on November 28, 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4-2">[2]</a></sup> Mariner 4 performed the first successful <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">flyby</a> of the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, returning the first close-up pictures of the Martian surface. It captured the first images of another planet ever returned from <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">deep space</a>; their depiction of a cratered, seemingly dead world, largely changed the scientific community\'s view of <a href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars" title="Life on Mars">life on Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Momsen1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Momsen1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Momsen2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Momsen2-4">[4]</a></sup>  Other mission objectives were to perform field and particle measurements in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Interplanetary" title="Outer space">interplanetary space</a> in the vicinity of Mars and to provide experience in and knowledge of the engineering capabilities for interplanetary flights of long duration. On December 21, 1967 communications with Mariner 4 were terminated.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft and subsystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Mariner 4 <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> consisted of an octagonal <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> frame, 127&#160;cm across a diagonal and 45.7&#160;cm high. Four <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> were attached to the top of the frame with an end-to-end span of 6.88 meters, including <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">solar pressure vanes</a> which extended from the ends. A 116.8&#160;cm diameter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-gain_antenna" title="High-gain antenna">high-gain</a> <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antenna</a> was mounted at the top of the frame as well. An omnidirectional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-gain_antenna" title="Low-gain antenna">low-gain antenna</a> was mounted on a seven-foot, four inch (223.5&#160;cm) tall mast next to the high-gain antenna. The overall height of the spacecraft was 2.89 meters. The octagonal frame housed the electronic equipment, cabling, midcourse propulsion system, and attitude control gas supplies and regulators.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The scientific instruments included:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650018349_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650018349-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reiff_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reiff-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>, mounted on the waveguide leading to the omnidirectional antenna, to measure the magnitude and other characteristics of the interplanetary and planetary magnetic fields.</li>\n<li>An <a href="/wiki/Ionization_chamber" title="Ionization chamber">ionization chamber</a>/<a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a>, mounted on the waveguide leading to the omnidirectional antenna nearer the body of the spacecraft, to measure the charged-particle intensity and distribution in interplanetary space and in the vicinity of Mars.</li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">trapped radiation detector</a>, mounted on the body with counter-axes pointing 70&#176; and 135&#176; from the solar direction, to measure the intensity and direction of low-energy particles.</li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> telescope, mounted inside the body pointing in anti-solar direction, to measure the direction and energy spectrum of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>.</li>\n<li>A solar <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> probe, mounted on the body pointing 10&#176; from the solar direction, to measure the very low energy charged particle flux from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.</li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> detector, mounted on the body with microphone plate approximately perpendicular to the plane of <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, to measure the <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>, distribution, density, and direction of cosmic dust.</li>\n<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_camera" title="Television camera">television camera</a>, mounted on a scan platform at the bottom center of the spacecraft, to obtain closeup pictures of the surface of Mars. This subsystem consisted of 4 parts, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassegrain_telescope" title="Cassegrain telescope">Cassegrain telescope</a> with a 1.05&#176; by 1.05&#176; field of view, a shutter and red/green filter assembly with 0.08s and 0.20s exposure times, a slow scan <a href="/wiki/Video_camera_tube#Vidicon" title="Video camera tube">vidicon tube</a> which translated the optical image into an electrical video signal, and the electronic systems required to convert the analogue signal into a digital bitstream for transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_-_Mars_TV_Camera_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC_-_Mars_TV_Camera-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1321" data-file-width="1702" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg/220px-Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg/330px-Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg/440px-Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_4_Launch_Preparations.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mariner 4 is prepared for a weight test on November 1, 1963</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> for the instruments and the radio transmitter of Mariner 4 was supplied by 28,224 <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> contained in the four 176 x 90&#160;cm solar panels, which could provide 310 watts at the distance of Mars. A rechargeable 1200 W&#183;h <a href="/wiki/Silver-oxide_battery" title="Silver-oxide battery">silver-zinc battery</a> was also used for maneuvers and backup. <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">Monopropellant</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> was used for <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion</a>, via a four-jet vane vector control motor, with 222-<a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> (50&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pound_(force)" title="Pound (force)">lbf</a>) thrust, installed on one of the sides of the octagonal structure. The space probe\'s <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a> was provided by 12 cold <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> gas jets mounted on the ends of the solar panels and three <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyros</a>. Solar pressure vanes, each with an area of 0.65 square meter (seven&#160;ft&#178;), were attached to the tips of the solar panels. Positional information was provided by four Sun sensors, and a sensor for either the Earth,  Mars, or the star <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a>, depending on the time in its spaceflight. Mariner 4 was the first space probe that needed a star for a navigational reference object, since earlier missions, which remained near either the Earth, the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, or the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, had sighted onto either the bright face of the home planet or the brightly lit target. During this flight, both the Earth and Mars would be too dim to lock onto. Another bright source at a wide angle away from the Sun was needed and Canopus filled this requirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650018349_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650018349-5">[5]</a></sup> Subsequently, Canopus was used as a reference point in many following missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goss_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goss-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The telecommunications equipment on Mariner 4 consisted of dual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-band" title="S-band">S-band</a> transmitters (with either a seven-watt <a href="/wiki/Triode" title="Triode">triode</a> cavity amplifier or a ten watt <a href="/wiki/Traveling-wave_tube" title="Traveling-wave tube">traveling-wave tube</a> amplifier) and a single <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> receiver which together could send and receive data via the low- and high-gain antennas at 8&#8531; or 33&#8531; bits per second. Data could also be stored onto a magnetic <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape recorder</a> with a capacity of 5.24 million bits for later transmission. All electronic operations were controlled by a command subsystem which could process any of 29 direct command words or three quantitative word commands for mid-course maneuvers. The central computer and sequencer operated stored time-sequence commands using a 38.4&#160;kHz synchronization frequency as a time reference. Temperature control was achieved through the use of adjustable louvers mounted on six of the electronics assemblies, plus multilayer insulating blankets, polished aluminum shields, and surface treatments. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_4_launch_2.jpg"><img alt="" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_4_launch_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch of Mariner 4</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After Mariner 3 was a total loss due to failure of the payload shroud to jettison, JPL engineers suggested that there had been a malfunction caused during separation of the metal fairing exterior from the fiberglass inner lining due to pressure differences between the inner and outer part of the shroud and that this could have caused the spring-loaded separation mechanism to become tangled and fail to detach properly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Testing at JPL confirmed this failure mode and an effort was made to develop a new, all-metal fairing. The downside of this was that the new fairing would be significantly heavier and reduce the Atlas-Agena\'s lift capacity. Convair and Lockheed-Martin had to make several performance enhancements to the booster to wring more power out of it. Despite fears that the work could not be completed before the 1964 Mars window closed, the new shroud was ready by November.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><br/>\nAfter launch from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Launch_Complex_12" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Launch Complex 12">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Launch Complex 12</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the protective shroud covering Mariner 4 was jettisoned and the <a href="/wiki/RM-81_Agena" title="RM-81 Agena">Agena-D</a>/Mariner 4 combination separated from the <a href="/wiki/SM-65D_Atlas" title="SM-65D Atlas">Atlas-D booster</a> at 14:27:23 UTC on November 28, 1964. The Agena\'s first burn took place from 14:28:14 to 14:30:38. The initial burn put the spacecraft into an Earth parking orbit and the second burn from 15:02:53 to 15:04:28 injected the craft into a Mars transfer orbit. Mariner 4 separated from the Agena at 15:07:09 and began cruise mode operations. The solar panels deployed and the scan platform was unlatched at 15:15:00. Sun acquisition occurred 16 minutes later.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lock_on_Canopus">Lock on Canopus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lock on Canopus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After Sun acquisition, the Canopus star tracker went searching for <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a>. The star tracker was set to respond to any object more than one-eighth as, and less than eight times as bright as Canopus. Including Canopus, there were seven such objects visible to the sensor. It took more than a day of "star-hopping" to find Canopus, as the sensor locked on to other stars instead:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650018349_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650018349-5">[5]</a></sup> a stray light pattern from the near Earth, <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Cephei" title="Alpha Cephei">Alderamin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulus" title="Regulus">Regulus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Puppis" title="Zeta Puppis">Naos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Velorum" title="Gamma Velorum">Gamma Velorum</a> were acquired before Canopus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Momsen1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Momsen1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goss_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goss-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A consistent problem that plagued the spacecraft during the early portion of its mission was that roll error signal transients would occur frequently and on occasion would cause loss of the Canopus star lock. The first attempt at a midcourse maneuver was aborted by a loss of lock shortly after the gyros began spinup. Canopus lock was lost six times within a period of less than three weeks after launch and each time a sequence of radio commands would be required to reacquire the star. After a study of the problem, the investigators concluded that the behavior was due to small dust particles that were being released from the spacecraft by some means and were drifting through the star sensor field-of-view. Sunlight scattered from the particles then appeared as illumination equivalent to that from a bright star. This would cause a roll error transient as the object passed through the field-of-view while the sensor was locked onto Canopus. When the object was bright enough that it exceeded the high gate limits at eight times the Canopus intensity, the spacecraft would automatically disacquire Canopus and initiate a roll search for a new star. Finally, a radio command was sent on December 17, 1964, that removed the high gate limit. There was no further loss of Canopus lock, although roll transients occurred 38 more times before encounter with Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650018349_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650018349-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goss_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goss-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midcourse_maneuver">Midcourse maneuver</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Midcourse maneuver">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 7&#189; month flight of Mariner 4 involved one midcourse maneuver on December 5, 1964. The maneuver was initially scheduled for December 4, but due to a loss of lock with Canopus, it was postponed. The maneuver was successfully completed on December 5; it consisted of a negative pitch turn of 39.16 degrees, a positive roll turn of 156.08 degrees, and a thrusting time of 20.07 seconds. The turns aimed the motor of the spacecraft back in the general direction of Earth, as the motor was initially pointed along the direction of flight. Both the pitch and roll changes were completed with better than 1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accuracy" title="Accuracy">accuracy</a>, the velocity change with about 2.5% accuracy. After the maneuver, Mariner 4 was on course for Mars as planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_19650018349_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_19650018349-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_flyby">Mars flyby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mars flyby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pickering-Johnson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="294" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Pickering-Johnson.jpg/300px-Pickering-Johnson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Pickering-Johnson.jpg/450px-Pickering-Johnson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Pickering-Johnson.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pickering-Johnson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_N._James" title="Jack N. James">Jack N. James</a> (center), JPL\'s Mariner 4 Project Manager, with a group in the White House presenting the spacecraft\'s famous picture Number 11 of Mars to US President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> (center right) in July 1965.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Mariner&#160;4 spacecraft flew by Mars on July 14 and July 15, 1965. Its closest approach was 9,846&#160;km from the Martian surface at 01:00:57 UT July 15, 1965 (8:00:57&#160;p.m. EST July 14), its distance to Earth was 216&#160;Gm, its speed was 7&#160;km/s relative to Mars, 1.7&#160;km/s relative to Earth.\n</p><p>Planetary science mode was turned on at 15:41:49 UT on July 14. The camera sequence started at 00:18:36 UT on July 15 (7:18:49&#160;p.m. EST on July 14) and 21 pictures using alternate red and green filters, plus 21 lines of a 22nd picture were taken. The images covered a discontinuous swath of Mars starting near 40&#176; N, 170&#176; E, down to about 35&#176; S, 200&#176; E, and then across to the terminator at 50&#176; S, 255&#176; E, representing about 1% of the planet\'s surface. The images taken during the flyby were stored in the on-board tape recorder. At 02:19:11 UT Mariner 4 passed behind Mars as seen from Earth and the radio signal ceased. The signal was reacquired at 03:13:04 UT when the spacecraft reappeared. Cruise mode was then re-established. Transmission of the taped images to Earth began about 8.5 hours after signal reacquisition and continued until August 3. All images were transmitted twice to ensure no data was missing or corrupt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4-2">[2]</a></sup> Each individual photograph took approximately six hours to be transmitted back to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196604-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The spacecraft performed all programmed activities successfully and returned useful data from launch until 22:05:07 UT on October 1, 1965, when the long distance to Earth (309.2 million km) and the imprecise antenna orientation led to a temporary loss of communication with the spacecraft until 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC_-_Mariner_4-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_image_hand_drawn">First image hand drawn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mariner_4_Tape_Recorder.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mariner 4 Tape recorder</div></div></div>\n<p>The on-board tape recorder used on Mariner 4 was a spare, not originally intended for the Mariner 4 flight.  Between the failure of Mariner 3, the fact that the Mariner 4 recorder was a spare, and some error readings suggesting an issue with the tape-recorder, it was determined that the team would test the camera function definitively.  This eventually led to the first digital image being hand-drawn.  While waiting for the image data to be computer processed, the team used a <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastel</a> set from an art supply store to hand-color (paint-by-numbers style) a numerical printout of the raw pixels. The resulting image provided early verification that the camera was functioning. The hand-drawn image compared favorably with the processed image when it became available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DanGoods_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-DanGoods-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Micrometeoroid_hits_and_end_of_communications">Micrometeoroid hits and end of communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Micrometeoroid hits and end of communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Data_acquisition" title="Data acquisition">Data acquisition</a> resumed in late 1967. The <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> detector registered 17 hits in a 15-minute span on September 15, part of an apparent <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">micrometeoroid</a> shower that temporarily changed the spacecraft attitude and probably slightly damaged its thermal shield. Later it was speculated that Mariner 4 passed through debris of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D/1895_Q1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="D/1895 Q1 (page does not exist)">D/1895 Q1</a> (D/Swift), and even made a flyby of that comet\'s possibly shattered nucleus at 20 million kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phillips2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips2-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On December 7 the gas supply in the attitude control system was exhausted, and between December 10 and 11, a total of 83 micrometeoroid hits were recorded which caused perturbation of the spacecraft\'s attitude and degradation of the signal strength. On December 21, 1967, communications with Mariner 4 were terminated. The spacecraft is now derelict in an exterior <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmer_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filmer-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pyle2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariner_4&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The total data returned by the mission was 5.2 million bits (about 634 <a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kB</a>). All instruments operated successfully with the exception of a part of the ionization chamber, namely the <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93M%C3%BCller_tube" title="Geiger&#8211;M&#252;ller tube">Geiger&#8211;M&#252;ller tube</a>, which failed in February 1965. In addition, the plasma probe had its performance degraded by a <a href="/wiki/Resistor" title="Resistor">resistor</a> failure on December 8, 1964, but experimenters were able to recalibrate the instrument and still interpret the data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-release_1965_0319_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-release_1965_0319-17">[17]</a></sup> The images returned showed a <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>-like cratered terrain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leighton_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leighton-18">[18]</a></sup> which scientists did not expect, although amateur astronomer Donald Cyr had predicted craters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ley196604-11">[11]</a></sup> Later missions showed that the craters were not typical for Mars, but only for the more ancient region imaged by Mariner 4. A surface <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 4.1 to 7.0 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">millibars</a> (410 to 700 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a>) and daytime temperatures of &#8722;100 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> were estimated. No <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O\'Gallagher_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-O\'Gallagher-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-20">[20]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Martian radiation belts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen-21">[21]</a></sup> or, again surprisingly, surface water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ley196604-11">[11]</a></sup> was detected.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bruce_C._Murray" title="Bruce C. Murray">Bruce C.
