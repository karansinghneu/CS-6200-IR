title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, ontology often deals with questions concerning what <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entities" title="Entities">entities</a> exist or may be said to exist and how such entities may be grouped, related within a <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>, and subdivided according to similarities and differences.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compound</a> word <i>ontology</i> ("study of being") combines <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/onto-" title="wikt:onto-">onto</a>-</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Gr.</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%A4%CE%BD" title="wikt:&#8036;&#957;">&#8004;&#957;</a>, <i>on</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">gen.</a> &#8004;&#957;&#964;&#959;&#962;, <i>ontos</i>, "being; that which is") and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia" title="wikt:-logia">-logia</a></i> (Gr. <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%CE%BB%CE%BF%CE%B3%CE%AF%CE%B1" title="wikt:-&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;">-&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;</a>, "logical discourse"). See <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">classical compounds</a> for this type of word formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineEtDict_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtDict-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSJ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is Greek, the oldest extant record of the word itself, the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> form <i>ontologia</i>, appeared in 1606 in the work <i>Ogdoas Scholastica</i> by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Lorhard" title="Jacob Lorhard">Jacob Lorhard</a> (<i>Lorhardus</i>) and in 1613 in the <i>Lexicon philosophicum</i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolf_G%C3%B6ckel" title="Rudolf G&#246;ckel">Rudolf G&#246;ckel</a> (<i>Goclenius</i>).\n</p><p>The first occurrence in English of <i>ontology</i> as recorded by the <i>OED</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, online edition, 2008) came in a work by Gideon Harvey (1636/7&#8211;1702): <i>Archelogia philosophica nova; or, New principles of Philosophy. Containing Philosophy in general, Metaphysicks or Ontology, Dynamilogy or a Discourse of Power, Religio Philosophi or Natural Theology, Physicks or Natural philosophy</i>, London, Thomson, 1663.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The word was first used in its Latin form by philosophers based on the Latin roots, which themselves are based on the Greek.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leibniz" title="Leibniz">Leibniz</a> is the only one of the great philosophers of the 17th century to have used the term <i>ontology</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some philosophers, notably in the traditions of the <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonic school</a>, contend that all nouns (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_noun" title="Abstract noun">abstract nouns</a>) refer to existent entities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other philosophers contend that nouns do not always name entities, but that some provide a kind of shorthand for reference to a collection either of <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object (philosophy)">objects</a> or of <a href="/wiki/Event_(philosophy)" title="Event (philosophy)">events</a>. In this latter view, <i><a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a></i>, instead of referring to an entity, refers to a collection of <i>mental events</i> experienced by a <i><a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a></i> refers to a collection of <i>persons</i> with some shared characteristics, and <i><a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a></i> refers to a collection of specific kinds of intellectual activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (October 2017)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> Between these poles of <a href="/wiki/Platonic_realism" title="Platonic realism">realism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nominalism" title="Nominalism">nominalism</a> stand a variety of <a href="/wiki/Moderate_realism" title="Moderate realism">other positions</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_fundamental_questions">Some fundamental questions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Some fundamental questions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Principal questions of ontology include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Ontology is an aspect of the <a href="/wiki/Samkhya" title="Samkhya">Samkhya</a> school of philosophy from the first millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guna" title="Guna">Guna</a> which describes the three properties (<a href="/wiki/Sattva" title="Sattva">sattva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rajas" title="Rajas">rajas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tamas_(philosophy)" title="Tamas (philosophy)">tamas</a>) present in differing proportions in all existing things, is a notable concept of this school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parmenides_and_monism">Parmenides and monism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Parmenides and monism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td In his prologue or <a href="/wiki/Parmenides#Proem" title="Parmenides">proem</a> he describes two views of <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">existence</a>; initially that nothing comes from nothing, and therefore <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">existence</a> is <a href="/wiki/Monism" title="Monism">eternal</a>. Consequently, our opinions about truth must often be false and deceitful. Most of <a href="/wiki/Western_philosophy" title="Western philosophy">western philosophy</a> &#8212; including the fundamental concepts of <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">falsifiability</a> &#8212; have emerged from this view. This posits that existence is what may be conceived of by thought, created, or possessed. Hence, there may be neither void nor vacuum; and true reality neither may come into being nor vanish from existence. Rather, the entirety of creation is eternal, uniform, and immutable, though not infinite (he characterized its shape as that of a perfect sphere). Parmenides thus posits that change, as perceived in everyday experience, is illusory. Everything that may be apprehended is but one part of a single entity. This idea somewhat anticipates the modern concept of an ultimate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unification_theory" title="Grand unification theory">grand unification theory</a> that finally describes all of existence in terms of one inter-related <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">sub-atomic</a> reality which applies to everything.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontological_pluralism">Ontological pluralism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ontological pluralism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological_pluralism" title="Ontological pluralism">Ontological pluralism</a></div>\n<p>The opposite of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleatic" title="Eleatic">eleatic</a> monism is the pluralistic conception of <a href="/wiki/Being" title="Being">Being</a>. In the 5th century BC, <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">Leucippus</a> replaced<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> the reality of Being (unique and unchanging) with that of <a href="/wiki/Becoming_(philosophy)" title="Becoming (philosophy)">Becoming</a> and therefore by a more fundamental and elementary <a href="/wiki/Ontic" title="Ontic">ontic</a> plurality. This thesis originated in the Hellenic world, stated in two different ways by Anaxagoras and by Leucippus. The first theory dealt with "seeds" (which Aristotle referred to as "homeomeries") of the various substances. The second was the atomistic theory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which dealt with reality as based on the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, the atoms and their intrinsic movement in it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The materialist <a href="/wiki/Atomism" title="Atomism">atomism</a> proposed by Leucippus was <a href="/wiki/Indeterminism" title="Indeterminism">indeterminist</a>, but then developed by <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">deterministic</a> way. It was later (4th century BC) that the original atomism was taken again as indeterministic by <a href="/wiki/Epicurus" title="Epicurus">Epicurus</a>. He confirmed the reality as composed of an infinity of indivisible, unchangeable corpuscles or <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> (<i>atomon</i>, lit. \'uncuttable\'), but he gives weight to characterize atoms while for Leucippus they are characterized by a "figure", an "order" and a "position" in the cosmos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> They are, besides, creating the whole with the intrinsic movement in the <i>vacuum</i>, producing the diverse flux of being. Their movement is influenced by the <i>parenklisis</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> names it <a href="/wiki/Clinamen" title="Clinamen">clinamen</a>) and that is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">chance</a>. These ideas foreshadowed our understanding of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">traditional physics</a> until the nature of atoms was discovered in the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plato">Plato</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Plato">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> developed this distinction between true reality and illusion, in arguing that what is real are eternal and unchanging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Forms" title="Theory of Forms">Forms</a> or Ideas (a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Universal_(metaphysics)" title="Universal (metaphysics)">universals</a>), of which things experienced in sensation are at best merely copies, and real only in so far as they copy (\'partake of\') such Forms. In general, Plato presumes that all nouns (e.g., \'Beauty\') refer to real entities, whether sensible bodies or insensible Forms. Hence, in <a href="/wiki/Sophist_(dialogue)" title="Sophist (dialogue)"><i>The Sophist</i></a> Plato argues that Being is a Form in which all existent things participate and which they have in common (though it is unclear whether \'Being\' is intended in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Existence" title="Existence">existence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">copula</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">identity</a>); and argues, against Parmenides, that Forms must exist not only of Being, but also of <a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">Negation</a> and of non-Being (or Difference).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In his <a href="/wiki/Categories_(Aristotle)" title="Categories (Aristotle)"><i>Categories</i></a>, Aristotle identifies ten possible kinds of things that may be the subject or the predicate of a proposition. For Aristotle there are four different ontological dimensions:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="three of these are discussed in Book Theta, but I do not see clear support for">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>according to the various categories or ways of addressing a being as such</li>\n<li>according to its truth or falsity (e.g. fake gold, counterfeit money)</li>\n<li>whether it exists in and of itself or simply \'comes along\' by accident</li>\n<li>according to its potency, movement (energy) or finished presence (<a href="/wiki/Metaphysics_(Aristotle)" title="Metaphysics (Aristotle)"><i>Metaphysics</i> Book Theta</a>).</li></ol>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, and in an interpretation of Greek Aristotelian and Platonist ontological doctrines in medieval <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>, being is either necessary, contingent <i>qua</i> possible, or impossible. Necessary being is that which cannot but be, since its non-being entails a contradiction. Contingent <i>qua</i> possible being is neither necessary nor impossible for it to be or not to be. It is ontologically neutral, and is brought from potential existing into actual existence by way of a cause that is external to its essence. Its being is borrowed unlike the necessary existent, which is self-subsisting and is impossible for it not to be. As for the impossible, it necessarily does not exist, and the affirmation of its being is a contradiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_ontological_topics">Other ontological topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other ontological topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontological_formations">Ontological formations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ontological formations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The concept of \'ontological formations\' refers to formations of social relations understood as dominant ways of living. Temporal, spatial, corporeal, epistemological and performative relations are taken to be central to understanding a dominant formation. That is, a particular ontological formation is based on how ontological categories of time, space, embodiment, knowing and performing are lived&#8212;objectively and subjectively. Different ontological formations include the customary (including the tribal), the traditional, the modern and the postmodern. The concept was first introduced by <a href="/wiki/Paul_James_(academic)" title="Paul James (academic)">Paul James\'</a> <i>Globalism, Nationalism, Tribalism</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> together with a series of writers including Damian Grenfell and <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Steger" title="Manfred Steger">Manfred Steger</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Engaged_theory" title="Engaged theory">engaged theory</a> approach, ontological formations are seen as layered and intersecting rather than singular formations. They are \'formations of being\'. This approach avoids the usual problems of a Great Divide being posited between the modern and the pre-modern. From a philosophical distinction concerning different formations of being, the concept then provides a way of translating into practical understandings concerning how humans might design cities and communities that live creatively across different ontological formations, for example cities that are not completely dominated by modern valences of spatial configuration. Here the work of Tony Fry is important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontological_and_epistemological_certainty">Ontological and epistemological certainty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ontological and epistemological certainty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren&#233; Descartes">Ren&#233; Descartes</a>, with <i>je pense donc je suis</i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cogito_ergo_sum" title="Cogito ergo sum">cogito ergo sum</a></i> or "I think, therefore I am", argued that "the self" is something that we can know exists with <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemological</a> certainty. Descartes argued further that this knowledge could lead to a proof of the certainty of the <a href="/wiki/Existence_of_God" title="Existence of God">existence of God</a>, using the <a href="/wiki/Ontological_argument" title="Ontological argument">ontological argument</a> that had been formulated first by <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Certainty about the existence of "the self" and "the other", however, came under increasing criticism in the 20th century. Sociological theorists, most notably <a href="/wiki/George_Herbert_Mead" title="George Herbert Mead">George Herbert Mead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erving_Goffman" title="Erving Goffman">Erving Goffman</a>, saw the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_Other" title="Cartesian Other">Cartesian Other</a> as a "Generalized Other", the imaginary audience that individuals use when thinking about the self. According to Mead, "we do not assume there is a self to begin with. Self is not presupposed as a stuff out of which the world arises. Rather, the self arises in the world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyde_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hyde-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\nThe Cartesian Other was also used by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>, who saw the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ego,_superego,_and_id" title="Ego, superego, and id">superego</a> as an abstract regulatory force, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="&#201;mile Durkheim">&#201;mile Durkheim</a> who viewed this as a psychologically manifested entity which represented God in society at large.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Body_and_environment.2C_questioning_the_meaning_of_being"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Body_and_environment,_questioning_the_meaning_of_being">Body and environment, questioning the meaning of being</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Body and environment, questioning the meaning of being">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Schools of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metaphysical_subjectivism" title="Metaphysical subjectivism">subjectivism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metaphysical_objectivism" title="Metaphysical objectivism">objectivism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relativism" title="Relativism">relativism</a> existed at various times in the 20th century, and the <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embodied_philosophy" title="Embodied philosophy">body philosophers</a> tried to reframe all these questions in terms of bodies taking some specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_action" title="Philosophy of action">action</a> in an environment. This relied to a great degree on insights derived from scientific research into animals taking instinctive action in natural and artificial settings&#8212;as studied by <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The processes by which bodies related to environments became of great concern, and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Being" title="Being">being</a> itself became difficult to really define. What did people mean when they said "A is B", "A must be B", "A was B"...? Some linguists advocated dropping the verb "to be" from the English language, leaving "<a href="/wiki/E-Prime" title="E-Prime">E&#160;Prime</a>", supposedly less prone to bad abstractions. Others, mostly philosophers, tried to dig into the word and its usage. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a> distinguished <i>human being</i> as <i>existence</i> from the being of things in the world. Heidegger proposes that our way of being human and the way the world is for us are cast historically through a fundamental ontological questioning. These fundamental ontological categories provide the basis for communication in an age: a horizon of unspoken and seemingly unquestionable background meanings, such as human beings understood unquestioningly as subjects and other entities understood unquestioningly as objects. Because these basic ontological meanings both generate and are regenerated in everyday interactions, the locus of our way of being in a historical epoch is the communicative event of language in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyde_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hyde-20">[20]</a></sup> For Heidegger, however, communication in the <i>first</i> place is not among human beings, but language itself shapes up in response to questioning (the inexhaustible meaning of) being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Even the focus of traditional ontology on the \'whatness\' or <i>quidditas</i> of beings in their substantial, standing presence can be shifted to pose the question of the \'whoness\' of human being itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontology_and_language">Ontology and language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ontology and language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some philosophers suggest that the question of "What is?" is (at least in part) an issue of <i>usage</i> rather than a question about facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This perspective is conveyed by an analogy made by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Davidson_(philosopher)" title="Donald Davidson (philosopher)">Donald Davidson</a>: Suppose a person refers to a \'cup\' as a \'chair\' and makes some comments pertinent to a cup, but uses the word \'chair\' consistently throughout instead of \'cup\'. One might readily catch on that this person simply calls a \'cup\' a \'chair\' and the oddity is explained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davidson_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-26">[26]</a></sup> Analogously, if we find people asserting \'there are\' such-and-such, and we do not ourselves think that \'such-and-such\' exist, we might conclude that these people are not nuts (Davidson calls this assumption \'charity\'), they simply use \'there are\' differently than we do. The question of <i>What is?</i> is at least partially a topic in the philosophy of language, and is not entirely about ontology itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krieger_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krieger-27">[27]</a></sup> This viewpoint has been expressed by <a href="/wiki/Eli_Hirsch" title="Eli Hirsch">Eli Hirsch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hirsch interprets <a href="/wiki/Hilary_Putnam" title="Hilary Putnam">Hilary Putnam</a> as asserting that different concepts of "the existence of something" can be correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1-29">[29]</a></sup> This position does not contradict the view that some things do exist, but points out that different \'languages\' will have different rules about assigning this property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch1_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch1-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirsch2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch2-30">[30]</a></sup> How to determine the \'fitness\' of a \'language\' to the world then becomes a subject for investigation.\n</p><p>Common to all <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_copula" title="Indo-European copula">Indo-European copula</a> languages is the double use of the verb "to be" in both stating that entity X exists ("X is.") as well as stating that X has a property ("X is P"). It is sometimes argued that a third use is also distinct, stating that X is a member of a class ("X is a C").   In other language families these roles may have completely different verbs and are less likely to be confused with one another. For example they might say something like "the car has redness" rather than "the car is red".  Hence any discussion of "being" in Indo-European language philosophy may need to make distinctions between these senses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontology_and_human_geography">Ontology and human geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ontology and human geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In human geography there are two types of ontology: small "o" which accounts for the practical orientation, describing functions of being a part of the group, thought to oversimplify and ignore key activities. The other "o", or big "O", systematically, logically, and rationally describes the essential characteristics and universal traits. This concept relates closely to Plato\'s view that the human mind can only perceive a bigger world if they continue to live within the confines of their "caves". However, in spite of the differences, ontology relies on the symbolic agreements among members. That said, ontology is crucial for the axiomatic language frameworks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reality_and_actuality">Reality and actuality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reality and actuality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead" title="Alfred North Whitehead">A.N. Whitehead</a>, for ontology, it is useful to distinguish the terms \'reality\' and \'actuality\'. In this view, an \'actual entity\' has a philosophical status of fundamental ontological priority, while a \'real entity\' is one which may be actual, or may derive its reality from its logical relation to some actual entity or entities. For example, an occasion in the life of Socrates is an actual entity. But Socrates\' being a man does not make \'man\' an actual entity, because it refers indeterminately to many actual entities, such as several occasions in the life of Socrates, and also to several occasions in the lives of Alcibiades, and of others. But the notion of man is real; it derives its reality from its reference to those many actual occasions, each of which is an actual entity. An actual occasion is a concrete entity, while terms such as \'man\' are abstractions from many concrete relevant entities.\n</p><p>According to Whitehead, an actual entity must earn its philosophical status of fundamental ontological priority by satisfying several philosophical criteria, as follows.\n</p>\n<ul><li>There is no going behind an actual entity, to find something more fundamental in fact or in efficacy. This criterion is to be regarded as expressing an axiom, or postulated distinguished doctrine.</li>\n<li>An actual entity must be completely determinate in the sense that there may be no confusion about its identity that would allow it to be confounded with another actual entity. In this sense an actual entity is completely concrete, with no potential to be something other than itself. It is what it is. It is a source of potentiality for the creation of other actual entities, of which it may be said to be a part cause. Likewise it is the concretion or realization of potentialities of other actual entities which are its partial causes.</li>\n<li>Causation between actual entities is essential to their actuality. Consequently, for Whitehead, each actual entity has its distinct and definite extension in physical <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">Minkowski space</a>, and so is uniquely identifiable. A description in Minkowski space supports descriptions in time and space for particular observers.</li>\n<li>It is part of the aim of the philosophy of such an ontology as Whitehead\'s that the actual entities should be all alike, <i>qua</i> actual entities; they should all satisfy a single definite set of well stated ontological criteria of actuality.</li></ul>\n<p>Whitehead proposed that his notion of an occasion of experience satisfies the criteria for its status as the philosophically preferred definition of an actual entity. From a purely logical point of view, each occasion of experience has in full measure the characters of both objective and subjective reality. Subjectivity and objectivity refer to different aspects of an occasion of experience, and in no way do they exclude each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Examples of other philosophical proposals or candidates as actual entities, in this view, are Aristotle\'s \'substances\', Leibniz\' monads, and Descartes <i> &#8242;res verae\' </i>, and the more modern \'states of affairs\'. Aristotle\'s substances, such as Socrates, have behind them as more fundamental the \'primary substances\', and in this sense do not satisfy Whitehead\'s criteria. Whitehead is not happy with Leibniz\' monads as actual entities because they are "windowless" and do not cause each other. \'States of affairs\' are often not closely defined, often without specific mention of extension in physical Minkowski space; they are therefore not necessarily processes of becoming, but may be as their name suggests, simply static states in some sense. States of affairs are contingent on particulars, and therefore have something behind them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> One summary of the Whiteheadian actual entity is that it is a process of becoming. Another summary, referring to its causal linkage to other actual entities, is that it is "all window", in contrast with Leibniz\' windowless monads.\n</p><p>This view allows philosophical entities other than actual entities to really exist, but not as fundamentally and primarily factual or causally efficacious; they have existence as abstractions, with reality only derived from their reference to actual entities. A Whiteheadian actual entity has a unique and completely definite place and time. Whiteheadian abstractions are not so tightly defined in time and place, and in the extreme, some are timeless and placeless, or \'eternal\' entities. All abstractions have logical or conceptual rather than efficacious existence; their lack of definite time does not make them unreal if they refer to actual entities. Whitehead calls this \'the ontological principle\'.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microcosmic_ontology">Microcosmic ontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Microcosmic ontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There is an established and long philosophical history of the concept of atoms as microscopic physical objects.They are far too small to be visible to the naked eye. It was as recent as the nineteenth century that precise estimates of the sizes of putative physical <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> began to become plausible. Almost direct empirical observation of atomic effects was due to the theoretical investigation of <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> in the very early twentieth century. But even then, the real existence of atoms was debated by some. Such debate might be labeled \'microcosmic ontology\'. Here the word \'microcosm\' is used to indicate a physical world of small entities, such as for example atoms.\n</p><p>Subatomic particles are usually considered to be much smaller than atoms. Their real or actual existence may be very difficult to demonstrate empirically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A distinction is sometimes drawn between actual and <a href="/wiki/Virtual_particle" title="Virtual particle">virtual</a> subatomic particles. Reasonably, one may ask, in what sense, if any, do virtual particles exist as physical entities? For atomic and subatomic particles, difficult questions arise, such as do they possess a precise position, or a precise momentum? A question that continues to be controversial is \'to what kind of physical thing, if any, does the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical</a> <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> refer?\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isham_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Isham-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontological_argument">Ontological argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ontological argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ontological_argument" title="Ontological argument">Ontological argument</a></div>\n<p>The first ontological argument in the <a href="/wiki/Christian_philosophy" title="Christian philosophy">Western Christian</a> tradition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury" title="Anselm of Canterbury">Anselm of Canterbury</a> in his 1078 work <i><a href="/wiki/Proslogion" title="Proslogion">Proslogion</a></i>. Anselm defined God as "that than which nothing greater can be thought", and argued that this being must exist in the mind, even in the mind of the person who denies the existence of God. He suggested that, if the greatest possible being exists in the mind, it must also exist in reality. If it only exists in the mind, then an even greater being must be possible&#8212;one which exists both in the mind and in reality. Therefore, this greatest possible being must exist in reality. Seventeenth century French philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren&#233; Descartes">Ren&#233; Descartes</a> deployed a similar argument. Descartes published several variations of his argument, each of which centred on the idea that God\'s existence is immediately inferable from a "clear and distinct" idea of a supremely perfect being. In the early eighteenth century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Leibniz">Gottfried Leibniz</a> augmented Descartes\' ideas in an attempt to prove that a "supremely perfect" being is a coherent concept. A more recent ontological argument came from <a href="/wiki/Kurt_G%C3%B6del" title="Kurt G&#246;del">Kurt G&#246;del</a>, who proposed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_logic" title="Formal logic">formal argument</a> for God\'s existence. Norman Malcolm revived the ontological argument in 1960 when he located a second, stronger ontological argument in Anselm\'s work; <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Plantinga" title="Alvin Plantinga">Alvin Plantinga</a> challenged this argument and proposed an alternative, based on <a href="/wiki/Modal_logic" title="Modal logic">modal logic</a>. Attempts have also been made to validate Anselm\'s proof using an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_theorem_prover" title="Automated theorem prover">automated theorem prover</a>. Other arguments have been categorised as ontological, including those made by Islamic philosophers <a href="/wiki/Mulla_Sadra" title="Mulla Sadra">Mulla Sadra</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allama_Tabatabai" title="Allama Tabatabai">Allama Tabatabai</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Hintikka.27s_locution_for_existence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hintikka\'s_locution_for_existence">Hintikka\'s locution for existence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ontology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hintikka\'s locution for existence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a
