title="Military">military</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Aerospace engineering</b> is the primary field of <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> concerned with the development of <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  It has two major and overlapping branches: aeronautical engineering and astronautical engineering.  <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">Avionics</a> engineering is similar, but deals with the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_engineering" title="Electronic engineering">electronics</a> side of aerospace engineering.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">Aeronautical engineering</a> was the original term for the field. As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_technology" title="Flight technology">flight technology</a> advanced to include craft operating in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> (<a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>), the broader term "<a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> engineering" has come into common use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_Engineering_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica_Engineering-4">[4]</a></sup>  Aerospace engineering, particularly the astronautics branch is often colloquially referred to as "<a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Rocket_science_(disambiguation)" title="Rocket science (disambiguation)">rocket science</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerospace_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Flight vehicles are subjected to demanding conditions such as those caused by changes in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Structural_load" title="Structural load">structural loads</a> applied upon vehicle components. Consequently, they are usually the products of various technological and <a href="/wiki/List_of_engineering_branches" title="List of engineering branches">engineering disciplines</a> including <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_propulsion" title="Air propulsion">propulsion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Structural_analysis" title="Structural analysis">structural analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>. The interaction between these technologies is known as aerospace engineering. Because of the complexity and number of disciplines involved, aerospace engineering is carried out by teams of engineers, each having their own specialized area of expertise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Princeton_review_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Princeton_review-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerospace_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviation_history" title="Aviation history">Aviation history</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:First_flight2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4280" data-file-width="6603" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_flight2.jpg/220px-First_flight2.jpg" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_flight2.jpg/440px-First_flight2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:First_flight2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orville_and_Wilbur_Wright" title="Orville and Wilbur Wright">Orville and Wilbur Wright</a> flew the <a href="/wiki/Wright_Flyer" title="Wright Flyer">Wright Flyer</a> in 1903 at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina.</div></div></div>\n<p>The origin of aerospace engineering can be traced back to the aviation pioneers around the late 19th to early 20th centuries, although the work of Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> dates from the last decade of the 18th to mid-19th century. One of the most important people in the history of <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Cayley was a pioneer in aeronautical engineering<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and is credited as the first person to separate the forces of <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>, which are in effect on any flight vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early knowledge of aeronautical engineering was largely empirical with some concepts and skills imported from other branches of engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-americana_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-americana-10">[10]</a></sup> Scientists understood some key elements of aerospace engineering, like <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, in the 18th century. Many years later after the successful flights by the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a>, the 1910s saw the development of aeronautical engineering through the design of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> military aircraft.\n</p><p>Between World Wars I and II, great leaps were made in Aeronautical Engineering. The invention of <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">Turboprop</a> engines and the advent of mainstream civil aviation greatly accelerated this process. Notable Airplanes of this era include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curtiss_JN_4" title="Curtiss JN 4">Curtiss JN 4</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Farman_F.60_Goliath" title="Farman F.60 Goliath">Farman F.60 Goliath</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fokker_trimotor" title="Fokker trimotor">Fokker trimotor</a>. Notable Military airplanes of the Interwar Period Include the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_A6M_Zero" title="Mitsubishi A6M Zero">Mitsubishi A6M Zero</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Bf_109" title="Messerschmitt Bf 109">Messerschmitt Bf 109</a> from Japan, Great Britain, and Germany respectively. A significant development in Aerospace egineering came with the first <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">Jet engine</a>-powered airplane, the <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_262" title="Messerschmitt Me 262">Messerschmitt Me 262</a> which entered service in 1944 towards the end of the second World War. \n</p><p>The first definition of aerospace engineering appeared in February 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_Engineering_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica_Engineering-4">[4]</a></sup> The definition considered the Earth\'s atmosphere and the outer space as a single realm, thereby encompassing both aircraft (<i>aero</i>) and spacecraft (<i>space</i>) under a newly coined word <i><a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a></i>. In response to the USSR launching the first satellite, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sputnik" title="Sputnik">Sputnik</a> into space on October 4, 1957, U.S. aerospace engineers launched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer_I" title="Explorer I">first American satellite</a> on January 31, 1958.  The <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> was founded in 1958 as a response to the Cold War. In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>, the first manned space mission to the moon took place. It saw three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronauts" title="Astronauts">Astronauts</a> enter orbit around the Moon, with two, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a>, visiting the lunar surface. The third <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">Astronaut</a>, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Michael_Collins" title="Michael Collins">Michael Collins</a>, staying behind in orbit to rendezvous with Armstrong and Aldrin after their visit to the lunar surface. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="box-Expand_section aerospace engineering are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-open-site_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-open-site-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gruntman_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gruntman-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="501" data-file-width="750" decoding="async" height="147" href="/wiki/File:Engine.f15.arp.750pix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fighter jet engine undergoing testing. The tunnel behind the engine allows noise and exhaust to escape.</div></div></div>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">Radar cross-section</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of vehicle signature apparent to <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a> <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_flow" title="Fluid flow">fluid flow</a> around objects. Specifically <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> concerning the flow of air over bodies such as <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> or through objects such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnels</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>).</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">Astrodynamics</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_mechanics" title="Orbital mechanics">orbital mechanics</a> including prediction of orbital elements when given a select few variables.  While few schools in the United States teach this at the undergraduate level, several have graduate programs covering this topic (usually in conjunction with the Physics department of said college or university).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Statics" title="Statics">Statics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamics_(physics)" title="Dynamics (physics)">Dynamics</a> (engineering mechanics)&#160;&#8211; the study of movement, forces, moments in mechanical systems.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a>&#160;&#8211; in particular, <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">differential equations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrotechnology" title="Electrotechnology">Electrotechnology</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> within engineering.</li>\n<li>Propulsion&#160;&#8211; the energy to move a vehicle through the air (or in outer space) is provided by <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turbomachinery" title="Turbomachinery">turbomachinery</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> (see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propeller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft propulsion</a>). A more recent addition to this module is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_propulsion" title="Electric propulsion">electric propulsion</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_propulsion" title="Ion propulsion">ion propulsion</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Control_engineering" title="Control engineering">Control engineering</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_modeling" title="Mathematical modeling">mathematical modeling</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">dynamic</a> behavior of systems and designing them, usually using feedback signals, so that their dynamic behavior is desirable (stable, without large excursions, with minimum error). This applies to the dynamic behavior of aircraft, spacecraft, propulsion systems, and subsystems that exist on aerospace vehicles.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_structures" title="Aircraft structures">Aircraft structures</a>&#160;&#8211; design of the physical configuration of the craft to withstand the forces encountered during flight. Aerospace engineering aims to keep structures lightweight and low-cost while maintaining structural integrity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">Materials science</a>&#160;&#8211; related to structures, aerospace engineering also studies the materials of which the aerospace structures are to be built. New materials with very specific properties are invented, or existing ones are modified to improve their performance.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Solid_mechanics" title="Solid mechanics">Solid mechanics</a>&#160;&#8211; Closely related to material science is solid mechanics which deals with stress and strain analysis of the components of the vehicle. Nowadays there are several Finite Element programs such as MSC Patran/Nastran which aid engineers in the analytical process.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aeroelasticity" title="Aeroelasticity">Aeroelasticity</a>&#160;&#8211; the interaction of aerodynamic forces and structural flexibility, potentially causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeroelastic_flutter" title="Aeroelastic flutter">flutter</a>, divergence, etc.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">Avionics</a>&#160;&#8211; the design and programming of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_systems" title="Computer systems">computer systems</a> on board an aircraft or spacecraft and the <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a> of systems.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">Software</a>&#160;&#8211; the specification, design, development, test, and implementation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_software" title="Computer software">computer software</a> for aerospace applications, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_software" title="Flight software">flight software</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ground_control_software&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ground control software (page does not exist)">ground control software</a>, test &amp; evaluation software, etc.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">Risk and reliability</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of risk and reliability assessment techniques and the mathematics involved in the quantitative methods.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Noise_control" title="Noise control">Noise control</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of the mechanics of sound transfer.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aeroacoustics" title="Aeroacoustics">Aeroacoustics</a>&#160;&#8211; the study of noise generation via either turbulent fluid motion or aerodynamic forces interacting with surfaces.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Flight_test" title="Flight test">Flight test</a>&#160;&#8211; designing and executing flight test programs in order to gather and analyze performance and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Handling_qualities" title="Handling qualities">handling qualities</a> data in order to determine if an aircraft meets its design and performance goals and certification requirements.</li></ul>\n<p>The basis of most of these elements lies in theoretical <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> for aerodynamics or the <a href="/wiki/Equations_of_motion" title="Equations of motion">equations of motion</a> for <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">flight dynamics</a>. There is also a large <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empirical</a> component. Historically, this empirical component was derived from testing of scale models and prototypes, either in <a href="/wiki/Wind_tunnel" title="Wind tunnel">wind tunnels</a> or in the free atmosphere. More recently, advances in <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a> have enabled the use of <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">computational fluid dynamics</a> to simulate the behavior of the fluid, reducing time and expense spent on wind-tunnel testing. Those studying hydrodynamics or <a href="/wiki/Hydroacoustics" title="Hydroacoustics">Hydroacoustics</a> often obtained degrees in Aerospace Engineering.\n</p><p>Additionally, aerospace engineering addresses the integration of all components that constitute an aerospace vehicle (subsystems including power, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_bearing" title="Aerospace bearing">aerospace bearings</a>, communications, <a href="/wiki/Temperature_control" title="Temperature control">thermal control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a>, etc.) and its life cycle (design, temperature, pressure, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">lifetime</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_programs">Degree programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerospace_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Degree programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_aerospace_engineering_schools" title="List of aerospace engineering schools">List of aerospace engineering schools</a></div>\n<p>Aerospace engineering may be studied at the <a href="/wiki/Diploma" title="Diploma">advanced diploma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor\'s degree">bachelor\'s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master\'s degree">master\'s</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> levels in aerospace engineering departments at many universities, and in <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a> departments at others. A few departments offer degrees in space-focused astronautical engineering.  Some institutions differentiate between aeronautical and astronautical engineering.  Graduate degrees are offered in advanced or specialty areas for the aerospace industry.\n</p><p>A background in chemistry, physics, computer science and mathematics is important for students pursuing an aerospace engineering degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture<span id="Rocket_scientist"></span><span id="RocketScientist"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerospace_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rocket_scientist" title="wikt:rocket scientist">rocket scientist</a>" is sometimes used to describe a person of great <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligence</a> since rocket science is seen as a practice requiring great mental ability, especially technically and mathematically. The term is used ironically in the expression "It\'s not rocket science" to indicate that a task is simple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Strictly speaking, the use of "science" in "rocket science" is a misnomer since science is about understanding the origins, nature, and behavior of the universe; engineering is about using scientific and engineering principles to solve problems and develop new technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SA-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-17">[17]</a></sup> However, "science" and "engineering" are often misused as synonyms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-SA-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEE_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEEE-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
