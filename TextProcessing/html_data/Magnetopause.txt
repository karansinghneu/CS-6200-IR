The position of the Sun would be far to the left in this image.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>magnetopause</b> is the abrupt boundary between a <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> and the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>.  For <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a>, the magnetopause is the boundary between the planet&#8217;s magnetic field and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. The location of the magnetopause is determined by the balance between the pressure of the dynamic planetary magnetic field and the dynamic pressure of the solar wind. As the solar wind pressure increases and decreases, the magnetopause moves inward and outward in response. Waves (ripples and flapping motion) along the magnetopause move in the direction of the solar wind flow in response to small-scale variations in the solar wind pressure and to <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability" title="Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz instability">Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz instability</a>.\n</p><p>The solar wind is supersonic and passes through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a> where the direction of flow is changed so that most of the solar wind plasma is deflected to either side of the magnetopause, much like water is deflected before the bow of a ship. The zone of shocked solar wind plasma is the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosheath" title="Magnetosheath">magnetosheath</a>. At Earth and all the other planets with intrinsic magnetic fields, some solar wind plasma succeeds in entering and becoming trapped within the magnetosphere.  At Earth, the solar wind plasma which enters the magnetosphere forms the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_sheet" title="Plasma sheet">plasma sheet</a>. The amount of solar wind plasma and energy that enters the magnetosphere is regulated by the orientation of the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a>, which is embedded in the solar wind.\n</p><p>The Sun and other stars with magnetic fields and stellar winds have a solar magnetopause or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a> where the stellar environment is bounded by the interstellar environment.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="1160" decoding="async" height="168" href="/wiki/File:Dipole-and-image-dipole.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic representation of a planetary dipole magnetic field in a vacuum (right side) deformed by a region of plasma with infinite conductivity. The Sun is to the left. The configuration is equivalent to an image dipole (green arrow) being placed at twice the distance from the planetary dipole to the interaction boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB1940_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CB1940-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Prior to the age of space exploration, interplanetary space was considered to be a vacuum.  The coincidence of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carrington_super_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carrington super flare (page does not exist)">Carrington super flare</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">super geomagnetic event of 1859</a> was evidence that plasma was ejected from the Sun during a flare event.  Chapman and Ferraro <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> proposed that a plasma was emitted by the Sun in a burst as part of a flare event which disturbed the planet\'s magnetic field in a manner known as a geomagnetic storm.  The collision frequency of particles in the plasma in the interplanetary medium is very low and the electrical conductivity is so high that it could be approximated to an infinite conductor.  A magnetic field in a vacuum cannot penetrate a volume with infinite conductivity.  Chapman and Bartels (1940)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB1940_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CB1940-1">[1]</a></sup> illustrated this concept by postulating a plate with infinite conductivity placed on the dayside of a planet&#8217;s dipole as shown in the schematic.  The field lines on the dayside are bent.  At low latitudes, the magnetic field lines are pushed inward.  At high latitudes, the magnetic field lines are pushed backwards and over the polar regions.  The boundary between the region dominated by the planet&#8217;s magnetic field (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>) and the plasma in the interplanetary medium is the magnetopause.  The configuration equivalent to a flat, infinitely conductive plate is achieved by placing an image dipole (green arrow at left of schematic) at twice the distance from the planet&#8217;s dipole to the magnetopause along the planet-Sun line.  Since the solar wind is continuously flowing outward, the magnetopause above, below and to the sides of the planet are swept backward into the geomagnetic tail as shown in the artist&#8217;s concept.  The region (shown in pink in the schematic) which separates field lines from the planet which are pushed inward from those which are pushed backward over the poles is an area of weak magnetic field or day-side cusp.  Solar wind particles can enter the planet&#8217;s magnetosphere through the cusp region.  Because the solar wind exists at all times and not just times of solar flares, the magnetopause is a permanent feature of the space near any planet with a magnetic field.\n</p><p>The magnetic field lines of the planet&#8217;s magnetic field are not stationary.  They are continuously joining or merging with magnetic field lines of the interplanetary magnetic field.  The joined field lines are swept back over the poles into the planetary magnetic tail.  In the tail, the field lines from the planet&#8217;s magnetic field are re-joined and start moving toward night-side of the planet. The physics of this process was first explained by Dungey (1961).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>If one assumed that magnetopause was just a boundary between a magnetic field in a vacuum and a plasma with a weak magnetic field embedded in it, then the magnetopause would be defined by electrons and ions penetrating one gyroradius into the magnetic field domain.  Since the gyro-motion of electrons and ions is in opposite directions, an electric current flows along the boundary.
