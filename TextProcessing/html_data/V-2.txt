href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/V-weapons" title="V-weapons">Vergeltungswaffe</a> 2</i>, "Retribution Weapon 2"), technical name <i><a href="/wiki/Aggregat_(rocket_family)" title="Aggregat (rocket family)">Aggregat</a> 4</i> (A4), was the world\'s first long-range<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Missile_guidance" title="Missile guidance">guided</a> <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missile</a>. The missile, powered by a <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-propellant rocket</a> engine, was developed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a> as a "<a href="/wiki/V-weapons" title="V-weapons">vengeance weapon</a>", assigned to attack Allied cities as retaliation for the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II#The_British_later_in_the_war" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">Allied bombings</a> against German cities. The V-2 rocket also became the first man-made object to travel into space by crossing the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a> with the vertical launch of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MW_18014&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MW 18014 (page does not exist)">MW 18014</a> on 20 June 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Research into military use of long range rockets began when the studies of graduate student <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> attracted the attention of the German Army. A series of prototypes culminated in the A-4, which went to war as the V-2. Beginning in September 1944, over 3,000 V-2s were launched by the German <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> against Allied targets, first <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Li&#232;ge">Li&#232;ge</a>. According to a 2011 BBC documentary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the attacks from V-2s resulted in the deaths of an estimated 9,000 civilians and military personnel, and a further 12,000 forced laborers and concentration camp prisoners died as a result of their forced participation in the production of the weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As Germany collapsed, teams from the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a>&#8212;the United States, the United Kingdom, and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>&#8212;raced to capture key German manufacturing sites and technology. Wernher von Braun and over 100 key V-2 personnel surrendered to the Americans. <a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">Eventually</a>, many of the original V-2 team ended up working at the <a href="/wiki/Redstone_Arsenal" title="Redstone Arsenal">Redstone Arsenal</a>. The US also captured enough V-2 hardware to build approximately 80 of the missiles. The Soviets gained possession of the V-2 manufacturing facilities after the war, re-established V-2 production, and moved it to the Soviet Union.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toclevel-1 tocsection-34"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">15</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developmental_history">Developmental history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Developmental history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the late 1920s, a young <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a> bought a copy of <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Oberth" title="Hermann Oberth">Hermann Oberth</a>\'s book, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wege_zur_Raumschiffahrt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wege zur Raumschiffahrt (page does not exist)">Die Rakete zu den Planetenr&#228;umen</a></i> (<i>The Rocket into Interplanetary Spaces</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WvB_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WvB-8">[8]</a></sup> Starting in 1930, he attended the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Berlin" title="Technical University of Berlin">Technical University of Berlin</a>, where he assisted Oberth in liquid-fueled rocket motor tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WvB_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-WvB-8">[8]</a></sup> Von Braun was working on his doctorate when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Socialist_German_Workers_Party" title="National Socialist German Workers Party">Nazi Party</a> gained power in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrusRocketeer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-PrusRocketeer-9">[9]</a></sup> An artillery captain, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Dornberger" title="Walter Dornberger">Walter Dornberger</a>, arranged an Ordnance Department research grant for von Braun, who from then on worked next to Dornberger\'s existing solid-fuel rocket test site at <a href="/wiki/Kummersdorf" title="Kummersdorf">Kummersdorf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrusRocketeer_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-PrusRocketeer-9">[9]</a></sup> Von Braun\'s thesis, <i>Construction, Theoretical, and Experimental Solution to the Problem of the Liquid Propellant Rocket</i> (dated 16 April 1934), was kept classified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">German Army</a> and was not published until 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By the end of 1934, his group had successfully launched two rockets that reached heights of 2.2 and 3.5&#160;km (1.4 and 2.2&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrusRocketeer_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-PrusRocketeer-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the time, Germany was highly interested in American physicist <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a>\'s research. Before 1939, German engineers and scientists occasionally contacted Goddard directly with technical questions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrusRocketeer_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-PrusRocketeer-9">[9]</a></sup> Von Braun used Goddard\'s plans from various journals and incorporated them into the building of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_series" title="Aggregate series">Aggregat</a></i> (A) series of <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PrusRocketeer_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-PrusRocketeer-9">[9]</a></sup> named for the German word for mechanism or mechanical system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following successes at <a href="/wiki/Kummersdorf" title="Kummersdorf">Kummersdorf</a> with the first two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_series" title="Aggregate series">Aggregate series</a> rockets, Wernher von Braun and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Riedel" title="Walter Riedel">Walter Riedel</a> began thinking of a much larger rocket in the summer of 1936,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup> based on a projected 25,000&#160;kg (55,000&#160;lb) thrust engine.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1978-Anh.024-03,_Peenem%C3%BCnde,_Dornberger,_Olbricht,_Brandt,_v._Braun.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="568" data-file-width="800" href="/wiki/German_Museum_of_Technology" title="German Museum of Technology">German Museum of Technology</a> in Berlin</div></div></div>\n<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_series#A4_(V-2_rocket)" title="Aggregate series">A-4</a> project was postponed due to unfavourable aerodynamic stability testing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_series#A3" title="Aggregate series">A-3</a> in July 1936,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup>\nvon Braun specified the A-4 performance in 1937,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Middlebrook_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Middlebrook-15">[15]</a></sup> and, after an "extensive series of test firings of the A-5" scale test model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopher,_p.111_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christopher,_p.111-16">[16]</a></sup> using a motor redesigned from the troublesome A-3 by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Thiel" title="Walter Thiel">Walter Thiel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopher,_p.111_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christopher,_p.111-16">[16]</a></sup> A-4 design and construction was ordered <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1938/39.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braun_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braun-17">[17]</a></sup> During 28&#8211;30 September 1939, <i>Der Tag der Weisheit</i> (English: <i>The Day of Wisdom</i>) conference met at Peenem&#252;nde to initiate the funding of university research to solve rocket problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>40</span></sup>\n</p><p>By late 1941, the <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde_Army_Research_Center" title="Peenem&#252;nde Army Research Center">Army Research Center</a> at Peenem&#252;nde possessed the technologies essential to the success of the A-4. The four key technologies for the A-4 were large liquid-fuel rocket engines, supersonic aerodynamics, gyroscopic guidance and rudders in jet control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup> At the time, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> was not particularly impressed by the V-2; he pointed out that it was merely an artillery shell with a longer range and much higher cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irons_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irons-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In early September 1943, von Braun promised the Long-Range Bombardment Commission<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>224</span></sup> that the A-4 development was "practically complete/concluded,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>135</span></sup> but even by the middle of 1944, a complete A-4 parts list was still unavailable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>224</span></sup> Hitler was sufficiently impressed by the enthusiasm of its developers, and needed a "wonder weapon" to maintain German morale,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irons_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irons-18">[18]</a></sup> so he authorized its deployment in large numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The V-2s were constructed at the <a href="/wiki/Mittelwerk" title="Mittelwerk">Mittelwerk</a> site by prisoners from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mittelbau-Dora" title="Mittelbau-Dora">Mittelbau-Dora</a>, a concentration camp where 12,000-20,000 prisoners died during the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beon_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beon-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_details">Technical details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4677" data-file-width="3307" decoding="async" height="424" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg/300px-Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg/450px-Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg/600px-Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aggregat4-Schnitt-engl.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Layout of a V-2 rocket</div></div></div>\n<p>The A-4 used a 74% <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>/water mixture (<a href="/wiki/List_of_stoffs" title="List of stoffs"><i>B-Stoff</i></a>) for fuel and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) (<a href="/wiki/List_of_stoffs" title="List of stoffs"><i>A-Stoff</i></a>) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At launch the A-4 propelled itself for up to 65 seconds on its own power, and a program motor controlled the pitch to the specified angle at engine shutdown, after which the rocket continued on a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ballistic" title="wikt:ballistic">ballistic</a> free-fall trajectory. The rocket reached a height of 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi) after shutting off the engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fuel and oxidizer pumps were driven by a steam turbine, and the steam was produced by concentrated <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_permanganate" title="Sodium permanganate">sodium permanganate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>. Both the alcohol and oxygen tanks were an aluminium-magnesium alloy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The combustion burner reached a temperature of 2,500 to 2,700&#160;&#176;C (4,530 to 4,890&#160;&#176;F). The alcohol-water fuel was pumped along the double wall of the main combustion burner. This <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_cooling_(rocket)" title="Regenerative cooling (rocket)">regenerative cooling</a> heated the fuel and cooled the <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a>. The fuel was then pumped into the main burner chamber through 1,224 nozzles, which assured the correct mixture of alcohol and oxygen at all times. Small holes also permitted some alcohol to escape directly into the combustion chamber, forming a cooled <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> that further protected the wall of the chamber, especially at the throat where the chamber was narrowest. The boundary layer alcohol ignited on contact with the atmosphere, accounting for the long, diffuse exhaust plume. By contrast, later, post-V-2 engine designs not employing this alcohol boundary layer cooling show a translucent plume with <a href="/wiki/Shock_diamond" title="Shock diamond">shock diamonds</a>.\n</p><p>The warhead was another source of troubles. The explosive employed was <a href="/wiki/Amatol" title="Amatol">amatol 60/40</a> detonated by an electric <a href="/wiki/Contact_fuze" title="Contact fuze">contact fuze</a>. Amatol had the advantage of stability and the warhead was protected by a thick layer of <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a>, but even so it could still explode in the re-entry phase. The warhead weighed 975 kilograms (2,150&#160;lb) and contained 910 kilograms (2,010&#160;lb) of explosive. The warhead\'s percentage by weight that was explosive was 93%, a very high percentage when compared with other types of munition.\n</p><p>The protective layer was used for the fuel tanks as well and the A-4 did not have the tendency to form ice, which was common to other early missiles (like the <a href="/wiki/Balloon_tank" title="Balloon tank">balloon tank</a>-design <a href="/wiki/SM-65_Atlas" title="SM-65 Atlas">SM-65 Atlas</a>). The tanks held 4,173 kilograms (9,200&#160;lb) of ethyl alcohol and 5,553 kilograms (12,242&#160;lb) of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antwerp_V-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2582" data-file-width="3413" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Antwerp_V-2.jpg/220px-Antwerp_V-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Antwerp_V-2.jpg/330px-Antwerp_V-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Antwerp_V-2.jpg/440px-Antwerp_V-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antwerp_V-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captured V-2 on public display in Antwerp, 1945. Exhaust vanes and external rudders in tail section shown.</div></div></div>\n<p>The V-2 was guided by four external rudders on the tail fins, and four internal <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> vanes in the jet stream at the exit of the motor. The LEV-3 guidance system consisted of two free <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopes</a> (a horizontal and a vertical) for lateral stabilization, and a <a href="/wiki/PIGA_accelerometer" title="PIGA accelerometer">PIGA accelerometer</a> to control engine cutoff at a specified velocity. The V-2 was launched from a pre-surveyed location, so the distance and <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a> to the target were known. Fin 1 of the missile was aligned to the target azimuth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some later V-2s used "<a href="/wiki/Beam_riding" title="Beam riding">guide beams</a>", radio signals transmitted from the ground, to keep the missile on course, but the first models used a simple <a href="/wiki/Analog_computer" title="Analog computer">analog computer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> that adjusted the azimuth for the rocket, and the flying distance was controlled by the timing of the engine cut-off, <i>"<a href="/wiki/Brennschluss" title="Brennschluss">Brennschluss</a>"</i>, ground controlled by a <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler</a> system or by different types of on-board integrating <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">accelerometers</a>. The rocket stopped accelerating and soon reached the top of the approximately <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabolic</a> flight curve.\n</p><p>Dr. Friedrich Kirchstein of <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> of Berlin developed the V-2 <a href="/wiki/Radio_control" title="Radio control">radio control</a> for motor-cut-off (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Brennschluss</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>28,124</span></sup> For velocity measurement, Professor Wolman of Dresden created an alternative of his Doppler<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pocock_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pocock-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>18</span></sup> tracking system in 1940&#8211;41, which used a ground signal transponded by the A-4 to measure the velocity of the missile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>103</span></sup> By 9 February 1942, Peenem&#252;nde engineer De Beek had documented the radio interference area of a V-2 as 10,000 metres (33,000 feet) around the "Firing Point",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klee_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klee-29">[29]</a></sup> and the first successful A-4 flight on 3 October 1943, used radio control for <i>Brennschluss</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> Although Hitler commented on 22 September 1943 that "It is a great load off our minds that we have dispensed with the radio guiding-beam; now no opening remains for the British to interfere technically with the missile in flight",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup> about 20% of the operational V-2 launches were beam-guided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Pinguin</a> V-2 offensive began on 8 September 1944, when <i>Lehr- und Versuchsbatterie No. 444</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pocock_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pocock-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>51&#8211;2</span></sup> (English: <span lang="en">Training and Testing Battery 444</span>) launched a single rocket guided by a radio beam directed at Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klee_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klee-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>47</span></sup> Wreckage of combat V-2s occasionally contained the transponder for velocity and fuel cutoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>259&#8211;60</span></sup>\n</p><p>The painting of the operational V-2s was mostly a <a href="/wiki/Splittertarnmuster" title="Splittertarnmuster">ragged-edged pattern</a> with several variations, but at the end of the war a plain olive green rocket also appeared. During tests, the rocket was painted in a characteristic black-and-white <a href="/wiki/Chessboard" title="Chessboard">chessboard</a> pattern, which aided in determining if the rocket was spinning around its longitudinal axis.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Esquema_de_la_V-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" German designation of the rocket was "V2", unhyphenated &#8212; exactly as used for <a href="/wiki/RLM_aircraft_designation_system#Prototypes_and_variants" title="RLM aircraft designation system">any Third Reich-era "second prototype" example</a> of an <a href="/wiki/RLM_aircraft_by_manufacturer" title="RLM aircraft by manufacturer">RLM-registered</a> German aircraft design &#8212; but U.S. publications such as <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)"><i>Life</i> magazine</a> were using the hyphenated form "V-2" as early as December 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-V2_LIFE_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-V2_LIFE-30">[30]</a></sup> This hyphenated form has now become common usage.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_V-2_test_launches" title="List of V-2 test data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This third day of October, 1942, is the first of a new era in transportation, that of space travel...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup>17</sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<div class="thumb the Allies: <a href="/wiki/Sweden_during_World_War_II#The_B&#228;ckebo_rocket" title="Sweden during World War II">the B&#228;ckebo rocket</a>, the remnants of which landed in Sweden on 13 June 1944 and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_Army_and_V1_and_V2" title="Home Army and V1 and V2">recovered by the Polish resistance</a> on 30 May 1944<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a III</a>. The highest altitude reached during the war was 174.6 kilometres (108.5 miles) (20 June 1944).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup> Test launches of V-2 rockets were made at <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenem&#252;nde">Peenem&#252;nde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blizna" title="Blizna">Blizna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuchola_Forest" title="Tuchola Forest">Tuchola Forest</a>, and after the war, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Backfire_(WWII)" title="Operation Backfire (WWII)">Cuxhaven by the British</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Sands_Proving_Grounds" title="White Sands Proving Grounds">White Sands Proving Grounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> by the U.S., and <a href="/wiki/Kapustin_Yar" title="Kapustin Yar">Kapustin Yar</a> by the USSR.\n</p><p>Various design issues were identified and solved during V-2 development and testing:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><ul><li>To reduce tank pressure and weight, high flow turbopumps were used to boost pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>35</span></sup></li>\n<li>A short and lighter <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a> without burn-through was developed by using centrifugal injection nozzles, a mixing compartment, and a converging nozzle to the throat for homogeneous combustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>51</span></sup></li>\n<li>Film cooling was used to prevent burn-through at the nozzle throat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>52</span></sup></li>\n<li>Relay contacts were made more durable to withstand vibration and prevent thrust cut-off just after lift-off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>52</span></sup></li>\n<li>Ensuring that the fuel pipes had tension-free curves reduced the likelihood of explosions at 1,200&#8211;1,800&#160;m (4,000&#8211;6,000&#160;ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>215,217</span></sup></li>\n<li>Fins were shaped with clearance to prevent damage as the exhaust jet expanded with altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56,118</span></sup></li>\n<li>To control trajectory at liftoff and supersonic speeds, heat-resistant graphite vanes were used as rudders in the exhaust jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dornberger_13-9"><a href="#cite_note-Dornberger-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>35,58</span></sup></li></ul></dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_burst_problem">Air burst problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Through mid-March 1944, only four of the 26 successful Blizna launches had satisfactorily reached the <a href="/wiki/Sarnaki" title="Sarnaki">Sarnaki</a> target area<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klee_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Klee-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>112, 221&#8211;222, 282</span></sup> due to in-flight breakup (<i>Luftzerleger</i>) on re-entry into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>100</span></sup> (As mentioned above, one rocket was collected by the <a href="/wiki/Home_Army" title="Home Army">Home Army</a>, parts of it transported to London for tests.) Initially, the German developers suspected excessive alcohol tank pressure, but by April 1944 after five months of test firings, the cause was still not determined. Major-General Rossmann, the Army Weapons Office department chief, recommended stationing observers in the target area &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> May/June, Dornberger and von Braun set up a camp at the centre of the Poland target zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neufeld_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Neufeld-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span></span></sup> After moving to the <a href="/wiki/Tuchola_Forest" title="Tuchola Forest">Heidekraut</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>172,173</span></sup> SS Mortar Battery 500 of the 836th Artillery Battalion (Motorized) was ordered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klee_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Klee-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>47</span></sup> on 30 August<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pocock_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pocock-28">[28]</a></sup> to begin test launches of eighty \'sleeved\' rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>281</span></sup> Testing confirmed that the so-called \'tin trousers\' &#8211; a tube designed to strengthen the forward end of the rocket cladding &#8212; reduced the likelihood of air bursts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>100</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peenemunde-165515.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="524" data-file-width="804" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peenemunde-165515.jpg/220px-Peenemunde-165515.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peenemunde-165515.jpg/330px-Peenemunde-165515.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peenemunde-165515.jpg/440px-Peenemunde-165515.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peenemunde-165515.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>23 June 1943 RAF reconnaissance photo of V-2s at <a href="/wiki/Test_Stand_VII" title="Test Stand VII">Test Stand VII</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mittelwerk" title="Mittelwerk">Mittelwerk</a></div>\n<p>On 22 December 1942, Hitler signed the order for mass production, when <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Speer</a> assumed final technical data would be ready by July 1943. Eight main storage dumps were planned and four had been completed by July 1944 (the one at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mery-sur-Oise" title="Mery-sur-Oise">Mery-sur-Oise</a> was begun in August 1943 and completed by February 1944).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The missile could be launched practically anywhere, roads running through forests being a particular favourite. The system was so mobile and small that only one Meillerwagen was ever caught in action by Allied aircraft, during the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bodenplatte" title="Operation Bodenplatte">Operation Bodenplatte</a> attack on 1 January 1945<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> near <a href="/wiki/Lochem" title="Lochem">Lochem</a> by a USAAF <a during a launch and his wingman firing at the missile without hitting it.\n</p><p>It was estimated that a sustained rate of 350 V-2s could be launched per week, with 100 per day at maximum effort, given sufficient supply of the rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:V-2victimAntwerp1944.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2153" data-file-width="2773" decoding="async" height="171" A British military convoy was passing through the square at the time; 126 (including 26 Allied soldiers) were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LXV_Army_Corps_(Wehrmacht)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LXV Army Corps (Wehrmacht) (page does not exist)">LXV Armeekorps z.b.V.</a> formed during the last days of November 1943 in France commanded by General der Artillerie z.V. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erich_Heinemann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erich Heinemann (page does not exist)">Erich Heinemann</a> was responsible for the operational use of V-2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Hitler\'s 29 August 1944 declaration to begin V-2 attacks as soon as possible, the offensive began on 8 September 1944 with a single launch at Paris, which caused modest damage near <a href="/wiki/Porte_d%27Italie" title="Porte d\'Italie">Porte d\'Italie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>218,220,467</span></sup> Two more launches by the 485th followed, including one from <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a> against London on the same day at 6:43&#160;p.m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>285</span></sup> &#8211; the first landed at <a href="/wiki/Staveley_Road" title="Staveley Road">Staveley Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiswick" title="Chiswick">Chiswick</a>, killing 63-year-old Mrs. Ada Harrison, 3-year-old Rosemary Clarke, and <a href="/wiki/Sapper" title="Sapper">Sapper</a> Bernard Browning on leave from the Royal Engineers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Middlebrook_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Middlebrook-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup> and one that hit Epping with no casualties. Upon hearing <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">the double-crack</a> of the supersonic rocket (London\'s first ever), <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Sandys" title="Duncan Sandys">Duncan Sandys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Victor_Jones" title="Reginald Victor Jones">Reginald Victor Jones</a> looked up from different parts of the city and exclaimed "That was a rocket!", and a short while after the double-crack, the sky was filled with the sound of a heavy body rushing through the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>286</span></sup>\n</p><p>The British government initially attempted to conceal the cause of the explosions by blaming them on defective <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">gas</a> mains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The public therefore began referring to the V-2s as "flying gas pipes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The Germans themselves finally announced the V-2 on 8 November 1944 and only then, on 10 November 1944, did <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> inform Parliament, and the world, that England had been under rocket attack "for the last few weeks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 1944 control of the V-2 mission was taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Waffen-SS" title="Waffen-SS">Waffen-SS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Positions of the German launch units changed a number of times. For example, <i>Artillerie Init 444</i> arrived in the southwest Netherlands (in <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a>) in September 1944. From a field near the village of <a href="/wiki/Serooskerke,_Walcheren" title="Serooskerke, Walcheren">Serooskerke</a>, five V-2s were launched on 15 and 16 September, with one more successful and one failed launch on the 18th. That same date, a transport carrying a missile took a wrong turn and ended up in Serooskerke itself, giving a villager the opportunity to surreptitiously take some photographs of the weapon; these were smuggled to London by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Resistance" title="Dutch Resistance">Dutch Resistance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> After that the unit moved to the woods near <a href="/wiki/Rijs" title="Rijs">Rijs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaasterland" title="Gaasterland">Gaasterland</a> in the northwest Netherlands, to ensure that the technology did not fall into Allied hands. From Gaasterland V-2s were launched against <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a> from 25 September (London being out of range). Because of their inaccuracy, these V-2s did not hit their target cities. Shortly after that only London and Antwerp remained as designated targets as ordered by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> himself, Antwerp being targeted in the period of 12 to 20 October, after which time the unit moved to The Hague.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain,_1945_HU88803.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1772" data-file-width="2278" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg/220px-Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg/330px-Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg/440px-Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain%2C_1945_HU88803.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Damage_Caused_by_V2_Rocket_Attacks_in_Britain,_1945_HU88803.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruined buildings at <a href="/wiki/Whitechapel" title="Whitechapel">Whitechapel</a>, London, left by the penultimate V-2 to strike the city on 27 March 1945; the rocket killed 134 people. The final V-2 to fall on London killed one person at <a href="/wiki/Orpington" title="Orpington">Orpington</a> later the same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Over the next few months about 3,172 V-2 rockets were fired at the following targets:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><b>Belgium</b>, 1664: <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> (1610), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)" title="Li&#232;ge (city)">Li&#232;ge</a> (27), <a href="/wiki/Hasselt" title="Hasselt">Hasselt</a> (13), <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">Tournai</a> (9), <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">Mons</a> (3), <a href="/wiki/Diest" title="Diest">Diest</a> (2)</dd>\n<dd><b>United Kingdom</b>, 1402: <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> (1358), <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a> (43),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irving_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-Irving-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>289</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a> (1)</dd>\n<dd><b>France</b>, 76: <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">Lille</a> (25), <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> (22), <a href="/wiki/Tourcoing" title="Tourcoing">Tourcoing</a> (19), <a href="/wiki/Arras" title="Arras">Arras</a> (6), <a href="/wiki/Cambrai" title="Cambrai">Cambrai</a> (4)</dd>\n<dd><b>Netherlands</b>, 19: <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">Maastricht</a> (19)</dd>\n<dd><b>Germany</b>, 11: <a href="/wiki/Remagen" title="Remagen">Remagen</a> (11)</dd></dl>\n<p>An estimated 2,754 civilians were killed in London by V-2 attacks with another 6,523 injured,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> which is two people killed per V-2 rocket. However, this understates the potential of the V-2, since many rockets were misdirected and exploded harmlessly. Accuracy increased over the course of the war, particularly for batteries where the <i>Leitstrahl</i> (radio guide beam) system was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Missile strikes that found targets could cause large numbers of deaths &#8212; 160 were killed and 108 seriously injured in one explosion at 12:26 pm on 25 November 1944, at a <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Company" title="F. W. Woolworth Company">Woolworth\'s</a> department store in <a href="/wiki/New_Cross" title="New Cross">New Cross</a>, south-east London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>\n</p><p>British intelligence sent false reports via their <a href="/wiki/Double-Cross_System" title="Double-Cross System">Double-Cross System</a> implying that the rockets were over-shooting their London target by 10 to 20 miles (16 to 32&#160;km). This tactic worked; more than half of the V-2s aimed at London landed outside the London Civil Defense Region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p. 459</span></sup> Most landed on less-heavily populated areas in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> due to erroneous recalibration. For the remainder of the war, British intelligence kept up the ruse by repeatedly sending bogus reports implying that the rockets were now striking the British capital with heavy loss of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blitz_Street;_Channel_4,_10.5.2010_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blitz_Street;_Channel_4,_10.5.2010-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>, Belgium was also the target for a large number of V-weapon attacks from October 1944 through March 1945, leaving 1,736 dead and 4,500 injured in greater Antwerp. Thousands of buildings were damaged or destroyed as the city was struck by 590 direct hits. The largest loss of life in a single attack came on 16 December 1944, when the roof of the crowded cinema <a href="/wiki/Cine_Rex" title="Cine Rex">REX</a> was struck, leaving 567 dead and 291 injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_use_during_Operation_Bodenplatte">Possible use during Operation <i>Bodenplatte</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Possible use during Operation Bodenplatte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At least one V-2 missile on a mobile <i><a href="/wiki/Meillerwagen" title="Meillerwagen">Meillerwagen</a></i> launch trailer was observed being elevated to launch position by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USAAF" title="USAAF">USAAF</a> <a href="/wiki/4th_Fighter_Group" title="4th Fighter Group">4th Fighter Group</a> pilot defending against the massive New Year\'s Day 1945 <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bodenplatte" title="Operation Bodenplatte">Operation Bodenplatte</a> strike by the Luftwaffe over the northern German attack route near the town of <a href="/wiki/Lochem" title="Lochem">Lochem</a> on 1 January 1945. Possibly, from the potential sighting of the American fighter by the missile\'s launch crew, the rocket was quickly lowered from a near launch-ready 85&#176; elevation to 30&#176;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactical_use">Tactical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tactical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the US Army captured the <a href="/wiki/Ludendorff_Bridge" title="Ludendorff Bridge">Ludendorff Bridge</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Remagen" title="Battle of Remagen">Battle of Remagen</a>, the Germans were desperate to destroy it. On 17 March 1945, they fired eleven V-2 missiles at the bridge, their first use against a tactical target. They could not employ the more accurate <i>Leitstrahl</i> device because it was oriented towards Antwerp and could not be easily adjusted for another target. Fired from near <a href="/wiki/Hellendoorn" title="Hellendoorn">Hellendoorn</a>, the Netherlands, one of the missiles landed as far away as Cologne, 40 miles (64&#160;km) to the north, while one missed the bridge by only 500 to 800 yards (460 to 730&#160;m). They also struck the town of Remagen, destroying a number of buildings and killing at least six American soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v2rocket_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-v2rocket-57">[57]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_use">Final use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Final use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bomb_Damage_in_London,_England,_April_1945_CH15111.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="662" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg/220px-Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg/330px-Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg/440px-Bomb_Damage_in_London%2C_England%2C_April_1945_CH15111.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bomb_Damage_in_London,_England,_April_1945_CH15111.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The extent of damage caused to a London residential area due to single V-2 strike in January 1945</div></div></div>\n<p>The final two rockets exploded on 27 March 1945. One of these was the last V-2 to kill a British civilian: Mrs. Ivy Millichamp, aged 34, killed in her home in Kynaston Road, <a href="/wiki/Orpington" title="Orpington">Orpington</a> in Kent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A scientific reconstruction carried out in 2010 demonstrated that the V-2 creates a crater 20 metres (66 feet) wide and 8 metres (26 feet) deep, ejecting approximately 3,000 tons of material into the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blitz_Street;_Channel_4,_10.5.2010_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blitz_Street;_Channel_4,_10.5.2010-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_history">Post-war history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Post-war history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the Nazi defeat, German engineers were moved to the United States, the United Kingdom and the USSR, where they further developed the V-2 rocket for military and civilian purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The V-2 rocket also laid the foundation for the liquid fuel missiles and space launchers used later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Crossbow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_Big_Ben" title="Project Big Ben">Project Big Ben</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="2174" decoding="async" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG/220px-V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG/330px-V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG/440px-V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:V-2_f%C3%B6rbr%C3%A4nningskammare.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">Rocket engine</a> used by V-2, <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Historisches_Museum" title="Deutsches Historisches Museum">Deutsches Historisches Museum</a></i>, Berlin (2014)</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Big_Ben_and_Crossbow">Big Ben and Crossbow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Big Ben and Crossbow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1</a>, the V-2\'s speed and trajectory made it practically invulnerable to anti-aircraft guns and fighters, as it dropped from an altitude of 100&#8211;110&#160;km (62&#8211;68&#160;mi) at up to three times the speed of sound at sea level (approximately 3550&#160;km/h). Nevertheless, the threat of what was then code-named "Big Ben" was great enough that efforts were made to seek countermeasures. The situation was similar to the pre-war concerns about manned bombers and led to a similar solution, the formation of the Crossbow Committee to collect, examine and develop countermeasures.\n</p><p>Early on, it was believed that the V-2 employed some form of radio guidance, a belief that persisted in spite of several rockets being examined without discovering anything like a radio receiver. This led to efforts to jam this non-existent guidance system as early as September 1944, using both ground and air-based jammers flying over the UK. In October, a group had been sent to jam the missiles during launch. By December it was clear these systems were having no obvious effect, and jamming efforts ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stock25_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-stock25-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-aircraft_gun_system">Anti-aircraft gun system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Anti-aircraft gun system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>General <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Alfred_Pile" title="Frederick Alfred Pile">Frederick Alfred Pile</a>, commander of <a href="/wiki/Anti-Aircraft_Command" title="Anti-Aircraft Command">Anti-Aircraft Command</a>, studied the problem and proposed that enough <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_gun" title="Anti-aircraft gun">anti-aircraft guns</a> were available to produce a barrage of fire in the rocket\'s path, but only if provided with a reasonable prediction of the trajectory. The first estimates suggested that 320,000 shells would have to be fired for each rocket. About 2% of these were expected to fall back to the ground, almost 90 tons of rounds, which would cause far more damage than the missile. At a 25 August 1944 meeting of the Crossbow Committee, the concept was rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stock25_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-stock25-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pile continued studying the problem, and returned with a proposal to fire only 150 shells at a single rocket, with those shells using a new fuse that would greatly reduce the number that fell back to Earth unexploded. Some low-level analysis suggested that this would be successful against 1 in 50 rockets, provided that accurate trajectories were forwarded to the gunners in time. Work on this basic concept continued and developed into a plan to deploy a large number of guns in <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a> that were provided with pre-configured firing data for 2.5-mile (4.0-kilometre) grids of the London area. After the trajectory was determined, the guns would aim and fire between 60 and 500 rounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stock25_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-stock25-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At a Crossbow meeting on 15 January 1945 Pile\'s updated plan was presented with some strong advocacy from <a href="/wiki/Roderic_Hill" title="Roderic Hill">Roderic Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Drummond_Ellis" title="Charles Drummond Ellis">Charles Drummond Ellis</a>. However, the Committee suggested that a test not be carried out as no technique for tracking the missiles with sufficient accuracy had yet been developed. By March this had changed significantly, with 81% of incoming missiles correctly allotted to the grid square each fell into, or the one beside it. At a 26 March meeting the plan moved ahead, and Pile was directed to a subcommittee with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RV_Jones" title="RV Jones">RV Jones</a> and Ellis to further develop the statistics. Three days later the team returned a report stating that if the guns fired 2,000 rounds at a missile there was a 1 in 60 chance of shooting it down. Plans for an operational test began, but as Pile later put it, "<a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery" title="Bernard Montgomery">Monty</a> beat us to it", as the attacks ended with the Allied liberation of their launching areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stock25_61-3"><a href="#cite_note-stock25-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the Germans no longer in control of any part of the continent that could be used as a launching site capable of striking London, they turned their attention on Antwerp. Plans were made to move the Pile system to protect that city, but the war ended before anything could be done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stock25_61-4"><a href="#cite_note-stock25-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_attack">Direct attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Direct attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another defence against the V-2 campaign was to destroy the launch infrastructure&#8212;expensive in terms of bomber resources and casualties&#8212;or to cause the Germans to aim at the wrong place through <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a>. The British were able to convince the Germans to direct <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1s</a> and V-2s aimed at London to less populated areas east of the city. This was done by sending deceptive reports on the damage caused and sites hit via the German espionage network in Britain, which was controlled by the British (the <a href="/wiki/Double-Cross_System" title="Double-Cross System">Double-Cross System</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>According to the BBC television presenter <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Baxter" title="Raymond Baxter">Raymond Baxter</a>, who served with the RAF during the war, in February 1945 his squadron was carrying out a mission against a V2 launch site, when one missile was launched in front of them. One member of Baxter\'s squadron opened fire on it, without effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 3 March 1945 the Allies attempted to destroy V-2s and launching equipment in the "Haagse Bos" in The Hague by a <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_the_Bezuidenhout" title="Bombing of the Bezuidenhout">large-scale bombardment</a>, but due to navigational errors the <a href="/wiki/Bezuidenhout" title="Bezuidenhout">Bezuidenhout</a> quarter was destroyed, killing 511 Dutch civilians. Churchill sent a scathing minute to General Ismay requesting a thorough explanation for "this extraordinarily bad aiming".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assessment">Assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The German V-weapons (V-1 and V-2) cost the equivalent of around USD $40 billion (2015 dollars), which was 50 percent more than the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> that produced the atomic bomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>178</span></sup>\n6,048 V-2s were built, at a cost of approximately 100,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_reichsmark" title="German reichsmark">Reichsmark</a> (GB&#163;2,370,000 (2011)) each; 3,225 were launched.\nSS General <a href="/wiki/Hans_Kammler" title="Hans Kammler">Hans Kammler</a>, who as an <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> had constructed several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" title="Concentration camp">concentration camps</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auschwitz" title="Auschwitz">Auschwitz</a>, had a reputation for brutality and had originated the idea of using concentration camp prisoners as <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slave</a> laborers in the rocket program. More people died manufacturing the V-2 than were killed by its deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="infobox" style="width:20em;">\n<p>"... those of us who were seriously engaged in the war were very grateful to Wernher von Braun. We knew that each V-2 cost as much to produce as a high-performance fighter airplane. We knew that German forces on the fighting fronts were in desperate need of airplanes, and that the V-2 rockets were doing us no military damage. From our point of view, the V-2 program was almost as good as if Hitler had adopted a policy of unilateral disarmament." (<a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>The V-2 consumed a third of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a>\'s fuel alcohol production and major portions of other critical technologies:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oberg_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oberg-66">[66]</a></sup> to distil the fuel alcohol for one V-2 launch required 30 tonnes of potatoes at a time when food was becoming scarce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Due to a lack of explosives, some warheads were simply filled in with concrete, using the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> alone for destruction, and sometimes the warhead contained photographic propaganda of German citizens who had died in Allied bombings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irons2_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irons2-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The V-2 lacked a <a href="/wiki/Proximity_fuze" title="Proximity fuze">proximity fuze</a>, so it could not be set for <a href="/wiki/Air_burst" title="Air burst">air burst</a>; it buried itself in the target area before or just as the warhead detonated. This reduced its effectiveness. Furthermore, its early guidance systems were too primitive to hit specific targets and its costs were approximately equivalent to 40% of the cost of a two-engine Ju-88 bomber,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> which was more accurate (though only in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Bombing_During_World_War_II#Effectiveness" title="Strategic Bombing During World War II">relative sense</a>), which could carry more warheads and was reusable. In comparison, in one 24-hour period during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hurricane_(1944)" title="Operation Hurricane (1944)">Operation Hurricane</a>, the RAF dropped over 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a> of bombs on <a href="/wiki/Braunschweig" title="Braunschweig">Brunswick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a>, roughly equivalent to the amount of explosives that could be delivered by 10,000 V-2 rockets.\n</p><p>While the V-programs did in fact divert resources needed for other, more effective programs, this aspect should not be overstated, as the limiting factor for German aviation after 1941 was always the availability of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel#History" title="Synthetic fuel">high test aviation gas</a>, not planes or pilots, so criticisms that compare their cost to hypothetical increases in fighter or bomber production are misguided. On the other hand, the psychological effect of the V-2 was considerable, as the V-2, travelling faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, gave no warning before impact (unlike bombing planes or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-1_Flying_Bomb" title="V-1 Flying Bomb">V-1 Flying Bomb</a>, which made a characteristic buzzing sound). There was no effective defence and no risk of pilot and crew casualties.\n</p><p>With the war all but lost, regardless of the factory output of conventional weapons, the Nazis resorted to V-weapons as a tenuous last hope to influence the war militarily (hence Antwerp as V-2 target), as an extension of their desire to "punish" their foes and most importantly to give hope to their supporters with their <a href="/wiki/Wunderwaffe" title="Wunderwaffe">miracle weapon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irons_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Irons-18">[18]</a></sup> The V-2 had no effect on the outcome of the war, but it led to the <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBMs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> that were used for space exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unfulfilled_plans">Unfulfilled plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Unfulfilled plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A submarine-towed launch platform was tested successfully, making it the prototype for <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_ballistic_missile" title="Submarine-launched ballistic missile">submarine-launched ballistic missiles</a>. The project codename was <i>Pr&#252;fstand XII</i> ("Test stand XII"), sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_U-boat" title="Rocket U-boat">rocket U-boat</a>. If deployed, it would have allowed a <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> to launch V-2 missiles against United States cities, though only with considerable effort (and limited effect).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>\nHitler, in July 1944 and Speer, in January 1945, made speeches alluding to the scheme,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> though Germany did not possess the capability to fulfill these threats. These schemes were met by the Americans with <a href="/wiki/Operation_Teardrop" title="Operation Teardrop">Operation Teardrop</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>While interned after the war by the British at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSDIC" title="CSDIC">CSDIC</a> camp 11, Dornberger was recorded saying that he had begged the F&#252;hrer to stop the V-weapon propaganda, because nothing more could be expected from one ton of explosive. To this Hitler had replied that <i>Dornberger</i> might not expect more, but <i>he</i> (Hitler) certainly did.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>According to decrypted messages from the Japanese embassy in Germany, twelve dismantled V-2 rockets were shipped to Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> These left <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> in August 1944 on the transport U-boats <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-219" title="German submarine U-219"><i>U-219</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-195" title="German submarine U-195"><i>U-195</i></a>, which reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Djakarta" title="Djakarta">Djakarta</a> in December 1944. A civilian V-2 expert was a passenger on <i><a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-234" title="German submarine U-234"><i>U-234</i></a></i>, bound for Japan in May 1945 when the war ended in Europe. The fate of these V-2 rockets is unknown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_use">Post-war use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Post-war use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At the end of the war, a race began between the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> to retrieve as many V-2 rockets and staff as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Three hundred rail-car loads of V-2s and parts were captured and shipped to the United States and 126 of the principal designers, including Wernher von Braun and Walter Dornberger, were in American hands. Von Braun, his brother <a href="/wiki/Magnus_von_Braun" title="Magnus von Braun">Magnus von Braun</a>, and seven others decided to surrender to the United States military (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">Operation Paperclip</a>) to ensure they were not captured by the advancing Soviets or shot dead by the Nazis to prevent their capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britain">Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:V-2_Rocket_On_Meillerwagen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="86" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:V-2_Rocket_On_Meillerwagen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Backfire_(WWII)" title="Operation Backfire (WWII)">Operation Backfire</a> V-2 rocket on <i><a href="/wiki/Meillerwagen" title="Meillerwagen">Meillerwagen</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">S.I.</a> Negative #76-2755).</div></div></div>\n<p>In October 1945, British <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Backfire_(WWII)" title="Operation Backfire (WWII)">Operation Backfire</a></i> assembled a small number of V-2 missiles and launched three of them from a site in northern Germany. The engineers involved had already agreed to move to the US when the test firings were complete. The Backfire report remains the most extensive technical documentation of the rocket, including all support procedures, tailored vehicles and fuel composition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In 1946, the <a href="/wiki/British_Interplanetary_Society" title="British Interplanetary Society">British Interplanetary Society</a> proposed an enlarged man-carrying version of the V-2, called Megaroc. It could have enabled <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a> similar to, but at least a decade earlier than, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone" title="Mercury-Redstone">Mercury-Redstone</a> flights of 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/V-2_sounding_rocket" title="V-2 sounding rocket">V-2 sounding rocket</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2310" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg/220px-Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg/330px-Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg/440px-Bumper8_launch-GPN-2000-000613.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a (rocket)"><i>Bumper</i> V-2</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">Operation Paperclip</a> recruited German engineers and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip#Related_operations" title="Operation Paperclip">Special Mission V-2</a> transported the captured V-2 parts to the United States. At the close of the Second World War, over 300 rail cars filled with V-2 engines, <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellant</a> tanks, gyroscopes, and associated equipment were brought to the railyards in <a href="/wiki/Las_Cruces,_New_Mexico" title="Las Cruces, New Mexico">Las Cruces, New Mexico</a>, so they could be placed on trucks and driven to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Sands_Proving_Grounds" title="White Sands Proving Grounds">White Sands Proving Grounds</a>, also in New Mexico.\n</p><p>In addition to V-2 hardware, the U.S. Government delivered German mechanization equations for the V-2 guidance, navigation, and control systems, as well as for advanced development concept vehicles, to U.S. defence contractors for analysis. In the 1950s some of these documents were useful to U.S. contractors in developing direction cosine matrix transformations and other inertial navigation architecture concepts that were applied to early U.S. programs such as the Atlas and Minuteman guidance systems as well as the Navy\'s Subs Inertial Navigation System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A committee was formed with military and civilian scientists to review payload proposals for the reassembled V-2 rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> This led to an eclectic array of experiments that flew on V-2s and paved the way for American manned <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>. Devices were sent aloft to sample the air at all levels to determine <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressures</a> and to see what <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a> were present. Other instruments measured the level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" title="Cosmic radiation">cosmic radiation</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:First_photo_from_space.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1619" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/First_photo_from_space.jpg/220px-First_photo_from_space.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/First_photo_from_space.jpg/330px-First_photo_from_space.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/First_photo_from_space.jpg/440px-First_photo_from_space.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:First_photo_from_space.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first photo from space was taken from a V-2 launched by US scientists on 24 October 1946.</div></div></div>\n<p>Only 68 percent of the V-2 trials were considered successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> A supposed V-2 launched on 29 May 1947 landed near Juarez, Mexico and was actually a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermes_project" title="Hermes project">Hermes B-1 vehicle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> attempted to launch a German V-2 rocket at sea&#8212;one test launch from the aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_Midway_(CV-41)" title="USS Midway (CV-41)">USS Midway</a> was performed on 6 September 1947 as part of the Navy\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Sandy" title="Operation Sandy">Operation Sandy</a>. The test launch was a partial success; the V-2 went off the pad but splashed down in the ocean only some 10&#160;km (6&#160;mi) from the carrier. The launch setup on the Midway\'s deck is notable in that it used foldaway arms to prevent the missile from falling over. The arms pulled away just after the engine ignited, releasing the missile. The setup may look similar to the <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7</a> launch procedure but in the case of the R-7 the trusses hold the full weight of the rocket, rather than just reacting to side forces.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/PGM-11_Redstone" title="PGM-11 Redstone">PGM-11 Redstone</a> rocket is a direct descendant of the V-2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USSR">USSR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=V-2_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: USSR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> also captured a number of V-2s and staff, letting them stay in Germany for a time. The first work contracts were signed in the middle of 1945. In 1946 (as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Osoaviakhim" title="Operation Osoaviakhim">Operation Osoaviakhim</a>) they were obliged to move to <a href="/wiki/Kapustin_Yar" title="Kapustin Yar">Kapustin Yar</a> in the USSR, where <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Gr%C3%B6ttrup" title="Helmut Gr&#246;ttrup">Helmut Gr&#246;ttrup</a> headed up a group of just under 250 engineers. The first Soviet missile was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-1_rocket" title="R-1 rocket">R-1</a>, a duplicate of the V-2. Most of the German team was sent home after that project but some remained to do research until as late as 1951. Unknown to the Germans, work immediately began on larger missiles, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-2_rocket" title="R-2 rocket">R-2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-5_missile" title="R-5 missile">R-5</a>, based on extension of the V-2 technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In the autumn of 1945, the group led by M. Tikhonravov K. and N. G. Chernyshov at NII-4 rocket artillery institute of the USSR Academy of Sciences developed on their own initiative the first stratospheric rocket project. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_BP-190" title="Project BP-190">BP-190</a> called for vertical flight of two pilots to an altitude of 200&#160;km using captured German V-2 rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_V-2_examples_and_components">Surviving V-2 examples and components</span><span
