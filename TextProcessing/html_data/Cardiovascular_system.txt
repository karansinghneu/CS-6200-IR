class="extiw" href="https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q11068" title="d:Q11068">edit on Wikidata</a>]</small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>circulatory system</b>, also called the <b>cardiovascular system</b> or the <b>vascular system</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">organ system</a> that permits <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> to circulate and transport <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolytes" title="Electrolytes">electrolytes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blood_cell" title="Blood cell">blood cells</a> to and from the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> in the body to provide nourishment and help in <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">fighting diseases</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">stabilize temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and maintain <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>.\n</p><p>The circulatory system includes the <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a>, which circulates <a href="/wiki/Lymph" title="Lymph">lymph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> The passage of lymph for example takes much longer than that of blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRU_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRU-2">[2]</a></sup> Blood is a fluid consisting of <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_blood_cell" title="White blood cell">white blood cells</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">platelets</a> that is circulated by the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> through the vertebrate vascular system, carrying oxygen and nutrients to and waste materials away from all body tissues. Lymph is essentially recycled excess blood plasma after it has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillary_filtration" title="Capillary filtration">filtered</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a> (between cells) and returned to the lymphatic system. The cardiovascular (from Latin words meaning "heart" and "vessel") system comprises the blood, heart, and <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The lymph, <a href="/wiki/Lymph_node" title="Lymph node">lymph nodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_vessel" title="Lymphatic vessel">lymph vessels</a> form the lymphatic system, which returns filtered blood plasma from the interstitial fluid (between cells) as lymph.\n</p><p>The circulatory system of the blood is seen as having two components, a systemic circulation and a pulmonary circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PubMed_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PubMed-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While humans, as well as other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>, have a closed cardiovascular system (meaning that the blood never leaves the network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veins" title="Veins">veins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>), some <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> groups have an open cardiovascular system. The lymphatic system, on the other hand, is an open system providing an accessory route for excess interstitial fluid to be returned to the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood,_Lauralee_2011_401_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood,_Lauralee_2011_401-5">[5]</a></sup> The more primitive, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploblastic" title="Diploblastic">diploblastic</a> animal <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phyla</a> lack circulatory systems.\n</p><p>Many diseases affect the circulatory system. This includes <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>, affecting the cardiovascular system, and <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_disease" title="Lymphatic disease">lymphatic disease</a> affecting the lymphatic system.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiologist" title="Cardiologist">Cardiologists</a> are medical professionals which specialise in the heart, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiothoracic_surgeon" title="Cardiothoracic surgeon">cardiothoracic surgeons</a> specialise in operating on the heart and its surrounding areas. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascular_surgeon" title="Vascular surgeon">Vascular surgeons</a> focus on other parts of the circulatory system.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" to major organ systems</div></div></div>\n<p>The essential components of the human cardiovascular system are the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It includes the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary circulation</a>, a "loop" through the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> where blood is oxygenated; and the systemic circulation, a "loop" through the rest of the body to provide <a href="/wiki/Oxygenate" title="Oxygenate">oxygenated</a> blood. The systemic circulation can also be seen to function in two parts &#8211; a <i>macrocirculation</i> and a <a href="/wiki/Microcirculation" title="Microcirculation">microcirculation</a>. An average adult contains five to six quarts (roughly 4.7 to 5.7 liters) of blood, accounting for approximately 7% of their total body weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Blood consists of <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_blood_cells" title="White blood cells">white blood cells</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platelets" title="Platelets">platelets</a>. Also, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a> works with the circulatory system to provide the nutrients the system needs to keep the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> pumping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The cardiovascular systems of humans are closed, meaning that the blood never leaves the network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels">blood vessels</a>. In contrast, oxygen and nutrients diffuse across the blood vessel layers and enter  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a>, which carries oxygen and nutrients to the target cells, and carbon dioxide and wastes in the opposite direction. The other component of the circulatory system, the <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a>, is open.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arteries">Arteries</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arterial_tree" title="Arterial tree">Arterial tree</a></div>\n<p>Oxygenated blood enters the systemic circulation when leaving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic semilunar valve</a>. The first part of the systemic circulation is the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a>, a massive and thick-walled artery. The aorta arches and gives branches supplying the upper part of the body after passing through the aortic opening of the diaphragm at the level of thoracic ten vertebra, it enters the abdomen. Later it descends down and supplies branches to abdomen, pelvis, perineum and the lower limbs. The walls of aorta are elastic. This elasticity helps to maintain the <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> throughout the body. When the aorta receives almost five litres of blood from the heart, it recoils and is responsible for pulsating blood pressure. Moreover, as aorta branches into smaller arteries, their elasticity goes on decreasing and their compliance goes on increasing.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Capillaries">Capillaries</span></h4>\n<p>Arteries branch into small passages called <a href="/wiki/Arteriole" title="Arteriole">arterioles</a> and then into the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The capillaries merge to bring blood into the venous system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Veins">Veins</span></h4>\n<p>After their passage through body tissues, capillaries merge once again into <a href="/wiki/Venule" title="Venule">venules</a>, which continue to merge into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veins" title="Veins">veins</a>. The venous system finally coalesces into two major veins: the <a href="/wiki/Superior_vena_cava" title="Superior vena cava">superior vena cava</a> (roughly speaking draining the areas above the heart) and the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior vena cava</a> (roughly speaking from areas below the heart). These two great vessels empty into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronary_vessels">Coronary vessels</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">Coronary circulation</a></div>\n<p>The heart itself is supplied with oxygen and nutrients through a small "loop" of the systemic circulation and derives very little from the blood contained within the four chambers.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_veins">Portal veins</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein">Portal vein</a></div>\n<p>The general rule is that arteries from the heart branch out into capillaries, which collect into veins leading back to the heart. <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein">Portal veins</a> are a slight exception to this. In humans the only significant example is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a> which combines from capillaries around the <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a> where the blood absorbs the various products of digestion; rather than leading directly back to the heart, the hepatic portal vein branches into a second capillary system in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart">Heart</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">Heart</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_the_human_heart_(cropped).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="651" data-file-width="663" decoding="async" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg/250px-Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg/375px-Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg/500px-Diagram_of_the_human_heart_%28cropped%29.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_the_human_heart_(cropped).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View from the front</div></div></div>\n<p>The heart pumps oxygenated blood to the body and deoxygenated blood to the lungs. In the human <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> there is one <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(heart)" title="Atrium (heart)">atrium</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricle</a> for each circulation, and with both a systemic and a pulmonary circulation there are four chambers in total: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_atrium" title="Left atrium">left atrium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_ventricle" title="Right ventricle">right ventricle</a>. The right atrium is the upper chamber of the right side of the heart. The blood that is returned to the right atrium is deoxygenated (poor in oxygen) and passed into the right ventricle to be pumped through the pulmonary artery to the lungs for re-oxygenation and removal of carbon dioxide. The left atrium receives newly oxygenated blood from the lungs as well as the pulmonary vein which is passed into the strong left ventricle to be pumped through the aorta to the different organs of the body.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">coronary circulation</a> system provides a blood supply to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myocardium" title="Myocardium">heart muscle</a> itself. The coronary circulation begins near the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> by two <a href="/wiki/Coronary_arteries" title="Coronary arteries">coronary arteries</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Right_coronary_artery" title="Right coronary artery">right coronary artery</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Left_coronary_artery" title="Left coronary artery">left coronary artery</a>. After nourishing the heart muscle, blood returns through the coronary veins into the <a href="/wiki/Coronary_sinus" title="Coronary sinus">coronary sinus</a> and from this one into the right atrium. Back flow of blood through its opening during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atrial_systole" title="Atrial systole">atrial systole</a> is prevented by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thebesian_valve" title="Thebesian valve">Thebesian valve</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Smallest_cardiac_veins" title="Smallest cardiac veins">smallest cardiac veins</a> drain directly into the heart chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lungs">Lungs</span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="868" data-file-width="1507" decoding="async" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg/370px-2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg/555px-2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg/740px-2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg 2x" width="370"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2119_Pulmonary_Circuit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The pulmonary circulation as it passes from the heart. Showing both the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronchial_artery" title="Bronchial artery">bronchial arteries</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">Pulmonary circulation</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">circulatory system of the lungs</a> is the portion of the cardiovascular system in which <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>-depleted <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> is pumped away from the heart, via the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a>, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a> and returned, oxygenated, to the heart via the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary vein</a>.\n</p><p>Oxygen deprived blood from the superior and inferior <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vena_cava" title="Vena cava">vena cava</a> enters the right atrium of the heart and flows through the <a href="/wiki/Tricuspid_valve" title="Tricuspid valve">tricuspid valve</a> (right atrioventricular valve) into the right ventricle, from which it is then pumped through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_semilunar_valve" title="Pulmonary semilunar valve">pulmonary semilunar valve</a> into the pulmonary artery to the lungs. <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">Gas exchange</a> occurs in the lungs, whereby <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> is released from the blood, and oxygen is absorbed. The pulmonary vein returns the now oxygen-rich blood to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_atrium" title="Left atrium">left atrium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A separate system known as the <a href="/wiki/Bronchial_circulation" title="Bronchial circulation">bronchial circulation</a> supplies blood to the tissue of the larger airways of the lung.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Systemic_circulation">Systemic circulation</span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1317" data-file-width="1942" decoding="async" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/350px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/525px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/700px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The systemic circulation and capillary networks shown and also as separate from the pulmonary circulation</div></div></div>\n<p>Systemic circulation is the portion of the cardiovascular system which transports oxygenated blood away from the heart through the aorta from the left ventricle where the blood has been previously deposited from pulmonary circulation, to the rest of the body, and returns oxygen-depleted blood back to the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brain">Brain</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_circulation" title="Cerebral circulation">Cerebral circulation</a></div>\n<p>The brain has a dual blood supply that comes from arteries at its front and back. These are called the "anterior" and "posterior" circulation respectively. The anterior circulation arises from the <a href="/wiki/Internal_carotid_artery" title="Internal carotid artery">internal carotid arteries</a> and supplies the front of the brain. The posterior circulation arises from the <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_artery" title="Vertebral artery">vertebral arteries</a>, and supplies the back of the brain and <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a>. The circulation from the front and the back join together (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anastomise" title="Anastomise">anastomise</a>) at the <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_Willis" title="Circle of Willis">Circle of Willis</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidneys">Kidneys</span></h4>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_circulation" title="Renal circulation">renal circulation</a> receives around 20% of the cardiac output. It branches from the <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_aorta" title="Abdominal aorta">abdominal aorta</a> and returns blood to the ascending <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vena_cava" title="Vena cava">vena cava</a>. It is the blood supply to the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>, and contains many specialized blood vessels.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lymphatic_system">Lymphatic system</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">Lymphatic system</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a> is part of the circulatory system. It is a network of <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_vessel" title="Lymphatic vessel">lymphatic vessels</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_capillaries" title="Lymph capillaries">lymph capillaries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_nodes" title="Lymph nodes">lymph nodes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatic_organs" title="Lymphatic organs">organs</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatic_tissues" title="Lymphatic tissues">lymphatic tissues</a> and circulating <a href="/wiki/Lymph" title="Lymph">lymph</a>. One of its major functions is to carry the lymph, draining and returning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a> back towards the heart for return to the cardiovascular system, by emptying into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatic_duct" title="Lymphatic duct">lymphatic ducts</a>. Its other main function is in the <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system" title="Adaptive immune system">adaptive immune system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberts_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alberts-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fetal_circulation" title="Fetal circulation">Fetal circulation</a></div>\n<p>The development of the circulatory system starts with <a href="/wiki/Vasculogenesis" title="Vasculogenesis">vasculogenesis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>. The human arterial and venous systems develop from different areas in the embryo. The arterial system develops mainly from the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arches" title="Aortic arches">aortic arches</a>, six pairs of arches which develop on the upper part of the embryo. The venous system arises from three bilateral veins during weeks 4&#160;&#8211; 8 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_embryogenesis" title="Human embryogenesis">embryogenesis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fetal_circulation" title="Fetal circulation">Fetal circulation</a> begins within the 8th week of development. Fetal circulation does not include the lungs, which are bypassed via the <a href="/wiki/Truncus_arteriosus" title="Truncus arteriosus">truncus arteriosus</a>. Before birth the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> obtains <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>) from the mother through the <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">placenta</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cord" title="Umbilical cord">umbilical cord</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitaker_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitaker-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart_2">Heart</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heart_development" title="Heart development">Heart development</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arteries_2">Arteries</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aortic_arches" title="Aortic arches">Aortic arches</a></div>\n<p>The human arterial system originates from the aortic arches and from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsal_aortae" title="Dorsal aortae">dorsal aortae</a> starting from week 4 of embryonic life. The first and second aortic arches regress and forms only the <a href="/wiki/Maxillary_artery" title="Maxillary artery">maxillary arteries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stapedial_artery" title="Stapedial artery">stapedial arteries</a> respectively. The arterial system itself arises from aortic arches 3, 4 and 6 (aortic arch 5 completely regresses).\n</p><p>The dorsal aortae, present on the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dorsal" title="wikt:dorsal">dorsal</a> side of the embryo, are initially present on both sides of the embryo. They later fuse to form the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> itself. Approximately thirty smaller arteries branch from this at the back and sides. These branches form the <a href="/wiki/Intercostal_arteries" title="Intercostal arteries">intercostal arteries</a>, arteries of the arms and legs, lumbar arteries and the lateral sacral arteries. Branches to the sides of the aorta will form the definitive <a href="/wiki/Renal_artery" title="Renal artery">renal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inferior_suprarenal_artery" title="Inferior suprarenal artery">suprarenal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gonadal_artery" title="Gonadal artery">gonadal arteries</a>. Finally, branches at the front of the aorta consist of the <a href="/wiki/Vitelline_arteries" title="Vitelline arteries">vitelline arteries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbilical_arteries" title="Umbilical arteries">umbilical arteries</a>. The vitelline arteries form the <a href="/wiki/Celiac_artery" title="Celiac artery">celiac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery" title="Superior mesenteric artery">superior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inferior_mesenteric_artery" title="Inferior mesenteric artery">inferior mesenteric arteries</a> of the gastrointestinal tract. After birth, the umbilical arteries will form the <a href="/wiki/Internal_iliac_artery" title="Internal iliac artery">internal iliac arteries</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veins_2">Veins</span></h3>\n<p>The human venous system develops mainly from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitelline_vein" title="Vitelline vein">vitelline veins</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_vein" title="Umbilical vein">umbilical veins</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Posterior_cardinal_vein" title="Posterior cardinal vein">cardinal veins</a>, all of which empty into the <a href="/wiki/Sinus_venosus" title="Sinus venosus">sinus venosus</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_system_2">Cardiovascular system</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="320" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif/220px-Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of a typical human red blood cell cycle in the circulatory system. This animation occurs at a faster rate (~20 seconds of the average <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell#Human_erythrocytes" title="Red blood cell">60-second cycle</a>) and shows the red blood cell deforming as it enters capillaries, as well as the bars changing color as the cell alternates in states of oxygenation along the circulatory system.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blood#Oxygen_transport" title="Blood">Blood &#167;&#160;Oxygen transport</a></div>\n<p>About 98.5% of the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in a sample of arterial blood in a healthy human, breathing air at sea-level pressure, is chemically combined with <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> molecules. About 1.5% is physically dissolved in the other blood liquids and not connected to hemoglobin. The hemoglobin molecule is the primary transporter of oxygen in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> and many other species.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lymphatic_system_2">Lymphatic system</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system#Function" title="Lymphatic system">Lymphatic system &#167;&#160;Function</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span></h2>\n<p><span id="Health_and_disease"></span>\nMany diseases affect the circulatory system. These include a number of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular diseases</a>, affecting the cardiovascular system, and <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_disease" title="Lymphatic disease">lymphatic diseases</a> affecting the lymphatic system.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiologist" title="Cardiologist">Cardiologists</a> are medical professionals which specialise in the heart, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiothoracic_surgeon" title="Cardiothoracic surgeon">cardiothoracic surgeons</a> specialise in operating on the heart and its surrounding areas. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascular_surgeon" title="Vascular surgeon">Vascular surgeons</a> focus on other parts of the circulatory system.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_disease">Cardiovascular disease</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">Cardiovascular disease</a></div>\n<p>Diseases affecting the cardiovascular system are called <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease" title="Cardiovascular disease">cardiovascular disease</a>.\n</p><p>Many of these diseases are called "<a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_disease" title="Lifestyle disease">lifestyle diseases</a>" because they develop over time and are related to a person\'s exercise habits, diet, whether they smoke, and other lifestyle choices a person makes. <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">Atherosclerosis</a> is the precursor to many of these diseases. It is where small <a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheromatous plaques</a> build up in the walls of medium and large arteries. This may eventually grow or rupture to occlude the arteries. It is also a risk factor for <a href="/wiki/Acute_coronary_syndrome" title="Acute coronary syndrome">acute coronary syndromes</a>, which are diseases which are characterised by a sudden deficit of oxygenated blood to the heart tissue. Atherosclerosis is also associated with problems such as <a href="/wiki/Aneurysm" title="Aneurysm">aneurysm</a> formation or splitting ("dissection") of arteries.\n</p><p>Another major cardiovascular disease involves the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Thrombus" title="Thrombus">clot, called a "thrombus"</a>. These can originate in veins or arteries. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_venous_thrombosis" title="Deep venous thrombosis">Deep venous thrombosis</a>, which mostly occurs in the legs, is one cause of clots in the veins of the legs, particularly when a person has been stationary for a long time. These clots may <a href="/wiki/Embolus" title="Embolus">embolise</a>, meaning travel to another location in the body. The results of this may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_embolus" title="Pulmonary embolus">pulmonary embolus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transient_ischaemic_attack" title="Transient ischaemic attack">transient ischaemic attacks</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>.\n</p><p>Cardiovascular diseases may also be congenital in nature, such as <a href="/wiki/Congenital_heart_defect" title="Congenital heart defect">heart defects</a> or <a href="/wiki/Persistent_fetal_circulation" title="Persistent fetal circulation">persistent fetal circulation</a>, where the circulatory changes that are supposed to happen after birth do not. Not all congenital changes to the circulatory system are associated with diseases, a large number are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatomical_variation" title="Anatomical variation">anatomical variations</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigations">Investigations</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="746" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif/220px-Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif/330px-Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif/440px-Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arteria-lusoria_MRA_MIP.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_angiography" title="Magnetic resonance angiography">Magnetic resonance angiography</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aberrant_subclavian_artery" title="Aberrant subclavian artery">aberrant subclavian artery</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The function and health of the circulatory system and its parts are measured in a variety of manual and automated ways. These include simple methods such as those that are part of the <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_examination" title="Cardiovascular examination">cardiovascular examination</a>, including the taking of a person\'s <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a> as an indicator of a person\'s <a href="/wiki/Heart_rate" title="Heart rate">heart rate</a>, the taking of <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a> or the use of a <a href="/wiki/Stethoscope" title="Stethoscope">stethoscope</a> to listen to the heart for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_murmur" title="Cardiac murmur">murmurs</a> which may indicate problems with the <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">heart\'s valves</a>. An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" title="Electrocardiogram">electrocardiogram</a> can also be used to evaluate the way in which electricity is conducted through the heart.\n</p><p>Other more invasive means can also be used. A <a href="/wiki/Cannula" title="Cannula">cannula</a> or <a href="/wiki/Catheter" title="Catheter">catheter</a> inserted into an artery may be used to measure <a href="/wiki/Pulse_pressure" title="Pulse pressure">pulse pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_wedge_pressure" title="Pulmonary wedge pressure">pulmonary wedge pressures</a>. Angiography, which involves injecting a dye into an artery to visualise an arterial tree, can be used in the heart (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_angiography" title="Coronary angiography">coronary angiography</a>) or brain. At the same time as the arteries are visualised, blockages or narrowings may be fixed through the insertion of <a href="/wiki/Stent" title="Stent">stents</a>, and active bleeds may be managed by the insertion of coils. An MRI may be used to image arteries, called an <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_angiography" title="Magnetic resonance angiography">MRI angiogram</a>. For evaluation of the blood supply to the lungs a <a href="/wiki/CT_pulmonary_angiogram" title="CT pulmonary angiogram">CT pulmonary angiogram</a> may be used.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasound#Angiology_(vascular)" title="Medical ultrasound">Vascular ultrasonography</a> include for example:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Intravascular_ultrasound" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>\n<p>While humans, as well as other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>, have a closed cardiovascular system (meaning that the blood never leaves the network of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veins" title="Veins">veins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>), some <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> groups have an open cardiovascular system. The lymphatic system, on the other hand, is an open system providing an accessory route for excess interstitial fluid to be returned to the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood,_Lauralee_2011_401_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood,_Lauralee_2011_401-5">[5]</a></sup> The more primitive, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploblastic" title="Diploblastic">diploblastic</a> animal <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phyla</a> lack circulatory systems.\n</p><p>The blood vascular system first appeared probably in an ancestor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triploblasts" title="Triploblasts">triploblasts</a> over 600 million years ago, overcoming the time-distance constraints of diffusion, while <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelium</a> evolved in an ancestral <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> some 540&#8211;510 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_circulatory_system">Open circulatory system</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">Hemolymph</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Open_Circulatroy.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Open_Circulatroy.gif/220px-Open_Circulatroy.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Open_Circulatroy.gif/330px-Open_Circulatroy.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Open_Circulatroy.gif/440px-Open_Circulatroy.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Open_Circulatroy.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The open circulatory system of the grasshopper &#8211; made up of a heart, vessels and hemolymph. The hemolymph is pumped through the heart, into the aorta, dispersed into the head and throughout the hemocoel, then back through the ostia in the heart and the process repeated.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, the open circulatory system is a system in which a fluid in a cavity called the hemocoel bathes the organs directly with oxygen and nutrients and there is no distinction between <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a>; this combined fluid is called <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">hemolymph</a> or haemolymph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Muscular movements by the animal during <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a> can facilitate hemolymph movement, but diverting flow from one area to another is limited. When the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> relaxes, blood is drawn back toward the heart through open-ended pores (ostia).\n</p><p>Hemolymph fills all of the interior hemocoel of the body and surrounds all <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>. Hemolymph is composed of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic compounds</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>). The primary oxygen transporter molecule is <a href="/wiki/Hemocyanin" title="Hemocyanin">hemocyanin</a>.\n</p><p>There are free-floating cells, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemocyte" title="Hemocyte">hemocytes</a>, within the hemolymph. They play a role in the arthropod <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1145" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg/220px-Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg/330px-Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg/440px-Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pseudoceros_bifurcus_-_Blue_Pseudoceros_Flatworm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flatworms, such as this <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudoceros_bifurcus" title="Pseudoceros bifurcus">Pseudoceros bifurcus</a></i>, lack specialized circulatory organs</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closed_circulatory_system">Closed circulatory system</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Two_chamber_heart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Two_chamber_heart.svg/220px-Two_chamber_heart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Two_chamber_heart.svg/330px-Two_chamber_heart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Two_chamber_heart.svg/440px-Two_chamber_heart.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Two_chamber_heart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two-chambered heart of a fish</div></div></div>\n<p>The circulatory systems of all <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, as well as of <a href="/wiki/Annelid" title="Annelid">annelids</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">earthworms</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> (<a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopuses</a> and relatives) are <i>closed</i>, just as in humans. Still, the systems of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> show various stages of the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of the circulatory system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In fish, the system has only one circuit, with the blood being pumped through the capillaries of the <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> and on to the capillaries of the body tissues. This is known as <i>single cycle</i> circulation. The heart of fish is, therefore, only a single pump (consisting of two chambers).\n</p><p>In amphibians and most reptiles, a double circulatory system is used, but the heart is not always completely separated into two pumps. Amphibians have a three-chambered heart.\n</p><p>In reptiles, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventricular_septum" title="Ventricular septum">ventricular septum</a> of the heart is incomplete and the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a> is equipped with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphincter_muscle" title="Sphincter muscle">sphincter muscle</a>. This allows a second possible route of blood flow. Instead of blood flowing through the pulmonary artery to the lungs, the sphincter may be contracted to divert this blood flow through the incomplete ventricular septum into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a> and out through the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a>. This means the blood flows from the capillaries to the heart and back to the capillaries instead of to the lungs. This process is useful to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectothermic" title="Ectothermic">ectothermic</a> (cold-blooded) animals in the regulation of their body temperature.\n</p><p>Birds, mammals, and <a href="/wiki/Crocodilia" title="Crocodilia">crocodilians</a> show complete separation of the heart into two pumps, for a total of four heart chambers; it is thought that the four-chambered heart of birds and crocodilians evolved independently from that of mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="No_circulatory_system">No circulatory system</span></h3>\n<p>Circulatory systems are absent in some animals, including <a href="/wiki/Flatworm" title="Flatworm">flatworms</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Body_cavity" title="Body cavity">body cavity</a> has no lining or enclosed fluid. Instead a muscular <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a> leads to an extensively branched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a> that facilitates direct <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> of nutrients to all cells. The flatworm\'s dorso-ventrally flattened body shape also restricts the distance of any cell from the digestive system or the exterior of the organism. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> can diffuse from the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> into the cells, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can diffuse out. Consequently, every cell is able to obtain nutrients, water and oxygen without the need of a transport system.\n</p><p>Some animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>, have more extensive branching from their <a href="/wiki/Gastrovascular_cavity" title="Gastrovascular cavity">gastrovascular cavity</a> (which functions as both a place of digestion and a form of circulation), this branching allows for bodily fluids to reach the outer layers, since the digestion begins in the inner layers.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" "Epitome", fol.14a. HMD Collection, WZ 240 V575dhZ 1543.</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest known writings on the circulatory system are found in the <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> (16th century BCE), an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_medicine" title="Ancient Egyptian medicine">ancient Egyptian medical papyrus</a> containing over 700 prescriptions and remedies, both physical and spiritual. In the <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, it acknowledges the connection of the heart to the arteries. The Egyptians thought air came in through the mouth and into the lungs and heart. From the heart, the air travelled to every member through the arteries. Although this concept of the circulatory system is only partially correct, it represents one of the earliest accounts of scientific thought.\n</p><p>In the 6th century BCE, the knowledge of circulation of vital fluids through the body was known to the <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurvedic</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Sushruta" title="Sushruta">Sushruta</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07-18">[18]</a></sup> He also seems to have possessed knowledge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>, described as \'channels\' by Dwivedi &amp; Dwivedi (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">valves of the heart</a> were discovered by a physician of the <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocratean</a> school around the 4th century BCE. However their function was not properly understood then. Because blood pools in the veins after death, arteries look empty. Ancient anatomists assumed they were filled with air and that they were for transport of air.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Medicine" title="Ancient Greek Medicine">Greek physician</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herophilus" title="Herophilus">Herophilus</a>, distinguished veins from arteries but thought that the <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a> was a property of arteries themselves. Greek anatomist <a href="/wiki/Erasistratus" title="Erasistratus">Erasistratus</a> observed that arteries that were cut during life bleed. He ascribed the fact to the phenomenon that air escaping from an artery is replaced with blood that entered by very small vessels between veins and arteries. Thus he apparently postulated capillaries but with reversed flow of blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2nd century AD <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Medicine" title="Ancient Greek Medicine">Greek</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> knew that blood vessels carried blood and identified venous (dark red) and arterial (brighter and thinner) blood, each with distinct and separate functions. Growth and energy were derived from venous blood created in the liver from chyle, while arterial blood gave vitality by containing pneuma (air) and originated in the heart. Blood flowed from both creating organs to all parts of the body where it was consumed and there was no return of blood to the heart or liver. The heart did not pump blood around, the heart\'s motion sucked blood in during diastole and the blood moved by the pulsation of the arteries themselves.\n</p><p>Galen believed that the arterial blood was created by venous blood passing from the left ventricle to the right by passing through \'pores\' in the interventricular septum, air passed from the lungs via the pulmonary artery to the left side of the heart. As the arterial blood was created \'sooty\' vapors were created and passed to the lungs also via the pulmonary artery to be exhaled.\n</p><p>In 1025, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Iranian_Medicine" title="Ancient Iranian Medicine">Persian physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, "erroneously accepted the Greek notion regarding the existence of a hole in the ventricular septum by which the blood traveled between the ventricles." Despite this, Avicenna "correctly wrote on the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_cycle" title="Cardiac cycle">cardiac cycles</a> and valvular function", and "had a vision of blood circulation" in his <i>Treatise on Pulse</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (September 2010)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While also refining Galen\'s erroneous theory of the pulse, Avicenna provided the first correct explanation of pulsation: "Every beat of the pulse comprises two movements and two pauses. Thus, expansion&#160;: pause&#160;: contraction&#160;: pause. [...] The pulse is a movement in the heart and arteries ... which takes the form of alternate expansion and contraction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajar_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hajar-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1242, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medicine_in_medieval_Islam" title="Medicine in medieval Islam">Arabian physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Nafis" title="Ibn al-Nafis">Ibn al-Nafis</a>, became the first person to accurately describe the process of <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary circulation</a>, for which he is sometimes considered the father of <a href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_physiology" title="Cardiovascular physiology">circulatory physiology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2010)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Ibn al-Nafis stated in his <i>Commentary on Anatomy in Avicenna\'s Canon</i>:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"...the blood from the right chamber of the heart must arrive at the left chamber but there is no direct pathway between them. The thick septum of the heart is not perforated and does not have visible pores as some people thought or invisible pores as Galen thought. The blood from the right chamber must flow through the vena arteriosa (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a>) to the lungs, spread through its substances, be mingled there with air, pass through the arteria venosa (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary vein</a>) to reach the left chamber of the heart and there form the vital spirit..."</p></blockquote>\n<p>In addition, Ibn al-Nafis had an insight into what would become a larger theory of the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> circulation. He stated that "there must be small communications or pores (<i>manafidh</i> in Arabic) between the pulmonary artery and vein," a prediction that preceded the discovery of the capillary system by more than 400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Ibn al-Nafis\' theory, however, was confined to blood transit in the lungs and did not extend to the entire body.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">Michael Servetus</a> was the first European to describe the function of pulmonary circulation, although his achievement was not widely recognized at the time, for a few reasons. He firstly described it in the "Manuscript of Paris"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> (near 1546), but this work was never published. And later he published  this description, but in a theological treatise, <i>Christianismi Restitutio</i>, not in a book on medicine. Only three copies of the book survived but these remained hidden for decades, the rest were burned  shortly after its publication in 1553 because of persecution of Servetus by religious authorities.\n</p><p>Better known discovery of pulmonary circulation was by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesalius" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a>\'s successor at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Padua" title="University of Padua">Padua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Realdo_Colombo" title="Realdo Colombo">Realdo Colombo</a>, in 1559.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Harvey_(1578-1657)_Venenbild.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="901" data-file-width="1293" Harvey">William Harvey</a>\'s <i>Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus</i>, 1628</div></div></div>\n<p>Finally, <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>, a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Fabricius" title="Hieronymus Fabricius">Hieronymus Fabricius</a> (who had earlier described the valves of the veins without recognizing their function), performed a sequence of experiments, and published <i>Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus</i> in 1628, which "demonstrated that there had to be a direct connection between the venous and arterial systems throughout the body, and not just the lungs. Most importantly, he argued that the beat of the heart produced a continuous circulation of blood through minute connections at the extremities of the body. This is a conceptual leap that was quite different from Ibn al-Nafis\' refinement of the anatomy and bloodflow in the heart and lungs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This work, with its essentially correct exposition, slowly convinced the medical world. However, Harvey was not able to identify the capillary system connecting arteries and veins; these were later discovered by <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Malpighi" title="Marcello Malpighi">Marcello Malpighi</a> in 1661.\n</p><p>In 1956, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Cournand" title="Andr&#233; Fr&#233;d&#233;ric Cournand">Andr&#233; Fr&#233;d&#233;ric Cournand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Werner_Forssmann" title="Werner Forssmann">Werner Forssmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dickinson_W._Richards" title="Dickinson W. Richards">Dickinson W. Richards</a> were awarded the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="List of Nobel laureates in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize</a> in Medicine "for their discoveries concerning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_catheterization" title="Heart catheterization">heart catheterization</a> and pathological changes in the circulatory system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\nIn his Nobel lecture, Forssmann credits Harvey as birthing cardiology with the publication of his book in 1628.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Diana_McSherry" title="Diana McSherry">Diana McSherry</a> developed computer-based systems to create images of the circulatory system and heart without the need for surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayne_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayne-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
