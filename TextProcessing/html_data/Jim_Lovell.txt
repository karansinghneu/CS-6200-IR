colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>James Arthur Lovell Jr.</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'l\' in \'lie\'">l</span><span title="/&#652;/: \'u\' in \'cut\'">&#652;</span><span title="\'v\' in \'vie\'">v</span><span title="/&#601;l/: \'le\' in \'bottle\'">&#601;l</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born March 25, 1928) is a former <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Aviator" title="Naval Aviator">Naval Aviator</a>, and retired <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States_O-6)" title="Captain (United States O-6)">captain</a>. Lovell is known for being the commander of the ill-fated <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> mission, which suffered a critical failure en route to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> but was brought back safely to Earth through the efforts of the crew and mission control.  In addition to being part of the Apollo 13 crew, Lovell was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">command module</a> pilot of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a>, the first Apollo mission to enter lunar orbit.\n</p><p>He is one of only <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts#Apollo_astronauts_who_flew_to_the_Moon_without_landing" title="List of Apollo astronauts">24 people to have flown to the Moon</a> and the first of only three people to fly to the Moon twice as well as the only one to have flown there twice without making a <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">landing</a>. Lovell was the first person to fly in space four times.\n</p><p>He is a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Space_Medal_of_Honor" title="Congressional Space Medal of Honor">Congressional Space Medal of Honor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a> (in 1970, one of 17 recipients in the group Space Exploration).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="tocnumber">12</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>\n<p>Born in 1928 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, James Lovell was the only child of his mother Blanche (Masek), who was of <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czech</a> descent, and his father, James, Sr., an <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada-born coal furnace salesman, who died in a car accident in 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMS-2">[2]</a></sup> For about two years, Lovell and his mother lived with a relative in <a href="/wiki/Terre_Haute,_Indiana" title="Terre Haute, Indiana">Terre Haute, Indiana</a>. After relocating with his mother to <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a>, he graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solomon_Juneau_Business_High_School" title="Solomon Juneau Business High School">Juneau High School</a>. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts</a> during his childhood, Lovell eventually achieved <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eagle_Scout_rank_(Boy_Scouts_of_America)" title="Eagle Scout rank (Boy Scouts of America)">Eagle Scout</a>, the organization\'s highest level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SMS-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honor_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-honor-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honor2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-honor2-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lovell became interested in rocketry and built flying models as a boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostmoon_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lostmoon-7">[7]</a></sup> After graduating from high school, he attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin&#8211;Madison">University of Wisconsin&#8211;Madison</a> for two years under the <a href="/wiki/Aviation_Cadet_Training_Program_(USN)#Aviation_Midshipman_(AVMIDN)_Program_(1946&#8211;1950)" title="Aviation Cadet Training Program (USN)">"Flying Midshipman" program</a> from 1946 to 1948.  While at Madison, he played football and pledged to the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Phi_Omega" title="Alpha Phi Omega">Alpha Phi Omega</a> fraternity.\n</p><p>While Lovell was attending pre-flight training in the summer of 1948, the navy was beginning to make cutbacks in the program, and cadets were under a great deal of pressure to transfer out. There were concerns that some or most of the students who graduated as Naval Aviators would not have pilot billets to fill. This threat persisted until the outbreak of the Korean War in 1950.  Lovell  applied and was accepted to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy</a> in the fall of 1948. During his first year he wrote a treatise on the liquid-fueled rocket engine. He attended Annapolis for the full four years, graduating as an ensign in the spring of 1952 with a B.S. degree. He then went to flight training at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAS_Pensacola" title="NAS Pensacola">NAS Pensacola</a> from October 1952 to February 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SMS-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" graduation from the Naval Academy, Lovell married his high school sweetheart, Marilyn Lillie Gerlach (born July 11, 1930), the daughter of Lillie (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" title="N&#233;e">n&#233;e</a> Nordrum)  and Carl Gerlach. The two had attended Juneau High School in Milwaukee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  While she was a college student, Gerlach transferred from <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_State_College_of_Milwaukee" title="Wisconsin State College of Milwaukee">Wisconsin State Teachers College</a> to <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> so she could be near him while he was training in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annapolis" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The couple have four children: Barbara, James, Susan, and Jeffrey.  The 1995 film <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i> portrayed the family\'s home life during the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a> mission of 1970 with actress <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Quinlan" title="Kathleen Quinlan">Kathleen Quinlan</a> being nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">supporting actress Oscar</a> for her performance as Marilyn Lovell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999 the Lovell family opened "Lovell\'s of Lake Forest", a fine dining restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Forest,_Illinois" title="Lake Forest, Illinois">Lake Forest, Illinois</a>. The restaurant displayed many artifacts from Lovell\'s time with NASA, as well as from the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i>. The restaurant was sold to son and executive chef James ("Jay") in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  The restaurant was put on the market for sale in February 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and closed in April 2015, with the property auctioned the same month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Navy">United States Navy</span></h2>\n<p>Lovell was designated a Naval Aviator on February 1, 1954. Upon completion of pilot training, he was assigned to <a href="/wiki/VFAW-3" title="VFAW-3">VC-3</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moffett_Field" title="Moffett Field">Moffett Field</a> near San Francisco, California. From 1954 to 1956 he flew <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F2H_Banshee" title="F2H Banshee">F2H-3 Banshee</a> night fighters. This included a WestPac deployment aboard the carrier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Shangri-La_(CVA-38)" title="USS Shangri-La (CVA-38)">USS&#160;<i>Shangri-La</i></a>, when the ship emerged from refit as only the second USN carrier with the new angled deck. Upon his return to shore duty, he was reassigned to provide pilot transition training for the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_F3H_Demon" title="McDonnell F3H Demon">F3H Demon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In January 1958, Lovell entered a six-month <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a> training course at what was then the Naval Air Test Center (now the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Test_Pilot_School" title="United States Naval Test Pilot School">U.S. Naval Test Pilot School</a>) at <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Patuxent_River" title="Naval Air Station Patuxent River">Naval Air Station Patuxent River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>. Two of his classmates were <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">Charles (Peter) Conrad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wally_Schirra" title="Wally Schirra">Wally Schirra</a>; Lovell graduated first in his class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostmoon_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lostmoon-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Later that year, Lovell, Conrad, and Schirra became three of 110 military test pilots selected as potential <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> candidates for <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>. Schirra went on to become one of the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Seven" title="Mercury Seven">Mercury Seven</a>, with Lovell and Conrad failing to make the cut for medical reasons: Lovell because of a temporarily high <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a> count in his blood<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostmoon_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-lostmoon-7">[7]</a></sup> and Conrad for refusing to take the second round of invasive medical tests.\n</p><p>At NAS Patuxent River, Lovell was assigned to Electronics Test (later Weapons Test), with his assigned <a href="/wiki/Aviator_call_sign" title="Aviator call sign">call sign</a> being "Shaky", a nickname given him by Conrad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He became <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-4_Phantom_II" title="McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II">F4H</a> program manager, during which time <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">John Young</a> served under him. In 1961 he received orders for <a href="/wiki/VFA-101" title="VFA-101">VF-101 "Detachment Alpha"</a> as an F4H instructor for the first East Coast squadron personnel assigned to that aircraft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_career">NASA career</span></h2>\n<p>In 1962 NASA needed a second group of astronauts for the <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> programs. Lovell applied a second time and was accepted into <a href="/wiki/NASA_Astronaut_Group_2" title="NASA Astronaut Group 2">NASA Astronaut Group 2</a>, "The New Nine", as was Conrad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gemini_program">Gemini program</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="1983" decoding="async" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg/220px-Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg/330px-Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg/440px-Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lovell_and_Aldrin_on_Deck_-_GPN-2000-001356.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> and Lovell on the deck of their recovery ship after Gemini 12.</div></div></div>\n<p>Lovell was selected as backup pilot for <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">Gemini 4</a>. This put him in position for his first space flight three missions later, as pilot of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a> with Command Pilot <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a> in December 1965. The flight\'s objective was to evaluate the effects on the crew and spacecraft from fourteen days in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This fourteen-day flight set an endurance record making 206 orbits. It was also the target vehicle for the first <a href="/wiki/Space_rendezvous" title="Space rendezvous">space rendezvous</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gemini_6A" title="Gemini 6A">Gemini 6A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa1-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lovell was later scheduled to be the backup command pilot of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">Gemini 10</a>. But after the <a href="/wiki/1966_NASA_T-38_crash" title="1966 NASA T-38 crash">deaths of the Gemini 9 prime crew</a> <a href="/wiki/Elliot_See" title="Elliot See">Elliot See</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bassett" title="Charles Bassett">Charles Bassett</a>, he replaced <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Stafford" title="Thomas P. Stafford">Thomas P. Stafford</a> as backup commander of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">Gemini 9A</a>. This again positioned Lovell for his second flight and first command, of <a href="/wiki/Gemini_12" title="Gemini 12">Gemini 12</a> in November 1966 with Pilot <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a>. This flight had three <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">extravehicular activities</a>, made 59 <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbits</a>, and achieved the fifth space rendezvous and fourth <a href="/wiki/Docking_and_berthing_of_spacecraft" title="Docking and berthing of spacecraft">space docking</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Agena_target_vehicle" title="Agena target vehicle">Agena target vehicle</a>. This mission was successful because it proved that humans can work effectively outside the spacecraft, paved the way for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> missions, and helped reach the goal of getting man on the Moon by the end of the decade.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_program">Apollo program</span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_8">Apollo 8</span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="562" data-file-width="750" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg/200px-Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg/300px-Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg/400px-Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_8_Lovell_at_Guidance_and_Navigation_station.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lovell at the Command Module Guidance and Navigation station during the Apollo 8 mission.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a></div>\n<p>Lovell was originally chosen as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command Module</a> pilot (CMP) on the backup crew for Apollo 9 along with <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> as commander and Buzz Aldrin as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_module" title="Lunar module">lunar module</a> pilot (LMP). Apollo 9 was planned as a high-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">apogee</a> Earth orbital test of the Lunar Module (LM). Lovell later replaced <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(astronaut)" title="Michael Collins (astronaut)">Michael Collins</a> as CMP on the Apollo 9 prime crew when Collins needed to have surgery for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_spur" title="Bone spur">bone spur</a> on his spine. This reunited Lovell with his Gemini 7 commander Frank Borman, and LM pilot <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Construction delays of the first manned LM prevented it from being ready in time to fly on Apollo 8, planned as a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> test. It was decided to swap the Apollo 8 and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">Apollo 9</a> prime and backup crews in the flight schedule so that the crew trained for the low-orbit test could fly it as Apollo 9, when the LM would be ready. A lunar orbital flight, now Apollo 8 replaced the original Apollo 9 medium Earth orbit test. Borman, Lovell and Anders were launched on December 21, 1968, becoming the first men to travel to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-augsep1968_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-augsep1968-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As CM Pilot, Lovell served as the navigator, using the spacecraft\'s built-in <a href="/wiki/Sextant" title="Sextant">sextant</a> to determine its position by measuring star positions. This information was then used to calculate required mid-course corrections. The craft entered lunar orbit on Christmas Eve and made a total of ten orbits, most of them circular at an altitude of approximately 70 miles (110&#160;km) for a total of twenty hours. They broadcast black-and-white television pictures of the lunar surface back to Earth. Lovell took his turn with Borman and Anders in reading a passage from the Biblical creation story in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8_Genesis_reading" title="Apollo 8 Genesis reading">Book of Genesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>They began their return to Earth on Christmas Day with a rocket burn made on the Moon\'s far side, out of radio contact with Earth. (For this reason, the lunar orbit insertion and trans-Earth injection burns were the two most tense moments of this first lunar mission.) When contact was re-established, Lovell was the first to announce the good news, "Please be informed, there is a <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">Santa Claus</a>." The crew splashed down safely on Earth on December 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_13">Apollo 13</span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Lovell_at_LC-39.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Lovell_newspaper.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2596" data-file-width="3998" decoding="async" height="130" Iwo Jima (LPH-2)">USS <i>Iwo Jima</i></a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">Apollo 13</a></div>\n<p>Lovell was backup commander of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> and was scheduled to command <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">Apollo 14</a>. Instead, he and his crew swapped missions with the crew of Apollo 13, as it was felt the commander of the other crew, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a>, needed more time to train after having been grounded for a long period by an ear problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slayton_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-slayton-24">[24]</a></sup> Lovell lifted off aboard Apollo 13 on April 11, 1970, with CM Pilot <a href="/wiki/Jack_Swigert" title="Jack Swigert">Jack Swigert</a> and LM Pilot <a href="/wiki/Fred_Haise" title="Fred Haise">Fred Haise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He and Haise were to land on the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During a routine <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">cryogenic oxygen</a> tank stir in transit to the Moon, a fire started inside an oxygen tank. The most probable cause determined by NASA was damaged electrical insulation on wiring that created a spark that started the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-27">[27]</a></sup> Liquid oxygen rapidly turned into a high-pressure gas, which burst the tank and caused the leak of a second oxygen tank. In just over two hours, all on-board oxygen was lost, disabling the hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> that provided electrical power to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command/Service Module</a> <i>Odyssey</i>. This required an immediate abort of the Moon landing mission; the sole objective now was to safely return the crew to Earth.\n</p><p>Apollo 13 was the second mission not to use a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">free return trajectory</a>, so that they could explore the western lunar regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008298_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008298-28">[28]</a></sup> Using the LM as a "life boat" providing battery power, oxygen, and propulsion, Lovell and his crew re-established the free return trajectory that they had left, and swung around the Moon to return home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008304&#8211;305_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008304&#8211;305-29">[29]</a></sup> Based on the flight controllers\' calculations made on Earth, Lovell had to adjust the course twice by manually controlling the Lunar Module\'s thrusters and engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008314,_322_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008314,_322-30">[30]</a></sup> Apollo 13 returned safely to Earth on April 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008611_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChaikin2008611-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lovell is one of only three men to travel to the Moon twice, but unlike <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">John Young</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a>, he never walked on it. He accrued over 715 hours, and had seen a total of 269 sunrises from space, on his Gemini and Apollo flights. This was a personal record that stood until the <a href="/wiki/Skylab_3" title="Skylab 3">Skylab 3</a> mission in July through September 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Apollo 13\'s flight trajectory gives Lovell, Haise, and Swigert the record for the farthest distance that humans have ever traveled from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Lovell.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2248" data-file-width="3378" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jim_Lovell.jpg/220px-Jim_Lovell.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jim_Lovell.jpg/330px-Jim_Lovell.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jim_Lovell.jpg/440px-Jim_Lovell.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Lovell.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lovell speaking at the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Science_Festival" title="North Carolina Science Festival">North Carolina Science Festival</a> in April 2017</div></div></div>\n<p>Lovell retired from the Navy and the space program in 1973 and went to work at the Bay-Houston Towing Company in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, becoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CEO" title="CEO">CEO</a> in 1975. He became president of Fisk Telephone Systems in 1977, and later worked for <a href="/wiki/Centel" title="Centel">Centel</a>, retiring as an executive vice president on January 1, 1991. Lovell, a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Eagle_Scout_Award" title="Distinguished Eagle Scout Award">Distinguished Eagle Scout Award</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desalista_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-desalista-36">[36]</a></sup> later served as the President of the <a href="/wiki/National_Eagle_Scout_Association" title="National Eagle Scout Association">National Eagle Scout Association</a> in the mid-1990s. He was also recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts of America</a> with their prestigious <a href="/wiki/Silver_Buffalo_Award" title="Silver Buffalo Award">Silver Buffalo Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Kluger" title="Jeffrey Kluger">Jeffrey Kluger</a>, Lovell wrote a book about the Apollo 13 mission, <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_Moon" title="Lost Moon">Lost Moon: The Perilous Voyage of Apollo 13</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostmoon_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-lostmoon-7">[7]</a></sup> It was the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a> movie <i>Apollo 13</i>. Lovell\'s first impression on being approached about the film was that <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a> would be a good choice to portray him, given the physical resemblance,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVDLovellCommentary_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVDLovellCommentary-38">[38]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> was cast in the role. In order to prepare, Hanks visited Lovell and his wife at their home in Texas and even flew with Lovell in his private airplane.\n</p><p>In the film, Lovell has a cameo as the captain of the <a href="/wiki/USS_Iwo_Jima_(LPH-2)" title="USS Iwo Jima (LPH-2)">USS <i>Iwo Jima</i></a>, the naval vessel that led the operation to recover the Apollo 13 astronauts after their successful <a href="/wiki/Splashdown" title="Splashdown">splashdown</a>. Lovell can be seen as the naval officer shaking Hanks\' hand, as Hanks speaks in <a href="/wiki/Voice-over" title="Voice-over">voice-over</a>, in the scene where the astronauts come aboard the <i>Iwo Jima</i>. Filmmakers initially offered to make Lovell\'s character an admiral aboard the ship (presumably Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Donald_C._Davis" title="Donald C. Davis">Donald C. Davis</a>, Commander Task Force 130 (CTF 130), who was the senior officer aboard and welcomed them home). However, Lovell said "I retired as a Captain and a Captain I will be." He was cast as the ship\'s skipper, Captain Leland E. Kirkemo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Along with his wife Marilyn, who also has a cameo in the film, he provided a commentary track on both the single disc and the two-disc special edition DVD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He has served on the Board of Directors for several organizations, including <a href="/wiki/Federal_Signal_Corporation" title="Federal Signal Corporation">Federal Signal Corporation</a> in Chicago (1984&#8211;2003), <a href="/wiki/Astronautics_Corporation_of_America" title="Astronautics Corporation of America">Astronautics Corporation of America</a> in his hometown of Milwaukee (1990&#8211;1999), and <a href="/wiki/Centel" title="Centel">Centel Corporation</a> in Chicago (1987&#8211;1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lovell also visits colleges and universities where he gives speeches on his experiences as an astronaut and businessman. He strongly urges students to get involved in science and the space program, and he credits NASA in the 1960s with bringing much of the country together for a common goal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span></h2>\n<p>A small crater on the <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">far side of the Moon</a> is named <i><a href="/wiki/Lovell_(crater)" title="Lovell (crater)">Lovell</a></i> in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>North James Lovell Street</i> is the stretch of 7th Street between W. State Street and W. Clybourn Street in downtown <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Discovery_World" title="Discovery World">Discovery World</a> in Milwaukee was also named <i>The James Lovell Museum of Science, Economics and Technology</i>. It was located on James Lovell St., also named for Lovell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_James_A._Lovell_Federal_Health_Care_Center" title="Captain James A. id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span></h2>\n<p>About a month after the return to Earth of Apollo 13, Lovell and his crewmates, Fred Haise and Jack Swigert, appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show" title="The Tonight Show">The Tonight Show</a></i> with host <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Carson" title="Johnny Carson">Johnny Carson</a>. The introduction of this segment of the show is featured on <i>Lost Moon: The Triumph of Apollo 13</i> on the single disc DVD version of the movie. In 1976, Lovell made a cameo appearance in the Nicolas Roeg movie <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Fell_to_Earth_(film)" title="The Man Who Fell to Earth (film)">The Man Who Fell to Earth</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1995, actor <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> portrayed Lovell in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_13_(film)" title="Apollo 13 (film)">Apollo 13</a></i>, based on Lovell\'s book <i>Lost Moon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Lovell himself makes a cameo in this movie, playing the captain of the USS <i>Iwo Jima</i> at the end of the film. In 1998, actor <a href="/wiki/Tim_Daly" title="Tim Daly">Tim Daly</a> portrayed Lovell in portions of the <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon_(miniseries)" title="From the Earth to the Moon (miniseries)">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The film depicts Lovell during his missions aboard Gemini 12, Apollo 8, and Apollo 13, though he is not seen on screen during the latter mission.\n</p><p>Lovell is one of the astronauts featured in the book and documentaries <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Shadow_of_the_Moon_(film)" title="In the Shadow of the Moon (film)">In the Shadow of the Moon</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/When_We_Left_Earth" title="When We Left Earth">When We Left Earth</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>On November 13, 2008, Lovell and fellow Apollo 8 crew members Frank Borman and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Anders" title="Bill Anders">Bill Anders</a> appeared on the <a href="/wiki/NASA_TV" title="NASA TV">NASA TV</a> channel to discuss the Apollo 8 mission. The three former astronauts later appeared together for a panel discussion centering on Apollo 8 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Baines_Johnson_Library" title="Lyndon Baines Johnson Library">Lyndon Baines Johnson Library</a> on April 23, 2009, a discussion that was videotaped by <a href="/wiki/C-SPAN" title="C-SPAN">C-SPAN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On July 20, 2014, the 45th anniversary of Apollo 11\'s landing on the Moon, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBS_Sunday_Morning" title="CBS Sunday Morning">CBS Sunday Morning</a></i> correspondent <a href="/wiki/Lee_Cowan" title="Lee Cowan">Lee Cowan</a> and his crew did a piece called "Astronaut wives: The other space pioneers" featuring James Lovell and his wife Marilyn. They celebrated their 62nd wedding anniversary in June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, actor <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Schreiber" title="Pablo Schreiber">Pablo Schreiber</a> portrayed Lovell in the film <i><a href="/wiki/First_Man_(film)" title="First Man (film)">First Man</a></i>.<sup class="reference"
