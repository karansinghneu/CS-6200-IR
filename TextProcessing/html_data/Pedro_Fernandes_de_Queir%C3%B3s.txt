href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Pedro Fern&#225;ndez de Quir&#243;s</i>) (1563&#8211;1614) was a <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> navigator in the service of Spain best known for his involvement with Spanish voyages of discovery in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, in particular the 1595&#8211;1596 voyage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira" title="Alvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira">Alvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira</a>, and for leading a 1605&#8211;1606 expedition which crossed the Pacific in search of <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Queir&#243;s (or Quir&#243;s as he signed) was born in <a href="/wiki/%C3%89vora" title="&#201;vora">&#201;vora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> in 1563.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLNSW_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SLNSW-2">[2]</a></sup> As the Portuguese and Spanish monarchies had been unified under the king of Spain in 1580 (following the vacancy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_throne" title="Portuguese throne">Portuguese throne</a>, which lasted for sixty years, until 1640, when the Portuguese monarchy was restored), Queir&#243;s entered <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> service as a young man and became an experienced seaman and navigator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> In April 1595 he joined <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira" title="&#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira">&#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira</a> on his voyage to colonise the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, serving as chief pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> After Menda&#241;a&#8217;s death in October 1595 he is credited with taking command and saving the only remaining ship of the expedition, arriving in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> in February 1596.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Isabel_Barreto" title="Isabel Barreto">Isabel Barreto</a>, Menda&#241;a\'s young wife, is often considered to be the cause of the voyage\'s low morale, and the eventual failure of the mission. She lived a life of luxury in the grand cabin while the crew were fed on meagre rotting rations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> The story is told in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Islands_of_Unwisdom" title="The Islands of Unwisdom">The Islands of Unwisdom</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Historical_fiction" title="Historical fiction">historic novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">Robert Graves</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_search_for_Terra_Australis">The search for Terra Australis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The search for Terra Australis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Queir&#243;s spent eighteen months in the Philippines, but returned to Spain in 1598, via <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Upon his return to Spain, he petitioned King <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> to support another voyage into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, hoping to find a large southern continent to claim for Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> A devout Catholic, Queir&#243;s also visited <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in 1600, where he obtained the support of the Pope, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VIII" title="Clement VIII">Clement VIII</a>, for further explorations. He greatly impressed the Spanish Ambassador in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, the Duke of Sesa, who described him as a &#8220;man of good judgement, experienced in his profession, hard working, quiet and disinterested.&#8221; <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> While in Rome Queir&#243;s also first wrote his <i>Treatise on Navigation</i> as a letter to the king, further reinforcing his reputation as a navigator, and invented two navigational tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>  In March 1603 Queir&#243;s was finally authorized to return to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> to establish another expedition, with the intention of finding <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>, the mythical "great south land," and claiming it for <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and the Church. Queir&#243;s was shipwrecked in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, but made it to Peru by March 1605.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Queir&#243;s\'s party of 160 men on three ships, <i>San Pedro y San Pablo</i> (150 tons),  <i>San Pedro</i>  (120 tons) and the tender (or launch) <i>Los Tres Reyes</i> left <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a> on 21 December 1605.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 1606 the expedition discovered <a href="/wiki/Henderson_Island_(Pitcairn_Islands)" title="Henderson Island (Pitcairn Islands)">Henderson Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ducie_Island" title="Ducie Island">Ducie Island</a>. It discovered the <a href="/wiki/Makin_(islands)" title="Makin (islands)">Buen Viaje Islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Butaritari" title="Butaritari">Butaritari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Makin_(islands)" title="Makin (islands)">Makin</a>) in the present-day island nation of <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEMaude59_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HEMaude59-4">[4]</a></sup> It is also probable that his expedition sighted <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> and other islands in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuamotu" title="Tuamotu">Tuamotu</a> archipelago. Queir&#243;s narrowly missed the <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">Marquesas</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, thanks to a change in his planned itinerary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 1606 the expedition reached the islands later called the <a href="/wiki/New_Hebrides" title="New Hebrides">New Hebrides</a> and now the independent nation of <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>. Queir&#243;s landed on a large island which he took to be part of the southern continent, and named it <i><b>Australia del Espiritu Santo</b></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In his printed memorials, notably the Eighth (which was published in Italy, Holland, France, Germany and England), this was altered to <i>Austrialia del Espiritu Santo</i> (The Australian Land of the Holy Spirit), a pun on "Austria", to flatter King <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a>, who was of the House of Austria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The island is still called <a href="/wiki/Espiritu_Santo" title="Espiritu Santo">Espiritu Santo</a>. Here he stated his intention to establish a colony, to be called Nova Jerusalem. He seems to have identified <i>Australia/Austrialia del Espiritu Santo</i> with the huge northward extension of the Austral continent joining it to New Guinea, as depicted in maps like those of <a href="/wiki/Gerard_de_Jode" title="Gerard de Jode">Gerard de Jode</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Plancius" title="Petrus Plancius">Petrus Plancius</a>. For, as he said in his Tenth Memorial (page 5): &#8220;It should be noted that New Guinea is the top end of the Austral Land of which I treat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memorial5_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memorial5-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Queir&#243;s\'s religious fervour found expression with the founding of a new Order of Chivalry, the Knights of the Holy Ghost. The Order&#8217;s purpose was to protect the new colony.  However, within weeks the idea of a colony was abandoned due to the hostility of the Ni-Vanuatu and to disagreements among the crew.\n</p><p>After six weeks Queir&#243;s\'s ships put to sea to explore the coastline. On the night of June 11, 1606 Queir&#243;s in the <i>San Pedro y San Pablo</i> became separated from the other ships in bad weather and was unable (or so he later said) to return to safe anchorage at <a href="/wiki/Espiritu_Santo" title="Espiritu Santo">Espiritu Santo</a>. He then sailed to <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, where he arrived in November 1606. In the <a class="external text" href="http://acmssearch.sl.nsw.gov.au/search/itemDetailPaged.cgi?itemID=442273" rel="nofollow">account of Diego de Prado y Tovar</a>,  which is highly critical of Queir&#243;s, mutiny and poor leadership is given as the reason for Queir&#243;s\'s disappearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two weeks later, his second-in-command, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_V%C3%A1ez_de_Torres" title="Luis V&#225;ez de Torres">Luis V&#225;ez de Torres</a>, after searching in vain for Queir&#243;s and assuming his ship was wrecked, left <a href="/wiki/Espiritu_Santo" title="Espiritu Santo">Espiritu Santo</a>. Torres successfully reached <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, the center of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Spanish East Indies</a> in May 1607, after charting the southern coastline of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> on the way and in doing so sailing through the <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait" title="Torres Strait">strait</a> that now bears his name, between Australia and New Guinea. Torres was unaware of his proximity to Australia\'s northern coast, just over the horizon from his route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Pedro de Queir&#243;s returned to Madrid in 1607. Regarded as a crank, he spent the next seven years in poverty, writing numerous accounts of his voyage and begging King <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> for money for a new voyage, sending the king more than 65 letters over a seven-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> He was finally despatched to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> with letters of support, but the king had no real intention of funding another expedition, as the royal council feared that Spain could not afford new discoveries in the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> Queir&#243;s died on the way, in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, in 1614. He had married Dona Ana Chacon of <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> in 1589; the couple had one son and one daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Accounts_of_Queir.C3.B3s.27s_voyage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounts_of_Queir&#243;s\'s_voyage">Accounts of Queir&#243;s\'s voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accounts of Queir&#243;s\'s voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are a number of documents describing the Queir&#243;s &#8211; Torres voyages still in existence. Most significant are\n</p>\n<ul><li>Quir&#243;s\' many subsequent Memorials to the King <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> regarding the voyage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-10">[10]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Torres brief account to the king (1607),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Prado y Tobar narrative <i>Relacion sumaria del del descubrimto. que enpeco pero fernandez de quiros</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> which is a valuable supplement to the letter of Torres written to the King of Spain on 12 July, 1607. Prado\'s narrative is the only record, apart from the letter, to cover the voyage to Manila <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Four charts of New Guinea <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Juan Luis Arias de Loyola&#8217;s memorial to King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a>  (written about 1630 and based on discussions between Queir&#243;s and Loyola ) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Most documents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_V%C3%A1ez_de_Torres" title="Luis V&#225;ez de Torres">Luis V&#225;ez de Torres</a>\'s discoveries were not published but filed away in Spanish archives, including Prado&#8217;s lengthy account and accompanying charts.\n</p><p>Some time between 1762 and 1765, written accounts of the Torres expedition were seen by British Admiralty Hydrographer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dalrymple" title="Alexander Dalrymple">Alexander Dalrymple</a>. Dalrymple provided a sketch map which included the Queir&#243;s -Torres voyages to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a> who undoubtedly passed this information to <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Queir&#243;s sent at least 50,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SLNSW_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SLNSW-2">[2]</a></sup> possibly 65,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HOAO_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-HOAO-18">[18]</a></sup> memorials to the King between 1607 and 1614. The <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Archbishop_of_Sydney" title="Anglican Archbishop of Sydney">Archbishop of Sydney</a> from 1884 to 1911, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Francis_Moran" title="Patrick Francis Moran">Patrick Francis Moran</a>, asserted this to be a fact, and it was taught in Catholic schools for many years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> He claimed that the real site of Queir&#243;s\'s New Jerusalem was near <a href="/wiki/Gladstone,_Queensland" title="Gladstone, Queensland">Gladstone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, supported by elements of Queir&#243;s\'s description of the land he had discovered, such as his assertion that "its length is as much as all Europe and Asia Minor as far as the Caspian and Persia, with all the islands of the Mediterranean and the ocean which encompasses, including the two islands of England and Ireland.
