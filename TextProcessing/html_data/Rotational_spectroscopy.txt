nucleus.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Rotational spectroscopy</b> is concerned with the measurement of the energies of transitions between quantized rotational states of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase" title="Gas phase">gas phase</a>. The spectra of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a> molecules can be measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_(optics)" title="Absorption (optics)">absorption</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Emission (electromagnetic radiation)">emission</a> by <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> spectroscopy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> or by <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">far infrared</a> spectroscopy. The rotational spectra of non-polar molecules cannot be observed by those methods, but can be observed and measured by <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">Raman spectroscopy</a>. Rotational spectroscopy is sometimes referred to as <i>pure</i> rotational spectroscopy to distinguish it from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational-vibrational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational-vibrational spectroscopy">rotational-vibrational spectroscopy</a> where changes in rotational energy occur together with changes in vibrational energy, and also from ro-vibronic spectroscopy (or just <a href="/wiki/Vibronic_spectroscopy" title="Vibronic spectroscopy">vibronic spectroscopy</a>) where rotational, vibrational and electronic energy changes occur simultaneously.\n</p><p>For rotational spectroscopy, molecules are classified according to symmetry into spherical top, linear and symmetric top; analytical expressions can be derived for the rotational energy terms of these molecules. Analytical expressions can be derived for the fourth category, asymmetric top, for rotational levels up to J=3, but higher <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy levels</a> need to be determined using numerical methods. The rotational energies are derived theoretically by considering the molecules to be <a href="/wiki/Rigid_rotor" title="Rigid rotor">rigid rotors</a> and then applying extra terms to account for <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">centrifugal distortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fine_structure" title="Fine structure">fine structure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">hyperfine structure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis coupling</a>. Fitting the spectra to the theoretical expressions gives numerical values of the angular <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moments of inertia</a> from which very precise values of molecular bond lengths and angles can be derived in favorable cases. In the presence of an electrostatic field there is <a href="/wiki/Stark_effect" title="Stark effect">Stark splitting</a> which allows molecular <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">electric dipole moments</a> to be determined.\n</p><p>An important application of rotational spectroscopy is in exploration of the chemical composition of the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> using <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Rotational spectroscopy has primarily been used to investigate fundamental aspects of molecular physics. It is a uniquely precise tool for the determination of molecular structure in gas phase molecules.  It can be used to establish barriers to internal rotation such as that associated with the rotation of the <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> group relative to the <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>Cl</span> group in <a href="/wiki/Chlorotoluene" title="Chlorotoluene">chlorotoluene</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span>Cl</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> When fine or hyperfine structure can be observed, the technique also provides information on the electronic structures of molecules. Much of current understanding of the nature of weak molecular interactions such as <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halogen_bond" title="Halogen bond">halogen</a> bonds has been established through rotational spectroscopy. In connection with <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, the technique has a key role in exploration of the chemical composition of the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. Microwave transitions are measured in the laboratory and matched\nto emissions from the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></a></span> was the first stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyatomic" title="Polyatomic">polyatomic</a> molecule to be identified in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The measurement of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_monoxide" title="Chlorine monoxide">chlorine monoxide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup>  is important for <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">atmospheric chemistry</a>. Current projects in astrochemistry involve both laboratory microwave spectroscopy and observations made using modern radiotelescopes such as the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">Atacama Large Millimetre Array</a> (ALMA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A molecule in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase" title="Gas phase">gas phase</a> is free to rotate relative to a set of mutually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal" title="Orthogonal">orthogonal</a> axes of fixed orientation in space, centered on the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> of the molecule. Free rotation is not possible for molecules in liquid or solid phases due to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a>. Rotation about each unique axis is associated with a set of quantized energy levels dependent on the moment of inertia about that axis and a quantum number. Thus, for linear molecules the energy levels are described by a single moment of inertia and a single quantum number, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle J}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>J</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle J}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="J" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.471ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, which defines the magnitude of the rotational angular momentum.\n</p><p>For nonlinear molecules which are symmetric rotors (or symmetric tops - see next section), there are two moments of inertia and the energy also depends on a second rotational quantum number, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle K}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>K</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle K}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="K" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:2.066ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, which defines the vector component of rotational angular momentum along the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">principal symmetry axis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> Analysis of spectroscopic data with the expressions detailed below results in quantitative determination of the value(s) of the moment(s) of inertia. From these precise values of the molecular structure and dimensions may be obtained.\n</p><p>For a linear molecule, analysis of the rotational spectrum provides values for the <a href="/wiki/Rigid_rotor#Quantum_mechanical_linear_rigid_rotor" title="Rigid rotor">rotational constant</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[notes 2]</a></sup> and the moment of inertia of the molecule, and, knowing the atomic masses, can be used to determine the <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond length</a> directly. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic molecules</a> this process is straightforward. For linear molecules with more than two atoms it is necessary to measure the spectra of two or more <a href="/wiki/Isotopologue" title="Isotopologue">isotopologues</a>, such as <sup>16</sup>O<sup>12</sup>C<sup>32</sup>S and <sup>16</sup>O<sup>12</sup>C<sup>34</sup>S. This allows a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equations" title="Simultaneous equations">simultaneous equations</a> to be set up and solved for the <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond lengths</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[notes 3]</a></sup> It should be noted that a bond length obtained in this way is slightly different from the equilibrium bond length. This is because there is <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a> in the vibrational ground state, to which the rotational states refer, whereas the equilibrium bond length is at the minimum in the potential energy curve. The relation between the rotational constants is given by\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle B_{v}=B-\\alpha (v+{1 \\over 2})}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>B</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>v</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mi>B</mi>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mi>&#945;<!-- &#945; --></mi>\n<mo stretchy="false">(</mo>\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo stretchy="false">)</mo>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle B_{v}=B-\\alpha (v+{1 \\over 2})}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="B_v=B-\\alpha(v+{1\\over 2})" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40b19cf838c2724665522caf373b00627c474f84" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:19.76ex; height:5.176ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where v is a vibrational quantum number and &#945; is a vibration-rotation interaction constant which can be calculated if the B values for two different vibrational states can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For other molecules, if the spectra can be resolved and individual transitions assigned both <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond lengths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">bond angles</a> can be deduced. When this is not possible, as with most asymmetric tops, all that can be done is to fit the spectra to three moments of inertia calculated from an assumed molecular structure. By varying the molecular structure the fit can be improved, giving a qualitative estimate of the structure. Isotopic substitution is invaluable when using this approach to the determination of molecular structure.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_molecular_rotors">Classification of molecular rotors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification of molecular rotors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> the free rotation of a molecule is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_quantization" title="Angular momentum quantization">quantized</a>, so that the <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">rotational energy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> can take only certain fixed values, which are related simply to the <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moment of inertia</a>, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle I}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>I</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle I}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="I" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.172ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, of the molecule. far-infrared spectra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microwave and far-infrared spectra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Transitions between rotational states can be observed in molecules with a permanent <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">electric dipole moment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[notes 4]</a></sup> A consequence of this rule is that no microwave spectrum can be observed for centrosymmetric linear molecules such as <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen" title="Dinitrogen">dinitrogen</a>) or HCCH (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethyne" title="Ethyne">ethyne</a>), which are non-polar. Tetrahedral molecules such as <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (<a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>), which have both a zero dipole moment and isotropic polarizability, would not have a pure rotation spectrum but for the effect of centrifugal distortion; when the molecule rotates about a 3-fold symmetry axis a small dipole moment is created, allowing a weak rotation spectrum to be observed by microwave spectroscopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With symmetric tops, the selection rule for electric-dipole-allowed pure rotation transitions is &#916;<i>K</i> = 0, &#916;<i>J</i> = &#177;1. Since these transitions are due to absorption (or emission) of a single photon with a spin of one, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">conservation of angular momentum</a> implies that the molecular angular momentum can change by at most one unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[11]</a></sup> Moreover, the quantum number <i>K</i> is limited to have values between and including +<i>J</i> to -<i>J</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Raman_spectra">Raman spectra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Raman spectra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>For <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">Raman spectra</a> the molecules undergo transitions in which an <i>incident</i> photon is absorbed and another <i>scattered</i> photon is emitted. The general selection rule for such a transition to be allowed is that the molecular <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> must be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anisotropic" title="Anisotropic">anisotropic</a>, which means that it is not the same in all directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[13]</a></sup> Polarizability is a 3-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensor</a> that can be represented as an ellipsoid. The polarizability ellipsoid of spherical top molecules is in fact spherical so those molecules show no rotational Raman spectrum. For all other molecules both <a href="/wiki/Stokes_line" title="Stokes line">Stokes</a> and anti-Stokes lines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[notes 5]</a></sup> can be observed and they have similar intensities due to the fact that many rotational states are thermally populated. The selection rule for linear molecules is &#916;J = 0, &#177;2. The reason for the values &#177;2 is that the polarizability returns to the same value twice during a rotation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[14]</a></sup> The value &#916;J = 0 does not correspond to a molecular transition but rather to <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> in which the incident photon merely changes direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra-20">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The selection rule for symmetric top molecules is\n</p>\n<dl><dd>&#916;<i>K</i> = 0</dd>\n<dd>If <i>K</i> = 0, then &#916;<i>J</i> = &#177;2</dd>\n<dd>If <i>K</i> &#8800; 0, then &#916;<i>J</i> = 0, &#177;1, &#177;2</dd></dl>\n<p>Transitions with &#916;<i>J</i> = +1 are said to belong to the <i>R</i> series, whereas transitions with &#916;<i>J</i> = +2 belong to an <i>S</i> series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra-20">[15]</a></sup> Since Raman transitions involve two photons, it is possible for the molecular angular momentum to change by two units.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The units used for rotational constants depend on the type of measurement. As the moment of inertia is higher when a vibration is excited, the rotational constants (<i>B</i>) decrease. Consequently, the rotation frequencies in each vibration state are different from each other. This can give rise to "satellite" lines in the rotational spectrum. An example is provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyanodiacetylene" title="Cyanodiacetylene">cyanodiacetylene</a>, H&#8722;C&#8801;C&#8722;C&#8801;C&#8722;C&#8801;N.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Further, there is a <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_force" title="Fictitious force">fictitious force</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis coupling</a>, between the vibrational motion of the nuclei in the rotating (non-inertial) frame. However, as long as the vibrational quantum number does not change (i.e., the molecule is in only one state of vibration), the effect of vibration on rotation is not important, because the time for vibration is much shorter than the time required for rotation. The Coriolis coupling is often negligible, too, if one is interested in low vibrational and rotational quantum numbers only.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_rotation_on_vibrational_spectra">Effect of rotation on vibrational spectra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect of rotation on vibrational spectra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational-vibrational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational-vibrational spectroscopy">Rotational-vibrational spectroscopy</a></div>\n<p>Historically, the theory of rotational energy levels was developed to account for observations of vibration-rotation spectra of gases in <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a>, which was used before microwave spectroscopy had become practical. A pure rotational spectrum cannot be observed by absorption or emission spectroscopy because there is no permanent dipole moment whose rotation can be accelerated by the electric field of an incident photon. Also the polarizability is isotropic, so that pure rotational transitions cannot be observed by Raman spectroscopy either. Nevertheless, rotational constants can be obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rovibrational_coupling" title="Rovibrational coupling">ro-vibrational spectroscopy</a>. This occurs when a molecule is polar in the vibrationally excited state. For example, the molecule <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is a symmetric top but the asymmetric C-H stretching band shows rotational fine structure in the infrared spectrum, illustrated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rovibrational_coupling" title="Rovibrational coupling">rovibrational coupling</a>. This spectrum is also interesting because it shows clear evidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis coupling</a> in the asymmetric structure of the band.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_molecules">Linear molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Linear molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rotational_spectrum_example.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="502" data-file-width="409" decoding="async" height="368" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Rotational_spectrum_example.png/300px-Rotational_spectrum_example.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/Rotational_spectrum_example.png 1.5x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rotational_spectrum_example.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Energy levels and line positions calculated in the rigid rotor approximation</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rigid_rotor" title="Rigid rotor">rigid rotor</a> is a good starting point from which to construct a model of a rotating molecule. It is assumed that component atoms are <a href="/wiki/Point_particle" title="Point particle">point masses</a> connected by rigid bonds. A linear molecule lies on a single axis and each atom moves on the surface of a sphere around the centre of mass. J defines the magnitude of the rotational angular momentum, and M its component about an axis fixed in space, such as an external electric or magnetic field. In the absence of external fields, the energy depends only on J. Since there are three independent moments of inertia, there are two other independent quantum numbers to consider, but the term values for an asymmetric rotor cannot be derived in closed form. They are obtained by individual <a href="/wiki/Diagonalizable_matrix#Diagonalization" title="Diagonalizable matrix">matrix diagonalization</a> for each <i>J</i> value. Formulae are available for molecules whose shape approximates to that of a symmetric top.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The water molecule is an important example of an asymmetric top. It has an intense pure rotation spectrum in the far infrared region, below about 200&#160;cm<sup>&#8722;1</sup>. For this reason far infrared spectrometers have to be freed of atmospheric water vapour either by purging with a dry gas or by evacuation. The spectrum has been analyzed in detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quadrupole_splitting">Quadrupole splitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Quadrupole splitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When a nucleus has a <a href="/wiki/Spin_quantum_number" title="Spin quantum number">spin quantum number</a>, <i>I</i>, greater than 1/2 it has a <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">quadrupole</a> moment. In that case, coupling of nuclear spin angular momentum with rotational angular momentum causes splitting of the rotational energy levels. If the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_angular_momentum" title="Total angular momentum">quantum number <i>J</i></a> of a rotational level is greater than <i>I</i>, 2<i>I</i>+1 levels are produced; but if <i>J</i> is less than <i>I</i>, 2<i>J</i>+1 levels result. The effect is one type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperfine_splitting" title="Hyperfine splitting">hyperfine splitting</a>. For example, with <sup>14</sup>N (<i>I</i> = 1) in HCN, all levels with J &gt; 0 are split into 3. The energies of the sub-levels are proportional to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrupole_moment" title="Quadrupole moment">nuclear quadrupole moment</a> and a function of <i>F</i> and <i>J</i>. where <i>F</i> = <i>J</i>+<i>I</i>, <i>J</i>+<i>I</i>-1, ..., |<i>J</i>-<i>I</i>|. Thus, observation of nuclear quadrupole splitting permits the magnitude of the nuclear quadrupole moment to be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[28]</a></sup>\nThis is an alternative method to the use of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_quadrupole_resonance" title="Nuclear quadrupole resonance">nuclear quadrupole resonance</a> spectroscopy. The selection rule for rotational transitions becomes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\Delta J=\\pm 1,\\Delta F=0,\\pm 1}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi mathvariant="normal">&#916;<!-- &#916; --></mi>\n<mi>J</mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mo>&#177;<!-- &#177; --></mo>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mo>,</mo>\n<mi mathvariant="normal">&#916;<!-- &#916; --></mi>\n<mi>F</mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mn>0</mn>\n<mo>,</mo>\n<mo>&#177;<!-- &#177; --></mo>\n<mn>1</mn>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\Delta J=\\pm 1,\\Delta F=0,\\pm 1}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\Delta J= \\pm 1, \\Delta F = 0, \\pm 1 " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e2e79aafd0b905f859682ec6013d2bcc7d701c" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:22.452ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stark_and_Zeeman_effects">Stark and Zeeman effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Stark and Zeeman effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the presence of a static external <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> the 2<i>J</i>+1 degeneracy of each rotational state is partly removed, an instance of a <a href="/wiki/Stark_effect" title="Stark effect">Stark effect</a>. For example, in linear molecules each energy level is split into <i>J</i>+1 components. The extent of splitting depends on the square of the electric field strength and the square of the dipole moment of the molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[30]</a></sup> In principle this provides a means to determine the value of the molecular dipole moment with high precision. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide" title="Carbonyl sulfide">carbonyl sulfide</a>, OCS, with &#956; = 0.71521 &#177; 0.00020 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debye_(unit)" title="Debye (unit)">Debye</a>. However, because the splitting depends on &#956;<sup>2</sup>, the orientation of the dipole must be deduced from quantum mechanical considerations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A similar removal of degeneracy will occur when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> molecule is placed in a magnetic field, an instance of the <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">Zeeman effect</a>. Most species which can be observed in the gaseous state are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a> . Exceptions are odd-electron molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, NO, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, some <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_oxide" title="Chlorine oxide">chlorine oxides</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical" title="Hydroxyl radical">hydroxyl radical</a>. The Zeeman effect has been observed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxygen" title="Dioxygen">dioxygen</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotational_Raman_spectroscopy">Rotational Raman spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Rotational Raman spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Molecular rotational transitions can also be observed by <a href="/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy" title="Raman spectroscopy">Raman spectroscopy</a>. Rotational transitions are  Raman-allowed for any molecule with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anisotropic" title="Anisotropic">anisotropic</a> <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> which includes all molecules except for spherical tops. This means that rotational transitions of molecules with no permanent dipole moment, which cannot be observed in absorption or emission, can be observed, by scattering,  in Raman spectroscopy. Very high resolution Raman spectra can be obtained by adapting a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_infrared_spectroscopy" title="Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy">Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer</a>. An example is the spectrum of <span class="chemf nowrap"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.5em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">15</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. It shows the effect of nuclear spin, resulting in intensities variation of 3:1 in adjacent lines. A bond length of 109.9985 &#177; 0.0010 pm was deduced from the data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments_and_methods">Instruments and methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Instruments and methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The great majority of contemporary spectrometers use a mixture of commercially available and bespoke components which users integrate according to their particular needs. Instruments can be broadly categorised according to their general operating principles. Although rotational transitions can be found across a very broad region of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, fundamental physical constraints exist on the operational bandwidth of instrument components. It is often impractical and costly to switch to measurements within an entirely different frequency region. The instruments and operating principals described below are generally appropriate to microwave spectroscopy experiments conducted at frequencies between 6 and 24&#160;GHz.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_cells_and_Stark_modulation">Absorption cells and Stark modulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Absorption cells and Stark modulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A microwave spectrometer can be most simply constructed using a source of microwave radiation, an absorption cell into which sample gas can be introduced and a detector such as a <a href="/wiki/Superheterodyne_receiver" title="Superheterodyne receiver">superheterodyne receiver</a>. A spectrum can be obtained by sweeping the frequency of the source while detecting the intensity of transmitted radiation. A simple section of <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguide</a> can serve as an absorption cell. An important variation of the technique in which an alternating current is applied across electrodes within the absorption cell results in a modulation of the frequencies of rotational transitions. This is referred to as Stark modulation and allows the use of <a href="/wiki/Lock-in_amplifier" title="Lock-in amplifier">phase-sensitive detection</a> methods offering improved sensitivity. Absorption spectroscopy allows the study of samples that are thermodynamically stable at room temperature. \nThe first study of the <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> spectrum of a molecule (<span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) was performed by Cleeton &amp; Williams in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cleeton_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cleeton-40">[34]</a></sup> Subsequent experiments exploited powerful sources of <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwaves</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystron</a>, many of which were developed for <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. The number of experiments in microwave spectroscopy surged immediately after the war. By 1948, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gordy" title="Walter Gordy">Walter Gordy</a> was able to prepare a review of the results contained in approximately 100 research papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[35]</a></sup> Commercial versions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[36]</a></sup>  of microwave absorption spectrometer were developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard" title="Hewlett Packard">Hewlett Packard</a> in the 1970s and were once widely used for fundamental research. Most research laboratories now exploit either Balle-<a href="/wiki/Willis_H._Flygare" title="Willis H. Flygare">Flygare</a> or chirped-pulse Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectrometers.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Fourier_transform_microwave_.28FTMW.29_spectroscopy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourier_transform_microwave_(FTMW)_spectroscopy">Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The theoretical framework<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[37]</a></sup> underpinning FTMW spectroscopy is analogous to that used to describe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">FT-NMR spectroscopy</a>. The behaviour of the evolving system is described by optical <a href="/wiki/Bloch_equations" title="Bloch equations">Bloch equations</a>. First, a short (typically 0-3 microsecond duration) microwave pulse is introduced on resonance with a rotational transition. Those molecules that absorb the energy from this pulse are induced to rotate coherently in phase with the incident radiation. De-activation of the polarisation pulse is followed by microwave emission that accompanies decoherence of the molecular ensemble. This <a href="/wiki/Free_induction_decay" title="Free induction decay">free induction decay</a> occurs on a timescale of 1-100 microseconds depending on instrument settings. Following pioneering work by Dicke and co-workers in the 1950s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[38]</a></sup> the first FTMW spectrometer was constructed by Ekkers and <a href="/wiki/Willis_H._Flygare" title="Willis H. Flygare">Flygare</a> in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Balle-Flygare_FTMW_spectrometer">The Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: The Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Balle, Campbell, Keenan  and Flygare demonstrated that the FTMW technique can be applied within a "free space cell" comprising an evacuated chamber containing a <a href="/wiki/Optical_cavity" title="Optical cavity">Fabry-Perot cavity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[40]</a></sup> This technique allows a sample to be probed only milliseconds after it undergoes rapid cooling to only a few <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a> in the throat of an expanding gas jet. This was a revolutionary development because (i) cooling molecules to low temperatures concentrates the available population in the lowest rotational energy levels. Coupled with benefits conferred by the use of a Fabry-Perot cavity, this brought a great enhancement in the sensitivity and resolution of spectrometers along with a reduction in the complexity of observed spectra; (ii) it became possible to isolate and study molecules that are very weakly bound because there is insufficient energy available for them to undergo fragmentation or chemical reaction at such low temperatures. <a href="/wiki/William_Klemperer" title="William Klemperer">William Klemperer</a> was a pioneer in using this instrument for the exploration of weakly bound interactions. While the Fabry-Perot cavity of a Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer can typically be tuned into resonance at any frequency between 6 and 18&#160;GHz, the bandwidth of individual measurements is restricted to about 1&#160;MHz. An animation illustrates the operation of this instrument which is currently the most widely used tool for microwave spectroscopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Chirped-Pulse_FTMW_spectrometer">The Chirped-Pulse FTMW spectrometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: The Chirped-Pulse FTMW spectrometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Noting that digitisers and related electronics technology had significantly progressed since the inception of FTMW spectroscopy, <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Pate" title="Brooks Pate">B.H. Pate</a> at the University of Virginia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[42]</a></sup>  designed a spectrometer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[43]</a></sup> which retains many advantages of the Balle-Flygare FT-MW spectrometer while innovating in (i) the use of a high speed (&gt;4 GS/s) arbitrary waveform generator to generate a "chirped" microwave polarisation pulse that sweeps up to 12&#160;GHz in frequency in less than a microsecond and (ii) the use of a high speed (&gt;40 GS/s) oscilloscope to digitise and Fourier transform the molecular free induction decay. The result is an instrument that allows the study of weakly bound molecules but which is able to exploit a measurement bandwidth (12&#160;GHz) that is greatly enhanced compared with the Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer. Modified versions of the original CP-FTMW spectrometer have been constructed by a number of groups in the United States, Canada and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[45]</a></sup> The instrument offers a broadband capability that is highly complementary to the high sensitivity and resolution offered by the Balle-Flygare design.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
