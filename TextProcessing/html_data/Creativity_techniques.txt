class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aleatory techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aleatoricism" title="Aleatoricism">Aleatoricism</a></div>\n<p>Aleatoricism is the incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">chance</a> (random elements) into the process of creation, especially the creation of art or media. Aleatoricism is commonly found in music, art, and literature, particularly in poetry.  In film, Andy Voda made a movie in 1979 called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chance_Chants&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chance Chants (page does not exist)">Chance Chants</a></i>, which he produced by a flip of a coin or roll of a die. In music, <a href="/wiki/John_Cage" title="John Cage">John Cage</a>, an avant-garde musician, composed music by using the <i><a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">I Ching</a></i> to determine the position of musical notes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> superimposing star maps on blank sheet music, by rolling dice and preparing open-ended scores that depended on the spontaneous decisions of the performers. (1) Other ways of practicing randomness include coin tossing, picking something out of a hat, or selecting random words from a dictionary. \n</p><p>The aleatory approach is also demonstrated in the case of the process called provocation, which was initially introduced by <a href="/wiki/Edward_de_Bono" title="Edward de Bono">Edward de Bono</a> as an aid to research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This method, which <a href="/wiki/Richard_Restak" title="Richard Restak">Richard Restak</a> said was also employed by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Burgess" title="Anthony Burgess">Anthony Burgess</a>, aims to achieve novel ideas in writing by directing a plot with creative connections through random words picked from a reference book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Restak explained that the two hundred billion interconnected neural cells in the brain are capable of an abundance of possibilities for long-range connections and creative interactions using random and unrelated words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In short, aleatoricism is a way to introduce new thoughts or ideas into a creative process.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improvisation">Improvisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Improvisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">Improvisation</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Improvisation" title="Improvisation">Improvisation</a> is a creative process which can be spoken, written, or composed without prior preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\nImprovisation, also called extemporization, can lead to the discovery of new ways to act, new patterns of thought and practices, or new structures. Improvisation is used in the creation of music, theater, and other various forms.  Many artists also use improvisational techniques to help their creative flow.\n</p><p>The following are two significant domains that use improvisation:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Improvisational_theater" title="Improvisational theater">Improvisational theater</a> is a form of theater in which actors use improvisational acting techniques to perform spontaneously. Many improvisational ("improv") techniques are taught in standard drama classes. The basic skills of listening, clarity, confidence, and performing instinctively and spontaneously are considered important skills for actors to develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Free_improvisation" title="Free improvisation">Free improvisation</a> is real-time composition. Musicians of all kinds improvise ("improv") music; such improvised music is not limited to a particular genre. Two contemporary musicians that use free improvisation are <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Braxton" title="Anthony Braxton">Anthony Braxton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Taylor" title="Cecil Taylor">Cecil Taylor</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_problem_solving">In problem solving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In problem solving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_problem_solving" title="Creative problem solving">Creative problem solving</a></div>\n<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Problem-solving" title="Problem-solving">problem-solving</a> contexts, the random-word creativity technique is perhaps the simplest method. A person confronted with a problem is presented with a randomly generated word, in the hopes of a solution arising from any associations between the word and the problem. This technique is based on associative thinking, the process of retrieving information from our knowledge and automatically find patterns across elements. While standard associative thinking generates associations between concepts that are strongly related and not very original, the unpredictability of a random word will lead to explore new associations that would not emerge automatically, and hopefully trigger novel solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A random image, sound, or article can be used instead of a random word as a kind of creativity goad or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/provocation" title="wikt:provocation">provocation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are many problem-solving tools and methodologies to support creativity:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/TRIZ" title="TRIZ">TRIZ</a> (theory which are derived from tools such as ARIZ or TRIZ contradiction matrix)</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Problem_Solving#Creative_Problem_Solving" title="Creative Problem Solving">Creative Problem Solving Process (CPS)</a> (complex strategy, also known as Osborn-Parnes-process)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lateral_thinking" title="Lateral thinking">Lateral thinking process</a>, of Edward de Bono</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Six_Thinking_Hats" title="Six Thinking Hats">Six Thinking Hats</a>, of Edward de Bono</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Herrmann_Brain_Dominance_Instrument" title="Herrmann Brain Dominance Instrument">Herrmann Brain Dominance Instrument</a> &#8211; right brain / left brain</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Brainstorming" title="Brainstorming">Brainstorming</a> and <a href="/wiki/6-3-5_Brainwriting" title="6-3-5 Brainwriting">Brainwriting</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Think_outside_the_box" title="Think outside the box">Think outside the box</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Business_war_games" title="Business war games">Business war games</a>, for the resolution of competitive problems</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/SWOT_analysis" title="SWOT analysis">SWOT analysis</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USIT_-_Unified_Structured_Inventive_Thinking" title="USIT - Unified Structured Inventive Thinking">The method USIT of convergent creativity</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">Thought experiment</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Five_Ws" title="Five Ws">Five Ws</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_project_management">In project management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In project management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a> purposes, <b>group creativity techniques</b> are creativity techniques used by a <a href="/wiki/Team" title="Team">team</a> in the course of executing a project. Some relevant techniques are <a href="/wiki/Brainstorming" title="Brainstorming">brainstorming</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nominal_group_technique" title="Nominal group technique">nominal group technique</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delphi_technique" title="Delphi technique">Delphi technique</a>, idea/<a href="/wiki/Mind_map" title="Mind map">mind mapping</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Affinity_diagram" title="Affinity diagram">affinity diagram</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicriteria_decision_analysis" title="Multicriteria decision analysis">multicriteria decision analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These techniques are referenced in the <i>Guide to the <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Body_of_Knowledge" title="Project Management Body of Knowledge">Project Management Body of Knowledge</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Projects_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Projects-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Group creativity techniques can be used in a sequence; for example:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Projects_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Projects-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>Gather <a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">requirements</a> using idea/<a href="/wiki/Mind_map" title="Mind map">mind mapping</a></li>\n<li>Continue generating ideas by <a href="/wiki/Brainstorming" title="Brainstorming">brainstorming</a></li>\n<li>Construct an <a href="/wiki/Affinity_diagram" title="Affinity diagram">affinity diagram</a> based on the generated ideas</li>\n<li>Identify the most important ideas by applying the <a href="/wiki/Nominal_group_technique" title="Nominal group technique">nominal group technique</a></li>\n<li>Obtain several rounds of independent feedback using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delphi_technique" title="Delphi technique">Delphi technique</a></li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affecting_factors">Affecting factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Affecting factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distraction">Distraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Multiple studies have confirmed that distraction actually increases creative cognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One such study done by Jonathan Schooler found that non-demanding distractions improve performance on a classic creativity task called the UUT (Unusual Uses Task) in which the subject must come up with as many possible uses for a common object. The results confirmed that decision-related neural processes occur during moments of unconscious thought while a person engages in a non-demanding task. The research showed that while distracted a subject isn&#8217;t maintaining one thought for a particularly long time, which in turn allows different ideas to float in and out of one&#8217;s consciousness&#8212;this sort of associative process leads to creative incubation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ambient noise is another variable that is conducive to distraction, yet it has been proven that a moderate level of noise actually heighten creativity. Professor Ravi Mehta conducted a study to research the degree of distraction induced by various noise levels and their effect on creativity. The series of experiments show that a moderate level of ambient noise (70&#160;dB) produces just enough distraction to induce processing disfluency, which leads to abstract cognition. These higher construal levels caused by moderate levels of noise consequently enhance creativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking">Walking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Walking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2014, a study found that walking increased creativity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> an activity favored by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleep_and_relaxation">Sleep and relaxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sleep and relaxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some advocate enhancing creativity by taking advantage of <a href="/wiki/Hypnagogia" title="Hypnagogia">hypnagogia</a>, the transition from wakefulness to sleep, using techniques such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucid_dreaming" title="Lucid dreaming">lucid dreaming</a>.  One technique used by <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dal&#237;">Salvador Dal&#237;</a> was to drift off to sleep in an armchair with a set of keys in his hand; when he fell completely asleep, the keys would fall and wake him up, allowing him to recall his mind\'s subconscious imaginings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> used the same technique, with <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearing" title="Ball bearing">ball bearings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meditation">Meditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Meditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> from 2014 conducted by researchers in China and the US, including the psychologist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Posner_(psychologist)" title="Michael Posner (psychologist)">Michael Posner</a> found that performing a short 30 minute <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditation</a> session each day, for seven days, was sufficient to improve verbal and visual creativity, as measured by the <a href="/wiki/Torrance_Tests_of_Creative_Thinking" title="Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking">Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking</a>, due to the positive effects of meditation on emotional regulation. The same researchers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> also showed in 2015 that short term meditation training could also improve insight-based problem solving (the type commonly associated with an "Ah-ha", or "eureka" type moment of realization) as measured by the <a href="/wiki/Remote_Associates_Test" title="Remote Associates Test">Remote Associates Test</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Creativity_techniques&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 22em;
