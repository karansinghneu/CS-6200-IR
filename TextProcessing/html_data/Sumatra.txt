title="Nias people">Nias</a> etc.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Sumatra</b> is a large island in western <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> that is part of the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Islands" title="Sunda Islands">Sunda Islands</a>. It is the largest island that is located entirely in Indonesia (after <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, which is shared between Indonesia and other countries) and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">sixth-largest island</a> in the world at 473,481&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (not including adjacent islands such as the Riau Islands and Bangka Belitung Islands).\n</p><p>Sumatra is an elongated landmass spanning a diagonal northwest-southeast axis. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> borders the west, northwest, and southwest coasts of Sumatra with the island chain of <a href="/wiki/Simeulue" title="Simeulue">Simeulue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nias" title="Nias">Nias</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentawai_Islands" title="Mentawai Islands">Mentawai</a> off the western coast. In the northeast the narrow <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a> separates the island from the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>, which is an extension of the Eurasian continent. In the southeast the narrow <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a> separates Sumatra from <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>. The northern tip of Sumatra borders the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a>, while off the southeastern coast lie the islands of Bangka and Belitung, <a href="/wiki/Karimata_Strait" title="Karimata Strait">Karimata Strait</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Java_Sea" title="Java Sea">Java Sea</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bukit_Barisan" title="Bukit Barisan">Bukit Barisan</a> mountains, which contain several active volcanoes, form the backbone of the island, while the northeastern area contains large plains and lowlands with swamps, mangrove forest and complex river systems. The <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> crosses the island at its center in <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riau" title="Riau">Riau</a> provinces. The climate of the island is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a>, hot and humid. Lush <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_forest" title="Tropical rain forest">tropical rain forest</a> once dominated the landscape.\n</p><p>Sumatra has a wide range of plant and animal species but has lost almost 50% of its tropical rainforest in the last 35 years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Many species are now <a href="/wiki/Critically_endangered" title="Critically endangered">critically endangered</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_elephant" title="Sumatran elephant">Sumatran elephant</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_rhinoceros" title="Sumatran rhinoceros">Sumatran rhinoceros</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan" title="Sumatran orangutan">Sumatran orangutan</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> on the island has also resulted in serious seasonal smoke haze over neighbouring countries, such as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Sumatra was known in ancient times by the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> names of <i>Swarnadw&#299;pa</i> ("Island of Gold") and <i>Swarnabh&#363;mi</i> ("Land of Gold"), because of the gold deposits in the island\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_(geography)" title="Highland (geography)">highlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first mention of the name of <i>Sumatra</i> was in the name of <a href="/wiki/Srivijaya" title="Srivijaya">Srivijayan</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Haji_Sumatrabhumi" title="Haji Sumatrabhumi">Haji<i> (king) </i>Sumatrabhumi</a></i> ("King of the land of Sumatra"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MUNOZ_175_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MUNOZ_175-3">[3]</a></sup> who sent an envoy to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 1017. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> geographers referred to the island as <i>Lamri</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamuri" title="Lamuri">Lamuri</a>, Lambri or Ramni) in the tenth through thirteenth centuries, in reference to a kingdom near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Banda_Aceh" title="Banda Aceh">Banda Aceh</a> which was the first landfall for traders. The island is also known by other names namely, Andalas <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or Percha Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Late in the 14th century the name Sumatra became popular in reference to the kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasai" title="Pasai">Samudra Pasai</a>, a rising power until replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Aceh" title="Sultanate of Aceh">Sultanate of Aceh</a>. Sultan Alauddin Shah of Aceh, in letters addressed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> in 1602, referred to himself as "king of Aceh and Samudra".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sneddon_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sneddon-6">[6]</a></sup> The word itself is from Sanskrit "<i><a href="/wiki/Samudra" title="Samudra">Samudra</a></i>", (&#2360;&#2350;&#2369;&#2342;&#2381;&#2352;), meaning "<i>gathering together of waters, sea or ocean</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> named the kingdom Samara or Samarcha in the late 13th century, while the 14th century traveller <a href="/wiki/Odoric_of_Pordenone" title="Odoric of Pordenone">Odoric of Pordenone</a> used Sumoltra for Samudra. Subsequent European writers then used similar forms of the name for the entire island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>European writers in the 19th century found that the indigenous inhabitants did not have a name for the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sumatra_Volcanoes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/220px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/330px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Sumatra_Volcanoes.png/440px-Sumatra_Volcanoes.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sumatra_Volcanoes.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of geological formation of Sumatra island</div></div></div>\n<p>The longest axis of the island runs approximately 1,790&#160;km (1,110&#160;mi) northwest&#8211;southeast, crossing the equator near the centre. At its widest point, the island spans 435&#160;km (270&#160;mi). The interior of the island is dominated by two geographical regions: the <a href="/wiki/Barisan_Mountains" title="Barisan Mountains">Barisan Mountains</a> in the west and swampy plains in the east. Sumatra is the closest Indonesian island to mainland Asia.\n</p><p>To the southeast is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Java_(island)" title="Java (island)">Java</a>, separated by the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a>. To the north is the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> (located on the Asian mainland), separated by the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a>. To the east is <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, across the <a href="/wiki/Karimata_Strait" title="Karimata Strait">Karimata Strait</a>. West of the island is the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Sumatran_fault" title="Great Sumatran fault">Great Sumatran fault</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">strike-slip</a> fault), and the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_megathrust" title="Sunda megathrust">Sunda megathrust</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a>), run the entire length of the island along its west coast. On 26 December 2004, the western coast and islands of Sumatra, particularly <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a> province, were struck by a <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">Indian Ocean earthquake</a>. This was the longest earthquake recorded, lasting between 500 and 600 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> More than 170,000 Indonesians were killed, primarily in Aceh. Other recent earthquakes to strike Sumatra include the <a href="/wiki/2005_Nias%E2%80%93Simeulue_earthquake" title="2005 Nias&#8211;Simeulue earthquake">2005 Nias&#8211;Simeulue earthquake</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2010_Mentawai_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2010 Mentawai earthquake and tsunami">2010 Mentawai earthquake and tsunami</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sinabung.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sinabung.jpg/220px-Sinabung.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sinabung.jpg/330px-Sinabung.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Sinabung.jpg/440px-Sinabung.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sinabung.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinabung" title="Mount Sinabung">Mount Sinabung</a>, North Sumatra</div></div></div>\n<p>To the east, big rivers carry silt from the mountains, forming the vast lowland interspersed by swamps. Even if mostly unsuitable for farming, the area is currently of great economic importance for Indonesia. It produces oil from both above and below the soil &#8211; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">palm oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>.\n</p><p>Sumatra is the largest producer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_coffee" title="Indonesian coffee">Indonesian coffee</a>. Small-holders grow Arabica coffee (<i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">Coffea arabica</a></i>) in the highlands, while Robusta (<i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_canephora" title="Coffea canephora">Coffea canephora</a></i>) is found in the lowlands. Arabica coffee from the regions of Gayo, Lintong and Sidikilang is typically processed using the <a href="/wiki/Giling_Basah" title="Giling Basah">Giling Basah</a> (wet hulling) technique, which gives it a heavy body and low acidity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_cities">Largest cities</span><span title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rafflesia_sumatra.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="227" id="cite_ref-Whitten1999_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten1999-20">[20]</a></sup> Unique species include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumatran_pine" title="Sumatran pine">Sumatran pine</a> which dominates the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tropical_pine_forests" title="Sumatran tropical pine forests">Sumatran tropical pine forests</a> of the higher mountainsides in the north of the island and rainforest plants such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rafflesia_arnoldii" title="Rafflesia arnoldii">Rafflesia arnoldii</a></i> (the world\'s largest individual flower), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_arum" title="Titan arum">titan arum</a> (the world\'s largest unbranched <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a>).\n</p><p>The island is home to 201 mammal species and 580 bird species, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>. There are 9 endemic mammal species on mainland Sumatra and 14 more endemic to the nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mentawai_Islands" title="Mentawai Islands">Mentawai Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten1999_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten1999-20">[20]</a></sup> There are about 300 freshwater fish species in Sumatra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> There are 93 <a href="/wiki/List_of_amphibians_of_Sumatra" title="List of amphibians of Sumatra">amphibian species</a> in Sumatra, 21 of which are endemic to Sumatra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> (<i>See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_amphibians_of_Sumatra" title="List of amphibians of Sumatra">List of amphibians of Sumatra</a></i>)\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_tiger" title="Sumatran tiger">Sumatran tiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_rhinoceros" title="Sumatran rhinoceros">Sumatran rhinoceros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_elephant" title="Sumatran elephant">Sumatran elephant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_ground_cuckoo" title="Sumatran ground cuckoo">Sumatran ground cuckoo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sumatran_orangutan" title="Sumatran orangutan">Sumatran orangutan</a> are all critically endangered, indicating the highest level of threat to their survival. In October 2008, the Indonesian government announced a plan to protect Sumatra\'s remaining forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island includes more than 10 national parks, including 3 which are listed as the <i><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainforest_Heritage_of_Sumatra" title="Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra">Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra</a></i> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> &#8211; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunung_Leuser_National_Park" title="Gunung Leuser National Park">Gunung Leuser National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerinci_Seblat_National_Park" title="Kerinci Seblat National Park">Kerinci Seblat National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bukit_Barisan_Selatan_National_Park" title="Bukit Barisan Selatan National Park">Bukit Barisan Selatan National Park</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Berbak_National_Park" title="Berbak National Park">Berbak National Park</a> is one of three national parks in Indonesia listed as a <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> of international importance under the <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention" title="Ramsar Convention">Ramsar Convention</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Minangprocession.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1054" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/170px-Minangprocession.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/255px-Minangprocession.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Minangprocession.jpg/340px-Minangprocession.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Minangprocession.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Minangkabau women carrying platters of food to a ceremony</div></div></div>\n<p>Sumatra is not particularly densely populated, with just over 90.4 people per km<sup>2</sup> &#8211; more than 50&#160;million people in total. Because of its great extent, it is nonetheless the fifth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_population" title="List of islands by population">most populous island in the world</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sumatra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are over 52 <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Indonesia" title="Languages of Indonesia">languages</a> spoken, all of them (except Chinese and Tamil) belong to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian languages">Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian</a> sub-branch of <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Malayo-Polynesian languages">Malayo-Polynesian</a> which in turn is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a> language family. Within Nuclear Malayo-Polynesian, they were divided into several sub-branches that is <a href="/wiki/Chamic_languages" title="Chamic languages">Chamic</a> (which are represented by <a href="/wiki/Acehnese_language" title="Acehnese language">Acehnese</a> in which its closest relatives are languages spoken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cham_people" title="Cham people">Ethnic Chams</a> in Cambodia and Vietnam), <a href="/wiki/Malayic_languages" title="Malayic languages">Malayic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_language" title="Minangkabau language">Minangkabau</a> and other closely related languages), <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Sumatran_languages" title="Northwest Sumatran languages">Northwest Sumatran</a> (<a href="/wiki/Batak_languages" title="Batak languages">Batak languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gayo_language" title="Gayo language">Gayo</a> and others), <a href="/wiki/Lampung_language" title="Lampung language">Lampungic</a> (includes Proper Lampung and <a href="/wiki/Komering_language" title="Komering language">Komering</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bornean_languages" title="Bornean languages">Bornean</a> (represented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rejangese_language" title="Rejangese language">Rejang</a> in which its closest linguistic relatives are <a href="/wiki/Bukar_Sadong_language" title="Bukar Sadong language">Bukar Sadong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_Dayak_languages" title="Land Dayak languages">Land Dayak</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/West_Kalimantan" title="West Kalimantan">West Kalimantan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sarawak" title="Sarawak">Sarawak</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>)). Northwest Sumatran and Lampungic branches are endemic to the island. Like all parts of Indonesia, <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> (which was based on Riau Malay) is the official language and the main Lingua franca. Although Sumatra has its own local Lingua franca, <a href="/wiki/Malayan_languages" title="Malayan languages">variants of Malay</a> like Medan Malay and <a href="/wiki/Musi_language" title="Musi language">Palembang Malay</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> are popular in North and South Sumatra, especially in urban areas. Minangkabau (Padang dialect)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> is popular in West Sumatra, some parts of North Sumatra, Bengkulu, Jambi and Riau (especially in <a href="/wiki/Pekanbaru" title="Pekanbaru">Pekanbaru</a> and areas bordered with <a href="/wiki/West_Sumatra" title="West Sumatra">West Sumatra</a>) while Acehnese is also used as an inter-ethnic means of communication in some parts of Aceh province.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
