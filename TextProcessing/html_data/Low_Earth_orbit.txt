article.)</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>low Earth orbit</b> (<b>LEO</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">Earth-centered orbit</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> of 2,000&#160;km (1,200&#160;mi) or less (approximately one third of the <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">radius of Earth</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNOOSA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNOOSA-3">[1]</a></sup> or with at least 11.25 periods per day (an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> of 128 minutes or less) and an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> less than 0.25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[2]</a></sup> Most of the manmade objects in space are in LEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> A histogram of the <a href="/wiki/Mean_motion" title="Mean motion">mean motion</a> of the cataloged objects shows that the number of objects drops significantly beyond 11.25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is a large variety of other sources<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> that define LEO in terms of <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>. The altitude of an object in an <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a> can vary significantly along the orbit. Even for <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">circular orbits</a>, the altitude above ground can vary by as much as 30&#160;km (19&#160;mi) (especially for <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbits</a>) due to the <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">oblateness</a> of <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">Earth\'s spheroid figure</a> and local <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>.  While definitions in terms of altitude are inherently ambiguous, most of them fall within the range specified by an orbit period of 128 minutes because, according to <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">Kepler\'s third law</a>, this corresponds to a <a href="/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes" title="Semi-major and semi-minor axes">semi-major axis</a> of 8,413&#160;km (5,228&#160;mi). For circular orbits, this in turn corresponds to an altitude of 2,042&#160;km (1,269&#160;mi) above the mean radius of Earth, which is consistent with some of the upper limits in the LEO definitions in terms of altitude.\n</p><p>The LEO region is defined by some sources as the region in space that LEO orbits occupy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNOOSA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNOOSA-3">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Highly_elliptical_orbit" title="Highly elliptical orbit">highly elliptical orbits</a> may pass through the LEO region near their lowest altitude (or <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perigee</a>) but are not in an LEO Orbit because their highest altitude (or <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apogee</a>) exceeds 2,000&#160;km (1,200&#160;mi). <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">Sub-orbital</a> objects can also reach the LEO region but are not in an LEO orbit because they <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">re-enter the atmosphere</a>. The distinction between LEO orbits and the LEO region is especially important for analysis of possible collisions between objects which may not themselves be in LEO but could collide with satellites or debris in LEO orbits.   \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> conducts operations in LEO. All crewed <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space stations</a> to date, as well as the majority of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, have been in LEO.  The altitude record for <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflights</a> in LEO was <a href="/wiki/Gemini_11" title="Gemini 11">Gemini 11</a> with an apogee of 1,374.1&#160;km (853.8&#160;mi). <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> was the first mission to carry humans beyond LEO on December 21&#8211;27, 1968. The <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> continued during the four-year period spanning 1968 through 1972 with <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts#Apollo_astronauts_who_flew_to_the_Moon_without_landing" title="List of Apollo astronauts">24 astronauts</a> who flew lunar flights but since then there have been no <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflights</a> beyond LEO.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_characteristics">Orbital characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The mean orbital velocity needed to maintain a stable low Earth orbit is about 7.8&#160;km/s, but reduces with increased orbital altitude. Calculated for circular orbit of 200&#160;km it is 7.79&#160;km/s and for 1500&#160;km it is 7.12&#160;km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> needed to achieve low Earth orbit starts around 9.4&#160;km/s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">Atmospheric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravity_drag" title="Gravity drag">gravity drag</a> associated with launch typically adds 1.3&#8211;1.8&#160;km/s to the launch vehicle <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> required to reach normal LEO orbital velocity of around 7.8&#160;km/s (28,080&#160;km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbitalaltitudes.jpg"><img alt="Orbitalaltitudes.jpg" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="4500" decoding="async" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/700px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/1050px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/1400px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg 2x" width="700"/></a></div></div>\n<p>The pull of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> in LEO is only slightly less than on the earth\'s surface. This is because the distance to LEO from the earth\'s surface is far less than the earth\'s radius. However, an object in orbit is, by definition, in free fall, since there is no force holding it up. As a result objects in orbit, including people, experience a sense of <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a>, even though they are not actually without weight.\n</p><p>Objects in LEO encounter atmospheric drag from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> (approximately 80&#8211;500&#160;km above the surface) or <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> (approximately 500&#160;km and up), depending on orbit height. Due to atmospheric drag, satellites do not usually orbit below 300&#160;km. Objects in LEO orbit Earth between the denser part of the atmosphere and below the inner <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>.\n</p><p>Equatorial low Earth orbits (ELEO) are a subset of LEO. These orbits, with low inclination to the Equator, allow rapid revisit times and have the lowest <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> requirement (i.e., fuel spent) of any orbit. Orbits with a high inclination angle to the equator are usually called <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbits</a>.\n</p><p>Higher orbits include <a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">medium Earth orbit</a> (MEO), sometimes called intermediate circular orbit (ICO), and further above, <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> (GEO). Orbits higher than low orbit can lead to early failure of electronic components due to intense <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and charge accumulation.\n</p><p>In 2017, a very-low LEO orbit began to be seen in <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_agency" title="Regulatory agency">regulatory</a> filings.  This orbit, referred to as "VLEO", requires the use of novel technologies for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbit_raising" title="Orbit raising">orbit raising</a> because they operate in orbits that would ordinarily decay too soon to be economically useful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20170303_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pa20170303-15">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_LEO">Use of LEO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use of LEO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:220px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:854px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv/854px--Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:854px;height:480px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="76" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/4/4c/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv.480p.webm" type="video/webm; It provides high bandwidth and low communication <a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">latency</a>. Satellites and space stations in LEO are more accessible for crew and servicing. \n</p><p>Since it requires less energy to place a satellite into a LEO, and a satellite there needs less powerful amplifiers for successful transmission, LEO is used for many communication applications, such as the <a href="/wiki/Iridium_Communications" title="Iridium Communications">Iridium phone system</a>. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">communication satellites</a> use much higher <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbits</a>, and move at the same angular velocity as the Earth as to appear stationary above one location on the planet.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Satellites in LEO have a small momentary <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">field of view</a>, only able to observe and communicate with a fraction of the Earth at a time, meaning a network (or "<a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">constellation</a>") of satellites is required to in order to provide continuous coverage. Satellites in lower regions of LEO also suffer from fast <a href="/wiki/Orbital_decay" title="Orbital decay">orbital decay</a>, requiring either periodic reboosting to maintain a stable orbit, or launching replacement satellites when old ones re-enter.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">spy satellites</a> use LEO as they are able to see the surface of the Earth clearly by being close to it. They are also able to traverse the surface of the Earth. A majority of artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> are placed in LEO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> making one complete revolution around the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in about 90 minutes.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> is in a LEO about 330&#160;km (210&#160;mi) to 420&#160;km (260&#160;mi) above Earth\'s surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> and needs reboosting a few times a year due to orbital decay.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Iridium_Communications#Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium Communications">Iridium satellites</a> orbit at about  780&#160;km (480&#160;mi).</li>\n<li>Lower orbits also aid <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> satellites because of the added detail that can be gained. Remote sensing satellites can also take advantage of <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous LEO orbits</a> at an altitude of about 800&#160;km (500&#160;mi) and near polar inclination. <a href="/wiki/Envisat" title="Envisat">Envisat</a> (2002&#8211;2012) is one example of an Earth observation satellite that makes use of this particular type of LEO (at 770&#160;km (480&#160;mi)).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gravity_Field_and_Steady-State_Ocean_Circulation_Explorer" title="Gravity Field and Steady-State Ocean Circulation Explorer">GOCE</a> orbited at about 255&#160;km (158&#160;mi) to measure Earth\'s gravity field.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a> were, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRACE-FO" title="GRACE-FO">GRACE-FO</a> are, orbiting at about 500&#160;km (310&#160;mi)</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> orbits at about 540&#160;km (340&#160;mi) above Earth.</li>\n<li>The Chinese <a href="/wiki/Tiangong-2" title="Tiangong-2">Tiangong-2</a> station orbits at about 370&#160;km (230&#160;mi).</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_debris">Space debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Space debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The LEO environment is becoming congested with <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> because of the frequency of object launches.  This has caused growing concern in recent years, since collisions at orbital velocities can easily be dangerous, and even deadly. Collisions can produce even more space debris in the process, creating a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a>, something known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kessler_Syndrome" title="Kessler Syndrome">Kessler Syndrome</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Space_Operations_Center" title="Joint Space Operations Center">Joint Space Operations Center</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Strategic_Command" title="United States Strategic Command">United States Strategic Command</a> (formerly the United States Space Command), currently tracks more than 8,500 objects larger than 10&#160;cm in LEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> However, a limited <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> study suggested there could be approximately one million objects larger than 2 millimeters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> which are too small to be visible from Earth-based observatories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
