href="/wiki/Mojave_Air_%26_Space_Port" title="Mojave Air &amp; Space Port">Mojave Air &amp; Space Port</a>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A <b>sub-orbital spaceflight</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> reaches <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>, but its <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a> intersects the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> or surface of the <a href="/wiki/Primary_(astronomy)" title="Primary (astronomy)">gravitating body</a> from which it was launched, so that it will not complete one <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbital</a> revolution.\n</p><p>For example, the path of an object launched from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> that reaches the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a> (at 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>), and then falls back to Earth, is considered a sub-orbital spaceflight. Some sub-orbital flights have been undertaken to test spacecraft and <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicles</a> later intended for <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital spaceflight</a>. Other vehicles are specifically designed only for sub-orbital flight; examples include manned vehicles, such as the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">X-15</a> and <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>, and unmanned ones, such as <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBMs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rockets</a>.\n</p><p>Flights which attain sufficient velocity to go into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De-orbit" title="De-orbit">de-orbit</a> before completing their first full orbit, are not considered sub-orbital. Examples of this include <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a>, and flights of the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_Orbital_Bombardment_System" title="Fractional Orbital Bombardment System">Fractional Orbital Bombardment System</a>.\n</p><p>Usually a rocket is used, but experimental sub-orbital spaceflight has also been achieved with a <a href="/wiki/Space_gun" title="Space gun">space gun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div This altitude, known as the K&#225;rm&#225;n line, was chosen by the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale">F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale</a> because it is roughly the point where a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a> flying fast enough to support itself with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_lift" title="Aerodynamic lift">aerodynamic lift</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> would be flying faster than <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">orbital speed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The US military and NASA award <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Badge" title="Astronaut Badge">astronaut wings</a> to those flying above 50&#160;mi (80&#160;km),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State_Department" title="U.S. State Department">U.S. State Department</a> appears to not support a distinct boundary between atmospheric flight and <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-orbital_spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freefall" title="Freefall">freefall</a> the trajectory is part of an <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a> as given by the <a href="/wiki/Orbit_equation" title="Orbit equation">orbit equation</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> distance is less than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radius_of_the_Earth" title="Radius of the Earth">radius of the Earth</a> <i>R</i> including atmosphere, hence the <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a> intersects the Earth, and hence the spacecraft will fail to complete an orbit. The major axis is vertical, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> <i>a</i> is more than <i>R</i>/2. width:3.763ex; height:5.009ex;"/></span>.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Speed.2C_range_and_altitude"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed,_range_and_altitude">Speed, range and altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-orbital_spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Speed, range and altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>To minimize the required <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> (an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">astrodynamical</a> measure which strongly determines the required <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>), the high-altitude part of the flight is made with the <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> off (this is technically called free-fall even for the upward part of the trajectory). (Compare with <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a>.) The maximum <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> in a flight is attained at the lowest altitude of this free-fall trajectory, both at the start and at the end of it.\n</p><p>If one\'s goal is simply to "reach space", for example in competing for the <a href="/wiki/Ansari_X_Prize" title="Ansari X Prize">Ansari X Prize</a>, horizontal motion is not needed. In this case the lowest required delta-v, to reach 100&#160;km altitude, is about 1.4&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Km/s" title="Km/s">km/s</a>. Moving slower, with less free-fall, would require more delta-v.\n</p><p>Compare this with orbital spaceflights: a low Earth orbit (LEO), with an altitude of about 300&#160;km, needs a speed around 7.7&#160;km/s, requiring a delta-v of about 9.2&#160;km/s. (If there were no atmospheric drag the theoretical minimum delta-v would be 8.1&#160;km/s to put a craft into a 300-km high orbit starting from a stationary point like the South Pole. The theoretical minimum can be up to 0.46&#160;km/s less if launching eastward from near the equator.)\n</p><p>For sub-orbital spaceflights covering a horizontal distance the maximum speed and required delta-v are in between those of a vertical flight and a LEO. The maximum speed at the lower ends of the trajectory are now composed of a horizontal and a vertical component. The higher the horizontal <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a> covered, the greater the horizontal speed will be. (The vertical velocity will increase with distance for short distances but will decrease with distance at longer distances.) For the <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a>, just reaching space but with a range of about 330&#160;km, the maximum speed was 1.6&#160;km/s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites_SpaceShipTwo" title="Scaled Composites SpaceShipTwo">Scaled Composites SpaceShipTwo</a> which is under development will have a similar free-fall orbit but the announced maximum speed is 1.1&#160;km/s (perhaps because of engine shut-off at a higher altitude).\n</p><p>For larger ranges, due to the elliptic orbit the maximum altitude can be much more than for a LEO. On a 10,000-km intercontinental flight, such as that of an intercontinental ballistic missile or possible future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_spaceflight" title="Commercial spaceflight">commercial spaceflight</a>, the maximum speed is about 7&#160;km/s, and the maximum altitude may be more than 1300&#160;km.\nAny <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> that returns to the surface, including sub-orbital ones, will undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric reentry</a>. The speed at the start of the reentry is basically the maximum speed of the flight. The <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_heating" title="Aerodynamic heating">aerodynamic heating</a> caused will vary accordingly: it is much less for a flight with a maximum speed of only 1&#160;km/s than for one with a maximum speed of 7 or 8&#160;km/s.\n</p><p>We can calculate the minimum delta-v and the corresponding maximum altitude for a given range, <i>d</i>, assuming a spherical earth of circumference 40 000&#160;km and neglecting the earth\'s rotation and atmosphere. Let &#952; be half the angle that the projectile is to go around the earth, so in degrees it is 45&#176;&#215;<i>d</i>/10 000&#160;km. The minimum-delta-v trajectory corresponds to an ellipse with one focus at the centre of the earth and the other at the point halfway between the launch point and the destination point (somewhere inside the earth). (This is the orbit that minimizes the semi-major axis, which is equal to the sum of the distances from a point on the orbit to the two foci. Minimizing the semi-major axis minimizes the <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a> and thus the delta-v, which is the speed of launch.) The very first ballistic missile to reach space was the German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a>, the work of the scientists at <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenem&#252;nde">Peenem&#252;nde</a>, on October 3, 1942 which reached an altitude of 60 miles (97&#160;km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Then in the late 1940s the USA and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> concurrently developed missiles all of which were based on the V-2 Rocket, and then much longer range Intercontinental Ballistic Missiles (ICBMs). There are now many countries who possess ICBMs and even more with shorter range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRBM" title="IRBM">IRBMs</a> (Intermediate Range Ballistic Missiles).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourist_flights">Tourist flights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-orbital_spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tourist flights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">Sub-orbital tourist flights</a> will initially focus on attaining the altitude required to qualify as reaching space. The flight path will probably be either vertical or very steep, with the spacecraft landing back at its take-off site.\n</p><p>The spacecraft will probably shut off its <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engines</a> well before reaching maximum altitude, and then coast up to its highest point. During a few minutes, from the point when the engines are shut off to the point where the atmosphere begins to slow down the downward acceleration, the passengers will experience <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a>.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaroc" title="Megaroc">Megaroc</a> had been planned for sub-orbital spaceflight by the <a href="/wiki/British_Interplanetary_Society" title="British Interplanetary Society">British Interplanetary Society</a> in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the autumn of 1945, the group M. Tikhonravov K. and N. G. Chernysheva at NII-4 rocket artillery Academy of Sciences technology on its own initiative the first stratospheric rocket project was developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_BP-190" title="Project BP-190">BP-190</a> for vertical flight two pilots to an altitude of 200&#160;km based on captured German ballistic rocket <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2004, a number of companies worked on vehicles in this class as entrants to the Ansari X Prize competition. The <a href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites" title="Scaled Composites">Scaled Composites</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites_SpaceShipOne" title="Scaled Composites SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a> was officially declared by <a href="/wiki/Richard_A._Searfoss" title="Richard A. Searfoss">Rick Searfoss</a> to have won the competition on October 4, 2004 after completing two flights within a two-week period.\n</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Branson" title="Richard Branson">Sir Richard Branson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Group" title="Virgin Group">Virgin Group</a> announced the creation of <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Galactic" title="Virgin Galactic">Virgin Galactic</a> and his plans for a 9-seat capacity SpaceShipTwo named <a href="/wiki/VSS_Enterprise" title="VSS Enterprise">VSS <i>Enterprise</i></a>. It has since been completed with eight seats (one pilot, one co-pilot and six passengers) and has taken part in captive-carry tests and with the first mother-ship <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WhiteKnightTwo" title="WhiteKnightTwo">WhiteKnightTwo</a>, or <a href="/wiki/VMS_Eve" title="VMS Eve">VMS Eve</a>. It has also completed solitary glides, with the movable tail sections in both fixed and "feathered" configurations.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_rocket" title="Hybrid rocket">hybrid rocket</a> motor has been fired multiple times in ground-based test stands, and was fired in a powered flight for the second time on 5 September 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Four additional SpaceShipTwos have been ordered and will operate from the new <a href="/wiki/Spaceport_America" title="Spaceport America">Spaceport America</a>. Commercial flights carrying passengers were expected in 2014, but became cancelled due to the <a href="/wiki/VSS_Enterprise_crash" title="VSS Enterprise crash">disaster during SS2 PF04 flight</a>. Branson stated, "[w]e are going to learn from what went wrong, discover how we can improve safety and performance and then move forwards together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_experiments">Scientific experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-orbital_spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scientific experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A major use of sub-orbital vehicles today are as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific" title="Scientific">scientific</a> <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rockets</a>. Scientific sub-orbital flights began in the 1920s when <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a> launched the first <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">liquid fueled</a> rockets, however they did not reach <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> altitude. In the late 1940s, captured German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 ballistic missiles</a> were converted into <a href="/wiki/V-2_sounding_rocket" title="V-2 sounding rocket">V-2 sounding rockets</a> which helped lay the foundation for modern sounding rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Today there are dozens of different sounding rockets on the market, from a variety of suppliers in various countries. Typically, researchers wish to conduct experiments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">microgravity</a> or above the atmosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-orbital_transportation">Sub-orbital transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sub-orbital_spaceflight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sub-orbital transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Research, such as that done for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-20_Dyna-Soar" title="X-20 Dyna-Soar">X-20 Dyna-Soar</a> project suggests that a semi-ballistic sub-orbital flight could travel from Europe to North America in less than an hour.\n</p><p>However, the size of rocket, relative to the payload, necessary to achieve this, is similar to an ICBM. ICBMs have delta-v\'s somewhat less than orbital; and therefore would be somewhat cheaper than the costs for reaching orbit, but the difference is not large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thus due to the high cost, this is likely to be initially limited to high value, very high urgency cargo such as <a href="/wiki/Courier" title="Courier">courier</a> flights, or as the ultimate <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jet</a>; or possibly as an <a href="/wiki/Extreme_sport" title="Extreme sport">extreme sport</a>, or for <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> fast-response.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Opinion" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:ASSERT" title="Wikipedia:ASSERT"><span title="This statement may be opinion presented as fact (April 2012)">opinion</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/SpaceLiner" title="SpaceLiner">SpaceLiner</a> is a hypersonic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suborbital_spaceplane" title="Suborbital spaceplane">suborbital spaceplane</a> concept that could transport 50 passengers from <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in 90 minutes or 100 passengers from Europe to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 60 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The main challenge lies in increasing the reliability of the different components, particularly the engines, in order to make their use for passenger transportation on a daily basis possible.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> is planning to use its <a href="/wiki/BFR_(rocket)" title="BFR (rocket)">BFR</a> launch vehicle as a sub-orbital point-to-point transport.\n</p>\n<h2><span
