It was founded in 2008 by <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Red_Whittaker" title="Red Whittaker">Red Whittaker</a> and his associates, with the goal of winning the <a href="/wiki/Google_Lunar_X_Prize" title="Google Lunar X Prize">Google Lunar X Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The company is based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>.\n</p><p>The first launch of one of its spacecraft, the <i>Peregrine</i> lunar lander, is expected to take place in 2020 on an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The team stated an ambitious goal from the start in 2008: to be the first commercial operation to land their <i>Red Rover</i> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, using their <i>Artemis Lander</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc20080222_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc20080222-2">[2]</a></sup> The company\'s first running prototype of <i>Red Rover</i> was completed the same year, and the concept lander was renamed <i>Griffin</i>.\n</p><p>On 28 July 2008, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> awarded Astrobotic funding for a concept study on "regolith moving methods",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the next year, Astrobotic began to receive Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) funding from NASA totaling over $795,000 to investigate prospecting for lunar resources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which eventually led to a concept called <i>Polar Excavator</i>.\n</p><p>On 15 October 2010, NASA awarded a contract to Astrobotic for Innovative Lunar Demonstrations Data (ILDD) firm-fixed price indefinite-delivery/indefinite-quantity contracts with a total value up to $30.1 million over a period of up to five years, and in December, NASA\'s $500,000 ILDD project for further Lunar Demonstrations Data was awarded to Astrobotic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Astrobotic\'s "Technologies Enabling Exploration of Skylights, Lava Tubes, and Caves", was a Phase I selection for <a href="/wiki/NASA_Institute_for_Advanced_Concepts" title="NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts">NASA Innovative Advanced Concepts</a> (NIAC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In April 2011, Astrobotic received a $599,000 two-year contract to develop a scalable gravity offload device for testing rover mobility in simulated lunar gravity under NASA\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_Business_Technology_Transfer_Program" title="Small Business Technology Transfer Program">Small Business Technology Transfer Program</a> (STTR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2012, David Gump left the position of President of Astrobotic and John Thornton took the reins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On April 30, 2014, NASA announced that Astrobotic Technologies was one of the three companies selected for the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_CATALYST" title="Lunar CATALYST">Lunar CATALYST</a> initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CATALYST_release_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CATALYST_release-9">[9]</a></sup>  NASA was negotiating a 3-year no-funds-exchanged Space Act Agreement (SAA) where the <i>Griffin</i> lander may be involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CATALYST_page_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CATALYST_page-10">[10]</a></sup> The CATALYST agreement was  extended in October 2017 for 2 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCAT_20171031_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCAT_20171031-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On June 2, 2016, Astrobotic Technology announced a new design of its <i>Griffin</i> concept lander and named it <i>Peregrine</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN_June_2016_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SN_June_2016-12">[12]</a></sup> Airbus Defence and Space signed a memorandum of understanding to provide engineering support for Astrobotic as it refines the lander\'s design. In December 2016 Astrobotic slipped their estimated launch date to 2019 and separated from the <a href="/wiki/Google_Lunar_X_Prize" title="Google Lunar X Prize">Google Lunar X Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astro_leave_XPrize_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Astro_leave_XPrize-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On November 29, 2018 Astrobotic was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Lunar_Payload_Services" title="Commercial Lunar Payload Services">Commercial Lunar Payload Services</a> contract by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, which makes it eligible to bid on delivering science and technology payloads to the Moon for NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLPS_win_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLPS_win-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_payload_pricing">Commercial payload pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial payload pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As of 2018, payload delivery to lunar orbit is $300,000 / kg; delivery to the lunar surface is $1,200,000 / kg; and $2,000,000 per kilogram ($910,000/lb) for deploying a rover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_missions">Lunar missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Icebreaker_to_the_north_pole"><i>Icebreaker</i> to the north pole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Icebreaker to the north pole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In April 2011, Astrobotic contracted with <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> launch of a lunar north pole mission for as early as December 2013.  The mission was intended to launch the <i>Griffin</i> lander and deliver "a small <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">rover</a> and up to about 240 pounds (110&#160;kg) of payload to the surface of the Moon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20110208_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw20110208-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xpf20110303_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-xpf20110303-17">[17]</a></sup> The launch date slipped to 2015, and it was first named <i>Polar Excavator</i>, and then <i>Icebreaker</i>, that would target the lunar north pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This expedition\'s rover was to be <i>Polaris</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbt20120529_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbt20120529-20">[20]</a></sup> A model of the <i>Polaris</i> rover was unveiled in October 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  and the company indicated that they were still under contract to SpaceX for a Falcon 9 mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fox20111027_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-fox20111027-22">[22]</a></sup> The launch date further slipped to 2016, and Astrobotic contracted with two other GLXP teams including Team <a href="/wiki/Hakuto" title="Hakuto">Hakuto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Team_AngelicvM" title="Team AngelicvM">Team AngelicvM</a> to share the launch expenses. The agreement was to launch the rovers of all teams on a single <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9_v1.1" title="Falcon 9 v1.1">Falcon 9</a> which would then use the Astrobotic <i>Griffin</i> lander.  After landing on the lunar surface, all teams would have competed against each other to achieve the specific GLXP objectives and earn the various prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glxp20150223_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-glxp20150223-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnet20150223_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnet20150223-24">[24]</a></sup> The <i>Griffin</i> lander was never built, and <i>Icebreaker</i> mission was not launched.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_1">Mission 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mission 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In July 2017, Astrobotic announced an agreement had been reached with United Launch Alliance (ULA) to launch their <i>Peregrine</i> lander aboard an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>. This announcement seemingly indicates Astrobotic canceled plans to launch on a Falcon 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By May 2018, its first lunar lander mission, simply called Mission 1 (M1) was reported to have 12 customers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12_customers_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-12_customers-26">[26]</a></sup> including small rovers from <a href="/wiki/Hakuto" title="Hakuto">Hakuto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Team_AngelicvM" title="Team AngelicvM">Team AngelicvM</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_Space_Agency" title="Mexican Space Agency">Mexican Space Agency</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_launch_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_launch-27">[27]</a></sup> and a larger rover from the Carnegie Mellon University named <i>Andy</i> that has a mass of 33&#160;kg (73&#160;lb) and is 103 cm tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andy_from_PRL_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andy_from_PRL-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the payloads aboard the lander is a library, in micro print on nickel, which will include <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> contents and <a href="/wiki/Long_Now_Foundation" title="Long Now Foundation">Long Now Foundation</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_Project" title="Rosetta Project">Rosetta Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  The Mission 1 is planned to be launched in 2020<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> on an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfn-astrobotic_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfn-astrobotic-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>M1 will carry a maximum payload of 35 kg, and it is planned to land on <a href="/wiki/Lacus_Mortis" title="Lacus Mortis">Lacus Mortis</a>, a relatively flat plateau at 44&#176;N 25&#176;E, and operate for about 8 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup> The payload mass for the future M2 mission is 175 kg, and the M3 and later missions would carry the full payload capacity of 265 kg. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peregrine_lander"><i>Peregrine</i> lander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Peregrine lander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width:25em;"><caption><i>Peregrine</i></caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <i>Peregrine</i> lander was announced in 2016. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN_June_2016_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-SN_June_2016-12">[12]</a></sup> It inherits designs from their previous concept lander called <i>Griffin</i>, which was larger but with the same payload capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN_June_2016_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-SN_June_2016-12">[12]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AT_Griffin_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-AT_Griffin-32">[32]</a></sup> Astrobotic had contracted <a href="/wiki/Airbus_Defence_and_Space" title="Airbus Defence and Space">Airbus Defence and Space</a> to provide additional engineering support as they refine the lander\'s design.\n</p><p><i>Peregrine</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> bus structure is mainly manufactured out of aluminum alloy, and it is reconfigurable for specific missions. Its propulsion system features a cluster of five ISE-100 thrusters, built by <a href="/wiki/Aerojet_Rocketdyne" title="Aerojet Rocketdyne">Aerojet Rocketdyne</a> that are based on the Divert and Attitude Control System thrusters it developed for missile defense applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup>  Each thruster produces 667 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">N</a> thrust. This propulsion system would propel the <a href="/wiki/Trans-lunar_injection" title="Trans-lunar injection">trans-lunar injection</a>, trajectory corrections, <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit insertion</a>, and powered descent.  The propulsion system is capable of delivering an orbiter to the Moon and then performing a powered soft landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup> The lander would carry up to 450&#160;kg (990&#160;lb) of bi-propellant mass in four tanks; its composition is MON-25/<a href="/wiki/Monomethylhydrazine" title="Monomethylhydrazine">MMH</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">hypergolic bi-propellant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a> (orientation), the spacecraft uses twelve thrusters (45 N each) also powered by MON-25/MMH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The spacecraft\'s avionics systems incorporate guidance and navigation to the Moon, and a Doppler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LiDAR" title="LiDAR">LiDAR</a> to assist the automated landing on four legs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN_June_2016_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-SN_June_2016-12">[12]</a></sup> Its landing ellipse is 24 km &#215; 6 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Peregrine</i> is about 2.5 m wide and 1.9 m tall, and it would be able to deliver up to 265&#160;kg (584&#160;lb) of payload to the surface of the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SN_June_2016_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-SN_June_2016-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SR_June_2016_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-SR_June_2016-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Its electrical systems will be powered by a lithium-ion battery that is recharged by a <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panel</a> made of GaInP/GaAs/Ge. Radiators and thermal insulators are used to dispose excess heat, but the lander does not carry heaters, so the first few <i>Peregrine</i> landers are not expected to survive the lunar night,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup> which lasts 14 Earth days. Future missions could be adapted to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-9"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For communications to Earth, the lander uses different frequencies within the <a href="/wiki/X_band" title="X band">X band</a> range for uplink as well as downlink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-10"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup> Following landing, a 2.4 GHz <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> modem enables wireless communication between the lander and deployed rovers on the lunar surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018_15-11"><a href="#cite_note-Peregrine_Payload_User_Guide_2018-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CubeRover">CubeRover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: CubeRover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/CubeRover" title="CubeRover">CubeRover</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/CubeRover" title="CubeRover">CubeRover</a> is a class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_rover" title="Planetary rover">planetary rovers</a> with a standardized format meant to accelerate the pace of space exploration. The idea is equivalent to that of the successful <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">CubeSat</a> format, with a standardized architecture to assemble new units that will be all compatible, modular, and inexpensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Campbell_2018_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Campbell_2018-36">[36]</a></sup> The rover class concept is being developed by Astrobotic Technology in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>, and it is partly funded by NASA awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Campbell_2018_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Campbell_2018-36">[36]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principal_Investigator" title="Principal Investigator">Principal Investigator</a> of the program is Andrew Horchler. The first CubeRover is planned to be deployed on the Moon in 2020 on board Astrobotic\'s <i>Peregrine</i> lander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_2018_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_2018-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It is called <i>Andy</i>, has a mass of 33&#160;kg (73&#160;lb) and is 103 cm tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andy_from_PRL_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Andy_from_PRL-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">Exploration of Mars</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">Exploration of the Moon</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">Lunar rover</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrobotic_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://www.lunarexplorers.net/node/386" rel="nofollow">"Astrobotic Technology and Raytheon Collaborate to Pursue Google Lunar X Prize"</a>. lunarexplorers.net<span class="reference-accessdate">.
