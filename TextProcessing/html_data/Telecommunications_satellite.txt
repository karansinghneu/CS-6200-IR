<b>communications satellite</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellite</a> that relays and amplifies <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> telecommunications signals via a <a href="/wiki/Transponder_(satellite_communications)" title="Transponder (satellite communications)">transponder</a>; it creates a <a href="/wiki/Communication_channel" title="Communication channel">communication channel</a> between a source <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">receiver</a> at different locations on Earth. Communications satellites are used for <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> applications. There are 2,134 communications satellites in Earth&#8217;s orbit, used by both private and government organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Many are in <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> 22,200 miles (35,700&#160;km) above the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, so that the satellite appears stationary at the same point in the sky, so the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_dish" title="Satellite dish">satellite dish</a> antennas of ground stations can be aimed permanently at that spot and do not have to move to track it.\n</p><p>The high frequency <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> used for telecommunications links travel by <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line of sight</a> and so are obstructed by the curve of the Earth.  The purpose of communications satellites is to relay the signal around the curve of the Earth allowing communication between widely separated geographical points.  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Communications satellites use a wide range of radio and <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a>. To avoid signal interference, international organizations have regulations for which frequency ranges or "bands" certain organizations are allowed to use. This allocation of bands minimizes the risk of signal interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerospace.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-aerospace.org-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The concept of the geostationary communications satellite was first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>, along with Vahid K. Sanadi building on work by <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a>.  In October 1945 Clarke published an article titled "Extraterrestrial Relays" in the British magazine <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_World" title="Wireless World">Wireless World</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The article described the fundamentals behind the deployment of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">artificial satellites</a> in geostationary orbits for the purpose of relaying radio signals. Thus, Arthur C. Clarke is often quoted as being the <a href="/wiki/Invention" title="Invention">inventor</a> of the communications satellite and the term \'Clarke Belt\' employed as a description of the orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Decades later a project named <a href="/wiki/Communication_Moon_Relay" title="Communication Moon Relay">Communication Moon Relay</a> was a telecommunication project carried out by the United States Navy. Its objective was to develop a secure and reliable method of wireless communication by using the Moon as a passive reflector and a natural communications satellite.\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">artificial Earth satellite</a> was <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>. Put into orbit by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> on October 4, 1957, it was equipped with an on-board <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>-<a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> that worked on two frequencies: 20.005 and 40.002&#160; MHz. Sputnik 1 was launched as a major step in the exploration of space and rocket development. However, it was not placed in orbit for the purpose of sending data from one point on earth to another.\n</p><p>The first satellite to relay communications was <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_1" title="Pioneer 1">Pioneer 1</a>, an intended lunar probe. Though the spacecraft only made it about halfway to the moon, it flew high enough to carry out the proof of concept relay of telemetry across the world, first from Cape Canaveral to Manchester, England; then from Hawaii to Cape Canaveral; and finally, across the world from Hawaii to Manchester.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneering2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pioneering2-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first satellite purpose-built to relay communications was NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/SCORE_(satellite)" title="SCORE (satellite)">Project SCORE</a> in 1958, which used a tape recorder to <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">store and forward</a> voice messages. It was used to send a Christmas greeting to the world from U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Courier 1B, built by <a href="/wiki/Philco" title="Philco">Philco</a>, launched in 1960, was the world\'s first active repeater satellite.\n</p><p>The first artificial satellite used solely to further advances in global communications was a balloon named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echo_1" title="Echo 1">Echo 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Echo 1 was the world\'s first artificial communications satellite capable of relaying signals to other points on Earth. It soared 1,600 kilometres (1,000&#160;mi) above the planet after its Aug. 12, 1960 launch, yet relied on humanity\'s oldest flight technology &#8212; ballooning. Launched by NASA, Echo 1 was a 30-metre (100&#160;ft) aluminized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_film_(biaxially_oriented)" title="PET film (biaxially oriented)">PET film</a> balloon that served as a <a href="/wiki/Passive_repeater" title="Passive repeater">passive reflector</a> for radio communications. The world\'s first inflatable satellite &#8212; or "satelloon", as they were informally known &#8212; helped lay the foundation of today\'s satellite communications. The idea behind a communications satellite is simple: Send data up into space and beam it back down to another spot on the globe. Echo 1 accomplished this by essentially serving as an enormous mirror, 10 stories tall, that could be used to reflect communications signals.\n</p><p>There are two major classes of communications satellites, <i><a href="/wiki/Balloon_satellite" title="Balloon satellite">passive</a></i> and <i>active</i>. Passive satellites only <a href="/wiki/Reflector_(antenna)" title="Reflector (antenna)">reflect</a> the signal coming from the source, toward the direction of the receiver. With passive satellites, the reflected signal is not amplified at the satellite, and only a very small amount of the transmitted energy actually reaches the receiver.  Since the satellite is so far above Earth, the radio signal is attenuated due to <a href="/wiki/Free-space_path_loss" title="Free-space path loss">free-space path loss</a>, so the signal received on Earth is very, very weak. Active satellites, on the other hand, amplify the received signal before retransmitting it to the receiver on the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerospace.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-aerospace.org-3">[3]</a></sup> Passive satellites were the first communications satellites, but are little used now.\n<a href="/wiki/Telstar" title="Telstar">Telstar</a> was the second active, direct relay communications satellite. Belonging to <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T</a> as part of a multi-national agreement between AT&amp;T, <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Telephone Laboratories</a>, NASA, the British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Post_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="General Post Office (United Kingdom)">General Post Office</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Orange_S.A." title="Orange S.A.">French National PTT</a> (Post Office) to develop satellite communications, it was launched by NASA from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> on July 10, 1962, in the first privately sponsored space launch. <a href="/wiki/Relay_program" title="Relay program">Relay 1</a> was launched on December 13, 1962, and it became the first satellite to transmit across the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> on November 22, 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-SP-93_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-SP-93-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>An immediate antecedent of the geostationary satellites was the <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Syncom" title="Syncom">Syncom 2</a>, launched on July 26, 1963. Syncom 2 was the first communications satellite in a <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous orbit</a>. It revolved around the earth once per day at constant speed, but because it still had north-south motion, special equipment was needed to track it. Its successor, <a href="/wiki/Syncom" title="Syncom">Syncom 3</a> was the first geostationary communications satellite. Syncom 3 obtained a geosynchronous orbit, without a north-south motion, making it appear from the ground as a stationary object in the sky.\n</p><p>Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rovers</a>, landers on the surface of Mars have used orbiting spacecraft as communications satellites for relaying their data to Earth. The landers use <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> transmitters to send their data to the orbiters, which then relay the data to Earth using either <a href="/wiki/X_band" title="X band">X band</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">Ka band</a> frequencies. These higher frequencies, along with more powerful transmitters and larger antennas, permit the orbiters to send the data much faster than the landers could manage transmitting directly to Earth, which conserves valuable time on the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">NASA Deep Space Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_orbits">Satellite orbits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Satellite orbits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Communications satellites usually have one of three primary types of <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, while other <a href="/wiki/List_of_orbits" title="List of orbits">orbital classifications</a> are used to further specify orbital details.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Geostationary satellites have a <i><a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a></i> (GEO), which is 36,000 kilometres (22,000&#160;mi) from Earth&#8217;s surface. This orbit has the special characteristic that the apparent position of the satellite in the sky when viewed by a ground observer does not change, the satellite appears to "stand still" in the sky.  This is because the satellite\'s orbital period is the same as the rotation rate of the Earth.  The advantage of this orbit is that ground antennas do not have to track the satellite across the sky, they can be fixed to point at the location in the sky the satellite appears.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">Medium Earth orbit</a></i> (MEO) satellites are closer to Earth. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_altitude" title="Orbital altitude">Orbital altitudes</a> range from 2,000 to 36,000 kilometres (1,200 to 22,400&#160;mi) above Earth.</li>\n<li>The region below medium orbits is referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a></i> (LEO), and is about 160 to 2,000 kilometres (99 to 1,243&#160;mi) above Earth.</li></ul>\n<p>As satellites in MEO and LEO orbit the Earth faster, they do not remain visible in the sky to a fixed point on Earth continually like a geostationary satellite, but appear to a ground observer to cross the sky and "set" when they go behind the Earth. Therefore, to provide continuous communications capability with these lower orbits requires a larger number of satellites, so one will always be in the sky for transmission of communication signals.  However, due to their relatively small distance to the Earth their signals are stronger.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Low_Earth_orbit_.28LEO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_Earth_orbit_(LEO)">Low Earth orbit (LEO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Low Earth orbit (LEO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4300" data-file-width="4300" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg/220px-Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg/330px-Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg/440px-Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orbits_around_earth_scale_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a> in Cyan</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO) typically is a circular orbit about 160 to 2,000 kilometres (99 to 1,243&#160;mi) above the earth\'s surface<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and, correspondingly, a period (time to revolve around the earth) of about 90 minutes.\n</p><p>Because of their low altitude, these satellites are only visible from within a radius of roughly 1,000 kilometres (620&#160;mi) from the sub-satellite point. In addition, satellites in low earth orbit change their position relative to the ground position quickly. So even for local applications, a large number of satellites are needed if the mission requires uninterrupted connectivity.\n</p><p>Low-Earth-orbiting satellites are less expensive to launch into orbit than geostationary satellites and, due to proximity to the ground, do not require as high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_strength" title="Signal strength">signal strength</a> (<i>Recall that signal strength falls off as the square of the distance from the source, so the effect is dramatic)</i>. Thus there is a trade off between the number of satellites and their cost.\n</p><p>In addition, there are important differences in the onboard and ground equipment needed to support the two types of missions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_constellation">Satellite constellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Satellite constellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">Satellite constellation</a></div>\n<p>A group of satellites working in concert is known as a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">satellite constellation</a>. Two such constellations, intended to provide <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">satellite phone</a> services, primarily to remote areas, are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridium_(satellite)" title="Iridium (satellite)">Iridium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Globalstar" title="Globalstar">Globalstar</a> systems. The Iridium system has 66 satellites.\n</p><p>It is also possible to offer discontinuous coverage using a low-Earth-orbit satellite capable of storing data received while passing over one part of Earth and transmitting it later while passing over another part.  This will be the case with the CASCADE system of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/CASSIOPE" title="CASSIOPE">CASSIOPE</a> communications satellite. Another system using this store and forward method is <a href="/wiki/Orbcomm" title="Orbcomm">Orbcomm</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Medium_Earth_orbit_.28MEO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium_Earth_orbit_(MEO)">Medium Earth orbit (MEO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medium Earth orbit (MEO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">Medium Earth orbit</a></div>\n<p>A MEO is a satellite in orbit somewhere between 2,000 and 35,786 kilometres (1,243 and 22,236&#160;mi) above the earth&#8217;s surface.  MEO satellites are similar to LEO satellites in functionality. MEO satellites are visible for much longer periods of time than LEO satellites, usually between 2 and 8 hours. MEO satellites have a larger coverage area than LEO satellites. A MEO satellite&#8217;s longer duration of visibility and wider footprint means fewer satellites are needed in a MEO network than a LEO network. One disadvantage is that a MEO satellite&#8217;s distance gives it a longer time delay and weaker signal than a LEO satellite, although these limitations are not as severe as those of a GEO satellite.\n</p><p>Like LEOs, these satellites don&#8217;t maintain a stationary distance from the earth.  This is in contrast to the geostationary orbit, where satellites are always 35,786 kilometres (22,236&#160;mi) from the earth.\n</p><p>Typically the orbit of a medium earth orbit satellite is about 16,000 kilometres (10,000&#160;mi) above earth. In various patterns, these satellites make the trip around earth in anywhere from 2 to 8 hours.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1962, the first communications satellite, <b>Telstar</b>, was launched. It was a medium earth orbit satellite designed to help facilitate high-speed telephone signals. Although it was the first practical way to transmit signals over the horizon, its major drawback was soon realized. Because its orbital period of about 2.5 hours did not match the Earth\'s rotational period of 24 hours, continuous coverage was impossible. It was apparent that multiple MEOs needed to be used in order to provide continuous coverage.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Geostationary_orbit_.28GEO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Geostationary_orbit_(GEO)">Geostationary orbit (GEO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geostationary orbit (GEO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">Geostationary orbit</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geostat.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Geostat.gif/220px-Geostat.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Geostat.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geostat.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">Geostationary orbit</a></div></div></div>\n<p>To an observer on Earth, a satellite in a geostationary orbit appears motionless, in a fixed position in the sky.  This is because it revolves around the Earth at Earth\'s own <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a> (one revolution per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">sidereal day</a>, in an <a href="/wiki/Non-inclined_orbit" title="Non-inclined orbit">equatorial orbit</a>).\n</p><p>A geostationary orbit is useful for communications because ground antennas can be aimed at the satellite without their having to track the satellite\'s motion. This is relatively inexpensive.\n</p><p>In applications that require a large number of ground antennas, such as <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> distribution, the savings in ground equipment can more than outweigh the cost and complexity of placing a satellite into orbit.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>The first geostationary satellite was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syncom_3" title="Syncom 3">Syncom 3</a>, launched on August 19, 1964, and used for communication across the Pacific starting with television coverage of the <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Summer Olympics</a>. Shortly after Syncom 3, <a href="/wiki/Intelsat_I" title="Intelsat I">Intelsat I</a>, aka <i>Early Bird</i>, was launched on April 6, 1965, and placed in orbit at 28&#176; west longitude.  It was the first geostationary satellite for telecommunications over the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.</li>\n<li>On November 9, 1966, Canada\'s first geostationary satellite serving the continent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anik_A1" title="Anik A1">Anik A1</a>, was launched by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telesat_Canada" title="Telesat Canada">Telesat Canada</a>, with the United States following suit with the launch of <a href="/wiki/Westar_1" title="Westar 1">Westar 1</a> by <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a> on April 13, 1967.</li>\n<li>On May 30, 1977, the first geostationary communications satellite in the world to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three-axis_stabilized" title="Three-axis stabilized">three-axis stabilized</a> was launched: the experimental satellite <a href="/wiki/ATS-6" title="ATS-6">ATS-6</a> built for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.</li>\n<li>After the launches of the Telstar through Westar 1 satellites, RCA Americom (later GE Americom, now <a href="/wiki/SES_S.A." title="SES S.A.">SES</a>) launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satcom_1" title="Satcom 1">Satcom 1</a> in 1975.  It was Satcom 1 that was instrumental in helping early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cable_TV" title="Cable TV">cable TV</a> channels such as WTBS (now <a href="/wiki/TBS_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="TBS (U.S. TV channel)">TBS</a>), <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christian_Broadcasting_Network" title="Christian Broadcasting Network">CBN</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Freeform_(TV_channel)" title="Freeform (TV channel)">Freeform</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Weather_Channel_(United_States)" title="The Weather Channel (United States)">The Weather Channel</a> become successful, because these channels distributed their programming to all of the local cable TV <a href="/wiki/Cable_television_headend" title="Cable television headend">headends</a> using the satellite.  Additionally, it was the first satellite used by broadcast television networks in the United States, like <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>, to distribute programming to their local affiliate stations.  Satcom 1 was widely used because it had twice the communications capacity of the competing Westar 1 in America (24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transponder_(Satellite_communications)" title="Transponder (Satellite communications)">transponders</a> as opposed to the 12 of Westar 1), resulting in lower transponder-usage costs. Satellites in later decades tended to have even higher transponder numbers.</li></ul>\n<p>By 2000, <b>Hughes Space and Communications</b> (now <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Satellite_Development_Center" title="Boeing Satellite Development Center">Boeing Satellite Development Center</a>) had built nearly 40 percent of the more than one hundred satellites in service worldwide. Other major satellite manufacturers include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Systems/Loral" title="Space Systems/Loral">Space Systems/Loral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Star_Bus" title="Star Bus">Star Bus</a> series, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">Indian Space Research Organisation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Space_Systems" title="Lockheed Martin Space Systems">Lockheed Martin</a> (owns the former RCA Astro Electronics/GE Astro Space business), <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a>, Alcatel Space, now <a href="/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space" title="Thales Alenia Space">Thales Alenia Space</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Spacebus" title="Spacebus">Spacebus</a> series, and <a href="/wiki/Astrium" title="Astrium">Astrium</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molniya_orbit">Molniya orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Molniya orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Molniya_orbit" title="Molniya orbit">Molniya orbit</a></div>\n<p>Geostationary satellites must operate above the equator and therefore appear lower on the horizon as the receiver gets farther from the equator. This will cause problems for extreme northerly latitudes, affecting connectivity and causing <a href="/wiki/Multipath_interference" title="Multipath interference">multipath</a> interference (caused by signals reflecting off the ground and into the ground antenna).\n</p><p>Thus, for areas close to the North (and South) Pole, a geostationary satellite may appear below the horizon. Therefore, Molniya orbit satellites have been launched, mainly in Russia, to alleviate this problem.\n</p><p>Molniya orbits can be an appealing alternative in such cases. The Molniya orbit is highly inclined, guaranteeing good elevation over selected positions during the northern portion of the orbit. (Elevation is the extent of the satellite\'s position above the horizon. Thus, a satellite at the horizon has zero elevation and a satellite directly overhead has elevation of 90 degrees.)\n</p><p>The Molniya orbit is designed so that the satellite spends the great majority of its time over the far northern latitudes, during which its ground footprint moves only slightly. Its period is one half day, so that the satellite is available for operation over the targeted region for six to nine hours every second revolution. In this way a constellation of three Molniya satellites (plus in-orbit spares) can provide uninterrupted coverage.\n</p><p>The first satellite of the <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(satellite)" title="Molniya (satellite)">Molniya</a> series was launched on April 23, 1965 and was used for experimental <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">transmission</a> of TV <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signalling_(telecommunication)" title="Signalling (telecommunication)">signals</a> from a Moscow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uplink" title="Uplink">uplink</a> station to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downlink" title="Downlink">downlink</a> stations located in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and the Russian Far East, in <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">Norilsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khabarovsk" title="Khabarovsk">Khabarovsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magadan" title="Magadan">Magadan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladivostok" title="Vladivostok">Vladivostok</a>. In November 1967 Soviet engineers created a unique <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> of national TV <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">network</a> of <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Orbita_(TV_system)" title="Orbita (TV system)">Orbita</a>, that was based on Molniya satellites.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_orbit">Polar orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Polar orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">Polar orbit</a></div>\n<p>In the United States, the National Polar-orbiting Operational Environmental Satellite System (NPOESS) was established in 1994 to consolidate the polar satellite operations of\nNASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration) \nNOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration). NPOESS manages a number of satellites for various purposes; for example, METSAT for meteorological satellite, EUMETSAT for the European branch of the program, and METOP for meteorological operations.\n</p><p>These orbits are sun synchronous, meaning that they cross the equator at the same local time each day. For example, the satellites in the NPOESS (civilian) orbit will cross the equator, going from south to north, at times 1:30 P.M., 5:30 P.M., and 9:30 P.M.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Communications Satellites are usually composed of the following subsystems:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Communication Payload, normally composed of <a href="/wiki/Transponder_(satellite_communications)" title="Transponder (satellite communications)">transponders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a>, and switching systems</li>\n<li>Engines used to bring the satellite to its desired orbit</li>\n<li>A <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping" title="Orbital station-keeping"> station keeping tracking and stabilization subsystem</a> used to keep the satellite in the right orbit, with its antennas pointed in the right direction, and its power system pointed towards the sun</li>\n<li>Power subsystem, used to power the Satellite systems, normally composed of <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>, and batteries that maintain power during <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a></li>\n<li>Command and Control subsystem, which maintains communications with ground control stations. The ground control Earth stations monitor the satellite performance and control its functionality during various phases of its life-cycle.</li></ul>\n<p>The bandwidth available from a satellite depends upon the number of transponders provided by the satellite. Each service (TV, Voice, Internet, radio) requires a different amount of bandwidth for transmission. This is typically known as link budgeting and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_simulator" title="Network simulator">network simulator</a> can be used to arrive at the exact value.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency_Allocation_for_satellite_systems">Frequency Allocation for satellite systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Frequency Allocation for satellite systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Allocating frequencies to satellite services is a complicated process which requires international coordination and planning. This is carried out under the auspices of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.itu.int/en/Pages/default.aspx" rel="nofollow">International Telecommunication Union (ITU).</a>\nTo facilitate frequency planning, the world is divided into three regions:\nRegion 1: Europe, Africa, what was formerly the Soviet Union, and Mongolia\nRegion 2: North and South America and Greenland\nRegion 3: Asia (excluding region 1 areas), Australia, and the southwest Pacific\n</p><p>Within these regions, frequency bands are allocated to various satellite services, although a given service may be allocated different frequency bands in different regions. Some of the services provided by satellites are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Fixed-satellite_service" title="Fixed-satellite service">Fixed satellite service (FSS)</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite" title="Direct-broadcast satellite">Broadcasting satellite service (BSS)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mobile-satellite_service" title="Mobile-satellite service">Mobile-satellite service</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">Radionavigation-satellite service</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Meteorological-satellite service</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telephone">Telephone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Telephone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_phone" title="Satellite phone">Satellite phone</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iridium_satellite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Iridium_satellite.jpg/170px-Iridium_satellite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Iridium_satellite.jpg/255px-Iridium_satellite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Iridium_satellite.jpg/340px-Iridium_satellite.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iridium_satellite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridium_(satellite)" title="Iridium (satellite)">Iridium</a> satellite</div></div></div>\n<p>The first and historically most important application for communication satellites was in intercontinental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_distance_telephony" title="Long distance telephony">long distance telephony</a>. The fixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Switched_Telephone_Network" title="Public Switched Telephone Network">Public Switched Telephone Network</a> relays <a href="/wiki/Telephone_call" title="Telephone call">telephone calls</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_line" title="Land line">land line</a> telephones to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_station" title="Earth station">earth station</a>, where they are then transmitted to a geostationary satellite.  The downlink follows an analogous path.  Improvements in <a href="/wiki/Submarine_communications_cable" title="Submarine communications cable">submarine communications cables</a> through the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber-optics" title="Fiber-optics">fiber-optics</a> caused some decline in the use of satellites for fixed telephony in the late 20th century.\n</p><p>Satellite communications are still used in many applications today. Remote islands such as <a href="/wiki/Ascension_Island" title="Ascension Island">Ascension Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diego_Garcia" title="Diego Garcia">Diego Garcia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">Easter Island</a>, where no submarine cables are in service, need satellite telephones. There are also regions of some continents and countries where landline telecommunications are rare to nonexistent, for example large regions of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, Canada, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, Russia, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Satellite communications also provide connection to the edges of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. Other land use for satellite phones are rigs at sea, a back up for hospitals, military, and recreation. Ships at sea, as well as planes, often use satellite phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Satellite phone systems can be accomplished by a number of means. On a large scale, often there will be a local telephone system in an isolated area with a link to the telephone system in a main land area. There are also services that will patch a radio signal to a telephone system. In this example, almost any type of satellite can be used. Satellite phones connect directly to a constellation of either geostationary or low-Earth-orbit satellites. Calls are then forwarded to a satellite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_station#Telecommunications_port" title="Earth station">teleport</a> connected to the Public Switched Telephone Network .\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">Satellite television</a></div>\n<p>As television became the main market, its demand for simultaneous delivery of relatively few signals of large <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> to many receivers being a more precise match for the capabilities of <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous</a> comsats.  Two satellite types are used for North American television and radio: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_broadcast_satellite" title="Direct broadcast satellite">Direct broadcast satellite</a> (DBS), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_Service_Satellite" title="Fixed Service Satellite">Fixed Service Satellite</a> (FSS).\n</p><p>The definitions of FSS and DBS satellites outside of North America, especially in Europe, are a bit more ambiguous. Most satellites used for direct-to-home television in Europe have the same high power output as DBS-class satellites in North America, but use the same linear polarization as FSS-class satellites. Examples of these are the <a href="/wiki/SES_Astra" title="SES Astra">Astra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eutelsat" title="Eutelsat">Eutelsat</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotbird" title="Hotbird">Hotbird</a> spacecraft in orbit over the European continent. Because of this, the terms FSS and DBS are more so used throughout the North American continent, and are uncommon in Europe.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fixed_Service_Satellite" title="Fixed Service Satellite">Fixed Service Satellites</a> use the <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C band</a>, and the lower portions of the <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a>. They are normally used for broadcast feeds to and from television networks and local affiliate stations (such as program feeds for network and syndicated programming, <a href="/wiki/Remote_broadcast" title="Remote broadcast">live shots</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Backhaul_(broadcasting)" title="Backhaul (broadcasting)">backhauls</a>), as well as being used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_learning" title="Distance learning">distance learning</a> by schools and universities, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_television" title="Business television">business television</a> (BTV), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Videoconferencing" title="Videoconferencing">Videoconferencing</a>, and general commercial telecommunications. FSS satellites are also used to distribute national cable channels to cable television headends.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Free-to-air" title="Free-to-air">Free-to-air</a> satellite TV channels are also usually distributed on FSS satellites in the K<sub>u</sub> band. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelsat_Americas_5" title="Intelsat Americas 5">Intelsat Americas 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_10R" title="Galaxy 10R">Galaxy 10R</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMC_3" title="AMC 3">AMC 3</a> satellites over <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> provide a quite large amount of FTA channels on their K<sub>u</sub> band <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponders</a>.\n</p><p>The American <a href="/wiki/Dish_Network" title="Dish Network">Dish Network</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-broadcast_satellite" title="Direct-broadcast satellite">DBS</a> service has also recently used FSS technology as well for their programming packages requiring their <a href="/wiki/SuperDish" title="SuperDish">SuperDish</a> antenna, due to Dish Network needing more capacity to carry local television stations per the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a>\'s "must-carry" regulations, and for more bandwidth to carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HDTV" title="HDTV">HDTV</a> channels.\n</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_broadcast_satellite" title="Direct broadcast satellite">direct broadcast satellite</a> is a communications satellite that transmits to small DBS <a href="/wiki/Satellite_dish" title="Satellite dish">satellite dishes</a> (usually 18 to 24 inches or 45 to 60&#160;cm in diameter). Direct broadcast satellites generally operate in the upper portion of the microwave <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a>. DBS technology is used for DTH-oriented (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct-To-Home" title="Direct-To-Home">Direct-To-Home</a>) satellite TV services, such as <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> and DISH Network in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Bell_TV" title="Bell TV">Bell TV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaw_Direct" title="Shaw Direct">Shaw Direct</a> in Canada, <a href="/wiki/Freesat" title="Freesat">Freesat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_(UK_and_Ireland)" title="Sky (UK and Ireland)">Sky</a> in the UK, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSTV" title="DSTV">DSTV</a> in South Africa.\n</p><p>Operating at lower frequency and lower power than DBS, FSS satellites require a much larger dish for reception (3 to 8 feet (1 to 2.5 m) in diameter for K<sub>u</sub> band, and 12 feet (3.6 m) or larger for C band).  They use <a href="/wiki/Linear_polarization" title="Linear polarization">linear polarization</a> for each of the transponders\' RF input and output (as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Circular_polarization" title="Circular polarization">circular polarization</a> used by DBS satellites), but this is a minor technical difference that users do not notice. FSS satellite technology was also originally used for DTH satellite TV from the late 1970s to the early 1990s in the United States in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TVRO" title="TVRO">TVRO</a> (TeleVision Receive Only) receivers and dishes. It was also used in its K<sub>u</sub> band form for the now-defunct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primestar" title="Primestar">Primestar</a> satellite TV service.\n</p><p>Some satellites have been launched that have transponders in the <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">K<sub>a</sub> band</a>, such as DirecTV\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SPACEWAY-1" title="SPACEWAY-1">SPACEWAY-1</a> satellite, and <a href="/wiki/Anik_(satellite)" title="Anik (satellite)">Anik F2</a>. NASA and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRO" title="ISRO">ISRO</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> have also launched experimental satellites carrying K<sub>a</sub> band beacons recently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some manufacturers have also introduced special antennas for mobile reception of DBS television. Using <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System (GPS)</a> technology as a reference, these antennas automatically re-aim to the satellite no matter where or how the vehicle (on which the antenna is mounted) is situated.  These mobile satellite antennas are popular with some <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">recreational vehicle</a> owners.  Such mobile DBS antennas are also used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JetBlue_Airways" title="JetBlue Airways">JetBlue Airways</a> for DirecTV (supplied by <a href="/wiki/LiveTV" title="LiveTV">LiveTV</a>, a subsidiary of JetBlue), which passengers can view on-board on LCD screens mounted in the seats.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_broadcasting">Radio broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Radio broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_radio" title="Satellite radio">Satellite radio</a></div>\n<p>Satellite radio offers audio <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast" title="Broadcast">broadcast</a> services in some countries, notably the United States. Mobile services allow listeners to roam a continent, listening to the same audio programming anywhere.\n</p><p>A satellite radio or subscription radio (SR) is a digital radio signal that is broadcast by a communications satellite, which covers a much wider geographical range than terrestrial radio signals.\n</p><p>Satellite radio offers a meaningful alternative to ground-based radio services in some countries, notably the United States. Mobile services, such as SiriusXM, and Worldspace, allow listeners to roam across an entire continent, listening to the same audio programming anywhere they go. Other services, such as Music Choice or Muzak\'s satellite-delivered content, require a fixed-location receiver and a dish antenna. In all cases, the antenna must have a clear view to the satellites. In areas where tall buildings, bridges, or even parking garages obscure the signal, repeaters can be placed to make the signal available to listeners.\n</p><p>Initially available for broadcast to stationary TV receivers, by 2004 popular mobile direct broadcast applications made their appearance with the arrival of two satellite radio systems in the United States: Sirius and XM Satellite Radio Holdings. Later they merged to become the conglomerate SiriusXM.\n</p><p>Radio services are usually provided by commercial ventures and are subscription-based. The various services are proprietary signals, requiring specialized hardware for decoding and playback. Providers usually carry a variety of news, weather, sports, and music channels, with the music channels generally being commercial-free.\n</p><p>In areas with a relatively high population density, it is easier and less expensive to reach the bulk of the population with terrestrial broadcasts. Thus in the UK and some other countries, the contemporary evolution of radio services is focused on Digital Audio Broadcasting (DAB) services or HD Radio, rather than satellite radio.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateur_radio">Amateur radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Amateur radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_satellite" title="Amateur radio satellite">Amateur radio satellite</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">Amateur radio</a> operators have access to amateur satellites, which have been designed specifically to carry amateur radio traffic. Most such satellites operate as spaceborne <a href="/wiki/Repeaters" title="Repeaters">repeaters</a>, and are generally accessed by amateurs equipped with <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VHF" title="VHF">VHF</a> radio equipment and highly directional <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yagi_antenna" title="Yagi antenna">Yagis</a> or dish antennas. Due to launch costs, most current amateur satellites are launched into fairly low Earth orbits, and are designed to deal with only a limited number of brief contacts at any given time. Some satellites also provide data-forwarding services using the <a href="/wiki/X.25" title="X.25">X.25</a> or similar protocols.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_access">Internet access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Internet access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Internet_access" title="Satellite Internet access">Satellite Internet access</a></div>\n<p>After the 1990s, satellite communication technology has been used as a means to connect to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> via broadband data connections. This can be very useful for users who are located in remote areas, and cannot access a <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> connection, or require high availability of services.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Communications_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Communications satellites are used for <a href="/wiki/Military_communications" title="Military communications">military communications</a> applications, such as <a href="/wiki/Global_Command_and_Control_System" title="Global Command and Control System">Global Command and Control Systems</a>. Examples of military systems that use communication satellites are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MILSTAR" title="MILSTAR">MILSTAR</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSCS" title="DSCS">DSCS</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FLTSATCOM" title="FLTSATCOM">FLTSATCOM</a> of the United States, <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> satellites, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> satellites (for instance <a href="/wiki/Skynet_(satellite)" title="Skynet (satellite)">Skynet</a>), and satellites of the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. India has launched its first Military Communication satellite <a href="/wiki/GSAT-7" title="GSAT-7">GSAT-7</a>, its transponders operate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHF" title="UHF">UHF</a>, <a href="/wiki/F_band_(NATO)" title="F band (NATO)">F</a>, <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ku_band" title="Ku band">K<sub>u</sub> band</a> bands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Typically military satellites operate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHF" title="UHF">UHF</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_High_Frequency" title="Super High Frequency">SHF</a> (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-band" title="X-band">X-band</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Extremely_high_frequency" title="Extremely high frequency">EHF</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Ka_band" title="Ka band">K<sub>a</sub> band</a>) frequency bands.\n</p>\n<div
