spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> with the Earth\'s atmospheric transmittance (or opacity) and the types of telescopes used to image parts of the spectrum.</div></div></div><p><b>Visible-light astronomy</b> encompasses a wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observations</a> via <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> that are sensitive in the range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> (<a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">optical telescopes</a>). Visible-light astronomy is part of optical astronomy, and differs from astronomies based on invisible types of light in the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic radiation spectrum</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">infrared waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_astronomy" title="Ultraviolet astronomy">ultraviolet waves</a>, <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">X-ray waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy" title="Gamma-ray astronomy">gamma-ray waves</a>. Visible light ranges from 380 to 750 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>. \n</p><p>Visible-light astronomy has existed as long as people have been looking up at the night sky, although it has since improved in its observational capabilities since the invention of the telescope, which is commonly credited to <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Hans Lippershey</a>, a German-Dutch spectacle-maker,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> although Galileo played a large role in the development and creation of telescopes. Visible-light astronomy continues to advance in the modern day, with projects such as the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Telescope</a> being projected for launch in the next few years. \n</p><p>Since visible-light astronomy is restricted to only visible light, no equipment is necessary for simply <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">star gazing</a>. This means that it\'s the most commonly participated in type of astronomy, as well as the oldest.  \n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning">Beginning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a></div><p>Before the advent of telescopes, astronomy was limited solely to unaided eyesight. Humans have been looking at stars and objects in the night sky for thousands of years, as is evident in the naming of many constellations, notably the largely Greek names used today. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="737" decoding="async" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg/220px-Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg/330px-Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg/440px-Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bertini_fresco_of_Galileo_Galilei_and_Doge_of_Venice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fresco by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Bertini" title="Giuseppe Bertini">Giuseppe Bertini</a> depicting Galileo showing the <a href="/wiki/Doge_of_Venice" title="Doge of Venice">Doge of Venice</a> how to use the telescope</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Hans Lippershey</a>, a German-Dutch spectacle-maker, is commonly credited as being the first to invent the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Lippershey</a> is the first recorded person to apply for a patent for a telescope,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> however it is unclear if <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Lippershey</a> was the first to build a telescope. Based only on uncertain descriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> which <a href="/wiki/Hans_Lippershey" title="Hans Lippershey">Lippershey</a> tried to obtain a patent for, Galileo made a telescope with about 3x magnification in the following year. Galileo later made improved versions with up to 30x magnification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With a <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope#Galileo\'s_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">Galilean telescope</a> the observer could see magnified, upright images on the earth&#8212;it was what is commonly known as a terrestrial telescope or a spyglass. Galileo could also use it to observe the sky, and for a time was one of those who could construct telescopes good enough for that purpose. On 25 August 1609, Galileo demonstrated one of his early telescopes, with a magnification of up to 8 or 9, to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venetian</a> lawmakers. Galileo\'s telescopes were also a profitable sideline; selling them to merchants who found them useful both at sea and as items of trade. He published his initial telescopic astronomical observations in March 1610 in a brief treatise entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Sidereus_Nuncius" title="Sidereus Nuncius">Sidereus Nuncius</a></i> (<i>Starry Messenger</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_day">Modern day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the modern day, visible-light astronomy is still practiced by many <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomers</a>, especially since <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> are much more widely available for the public, as compared to when they were first being invented. Government agencies, such as <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, are very involved in the modern day research and observation of visible objects and <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial bodies</a>. In the modern day, the highest quality pictures and data are obtained via <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">space telescopes</a>; <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> that are outside of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>. This allows for much clearer observations, as the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> is not disrupting the image and <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">viewing quality</a> of the telescope, meaning objects can be observed in much greater detail, and much more distant or low-light objects may be observed. Additionally, this means that observations are able to be made at any time, rather than only during the night.  \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">space telescope</a> created by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and was launched into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is still in operation today. The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>\'s four main instruments observe in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">near ultraviolet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">near infrared</a> <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectra</a>. Hubble\'s images are some of the most detailed images ever taken, leading to many breakthroughs in <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, such as accurately determining the rate of <a href="/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe" title="Expansion of the universe">expansion of the universe</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="James_Webb_Space_Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: James Webb Space Telescope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a></div>\n<p>The James Webb Space Telescope is the formal successor of the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is set to launch in March 30, 2021,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and is "one of the most ambitious and technically complex missions NASA has ever set its focus upon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The James Webb Space Telescope is a space-based telescope, and is set to orbit near the second <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrange point</a> of the Earth-Sun system, 1,500,000 kilometers (930,000 miles) from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_telescopes">Optical telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Optical telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">Optical telescope</a></div>\n<p>There are three main types of telescopes used in visible-light astronomy:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">Refracting telescopes</a>, which use lenses to form the image. Commonly used by amateur astronomers, especially for viewing brighter objects such as the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, due to lower cost and ease of usage.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">Reflecting telescopes</a>, which use mirrors to form the image. Commonly used for scientific purposes.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catadioptric_Telescope" title="Catadioptric Telescope">Catadioptric telescopes</a>, which use a combination of lenses and mirrors to form the image; essentially a combination of <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting</a> telescopes.</li></ul>\n<p>Each type of telescope suffers from different types of <a href="/wiki/Optical_aberration" title="Optical aberration">aberration</a>; <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescopes</a> have <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">chromatic aberration</a>, which causes colors to be shown on edges separating light and dark parts of the image, where there should not be such colors. This is due to the lens being unable to focus all colors to the same convergence point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">Reflecting telescopes</a> suffer from several types of optical inaccuracies, such as off-axis aberrations near the edges of the field of view. Catadioptric telescopes vary in the types of optical inaccuracies present, as there are numerous catadioptric telescope designs. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_ambient_brightness">Effect of ambient brightness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effect of ambient brightness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Light_pollution_europe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Light_pollution_europe.jpg/220px-Light_pollution_europe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Light_pollution_europe.jpg/330px-Light_pollution_europe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Light_pollution_europe.jpg/440px-Light_pollution_europe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Light_pollution_europe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Light pollution map of Europe</div></div></div><p>The visibility of celestial objects in the night sky is affected by light pollution, with the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> in the night sky historically hindering astronomical observation by increasing the amount of ambient lighting. With the advent of artificial light sources, however, <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> has been a growing problem for viewing the night sky. Special filters and modifications to light fixtures can help to alleviate this problem, but for the best views, both professional and amateur optical astronomers seek viewing sites located far from major urban areas. In order to avoid light pollution of Earth\'s sky, among other reasons, many telescopes are put outside of the Earth\'s atmosphere, where not only light pollution, but also atmospheric distortion and obscuration are minimized. \n</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commonly_observed_objects">Commonly observed objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible-light_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commonly observed objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most commonly observed objects tend to be ones that do not require a telescope to view, such as the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is a very commonly observed astronomical object, especially by <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skygazing" title="Skygazing">skygazers</a>. This is due to several reasons: the Moon is the brightest object in the night sky, the Moon is the largest object in the night sky, and the Moon has long been significant in many cultures, such as being the basis for many calendars. The Moon also does not require any kind of telescope or binoculars to see effectively, making it extremely convenient and common for people to observe.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">Meteors</a>, often called "shooting stars" are also commonly observed. <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">Meteor showers</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Perseids" title="Perseids">Perseids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonids" title="Leonids">Leonids</a>, make viewing meteors much easier, as a multitude of meteors are visible in a relatively short period of time.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">Planets</a> are usually observed with the aid of a telescope or binoculars. <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> is likely the easiest planet to observe without the aid of any instruments, as it is very bright, and can even be seen in daylight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> can also be seen without the aid of telescopes or binoculars.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">Constellations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> are also often observed, and have been used in the past for navigation, especially by ships at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> One of the most recognizable constellations is the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper" title="Big Dipper">Big Dipper</a>, which is part of the constellation <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Major" title="Ursa Major">Ursa Major</a>. Constellations also serve to help describe the location of other objects in the sky. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
