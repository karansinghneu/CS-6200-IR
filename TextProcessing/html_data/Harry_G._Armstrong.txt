(February 17, 1899 &#8211; February 5, 1983) was a <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>, a physician, and an airman. He is widely recognized as a pioneer in the field of aviation medicine. The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armstrong_Limit" title="Armstrong Limit">Armstrong Limit</a>", the altitude above which water boils at the temperature of the human body, is named after him.\n</p><p>Armstrong served in the Marines during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and the Army and Air Force from 1930 to 1957. As Director of the United States Aeromedical Research Laboratory, he applied his medical and aviation knowledge to the improvement of aircrew protection from temperature extremes and the lack of oxygen at high altitude.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" id="Early_life,_education,_and_military_career">Early life, education, and military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_George_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education, and military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Armstrong was born in <a href="/wiki/De_Smet,_South_Dakota" title="De Smet, South Dakota">De Smet, South Dakota</a>, in 1899. He attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>, but left after one year to enlist in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Marine_Corps" title="US Marine Corps">US Marine Corps</a>, serving with them as a private from October 1918 to March 1919. He then entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Dakota" title="University of South Dakota">University of South Dakota</a>, graduating in 1921. In 1925 he received his Doctor of Medicine Degree from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Louisville" title="University of Louisville">University of Louisville</a>. He entered the <a href="/wiki/Army_Medical_Department_(United_States)" title="Army Medical Department (United States)">Medical Corps Reserve</a> in April 1925. Armstrong entered the School of Aviation Medicine at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooks_Field,_Texas" title="Brooks Field, Texas">Brooks Field, Texas</a>, that September. Upon graduation in 1930, he was commissioned a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_First_Lieutenant" title="US First Lieutenant">first lieutenant</a> in the Regular Army Medical Corps.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_career">Military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_George_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1931, he was attached to the Air Corps and assigned as assistant surgeon. He was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States)" title="Captain (United States)">captain</a> in 1932, to <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_States)" title="Major (United States)">major</a> in 1938, <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">lieutenant colonel</a> (temporary) in February 1942, and to <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> (temporary) in August 1942. Serving alternately in England and the United States, in 1945 he became surgeon for the Air Division in the office of Military Government for Germany (U.S.), with headquarters in Berlin. In 1946, he was promoted to lieutenant colonel (permanent), returned to the School of Aviation Medicine at Randolph Field upon appointment as Assistant Commandant, and that July was named Commandant. In 1948, he was promoted <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general_(United_States)" title="Brigadier general (United States)">brigadier general</a> (temporary), then to <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">major general</a> (temporary).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surgeon_General_of_the_Air_Force">Surgeon General of the Air Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_George_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surgeon General of the Air Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In June 1949, he was assigned to Air Force headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, as Deputy Surgeon General of the U.S. Air Force, promoted to colonel (permanent) in October, and the following December was designated Surgeon General. Armstrong was promoted to brigadier general (permanent) October 9, 1951. In 1954, General Armstrong went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiesbaden,_Germany" title="Wiesbaden, Germany">Wiesbaden, Germany</a>, as Surgeon of the United States Air Forces in Europe.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_George_Armstrong&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Armstrong retired from the Air Force in 1957. He died in 1983 from heart disease.\n</p><p>In 1977, the Harry G. Armstrong Award for Scientific Excellence was created by the Air Force Aerospace Medical Research Laboratory.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_decorations">Awards and
