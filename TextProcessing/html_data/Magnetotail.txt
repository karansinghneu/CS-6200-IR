class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_lines" title="Magnetic field lines">magnetic field lines</a> of Earth\'s magnetosphere</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>magnetosphere</b> is a region of space surrounding an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particles</a> are manipulated or affected by that object\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ratcliffe_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ratcliffe-2">[2]</a></sup> It is created by a planet having an active interior <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo</a>.\n</p><p>In the space environment close to a planetary body, the magnetic field resembles a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole" title="Magnetic dipole">magnetic dipole</a>. Farther out, field lines can be significantly distorted by the flow of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrically_conducting" title="Electrically conducting">electrically conducting</a> <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>, as emitted from the Sun or a nearby star. e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen-4">[4]</a></sup> Planets having active magnetospheres, like the Earth, are capable of mitigating or blocking the effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" title="Cosmic radiation">cosmic radiation</a>, that also protects all living organisms from potentially detrimental and dangerous consequences. This is studied under the specialized scientific subjects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">plasma physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">space physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeronomy" title="Aeronomy">aeronomy</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_chronology" title="Magnetosphere chronology">Magnetosphere chronology</a></div>\n<p>Study of Earth\'s magnetosphere began in 1600, when <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">William Gilbert</a> discovered that the magnetic field on the surface of Earth resembled that on a <a href="/wiki/Terrella" title="Terrella">terrella</a>, a small, magnetized sphere. In the 1940s, <a href="/wiki/Walter_M._Elsasser" title="Walter M. Elsasser">Walter M. Elsasser</a> proposed the model of <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo theory</a>, which attributes <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">Earth\'s magnetic field</a> to the motion of  Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">outer core</a>. Through the use of <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometers</a>, scientists were able to study the variations in Earth\'s magnetic field as functions of both time and latitude and longitude. \n</p><p>Beginning in the late 1940s, rockets were used to study <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. In 1958, <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a>, the first of the Explorer series of space missions, was launched to study the intensity of cosmic rays above the atmosphere and measure the fluctuations in this activity. This mission observed the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a> (located in the inner region of Earth\'s magnetosphere), with the follow up <a href="/wiki/Explorer_3" title="Explorer 3">Explorer 3</a> later that year definitively proving its existence. Also during 1958, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">Eugene Parker</a> proposed the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, with the term \'magnetosphere\' being proposed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> in 1959 to explain how the solar wind interacted with the Earth\'s magnetic field. The later mission of Explorer 12 in 1961 led by the Cahill and Amazeen observation in 1963 of a sudden decrease in magnetic field strength near the noon-time meridian, later was named the <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">magnetopause</a>. By 1983, the <a href="/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer" title="International Cometary Explorer">International Cometary Explorer</a> observed the magnetotail, or the distant magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_behavior">Structure and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Magnetospheres are dependent on several variables: the type of astronomical object, the nature of sources of plasma and momentum, the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">period</a> of the object\'s spin, the nature of the axis about which the object spins, the axis of the magnetic dipole, and the magnitude and direction of the flow of <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.\n</p><p>The planetary distance where the magnetosphere can withstand the solar wind pressure is called the Chapman&#8211;Ferraro distance. article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">Bow shock</a></div>\n<p>The bow shock forms the outermost layer of the magnetosphere; the boundary between the magnetosphere and the ambient medium. For stars, this is usually the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>; for planets, the speed of the solar wind there decreases as it approaches the magnetopause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetosheath">Magnetosheath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Magnetosheath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetosheath" title="Magnetosheath">Magnetosheath</a></div>\n<p>The magnetosheath is the region of the magnetosphere between the bow shock and the magnetopause. It is formed mainly from shocked solar wind, though it contains a small amount of plasma from the magnetosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cluster_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-cluster-7">[7]</a></sup> It is an area exhibiting high particle <a href="/wiki/Energy_flux" title="Energy flux">energy flux</a>, where the direction and magnitude of the magnetic field varies erratically. This is caused by the collection of solar wind gas that has effectively undergone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermalization" title="Thermalization">thermalization</a>. It acts as a cushion that transmits the pressure from the flow of the solar wind and the barrier of the magnetic field from the object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Van_Allen_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Allen-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetopause">Magnetopause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Magnetopause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">Magnetopause</a></div>\n<p>The magnetopause is the area of the magnetosphere wherein the pressure from the planetary magnetic field is balanced with the pressure from the solar wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> It is the convergence of the shocked solar wind from the magnetosheath with the magnetic field of the object and plasma from the magnetosphere. Because both sides of this convergence contain magnetized plasma, the interactions between them are complex. The structure of the magnetopause depends upon the <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beta_(plasma_physics)" title="Beta (plasma physics)">beta</a> of the plasma, as well as the magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The magnetopause changes size and shape as the pressure from the solar wind fluctuates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetotail">Magnetotail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Magnetotail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Opposite the compressed magnetic field is the magnetotail, where the magnetosphere extends far beyond the astronomical object. It contains two lobes, referred to as the northern and southern tail lobes. Magnetic field lines in the northern tail lobe point towards the object while those in the southern tail lobe point away. The tail lobes are almost empty, with few charged particles opposing the flow of the solar wind. The two lobes are separated by a plasma sheet, an area where the magnetic field is weaker, and the density of charged particles is higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tail_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tail-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Earth.27s_magnetosphere"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth\'s_magnetosphere">Earth\'s magnetosphere</span><span href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, the magnetic field lines become almost horizontal, then return to reconnect at high latitudes. However, at high altitudes, the magnetic field is significantly distorted by the solar wind and its solar magnetic field. On the dayside of Earth, the magnetic field is significantly compressed by the solar wind to a distance of approximately 65,000 kilometers (40,000&#160;mi). Earth\'s bow shock is about 17 kilometers (11&#160;mi) thick<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and located about 90,000 kilometers (56,000&#160;mi) from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The magnetopause exists at a distance of several hundred kilometers above Earth\'s surface. Earth\'s magnetopause has been compared to a <a href="/wiki/Sieve" title="Sieve">sieve</a> because it allows solar wind particles to enter. <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability" title="Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz instability">Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz instabilities</a> occur when large swirls of plasma travel along the edge of the magnetosphere at a different velocity from the magnetosphere, causing the plasma to slip past. This results in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a>, and as the magnetic field lines break and reconnect, solar wind particles are able to enter the magnetosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On Earth\'s nightside, the magnetic field extends in the magnetotail, which lengthwise exceeds 6,300,000 kilometers (3,900,000&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> Earth\'s magnetotail is the primary source of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">polar aurora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tail_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-tail-10">[10]</a></sup> Also, NASA scientists have suggested that Earth\'s magnetotail might cause "dust storms" on the Moon by creating a potential difference between the day side and the night side.<sup class="reference" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Jupiter" title="Magnetosphere of Jupiter">magnetosphere of Jupiter</a> is the largest planetary magnetosphere in the Solar System, extending up to 7,000,000 kilometers (4,300,000&#160;mi) on the dayside and almost to the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> on the nightside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Jupiter\'s magnetosphere is stronger than Earth\'s by an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of
