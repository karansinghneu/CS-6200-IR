href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a> ever photographed. Except for the few stars in the foreground (which are bright and easily recognizable because only they have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffraction_spikes" title="Diffraction spikes">diffraction spikes</a>), every speck of light in the photo is an individual galaxy, some of them as old as 13.2 billion years; the observable universe is estimated to contain more than 2 trillion galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Cosmology</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> &#954;&#972;&#963;&#956;&#959;&#962;, <i>kosmos</i> "world" and -&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;, <i>-logia</i> "study of") is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> concerned with the studies of the origin and evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> to today and on into the <a href="/wiki/Future" title="Future">future</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific study</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe" title="Chronology of the universe">origin, evolution, and eventual fate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">Physical cosmology</a> is the scientific study of the universe\'s origin, its large-scale structures and dynamics, and its <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe" title="Ultimate fate of the universe">ultimate fate,</a> as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_science" title="Laws of science">laws of science</a> that govern these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term <i>cosmology</i> was first used in English in 1656 in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Blount_(lexicographer)" title="Thomas Blount (lexicographer)">Thomas Blount</a>\'s <i>Glossographia</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in 1731 taken up in Latin by <a href="/wiki/German_philosophy" title="German philosophy">German philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_Wolff_(philosopher)" title="Christian Wolff (philosopher)">Christian Wolff</a>, in <i>Cosmologia Generalis</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Religious_cosmology" title="Religious cosmology">Religious</a> or mythological cosmology is a body of beliefs based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythological</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esotericism" title="Esotericism">esoteric</a> literature and traditions of <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation myths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eschatology" title="Eschatology">eschatology</a>.\n</p><p>Physical cosmology is studied by scientists, such as <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicists</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophers</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysicians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_physics" title="Philosophy of physics">philosophers of physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_space_and_time" title="Philosophy of space and time">philosophers of space and time</a>.  Because of this shared scope with <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theories</a> in physical cosmology may include both <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> and non-scientific propositions, and may depend upon assumptions that cannot be <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">tested</a>. Cosmology differs from astronomy in that the former is concerned with the Universe as a whole while the latter deals with individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_object" title="Celestial object">celestial objects</a>. Modern physical cosmology is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> theory, which attempts to bring together <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">observational astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> more specifically, a standard parameterization of the Big Bang with <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM" title="Lambda-CDM">Lambda-CDM</a> model.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Astrophysics#Theoretical_astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">Theoretical astrophysicist</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Spergel" title="David Spergel">David N. Spergel</a> has described cosmology as a "historical science" because "when we look out in space, we look back in time" due to the finite nature of the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" through both mathematics and observation in an analysis of the whole universe. The universe is generally understood to have begun with the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, followed almost instantaneously by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a>; an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expansion of space</a> from which the universe is thought to have emerged <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">13.799 &#177; 0.021</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,000,000,000_(number)" title="1,000,000,000 (number)">billion</a> years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planck_2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planck_2015-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">Cosmogony</a> studies the origin of the Universe, and <a href="/wiki/Cosmography" title="Cosmography">cosmography</a> maps the features of the Universe.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Diderot</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclop&#233;die">Encyclop&#233;die</a>, cosmology is broken down into uranology (the science of the heavens), aerology (the science of the air), geology (the science of the continents), and hydrology (the science of waters).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Metaphysical cosmology has also been described as the placing of humans in the universe in relationship to all other entities. This is exemplified by <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>\'s observation that a man\'s place in that relationship: "He who does not know what the world is does not know where he is, and he who does not know for what purpose the world exists, does not know who he is, nor what the world is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_cosmology">Physical cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical cosmology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">Physical cosmology</a></div>\n<p>Physical cosmology is the branch of physics and astrophysics that deals with the study of the physical origins and evolution of the Universe. It also includes the study of the nature of the Universe on a large scale. In its earliest form, it was what is now known as "<a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a>", the study of the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">heavens</a>. Greek philosophers <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">Aristarchus of Samos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> proposed different cosmological theories. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geocentric" title="Geocentric">geocentric</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_system" title="Ptolemaic system">Ptolemaic system</a> was the prevailing theory until the 16th century when <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a>, and subsequently <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>, proposed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric" title="Heliocentric">heliocentric</a> system. This is one of the most famous examples of <a href="/wiki/Epistemological_rupture" title="Epistemological rupture">epistemological rupture</a> in physical cosmology.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb href="/wiki/Philosophiae_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Principia Mathematica</a></i>, published in 1687, was the first description of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Law of universal gravitation">law of universal gravitation</a>. It provided a physical mechanism for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws" title="Kepler\'s laws">Kepler\'s laws</a> and also allowed the anomalies in previous systems, caused by gravitational interaction between the planets, to be resolved. A fundamental difference between Newton\'s cosmology and those preceding it was the <a href="/wiki/Copernican_principle" title="Copernican principle">Copernican principle</a>&#8212;that the bodies on earth obey the same <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">physical laws</a> as all the celestial bodies. This was a crucial philosophical advance in physical cosmology.\n</p><p>Modern scientific cosmology is usually considered to have begun in 1917 with <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>\'s publication of his final modification of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> in the paper "Cosmological Considerations of the General Theory of Relativity" (although this paper was not widely available outside of Germany until the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War&#160;I</a>). General relativity prompted <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogonists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Sitter" title="Willem de Sitter">Willem de Sitter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schwarzschild" title="Karl Schwarzschild">Karl Schwarzschild</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a> to explore its astronomical ramifications, which enhanced the ability of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomers</a> to study very distant objects. Physicists began changing the assumption that the Universe was static and unchanging. In 1922 <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann" title="Alexander Friedmann">Alexander Friedmann</a> introduced the idea of an expanding universe that contained moving matter. href="/wiki/Heber_Curtis" title="Heber Curtis">Heber Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_%C3%96pik" title="Ernst &#214;pik">Ernst &#214;pik</a> determining that some <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a> seen in telescopes were separate galaxies far distant from our own.\n</p>\n<table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks plainlist" dynamic approach to cosmology, one long-standing debate about the structure of the cosmos was coming to a climax. Mount Wilson astronomer <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a> championed the model of a cosmos made up of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> <a href="/wiki/Star_system" title="Star system">star system</a> only; while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heber_D._Curtis" title="Heber D. Curtis">Heber D. Curtis</a> argued for the idea that spiral nebulae were star systems in their own right as island universes. This difference of ideas came to a climax with the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy)" title="Great Debate (astronomy)">Great Debate</a> on 26 April 1920 at the meeting of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The debate was resolved when <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> detected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cepheid_Variables" title="Cepheid Variables">Cepheid Variables</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andromeda_galaxy" title="Andromeda galaxy">Andromeda galaxy</a> in 1923 and 1924. Their distance established spiral nebulae well beyond the edge of the Milky Way.\n</p><p>Subsequent modelling of the universe explored the possibility that the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, introduced by Einstein in his 1917 paper, may result in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expanding_universe" title="Expanding universe">expanding universe</a>, depending on its value. Thus the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> model was proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> priest <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema&#238;tre">Georges Lema&#238;tre</a> in 1927 which was subsequently corroborated by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a>\'s discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">red shift</a> in 1929 and later by the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arno_Penzias" title="Arno Penzias">Arno Penzias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson" title="Robert Woodrow Wilson">Robert Woodrow Wilson</a> in 1964. These findings were a first step to rule out some of many <a href="/wiki/Non-standard_cosmology" title="Non-standard cosmology">alternative cosmologies</a>.\n</p><p>Since around 1990, several dramatic advances in observational cosmology have transformed cosmology from a largely speculative science into a predictive science with precise agreement between theory and observation. These advances include observations of the microwave background from the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer" title="Cosmic Background Explorer">COBE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMAP" title="WMAP">WMAP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck_spacecraft" title="Planck spacecraft">Planck</a> satellites,  large new galaxy <a href="/wiki/Redshift_survey" title="Redshift survey">redshift surveys</a> including <a href="/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey" title="2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey">2dfGRS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey" title="Sloan Digital Sky Survey">SDSS</a>, and observations of distant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernovae" title="Supernovae">supernovae</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_lensing" title="Gravitational lensing">gravitational lensing</a>. These observations matched the predictions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a> theory, a modified <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> theory, and the specific version known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM" title="Lambda-CDM">Lambda-CDM</a> model.  This has led many to refer to modern times as the "golden age of cosmology".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 17 March 2014, astronomers at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard-Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics" title="Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics">Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics</a> announced the detection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_waves" title="Gravitational waves">gravitational waves</a>, providing strong evidence for <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">inflation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BICEP2-2014_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BICEP2-2014-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140317_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140317-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140317_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140317-12">[12]</a></sup> However, on 19 June 2014, lowered confidence in confirming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_inflation" title="Cosmic inflation">cosmic inflation</a> findings was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140619_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140619-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-20140619_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20140619-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRL-20140619_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-20140619-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 December 2014, at the <i>Planck 2014</i> meeting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrara,_Italy" title="Ferrara, Italy">Ferrara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, astronomers reported that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">13.8 billion years old</a> and is composed of 4.9% <a cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Philosophical title="Cosmology (philosophy)">Cosmology (philosophy)</a></div>\n<p>Cosmology deals with the world as the totality of space, time and all phenomena. Historically, it has had quite a broad scope, and in many cases was founded in religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In modern use metaphysical cosmology addresses questions about the Universe which are beyond the scope of science. It is distinguished from religious cosmology in that it approaches these questions using philosophical methods like <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectics</a>. Modern metaphysical cosmology tries to address questions such as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BICEP2-2014_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-BICEP2-2014-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>What is the origin of the Universe? What is its first cause? Is its existence necessary? (see <a href="/wiki/Monism" title="Monism">monism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emanationism" title="Emanationism">emanationism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">creationism</a>)</li>\n<li>What are the ultimate material components of the Universe? (see <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(philosophy)" title="Mechanism (philosophy)">mechanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamism_(metaphysics)" title="Dynamism (metaphysics)">dynamism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hylomorphism" title="Hylomorphism">hylomorphism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atomism" title="Atomism">atomism</a>)</li>\n<li>What is the ultimate reason for the existence of the Universe? Does the cosmos have a purpose? (see <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleology</a>)</li>\n<li>Does the existence of consciousness have a purpose? How do we know what we know about the totality of the cosmos? Does cosmological reasoning reveal metaphysical truths?
