in deep space and to circle the Moon\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>2007: First animal survives exposure to space\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>Non-human <b>animals in space</b> originally served to test the survivability of <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a>, before <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflights</a> were attempted. Later, other non-human animals were flown to investigate various biological processes and the effects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">microgravity</a> and space flight might have on them. <a href="/wiki/Bioastronautics" title="Bioastronautics">Bioastronautics</a> is an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioengineering" title="Bioengineering">bioengineering</a> research which spans the study and support of life in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>. To date, seven national space programs have flown animals into space: the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People\'s Republic of China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.\n</p><p>A wide variety of non-human animals have been launched into space, including <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogs_in_space" title="Dogs in space">dogs</a>, tortoises, mice, and insects.  The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> launched flights containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monkeys_in_space" title="Monkeys in space">monkeys and primates</a> primarily between 1948-1961 with one flight in 1969 and one in 1985. <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> launched two monkey-carrying flights in 1967. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> launched monkeys between 1983 and 1996. During the 1950s and 1960s, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a> used <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs" title="Soviet space dogs">a number of dogs</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-orbital" title="Sub-orbital">sub-orbital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital space flights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AT-20171103_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AT-20171103-1">[1]</a></sup> Two tortoises and a variety of insects were the first inhabitants of Earth to circle the Moon, on the 1968 <a href="/wiki/Zond_5" title="Zond 5">Zond 5</a> mission, and <a href="/wiki/Fe,_Fi,_Fo,_Fum,_and_Phooey" title="Fe, Fi, Fo, Fum, and Phooey">five mice</a> traveled in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_command_and_service_module" title="Apollo command and service module">orbiter</a> of the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a> mission.\n</p>\n<div href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Animals had been used in aeronautic exploration since 1783 when the <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier brothers</a> sent a sheep, a duck, and a rooster aloft in a hot air balloon to see if ground-dwelling animals can survive (the duck serving as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_control" title="Experimental control">experimental control</a>). The limited supply of captured German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-2" title="V-2">V-2</a> rockets led to the U.S. use of high-altitude balloon launches carrying fruit flies, mice, hamsters, guinea pigs, cats, dogs, frogs, goldfish and monkeys to heights of up to 44,000&#160;m (144,000&#160;ft; 27&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-2">[2]</a></sup> These high-altitude balloon flights from 1947 to 1960 tested radiation exposure, physiological response, life support and recovery systems. The U.S. high-altitude manned balloon flights occurred in the same time frame, one of which also carried fruit flies.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a into space.</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1940s">1940s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1940s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The  first animals sent into space were <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">fruit flies</a> aboard a U.S.-launched <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a> on 20 February 1947 from <a href="/wiki/White_Sands_Missile_Range" title="White Sands Missile Range">White Sands Missile Range</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The purpose of the experiment was to explore the effects of radiation exposure at high altitudes. The rocket reached 68 miles (109&#160;km) in 3 minutes and 10 seconds, past both the U.S. Air Force 50-mile and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karman_line" title="Karman line">international 100 km</a> definitions of the boundary of space. The Blossom capsule was ejected and successfully deployed its parachute. The fruit flies were recovered alive. Other V-2 missions carried biological samples, including <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a>.\n</p><p>Albert II, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhesus_monkey" title="Rhesus monkey">rhesus monkey</a>, became the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monkeys_in_space" title="Monkeys in space">monkey in space</a> on 14 June 1949, in a U.S.-launched V-2, after the failure of the original Albert\'s mission on ascent. Albert I reached only 30&#8211;39 miles (48&#8211;63&#160;km) altitude; Albert II reached about 83 miles (134&#160;km).  Albert II died on impact after a parachute failure. Numerous monkeys of several species were flown by the U.S. in the 1950s and 1960s. Monkeys were implanted with sensors to measure vital signs, and many were under anesthesia during launch. The death rate among monkeys at this stage was very high: about two-thirds of all monkeys launched in the 1940s and 1950s died on missions or soon after landing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Flight</i>, a 1953 US Air Force film</div></div></div>\n<p>On 31 August 1950, the U.S. launched a <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mouse</a> into space (137&#160;km) aboard a V-2 (the Albert V flight, which, unlike the Albert I-IV flights, did not have a monkey), but the rocket disintegrated because the parachute system failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The U.S. launched several other mice in the 1950s.\n</p><p>On 22 July 1951, the Soviet Union launched the <a href="/wiki/R-1_(missile)" title="R-1 (missile)">R-1</a> IIIA-1 flight, carrying the <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> Tsygan (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1062;&#1099;&#1075;&#1072;&#1085;</span>, "Gypsy") and Dezik (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1044;&#1077;&#1079;&#1080;&#1082;</span>) into space, but not into orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi,_p.95_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi,_p.95-7">[7]</a></sup> These two dogs were the first living higher organisms successfully recovered from a spaceflight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi,_p.95_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi,_p.95-7">[7]</a></sup> Both <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs" title="Soviet space dogs">space dogs</a> survived the flight, although one would die on a subsequent flight. The U.S. launched mice aboard spacecraft later that year; however, they failed to reach the altitude for true spaceflight.\n</p><p>On 3 November 1957, the second-ever orbiting spacecraft carried the first animal into orbit, the dog <a href="/wiki/Laika" title="Laika">Laika</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AT-20171103_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AT-20171103-1">[1]</a></sup> launched aboard the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">Sputnik 2</a> spacecraft (nicknamed \'Muttnik\' in the West). Laika died during the flight, as was intended because the technology to return from orbit had not yet been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AT-20171103_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AT-20171103-1">[1]</a></sup> At least 10 other dogs were launched into orbit and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs" title="Soviet space dogs">numerous others</a> on sub-orbital flights before the historic date of 12 April 1961, when <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> became the first human in space.\n</p><p>On 13 December 1958, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_IRBM" title="Jupiter IRBM">Jupiter IRBM</a>, AM-13, was launched from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a>, Florida, with a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>-trained South American <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkey" title="Squirrel monkey">squirrel monkey</a> named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordo_(space_monkey)" title="Gordo (space monkey)">Gordo</a> on board. The nose cone recovery parachute failed to operate and Gordo was lost. <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">Telemetry</a> data sent back during the flight showed that the monkey survived the 10&#160;<a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g</a> of launch, 8 minutes of weightlessness and 40&#160;g of reentry at 10,000 miles per hour (16,000&#160;km/h). The nose cone sank 1,302 nautical miles (2,411&#160;km) downrange from Cape Canaveral and was not recovered.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">Monkeys</a> Able and <a href="/wiki/Miss_Baker" title="Miss Baker">Baker</a> became the first monkeys to survive spaceflight after their 1959 flight. On 28 May 1959, aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_IRBM" title="Jupiter IRBM">Jupiter IRBM</a> AM-18, were a 7-pound (3.18&#160;kg) American-born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhesus_monkey" title="Rhesus monkey">rhesus monkey</a>, Able, from Independence, Kansas, and an 11-ounce (310-gram) <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkey" title="Squirrel monkey">squirrel monkey</a> from Peru, Baker. The monkeys rode in the nose cone of the missile to an altitude of 360 miles (579&#160;km) and a distance of 1,700 miles (2,735&#160;km) down the Atlantic Missile Range from Cape Canaveral, Florida. They withstood forces 38 times the normal pull of gravity and were weightless for about 9 minutes. A top speed of 10,000&#160;mph (16,000&#160;km/h) was reached during their 16-minute flight. The monkeys survived the flight in good condition. Able died four days after the flight from a reaction to anesthesia, while undergoing surgery to remove an infected medical electrode. Baker was the center of media attention for the next several months as she was watched closely for any ill-effects from her space flight. She was even mated in an attempt to test her reproductive system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Baker lived until 29 November 1984, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Space_and_Rocket_Center" title="US Space and Rocket Center">US Space and Rocket Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>.\n</p><p>On 2 July 1959, a launch of a Soviet R2 rocket, which reached 212 kilometers (132&#160;mi), carried two space dogs and Marfusa, the first rabbit to go into space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A 19 September 1959 launch, Jupiter AM-23, carried 2 frogs along with 12 mice but the rocket was destroyed during launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 19 August 1960 the Soviet Union launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sputnik_5" title="Sputnik 5">Sputnik 5</a> (also known as Korabl-Sputnik 2) which carried the dogs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belka_and_Strelka" title="Belka and Strelka">Belka and Strelka</a>, along with a gray rabbit, 40 mice, 2 rats, and 15 flasks of fruit flies and plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  It was the first spacecraft to carry animals into orbit and return them alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  One of Strelka\'s pups, Pushinka, bred and born after her mission, was given as a present to <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline Kennedy</a> by <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> in 1961, and many descendants are known to exist.\n</p><p>The United States sent three black mice: Sally, Amy and Moe 1,000&#160;km up and 8,000&#160;km distance from Cape Canaveral on 13 October 1960 using an Atlas D 71D launch vehicle.  The mice were retrieved from the nosecone near Ascension Island and were said to be in good condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 31 January 1961, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ham_the_Chimp" title="Ham the Chimp">Ham the Chimp</a> was launched in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_program" title="Mercury program">Mercury</a> capsule aboard a <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_Launch_Vehicle" title="Mercury-Redstone Launch Vehicle">Redstone rocket</a>. Ham\'s mission was <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_2" title="Mercury-Redstone 2">Mercury-Redstone 2</a>. The chimpanzee had been trained to pull levers to receive rewards of banana pellets and avoid electric shocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His flight demonstrated the ability to perform tasks during spaceflight. A little over 3 months later the United States sent <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a> into space. <a href="/wiki/Enos_(chimpanzee)" title="Enos (chimpanzee)">Enos the chimp</a> became the first chimpanzee in orbit on 29 November 1961, in another Mercury capsule, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_rocket" title="Atlas rocket">Atlas rocket</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_5" title="Mercury 5">Mercury-Atlas 5</a>.\n</p><p>On 9 March 1961 the Soviet Union launched the <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_4" title="Korabl-Sputnik 4">Korabl-Sputnik 4</a> that carried a dog named <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Chernushka" title="Soviet space dogs">Chernushka</a>, some mice, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> and, for the first time into space, a <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">guinea pig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> All were successfully recovered.\n</p><p>France flew their first <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rat</a> (Hector) into space on 22 February 1961. Two more rats were flown in October 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 18 October 1963, France launched <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9licette" title="F&#233;licette">F&#233;licette</a> the cat aboard Veronique AGI sounding rocket No. 47. The launch was directed by the French Centre d\'Enseignement et de Recherches de M&#233;decine A&#233;ronautique (CERMA). F&#233;licette was recovered alive after a 15-minute flight and a descent by parachute. F&#233;licette had electrodes implanted into her brain, and the recorded neural impulses were transmitted back to Earth. A second cat was sent to space by CERMA on 24 October 1963, but the flight ran into difficulties that prevented recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The final French animal launches were of two monkeys in March 1967.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>China launched mice and rats in 1964 and 1965, and two dogs in 1966.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voskhod_program" title="Voskhod program">Voskhod program</a>, two Soviet space dogs, Veterok (&#1042;&#1077;&#1090;&#1077;&#1088;&#1086;&#1082;, Little Wind) and Ugolyok (&#1059;&#1075;&#1086;&#1083;&#1105;&#1082;, Blackie), were launched on 22 February 1966, on board <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmos_110" title="Cosmos 110">Cosmos 110</a> and spent 22 days in orbit before landing on 16 March. This spaceflight of record-breaking duration was not surpassed by humans until <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> in 1971 and still stands as the longest space flight by dogs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The United States launched Biosatellite I in 1966 and Biosatellite I/II in 1967 with fruit flies, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitic_wasp" title="Parasitic wasp">parasitic wasps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flour_beetle" title="Flour beetle">flour beetles</a> and frog eggs, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amoebae" title="Amoebae">amoebae</a>, plants and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 11 April 1967, Argentina also launched the rat Belisario, atop a Yarar&#225; rocket,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leon_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leon-17">[17]</a></sup> from Cordoba military range, which was recovered successfully. This flight was followed by a series of subsequent flights using rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It is unclear if any Argentinean biological flights passed the 100&#160;km limit of space.\n</p><p>The first animals in deep space, the first to circle the Moon, and the first two <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoises</a> in space were launched on <a href="/wiki/Zond_5" title="Zond 5">Zond 5</a> on 14 September 1968 by the Soviet Union. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horsfield%27s_tortoise" title="Horsfield\'s tortoise">Horsfield\'s tortoises</a> were sent on a circumlunar voyage to the Moon along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_fly" title="Wine fly">wine flies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meal_worm" title="Meal worm">meal worms</a>, and other biological specimens. These were the first inhabitants of Earth to travel around the Moon. The capsule overshot its terrestrial landing site but was successfully recovered at sea on 21 September. The animals survived but suffered some weight loss.\n</p><p>On 28 June 1969, the United States launched the monkey Bonny, a macaque, on <a href="/wiki/Biosatellite_3" title="Biosatellite 3">Biosatellite 3</a> in what was intended to have been a 30-day orbit around the Earth, with the monkey being fed by food pellets from a dispenser that he had been trained to operate.  However, Bonny\'s health deteriorated rapidly and he was returned to Earth on July 7,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> but died the next day after the Biosatellite capsule was recovered in the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In total in the 1950s and 1960s, the Soviet Union launched missions with passenger slots for at least 57 dogs. The actual number of dogs in space is smaller, because some dogs flew more than once.\n</p><p>On 23 December 1969, as part of the \'Operaci&#243;n Navidad\' (Operation Christmas), Argentina launched Juan (a <a href="/wiki/Tufted_capuchin" title="Tufted capuchin">cai monkey</a>, native of Argentina\'s Misiones Province) using a <a href="/wiki/Canopus_2" title="Canopus 2">Canopus II</a> rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It ascended 82 kilometers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and then was recovered successfully. Later, on 1 February 1970 the experience was repeated with a female monkey of the same species using a X-1 Panther rocket. It reached a higher altitude than its predecessor, but it was lost after the capsule\'s parachute failed.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arabella_web_aboard_second_Skylab_mission_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spider_webs_in_space" title="Spider webs in space">spider web built in space</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Two <a href="/wiki/American_bullfrog" title="American bullfrog">bullfrogs</a> were launched on a one-way mission on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbiting_Frog_Otolith_satellite" title="Orbiting Frog Otolith satellite">Orbiting Frog Otolith satellite</a> on 9 November 1970, to understand more about space motion sickness.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">Apollo 16</a> on 16 April , 1972, carried <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematodes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>, launched on 7 December 1972, carried five <a href="/wiki/Little_pocket_mouse" title="Little pocket mouse">pocket mice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fe,_Fi,_Fo,_Fum,_and_Phooey" title="Fe, Fi, Fo, Fum, and Phooey">Fe, Fi, Fo, Fum, and Phooey</a>, although one died on the circumlunar trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bioCoreSP368_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-bioCoreSP368-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Skylab_3" title="Skylab 3">Skylab 3</a> carried pocket mice and the first <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> in space (a <a href="/wiki/Mummichog" title="Mummichog">mummichog</a>), and the first <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> in space (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_spider" title="Garden spider">garden spiders</a> named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spider_webs_in_space" title="Spider webs in space">Arabella and Anita</a>). Mummichog were also flown by the U.S. on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz" title="Apollo-Soyuz">Apollo-Soyuz</a> joint mission, launched 15 July 1975.\n</p><p>The Soviets flew several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_program" title="Bion program">Bion program</a> missions which consisted of satellites with biological cargoes. On these launches they flew tortoises, rats, and mummichog. On <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_20" title="Soyuz 20">Soyuz 20</a>, launched 17 November 1975, tortoises set the duration record for an animal in space when they spent 90.5 days in space. <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a> on 22 June 1976, carried tortoises and a fish (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zebra_danio" title="Zebra danio">zebra danio</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Soviet Union sent eight monkeys into space in the 1980s on Bion flights. In 1985, the U.S. sent two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkeys" title="Squirrel monkeys">squirrel monkeys</a> aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STS-51B" title="STS-51B">Spacelab 3</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> with 24 male albino <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rattus_norvegicus" title="Rattus norvegicus">rats</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stick_insect" title="Stick insect">stick insect</a> eggs. Bion flights also flew zebra danio, fruit flies, rats, stick insect eggs and the first <a href="/wiki/Iberian_ribbed_newt" title="Iberian ribbed newt">newts</a> in space.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_7" title="Bion 7">Bion 7</a> (1985) had 10 newts (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleurodeles_waltl" title="Pleurodeles waltl">Pleurodeles waltl</a></i>) on board. The newts had part of their front limbs amputated, to study the rate of <a href="/wiki/Regeneration_(biology)" title="Regeneration (biology)">regeneration</a> in space, knowledge to understand human recovery from space injuries.\n</p><p>After an experiment was lost in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster">Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i> disaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chicken</a> embryos (fertilized eggs) were sent into space in an experiment on <a href="/wiki/STS-29" title="STS-29">STS-29</a> in 1989. The experiment was designed for a student contest.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Four monkeys flew aboard the last Bion flights of the Soviet Union as well as frogs and fruit flies. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foton_program" title="Foton program">Foton program</a> flights carried dormant <a href="/wiki/Brine_shrimp" title="Brine shrimp">brine shrimp</a> (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Artemia_franciscana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Artemia franciscana (page does not exist)">Artemia franciscana</a></i>), newts, fruit flies, and sand desert beetles (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trigonoscelis_gigas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trigonoscelis gigas (page does not exist)">Trigonoscelis gigas</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinea_pigs" title="Guinea pigs">guinea pigs</a> in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Toyohiro_Akiyama" title="Toyohiro Akiyama">Toyohiro Akiyama</a>, a Japanese journalist carried <a href="/wiki/Japanese_tree_frog" title="Japanese tree frog">Japanese tree frogs</a> with him during his trip to the <i><a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a></i> space station in December 1990. Other biological experiments aboard Mir involved <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quail</a> eggs.\n</p><p>Japan launched its first animals, a species of newt, into space on 18 March 1995 aboard the <a href="/wiki/Space_Flyer_Unit" title="Space Flyer Unit">Space Flyer Unit</a>.\n</p><p>During the 1990s the U.S. carried <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">crickets</a>, mice, rats, frogs, newts, fruit flies, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medaka" title="Medaka">medaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyster_toadfish" title="Oyster toadfish">oyster toadfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swordtail_fish" title="Swordtail fish">swordtail fish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gypsy_moth" title="Gypsy moth">gypsy moth</a> eggs, stick insect eggs, <a href="/wiki/Brine_shrimp" title="Brine shrimp">brine shrimp</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Artemia_salina" title="Artemia salina">Artemia salina</a></i>), quail eggs, and <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttles</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/STS-107" title="STS-107">last flight of <i>Columbia</i> in 2003</a> carried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkworm" title="Silkworm">silkworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eriophora" title="Eriophora">garden orb spiders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carpenter_bee" title="Carpenter bee">carpenter bees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvester_ant" title="Harvester ant">harvester ants</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_killifish" title="Japanese killifish">Japanese killifish</a> (medaka). <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">Nematodes</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i>) from one experiment were found still alive in the debris after the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">Space Shuttle <i>Columbia</i> disaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans">C. elegans</a></i> are also part of experiments aboard the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> as well as research using quail eggs.\n</p><p>Earlier Space Shuttle missions included grade school, junior high and high school projects; some of these included <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>, stick insect eggs and brine shrimp cysts. Other science missions included gypsy moth eggs.\n</p><p>On 12 July 2006, <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Aerospace" title="Bigelow Aerospace">Bigelow Aerospace</a> launched their <i><a href="/wiki/Genesis_I" title="Genesis I">Genesis I</a></i> inflatable space module, containing many small items such as toys and simple experiments chosen by company employees that would be observed via camera. These items included insects, perhaps making it the first private flight to launch animals into space. Included were <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_hissing_cockroach" title="Madagascar hissing cockroach">Madagascar hissing cockroaches</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_jumping_beans" title="Mexican jumping beans">Mexican jumping beans</a> &#8212; seeds containing live larvae of the moth <i><a href="/wiki/Cydia_deshaisiana" title="Cydia deshaisiana">Cydia deshaisiana</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LVRJ1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-LVRJ1-28">[28]</a></sup>  On 28 June 2007, Bigelow launched <i><a href="/wiki/Genesis_II" title="Genesis II">Genesis II</a></i>, a near-twin to <i>Genesis I</i>.  This spacecraft also carried Madagascar hissing cockroaches and added South African flat rock scorpions (<i>Hadogenes troglodytes</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_harvester_ant" title="Red harvester ant">seed-harvester ants</a> (<i>Pogonomyrmex californicus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BAlifebox_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BAlifebox-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2007, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">European Space Agency</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FOTON-M3" title="FOTON-M3">FOTON-M3</a> mission, <a href="/wiki/Tardigrade" title="Tardigrade">tardigrades</a>, also known as water-bears, were able to survive 10 days of exposure to open-space with only their natural protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On the same mission, a number of cockroaches were carried inside a sealed container and at least one of the females conceived during the mission. After they were returned to earth, the one named <a href="/wiki/Nadezhda_(cockroach)" title="Nadezhda (cockroach)">Nadezhda</a> became the first Earth creature to produce young that had been conceived in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 15 March 2009, during the countdown of the <a href="/wiki/STS-119" title="STS-119">STS-119</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Free-tailed_bat" title="Free-tailed bat">free-tailed bat</a> was seen clinging to the fuel tank. NASA observers believed the bat would fly off once the shuttle started to launch, but it did not. Upon analyzing the images, a wildlife expert who provided support to the center said it likely had a broken left wing and some problem with its right shoulder or wrist. The animal most likely perished quickly during <i>Discovery</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s climb into orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2009, <a href="/wiki/STS-129" title="STS-129">STS-129</a> took <a href="/wiki/Painted_lady" title="Painted lady">painted lady</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monarch_butterfly" title="Monarch butterfly">monarch butterfly</a> larvae into space for a school experiment as well as thousands of <a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans"><i>C. elegans</i></a> roundworms for long-term weight loss studies.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Animals_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 3 February 2010, on the 31st anniversary of its revolution, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_Republic_of_Iran" title="Islamic Republic of Iran">Iran</a> became the latest country to launch animals into space. The animals (a mouse, two <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a> and some worms) were launched on top of the <a href="/wiki/Safir_(rocket)" title="Safir (rocket)">Kavoshgar 3</a> rocket and returned alive to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tehran_Times_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tehran_Times-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2011, the last flight of <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Endeavour" title="Space Shuttle Endeavour">Space Shuttle <i>Endeavour</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/STS-134" title="STS-134">STS-134</a>) carried two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_orb_spider" title="Golden orb spider">golden orb spiders</a>, named Gladys and Esmeralda, as well as a fruit fly colony as their food source in order to study the effects of microgravity on spiders\' behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Tardigrades and extremophiles were also sent into orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Living_Interplanetary_Flight_Experiment" title="Living Interplanetary Flight Experiment">Living Interplanetary Flight Experiment</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">Fobos-Grunt</a> mission planned to carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tardigrades" title="Tardigrades">tardigrades</a> to Mars and back; however, the mission failed to leave Earth orbit.\n</p><p>In October 2012, 32 medaka fish were delivered to the International Space Station by <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-06M" title="Soyuz TMA-06M">Soyuz TMA-06M</a> for the new Aquatic Habitat in the <a href="/wiki/Kibo_(ISS_module)" title="Kibo (ISS module)">Kibo module</a>.\n</p><p>On 28 January 2013, Iranian news agencies reported that Iran sent a monkey in a "<a href="/wiki/Pishgam" title="Pishgam">Pishgam</a>" rocket to a height of 72 miles (116&#160;km) and retrieved a "shipment". Later Iran\'s space research website uploaded an 18-minute video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The video was uploaded later on YouTube.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2014, the search strategies of <a href="/wiki/Pavement_ant" title="Pavement ant">pavement ants</a> were studied on the ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 19 July 2014, Russia announced that they launched their Foton-M4 satellite into low Earth orbit (575 kilometers) with one male and four female geckos (possibly <a href="/wiki/Gold_dust_day_gecko" title="Gold dust day gecko">gold dust day geckos</a>) as the payload. This was an effort to study the effects of microgravity on reproductive habits of reptiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On 24 July 2014, it was announced that Russia had lost control of the Foton-M4 satellite, leaving only two months to restore contact before the geckos\' food supply was exhausted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Control of the satellite was subsequently restored on 28 July 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> On 1 September 2014 Russia confirmed the death of all five geckos, stating that their mummified bodies seem to indicate they froze to death. Russia is said to have appointed an emergency commission to investigate the animals\' deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 23 September 2014, the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_CRS-4" title="SpaceX CRS-4">CRS-4 mission</a> delivered 20 mice to live on the ISS for study of the long-term effects of microgravity on the rodents. This was the first use of the <a href="/wiki/Rodent_Research_Hardware_System" title="Rodent Research Hardware System">Rodent Research Hardware System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousetronauts_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mousetronauts-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 14 April 2015, the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_CRS-6" title="SpaceX CRS-6">SpaceX CRS-6</a> delivered 20 <a href="/wiki/C57BL/6" title="C57BL/6">C57BL/6NTAC</a> mice to live on the ISS for evaluating microgravity as the extreme opposite of a healthy active lifestyle. In the absence of gravity, astronauts are subject to a decrease in muscle, bone, and tendon mass. "Although, we\'re not out to treat couch potatoes," states head Novartis Institute for Biomedical Research (NIBR) scientist on the project Dr. Sam Cadena, "we\'re hoping that these experiments will help us to better understand muscle loss in populations where physical activity in any form is not an option; e.g., in the frail elderly or those subjected to bed rest or immobilization due to surgery or chronic disease." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 8 April 2016, Rodent Research 3 delivered 20 mice on <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_CRS-8" title="SpaceX CRS-8">SpaceX CRS-8</a>. The experiment sponsored by Eli Lilly and Co. was a study of myostatin inhibition for the prevention of skeletal and muscle atrophy and weakness. Mice are known to suffer from rapid loss of muscle and bone mass after as little as 12 days of space flight exposure. The mice were euthanized and dissected on the station and then frozen for eventual return to Earth for further study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 June 2018, a <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">SpaceX Dragon</a> spaceship blasted off from Florida carrying 20 mice. The rodent crew arrived at the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a> on 2 July 2018. Their record-breaking journey &#8212; this was the longest mice have been off the planet &#8212; was part of a study on how Earth-dwellers\' physiology and sleep schedules responded to the stress of being in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Chinese lunar lander <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_4" title="Chang\'e 4">Chang\'e 4</a> carries a 3 kg sealed container with seeds and insect eggs to test whether plants and insects could hatch and grow together in synergy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_2017_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_2017-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_May_2018_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA_May_2018-53">[53]</a></sup> The experiment includes six types of organisms: <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeds_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seeds-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cottonseed" title="Cottonseed">cottonseed</a>, potato, <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> (a flowering plant), as well as yeast and <a href="/wiki/Drosophilidae" title="Drosophilidae">fruit fly</a> eggs. If the eggs hatch, the larvae would produce carbon dioxide, while the germinated plants would release oxygen through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sputnik_2017_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sputnik_2017-52">[52]</a></sup>  A miniature camera is imaging the growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeds_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seeds-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
