href="/wiki/Hypersonic_speed" title="Hypersonic speed">hypersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft" title="Rocket-powered aircraft">rocket-powered aircraft</a> operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_X-planes" title="List of X-planes">X-plane</a> series of <a href="/wiki/Experimental_aircraft" title="Experimental aircraft">experimental aircraft</a>. The X-15 set speed and altitude records in the 1960s, reaching the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">edge of outer space</a> and returning with valuable data used in <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> design. The X-15\'s <a href="/wiki/Flight_airspeed_record" title="Flight airspeed record">official world record</a> for the highest speed ever recorded by a manned, powered aircraft, set in October 1967 when <a href="/wiki/William_J._Knight" title="William J. Knight">William J. Knight</a> flew <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 6.70 at 102,100 feet (31,120&#160;m), a speed of 4,520 miles per hour (7,274&#160;km/h; 2,021&#160;m/s), has remained unbroken as of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20140228_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa20140228-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fastest_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fastest-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the X-15 program, 13 flights by eight pilots met the Air Force <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">spaceflight</a> criterion by exceeding the altitude of 50 miles (80&#160;km), thus qualifying these pilots as being <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a>. The Air Force pilots qualified for astronaut wings immediately, while the civilian pilots were eventually awarded NASA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_wings" title="Astronaut wings">astronaut wings</a> in 2005, 35 years after the last X-15 flight.  The only Navy pilot in the X-15 program never took the aircraft above the requisite 50 mile (80 km) altitude and thus never earned astronaut wings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-15_in_flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1992" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/X-15_in_flight.jpg/220px-X-15_in_flight.jpg" href="/wiki/File:X-15_in_flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>X-15 after igniting rocket engine</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a test</div></div></div>\n<p>The X-15 was based on a concept study from <a href="/wiki/Walter_Dornberger" title="Walter Dornberger">Walter Dornberger</a> for the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> (NACA) for a <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic_speed" title="Hypersonic speed">hypersonic</a> research aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEK&#228;smann1999105_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEK&#228;smann1999105-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Request_for_proposal" title="Request for proposal">requests for proposal</a> (RFPs) were published on 30 December 1954 for the airframe and on 4 February 1955 for the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a>. The X-15 was built by two manufacturers: <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aviation" title="North American Aviation">North American Aviation</a> was contracted for the airframe in November 1955, and <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Motors" title="Reaction Motors">Reaction Motors</a> was contracted for building the engines in 1956.\n</p><p>Like many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-plane" title="X-plane">X-series</a> aircraft, the X-15 was designed to be carried aloft and <a href="/wiki/Drop_test" title="Drop test">drop launched</a> from under the wing of a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">B-52</a> <a href="/wiki/Mother_ship" title="Mother ship">mother ship</a>.  Air Force NB-52A, "The High and Mighty One" (serial 52-0003), and NB-52B, "The Challenger" (serial 52-0008, a.k.a. <i><a href="/wiki/Balls_8" title="Balls 8">Balls 8</a></i>) served as carrier planes for all X-15 flights.  Release took place at an altitude of about 8.5 miles (13.7&#160;km) and a speed of about 500 miles per hour (805&#160;km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E-4942_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-E-4942-7">[7]</a></sup> The X-15 fuselage was long and cylindrical, with rear <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fairing" title="Aircraft fairing">fairings</a> that flattened its appearance, and thick, dorsal and ventral wedge-fin stabilizers. Parts of the fuselage were heat-resistant <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Inconel" title="Inconel">Inconel</a>-X 750).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEK&#228;smann1999105_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEK&#228;smann1999105-6">[6]</a></sup> The retractable <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> comprised a nose-wheel carriage and two rear skids. The skids did not extend beyond the ventral fin, which required the pilot to jettison the lower fin just before landing.  The lower fin was recovered by parachute.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cockpit_and_pilot_systems">Cockpit and pilot systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cockpit and pilot systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1189" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg/220px-North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg/330px-North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg/440px-North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cockpit of an X-15</div></div></div>\n<p>The X-15 was the product of developmental research and changes were made to various systems over the course of the program and between the different models. The X-15 was operated under several different scenarios, including attachment to a launch aircraft, drop, main engine start and acceleration, ballistic flight into thin air/space, re-entry into thicker air, unpowered glide to landing, and direct landing without a main-engine start. The main rocket engine operated only for a relatively short part of the flight, but boosted the X-15 to its high speeds and altitudes. Without main engine thrust, the X-15\'s instruments and control surfaces remained functional, but the aircraft could not maintain altitude.\n</p><p>Because the X-15 also had to be controlled in an environment where there was too little air for aerodynamic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_control_surface" title="Flight control surface">flight control surfaces</a>, it had a <a href="/wiki/Reaction_control_system" title="Reaction control system">reaction control system</a> (RCS) that used rocket thrusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-paul-8">[8]</a></sup> There were two different X-15 pilot control setups: one used three joysticks, the other, one joystick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jarvis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-jarvis-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The X-15 type with multiple control sticks for the pilot placed a traditional rudder and stick between a left joystick that sent commands to the Reaction Control System,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> and a third joystick on the right used during high-G maneuvers to augment the center stick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition to pilot input, the X-15 "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stability_Augmentation_System" title="Stability Augmentation System">Stability Augmentation System</a>" (SAS) sent inputs to the aerodynamic controls to help the pilot maintain <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup>  The Reaction Control System (RCS) could be operated in two modes&#160;&#8212; manual and automatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jarvis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-jarvis-9">[9]</a></sup> The automatic mode used a feature called "Reaction Augmentation System" (RAS) that helped stabilize the vehicle at high altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jarvis_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-jarvis-9">[9]</a></sup>  The RAS was typically used for approximately three minutes of an X-15 flight before automatic power off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jarvis_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-jarvis-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The alternative control setup used the MH-96 flight control system, which allowed one joystick in place of three and simplified pilot input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goleta_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-goleta-11">[11]</a></sup> The MH-96 could automatically blend aerodynamic and rocket controls, depending on how effective each system was at controlling the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goleta_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-goleta-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Among the many controls were the rocket engine throttle and a control for jettisoning the ventral tail fin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> Other features of the cockpit included heated windows to prevent icing and a forward headrest for periods of high deceleration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The X-15 had an ejection seat designed to operate at speeds up to Mach&#160;4 (4,480&#160;km/h; 2,784&#160;mph) and/or 120,000 feet (37&#160;km) altitude, although it was never used during the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> In the event of ejection, the seat was designed to deploy fins, which were used until it reached a safer speed/altitude at which to deploy its main parachute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> Pilots wore pressure suits, which could be pressurized with nitrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup> Above 35,000 feet (11&#160;km) altitude, the cockpit was pressurized to 3.5&#160;psi (0.24&#160;atm) with nitrogen gas, while oxygen for breathing was fed separately to the pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul2_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-paul2-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propulsion">Propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg/220px-North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg/330px-North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg/440px-North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_American_X-15_Nozzle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>X-15 tail with XLR-99</div></div></div>\n<p>The initial 24 powered flights used two <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR11" title="Reaction Motors XLR11">Reaction Motors XLR11</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-propellant rocket</a> engines, enhanced to provide a total of 16,000 pounds-force (71&#160;kN) of thrust as compared to the 6,000 pounds-force (27&#160;kN) that a single XLR11 provided in 1947 to make the <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-1" title="Bell X-1">Bell X-1</a> the first aircraft to fly faster than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>.  The XLR11 used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethyl_alcohol" title="Ethyl alcohol">ethyl alcohol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a>.\n</p><p>By November 1960, Reaction Motors was able to deliver the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR99" title="Reaction Motors XLR99">XLR99</a> rocket engine, generating 57,000 pounds-force (250&#160;kN) of thrust. The remaining 175 flights of the X-15 used XLR99 engines, in a single engine configuration. The XLR99 used anhydrous ammonia and liquid oxygen as propellant, and hydrogen peroxide to drive the high-speed turbopump that delivered propellants to the engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-paul-8">[8]</a></sup> It could burn 15,000 pounds (6,804&#160;kg) of propellant in 80 seconds;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-paul-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jules_Bergman" title="Jules Bergman">Jules Bergman</a> titled his book on the program <i>Ninety Seconds to Space</i> to describe the total powered flight time of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gale196110_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gale196110-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The X-15 <a href="/wiki/Reaction_control_system" title="Reaction control system">reaction control system</a> (RCS), for maneuvering in low-pressure/density environment, used <a href="/wiki/High-test_peroxide" title="High-test peroxide">high-test peroxide</a> (HTP), which decomposes into water and oxygen in the presence of a catalyst and could provide a <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> of 140 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jarvis_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-jarvis-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies20038.28_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies20038.28-13">[13]</a></sup> The HTP also fueled a turbopump for the main engines and auxiliary power units (APUs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-paul-8">[8]</a></sup> Additional tanks for helium and liquid nitrogen performed other functions, for example the fuselage interior was purged with helium gas, and the liquid nitrogen was used as coolant for various systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paul_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-paul-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wedge_tail_and_hypersonic_stability">Wedge tail and hypersonic stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-15_and_B-52_Mother_ship.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="772" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="166" Talon">T-38</a> flying nearby</div></div></div>\n<p>The X-15 had a thick wedge tail to enable it to fly in a steady manner at hypersonic speeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendell_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-wendell-14">[14]</a></sup> This produced a significant amount of drag at lower speeds;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendell_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-wendell-14">[14]</a></sup> the blunt end at the rear of the X-15 could produce as much drag as an entire <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter" title="Lockheed F-104 Starfighter">F-104 Starfighter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendell_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-wendell-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em A vertical-tail area equal to 60 percent of the wing area was required to give the X-15 adequate directional stability.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Wendell H. aided by side panels which could be extended from the tail to increase the overall surface area, and these panels doubled as air-brakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wendell_14-3"><a However, the X-15 ranks supreme among manned rocket-powered aircraft, becoming the world\'s first operational <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a> in the early 1960s.\n</p><p>Before 1958, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> (USAF) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NACA" title="NACA">NACA</a> officials discussed an orbital X-15 <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a>, the <b>X-15B</b> that would launch into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> from atop an <a href="/wiki/SM-64_Navaho" title="SM-64 Navaho">SM-64 Navaho</a> missile.  This was canceled when the NACA became <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and adopted <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a> instead.\n</p><p>By 1959, the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_X-20_Dyna-Soar" title="Boeing X-20 Dyna-Soar">Boeing X-20 Dyna-Soar</a> space-glider program was to become the USAF\'s preferred means for launching military manned spacecraft into orbit. This program was canceled in the early 1960s before an operational vehicle could be built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110-4">[4]</a></sup>  Various configurations of the Navajo were considered, and another proposal involved a Titan I stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wade_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-wade-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three X-15s were built, flying 199 test flights, the last on 24 October 1968.\n</p><p>The first X-15 flight was an unpowered glide flight by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scott_Crossfield" title="Scott Crossfield">Scott Crossfield</a>, on 8 June 1959. Crossfield also piloted the first powered flight on 17 September 1959, and his first flight with the XLR-99 <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a> on 15 November 1960. Twelve test pilots flew the X-15. Among these were <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>, later a NASA <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> and first man to set foot on the Moon, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Engle" title="Joe Engle">Joe Engle</a>, later a commander of <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">NASA Space Shuttle</a> missions.\n</p><p>In a 1962 proposal, NASA considered using the B-52/X-15 as a launch platform for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Scout" title="Blue Scout">Blue Scout</a> rocket to place satellites weighing up to 150 pounds (68&#160;kg) into orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wade_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-wade-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cis20120321_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cis20120321-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July and August 1963, pilot <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Walker" title="Joseph A. Walker">Joseph A. Walker</a> exceeded <span class="nowrap">100 km</span> in altitude, joining NASA astronauts and Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonauts</a> as the first human beings to cross that line on their way to <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.  The USAF awarded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_wings" title="Astronaut wings">astronaut wings</a> to anyone achieving an altitude of 50 miles (80&#160;km), while the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale">FAI</a> set the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">limit of space</a> at 100 kilometers (62.1&#160;mi).\n</p><p>On 15 November 1967, U.S. Air Force test pilot Major <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Adams" title="Michael J. Adams">Michael J. Adams</a> was killed during <a href="/wiki/X-15_Flight_3-65-97" title="X-15 Flight 3-65-97">X-15 Flight 191</a> when X-15-3, AF Ser. No. 56-6672, entered a hypersonic spin while descending, then oscillated violently as aerodynamic forces increased after re-entry. As his aircraft\'s flight control system operated the control surfaces to their limits, acceleration built to 15&#160;<a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity"><i>g<sub>0</sub></i></a> (150&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared" title="Metre per second squared">m/s<sup>2</sup></a>) vertical and 8.0&#160;<a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity"><i>g<sub>0</sub></i></a> (78&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared" title="Metre per second squared">m/s<sup>2</sup></a>) lateral.  The airframe broke apart at 60,000 feet (18&#160;km) altitude, scattering the X-15\'s wreckage for 50 square miles (130&#160;km<sup>2</sup>).  On 8 May 2004, a monument was erected at the cockpit\'s locale, near <a href="/wiki/Randsburg,_California" title="Randsburg, California">Randsburg, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xpress2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-xpress2004-17">[17]</a></sup> Major Adams was posthumously awarded Air Force astronaut wings for his final flight in X-15-3, which had reached an altitude of 50.4 miles (81.1&#160;km). In 1991, his name was added to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Mirror_Memorial" title="Space Mirror Memorial">Astronaut Memorial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xpress2004_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-xpress2004-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The second plane, X-15-2, was rebuilt after a landing accident on 9 November 1962 which damaged the craft and injured its pilot, <a href="/wiki/John_B._McKay" title="John B. McKay">John McKay</a>.  It was lengthened by 2.4 feet (73&#160;cm), had a pair of auxiliary fuel tanks attached beneath its <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> and wings, and a complete heat-resistant <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry#Ablative" title="Atmospheric entry">ablative</a> coating was added. The plane was renamed the <b>X-15A-2</b>, and took flight for the first time on 25 June 1964. It reached its maximum speed of 4,520 miles per hour (7,274&#160;km/h) in October 1967 with pilot <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_%22Pete%22_Knight" title=\'William "Pete" Knight\'>William "Pete" Knight</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> in control.\n</p><p>Five principal aircraft were used during the X-15 program: three X-15 planes and two modified "nonstandard" <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">NB-52</a> bombers:\n</p>\n<ul><li><span class="nowrap"><b>X-15-1</b> &#8211;</span> <i>56-6670</i>, 81 free flights</li>\n<li><span class="nowrap"><b>X-15-2</b> (later <b>X-15A-2</b>) &#8211;</span> <i>56-6671</i>, 31 free flights as X-15-2, 22 free flights as X-15A-2, and 53 in total</li>\n<li><span class="nowrap"><b>X-15-3</b> &#8211;</span> <i>56-6672</i>, 65 free flights, including the <a href="/wiki/X-15_Flight_3-65-97" title="X-15 Flight 3-65-97">Flight 191</a> disaster</li>\n<li><span class="nowrap"><b>NB-52A</b> &#8211;</span> <i>52-003</i> nicknamed <i>The High and Mighty One</i> (retired in October 1969)</li>\n<li><span class="nowrap"><b>NB-52B</b> &#8211;</span> <i><a href="/wiki/Balls_8" title="Balls 8">52-008</a></i> nicknamed <i>The Challenger</i>, later <i>Balls 8</i> (retired in November 2004)</li></ul>\n<p>Additionally, <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-100_Super_Sabre" title="North American F-100 Super Sabre">F-100</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter" title="Lockheed F-104 Starfighter">F-104</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_F5D_Skylancer" title="Douglas F5D Skylancer">F5D</a> <a href="/wiki/Chase_plane" title="Chase plane">chase</a> aircraft and <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules" title="Lockheed C-130 Hercules">C-130</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">C-47</a> transports supported the program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A 200th flight over Nevada was first scheduled for 21 November 1968, to be flown by William "Pete" Knight. Numerous technical problems and outbreaks of bad weather delayed this proposed flight six times, and it was permanently canceled on 20 December 1968. This X-15 was detached from the B-52 and then put into indefinite storage. The aircraft was later donated to the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="National Museum of the United States Air Force">Air Force Museum</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wright-Patterson_Air_Force_Base" title="Wright-Patterson Air Force Base">Wright-Patterson Air Force Base</a> for display.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n<li All five Air Force pilots flew above 50 miles and were awarded military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_wings" title="Astronaut wings">astronaut wings</a> contemporaneously with their achievements, including Adams, who received the distinction posthumously following the flight 191 disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  However the other three were NASA employees, and did not receive a comparable decoration at the time.  In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> conferred its first-ever commercial astronaut wings on <a href="/wiki/Mike_Melvill" title="Mike Melvill">Mike Melvill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Binnie" title="Brian Binnie">Brian Binnie</a>, pilots of the commercial <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>, another spaceplane with a flight profile comparable to the X-15\'s.
