href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Mojave Desert</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="\'m\' in \'my\'">m</span><span title="/o&#650;/: \'o\' in \'code\'">o&#650;</span><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'h\' in \'hi\'">h</span><span title="/&#593;&#720;/: \'a\' in \'father\'">&#593;&#720;</span><span title="\'v\' in \'vie\'">v</span><span title="/i/: \'y\' in \'happy\'">i</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="\'m\' in \'my\'">m</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span></span>-/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">mo-<span style="font-size:90%">HAH</span>-vee</i></a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is an arid <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain-shadow</a> desert and the driest <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDW_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MDW-5">[5]</a></sup> It is in the southwestern United States, primarily within southeastern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, and it occupies 47,877&#160;sq&#160;mi (124,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Very small areas also extend into <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its boundaries are generally noted by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Yucca_brevifolia" title="Yucca brevifolia">Joshua trees</a>, which are native only to the Mojave Desert and are considered an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a>, and it is believed to support an additional 1,750 to 2,000 species of plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The central part of the desert is sparsely populated, while its peripheries support large communities such as <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barstow,_California" title="Barstow, California">Barstow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_California" title="Lancaster, California">Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palmdale,_California" title="Palmdale, California">Palmdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victorville,_California" title="Victorville, California">Victorville</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._George,_Utah" title="St. George, Utah">St. George</a>.\n</p><p>The Mojave Desert is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin_Desert" title="Great Basin Desert">Great Basin Desert</a> to its north<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDW_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-MDW-5">[5]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a> to its south and east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDW_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-MDW-5">[5]</a></sup> Topographical boundaries include the <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi_Mountains" title="Tehachapi Mountains">Tehachapi Mountains</a> to the west, and the <a href="/wiki/San_Gabriel_Mountains" title="San Gabriel Mountains">San Gabriel Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Bernardino_Mountains" title="San Bernardino Mountains">San Bernardino Mountains</a> to the south. The mountain boundaries are distinct because they are outlined by the two largest faults in California &#8211; the <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">San Andreas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garlock_Fault" title="Garlock Fault">Garlock</a> faults. The Mojave Desert displays typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basin_and_range" title="Basin and range">basin and range</a> topography. Higher elevations above 2,000&#160;ft (610&#160;m)) in the Mojave are commonly referred to as the High Desert; however, <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a> is the lowest elevation in North America at 280&#160;ft (85&#160;m) below sea level and is one of the Mojave Desert\'s more notorious places. The Mojave Desert occupies less than 50,000&#160;sq&#160;mi (130,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), making it the smallest of the North American deserts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MDW_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-MDW-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Mojave Desert is often referred to as the "high desert", in contrast to the "low desert", the Sonoran Desert to the south. However, the Mojave Desert is generally lower than the Great Basin Desert to the north.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The spelling <i>Mojave</i> originates from the Spanish language while the spelling <i>Mohave</i> comes from modern English. Both are used today, although the <a href="/wiki/Mohave_people" title="Mohave people">Mojave</a> Tribal Nation officially uses the spelling <i>Mojave</i>; the word is a shortened form of <b>Hamakhaave</b>, their <a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" title="Exonym and endonym">endonym</a> in their native language, which means \'beside the water\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mojave_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Mojave Desert receives less than 2 inches (51&#160;mm)  of rain a year and is generally between 2,000 and 5,000 feet (610 and 1,520&#160;m) in elevation. The Mojave Desert also contains the <a href="/wiki/Mojave_National_Preserve" title="Mojave National Preserve">Mojave National Preserve</a>, as well as the lowest and hottest place in North America: <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_National_Park" title="Death Valley National Park">Death Valley</a> at 282&#160;ft (86&#160;m) below sea level, where the temperature often surpasses 120&#160;&#176;F (49&#160;&#176;C) from late June to early August. <a href="/wiki/Zion_National_Park" title="Zion National Park">Zion National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> lies at the junction of the Mojave, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin_Desert" title="Great Basin Desert">Great Basin Desert</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a>. Despite its aridity, the Mojave (and particularly the <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Valley" title="Antelope Valley">Antelope Valley</a> in its southwest) has long been a center of <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> production, fed by irrigation coming from <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and from the <a href="/wiki/California_Aqueduct" title="California Aqueduct">California Aqueduct</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2571" data-file-width="2112" decoding="async" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg/220px-Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg/330px-Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg/440px-Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mustard_Blue_Sunset.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mustard blue summer sunset at <a href="/wiki/Landers,_California" title="Landers, California">Landers, California</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The Mojave is a desert of temperature extremes and two distinct seasons. Winter months bring comfortable daytime temperatures, which occasionally drop to around 25&#160;&#176;F (&#8722;4&#160;&#176;C) on valley floors, and below 0&#160;&#176;F (&#8722;18&#160;&#176;C) at the highest elevations. Storms moving from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> can bring rain and in some places even snow. More often, the <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> created by the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> as well as mountain ranges within the desert such as the <a href="/wiki/Spring_Mountains" title="Spring Mountains">Spring Mountains</a>, bring only clouds and wind. In longer periods between storm systems, winter temperatures in valleys can approach 80&#160;&#176;F (27&#160;&#176;C).\n</p><p>Spring weather continues to be influenced by Pacific storms, but rainfall is more widespread and occurs less often after April. By early June, it is rare for another Pacific storm to have a significant impact on the region\'s weather; and temperatures after the middle of May are normally above 90&#160;&#176;F (32&#160;&#176;C) and frequently above 100&#160;&#176;F (38&#160;&#176;C).\n</p><p>Summer weather is dominated by heat. Temperatures on valley floors can soar above 120&#160;&#176;F (49&#160;&#176;C) and above 130&#160;&#176;F (54&#160;&#176;C) at the lowest elevations. Low humidity, high temperatures, and low pressure, draw in moisture from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> creating thunderstorms across the desert southwest known as the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Monsoon" title="North American Monsoon">North American monsoon</a>. While the Mojave does not get nearly the amount of rainfall the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonoran_desert" title="Sonoran desert">Sonoran desert</a> to the south receives, monsoonal moisture will create thunderstorms as far west as California\'s <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a> from mid-June through early September.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Desert_Diablo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Desert_Diablo.jpg/220px-Desert_Diablo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Desert_Diablo.jpg/330px-Desert_Diablo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Desert_Diablo.jpg/440px-Desert_Diablo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Desert_Diablo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clouds reflecting high wind conditions from <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a> in the Mojave Desert</div></div></div>\n<p>Autumn is generally pleasant, with one to two Pacific storm systems creating regional rain events. October is one of the driest and sunniest months in the Mojave; and average high temperatures usually remain between 70&#160;&#176;F (21&#160;&#176;C) and 90&#160;&#176;F (32&#160;&#176;C) on the valley floors.\n</p><p>After temperature, wind is the most significant weather phenomenon in the Mojave. Across the region windy days are common; and also common in areas near the transition between the Mojave and the California low valleys, including near <a href="/wiki/Cajon_Pass" title="Cajon Pass">Cajon Pass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soledad_Canyon" title="Soledad Canyon">Soledad Canyon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tehachapi,_California" title="Tehachapi, California">Tehachapi</a> areas. During the <a href="/wiki/June_Gloom" title="June Gloom">June Gloom</a>, cooler air can be pushed into the desert from Southern California. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Ana_wind" title="Santa Ana wind">Santa Ana wind</a> events, hot air from the desert blows into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_basin" title="Los Angeles basin">Los Angeles basin</a> and other coastal areas. <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">Wind farms</a> in these areas generate power from these winds.\n</p><p>The other major weather factor in the region is elevation. The highest peak within the Mojave is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charleston_Peak" title="Charleston Peak">Charleston Peak</a> at 11,918 feet (3,633&#160;m),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-State_of_Nevada_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-State_of_Nevada-9">[9]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Badwater_Basin" title="Badwater Basin">Badwater Basin</a> in Death Valley is 279 feet (85&#160;m) below sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NED_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NED-10">[10]</a></sup> Accordingly, temperature and precipitation ranges wildly in all seasons across the region.\n</p><p>The Mojave Desert has not historically supported a fire regime because of low fuel loads and connectivity. However, in the last few decades, invasive annual plants such as some within the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Bromus" title="Bromus">Bromus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Schismus" title="Schismus">Schismus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">Brassica</a></i> have facilitated fire. This has significantly altered many areas of the desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> At higher elevations, fire regimes are regular but infrequent.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable collapsible" style="width:100%; These ranges often create valleys, <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_pan_(geology)" title="Salt pan (geology)">salt pans</a>, and seasonal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saline_lake" title="Saline lake">saline lakes</a> when precipitation is high enough. These mountain ranges and valleys are part of the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range Province</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a>, a geologic area of crustal thinning which pulls open valleys over millions of years. Most of the valleys are internally drained (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorheic" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a> basins), so all precipitation that falls within the valley does not eventually flow to the ocean. Some of the Mojave (toward the east, in and around the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>/<a href="/wiki/Virgin_River_Gorge" title="Virgin River Gorge">Virgin River Gorge</a>) is within a different geographic domain called the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" The metropolitan areas include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas</a>, the largest city in the Mojave with a metropolitan population of around 2.3 million in 2015; <a href="/wiki/St._George,_Utah" title="St. George, Utah">St. George</a> is the northeastern-most metropolitan area in the Mojave, and is located at the convergence of the Mojave, Great Basin and Colorado Plateau. <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_California" title="Lancaster, California">Lancaster</a> is the largest California city in the desert; over 850,000 people live in areas of the Mojave attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Los_Angeles_area" title="Greater Los Angeles area">Greater Los Angeles</a> metropolitan area, including Palmdale and Lancaster, (referred to as the Antelope Valley), <a href="/wiki/Victorville,_California" title="Victorville, California">Victorville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Valley,_California" title="Apple Valley, California">Apple Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hesperia,_California" title="Hesperia, California">Hesperia</a> (referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Valley,_California" title="Victor Valley, California">Victor Valley</a>) attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inland_Empire_(CA)" title="Inland Empire (CA)">Inland Empire</a> metropolitan area, the 14th largest in the nation.\n</p><p>Smaller cities or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micropolitan" title="Micropolitan">micropolitan</a> areas in the Mojave Desert include <a href="/wiki/Helendale,_California" title="Helendale, California">Helendale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Havasu_City,_Arizona" title="Lake Havasu City, Arizona">Lake Havasu City</a>,\n<a href="/wiki/Kingman,_Arizona" title="Kingman, Arizona">Kingman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laughlin,_Nevada" title="Laughlin, Nevada">Laughlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bullhead_City,_Arizona" title="Bullhead City, Arizona">Bullhead City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pahrump,_Nevada" title="Pahrump, Nevada">Pahrump</a>. All have experienced rapid population growth since 1990.\n</p><p>Notable towns with fewer than 30,000 people in the Mojave include <a href="/wiki/Barstow,_California" title="Barstow, California">Barstow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron,_California" title="Boron, California">Boron</a>, \n<a href="/wiki/California_City,_California" title="California City, California">California City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helendale,_California" title="Helendale, California">Helendale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Tree,_California" title="Joshua Tree, California">Joshua Tree</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landers,_California" title="Landers, California">Landers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lone_Pine,_California" title="Lone Pine, California">Lone Pine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucerne_Valley" title="Lucerne Valley">Lucerne Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mojave,_California" title="Mojave, California">Mojave</a>, <a href="/wiki/Needles,_California" title="Needles, California">Needles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nipton,_California" title="Nipton, California">Nipton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pioneertown,_California" title="Pioneertown, California">Pioneertown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Randsburg,_California" title="Randsburg, California">Randsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ridgecrest,_California" title="Ridgecrest, California">Ridgecrest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rosamond,_California" title="Rosamond, California">Rosamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yucca_Valley,_California" title="Yucca Valley, California">Yucca Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twentynine_Palms,_California" title="Twentynine Palms, California">Twentynine Palms</a> in California; <a href="/wiki/Mesquite,_Nevada" title="Mesquite, Nevada">Mesquite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moapa_Valley,_Nevada" title="Moapa Valley, Nevada">Moapa Valley</a> in Nevada; and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane,_Utah" title="Hurricane, Utah">Hurricane</a> in Utah.\n</p><p>The California portion of the desert also contains <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Weapons_Station_China_Lake" title="Naval Air Weapons Station China Lake">Naval Air Weapons Station China Lake</a>, noted for experimental aviation and weapons projects, and the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Air_Ground_Combat_Center_Twentynine_Palms" title="Marine Corps Air Ground Combat Center Twentynine Palms">largest Marine Corps base</a> in the world at Twentynine Palms. The US Army also maintains <a href="/wiki/Fort_Irwin_National_Training_Center#Geography" title="Fort Irwin National Training Center">Fort Irwin</a> &amp; the National Training Center (NTC) which is another major training area for the United States military. Mojave airport is also home to a long term storage facility for large airplanes due to extremely dry non-corrosive weather conditions and a hard ground ideal for parking aircraft. The airport also houses the Air and Space Port and was one of the test centres for the Virgin Galactic Fleet.\n</p><p>The Mojave Desert has several <a href="/wiki/Ghost_town" title="Ghost town">ghost towns</a>; the most significant are the gold-mining town of <a href="/wiki/Oatman,_Arizona" title="Oatman, Arizona">Oatman, Arizona</a>, the silver-mining town of <a href="/wiki/Calico,_San_Bernardino_County,_California" title="Calico, San Bernardino County, California">Calico, California</a>, and the old railroad depot of <a href="/wiki/Kelso,_California" title="Kelso, California">Kelso</a>. Some of the other ghost towns are more modern, created when <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_66" title="U.S. Route 66">U.S. Route 66</a> (and the lesser-known <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_91" title="U.S. Route 91">U.S. Route 91</a>) were abandoned in favor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_highway" title="Interstate highway">Interstates</a>. The Mojave Desert is crossed by major highways <a href="/wiki/Interstate_15" title="Interstate 15">Interstate 15</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_40" title="Interstate 40">Interstate 40</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_95" title="U.S. Route 95">U.S. Route 95</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_395" title="U.S. Route 395">U.S. Route 395</a> and <a href="/wiki/California_State_Route_58" title="California State Route 58">California State Route 58</a>.\n</p><p>Other than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(U.S.)" title="Colorado River (U.S.)">Colorado River</a> on the eastern half of the Mojave, few long streams cross the desert. The <a href="/wiki/Mojave_River" title="Mojave River">Mojave River</a> is an important source of water for the southern parts of the desert. The <a href="/wiki/Amargosa_River" title="Amargosa River">Amargosa River</a> flows from the Great Basin Desert south to near <a href="/wiki/Beatty,_Nevada" title="Beatty, Nevada">Beatty, Nevada</a>, then underground through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ash_Meadows" title="Ash Meadows">Ash Meadows</a> before returning to the surface near <a href="/wiki/Shoshone,_California" title="Shoshone, California">Shoshone, California</a>, disappearing underground again a short while later and has its final outlet into the southern end of Death Valley. The riverbed passes under SR 127 near Dumont Dunes before turning north into Death Valley National Park.\n</p><p>The Mojave Desert is also home to the <a href="/wiki/Devils_Playground" title="Devils Playground">Devils Playground</a>, about 40 miles (64&#160;km) of dunes and salt flats going in a northwest-southeasterly direction. The Devils Playground is a part of the Mojave National Preserve and is between the town of <a href="/wiki/Baker,_California" title="Baker, California">Baker, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Providence_Mountains" title="Providence Mountains">Providence Mountains</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cronese_Mountains" title="Cronese Mountains">Cronese Mountains</a> are within the Devils Playground.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_tourism">Parks and tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mojave_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parks and tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Mojave Desert is one of the most popular tourism spots in North America, primarily because of the gambling destination of Las Vegas. The Mojave is also known for its scenic beauty, playing host to <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley_National_Park" title="Death Valley National Park">Death Valley National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Tree_National_Park" title="Joshua Tree National Park">Joshua Tree National Park</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mojave_National_Preserve" title="Mojave National Preserve">Mojave National Preserve</a>. Lakes <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mead" title="Lake Mead">Mead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mohave" title="Lake Mohave">Mohave</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Havasu" title="Lake Havasu">Havasu</a> provide water sports recreation, and vast off-road areas entice off-road enthusiasts. The Mojave Desert also includes two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_State_Parks" title="California State Parks">California State Parks</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Valley_California_Poppy_Reserve" title="Antelope Valley California Poppy Reserve">Antelope Valley California Poppy Reserve</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_California" title="Lancaster, California">Lancaster</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saddleback_Butte_State_Park" title="Saddleback Butte State Park">Saddleback Butte State Park</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Hi_Vista,_California" title="Hi Vista, California">Hi Vista</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Dam" title="Hoover Dam">Hoover Dam</a> is a popular tourist destination. Visitors get a chance to see the structure, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> plant, and hear the history of the dam\'s construction during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Besides the major national parks, there are other areas of identified significance and tourist interest in the desert such as the <a href="/wiki/Big_Morongo_Canyon_Preserve" title="Big Morongo Canyon Preserve">Big Morongo Canyon Preserve</a>, within the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Desert" title="Colorado Desert">Colorado Desert</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Rock_Canyon_National_Conservation_Area" title="Red Rock Canyon National Conservation Area">Red Rock Canyon National Conservation Area</a>, 17 miles (27&#160;km) west of Las Vegas, both of which are managed by the Bureau of Land Management.\n</p><p>Among the more popular and unique tourist attractions in the Mojave is the self described <a href="/wiki/World%27s_tallest_thermometer" title="World\'s tallest thermometer">world\'s tallest thermometer</a> at 134 feet (41&#160;m) high, which is along <a href="/wiki/Interstate_15_in_California" title="Interstate 15 in California">Interstate 15</a> in Baker, California. The newly renovated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelso_Depot,_Restaurant_and_Employees_Hotel" title="Kelso Depot, Restaurant and Employees Hotel">Kelso Depot</a> is the Visitor Center for the Mojave National Preserve. Nearby the massive <a href="/wiki/Kelso_Dunes" title="Kelso Dunes">Kelso Dunes</a> are a popular recreation spot. Nipton, California, on the northern entrance to the Mojave National Preserve, is a restored ghost town founded in 1885.\n</p><p>Several attractions and natural features are in the <a href="/wiki/Calico_Mountains_(California)" title="Calico Mountains (California)">Calico Mountains</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calico_Ghost_Town" title="Calico Ghost Town">Calico Ghost Town</a>, in Yermo, is administered by San Bernardino County. The ghost town has several shops and attractions, and inspired <a href="/wiki/Walter_Knott" title="Walter Knott">Walter Knott</a> to build <a href="/wiki/Knott%27s_Berry_Farm" title="Knott\'s Berry Farm">Knott\'s Berry Farm</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management" title="Bureau of Land Management">BLM</a> also administers <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Basin" title="Rainbow Basin">Rainbow Basin</a> and Owl Canyon, two "off-the-beaten-path" scenic attractions together north of Barstow in the Calicos. The <a href="/wiki/Calico_Early_Man_Site" title="Calico Early Man Site">Calico Early Man Site</a>, in the Calico Hills east of <a href="/wiki/Yermo,_California" title="Yermo, California">Yermo</a>, is believed by some archaeologists, including the late <a href="/wiki/Louis_Leakey" title="Louis Leakey">Louis Leakey</a>, to show the earliest evidence with <a href="/wiki/Lithic_analysis" title="Lithic analysis">lithic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_tools" title="Stone tools">stone tools</a> found here of human activity in North America. The <a href="/wiki/Calico_Peaks" title="Calico Peaks">Calico Peaks</a> scenically rise above all the destinations.\n</p><p>A tour of the Mojave Desert inspired American songwriter <a href="/wiki/Carrie_Jacobs-Bond" title="Carrie Jacobs-Bond">Carrie Jacobs-Bond</a> to compose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parlor_song" title="Parlor song">parlor song</a> "<a class="mw-headline" id="Soil_and_plants_conditions">Soil and plants conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mojave_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soil and plants conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The soil types in the Mojave and Sonoran deserts are mostly derived from volcano, specifically the areas in California. As the topography goes down, particle sizes decreases as you move down the gradient, where you can also find low alkalinity. These erosional gradient is a habitat for many plant communities. As you go up the gradient of the desert, you will find more <a href="/wiki/Pediment_(geology)" title="Pediment (geology)">pediment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fan</a> soil. In this areas, the flora is mostly succulents. As you move down the gradient, this area is described to be the upper and lower <a href="/wiki/Bajada_(geography)" title="Bajada (geography)">bajada</a>, then you move into playa and salina, and finally you will reach the river. Drought deciduous plants are found in the alluvial fans and upper bajada. Evergreen perennials are found in some parts of the upper bajada, but it is mostly found in lower bajada. Once the salinity increases, you will find more salt-tolerant plants. In the river areas, deep-rooted plants reside. Due to the harsh and dry conditions in the desert, the plants have adapted to have succulent leaves, with CAM photosynthesis, spines, buried bulbs, hairy or waxy leaves, photosynthetic stems, deep tap roots, and ephemerals.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Mojave_plan_litigation">West Mojave plan litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mojave_Desert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: West Mojave plan litigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The U.S. Bureau of Land Management (BLM) manages public lands in the Mojave Desert as part of its "crown jewels of the <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">American West</a>" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Landscape_Conservation_System" title="National Landscape Conservation System">National Landscape Conservation System</a>. It has designated numerous large <a href="/wiki/Off-road_vehicle" title="Off-road vehicle">off-road vehicle</a> open use areas on public lands in the western Mojave Desert, including El Mirage, <a href="/wiki/Jawbone_Canyon" title="Jawbone Canyon">Jawbone Canyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rasor_Off-Highway_Vehicle_Area" title="Rasor Off-Highway Vehicle Area">Rasor</a>, Spangler Hills, Stoddard Valley, Dove Spring Canyon, <a href="/wiki/Dumont_Dunes" title="Dumont Dunes">Dumont Dunes</a>, and the world\'s largest open off-road vehicle use area, <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Valley,_California" title="Johnson Valley, California">Johnson Valley</a>. Open areas designated for unrestricted vehicle travel in the western Mojave Desert total 363,480 acres (1,471.0&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Several additional open areas dedicated to unrestricted vehicle travel on public lands have been designated in the northern and eastern Colorado (NECO) Desert. In 2002, BLM designated all washes in the southeastern third of the NECO planning area as also open to unrestricted vehicle travel. This was followed in 2003 by BLM expanding the off-road vehicle network in the western Mojave Desert to enhance off-road vehicle recreation opportunity. In 2004, relative to the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Center_for_Biological_Diversity" title="Center for Biological Diversity">Center for Biological Diversity</a>, et al., Plaintiffs, v. Bureau of Land Management, et al., Defendants</i>; the United States District court enjoined "all off-road vehicle use in the washes of the NECO Desert planning area pending issuance of a new biological opinion.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>a</span></sup> A new biological opinion was subsequently issued and BLM\'s open wash designation in the NECO planning area was reinstated. In 2006, several environmental groups protested an additional route network expansion designated under the West Mojave Desert (WEMO) plan.\n</p><p>In 2009, U.S. District Judge <a href="/wiki/Susan_Illston" title="Susan Illston">Susan Illston</a> ruled against the BLM\'s proposed designation of additional off-road vehicle use allowance in the western Mojave Desert. According to the ruling, the BLM violated its own regulations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wil_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-wil-20">[20]</a></sup> when it designated approximately 5,000 miles (8,000&#160;km) of <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">off-roading</a> routes in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merc_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-merc-21">[21]</a></sup> According to Judge Ilston, the BLM\'s designation was significantly "flawed because it does not contain a reasonable range of alternatives" to limit damage to sensitive habitat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-22">[22]</a></sup> Judge Illston found the bureau had inadequately analyzed the routes\' impacts on air quality, soils, plant communities, riparian habitats, and sensitive species such as the endangered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uma_scoparia" title="Uma scoparia">Mojave fringe-toed lizard</a>, pointing out that the desert and its resources are "extremely fragile, easily scarred, and slowly healed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-lat-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The court also found the BLM failed to follow route designation procedures established in the agency&#8217;s own California Desert Conservation Area Plan, which allowed visitors to create hundreds of illegal OHV routes during the past three decades. The plan normally requires the BLM to consider the impacts to private property, non-motorized recreation opportunity, and natural resources before establishing off-road areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wil_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-wil-20">[20]</a></sup> The adopted West Mojave plan amendment was found to have violated the BLM\'s own manual of regulations, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Land_Policy_and_Management_Act_of_1976" title="Federal Land Policy and Management Act of 1976">Federal Land Policy and Management Act of 1976</a> (FLPMA) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act_of_1969" title="National Environmental Policy Act of 1969">National Environmental Policy Act of 1969</a> (NEPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merc_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-merc-21">[21]</a></sup> The ruling was considered a success for a coalition of conservation groups, including the <a href="/wiki/California_Native_Plant_Society" title="California Native Plant Society">California Native Plant Society</a>, Friends of Juniper Flats, the Alliance for Responsible Recreation, Community Off-Road Vehicle Watch, The Center for Biological Diversity, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(United_States)" title="The Wilderness Society (United States)">The Wilderness Society</a>, who together initiated the legal challenge in late 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-lat-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2011, Judge Illston ruled on a remedy request submitted by the ten involved environmental organizations. BLM in this ruling was directed to complete a revised WEMO route designation complying with all laws and regulations by March 2014. The agency is also required per this ruling to place signs on all off-road vehicle routes which are legal to use, create a monitoring plan to determine if illegal vehicle use is occurring, and provide additional enforcement to prevent illegal use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span
