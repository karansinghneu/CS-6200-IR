Indies)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Diogo Fernandes Pereira</b>, sometimes called simply <b>Diogo Fernandes</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> 16th-century navigator, originally from <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Set&#250;bal">Set&#250;bal</a>, Portugal.  Diogo Fernandes was the first known European captain to visit the island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a> in 1503 and the discoverer of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mascarenes" title="Mascarenes">Mascarenes</a> archipelago (<a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="R&#233;union">R&#233;union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues" title="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a>) in 1507.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He may also have been the first European to sail east of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> island (\'outer route\' to the East Indies).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" tocsection-7"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Diogo Fernandes Pereira\'s name is usually given simply as \'Diogo Fernandes\'. He is sometimes referred to as Diogo Fernandes de <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Set&#250;bal">Set&#250;bal</a> (his hometown), to distinguish him from another <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> adventurer of that period with a similar name, known as Diogo Fernandes de <a href="/wiki/Beja,_Portugal" title="Beja, Portugal">Beja</a>.  In older chronicles, (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Dami%C3%A3o_de_G%C3%B3is" title="Dami&#227;o de G&#243;is">Dami&#227;o de G&#243;is</a>) his name is also written as Diogo Fernandes \'Piteira\' or \'Peteira\'.\n</p><p>Diogo Fernandes was a Portuguese seaman of obscure background.  According to <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Barros" title="Jo&#227;o de Barros">Jo&#227;o de Barros</a>, he was "a native of Set&#250;bal, a man much used at sea" (<i>muito usado no mar</i>).  He served as <a href="/wiki/Master_(naval)" title="Master (naval)">master</a> on several ships - that is, as third officer, below the pilot and captain, a position which required trained navigational expertise, and may have served as pilot on other occasions.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Outer_Route_and_Socotra_.281503.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_Route_and_Socotra_(1503)">Outer Route and Socotra (1503)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outer Route and Socotra (1503)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1503, Diogo Fernandes Pereira was appointed  <a href="/wiki/Master_(naval)" title="Master (naval)">master</a> <i>and</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_(nautical)" title="Captain (nautical)">captain</a> (a very unusual combination) of a <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Set&#250;bal">Set&#250;bal</a> ship bound for India. How a master was elevated to captain of an India nau (usually a position reserved for nobles or wealthy men who \'paid\' for the privilege) is uncertain.  One possible conjecture is that the ship was not a crown ship, but a privately outfitted ship.\n</p><p>In some secondary accounts, it is said that Diogo Fernandes\'s ship was named <i>Set&#250;bal</i>. That is almost certainly incorrect.  Known lists for the armada and the chronicles do not actually give his ship a name, but just call it "the ship from/of Set&#250;bal", a strong suggestion that it might have been privately outfitted by the merchant community of the city of <a href="/wiki/Set%C3%BAbal" title="Set&#250;bal">Set&#250;bal</a>.  If so, that might better explain how Diogo Fernandes got to be captain - the merchants of Set&#250;bal just naturally chose to entrust their capital in their most knowledgeable and experienced native son.\n</p><p>Diogo Fernandes\'s Set&#250;bal ship was made part of the <a href="/wiki/5th_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Albuquerque,_1503)" title="5th Portuguese India Armada (Albuquerque, 1503)">5th Portuguese India Armada</a> of <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Fernandes was originally assigned to the third squadron of that fleet, under the vice-admiral <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Saldanha" title="Ant&#243;nio de Saldanha">Ant&#243;nio de Saldanha</a>.  But navigational errors (on Saldanha\'s part) led to the separation of the ships shortly after <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>.  Diogo Fernandes was forced to sail on alone.\n</p><p>The chronicler <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Correia" title="Gaspar Correia">Gaspar Correia</a> (p.&#160;418) claims that after doubling the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> by himself, Fernandes did not turn into the <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Channel" title="Mozambique Channel">Mozambique Channel</a>, but rather pushed east, sailing <i>under</i> the island of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, and then turned north, sailing up east of Madagascar.  This would make him the first known ship to sail the \'<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India_Armadas#Timing" title="Portuguese India Armadas">outer route</a>\' to the East Indies. (Although there remains the possibility that <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Dias" title="Diogo Dias">Diogo Dias</a> also did precisely that in 1500.)\n</p><p>Although Correia\'s account is not corroborated by other chroniclers, Diogo Fernandes Pereira seems to almost certainly have missed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozambique_Island" title="Mozambique Island">Mozambique Island</a>, the usual collection point for Portuguese ships, and where one of his squadron, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rui_Louren%C3%A7o_Ravasco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rui Louren&#231;o Ravasco (page does not exist)">Rui Louren&#231;o Ravasco</a>, was known to be waiting.  Instead, we next hear of Fernandes up near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Guardafui" title="Cape Guardafui">Cape Guardafui</a>, which strongly suggests that he did take the outer route, as, sailing north by that route, he would not have sighted African coast before the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">horn</a>.\n</p><p>Around <a href="/wiki/Cape_Guardafui" title="Cape Guardafui">Cape Guardafui</a>, Diogo Fernandes stumbled on the island of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a> sometime in late 1503.  Although the island was long known to eastern merchants (Socotra <a href="/wiki/Aloe" title="Aloe">aloe</a> was a highly valued balm in the markets of Arabia and India), it was unknown to the Portuguese. Diogo Fernandes was also surprised to encounter a strong <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Church_of_the_East" title="Assyrian Church of the East">(Syriac) Christian</a> community on the island (after all, this was a predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> region). Diogo Fernandes spent the winter in Socotra, before crossing the Indian Ocean (again by himself) in early 1504.\n</p><p>Diogo Fernandes arrived in India just as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zamorin" title="Zamorin">Zamorin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Calicut</a> was launching an invasion of Portuguese-allied  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochin" title="Cochin">Cochin</a>.  During the ferocious <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cochin_(1504)" title="Battle of Cochin (1504)">Battle of Cochin (1504)</a>, the commander of the Portuguese garrison, <a href="/wiki/Duarte_Pacheco_Pereira" title="Duarte Pacheco Pereira">Duarte Pacheco Pereira</a>, is said to have placed a certain \'Diogo Pereira\' (possibly Diogo Fernandes?) in charge of the <a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">nau</a> <i>Concep&#231;&#227;o</i>, protecting the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Mascarenes_.281507.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascarenes_(1507)">Mascarenes (1507)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mascarenes (1507)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Diogo Fernandes returned to Portugal in 1505.  His report on Socotra generated much excitement in the Portuguese court. The strategic placement of the island at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> made it an optimal location to station a Portuguese patrol. It could prey on Arab shipping and shut down the competing <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spice trade</a> through that route.  The existence of the isolated Christian community only made it even more appealing (the Portuguese had long hoped to find a Christian port of call in the Muslim-dominated <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>).  Plans were immediately launched to outfit a squadron, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a>, to seize the island for Portugal.\n</p><p>The Socotra squadron was adjoined to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=8th_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Cunha,_1506)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="8th Portuguese India Armada (Cunha, 1506) (page does not exist)">8th Armada</a> of 1506 led by <a href="/wiki/Trist%C3%A3o_da_Cunha" title="Trist&#227;o da Cunha">Trist&#227;o da Cunha</a>.  Diogo Fernandes himself sailed on it, albeit only as a <a href="/wiki/Master_(naval)" title="Master (naval)">master</a> of Albuquerque\'s ship, the <i>Cisne</i> (\'the Swan\', usually transcribed as <i>Cirn&#233;</i>).\n</p><p>In late 1506, when the 8th Armada made the usual stop in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozambique_Island" title="Mozambique Island">Mozambique Island</a>, the admiral Trist&#227;o da Cunha ordered a pause in the expedition to explore the island of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> (then known as the <i>ilha de S&#227;o Louren&#231;o</i>).  During this interlude, it is speculated that Albuquerque (uninterested in such exploratory ventures) elected to stay in Mozambique, and temporarily passed his ship, the <i>Cirn&#233;</i>, over to the command of the master Diogo Fernandes Pereira.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" which he promptly named <i>ilha de Santa Apollonia</i> (in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Apollonia" title="Saint Apollonia">St. Apollonia</a> who\'s day it was, February 9, 1507).  He proceeded east to discover the island of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, which he named <i>ilha do Cirne</i> (the name of his ship). From there Fernandes went further east and discovered the island now known as <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues" title="Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a>, but which at the time was named on maps as the <i>ilha de Diogo Fernandes</i>, <i>Domigo Friz</i> or <i>Domingo Frias</i> (the latter two probably just poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographic" title="Cartographic">cartographic</a> transcriptions or abbreviations of \'Diogo Fernandes\').  He is said to have stopped for water at the first and third islands, before returning to Mozambique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Diogo Fernandes island (\'Domigo Friz\') was visited by <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Lopes_de_Sequeira" title="Diogo Lopes de Sequeira">Diogo Lopes de Sequeira</a> in 1509 and the name \'Don Galopes\' (another transcribed abbreviation) sometimes appears for that island in some maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  It went through its final name change, to Rodrigues island much later, after another Portuguese explorer <a href="/wiki/Diogo_Rodrigues" title="Diogo Rodrigues">Diogo Rodrigues</a> visited the area in 1528.\n</p><p>The entire archipelago was named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mascarenes" title="Mascarenes">Mascarenes</a> islands, after D. <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Mascarenhas" title="Pedro Mascarenhas">Pedro Mascarenhas</a>, who visited the islands in 1512, following up on Sequeira\'s report.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Afterwards">Afterwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Afterwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After returning from his Mascarene jaunt, Diogo Fernandes Pereira went on to participate with Albuquerque in the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Socotra" title="Socotra">Socotra</a> that summer.  We have no more records of him afterwards.  If Diogo Fernandes remained aboard the <i>Cirne</i> after Socotra, he would have participated in the battle of <a href="/wiki/Ormus" title="Ormus">Hormuz</a> in the Fall of 1507, but we have no record of him there.  More importantly, we have no record of his participation (or non-participation) in the mutiny against Albuquerque at Hormuz.  We do know Albuquerque elevated a certain \'Dinis Fernandes\' to replace one of the mutinous captains; and we also know a certain \'Diogo Pereira\' was involved in presenting the mutinous captains\' petition to the vice-roy in Cochin.  But there is no evidence or suggestion that either of these men can be identified with Diogo Fernandes Pereira.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Clara Pinto Correia (2003) <i>Return of the crazy bird: the sad, strange tale of the Dodo</i>.  New York: Springer.</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Quintella (1839: p.273); chronicler Gaspar Correia suggests, at various points (e.g. p.312, 320), that Fernandes may also have been on one of the ships of the <a href="/wiki/4th_Portuguese_India_Armada_(Gama,_1502)" title="4th Portuguese India Armada (Gama, 1502)">1502 armada</a> of Vasco da Gama, although this is clearly a mistake as that armada did not return to Lisbon before the 5th Armada left.</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Costa (1973) suggests Diogo Fernandes Pereira only discovered Socotra in April, 1504 and left for India only in May, which would suggest he did not make it to Cochin before the battle was largely over, and so this \'Diogo Pereira\' must be a different person.  But chronicles are very ambiguous on that. Nor do they explain satisfactorily what Fernandes was doing in East Africa up to such a late date, and how he could have missed his comrades Saldanha &amp; Ravasco, who were making a nuisance of themselves all over the area.  Late 1503 seems the likelier date for the discovery &amp; \'wintering\' in Socotra.</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Appendix in Le Guat (1891, v2.: 316)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-5">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Pinto-Correia (2003: 24)</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Appendix in Le Guat (1891, v.2: p.316)</span>\n</li>\n</ol></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_Fernandes_Pereira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Correia" title="Gaspar Correia">Gaspar Correia</a> (c. 1550s) <i>Lendas da &#205;ndia</i>, pub. 1858-64, Lisbon: Academia Real de Sciencias</li>\n<li>Costa, J.P.
