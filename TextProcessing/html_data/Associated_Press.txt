Founded in 1846, it operates as a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_association" title="Unincorporated association">unincorporated association</a>. The AP is owned by its contributing newspapers and radio and television stations in the United States, all of which contribute stories to the AP and use material written by its staff journalists. Its Statement of News Values and Principles spells out its standards and practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The AP has earned 52 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prizes" title="Pulitzer Prizes">Pulitzer Prizes</a>, including 31 for photography, since the award was established in 1917.\n</p><p>The AP has counted the vote in U.S. elections since 1848, including national, state and local races down to the legislative level in all 50 states, along with key ballot measures. AP collects and verifies returns in every county, parish, city and town across the U.S., and declares winners in over 5,000 contests.\n</p><p>The AP news report, distributed to its members and customers, is produced in English, Spanish and Arabic. AP content is also available on the agency\'s app, AP News. A 2017 study by <a href="/wiki/NewsWhip" title="NewsWhip">NewsWhip</a> revealed that AP content was more engaged with on Facebook than content from any individual English-language publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, news collected by the AP was published and republished by more than 1,300 newspapers and broadcasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The AP operates 263 <a href="/wiki/News_bureau" title="News bureau">news bureaus</a> in 106 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It also operates the AP Radio Network, which provides newscasts twice hourly for broadcast and satellite radio and television stations. Many newspapers and broadcasters outside the United States are AP subscribers, paying a fee to use AP material without being contributing members of the cooperative. As part of their cooperative agreement with the AP, most member news organizations grant automatic permission for the AP to distribute their <a href="/wiki/Local_news" title="Local news">local news</a> reports. The AP employs the "<a href="/wiki/Inverted_pyramid_(journalism)" title="Inverted pyramid (journalism)">inverted pyramid</a>" formula for writing which enables the news outlets to edit a story to fit its available publication area without losing the story\'s essentials.\n</p><p>Cutbacks at rival <a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">United Press International</a> in 1993 left the AP as the United States\' primary news service, although UPI still produces and distributes stories and photos daily. Other English-language news services, such as the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> and the English-language service of <a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">10</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Associated Press was formed in May 1846<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> by five daily newspapers in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to share the cost of transmitting news of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican&#8211;American War">Mexican&#8211;American War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The venture was organized by <a href="/wiki/Moses_Yale_Beach" title="Moses Yale Beach">Moses Yale Beach</a> (1800&#8211;68), second publisher of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sun_(New_York)" title="The Sun (New York)">The Sun</a></i>, joined by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald" title="New York Herald">New York Herald</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Courier_and_Enquirer" title="New York Courier and Enquirer">New York Courier and Enquirer</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Journal_of_Commerce" title="The Journal of Commerce">The Journal of Commerce</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Evening_Express" title="New York Evening Express">New York Evening Express</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some historians<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tribune_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tribune-10">[10]</a></sup> believe that the <i>Tribune</i> joined at this time; documents show it was a member in 1849. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> became a member shortly after its founding in September 1851. Initially known as the New York Associated Press (NYAP), the organization faced competition from the Western Associated Press (1862), which criticized its monopolistic news gathering and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_setting" title="Price setting">price setting</a> practices. An investigation completed in 1892 by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Lawson" title="Victor Lawson">Victor Lawson</a>, editor and publisher of the <i>Chicago Daily News</i>, revealed that several principals of the NYAP had entered into a secret agreement with United Press, a rival organization, to share NYAP news and the profits of reselling it. The revelations led to the demise of the NYAP and in December 1892, the Western Associated Press was incorporated in Illinois as The Associated Press. A 1900 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illinois_Supreme_Court" title="Illinois Supreme Court">Illinois Supreme Court</a> decision (<i>Inter Ocean Publishing Co. v. Associated Press</i>)&#8212;that the AP was a <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">public utility</a> and operating in restraint of trade&#8212;resulted in AP\'s move from Chicago to New York City, where corporation laws were more favorable to cooperatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>When the AP was founded, news became a salable commodity. The invention of the rotary press allowed the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Tribune" title="New York Tribune">New York Tribune</a></i> in the 1870s to print 18,000 papers per hour. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish&#8211;American War">Spanish&#8211;American War</a>, there was a new incentive to print vivid, on-the-spot reporting. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melville_Stone" title="Melville Stone">Melville Stone</a>, who had founded the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Daily_News" title="Chicago Daily News">Chicago Daily News</a></i> in 1875, served as AP General Manager from 1893 to 1921. He embraced the standards of accuracy, impartiality, and integrity. The cooperative grew rapidly under the leadership of Kent Cooper (served 1925&#8211;48), who built up bureau staff in South America, Europe and (after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War&#160;II</a>), the Middle East. He introduced the "telegraph typewriter" or teletypewriter into newsrooms in 1914. In 1935, AP launched the Wirephoto network, which allowed transmission of news photographs over leased private telephone lines on the day they were taken. This gave AP a major advantage over other news media outlets. While the first network was only between New York, Chicago, and San Francisco, eventually AP had its network across the whole United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1945, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> held in <i><a href="/wiki/Associated_Press_v._United_States" title="Associated Press v. United States">Associated Press v. United States</a></i> that the AP had been violating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sherman_Antitrust_Act" title="Sherman Antitrust Act">Sherman Antitrust Act</a> by prohibiting member newspapers from selling or providing news to nonmember organizations as well as making it very difficult for nonmember newspapers to join the AP. The decision facilitated the growth of its main rival <a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">United Press International</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Baillie" title="Hugh Baillie">Hugh Baillie</a> from 1935 to 1955.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg/170px-The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg/255px-The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg/340px-The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_associated_press_building_in_new_york_city.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Logo on the former AP Building in New York City</div></div></div>\n<p>AP entered the broadcast field in 1941 when it began distributing news to radio stations; it created its own radio network in 1974. In 1994, it established APTV, a global video newsgathering agency. APTV merged with WorldWide Television News in 1998 to form APTN, which provides video to international broadcasters and websites. In 2004, AP moved its world headquarters from its longtime home at 50 Rockefeller Plaza to a huge building at <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_West" title="Manhattan West">450 West 33rd Street</a> in Manhattan&#8212;which also houses the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i> and the studios of New York\'s public television station, <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>. In 2009, AP had more than 240 bureaus globally. Its mission&#8212;"to gather with economy and efficiency an accurate and impartial report of the news"&#8212;has not changed since its founding, but digital technology has made the distribution of the AP news report an interactive endeavor between AP and its 1,400 U.S. newspaper members as well as broadcasters, international subscribers, and online customers.\n</p><p>The AP began diversifying its news gathering capabilities and by 2007 AP was generating only about 30% of its revenue from United States newspapers. 37% came from the global broadcast customers, 15% from online ventures and 18% came from international newspapers and from photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Web_resource">Web resource</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Web resource">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The AP\'s multi-topic structure has resulted in web portals such as <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> and <a href="/wiki/MSN" title="MSN">MSN</a> posting its articles, often relying on the AP as their first source for news coverage of breaking news items. This and the constant updating evolving stories require has had a major impact on the AP\'s public image and role, giving new credence to the AP\'s ongoing mission of having staff for covering every area of news fully and promptly. href="#cite_note-washington_post-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2017, following a US court appearance, a judge ruled in favor of the AP in a lawsuit against the FBI for frauduently id="cite_ref-chiacagotribune_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-chiacagotribune-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fair-use_controversies">Fair-use controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fair-use controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In June 2008, the AP sent numerous <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">DMCA</a> take down demands and threatened legal action against several <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a>. The AP contended that the internet blogs were violating AP\'s <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> by linking to AP material and using headlines and short summaries in those links. Many bloggers and experts noted that the use of the AP news fell squarely under commonly accepted internet practices and within <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair-use</a> standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Others noted and demonstrated that AP routinely takes similar excerpts from other sources, often without attribution or licenses. AP responded that it was defining standards regarding citations of AP news.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright_and_intellectual_property">Copyright and intellectual property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Copyright and intellectual property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In August 2005, Ken Knight, a <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> photographer, sued the AP claiming that it had willfully and negligently violated Knight\'s copyright by distributing a photograph of celebrity <a href="/wiki/Britney_Spears" title="Britney Spears">Britney Spears</a> to various media outlets including, but not limited to: <a href="/wiki/TruTV" title="TruTV">truTV</a> (formerly CourtTV), America Online and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The case was settled in November 2006.\n</p><p>In a case filed February 2005, <i>McClatchey v. The Associated Press</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> photographer sued the AP for <a href="/wiki/Cropping_(image)" title="Cropping (image)">cropping</a> a picture to remove the plaintiff\'s embedded title and copyright notice and later distributed it to news organizations without the plaintiff\'s permission or credit. The parties settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Shepard_Fairey">Shepard Fairey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Shepard Fairey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In March 2009, the Associated Press counter-sued artist <a href="/wiki/Shepard_Fairey" title="Shepard Fairey">Shepard Fairey</a> over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_HOPE_poster" title="Obama HOPE poster">his famous image of Barack Obama</a>, saying the uncredited, uncompensated use of an AP photo violated <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">copyright laws</a> and signaled a threat to <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>. Fairey had sued the AP the previous month over his artwork, titled "Obama Hope" and "Obama Progress", arguing that he did not violate copyright law because he dramatically changed the image. The artwork, based on an April 2006 picture taken for the AP by <a href="/wiki/Mannie_Garcia" title="Mannie Garcia">Mannie Garcia</a>, was a popular image during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008 presidential election</a> and now hangs in the <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery_(United_States)" title="National Portrait Gallery (United States)">National Portrait Gallery</a> in Washington, D.C. According to the AP lawsuit filed in federal court in Manhattan, Fairey knowingly "misappropriated The AP\'s rights in that image". The suit asked the court to award AP profits made off the image and damages. Fairey said he looked forward to "upholding the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">free expression</a> rights at stake here" and disproving the AP\'s accusations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In January 2011 this suit was settled with neither side declaring their position to be wrong but agreeing to share reproduction rights and profits from Fairey\'s work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPRFaireyvAPSettlement_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPRFaireyvAPSettlement-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_News">Hot News</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hot News">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In January 2008, Associated Press sued competitor <a href="/wiki/All_Headline_News" title="All Headline News">All Headline News</a> (AHN) claiming that AHN allegedly infringed on its copyrights and a contentious "quasi-property" right to facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The AP complaint asserted that AHN reporters had copied facts from AP news reports without permission and without paying a syndication fee. After AHN moved to dismiss all but the copyright claims set forth by AP, a majority of the lawsuit was dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The case has been dismissed and both parties settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2010, Associated Press was accused<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> of having unfair and hypocritical policies after it was demonstrated that AP reporters had copied original reporting from the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_Engine_Land" title="Search Engine Land">Search Engine Land</a>" website without permission, attribution, or credit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".22Illegal_immigrant.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'"Illegal_immigrant"\'>"Illegal immigrant"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title=\'Edit section: "Illegal immigrant"\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In April 2013, AP stated that it had dropped the term "<a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration" title="Illegal immigration">illegal immigrant</a>" from its <a href="/wiki/AP_Stylebook" title="AP Stylebook">stylebook</a>. AP follows ABC, NBC, and CNN in not using the term. <a href="/wiki/Jose_Antonio_Vargas" title="Jose Antonio Vargas">Jose Antonio Vargas</a> commended The Associated Press for its decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Syndicated writer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruben_Navarrette&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruben Navarrette (page does not exist)">Ruben Navarrette</a> criticized the decision, stating the reasoning behind the decision was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_correct" title="Political correct">political correctness</a> and called the blog "incomprehensible".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Homeland_Security" title="Secretary of Homeland Security">Secretary of Homeland Security</a> <a href="/wiki/Janet_Napolitano" title="Janet Napolitano">Janet Napolitano</a> said of the decision, that she doesn\'t get involved in "vocabulary wars" and then stated "They are immigrants who are here illegally, that\'s an illegal immigrant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FN3APR2013_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FN3APR2013-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoax_tweet_and_flash_crash">Hoax tweet and flash crash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Associated_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hoax tweet and flash crash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On April 23, 2013, the AP\'s <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account was hacked to release a hoax tweet about fictional attacks in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> that left <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">President Obama</a> injured.<sup Jones Industrial Average">Dow Jones Industrial Average</a>, removal of $136 billion from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%26P_500" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> index,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and the temporary suspension of their Twitter account. Although all executed trades were considered final, the Dow Jones later restored its session gains.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Justice_Department_subpoena_of_phone_records">Justice Cole</a>, was in charge of the AP investigation and would have ordered the subpoenas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcpolitics_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcpolitics-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Claims_of_biased_reporting">Claims of biased reporting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
