11</a></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Daedalus</b> is a prominent <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> located near the center of the <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">far side</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>. The inner wall is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terrace" title="wiktionary:terrace">terraced</a>, and there is a cluster of central peaks on the relatively flat floor. Because of its location (shielded from radio emissions from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>), it has been proposed as the site of a future giant <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a>, which would be scooped out of the crater itself, much like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arecibo_radio_telescope" title="Arecibo radio telescope">Arecibo radio telescope</a>, but on a vastly larger scale.\n</p><p>The crater is named after <a href="/wiki/Daedalus" title="Daedalus">Daedalus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_myth" title="Greek myth">Greek myth</a>. It is pictured in famous photographs taken by the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> astronauts.  In contemporary sources it was called "Crater 308" (this was a temporary <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">IAU</a> designation that preceded the establishment of far-side lunar nomenclature).\n</p><p>Nearby craters of note include <a href="/wiki/Icarus_(crater)" title="Icarus (crater)">Icarus</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Racah_(crater)" title="Racah (crater)">Racah</a> to the south.
