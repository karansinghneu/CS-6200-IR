href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit is named after <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a>. As with every International System of Units (SI) unit named for a person, the first letter of its <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> is <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">upper case</a> (J). However, when an SI unit is spelled out in English, it is treated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_noun" title="Common noun">common noun</a> and should always begin with a <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">lower case</a> letter (<i>joule</i>)&#8212;except in a situation where <i>any</i> word in that position would be capitalized, such as at the beginning of a sentence or in material using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_case" title="Title case">title case</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exception_of_newton_metre">Exception of newton metre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Exception of newton metre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">Newton metre</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a>, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> (in some direction) has a close analog in the concept of <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> (about some angle):\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Linear</th>\n<th>Angular\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Force</td>\n<td>Torque\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">Mass</a></td>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">Moment of inertia</a>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Displacement\n<p>(sometimes position)\n</p>\n</td>\n<td>Angle\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A result of this similarity is that the SI unit for torque is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a>, which works out <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraically</a> to have the same <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensions</a> as the joule. But they are not interchangeable. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGPM" title="CGPM">CGPM</a> has given the unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> the name <i>joule</i>, but has not given the unit of torque any special name, hence it is simply the newton metre (N&#8901;m) &#8211; a compound name derived from its constituent parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup> The use of newton metres for torque and joules for energy is helpful to avoid misunderstandings and miscommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The distinction may be seen also in the fact that energy is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> &#8211; the <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vector</a> force and a vector displacement. While the watt-second is equivalent to the joule in both units and meaning, there are some contexts in which the term "watt-second" is used instead of "joule".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Guide_number" title="Guide number">Guide number</a></div>\n<p>In photography, the unit for <a href="/wiki/Flash_(photography)" title="Flash (photography)">flashes</a> is the watt-second. A flash can be rated in watt-seconds (e.g. 300&#160;W&#8901;s) or in joules (different names for the same thing), but historically the term "watt-second" has been used and continues to be used. An on-camera flash, using a 1000 <a href="/wiki/Farad" title="Farad">microfarad</a> capacitor at 300 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a>, would be 45 watt-seconds.  Studio flashes, using larger capacitors and higher voltages, are in the 200&#8211;2000 watt-second range.\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\text{Energy of a flash in joules or watt-seconds}}={\\dfrac {1}{2}}\\cdot {\\text{capacitance of the storage capacitor in farads}}\\cdot {\\text{working voltage}}^{2}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mtext>Energy of a flash in joules or watt-seconds</mtext>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mfrac>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mfrac>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<mo>&#8901;<!-- &#8901; Some companies specify their products in "true" watt-seconds, and some specify their products in "nominal" watt-seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a
