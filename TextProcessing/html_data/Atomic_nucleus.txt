In this diagram, protons and neutrons look like little balls stuck together, but an actual nucleus (as understood by modern <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a>) cannot be explained like this, but only by using <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>. In a nucleus which occupies a certain <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy level</a> (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a>), each nucleon can be said to occupy a range of locations.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>atomic nucleus</b> is the small, dense region consisting of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> at the center of an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>, discovered in 1911 by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> based on the 1909 <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93Marsden_experiment" title="Geiger&#8211;Marsden experiment">Geiger&#8211;Marsden gold foil experiment</a>.  After the discovery of the neutron in 1932, models for a nucleus composed of protons and neutrons were quickly developed by <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Ivanenko" title="Dmitri Ivanenko">Dmitri Ivanenko</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  An atom is composed of a positively-charged nucleus, with a cloud of negatively-charged <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> surrounding it, bound together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_force" title="Electrostatic force">electrostatic force</a>.  Almost all of the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of an atom is located in the nucleus, with a very small contribution from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">electron cloud</a>.  Protons and neutrons are bound together to form a nucleus by the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a>.\n</p><p>The diameter of the nucleus is in the range of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6985175659999999999&#9824;</span>1.7566&#160;<a href="/wiki/Femtometre" title="Femtometre">fm</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6985175659999999999&#9824;</span>1.7566<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;15</sup>&#160;m</span>) for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (the diameter of a single proton) to about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6986117142000000000&#9824;</span>11.7142&#160;<a href="/wiki/Femtometre" title="Femtometre">fm</a></span> for the heaviest atom <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atomic_Data_and_Nuclear_Data_Tables_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atomic_Data_and_Nuclear_Data_Tables-7">[7]</a></sup> These dimensions are much smaller than the diameter of the atom itself (nucleus + electron cloud), by a factor of about 26,634 (uranium atomic radius is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990156000000000000&#9824;</span>156&#160;<a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">pm</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990156000000000000&#9824;</span>156<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;12</sup>&#160;m</span>))<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> to about 60,250 (<a href="/wiki/Bohr_radius" title="Bohr radius">hydrogen atomic radius</a> is about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6989529200000000000&#9824;</span>52.92&#160;<a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">pm</a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The branch of physics concerned with the study and understanding of the atomic nucleus, including its composition and the forces which bind it together, is called <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-15"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_model" title="Rutherford model">Rutherford model</a></div>\n<p>The nucleus was discovered in 1911, as a result of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a>\'s efforts to test Thomson\'s "<a href="/wiki/Plum_pudding_model" title="Plum pudding model">plum pudding model</a>" of the atom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The electron had already been discovered earlier by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.J._Thomson" title="J.J. Thomson">J.J. Thomson</a> himself. Knowing that atoms are electrically neutral, Thomson postulated that there must be a positive charge as well. In his plum pudding model, Thomson suggested that an atom consisted of negative electrons randomly scattered within a sphere of positive charge. Ernest Rutherford later devised an experiment with his research partner <a href="/wiki/Hans_Geiger" title="Hans Geiger">Hans Geiger</a> and with help of <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Marsden" title="Ernest Marsden">Ernest Marsden</a>, that involved the deflection of <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a> (helium nuclei) directed at a thin sheet of metal foil. He reasoned that if Thomson\'s model were correct, the positively charged alpha particles would easily pass through the foil with very little deviation in their paths, as the foil should act as electrically neutral if the negative and positive charges are so intimately mixed as to make it appear neutral. To his surprise, many of the particles were deflected at very large angles. Because the mass of an alpha particle is about 8000 times that of an electron, it became apparent that a very strong force must be present if it could deflect the massive and fast moving alpha particles. He realized that the plum pudding model could not be accurate and that the deflections of the alpha particles could only be explained if the positive and negative charges were separated from each other and that the mass of the atom was a concentrated point of positive charge. This justified the idea of a nuclear atom with a dense center of positive charge and mass.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term <b>nucleus</b> is from the Latin word <i>nucleus</i>, a diminutive of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nux" title="wikt:nux"><i>nux</i></a> ("nut"), meaning the kernel (i.e., the "small nut") inside a watery type of fruit (like a peach). In 1844, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> used the term to refer to the "central point of an atom". The modern atomic meaning was proposed by Ernest Rutherford in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The adoption of the term "nucleus" to atomic theory, however, was not immediate. In 1916, for example, <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis" title="Gilbert N. Lewis">Gilbert N. Lewis</a> stated, in his famous article <i>The Atom and the Molecule</i>, that "the atom is composed of the <i>kernel</i> and an outer atom or <i>shell</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_makeup">Nuclear makeup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuclear makeup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Helium_atom_QM.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="665" decoding="async" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Helium_atom_QM.svg/300px-Helium_atom_QM.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Helium_atom_QM.svg/450px-Helium_atom_QM.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Helium_atom_QM.svg/600px-Helium_atom_QM.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Helium_atom_QM.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A figurative depiction of the <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>-4 atom with the electron cloud in shades of gray. In the nucleus, the two protons and two neutrons are depicted in red and blue. This depiction shows the particles as separate, whereas in an actual helium atom, the protons are superimposed in space and most likely found at the very center of the nucleus, and the same is true of the two neutrons. Thus, all four particles are most likely found in exactly the same space, at the central point. Classical images of separate particles fail to model known charge distributions in very small nuclei. A more accurate image is that the spatial distribution of nucleons in a helium nucleus is much closer to the helium <b>electron cloud</b> shown here, although on a far smaller scale, than to the fanciful nucleus image.</div></div></div>\n<p>The nucleus of an atom consists of neutrons and protons, which in turn are the manifestation of more elementary particles, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quarks" title="Quarks">quarks</a>, that are held in association by the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">nuclear strong force</a> in certain stable combinations of <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a>. The nuclear strong force extends far enough from each baryon so as to bind the neutrons and protons together against the repulsive electrical force between the positively charged protons. The nuclear strong force has a very short range, and essentially drops to zero just beyond the edge of the nucleus. The collective action of the positively charged nucleus is to hold the electrically negative charged electrons in their orbits about the nucleus. The collection of negatively charged electrons orbiting the nucleus display an affinity for certain configurations and numbers of electrons that make their orbits stable. Which <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> an atom represents is determined by the number of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> in the nucleus; the neutral atom will have an equal number of electrons orbiting that nucleus. Individual chemical elements can create more stable electron configurations by combining to share their electrons. It is that sharing of electrons to create stable electronic orbits about the nucleus that appears to us as the chemistry of our macro world.\n</p><p>Protons define the entire charge of a nucleus, and hence its <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical identity</a>. Neutrons are electrically neutral, but contribute to the mass of a nucleus to nearly the same extent as the protons. Neutrons can explain the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> (same atomic number with different atomic mass.) The main role of neutrons is to reduce electrostatic repulsion inside the nucleus.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_shape">Composition and shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Composition and shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Protons and neutrons are <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>, with different values of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_isospin" title="Strong isospin">strong isospin</a> <a href="/wiki/Quantum_number" title="Quantum number">quantum number</a>, so two protons and two neutrons can share the same space <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a> since they are not identical quantum entities. They are sometimes viewed as two different quantum states of the same particle, the <i><a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleon</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sitenko_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sitenko-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srednicki_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Srednicki-14">[13]</a></sup> Two fermions, such as two protons, or two neutrons, or a proton + neutron (the deuteron) can exhibit <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosonic</a> behavior when they become loosely bound in pairs, which have integer spin.\n</p><p>In the rare case of a <a href="/wiki/Hypernucleus" title="Hypernucleus">hypernucleus</a>, a third <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryon</a> called a <a href="/wiki/Hyperon" title="Hyperon">hyperon</a>, containing one or more <a href="/wiki/Strangeness" title="Strangeness">strange quarks</a> and/or other unusual quark(s), can also share the wave function. However, this type of nucleus is extremely unstable and not found on Earth except in high energy physics experiments.\n</p><p>The neutron has a positively charged core of radius &#8776; 0.3 fm surrounded by a compensating negative charge of radius between 0.3 fm and 2 fm. The proton has an approximately exponentially decaying positive charge distribution with a mean square radius of about 0.8 fm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basdevant2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basdevant2-15">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nuclei can be spherical, rugby ball-shaped (prolate deformation), discus-shaped (oblate deformation), triaxial (a combination of oblate and prolate deformation) or pear-shaped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaffney2013_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaffney2013-17">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forces">Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Nuclei are bound together by the residual strong force (<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">nuclear force</a>). The residual strong force is a minor residuum of the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a> which binds quarks together to form protons and neutrons. This force is much weaker <i>between</i> neutrons and protons because it is mostly neutralized within them, in the same way that electromagnetic forces <i>between</i> neutral atoms (such as <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a> that act between two inert gas atoms) are much weaker than the electromagnetic forces that hold the parts of the atoms together internally (for example, the forces that hold the electrons in an inert gas atom bound to its nucleus).\n</p><p>The nuclear force is highly attractive at the distance of typical nucleon separation, and this overwhelms the repulsion between protons due to the electromagnetic force, thus allowing nuclei to exist. However, the residual strong force has a limited range because it decays quickly with distance (see <a href="/wiki/Yukawa_potential" title="Yukawa potential">Yukawa potential</a>); thus only nuclei smaller than a certain size can be completely stable. The largest known completely stable nucleus (i.e. stable to alpha, beta, and gamma decay) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead-208" title="Lead-208">lead-208</a> which contains a total of 208 nucleons (126 neutrons and 82 protons). Nuclei larger than this maximum are unstable and tend to be increasingly short-lived with larger numbers of nucleons. However, <a href="/wiki/Bismuth-209" title="Bismuth-209">bismuth-209</a> is also stable to beta decay and has the longest half-life to alpha decay of any known isotope, estimated at a billion times longer than the age of the universe.\n</p><p>The residual strong force is effective over a very short range (usually only a few <a href="/wiki/Femtometre" title="Femtometre">femtometres</a> (fm); roughly one or two nucleon diameters) and causes an attraction between any pair of nucleons. For example, between <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> to form [NP] <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deuteron" title="Deuteron">deuteron</a>, and also between protons and protons, and neutrons and neutrons.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Halo_nuclei_and_strong_force_range_limits">Halo nuclei and strong force range limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Halo nuclei and strong force range limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The effective absolute limit of the range of the strong force is represented by <a href="/wiki/Halo_nucleus" title="Halo nucleus">halo nuclei</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-11" title="Lithium-11">lithium-11</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron-14" title="Boron-14">boron-14</a>, in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dineutron" title="Dineutron">dineutrons</a>, or other collections of neutrons, orbit at distances of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6986100000000000000&#9824;</span>10&#160;fm</span> (roughly similar to the <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6985800000000000000&#9824;</span>8&#160;fm</span> radius of the nucleus of uranium-238). These nuclei are not maximally dense. Halo nuclei form at the extreme edges of the chart of the nuclides&#8212;the neutron drip line and proton drip line&#8212;and are all unstable with short half-lives, measured in <a href="/wiki/Millisecond" title="Millisecond">milliseconds</a>; for example, lithium-11 has a half-life of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997880000000000000&#9824;</span>8.8&#160;ms</span>.\n</p><p>Halos in effect represent an excited state with nucleons in an outer quantum shell which has unfilled energy levels "below" it (both in terms of radius and energy). The halo may be made of either neutrons [NN, NNN] or protons [PP, PPP]. Nuclei which have a single neutron halo include <sup>11</sup>Be and <sup>19</sup>C. A two-neutron halo is exhibited by <sup>6</sup>He, <sup>11</sup>Li, <sup>17</sup>B, <sup>19</sup>B and <sup>22</sup>C. Two-neutron halo nuclei break into three fragments, never two, and are called <i><a href="/wiki/Borromean_nucleus" title="Borromean nucleus">Borromean nuclei</a></i> because of this behavior (referring to a system of three interlocked rings in which breaking any ring frees both of the others). <sup>8</sup>He and <sup>14</sup>Be both exhibit a four-neutron halo. Nuclei which have a proton halo include <sup>8</sup>B and <sup>26</sup>P. A two-proton halo is exhibited by <sup>17</sup>Ne and <sup>27</sup>S. Proton halos are expected to be more rare and unstable than the neutron examples, because of the repulsive electromagnetic forces of the excess proton(s).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_models">Nuclear models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nuclear models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_structure" title="Nuclear structure">nuclear structure</a></div>\n<p>Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_model" title="Standard model">standard model</a> of physics is widely believed to completely describe the composition and behavior of the nucleus, generating predictions from theory is much more difficult than for most other areas of <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>.  This is due to two reasons:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In principle, the physics within a nucleus can be derived entirely from <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a> (QCD). In practice however, current computational and mathematical approaches for solving QCD in low-energy systems such as the nuclei are extremely limited. This is due to the <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a> that occurs between high-energy <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quark</a> matter and low-energy <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadronic</a> matter, which renders <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)">perturbative techniques</a> unusable, making it difficult to construct an accurate QCD-derived model of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_force" title="Nuclear force">forces between nucleons</a>. Current approaches are limited to either phenomenological models such as the Argonne v18 potential or <a href="/wiki/Chiral_perturbation_theory" title="Chiral perturbation theory">chiral effective field theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Machleidt_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Machleidt-18">[17]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Even if the nuclear force is well constrained, a significant amount of computational power is required to accurately compute the properties of nuclei <a href="/wiki/Ab_initio_methods_(nuclear_physics)" title="Ab initio methods (nuclear physics)"><i>ab initio</i></a>. Developments in <a href="/wiki/Many-body_theory" title="Many-body theory">many-body theory</a> have made this possible for many low mass and relatively stable nuclei, but further improvements in both computational power and mathematical approaches are required before heavy nuclei or highly unstable nuclei can be tackled.</li></ul>\n<p>Historically, experiments have been compared to relatively crude models that are necessarily imperfect. None of these models can completely explain experimental data on nuclear structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-19">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_size" title="Nuclear size">nuclear radius</a> (<i>R</i>) is considered to be one of the basic quantities that any model must predict. For stable nuclei (not halo nuclei or other unstable distorted nuclei) the nuclear radius is roughly proportional to the cube root of the <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> (<i>A</i>) of the nucleus, and particularly in nuclei containing many nucleons, as they arrange in more spherical configurations:\n</p><p>The stable nucleus has approximately a constant density and therefore the nuclear radius R can be approximated by the following formula,\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle R=r_{0}A^{1/3}\\,}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>R</mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<msub>\n<mi>r</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>0</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<msup>\n<mi>A</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>/</mo>\n</mrow>\n<mn>3</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msup>\n<mspace width="thinmathspace"></mspace>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle R=r_{0}A^{1/3}\\,}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="R=r_{0}A^{{1/3}}\\," aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0019db781a99ca4fc1bd2ac77f92ded1bf24eebb" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:11.794ex; height:3.176ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where <i>A</i> = Atomic <a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">mass number</a> (the number of protons <i>Z</i>, plus the number of neutrons <i>N</i>) and <i>r</i><sub>0</sub>&#160;=&#160;1.25&#160;fm&#160;=&#160;1.25&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>&#8722;15</sup>&#160;m. In this equation, the "constant" <i>r</i><sub>0</sub> varies by 0.2&#160;fm, depending on the nucleus in question, but this is less than 20% change from a constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In other words, packing protons and neutrons in the nucleus gives <i>approximately</i> the same total size result as packing hard spheres of a constant size (like marbles) into a tight spherical or almost spherical bag (some stable nuclei are not quite spherical, but are known to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate" title="Prolate">prolate</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Models of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_structure" title="Nuclear structure">nuclear structure</a> include&#160;:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_drop_model">Liquid drop model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Liquid drop model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Semi-empirical_mass_formula" title="Semi-empirical mass formula">Semi-empirical mass formula</a></div>\n<p>Early models of the nucleus viewed the nucleus as a rotating liquid drop. In this model, the trade-off of long-range electromagnetic forces and relatively short-range nuclear forces, together cause behavior which resembled surface tension forces in liquid drops of different sizes. This formula is successful at explaining many important phenomena of nuclei, such as their changing amounts of <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">binding energy</a> as their size and composition changes (see <a href="/wiki/Semi-empirical_mass_formula" title="Semi-empirical mass formula">semi-empirical mass formula</a>), but it does not explain the special stability which occurs when nuclei have special "magic numbers" of protons or neutrons.\n</p><p>The terms in the semi-empirical mass formula, which can be used to approximate the binding energy of many nuclei, are considered as the sum of five types of energies (see below). Then the picture of a nucleus as a drop of incompressible liquid roughly accounts for the observed variation of binding energy of the nucleus:\n</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liquid_drop_model.svg"><img alt="Liquid drop model.svg" data-file-height="198" data-file-width="765" decoding="async" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Liquid_drop_model.svg/765px-Liquid_drop_model.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Liquid_drop_model.svg/1148px-Liquid_drop_model.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Liquid_drop_model.svg/1530px-Liquid_drop_model.svg.png 2x" width="765"/></a>\n</p><p><b>Volume energy</b>.  When an assembly of nucleons of the same size is packed together into the smallest volume, each interior nucleon has a certain number of other nucleons in contact with it. So, this nuclear energy is proportional to the volume.\n</p><p><b>Surface energy</b>.  A nucleon at the surface of a nucleus interacts with fewer other nucleons than one in the interior of the nucleus and hence its binding energy is less. This surface energy term takes that into account and is therefore negative and is proportional to the surface area.\n</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb\'s law">Coulomb</a> Energy</b>. The electric repulsion between each pair of protons in a nucleus contributes toward decreasing its binding energy.\n</p><p><b>Asymmetry energy</b> (also called <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Pauli</a> Energy).  An energy associated with the <a href="/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle" title="Pauli exclusion principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>. Were it not for the Coulomb energy, the most stable form of nuclear matter would have the same number of neutrons as protons, since unequal numbers of neutrons and protons imply filling higher energy levels for one type of particle, while leaving lower energy levels vacant for the other type.\n</p><p><b>Pairing energy</b>.  An energy which is a correction term that arises from the tendency of proton pairs and neutron pairs to occur. An even number of particles is more stable than an odd number.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell_models_and_other_quantum_models">Shell models and other quantum models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shell models and other quantum models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model" title="Nuclear shell model">Nuclear shell model</a></div>\n<p>A number of models for the nucleus have also been proposed in which nucleons occupy orbitals, much like the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">atomic orbitals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">atomic physics</a> theory. These wave models imagine nucleons to be either sizeless point particles in potential wells, or else probability waves as in the "optical model", frictionlessly orbiting at high speed in potential wells.\n</p><p>In the above models, the nucleons may occupy orbitals in pairs, due to being fermions, which allows explanation of <a href="/wiki/Even_and_odd_atomic_nuclei" title="Even and odd atomic nuclei">even/odd <i>Z</i> and <i>N</i> effects</a> well-known from experiments. The exact nature and capacity of nuclear shells differs from those of electrons in atomic orbitals, primarily because the potential well in which the nucleons move (especially in larger nuclei) is quite different from the central electromagnetic potential well which binds electrons in atoms. Some resemblance to atomic orbital models may be seen in a small atomic nucleus like that of <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a>, in which the two protons and two neutrons separately occupy 1s orbitals analogous to the 1s orbital for the two electrons in the helium atom, and achieve unusual stability for the same reason. Nuclei with 5 nucleons are all extremely unstable and short-lived, yet, <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">helium-3</a>, with 3 nucleons, is very stable even with lack of a closed 1s orbital shell. Another nucleus with 3 nucleons, the triton <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-3" title="Hydrogen-3">hydrogen-3</a> is unstable and will decay into helium-3 when isolated. Weak nuclear stability with 2 nucleons {NP} in the 1s orbital is found in the deuteron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-2" title="Hydrogen-2">hydrogen-2</a>, with only one nucleon in each of the proton and neutron potential wells. While each nucleon is a fermion, the {NP} deuteron is a boson and thus does not follow Pauli Exclusion for close packing within shells. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium-6" title="Lithium-6">Lithium-6</a> with 6 nucleons is highly stable without a closed second 1p shell orbital. For light nuclei with total nucleon numbers 1 to 6 only those with 5 do not show some evidence of stability. Observations of beta-stability of light nuclei outside closed shells indicate that nuclear stability is much more complex than simple closure of shell orbitals with <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(physics)" title="Magic number (physics)">magic numbers</a> of protons and neutrons.\n</p><p>For larger nuclei, the shells occupied by nucleons begin to differ significantly from electron shells, but nevertheless, present nuclear theory does predict the magic numbers of filled nuclear shells for both protons and neutrons. The closure of the stable shells predicts unusually stable configurations, analogous to the noble group of nearly-inert gases in chemistry. An example is the stability of the closed shell of 50 protons, which allows <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> to have 10 stable isotopes, more than any other element. Similarly, the distance from shell-closure explains the unusual instability of isotopes which have far from stable numbers of these particles, such as the radioactive elements 43 (<a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium">technetium</a>) and 61 (<a href="/wiki/Promethium" title="Promethium">promethium</a>), each of which is preceded and followed by 17 or more stable elements.\n</p><p>There are however problems with the shell model when an attempt is made to account for nuclear properties well away from closed shells. This has led to complex <i>post hoc</i> distortions of the shape of the potential well to fit experimental data, but the question remains whether these mathematical manipulations actually correspond to the spatial deformations in real nuclei. Problems with the shell model have led some to propose realistic two-body and three-body nuclear force effects involving nucleon clusters and then build the nucleus on this basis. Three such cluster models are the 1936 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Resonating_Group_Structure&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Resonating Group Structure (page does not exist)">Resonating Group Structure</a> model of John Wheeler, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Close-Packed_Spheron_Model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Close-Packed Spheron Model (page does not exist)">Close-Packed Spheron Model</a> of Linus Pauling and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=2D_Ising_Model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="2D Ising Model (page does not exist)">2D Ising Model</a> of MacGregor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-19">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consistency_between_models">Consistency between models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Consistency between models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_structure" title="Nuclear structure">Nuclear structure</a></div>\n<p>As with the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfluid" title="Superfluid">superfluid</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a>, atomic nuclei are an example of a state in which both (1) "ordinary" particle physical rules for volume and (2) non-intuitive quantum mechanical rules for a wave-like nature apply. In superfluid helium, the helium atoms have volume, and essentially "touch" each other, yet at the same time exhibit strange bulk properties, consistent with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensation" title="Bose&#8211;Einstein condensation">Bose&#8211;Einstein condensation</a>. The nucleons in atomic nuclei also exhibit a wave-like nature and lack standard fluid properties, such as friction. For nuclei made of <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a> which are <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>, Bose-Einstein condensation does not occur, yet nevertheless, many nuclear properties can only be explained similarly by a combination of properties of particles with volume, in addition to the frictionless motion characteristic of the wave-like behavior of objects trapped in <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger" title="Erwin Schr&#246;dinger">Erwin Schr&#246;dinger</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital#Formal_quantum_mechanical_definition" title="Atomic orbital">quantum orbitals</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atomic_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
