flux</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Radiation pressure</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> exerted upon any surface due to the exchange of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> between the object and the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic field</a>. This includes the momentum of light or <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> of any <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> which is <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorbed</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected</a>, or otherwise emitted (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_body_radiation" title="Black body radiation">black body radiation</a>) by matter on any scale (from macroscopic objects to dust particles to gas molecules).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The forces generated by radiation pressure are generally too small to be noticed under everyday circumstances; however, they are important in some physical processes. This particularly includes objects in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> where it is usually the main force acting on objects besides gravity, and where the net effect of a tiny force may have a large cumulative effect over long periods of time. For example, had the effects of the sun\'s radiation pressure on the spacecraft of the <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking program</a> been ignored, the spacecraft would have missed Mars orbit by about 15,000&#160;km (9,300&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Radiation pressure from starlight is crucial in a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrophysical" title="Astrophysical">astrophysical</a> processes as well. The significance of radiation pressure increases rapidly at extremely high temperatures, and can sometimes dwarf the usual <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">gas pressure</a>, for instance in <a href="/wiki/Stellar_structure" title="Stellar structure">stellar interiors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapons" title="Thermonuclear weapons">thermonuclear weapons</a>.\n</p><p>Radiation pressure can equally well be accounted for by considering the momentum of a classical electromagnetic field or in terms of the momenta of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a>, particles of light. The interaction of electromagnetic waves or photons with matter may involve an exchange of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>. Due to the law of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">conservation of momentum</a>, any change in the total momentum of the waves or photons must involve an equal and opposite change in the momentum of the matter it interacted with (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_third_law_of_motion" title="Newton\'s third law of motion">Newton\'s third law of motion</a>), as is illustrated in the accompanying figure for the case of light being perfectly reflected by a surface. This transfer of momentum is the general explanation for what we term radiation pressure.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> put forward the concept of radiation pressure back in 1619 to explain the observation that a tail of a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> always points away from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The assertion that light, as <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, has the property of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> and thus exerts a <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> upon any surface it is exposed to was published by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> in 1862, and proven experimentally by Russian physicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyotr_Nikolaevich_Lebedev" title="Pyotr Nikolaevich Lebedev">Pyotr Lebedev</a> in 1900<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Fox_Nichols" title="Ernest Fox Nichols">Ernest Fox Nichols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Ferrie_Hull" title="Gordon Ferrie Hull">Gordon Ferrie Hull</a> in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The pressure is very feeble, but can be detected by allowing the radiation to fall upon a delicately poised vane of reflective metal in a <a href="/wiki/Nichols_radiometer" title="Nichols radiometer">Nichols radiometer</a> (this should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Crookes_radiometer" title="Crookes radiometer">Crookes radiometer</a>, whose characteristic motion is <i>not</i> caused by radiation pressure but by impacting gas molecules).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote title="Speed of light">Speed of light</a></div>\n<p>Radiation pressure can be viewed as a consequence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">conservation of momentum</a> given the momentum attributed to electromagnetic radiation. That momentum can be equally well calculated on the basis of electromagnetic theory or from the combined momenta of a stream of photons, giving identical results as is shown below.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_pressure_from_momentum_of_an_electromagnetic_wave">Radiation pressure from momentum of an electromagnetic wave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiation pressure from momentum of an electromagnetic wave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Poynting_vector" title="Poynting vector">Poynting vector</a></div>\n<p>According to Maxwell\'s theory of electromagnetism, an electromagnetic wave carries momentum, which will be transferred to an opaque surface it strikes.\n</p><p>The energy flux (irradiance) of a plane wave is calculated using the <a href="/wiki/Poynting_vector" title="Poynting vector">Poynting vector</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\mathbf {S} =\\mathbf {E} \\times \\mathbf {H} }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi mathvariant="bold">S</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi mathvariant="bold">E</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mo>&#215;<!-- &#215; All spacecraft experience such a pressure except when they are behind the shadow of a larger <a href="/wiki/Orbiting_body" title="Orbiting body"> orbiting body</a>.\n</p><p>Solar radiation pressure on objects near the earth may be calculated using the sun\'s <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> at 1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a> or <i>G<sub>SC</sub></i>, whose value is set at <b>1361&#160;<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a>/<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a><sup>2</sup></b> as of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>All stars have a <a href="/wiki/Spectral_energy_distribution" title="Spectral energy distribution">spectral energy distribution</a> that depends on their surface temperature.  The distribution is approximately that of <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>. It significantly affects the orbits and trajectories of small bodies including all spacecraft.\n</p><p>Solar radiation pressure affects bodies throughout much of the Solar System.  Small bodies are more affected than large because of their lower mass relative to their surface area.  Spacecraft are affected along with natural bodies (comets, asteroids, dust grains, gas molecules).\n</p><p>The radiation pressure results in forces and torques on the bodies that can change their translational and rotational motions.  Translational changes affect the orbits of the bodies.  Rotational rates may increase or decrease.  Loosely aggregated bodies may break apart under high rotation rates.  Dust grains can either leave the Solar System or spiral into the Sun.\n</p><p>A whole body is typically composed of numerous surfaces that have different orientations on the body.  The facets may be flat or curved.  They will have different areas.  They may have optical properties differing from other aspects.\n</p><p>At any particular time, some facets will be exposed to the Sun and some will be in shadow.  Each surface exposed to the Sun will be reflecting, absorbing, and emitting radiation.  Facets in shadow will be emitting radiation.  The summation of pressures across all of the facets will define the net force and torque on the body.  These can be calculated using the equations in the preceding sections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wright_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMG_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-RMG-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect" title="Yarkovsky effect">Yarkovsky effect</a> affects the translation of a small body.  It results from a face leaving solar exposure being at a higher temperature than a face approaching solar exposure.  The radiation emitted from the warmer face will be more intense than that of the opposite face, resulting in a net force on the body that will affect its motion.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YORP_effect" title="YORP effect">YORP effect</a> is a collection of effects expanding upon the earlier concept of the Yarkovsky effect, but of a similar nature.  It affects the spin properties of bodies.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Poynting%E2%80%93Robertson_effect" title="Poynting&#8211;Robertson effect">Poynting&#8211;Robertson effect</a> applies to grain-size particles.  From the perspective of a grain of dust circling the Sun, the Sun\'s radiation appears to be coming from a slightly forward direction (<a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">aberration of light</a>). Therefore, the absorption of this radiation leads to a force with a component against the direction of movement. (The angle of aberration is tiny since the radiation is moving at the speed of light while the dust grain is moving many orders of magnitude slower than that.)  The result is a gradual spiral of dust grains into the Sun.  Over long periods of time, this effect cleans out much of the dust in the Solar System.\n</p><p>While rather small in comparison to other forces, the radiation pressure force is inexorable.  Over long periods of time, the net effect of the force is substantial.  Such feeble pressures can produce marked effects upon minute particles like <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, and are essential in the theory of electron emission from the Sun, of <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">cometary</a> material, and so on.\n</p><p>Because the ratio of surface area to volume (and thus mass) increases with decreasing particle size, dusty (<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a>-size) particles are susceptible to radiation pressure even in the outer solar system.  For example, the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Outer_rings" title="Rings of Saturn">outer rings of Saturn</a> is significantly influenced by radiation pressure.\n</p><p>As a consequence of light pressure, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in 1909 predicted the existence of "radiation friction" which would oppose the movement of matter. He wrote, &#8220;radiation will exert pressure on both sides of the plate. The forces of pressure exerted on the two sides are equal if the plate is at rest. However, if it is in motion, more radiation will be reflected on the surface that is ahead during the motion (front surface) than on the back surface. The backward acting force of pressure exerted on the front surface is thus larger than the force of pressure acting on the back. Hence, as the resultant of the two forces, there remains a force that counteracts the motion of the plate and that increases with the velocity of the plate. We will call this resultant \'radiation friction\' in brief.&#8221;\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_sails">Solar sails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solar sails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">Solar sail</a></div>\n<p>Solar sailing, an experimental method of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft propulsion</a>, uses radiation pressure from the Sun as a motive force. The idea of interplanetary travel by light was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a> in <i>From the Earth to the Moon</i>.\n</p><p>A sail reflects about 90% of the incident radiation.  The 10% that is absorbed is radiated away from both surfaces, with the proportion emitted from the unlit surface depending on the thermal conductivity of the sail.  A sail has curvature, surface irregularities, and other minor factors that affect its performance.\n</p><p>The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (<a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a>) has successfully unfurled a solar sail in space which has already succeeded in propelling its payload with the <a href="/wiki/IKAROS" title="IKAROS">IKAROS</a> project.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_effects_of_radiation_pressure">Cosmic effects of radiation pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cosmic effects of radiation pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Radiation pressure has had a major effect on the development of the cosmos, from the birth of the universe to ongoing formation of stars and shaping of clouds of dust and gasses on a wide range of scales.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_early_universe">The early universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The early universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Photon_epoch" title="Photon epoch">photon epoch</a> is a phase when the energy of the universe was dominated by photons, between 10 seconds and 380,000 years after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxy_formation_and_evolution">Galaxy formation and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Galaxy formation and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The process of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and Observations of the early universe strongly suggest that objects grew from bottom-up (i.e., smaller objects merging to form larger ones). As stars are thereby formed and become sources of electromagnetic radiation, radiation pressure from the stars becomes a factor in the dynamics of remaining <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_disc" title="Circumstellar disc">circumstellar</a> material.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clouds_of_dust_and_gases">Clouds of dust and gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Clouds of dust and gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4276" data-file-width="4100" decoding="async" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg/220px-Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg/330px-Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg/440px-Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pillars_2014_HST_denoise_0.6_12.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Pillars of Creation</i> clouds within the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Nebula" title="Eagle Nebula">Eagle Nebula</a> shaped by radiation pressure and stellar winds.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_compression" title="Gravitational compression">gravitational compression</a> of clouds of dust and gases is strongly influenced by radiation pressure, especially when the condensations lead to star births.  The larger young stars forming within the compressed clouds emit intense levels of radiation that shift the clouds, causing either dispersion or condensations in nearby regions, which influences birth rates in those nearby regions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clusters_of_stars">Clusters of stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Clusters of stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Stars predominantly form in regions of large clouds of dust and gases, giving rise to <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a>.  Radiation pressure from the member stars eventually disperses the clouds, which can have a profound effect on the evolution of the cluster.\n</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">open clusters</a> are inherently unstable, with a small enough mass that the <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a> of the system is lower than the average <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the constituent stars. These clusters will rapidly disperse within a few million years. In many cases, the stripping away of the gas from which the cluster formed by the radiation pressure of the hot young stars reduces the cluster mass enough to allow rapid dispersal.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_formation">Star formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Star formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">Star formation</a> is the process by which dense regions within <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular clouds</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">interstellar space</a> collapse to form <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. As a branch of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, star formation includes the study of the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_molecular_cloud" title="Giant molecular cloud">giant molecular clouds</a> (GMC) as precursors to the star formation process, and the study of <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Young_stellar_object" title="Young stellar object">young stellar objects</a> as its immediate products.  Star formation theory, as well as accounting for the formation of a single star, must also account for the statistics of <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary stars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Initial_mass_function" title="Initial mass function">initial mass function</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_planetary_systems">Stellar planetary systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Stellar planetary systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2672" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg/220px-David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg/330px-David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg/440px-David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:David_A._Aguilar%27s_Red_Dwarf_Stars.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A protoplanetary disk with a cleared central region (artist\'s conception).</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">Planetary systems</a> are generally believed to form as part of the same process that results in <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a> forms by gravitational collapse of a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular cloud</a>, called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a>,  and then evolves into a planetary system by collisions and gravitational capture. Radiation pressure can clear a region in the immediate vicinity of the star. As the formation process continues, radiation pressure continues to play a role in affecting the distribution of matter.  In particular, dust and grains can spiral into the star or escape the stellar system under the action of radiation pressure.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_interiors">Stellar interiors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Stellar interiors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stellar</a> interiors the temperatures are very high. Stellar models predict a temperature of 15&#160;MK in the center of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, and at the cores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">supergiant</a> stars the temperature may exceed 1&#160;GK. As the radiation pressure scales as the fourth power of the temperature, it becomes important at these high temperatures. In the Sun, radiation pressure is still quite small when compared to the gas pressure. In the heaviest non-degenerate stars, radiation pressure is the dominant pressure component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comets">Comets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Comets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg/220px-Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg/330px-Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg/440px-Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comet_Hale-Bopp_1995O1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Comet_Hale%E2%80%93Bopp" title="Comet Hale&#8211;Bopp">Comet Hale&#8211;Bopp</a> (C/1995 O1). Radiation pressure and solar wind effects on the dust and gas tails are clearly seen.</div></div></div>\n<p>Solar radiation pressure strongly affects <a href="/wiki/Comet_tail" title="Comet tail">comet tails</a>.  Solar heating causes gases to be released from the <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">comet nucleus</a>, which also carry away dust grains.  Radiation pressure and <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> then drive the dust and gases away from the Sun\'s direction.  The gases form a generally straight tail, while slower moving dust particles create a broader, curving tail.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laser_applications_of_radiation_pressure">Laser applications of radiation pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Laser applications of radiation pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_tweezers">Optical tweezers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Optical tweezers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lasers can be used as a source of monochromatic light with wavelength <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\lambda }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>&#955;<!-- &#955; --></mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\lambda }</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\lambda " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.355ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>. Atoms traveling towards a laser light source perceive a <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">doppler effect</a> tuned to the absorption frequency of the target element. The radiation pressure on the atom slows movement in a particular direction until the Doppler effect moves out of the frequency range of the element, causing an overall cooling effect.\n</p><p>Large lasers operating in space have been suggested as a means of propelling sail craft in <a href="/wiki/Beam-powered_propulsion" title="Beam-powered propulsion">beam-powered propulsion</a>.\n</p><p>The reflection of a laser pulse from the surface of an elastic solid gives rise to various types of elastic waves that propagate inside the solid. The weakest waves are generally those that are generated by the radiation pressure acting during the reflection of the light. Recently, such light-pressure-induced elastic waves were observed inside an ultrahigh-reflectivity <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_mirror" title="Dielectric mirror">dielectric mirror</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These waves are the most basic fingerprint of a light-solid matter interaction on the macroscopic scale.<sup class="reference"
