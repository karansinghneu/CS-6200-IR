title="Voodoo (aircraft)"><i>Voodoo</i></a>, a modified P 51 Mustang, is the 2014 Reno Air Race Champion.</div></div></div>\n<p>An <b>aircraft</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machine</a> that is able to <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">fly</a> by gaining support from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>. It counters the force of gravity by using either <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">static lift</a> or by using the <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">dynamic lift</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or in a few cases the <a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">downward thrust</a> from <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a>.  Common examples of aircraft include <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Blimp" title="Blimp">blimps</a>), <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wingsoverkansas_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wingsoverkansas-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The human activity that surrounds aircraft is called <i><a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a></i>. The science of aviation, including designing and building aircraft, is called <i><a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>.</i> <a href="/wiki/Aircrew" title="Aircrew">Crewed</a> aircraft are flown by an onboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> may be remotely controlled or self-controlled by onboard computers. Aircraft may be classified by different criteria, such as lift type, <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft#Methods_of_propulsion" title="Powered aircraft">aircraft propulsion</a>, usage and others.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_aviation" title="Timeline of aviation">Timeline of aviation</a></div>\n<p>Flying model craft and stories of manned <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> go back many centuries, however the first manned ascent &#8211; and safe descent &#8211; in modern times took place by larger hot-air balloons developed in the 18th century. Each of the two World Wars led to great technical advances. Consequently, the history of aircraft can be divided into five eras:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Early_flying_machines" title="Early flying machines">Pioneers of flight</a>, from the earliest experiments to 1914.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aviation_in_World_War_I" title="Aviation in World War I">First World War</a>, 1914 to 1918.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aviation_between_the_World_Wars" title="Aviation between the World Wars">Aviation between the World Wars</a>, 1918 to 1939.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Air_warfare_of_World_War_II" title="Air warfare of World War II">Second World War</a>, 1939 to 1945.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">Postwar era</a>, also called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_age" title="Jet age">jet age</a>, 1945 to the present day.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_of_lift">Methods of lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods of lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Lighter_than_air_.E2.80.93_aerostats"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighter_than_air_&#8211;_aerostats">Lighter than air &#8211; aerostats<span id="Lighter_than_air"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lighter than air &#8211; aerostats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostat</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/220px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/330px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg/440px-Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colorado_Springs_Hot_Air_Balloon_Competition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hot air <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">balloons</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostats</a> use <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> to float in the air in much the same way that ships float on the water. They are characterized by one or more large gasbags or canopies, filled with a relatively low-density gas such as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air</a>, which is less dense than the surrounding air. When the weight of this is added to the weight of the aircraft structure, it adds up to the same weight as the air that the craft displaces.\n</p><p>Small hot-air balloons called <a href="/wiki/Sky_lantern" title="Sky lantern">sky lanterns</a> were first invented in ancient China prior to the 3rd century BC and used primarily in cultural celebrations, and were only the second type of aircraft to fly, the first being <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kites</a> which were first invented in ancient China over two thousand years ago (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_China#Han_Dynasty" title="History of science and technology in China">Han Dynasty</a>).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)_in_flight_over_Manhattan,_circa_1931-1933.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4241" data-file-width="5516" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/220px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/330px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg/440px-USS_Akron_%28ZRS-4%29_in_flight_over_Manhattan%2C_circa_1931-1933.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)_in_flight_over_Manhattan,_circa_1931-1933.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airship <a href="/wiki/USS_Akron_(ZRS-4)" title="USS Akron (ZRS-4)">USS Akron</a> over Manhattan in the 1930s</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">balloon</a> was originally any aerostat, while the term <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airship</a> was used for large, powered aircraft designs &#8211; usually fixed-wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1919 <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Handley_Page" title="Frederick Handley Page">Frederick Handley Page</a> was reported as referring to "ships of the air," with smaller passenger types as "Air yachts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 1930s, large intercontinental flying boats were also sometimes referred to as "ships of the air" or "flying-ships".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  &#8211; though none had yet been built. The advent of powered balloons, called dirigible balloons, and later of rigid hulls allowing a great increase in size, began to change the way these words were used. Huge powered aerostats, characterized by a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">rigid</a> outer framework and separate aerodynamic skin surrounding the gas bags, were produced, the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelins</a> being the largest and most famous. There were still no fixed-wing aircraft or non-rigid balloons large enough to be called airships, so "airship" came to be synonymous with these aircraft. Then several accidents, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster" title="Hindenburg disaster">Hindenburg disaster</a> in 1937, led to the demise of these airships. Nowadays a "balloon" is an unpowered aerostat and an "airship" is a powered one.\n</p><p>A powered, steerable aerostat is called a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirigible" title="Dirigible">dirigible</a></i>. Sometimes this term is applied only to non-rigid balloons, and sometimes <i>dirigible balloon</i> is regarded as the definition of an airship (which may then be rigid or non-rigid). Non-rigid dirigibles are characterized by a moderately aerodynamic gasbag with stabilizing fins at the back. These soon became known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-rigid_airship" title="Non-rigid airship">blimps</a></i>. During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, this shape was widely adopted for <a href="/wiki/Barrage_balloon" title="Barrage balloon">tethered balloons</a>; in windy weather, this both reduces the strain on the tether and stabilizes the balloon. The nickname <i>blimp</i> was adopted along with the shape. In modern times, any small dirigible or airship is called a blimp, though a blimp may be unpowered as well as powered.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Heavier-than-air_.E2.80.93_aerodynes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavier-than-air_&#8211;_aerodynes">Heavier-than-air &#8211; aerodynes<span id="Heavier_than_air"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heavier-than-air &#8211; aerodynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Heavier-than-air aircraft, such as <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, must find some way to push air or gas downwards, so that a reaction occurs (by Newton\'s laws of motion) to push the aircraft upwards. This dynamic movement through the air is the origin of the term <i>aerodyne</i>. There are two ways to produce dynamic upthrust: <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamic lift</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">powered lift</a> in the form of engine thrust.\n</p><p>Aerodynamic lift involving <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> is the most common, with <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> being kept in the air by the forward movement of wings, and <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> by spinning wing-shaped rotors sometimes called rotary wings. A wing is a flat, horizontal surface, usually shaped in cross-section as an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">aerofoil</a>. To fly, air must flow over the wing and generate <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>. A <i>flexible wing</i> is a wing made of fabric or thin sheet material, often stretched over a rigid frame. A <i><a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a></i> is tethered to the ground and relies on the speed of the wind over its wings, which may be flexible or rigid, fixed, or rotary.\n</p><p>With powered lift, the aircraft directs its engine thrust <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_direction" title="Vertical direction">vertically</a> downward. <a href="/wiki/V/STOL" title="V/STOL">V/STOL</a> aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harrier Jump Jet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-35_Lightning_II#F-35B" title="F-35 Lightning II">F-35B</a> take off and land vertically using powered lift and transfer to aerodynamic lift in steady flight.\n</p><p>A pure <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> is not usually regarded as an aerodyne, because it does not depend on the air for its lift (and can even fly into space); however, many aerodynamic lift vehicles have been powered or assisted by rocket motors. Rocket-powered missiles that obtain aerodynamic lift at very high speed due to airflow over their bodies are a marginal case.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed-wing">Fixed-wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fixed-wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="4096" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/220px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/330px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg/440px-Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emirates_Airbus_A380-861_A6-EER_MUC_2015_04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>, the world\'s largest passenger <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a></div>\n<p>The forerunner of the fixed-wing aircraft is the <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kite</a>. Whereas a fixed-wing aircraft relies on its forward speed to create airflow over the wings, a kite is tethered to the ground and relies on the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> blowing over its wings to provide lift. Kites were the first kind of aircraft to fly, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_science_and_technology_in_China" title="History of science and technology in China">were invented in China</a> around 500 BC. Much aerodynamic research was done with kites before test aircraft, wind tunnels, and computer modelling programs became available.\n</p><p>The first heavier-than-air craft capable of controlled free-flight were <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>. A glider designed by <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> carried out the first true manned, controlled flight in 1853.\n</p><p>Practical, powered, fixed-wing aircraft (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeroplane" title="Aeroplane">aeroplane</a> or airplane) were invented by <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wilbur and Orville Wright</a>. Besides the method of <a href="#Propulsion">propulsion</a>, fixed-wing aircraft are in general characterized by their <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration" title="Wing configuration">wing configuration</a>. The most important wing characteristics are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Number of wings &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">Monoplane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a>, etc.</li>\n<li>Wing support &#8211; Braced or cantilever, rigid, or flexible.</li>\n<li>Wing planform &#8211; including <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio" title="Aspect ratio">aspect ratio</a>, angle of <a href="/wiki/Swept_wing" title="Swept wing">sweep</a>, and any variations along the span (including the important class of <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wings</a>).</li>\n<li>Location of the horizontal stabilizer, if any.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">Dihedral angle</a> &#8211; positive, zero, or negative (anhedral).</li></ul>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Wing_configuration#Variable_geometry" title="Wing configuration">variable geometry</a> aircraft can change its wing configuration during flight.\n</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Flying_wing" title="Flying wing">flying wing</a></i> has no fuselage, though it may have small blisters or pods. The opposite of this is a <i><a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a></i>, which has no wings, though it may have small stabilizing and control surfaces.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing-In-Ground_effect_vehicle" title="Wing-In-Ground effect vehicle">Wing-in-ground-effect</a> vehicles are not considered aircraft. They "fly" efficiently close to the surface of the ground or water, like conventional aircraft during takeoff. An example is the Russian ekranoplan (nicknamed the "Caspian Sea Monster"). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-powered_flight" title="Human-powered flight">Man-powered aircraft</a> also rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_effect_in_aircraft" title="Ground effect in aircraft">ground effect</a> to remain airborne with a minimal pilot power, but this is only because they are so underpowered&#8212;in fact, the airframe is capable of flying higher.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rotorcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2015" data-file-width="3034" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/220px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/330px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg/440px-20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20100627_Xenon2_Krakow_1328.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">autogyro</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</a></div>\n<p>Rotorcraft, or rotary-wing aircraft, use a spinning rotor with aerofoil section blades (a <i>rotary wing</i>) to provide lift. Types include <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">autogyros</a>, and various hybrids such as <a href="/wiki/Gyrodyne" title="Gyrodyne">gyrodynes</a> and compound rotorcraft.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">Helicopters</a></i> have a rotor turned by an engine-driven shaft. The rotor pushes air downward to create lift. By tilting the rotor forward, the downward flow is tilted backward, producing thrust for forward flight. Some helicopters have more than one rotor and a few have rotors turned by gas jets at the tips.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Autogyro" title="Autogyro">Autogyros</a></i> have unpowered rotors, with a separate power plant to provide thrust. The rotor is tilted backward. As the autogyro moves forward, air blows upward across the rotor, making it spin. This spinning increases the speed of airflow over the rotor, to provide lift. <a href="/wiki/Rotor_kite" title="Rotor kite">Rotor kites</a> are unpowered autogyros, which are towed to give them forward speed or tethered to a static anchor in high-wind for kited flight.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Cyclogyro" title="Cyclogyro">Cyclogyros</a></i> rotate their wings about a horizontal axis.\n</p><p><i>Compound rotorcraft</i> have wings that provide some or all of the lift in forward flight. They are nowadays classified as <i><a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">powered lift</a></i> types and not as rotorcraft. <i><a href="/wiki/Tiltrotor" title="Tiltrotor">Tiltrotor</a></i> aircraft (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-22_Osprey" title="V-22 Osprey">V-22 Osprey</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tiltwing" title="Tiltwing">tiltwing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tailsitter" title="Tailsitter">tailsitter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coleopter" title="Coleopter">coleopter</a> aircraft have their rotors/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propellers</a> horizontal for vertical flight and vertical for forward flight.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods_of_lift">Other methods of lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other methods of lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2589" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/220px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/330px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg/440px-X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X-24B_on_Lakebed_-_GPN-2000-000209.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>X-24B lifting body.</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>A <i><a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a></i> is an aircraft body shaped to produce lift. If there are any wings, they are too small to provide significant lift and are used only for stability and control. Lifting bodies are not efficient: they suffer from high drag, and must also travel at high speed to generate enough lift to fly. Many of the research prototypes, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin-Marietta_X-24" title="Martin-Marietta X-24">Martin-Marietta X-24</a>, which led up to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, were lifting bodies (though the shuttle itself is not), and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a> obtain lift from the airflow over a tubular body.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Powered_lift" title="Powered lift">Powered lift</a></i> types rely on engine-derived lift for vertical takeoff and landing (<a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a>). Most types transition to fixed-wing lift for horizontal flight. Classes of powered lift types include <a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a> jet aircraft (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harrier_jump-jet" title="Harrier jump-jet">Harrier jump-jet</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiltrotors" title="Tiltrotors">tiltrotors</a> (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-22_Osprey" title="V-22 Osprey">V-22 Osprey</a>), among others. A few experimental designs rely entirely on engine thrust to provide lift throughout the whole flight, including personal fan-lift hover platforms and jetpacks.  <a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a> research designs include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_Bedstead" title="Flying Bedstead">flying Bedstead</a>.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Flettner_airplane" title="Flettner airplane">Flettner airplane</a></i> uses a rotating cylinder in place of a fixed wing, obtaining lift from the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">magnus effect</a>.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a></i> obtains thrust by flapping its wings.</li></ul>\n<h2><span id="Scale.2C_sizes_and_speeds"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Scale,_sizes_and_speeds">Scale, sizes and speeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scale, sizes and speeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sizes">Sizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b>smallest aircraft</b> are toys, and&#8212;even smaller &#8211; class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unpowered_aircraft">Unpowered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unpowered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unpowered_aircraft" title="Unpowered aircraft">Unpowered aircraft</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">Gliders</a> are heavier-than-air aircraft that do not employ propulsion once airborne. Take-off may be by launching forward and downward from a high location, or by pulling into the air on a tow-line, either by a ground-based winch or vehicle, or by a powered "tug" aircraft. For a glider to maintain its forward air speed and lift, it must descend in relation to the air (but not necessarily in relation to the ground). Many gliders can \'soar\' &#8211; gain height from updrafts such as thermal currents. The first practical, controllable example was designed and built by the British scientist and pioneer <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a>, whom many recognise as the first aeronautical engineer. Common examples of gliders are <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">sailplanes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hang_glider" title="Hang glider">hang gliders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraglider" title="Paraglider">paragliders</a>.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">Balloons</a> drift with the wind, though normally the pilot can control the altitude, either by heating the air or by releasing ballast, giving some directional control (since the wind direction changes with altitude). A wing-shaped hybrid balloon can glide directionally when rising or falling; but a spherically shaped balloon does not have such directional control.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">Kites</a> are aircraft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> that are tethered to the ground or other object (fixed or mobile) that maintains tension in the tether or <a href="/wiki/Kite_line" title="Kite line">kite line</a>; they rely on virtual or real wind blowing over and under them to generate lift and drag. <a href="/wiki/Kytoon" title="Kytoon">Kytoons</a> are balloon-kite hybrids that are shaped and tethered to obtain kiting deflections, and can be lighter-than-air, neutrally buoyant, or heavier-than-air.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powered_aircraft">Powered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Powered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">Powered aircraft</a></div>\n<p>Powered aircraft have one or more onboard sources of mechanical power, typically <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a> although rubber and manpower have also been used. Most aircraft engines are either lightweight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piston_engine" title="Piston engine">piston engines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>. Engine fuel is stored in tanks, usually in the wings but larger aircraft also have additional <a href="/wiki/Fuel_tank" title="Fuel tank">fuel tanks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Propeller_aircraft">Propeller aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Propeller aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_aircraft" title="Propeller aircraft">Propeller aircraft</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="768" decoding="async" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg/330px-WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg/440px-WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WestCoastAirFloatplane.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a>-engined <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_DHC-6_Twin_Otter" title="De Havilland Canada DHC-6 Twin Otter">DeHavilland Twin Otter</a> adapted as a <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">floatplane</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_aircraft" title="Propeller aircraft">Propeller aircraft</a> use one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propellers</a> (airscrews) to create thrust in a forward direction. The propeller is usually mounted in front of the power source in <i><a href="/wiki/Tractor_configuration" title="Tractor configuration">tractor configuration</a></i> but can be mounted behind in <i><a href="/wiki/Pusher_configuration" title="Pusher configuration">pusher configuration</a></i>. Variations of propeller layout include <i><a href="/wiki/Contra-rotating_propellers" title="Contra-rotating propellers">contra-rotating propellers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ducted_fan" title="Ducted fan">ducted fans</a></i>.\n</p><p>Many kinds of power plant have been used to drive propellers. Early airships used man power or <a href="/wiki/Steam_aircraft" title="Steam aircraft">steam engines</a>. The more practical <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion piston engine</a> was used for virtually all fixed-wing aircraft until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and is still used in many smaller aircraft. Some types use turbine engines to drive a propeller in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a> or <a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">propfan</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-powered_flight" title="Human-powered flight">Human-powered flight</a> has been achieved, but has not become a practical means of transport. Unmanned aircraft and models have also used power sources such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_aircraft" title="Electric aircraft">electric motors</a> and rubber bands.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_aircraft">Jet aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jet aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">Jet aircraft</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1822" data-file-width="2736" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/220px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/330px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg/440px-Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_F-22A_Raptor_JSOH.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">Lockheed Martin F-22A Raptor</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">Jet aircraft</a> use <a href="/wiki/Airbreathing_jet_engine" title="Airbreathing jet engine">airbreathing jet engines</a>, which take in air, burn fuel with it in a <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a>, and accelerate the exhaust rearwards to provide thrust.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">Turbojet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines use a spinning turbine to drive one or more fans, which provide additional thrust. An <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a> may be used to inject extra fuel into the hot exhaust, especially on military "fast jets". Use of a turbine is not absolutely necessary: other designs include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulse_jet_engine" title="Pulse jet engine">pulse jet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a>. These mechanically simple designs cannot work when stationary, so the aircraft must be launched to flying speed by some other method. Other variants have also been used, including the <a href="/wiki/Motorjet" title="Motorjet">motorjet</a> and hybrids such as the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J58" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J58">Pratt &amp; Whitney J58</a>, which can convert between turbojet and ramjet operation.\n</p><p>Compared to propellers, jet engines can provide much higher thrust, higher speeds and, above about 40,000&#160;ft (12,000&#160;m), greater efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hist8_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-hist8-42">[42]</a></sup> They are also much more fuel-efficient than <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>. As a consequence nearly all large, high-speed or high-altitude aircraft use jet engines.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotorcraft_2">Rotorcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rotorcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">Rotorcraft</a></div>\n<p>Some rotorcraft, such as <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, have a powered rotary wing or <i>rotor</i>, where the rotor disc can be angled slightly forward so that a proportion of its lift is directed forwards. The rotor may, like a propeller, be powered by a variety of methods such as a piston engine or turbine. Experiments have also used <a href="/wiki/Tip_jet" title="Tip jet">jet nozzles at the rotor blade tips</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_powered_aircraft">Other types of powered aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other types of powered aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft" title="Rocket-powered aircraft">Rocket-powered aircraft</a></i> have occasionally been experimented with, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Me_163" title="Messerschmitt Me 163">Messerschmitt <i>Komet</i></a> fighter even saw action in the Second World War. Since then, they have been restricted to research aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">North American X-15</a>, which traveled up into space where air-breathing engines cannot work (rockets carry their own oxidant). Rockets have more often been used as a supplement to the main power plant, typically for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket-assisted_take_off" title="Rocket-assisted take off">rocket-assisted take off</a> of heavily loaded aircraft, but also to provide high-speed dash capability in some hybrid designs such as the <a href="/wiki/Saunders-Roe_SR.53" title="Saunders-Roe SR.53">Saunders-Roe SR.53</a>.</li>\n<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Ornithopter" title="Ornithopter">ornithopter</a></i> obtains thrust by flapping its wings. It has found practical use in a <a href="/wiki/Ornithopter#Recent_developments" title="Ornithopter">model hawk</a> used to freeze prey animals into stillness so that they can be captured, and in toy birds.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_construction">Design and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Design and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Aircraft are <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_design_process" title="Aircraft design process">designed</a> according to many factors such as customer and manufacturer demand, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_safety" title="Air safety">safety</a> protocols and physical and economic constraints. For many types of aircraft the design process is regulated by national airworthiness authorities.\n</p><p>The key parts of an aircraft are generally divided into three categories:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <i>structure</i> comprises the main load-bearing elements and associated equipment.</li>\n<li>The <i>propulsion system</i> (if it is powered) comprises the power source and associated equipment, as described above.</li>\n<li>The <i>avionics</i> comprise the control, navigation and communication systems, usually electrical in nature.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The approach to structural design varies widely between different types of aircraft. Some, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraglider" title="Paraglider">paragliders</a>, comprise only flexible materials that act in tension and rely on aerodynamic pressure to hold their shape. A <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloon</a> similarly relies on internal gas pressure but may have a rigid basket or gondola slung below it to carry its payload. Early aircraft, including <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>, often employed flexible <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_dope" title="Aircraft dope">doped</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fabric_covering" title="Aircraft fabric covering">aircraft fabric covering</a> to give a reasonably smooth aeroshell stretched over a rigid frame. Later aircraft employed semi-<a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> techniques, where the skin of the aircraft is stiff enough to share much of the flight loads. In a true monocoque design there is no internal structure left.\n</p><p>The key structural parts of an aircraft depend on what type it is.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerostats">Aerostats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aerostats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostat</a></div>\n<p>Lighter-than-air types are characterised by one or more gasbags, typically with a supporting structure of flexible cables or a rigid framework called its hull. Other elements such as engines or a gondola may also be attached to the supporting structure.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynes">Aerodynes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Aerodynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="487" data-file-width="663" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/220px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/330px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg/440px-Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Merlin_Airframe_Material.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airframe diagram for an <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">AgustaWestland AW101</a> <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Heavier-than-air types are characterised by one or more wings and a central <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>. The fuselage typically also carries a tail or <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a> for stability and control, and an undercarriage for takeoff and landing. Engines may be located on the fuselage or wings. On a <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> the wings are rigidly attached to the fuselage, while on a <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> the wings are attached to a rotating vertical shaft. Smaller designs sometimes use flexible materials for part or all of the structure, held in place either by a rigid frame or by air pressure. The fixed parts of the structure comprise the <a href="/wiki/Airframe" title="Airframe">airframe</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avionics">Avionics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Avionics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">Avionics</a></div>\n<p>The avionics comprise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_control_system" title="Flight control system">flight control systems</a> and related equipment, including the <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> instrumentation, navigation, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a>, monitoring, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_system" title="Communication system">communication systems</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_characteristics">Flight characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Flight characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_envelope">Flight envelope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Flight envelope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flight_envelope" title="Flight envelope">Flight envelope</a></div>\n<p>The flight envelope of an aircraft refers to its capabilities in terms of <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Load_factor_(aeronautics)" title="Load factor (aeronautics)">load factor</a> or altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The term can also refer to other measurements such as maneuverability. When a craft is pushed, for instance by diving it at high speeds, it is said to be flown <i>outside the envelope</i>, something considered unsafe.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1196" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="146" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg/330px-Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg/440px-Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_777-200LR_banking_over_mountain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200LR" title="Boeing 777-200LR">Boeing 777-200LR</a> is the longest-range airliner, capable of flights of more than halfway around the world.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range (aeronautics)</a></div>\n<p>The range is the distance an aircraft can fly between <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a>, as limited by the time it can remain airborne.\n</p><p>For a powered aircraft the time limit is determined by the fuel load and rate of consumption.\n</p><p>For an unpowered aircraft, the maximum flight time is limited by factors such as weather conditions and pilot endurance. Many aircraft types are restricted to daylight hours, while balloons are limited by their supply of lifting gas. The range can be seen as the average ground speed multiplied by the maximum time in the air.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_dynamics">Flight dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Flight dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(aircraft)" title="Flight dynamics (aircraft)">Flight dynamics (aircraft)</a></div>\n<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flight_dynamics_with_text.png"><img alt="Flight dynamics with text.png" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/200px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/300px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/400px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div>\n<p><b>Flight dynamics</b> is the science of air vehicle orientation and control in three dimensions. The three critical flight dynamics parameters are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angles_of_rotation" title="Angles of rotation">angles of rotation</a> around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_axis" title="Aircraft axis">three axes</a> which pass through the vehicle\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_of_gravity" title="Center of gravity">center of gravity</a>, known as <i>pitch</i>, <i>roll</i>, and <i>yaw</i>.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Roll is a rotation about the longitudinal axis (equivalent to the rolling or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heeling_(sailing)" title="Heeling (sailing)">heeling</a> of a ship) giving an up-down movement of the wing tips measured by the roll or bank angle.</li>\n<li>Pitch is a rotation about the sideways horizontal axis giving an up-down movement of the aircraft nose measured by the <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a>.</li>\n<li>Yaw is a rotation about the vertical axis giving a side-to-side movement of the nose known as sideslip.</li></ul>\n<p>Flight dynamics is concerned with the stability and control of an aircraft\'s rotation about each of these axes.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aircraft_tail.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/220px-Aircraft_tail.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/330px-Aircraft_tail.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Aircraft_tail.JPG/440px-Aircraft_tail.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aircraft_tail.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tail_assembly" title="Tail assembly">tail assembly</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747&#8211;200</a></div></div></div>\n<p>An aircraft that is unstable tends to diverge from its current flight path and so is difficult to fly. A very stable aircraft tends to stay on its current flight path and is difficult to manoeuvre&#8212;so it is important for any design to achieve the desired degree of stability. Since the widespread use of digital computers, it is increasingly common for designs to be inherently unstable and rely on computerised control systems to provide artificial stability.\n</p><p>A fixed wing is typically unstable in pitch, roll, and yaw. Pitch and yaw stabilities of conventional fixed wing designs require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stabilizer_(aircraft)" title="Stabilizer (aircraft)">horizontal and vertical stabilisers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crane_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GroundUp_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-GroundUp-46">[46]</a></sup> which act similarly to the feathers on an arrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> These stabilizing surfaces allow equilibrium of aerodynamic forces and to stabilise the <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">flight dynamics</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitch_(flight)" title="Pitch (flight)">pitch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaw_angle" title="Yaw angle">yaw</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crane_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crane-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GroundUp_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-GroundUp-46">[46]</a></sup> They are usually mounted on the tail section (<a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a>), although in the <a href="/wiki/Canard_(aeronautics)" title="Canard (aeronautics)">canard</a> layout, the main aft wing replaces the canard foreplane as pitch stabilizer. <a href="/wiki/Tandem_wing" title="Tandem wing">Tandem wing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tailless_aircraft" title="Tailless aircraft">Tailless aircraft</a> rely on the same general rule to achieve stability, the aft surface being the stabilising one.\n</p><p>A rotary wing is typically unstable in yaw, requiring a vertical stabiliser.\n</p><p>A balloon is typically very stable in pitch and roll due to the way the payload is hung underneath.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces" title="Flight control surfaces">Flight control surfaces</a> enable the pilot to control an aircraft\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_attitude" title="Aircraft attitude">flight attitude</a> and are usually part of the wing or mounted on, or integral with, the associated stabilizing surface. Their development was a critical advance in the history of aircraft, which had until that point been uncontrollable in flight.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineer" title="Aerospace engineer">Aerospace engineers</a> develop <a href="/wiki/Control_system" title="Control system">control systems</a> for a vehicle\'s orientation (attitude) about its <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>. The control systems include actuators, which exert forces in various directions, and generate rotational forces or <a href="/wiki/Moment_(physics)" title="Moment (physics)">moments</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_center" title="Aerodynamic center">aerodynamic center</a> of the aircraft, and thus rotate the aircraft in pitch, roll, or yaw. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Pitching_moment" title="Pitching moment">pitching moment</a> is a vertical force applied at a distance forward or aft from the aerodynamic center of the aircraft, causing the aircraft to pitch up or down. Control systems are also sometimes used to increase or decrease drag, for example to slow the aircraft to a safe speed for landing.\n</p><p>The two main aerodynamic forces acting on any aircraft are lift supporting it in the air and <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> opposing its motion. Control surfaces or other techniques may also be used to affect these forces directly, without inducing any rotation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_of_aircraft_use">Impacts of aircraft use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Impacts of aircraft use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">Environmental impact of aviation</a></div>\n<p>Aircraft permit long distance, high speed <a href="/wiki/Air_travel" title="Air travel">travel</a> and may be a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency_in_transportation#Aircraft" title="Fuel efficiency in transportation">fuel efficient</a> mode of transportation in some circumstances. Aircraft have <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">environmental and climate impacts</a> beyond fuel efficiency considerations, however. They are also relatively <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_noise" title="Aircraft noise">noisy</a> compared to other forms of travel and high altitude aircraft generate <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a>, which experimental evidence suggests may <a href="/wiki/Global_dimming" title="Global dimming">alter weather patterns</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_for_aircraft">Uses for aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Uses for aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Aircraft are produced in several different types optimized for various uses; <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">military aircraft</a>, which includes not just combat types but many types of supporting aircraft, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aircraft</a>, which include all non-military types, experimental and model.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1181" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="144" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg/330px-Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg/440px-Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Color_Photographed_B-17E_in_Flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17E</a> in flight</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">Military aircraft</a></div>\n<p>A military aircraft is any aircraft that is operated by a legal or insurrectionary armed service of any type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunston_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunston-48">[48]</a></sup> Military aircraft can be either combat or non-combat:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Combat aircraft are aircraft designed to destroy enemy equipment using its own armament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunston_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-gunston-48">[48]</a></sup> Combat aircraft divide broadly into <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bombers</a>, with several in-between types such as <a href="/wiki/Fighter-bomber" title="Fighter-bomber">fighter-bombers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground-attack_aircraft" title="Ground-attack aircraft">ground-attack aircraft</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Attack_helicopter" title="Attack helicopter">attack helicopters</a>).</li>\n<li>Non-combat aircraft are not designed for combat as their primary function, but may carry weapons for self-defense. Non-combat roles include search and rescue, reconnaissance, observation, transport, training, and <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a>. These aircraft are often variants of civil aircraft.</li></ul>\n<p>Most military aircraft are powered heavier-than-air types. Other types such as gliders and <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloons</a> have also been used as military aircraft; for example, balloons were used for observation during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Military_glider" title="Military glider">military gliders</a> were used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to land troops.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil">Civil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Civil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2215" data-file-width="3322" decoding="async" height="147" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg/330px-Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg/440px-Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pilatus_Agusta_A109_Flug.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agusta_A109" title="Agusta A109">Agusta A109</a> helicopter of the <a href="/wiki/Rega_(air_rescue)" title="Rega (air rescue)">Swiss air rescue service</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">Civil aviation</a></div>\n<p>Civil aircraft divide into <i>commercial</i> and <i>general</i> types, however there are some overlaps.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Commercial_aviation" title="Commercial aviation">Commercial aircraft</a> include types designed for scheduled and charter airline flights, carrying passengers, <a href="/wiki/Airmail" title="Airmail">mail</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a>. The larger passenger-carrying types are the airliners, the largest of which are <a href="/wiki/Wide-body_aircraft" title="Wide-body aircraft">wide-body aircraft</a>. Some of the smaller types are also used in <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a>, and some of the larger types are used as <a href="/wiki/Air_transports_of_heads_of_state_and_government" title="Air transports of heads of state and government">VIP aircraft</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">General aviation</a> is a catch-all covering other kinds of <a href="/wiki/Private_aviation" title="Private aviation">private</a> (where the pilot is not paid for time or expenses) and commercial use, and involving a wide range of aircraft types such as <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jets (bizjets)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trainer_(aircraft)" title="Trainer (aircraft)">trainers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homebuilt_aircraft" title="Homebuilt aircraft">homebuilt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">gliders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warbird" title="Warbird">warbirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a> to name a few. The vast majority of aircraft today are general aviation types.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental">Experimental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Experimental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Experimental_aircraft" title="Experimental aircraft">Experimental aircraft</a></div>\n<p>An experimental aircraft is one that has not been fully proven in flight, or that carries an FAA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Airworthiness_Certificate" title="Special Airworthiness Certificate">Special Airworthiness Certificate</a> called an Experimental Certificate. Often, this implies that the aircraft is testing new aerospace technologies, though the term also refers to amateur and kit-built aircraft, many of which are based on proven designs.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1204" data-file-width="1864" decoding="async" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg/220px-MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg/330px-MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg/440px-MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MiniCeline_ultralight_aircraft.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A model aircraft, weighing six grams</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model">Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aircraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Model_aircraft" title="Model aircraft">Model aircraft</a></div>\n<p>A model aircraft is a small unmanned type made to fly for fun, for static display, for aerodynamic research or for other purposes. A <a href="/wiki/Scale_model" title="Scale model">scale model</a> is a replica of some larger design.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
