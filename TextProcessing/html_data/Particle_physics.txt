Although the word <i><a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particle</a></i> can refer to various types of very small objects (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a>, gas particles, or even household dust), <em>particle physics</em> usually investigates the irreducibly smallest detectable particles and the <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a> necessary to explain their behaviour. By our current understanding, these <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> are excitations of the <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)#Quantum_fields" title="Field (physics)">quantum fields</a> that also govern their interactions. The currently dominant theory explaining these fundamental particles and fields, along with their dynamics, is called the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>. Thus, modern particle physics generally investigates the Standard Model and its various possible extensions, e.g. to the newest "known" particle, the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>, or even to the oldest known force field, <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">Physics</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Modern&#160;particle&#160;physics&#160;research is focused on <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">subatomic particles</a>, including atomic constituents such as <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> (protons and neutrons are composite particles called <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryons</a>, made of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>), produced by <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a> processes, such as <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muon" title="Muon">muons</a>, as well as a wide range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exotic_particle" title="Exotic particle">exotic particles</a>.\nDynamics of particles is also governed by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>; they exhibit <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave&#8211;particle duality">wave&#8211;particle duality</a>, displaying particle-like behaviour under certain experimental conditions and <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a>-like behaviour in others. In more technical terms, they are described by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_state" title="Quantum state">quantum state</a> vectors in a <a href="/wiki/Hilbert_space" title="Hilbert space">Hilbert space</a>, which is also treated in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>. Following the convention of particle physicists, the term <i><a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a></i> is applied to those particles that are, according to current understanding, presumed to be indivisible and not composed of other particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-braibant_3-0"><a align="center"><b>61</b>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>All particles and their interactions observed to date can be described almost entirely by a quantum field theory called the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifj_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifj-4">[4]</a></sup> The Standard Model, as currently formulated, has 61 elementary particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-braibant_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-braibant-3">[3]</a></sup>\nThose elementary particles can combine to form composite particles, accounting for the hundreds of other species of particles that have been discovered since the 1960s.\n</p><p>The Standard Model has been found to agree with almost all the <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimental</a> tests conducted to date. However, most particle physicists believe that it is an incomplete description of nature and that a more fundamental theory awaits discovery (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Everything" title="Theory of Everything">Theory of Everything</a>). In recent years, measurements of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">mass</a> have provided the first experimental deviations from the Standard Model.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_subatomic_physics" title="History of subatomic physics">History of subatomic physics</a></div>\n<p>The idea that all <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> is composed of <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> dates from at least the 6th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a>, through his work on <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a>, concluded that each element of nature was composed of a single, unique type of particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The word <i><a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a></i>, after the Greek word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CF%84%CE%BF%CE%BC%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:&#7940;&#964;&#959;&#956;&#959;&#962;">atomos</a></i> meaning "indivisible", has since then denoted the smallest particle of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a>, but physicists soon discovered that atoms are not, in fact, the fundamental particles of nature, but are conglomerates of even smaller particles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. The early 20th century explorations of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a> led to proofs of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> in 1939 by <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a> (based on experiments by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a> in that same year; both discoveries also led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>. Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, a bewildering variety of particles were found in collisions of particles from increasingly high-energy beams. It was referred to informally as the "<a href="/wiki/Particle_zoo" title="Particle zoo">particle zoo</a>". That term was deprecated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> after the formulation of the Standard Model during the 1970s, in which the large number of particles was explained as combinations of a (relatively) small number of more fundamental particles.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_Model">Standard Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standard Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a></div>\n<p>The current state of the classification of all elementary particles is explained by the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>. It describes the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_interaction" title="Fundamental interaction">fundamental interactions</a>, using mediating <a href="/wiki/Gauge_boson" title="Gauge boson">gauge bosons</a>. The species of gauge bosons are eight <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>, <a href="/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons" title="W and Z bosons"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>W<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> and <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>Z<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> bosons</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifj_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ifj-4">[4]</a></sup> The Standard Model also contains 24&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_particle" title="Fundamental particle">fundamental</a> <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a> (12&#160;particles and their associated anti-particles), which are the constituents of all <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdg_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdg-7">[7]</a></sup> Finally, the Standard Model also predicted the existence of a type of <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">boson</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>. Early in the morning on 4 July 2012, physicists with the Large Hadron Collider at CERN announced they had found a new particle that behaves similarly to what is expected from the Higgs boson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_laboratories">Experimental laboratories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Experimental laboratories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The world\'s major particle physics laboratories are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its main facility is the <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_Heavy_Ion_Collider" title="Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider">Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider</a> (RHIC), which collides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_nuclear_collisions" title="Relativistic nuclear collisions">heavy ions</a> such as gold ions and polarized protons. It is the world\'s first heavy ion collider, and the world\'s only polarized proton collider.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Budker_Institute_of_Nuclear_Physics" title="Budker Institute of Nuclear Physics">Budker Institute of Nuclear Physics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">Novosibirsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>). Its main projects are now the electron-positron <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">colliders</a> <a href="/wiki/VEPP-2000" title="VEPP-2000">VEPP-2000</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> operated since 2006, and VEPP-4,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> started experiments in 1994. Earlier facilities include the first electron-electron beam-beam <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">collider</a> VEP-1, which conducted experiments from 1964 to 1968; the electron-positron <a href="/wiki/Collider" title="Collider">colliders</a> VEPP-2, operated from 1965 to 1974; and, its successor VEPP-2M,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> performed experiments from 1974 to 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> (European Organization for Nuclear Research) (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">Franco</a>-<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> border, near <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>). Its main project is now the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC), which had its first beam circulation on 10 September 2008, and is now the world\'s most energetic collider of protons. It also became the most energetic collider of heavy ions after it began colliding lead ions. Earlier facilities include the <a href="/wiki/Large_Electron%E2%80%93Positron_Collider" title="Large Electron&#8211;Positron Collider">Large Electron&#8211;Positron Collider</a> (LEP), which was stopped on 2 November 2000 and then dismantled to give way for LHC; and the <a href="/wiki/Super_Proton_Synchrotron" title="Super Proton Synchrotron">Super Proton Synchrotron</a>, which is being reused as a pre-accelerator for the LHC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/DESY" title="DESY">DESY</a> (Deutsches Elektronen-Synchrotron) (<a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>). Its main facility is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadron_Elektron_Ring_Anlage" title="Hadron Elektron Ring Anlage">Hadron Elektron Ring Anlage</a> (HERA), which collides electrons and positrons with protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab)</a> (<a href="/wiki/Batavia,_Illinois" title="Batavia, Illinois">Batavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its main facility until 2011 was the <a href="/wiki/Tevatron" title="Tevatron">Tevatron</a>, which collided protons and antiprotons and was the highest-energy particle collider on earth until the Large Hadron Collider surpassed it on 29 November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Institute_of_High_Energy_Physics" title="Institute of High Energy Physics">Institute of High Energy Physics</a> (IHEP) (<a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>). IHEP manages a number of China\'s major particle physics facilities, including the Beijing Electron Positron Collider (BEPC), the Beijing Spectrometer (BES), the Beijing Synchrotron Radiation Facility (BSRF), the International Cosmic-Ray Observatory at Yangbajing in Tibet, the Daya Bay Reactor Neutrino Experiment, the China Spallation Neutron Source, the Hard X-ray Modulation Telescope (HXMT), and the Accelerator-driven Sub-critical System (ADS) as well as the Jiangmen Underground Neutrino Observatory (JUNO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/KEK" title="KEK">KEK</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tsukuba,_Ibaraki" title="Tsukuba, Ibaraki">Tsukuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>). It is the home of a number of experiments such as the <a href="/wiki/K2K_experiment" title="K2K experiment">K2K experiment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation" title="Neutrino oscillation">neutrino oscillation</a> experiment and <a href="/wiki/Belle_experiment" title="Belle experiment">Belle</a>, an experiment measuring the <a href="/wiki/CP_violation" title="CP violation">CP violation</a> of <a href="/wiki/B_meson" title="B meson">B mesons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory" title="SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory">SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California" title="Menlo Park, California">Menlo Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). Its 2-mile-long linear particle accelerator began operating in 1962 and was the basis for numerous <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a> collision experiments until 2008. Since then the linear accelerator is being used for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linac_Coherent_Light_Source" title="Linac Coherent Light Source">Linac Coherent Light Source</a> <a href="/wiki/X-ray_laser" title="X-ray laser">X-ray laser</a> as well as advanced accelerator design research. SLAC staff continue to participate in developing and building many <a href="/wiki/Particle_detector" title="Particle detector">particle detectors</a> around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Many other <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a> also exist.\n</p><p>The techniques required for modern experimental particle physics are quite varied and complex, constituting a sub-specialty nearly completely distinct<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. See also <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a>. There are several major interrelated efforts being made in theoretical particle physics today.\nOne important branch attempts to better understand the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> and its tests. By extracting the parameters of the Standard Model, from experiments with less uncertainty, this work probes the limits of the Standard Model and therefore expands our understanding of nature\'s building blocks. Those efforts are made challenging by the difficulty of calculating quantities in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics" title="Quantum chromodynamics">quantum chromodynamics</a>. Some theorists working in this area refer to themselves as <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_physics_phenomenology" title="Particle physics phenomenology">phenomenologists</a></b> and they may use the tools of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effective_field_theory" title="Effective field theory">effective field theory</a>. Others make use of <a href="/wiki/Lattice_field_theory" title="Lattice field theory">lattice field theory</a> and call themselves <i>lattice theorists</i>.\n</p><p>Another major effort is in model building where <a href="/wiki/Model_building_(particle_physics)" title="Model building (particle physics)">model builders</a> develop ideas for what physics may lie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Beyond the Standard Model">beyond the Standard Model</a> (at higher energies or smaller distances). This work is often motivated by the <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy_problem" title="Hierarchy problem">hierarchy problem</a> and is constrained by existing experimental data. It may involve work on <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, alternatives to the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_mechanism" title="Higgs mechanism">Higgs mechanism</a>, extra spatial dimensions (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randall-Sundrum" title="Randall-Sundrum">Randall-Sundrum</a> models), <a href="/wiki/Preon" title="Preon">Preon</a> theory, combinations of these, or other ideas.\n</p><p>A third major effort in theoretical particle physics is <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a>. <i>String theorists</i> attempt to construct a unified description of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> by building a theory based on small strings, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branes" title="Branes">branes</a> rather than particles. If the theory is successful, it may be considered a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Everything" title="Theory of Everything">Theory of Everything</a>", or "TOE".\n</p><p>There are also other areas of work in theoretical particle physics ranging from <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics_in_cosmology" title="Particle physics in cosmology">particle cosmology</a> to <a href="/wiki/Loop_quantum_gravity" title="Loop quantum gravity">loop quantum gravity</a>.\n</p><p>This division of efforts in particle physics is reflected in the names of categories on the <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Preprint" title="Preprint">preprint</a> archive:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> hep-th (theory), hep-ph (phenomenology), hep-ex (experiments), hep-lat (<a href="/wiki/Lattice_gauge_theory" title="Lattice gauge theory">lattice gauge theory</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_applications">Practical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Practical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In principle, all physics (and practical applications developed therefrom) can be derived from the study of fundamental particles. In practice, even if "particle physics" is taken to mean only "high-energy atom smashers", many technologies have been developed during these pioneering investigations that later find wide uses in society. Particle accelerators are used to produce <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_in_medicine" title="Isotopes in medicine">medical isotopes</a> for research and treatment (for example, isotopes used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_imaging" title="PET imaging">PET imaging</a>), or used directly in <a href="/wiki/External_beam_radiotherapy" title="External beam radiotherapy">external beam radiotherapy</a>. The development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconductor" title="Superconductor">superconductors</a> has been pushed forward by their use in particle physics. The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and <a href="/wiki/Touchscreen" title="Touchscreen">touchscreen</a> technology were initially developed at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>. Additional applications are found in medicine, national security, industry, computing, science, and workforce development, illustrating a long and growing list of beneficial practical applications with contributions from particle physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The primary goal, which is pursued in several distinct ways, is to find and understand what physics may lie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beyond_the_standard_model" title="Beyond the standard model">beyond the standard model</a>. There are several powerful experimental reasons to expect new physics, including <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrino_mass" title="Neutrino mass">neutrino mass</a>. There are also theoretical hints that this new physics should be found at accessible energy scales.\n</p><p>Much of the effort to find this new physics are focused on new collider experiments. The <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC) was completed in 2008 to help continue the search for the <a href="/wiki/Higgs_boson" title="Higgs boson">Higgs boson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersymmetric_particle" title="Supersymmetric particle">supersymmetric particles</a>, and other new physics. An intermediate goal is the construction of the <a href="/wiki/International_Linear_Collider" title="International Linear Collider">International Linear Collider</a> (ILC), which will complement the LHC by allowing more precise measurements of the properties of newly found particles. In August 2004, a decision for the technology of the ILC was taken but the site has still to be agreed upon.\n</p><p>In addition, there are important non-collider experiments that also attempt to find and understand <a href="/wiki/Physics_beyond_the_Standard_Model" title="Physics beyond the Standard Model">physics beyond the Standard Model</a>. One important non-collider effort is the determination of the <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> masses, since these masses may arise from neutrinos mixing with very heavy particles. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> observations provide many useful constraints on the dark matter, although it may be impossible to determine the exact nature of the dark matter without the colliders. Finally, lower bounds on the very long <a href="/wiki/Proton_decay" title="Proton decay">lifetime of the proton</a> put constraints on <a href="/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory" title="Grand Unified Theory">Grand Unified Theories</a> at energy scales much higher than collider experiments will be able to probe any time soon.\n</p><p>In May 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Particle_Physics_Project_Prioritization_Panel" title="Particle Physics Project Prioritization Panel">Particle Physics Project Prioritization Panel</a> released its report on particle physics funding priorities for the United States over the next decade. This report emphasized continued U.S. participation in the LHC and ILC, and expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Underground_Neutrino_Experiment" title="Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment">Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment</a>, among other recommendations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_energy_physics_compared_to_low_energy_physics">High energy physics compared to low energy physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_physics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High energy physics compared to low energy physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term <i>high energy physics</i> requires elaboration. Intuitively, it might seem incorrect to associate "high energy" with the physics of very small, <i>low</i> <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> objects, like subatomic particles. By comparison, an example of a <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic</a> system, one <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, has ~ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023600000000000000&#9824;</span>6<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>23</sup></span> times<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the mass of a single proton. Even an entire <a href="/wiki/Particle_beam" title="Particle beam">beam</a> of protons circulated in the LHC contains ~ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7014323000000000000&#9824;</span>3.23<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>14</sup></span> protons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhcbeam_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-lhcbeam-23">[23]</a></sup> each with <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7012650000000000000&#9824;</span>6.5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>12</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a></span> of energy, for a total beam energy of ~ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7008336457062270000&#9824;</span>2.1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>27</sup>&#160;eV</span> or ~ 336.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">MJ</a>, which is still ~ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005270000000000000&#9824;</span>2.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>5</sup></span> times lower than the mass-energy of a single gram of hydrogen. Yet, the macroscopic realm is "low energy physics",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> while that of quantum particles is "high energy physics".\n</p><p>The interactions studied in other fields of physics and science have comparatively very low energy. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> is about 1.8 to 3.1 eV. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Bond-dissociation_energy" title="Bond-dissociation energy">bond-dissociation energy</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon&#8211;carbon bond">carbon&#8211;carbon bond</a> is about 3.6 eV. Other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> typically involve similar amounts of energy. Even photons with far higher energy, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> of the kind produced in <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a>, mostly have photon energy between <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6986160217648700000&#9824;</span>10<sup>5</sup>&#160;eV</span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6988160217648700000&#9824;</span>10<sup>7</sup>&#160;eV</span> &#8211; still two <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a> lower than the mass of a single proton. Radioactive decay gamma rays are considered as part of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">nuclear physics</a>, rather than high energy physics.\n</p><p>The proton has a mass of around <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990150604589778000&#9824;</span>9.4<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>8</sup>&#160;eV</span>; some other massive quantum particles, both elementary and <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadronic</a>, have yet higher masses. Due to these very high energies <i>at the single particle level</i>, particle physics is, in fact, high-energy physics.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
