noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The ionized part of Earth\'s upper atmosphere</div>\n<p>The <b>ionosphere</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/a&#618;/: \'i\' in \'tide\'">a&#618;</span><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/&#594;/: \'o\' in \'body\'">&#594;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="/&#716;/: href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) is the ionized part of Earth\'s upper atmosphere, from about 60&#160;km (37&#160;mi) to 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi) altitude, a region that includes the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> and parts of the  <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a>. The ionosphere is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionized" title="Ionized">ionized</a> by solar radiation. It plays an important role in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_electricity" title="Atmospheric electricity">atmospheric electricity</a> and forms the inner edge of the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. It has practical importance because, among other functions, it influences <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a> to distant places on the Earth.<sup class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As early as 1839, the German mathematician and physicist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> postulated that an electrically conducting region of the atmosphere could account for observed variations of Earth\'s magnetic field. Sixty years later, <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Guglielmo Marconi</a> received the first trans-Atlantic radio signal on December 12, 1901, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland" title="St. John\'s, Newfoundland">St. John\'s, Newfoundland</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>) using a 152.4&#160;m (500&#160;ft) kite-supported antenna for reception. The transmitting station in <a href="/wiki/Poldhu" title="Poldhu">Poldhu</a>, Cornwall, used a spark-gap <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitter</a> to produce a signal with a <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of approximately 500&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilohertz" title="Kilohertz">kHz</a> and a power of 100 times more than any radio signal previously produced. The message received was three dits, the <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> for the letter <b>S</b>. To reach Newfoundland the signal would have to bounce off the ionosphere twice. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Jack_Belrose" title="Jack Belrose">Jack Belrose</a> has contested this, however, based on theoretical and experimental work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, Marconi did achieve transatlantic wireless communications in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glace_Bay,_Nova_Scotia" title="Glace Bay, Nova Scotia">Glace Bay, Nova Scotia</a>, one year later.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In 1902, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">Oliver Heaviside</a> proposed the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Kennelly%E2%80%93Heaviside_layer" title="Kennelly&#8211;Heaviside layer">Kennelly&#8211;Heaviside layer</a> of the ionosphere which bears his name. Heaviside\'s proposal included means by which radio signals are transmitted around the Earth\'s curvature. Heaviside\'s proposal, coupled with Planck\'s law of black body radiation, may have hampered the growth of radio astronomy for the detection of electromagnetic waves from celestial bodies until 1932 (and the development of high-frequency radio transceivers). Also in 1902, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Edwin_Kennelly" title="Arthur Edwin Kennelly">Arthur Edwin Kennelly</a> discovered some of the ionosphere\'s radio-electrical properties.\n</p><p>In 1912, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> imposed the <a href="/wiki/Radio_Act_of_1912" title="Radio Act of 1912">Radio Act of 1912</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_operators" title="Amateur radio operators">amateur radio operators</a>, limiting their operations to frequencies above 1.5&#160;MHz (wavelength 200 meters or smaller). The government thought those frequencies were useless. This led to the discovery of HF radio propagation via the ionosphere in 1923.\n</p><p>In 1926, Scottish physicist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Watson-Watt" title="Robert Watson-Watt">Robert Watson-Watt</a> introduced the term <i>ionosphere</i> in a letter published only in 1969 in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We have in quite recent years seen the universal adoption of the term \'stratosphere\'..and..the companion term \'troposphere\'... The term \'ionosphere\', for the region in which the main characteristic is large scale ionisation with considerable mean free paths, appears appropriate as an addition to this series.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In the early 1930s, test transmissions of <a href="/wiki/Radio_Luxembourg" title="Radio Luxembourg">Radio Luxembourg</a> inadvertently provided evidence of the first radio modification of the ionosphere; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HAARP" title="HAARP">HAARP</a> ran a series of experiments in 2017 using the eponymous Luxembourg Effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gakona_HAARPoon_2017_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gakona_HAARPoon_2017-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_V._Appleton" title="Edward V. Appleton">Edward V. Appleton</a> was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in 1947 for his confirmation in 1927 of the existence of the ionosphere. <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Berkner" title="Lloyd Berkner">Lloyd Berkner</a> first measured the height and density of the ionosphere. This permitted the first complete theory of short-wave radio propagation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maurice_V._Wilkes" title="Maurice V. Wilkes">Maurice V. Wilkes</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._A._Ratcliffe" title="J. A. Ratcliffe">J. A. Ratcliffe</a> researched the topic of radio propagation of very long radio waves in the ionosphere. <a href="/wiki/Vitaly_Ginzburg" title="Vitaly Ginzburg">Vitaly Ginzburg</a> has developed a theory of electromagnetic wave propagation in plasmas such as the ionosphere.\n</p><p>In 1962, the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> satellite <a href="/wiki/Alouette_1" title="Alouette 1">Alouette 1</a> was launched to study the ionosphere. Following its success were <a href="/wiki/Alouette_2" title="Alouette 2">Alouette 2</a> in 1965 and the two <a href="/wiki/ISIS_(satellite)" title="ISIS (satellite)">ISIS</a> satellites in 1969 and 1971, further AEROS-A  and -B in 1972 and 1975, all for measuring the ionosphere.\n</p><p>On July 26, 1963 the first operational geosynchronous satellite Syncom 2 was launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The board radio beacons on this satellite (and its successors) enabled &#8211; for the first time &#8211; the measurement of <a href="/wiki/Total_electron_content" title="Total electron content">total electron content</a> (TEC) variation along a radio beam from geostationary orbit to an earth receiver. (The rotation of the plane of polarization directly measures TEC along the path.)  Australian geophysicist Elizabeth Essex-Cohen from 1969 onwards was using this technique to monitor the atmosphere above Australia and Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysics">Geophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The ionosphere is a shell of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>  and electrically charged <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> that surrounds the Earth, stretching from a height of about 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) to more than 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi). It exists primarily due to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.\n</p><p>The lowest part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> extends from the surface to about 10&#160;km (6.2&#160;mi). Above that is the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, followed by the mesosphere. In the stratosphere incoming solar radiation creates the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. At heights of above 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi), in the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>, the atmosphere is so thin that free electrons can exist for short periods of time before they are captured by a nearby positive <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a>. The number of these free electrons is sufficient to affect <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">radio propagation</a>. This portion of the atmosphere is partially <i>ionized</i> and contains a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_physics" title="Plasma physics">plasma</a> which is referred to as the ionosphere.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">Ultraviolet</a> (UV), <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> and shorter <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> are <i>ionizing,</i> since <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> at these frequencies contain sufficient energy to dislodge an electron from a neutral gas atom or molecule upon absorption. In this process the light electron obtains a high velocity so that the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the created electronic gas is much higher (of the order of thousand K) than the one of ions and neutrals. The reverse process to <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombination_(chemistry)" title="Recombination (chemistry)">recombination</a>, in which a free electron is "captured" by a positive ion. Recombination occurs spontaneously, and causes the emission of a photon carrying away the energy produced upon recombination. As gas density increases at lower altitudes, the recombination process prevails, since the gas molecules and ions are closer together. The balance between these two processes determines the quantity of ionization present.\n</p><p>Ionization depends primarily on the Sun and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">activity</a>. The amount of ionization in the ionosphere varies greatly with the amount of radiation received from the Sun. Thus there is a <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">diurnal</a> (time of day) effect and a seasonal effect. The local winter <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">hemisphere</a> is tipped away from the Sun, thus there is less received solar radiation. The activity of the Sun is associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspot_cycle" title="Sunspot cycle">sunspot cycle</a>, with more radiation occurring with more sunspots. Radiation received also varies with geographical location (polar, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auroral" title="Auroral">auroral</a> zones, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-latitudes" title="Mid-latitudes">mid-latitudes</a>, and equatorial regions). There are also mechanisms that disturb the ionosphere and decrease the ionization. There are disturbances such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a> and the associated release of charged particles into the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> which reaches the Earth and interacts with its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic" title="Geomagnetic">geomagnetic</a> field.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_ionospheric_layers">The ionospheric layers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The ionospheric layers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg/400px-Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg/600px-Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg/800px-Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ionosphere_Layers_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ionospheric layers....</div></div></div>\n<p>At night the F layer is the only layer of significant ionization present, while the ionization in the E and D layers is extremely low. During the day, the D and E layers become much more heavily ionized, as does the F layer, which develops an additional, weaker region of ionisation known as the F<sub>1</sub> layer. The F<sub>2</sub> layer persists by day and night and is the main region responsible for the refraction and reflection of radio waves.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D_layer">D layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: D layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The D layer is the innermost layer, 60&#160;km (37&#160;mi) to 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) above the surface of the Earth. Ionization here is due to <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman series</a>-alpha hydrogen radiation at a <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of 121.6 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometre</a> (nm) ionizing <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO). In addition, high <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">solar activity</a> can generate hard X-rays (wavelength <span class="nowrap">&lt; 1 nm</span>) that ionize N<sub>2</sub> and O<sub>2</sub>. Recombination rates are high in the D layer, so there are many more neutral air molecules than ions.\n</p><p>Medium frequency (MF) and lower high frequency (HF) <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> are significantly attenuated within the D layer, as the passing radio waves cause electrons to move, which then collide with the neutral molecules, giving up their energy. Lower frequencies experience greater absorption because they move the electrons farther, leading to greater chance of collisions. This is the main reason for <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_absorption" title="Ionospheric absorption">absorption of HF radio waves</a>, particularly at 10&#160;MHz and below, with progressively less absorption at higher frequencies. This effect peaks around noon and is reduced at night due to a decrease in the D layer\'s thickness; only a small part remains due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. A common example of the D layer in action is the disappearance of distant AM <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_band" title="Broadcast band">broadcast band</a> stations in the daytime.\n</p><p>During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">solar proton events</a>, ionization can reach unusually high levels in the D-region over high and polar latitudes. Such very rare events are known as Polar Cap Absorption (or PCA) events, because the increased ionization significantly enhances the absorption of radio signals passing through the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rose1962_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rose1962-8">[8]</a></sup> In fact, absorption levels can increase by many tens of dB during intense events, which is enough to absorb most (if not all) transpolar HF radio signal transmissions. Such events typically last less than 24 to 48 hours.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="E_layer">E layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: E layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kennelly%E2%80%93Heaviside_layer" title="Kennelly&#8211;Heaviside layer">E layer</a> is the middle layer, 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) to 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) above the surface of the Earth. Ionization is due to soft X-ray (1&#8211;10&#160;nm) and far ultraviolet (UV) solar radiation ionization of molecular <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>). Normally, at oblique incidence, this layer can only reflect radio waves having frequencies lower than about 10&#160;MHz and may contribute a bit to absorption on frequencies above. However, during intense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporadic_E" title="Sporadic E">sporadic E</a> events, the E<sub>s</sub> layer can reflect frequencies up to 50&#160;MHz and higher. The vertical structure of the E layer is primarily determined by the competing effects of ionization and recombination. At night the E layer weakens because the primary source of ionization is no longer present. After sunset an increase in the height of the E layer maximum increases the range to which radio waves can travel by reflection from the layer.\n</p><p>This region is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Kennelly%E2%80%93Heaviside_layer" title="Kennelly&#8211;Heaviside layer">Kennelly&#8211;Heaviside layer</a> or simply the Heaviside layer. Its existence was predicted in 1902 independently and almost simultaneously by the American electrical engineer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Edwin_Kennelly" title="Arthur Edwin Kennelly">Arthur Edwin Kennelly</a> (1861&#8211;1939) and the British physicist <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">Oliver Heaviside</a> (1850&#8211;1925). However, it was not until 1924 that its existence was detected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_V._Appleton" title="Edward V. Appleton">Edward V. Appleton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miles_Aylmer_Fulton_Barnett" title="Miles Aylmer Fulton Barnett">Miles Barnett</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Es">E<sub>s</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Es">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The E<sub>s</sub> layer (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sporadic#Adjective" title="wikt:sporadic">sporadic</a> E-layer) is characterized by small, thin clouds of intense ionization, which can support reflection of radio waves, rarely up to 225&#160;MHz. Sporadic-E events may last for just a few minutes to several hours. <a href="/wiki/Sporadic_E_propagation" title="Sporadic E propagation">Sporadic E propagation</a> makes VHF-operating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_high_bands" title="Amateur radio high bands">radio amateurs</a> very excited, as propagation paths that are generally unreachable can open up. There are multiple causes of sporadic-E that are still being pursued by researchers. This propagation occurs most frequently during the summer months when high signal levels may be reached. The skip distances are generally around 1,640&#160;km (1,020&#160;mi). Distances for one hop propagation can be anywhere from 900&#160;km (560&#160;mi) to 2,500&#160;km (1,600&#160;mi). Double-hop reception over 3,500&#160;km (2,200&#160;mi) is possible.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="F_layer">F layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: F layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/F_region" title="F region">F region</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/F_region" title="F region">F layer</a> or region, also known as the Appleton&#8211;Barnett layer, extends from about 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) to more than 500&#160;km (310&#160;mi) above the surface of Earth. It is the layer with the highest electron density, which implies signals penetrating this layer will escape into space. Electron production is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a> (UV, 10&#8211;100&#160;nm) radiation ionizing atomic oxygen. The F layer consists of one layer (F<sub>2</sub>) at night, but during the day, a secondary peak (labelled F<sub>1</sub>) often forms in the electron density profile. Because the F<sub>2</sub> layer remains by day and night, it is responsible for most <a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">skywave</a> propagation of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> waves and long distances <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">high frequency</a> (HF, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a>) radio communications.\n</p><p>Above the F layer, the number of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> ions decreases and lighter ions such as hydrogen and helium become dominant. This region above the F layer peak and below the <a href="/wiki/Plasmasphere" title="Plasmasphere">plasmasphere</a> is called the topside ionosphere.\n</p><p>From 1972 to 1975 <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/AEROS_(satellite)" title="AEROS (satellite)">AEROS and AEROS B</a> satellites to study the F region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yenne_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yenne-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionospheric_model"><span id="Ionospheric_model"></span>Ionospheric model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ionospheric model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An <b>ionospheric model</b> is a mathematical description of the ionosphere as a function of location, altitude, day of year, phase of the sunspot cycle and geomagnetic activity. Geophysically, the state of the ionospheric <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> may be described by four parameters: <i>electron density, electron and ion <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a></i> and, since several species of ions are present, <i>ionic composition</i>. <a href="/wiki/Radio_propagation" title="Radio propagation">Radio propagation</a> depends uniquely on electron density.\n</p><p>Models are usually expressed as computer programs.  The model may be based on basic physics of the interactions of the ions and electrons with the neutral atmosphere and sunlight, or it may be a statistical description based on a large number of observations or a combination of physics and observations. One of the most widely used models is the <a href="/wiki/International_Reference_Ionosphere" title="International Reference Ionosphere">International Reference Ionosphere</a> (IRI),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which is based on data and specifies the four parameters just mentioned. The IRI is an international project sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Space_Research" title="Committee on Space Research">Committee on Space Research</a> (COSPAR) and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Radio_Science" title="International Union of Radio Science">International Union of Radio Science</a> (URSI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The major data sources are the worldwide network of <a href="/wiki/Ionosonde" title="Ionosonde">ionosondes</a>, the powerful <a href="/wiki/Incoherent_scatter" title="Incoherent scatter">incoherent scatter</a> radars (Jicamarca, <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo</a>, Millstone Hill, Malvern, St Santin), the ISIS and Alouette topside sounders, and in situ instruments on several satellites and rockets. IRI is updated yearly. IRI is more accurate in describing the variation of the electron density from bottom of the ionosphere to the altitude of maximum density than in describing the <a href="/wiki/Total_electron_content" title="Total electron content">total electron content</a> (TEC). Since 1999 this model is "International Standard" for the terrestrial ionosphere (standard TS16457).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Persistent_anomalies_to_the_idealized_model">Persistent anomalies to the idealized model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Persistent anomalies to the idealized model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ionogram" title="Ionogram">Ionograms</a> allow deducing, via computation, the true shape of the different layers. Nonhomogeneous structure of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a>-<a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> produces rough echo traces, seen predominantly at night and at higher latitudes, and during disturbed conditions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_anomaly">Winter anomaly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Winter anomaly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At mid-latitudes, the F<sub>2</sub> layer daytime ion production is higher in the summer, as expected, since the Sun shines more directly on the Earth. However, there are seasonal changes in the molecular-to-atomic ratio of the neutral atmosphere that cause the summer ion loss rate to be even higher. The result is that the increase in the summertime loss overwhelms the increase in summertime production, and total F<sub>2</sub> ionization is actually lower in the local summer months. This effect is known as the winter anomaly. The anomaly is always present in the northern hemisphere, but is usually absent in the southern hemisphere during periods of low solar activity.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equatorial_anomaly">Equatorial anomaly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Equatorial anomaly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:264px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diurnal_ionospheric_current.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="262" decoding="async" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Diurnal_ionospheric_current.jpg" width="262"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Electric currents created in sunward ionosphere.</div></div></div>\n<p>Within approximately &#177; 20 degrees of the <i>magnetic equator</i>, is the <i><a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equatorial</a> anomaly</i>. It is the occurrence of a trough in the ionization in the F<sub>2</sub> layer at the equator and crests at about 17 degrees in magnetic latitude. The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth\'s magnetic field">Earth\'s magnetic field</a> lines are horizontal at the magnetic equator. Solar heating and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal" title="Tidal">tidal</a> oscillations in the lower ionosphere move plasma up and across the magnetic field lines. This sets up a sheet of electric current in the E region which, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" title="Horizontal plane">horizontal</a> magnetic field, forces ionization up into the F layer, concentrating at &#177; 20 degrees from the magnetic equator. This phenomenon is known as the <i>equatorial fountain</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equatorial_electrojet">Equatorial electrojet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Equatorial electrojet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The worldwide solar-driven wind results in the so-called Sq (solar quiet) current system in the E region of the Earth\'s ionosphere (<a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_dynamo_region" title="Ionospheric dynamo region">ionospheric dynamo region</a>) (100&#8211;130&#160;km (62&#8211;81&#160;mi) altitude). Resulting from this current is an electrostatic field directed west&#8211;east (dawn&#8211;dusk) in the equatorial day side of the ionosphere. At the magnetic dip equator, where the geomagnetic field is horizontal, this electric field results in an enhanced eastward current flow within &#177; 3 degrees of the magnetic equator, known as the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_electrojet" title="Equatorial electrojet">equatorial electrojet</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ephemeral_ionospheric_perturbations">Ephemeral ionospheric perturbations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ephemeral ionospheric perturbations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="X-rays:_sudden_ionospheric_disturbances_.28SID.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="X-rays:_sudden_ionospheric_disturbances_(SID)">X-rays: sudden ionospheric disturbances (SID)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: X-rays: sudden ionospheric disturbances (SID)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the Sun is active, strong <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a> can occur that hit the sunlit side of Earth with hard X-rays. The X-rays penetrate to the D-region, releasing electrons that rapidly increase absorption, causing a high frequency (3&#8211;30&#160;MHz) radio blackout. During this time very low frequency (3&#8211;30&#160;kHz) signals will be reflected by the D layer instead of the E layer, where the increased atmospheric density will usually increase the absorption of the wave and thus dampen it. As soon as the X-rays end, the <a href="/wiki/Sudden_ionospheric_disturbance" title="Sudden ionospheric disturbance">sudden ionospheric disturbance</a> (SID) or radio black-out ends as the electrons in the D-region recombine rapidly and signal strengths return to normal.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Protons:_polar_cap_absorption_.28PCA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Protons:_polar_cap_absorption_(PCA)">Protons: polar cap absorption (PCA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Protons: polar cap absorption (PCA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Associated with solar flares is a release of high-energy protons. These particles can hit the Earth within 15 minutes to 2 hours of the solar flare. The protons spiral around and down the magnetic field lines of the Earth and penetrate into the atmosphere near the magnetic poles increasing the ionization of the D and E layers. PCA\'s typically last anywhere from about an hour to several days, with an average of around 24 to 36 hours. <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">Coronal mass ejections</a> can also release energetic protons that enhance D-region absorption in the polar regions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetic_storms">Geomagnetic storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geomagnetic storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storm</a> is a temporary intense disturbance of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>.\n</p>\n<ul><li>During a geomagnetic storm the F&#8322; layer will become unstable, fragment, and may even disappear completely.</li>\n<li>In the Northern and Southern pole regions of the Earth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_aurora" title="Polar aurora">aurorae</a> will be observable in the sky.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightning">Lightning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Lightning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> can cause ionospheric perturbations in the D-region in one of two ways. The first is through VLF (very low frequency) radio waves launched into the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. These so-called "whistler" mode waves can interact with radiation belt particles and cause them to precipitate onto the ionosphere, adding ionization to the D-region. These disturbances are called "lightning-induced <a href="/wiki/Electron_precipitation" title="Electron precipitation">electron precipitation</a>" (LEP) events.\n</p><p>Additional ionization can also occur from direct heating/ionization as a result of huge motions of charge in lightning strikes. These events are called early/fast.\n</p><p>In 1925, C. T. R. Wilson proposed a mechanism by which electrical discharge from lightning storms could propagate upwards from clouds to the ionosphere. Around the same time, Robert Watson-Watt, working at the Radio Research Station in Slough, UK, suggested that the ionospheric sporadic E layer (E<sub>s</sub>) appeared to be enhanced as a result of lightning but that more work was needed. In 2005, C. Davis and C. Johnson, working at the Rutherford Appleton Laboratory in Oxfordshire, UK, demonstrated that the E<sub>s</sub> layer was indeed enhanced as a result of lightning activity. Their subsequent research has focused on the mechanism by which this process can occur.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_communication">Radio communication<span id="Radio_application"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Radio communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to the ability of ionized atmospheric gases to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refract" title="Refract">refract</a> high frequency (HF, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a>) radio waves, the ionosphere can reflect radio waves directed into the sky back toward the Earth.  Radio waves directed at an angle into the sky can return to Earth beyond the horizon.  This technique, called "skip" or "<a href="/wiki/Skywave" title="Skywave">skywave</a>" propagation, has been used since the 1920s to communicate at international or intercontinental distances.  The returning radio waves can reflect off the Earth\'s surface into the sky again, allowing greater ranges to be achieved with multiple <a href="/wiki/Hop_(telecommunications)" title="Hop (telecommunications)">hops</a>. This communication method is variable and unreliable, with reception over a given path depending on time of day or night, the seasons, weather, and the 11-year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspot_cycle" title="Sunspot cycle">sunspot cycle</a>.  During the first half of the 20th century it was widely used for transoceanic telephone and telegraph service, and business and diplomatic communication. Due to its relative unreliability, shortwave radio communication has been mostly abandoned by the telecommunications industry, though it remains important for high-latitude communication where satellite-based radio communication is not possible. Some broadcasting stations and automated services still use <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radio" title="Shortwave radio">shortwave radio</a> frequencies, as do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_amateur" title="Radio amateur">radio amateur</a> hobbyists for private recreational contacts.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism_of_refraction">Mechanism of refraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mechanism of refraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>When a radio wave reaches the ionosphere, the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> in the wave forces the electrons in the ionosphere into <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillation</a> at the same frequency as the radio wave. Some of the radio-frequency energy is given up to this resonant oscillation. The oscillating electrons will then either be lost to recombination or will re-radiate the original wave energy. Total refraction can occur when the collision frequency of the ionosphere is less than the radio frequency, and if the electron density in the ionosphere is great enough.\n</p><p>A qualitative understanding of how an electromagnetic wave propagates through the ionosphere can be obtained by recalling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric_optics" title="Geometric optics">geometric optics</a>.  Since the ionosphere is a plasma, it can be shown that the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> is less than unity.  Hence, the electromagnetic "ray" is bent away from the normal rather than toward the normal as would be indicated when the refractive index is greater than unity.  It can also be shown that the refractive index of a plasma, and hence the ionosphere, is frequency-dependent, see <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">Dispersion (optics)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Critical_frequency" title="Critical frequency">critical frequency</a> is the limiting frequency at or below which a radio wave is reflected by an ionospheric layer at vertical <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">incidence</a>. If the transmitted frequency is higher than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_frequency" title="Plasma frequency">plasma frequency</a> of the ionosphere, then the electrons cannot respond fast enough, and they are not able to re-radiate the signal. The <a href="/wiki/Space_tether" title="Space tether">space tether</a> uses plasma contactors and the ionosphere as parts of a circuit to extract energy from the Earth\'s magnetic field by <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">electromagnetic induction</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements">Measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Scientists explore the structure of the ionosphere by a wide variety of methods. They include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>passive observations of optical and radio emissions generated in the ionosphere</li>\n<li>bouncing radio waves of different frequencies from it</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Incoherent_scatter" title="Incoherent scatter">incoherent scatter</a> radars such as the <a href="/wiki/EISCAT" title="EISCAT">EISCAT</a>, Sondre Stromfjord, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millstone_Hill_Observatory" title="Millstone Hill Observatory">Millstone Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo</a>, Advanced Modular Incoherent Scatter Radar (AMISR) and <a href="/wiki/Jicamarca_Radio_Observatory" title="Jicamarca Radio Observatory">Jicamarca</a> radars</li>\n<li>coherent scatter radars such as the <a href="/wiki/Super_Dual_Auroral_Radar_Network" title="Super Dual Auroral Radar Network">Super Dual Auroral Radar Network (SuperDARN)</a> radars,</li>\n<li>special receivers to detect how the reflected waves have changed from the transmitted waves.</li></ul>\n<p>A variety of experiments, such as HAARP (<a href="/wiki/High_Frequency_Active_Auroral_Research_Program" title="High Frequency Active Auroral Research Program">High Frequency Active Auroral Research Program</a>), involve high power radio transmitters to modify the properties of the ionosphere. These investigations focus on studying the properties and behavior of ionospheric plasma, with particular emphasis on being able to understand and use it to enhance communications and surveillance systems for both civilian and military purposes. HAARP was started in 1993 as a proposed twenty-year experiment, and is currently active near Gakona, Alaska.\n</p><p>The SuperDARN radar project researches the high- and mid-latitudes using coherent backscatter of radio waves in the 8 to 20&#160;MHz range. Coherent backscatter is similar to Bragg scattering in crystals and involves the constructive interference of scattering from ionospheric density irregularities. The project involves more than 11 different countries and multiple radars in both hemispheres.\n</p><p>Scientists are also examining the ionosphere by the changes to radio waves, from satellites and stars, passing through it. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arecibo_radio_telescope" title="Arecibo radio telescope">Arecibo radio telescope</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, was originally intended to study Earth\'s ionosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionograms">Ionograms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Ionograms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ionogram" title="Ionogram">Ionogram</a></div>\n<p>Ionograms show the virtual heights and <b>critical frequencies</b> of the ionospheric layers and which are measured by an <a href="/wiki/Ionosonde" title="Ionosonde">ionosonde</a>. An ionosonde sweeps a range of frequencies, usually from 0.1 to 30&#160;MHz, transmitting at vertical incidence to the ionosphere. As the frequency increases, each wave is refracted less by the ionization in the layer, and so each penetrates further before it is reflected. Eventually, a frequency is reached that enables the wave to penetrate the layer without being reflected. For ordinary mode waves, this occurs when the transmitted frequency just exceeds the peak plasma, or critical, frequency of the layer. Tracings of the reflected high frequency radio pulses are known as ionograms. Reduction rules are given in: "URSI Handbook of Ionogram Interpretation and Reduction", edited by <a href="/wiki/William_Roy_Piggott" title="William Roy Piggott">William Roy Piggott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Rawer" title="Karl Rawer">Karl Rawer</a>, Elsevier Amsterdam, 1961 (translations into Chinese, French, Japanese and Russian are available).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incoherent_scatter_radars">Incoherent scatter radars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Incoherent scatter radars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Incoherent_scatter" title="Incoherent scatter">Incoherent scatter</a> radars operate above the critical frequencies. Therefore, the technique allows probing the ionosphere, unlike ionosondes, also above the electron density peaks. The thermal fluctuations of the electron density scattering the transmitted signals lack <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">coherence</a>, which gave the technique its name. Their power spectrum contains information not only on the density, but also on the ion and electron temperatures, ion masses and drift velocities.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GNSS_radio_occultation">GNSS radio occultation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: GNSS radio occultation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Radio_occultation" title="Radio occultation">Radio occultation</a> is a remote sensing technique where a GNSS signal tangentially scrapes the Earth, passing through the atmosphere, and is received by a Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite. As the signal passes through the atmosphere, it is refracted, curved and delayed. An LEO satellite samples the total electron content and bending angle of many such signal paths as it watches the GNSS satellite rise or set behind the Earth. Using an Inverse <a href="/wiki/Abel_transform" title="Abel transform">Abel\'s transform</a>, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Radial_profile&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Radial profile (page does not exist)">radial profile</a> of refractivity at that tangent point on earth can be reconstructed.\n</p><p>Major GNSS radio occultation missions include the <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">GRACE</a>, <a href="/wiki/CHAMP_(satellite)" title="CHAMP (satellite)">CHAMP</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Constellation_Observing_System_for_Meteorology,_Ionosphere,_and_Climate" title="Constellation Observing System for Meteorology, Ionosphere, and Climate">COSMIC</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indices_of_the_ionosphere">Indices of the ionosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Indices of the ionosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In empirical models of the ionosphere such as Nequick, the following indices are used as indirect indicators of the state of the ionosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_intensity">Solar intensity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Solar intensity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>F10.7 and R12 are two indices commonly used in ionospheric modelling. Both are valuable for their long historical records covering multiple solar cycles. F10.7 is a measurement of the intensity of solar radio emissions at a frequency of 2800&#160;MHz made using a ground <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a>. R12 is a 12 months average of daily sunspot numbers.  Both indices have been shown to be correlated to each other.\n</p><p>However, both indices are only indirect indicators of solar ultraviolet and X-ray emissions, which are primarily responsible for causing ionization in the Earth\'s upper atmosphere. We now have data from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">GOES</a> spacecraft that measures the background <b>X-ray flux</b> from the Sun, a parameter more closely related to the ionization levels in the ionosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomagnetic_disturbances">Geomagnetic disturbances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Geomagnetic disturbances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-index" title="A-index">A</a></i>- and <i><a href="/wiki/K-index" title="K-index">K</a></i>-indices are a measurement of the behavior of the horizontal component of the <b>geomagnetic field</b>. The <i>K</i>-index uses a scale from 0 to 9 to measure the change in the horizontal component of the geomagnetic field. A new <i>K</i>-index is determined at the <a href="/wiki/Boulder_Geomagnetic_Observatory" title="Boulder Geomagnetic Observatory">Boulder Geomagnetic Observatory</a>.</li>\n<li>The geomagnetic activity levels of the Earth are measured by the fluctuation of the Earth\'s magnetic field in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> units called <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">teslas</a> (or in non-SI <a href="/wiki/Gauss_(unit)" title="Gauss (unit)">gauss</a>, especially in older literature). The Earth\'s magnetic field is measured around the planet by many observatories. The data retrieved is processed and turned into measurement indices. Daily measurements for the entire planet are made available through an estimate of the <i>A</i><sub>p</sub>-index, called the <i>planetary A-index</i> (PAI).</li></ul>\n<h2><span id="GPS.2FGNSS_ionospheric_correction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="GPS/GNSS_ionospheric_correction">GPS/GNSS ionospheric correction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: GPS/GNSS ionospheric correction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" decoding="async" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ionosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">October 2013</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Total_electron_content" title="Total electron content">Total electron content</a></div>\n<p>There are a number of models used to understand the effects of the ionosphere global navigation satellite systems.
