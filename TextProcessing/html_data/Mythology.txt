href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Mythology&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Myth</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folklore_genre" title="Folklore genre">folklore genre</a> consisting of narratives that play a fundamental role in society, such as foundational tales. The main characters in myths are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demigod" title="Demigod">demigods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oflaherty_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-oflaherty-3">[3]</a></sup> Myths are often endorsed by rulers and priests and are closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> or spirituality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup> In fact, many societies group their myths, legends and history together, considering myths to be true accounts of their remote past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199823_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199823-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni1984102_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni1984102-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">Creation myths</a> particularly, take place in a primordial age when the world had not achieved its later form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDundes19841_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDundes19841-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19986_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19986-7">[7]</a></sup> Other myths explain how a society\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(social)" title="Norm (social)">customs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboos</a> were established and sanctified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19986_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19986-7">[7]</a></sup> There is a complex relationship between <a href="/wiki/Myth_and_ritual" title="Myth and ritual">recital of myths and enactment of rituals</a>.\n</p><p>The study of myth began in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient history</a>. Rival classes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_myths" title="Greek myths">Greek myths</a> by <a href="/wiki/Euhemerus" title="Euhemerus">Euhemerus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sallustius" title="Sallustius">Sallustius</a> were developed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoplatonists" title="Neoplatonists">Neoplatonists</a> and later revived by <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> mythographers. Today, the study of myth continues in a wide variety of academic fields, including <a href="/wiki/Folklore_studies" title="Folklore studies">folklore studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>. The term <b>mythology</b> may either refer to the study of myths in general, or a body of myths regarding a particular subject. The academic comparisons of bodies of myth is known as <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">comparative mythology</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Religion_and_mythology" title="Religion and mythology">Religion and mythology</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth"><i>Myth</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Definitions of <i>myth</i> to some extent vary by scholar. Finnish <a href="/wiki/Folklore_studies" title="Folklore studies">folklorist</a> Lauri Honko offers a widely cited definition:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Myth, a story of the gods, a religious account of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">beginning of the world</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation</a>, fundamental events, the exemplary deeds of the gods as a result of which the world, nature and culture were created together with all parts thereof and given their order, which still obtains. A myth expresses and confirms society\'s religious values and norms, it provides a pattern of behavior to be imitated, testifies to the efficacy of <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> with its practical ends and establishes the sanctity of <a href="/wiki/Cult" title="Cult">cult</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HONKO-1984_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HONKO-1984-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Scholars in other fields use the term <i>myth</i> in varied ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDundes1984147_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDundes1984147-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoty200411&#8211;12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoty200411&#8211;12-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20155_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20155-11">[11]</a></sup> In a broad sense, the word can refer to any <a href="/wiki/Traditional_story" title="Traditional story">traditional story</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk198457_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk198457-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk197374_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk197374-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApollodorus19763_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApollodorus19763-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_misconception" title="Popular misconception">popular misconception</a> or <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imaginary</a> entity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, while myth and other folklore genres may overlap, myth is often thought to differ from genres such as <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folklore_genre" title="Folklore genre">folktale</a> in that neither are considered to be sacred narratives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SALAMON-GOLDBERG-2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-SALAMON-GOLDBERG-2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19657_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19657-17">[17]</a></sup> Some kinds of folktales, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairy_stories" title="Fairy stories">fairy stories</a>, are not considered true by anyone, and may be seen as distinct from myths for this reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659,_17_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659,_17-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199810&#8211;11_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199810&#8211;11-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni198499&#8211;101_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPettazzoni198499&#8211;101-20">[20]</a></sup> Main characters in myths are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gods" title="Gods">gods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demigod" title="Demigod">demigods</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> humans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oflaherty_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-oflaherty-3">[3]</a></sup> while legends generally feature humans as their main characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom19659_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom19659-1">[1]</a></sup> However, many exceptions or combinations exist, as in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk197322,_32_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk197322,_32-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk198455_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk198455-22">[22]</a></sup> Moreover, as stories spread between cultures or as faiths change, myths can come to be considered folktales, their divine characters recast as either as humans or demihumans such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_(mythology)" title="Giant (mythology)">giants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elf" title="Elf">elves</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faerie" title="Faerie">faeries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythfolk_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-mythfolk-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoty2004114_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoty2004114-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBascom196513_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBascom196513-24">[24]</a></sup> Conversely, historical and literary material may acquire mythological qualities over time. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_Britain" title="Matter of Britain">Matter of Britain</a> (the legendary history of Great Britain, especially those focused on <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">King Arthur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Round_Table" title="Round Table">the knights of the Round Table</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Matter_of_France" title="Matter of France">Matter of France</a>, seem distantly to originate in historical events of the fifth and eighth-centuries respectively, and became mythologised over the following centuries.\n</p><p>In colloquial use, the word <i>myth</i> can also be used of a collectively held belief that has no basis in fact, or any false story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This usage, which is often <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> arose from labeling the religious myths and beliefs of other cultures as incorrect, but it has spread to cover non-religious beliefs as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, as commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Folklore_studies" title="Folklore studies">folklorists</a> and academics in other relevant fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, the term <i>myth</i> has no implication whether the narrative may be understood as true or otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the term <i>myth</i> is widely used to imply that a story is not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Objective_truth" title="Objective truth">objectively true</a>, the identification of a narrative as a myth can be highly political: many adherents of religions view their religion\'s stories as <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">true</a> and therefore object to the stories being characterised as myths. Nevertheless, scholars now routinely speak of <a href="/wiki/Christian_mythology" title="Christian mythology">Christian mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jewish_mythology" title="Jewish mythology">Jewish mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islamic_mythology" title="Islamic mythology">Islamic mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu_mythology" title="Hindu mythology">Hindu mythology</a>, and so forth. Traditionally, Western scholarship, with its Judaeo-Christian heritage, has viewed narratives in the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a> as being the province of <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> rather than mythology; meanwhile, identifying religious stories of colonised cultures, such as stories in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, as myths enabled Western scholars to imply that they were of lower truth-value than the stories of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. Labelling all religious narratives as <i>myths</i> can be thought of as treating different traditions with parity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leeming=preface_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-leeming=preface-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology"><i>Mythology</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Mythology" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mythology_(disambiguation)" title="Mythology (disambiguation)">Mythology (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>In present use, <i>mythology</i> usually refers to the collected myths of a group of people, but may also mean the study of such myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKirk19738_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKirk19738-31">[31]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hittite_mythology" title="Hittite mythology">Hittite mythology</a> all describe the body of myths retold among those cultures. Folklorist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Dundes" title="Alan Dundes">Alan Dundes</a> defines myth as a <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacred</a> <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> that explains how the world and humanity evolved into their present form. Dundes classified a sacred narrative as "a story that serves to define the fundamental worldview of a culture by explaining aspects of the natural world and delineating the psychological and social practices and ideals of a society".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grassie_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-grassie-32">[32]</a></sup> Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lincoln" title="Bruce Lincoln">Bruce Lincoln</a> defines <i>myth</i> as "ideology in narrative form."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythography"><i>Mythography</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mythography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The compilation or description of myths is sometimes known as <i>mythography</i>, a term which can also be used of a scholarly anthology of myths (or, confusingly, of the study of myths generally).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Key mythographers in the Classical tradition include <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a> (43 BCE&#8211;17/18 CE), whose tellings of myths have been profoundingly influential; <a href="/wiki/Fabius_Planciades_Fulgentius" title="Fabius Planciades Fulgentius">Fabius Planciades Fulgentius</a>, a Latin writer of the late fifth to early sixth centuries, whose <i>Mythologies</i> (Latin: <i>Mitologiarum libri III</i>) gathered and gave moralistic interpretations of a wide range of myths; the anonymous medieval <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Mythographers" title="Vatican Mythographers">Vatican Mythographers</a>, who developed anthologies of Classical myths that remained influential to the end of the Middle Ages; and the Renaissance scholar <a href="/wiki/Natalis_Comes" title="Natalis Comes">Natalis Comes</a>, whose ten-book <i>Mythologiae</i> became a standard source for classical mythology in later Renaissance Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Other prominent mythographies include the thirteenth-century <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i> attributed to the Icelander <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>, which is the main surviving survey of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_Mythology" title="Norse Mythology">Norse Mythology</a> from the Middle Ages.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythos"><i>Mythos</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mythos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Mythos" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Myth_(disambiguation)" title="Myth (disambiguation)">Myth (disambiguation)</a> and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mythos_(disambiguation)" title="Mythos (disambiguation)">Mythos (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>Because <i>myth</i> is sometimes used in a pejorative sense, some scholars have opted to use the term <i>mythos</i> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grassie_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-grassie-32">[32]</a></sup> However, <i>mythos</i> now more commonly refers to its <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Aristotelian</a> sense as a "plot point" or to \'a body of interconnected myths or stories, esp[ecially] those belonging to a particular religious or cultural tradition\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmthos_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedmthos-37">[37]</a></sup> It is sometimes used specifically for modern, fictional mythologies, such as the <a href="/wiki/Worldbuilding" title="Worldbuilding">world building</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.P._Lovecraft" title="H.P. Lovecraft">H.P. Lovecraft</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythopoeia"><i>Mythopoeia</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mythopoeia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeia" title="Mythopoeia">Mythopoeia</a></div>\n<p>"Conscious generation" of mythology was termed <i>mythopoeia</i> by, amongst others, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It was notoriously also suggested, separately, by Nazi ideologist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Rosenberg" title="Alfred Rosenberg">Alfred Rosenberg</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_terms_myth_and_mythology">Origins of the terms <i>myth</i> and <i>mythology</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origins of the terms myth and mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1444" data-file-width="2024" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg/220px-Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg/330px-Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg/440px-Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Francesco_Hayez_028.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Odysseus Overcome by Demodocus\' Song</i>, by Francesco Hayez, 1813&#8211;15</div></div></div>\n<p>The word <i>myth</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <i>&#956;&#8166;&#952;&#959;&#962;</i> [<i>m&#563;thos</i>], meaning \'speech, narrative, fiction, myth, plot\'. In Anglicised form, this Greek word began to be used in English (and was likewise adapted into other European languages) in the early nineteenth century, in a much narrower sense, as a scholarly term for \'a traditional story, typically involving supernatural beings or forces, which embodies and provides an explanation, aetiology, or justification for something such as the early history of a society, a religious belief or ritual, or a natural phenomenon\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythmythos_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-mythmythos-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In turn, Ancient Greek <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#956;&#965;&#952;&#959;&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;</span> [<i>mytholog&#237;a</i>] ("story," "lore," "legends," "the telling of stories") combines the word <i>m&#563;thos</i> with the suffix -<i>&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;</i> [<i>-logia</i>] ("study"), and meant \'romance, fiction, story-telling\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedlogy_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedlogy-41">[41]</a></sup> Accordingly, <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> used <i>mytholog&#237;a</i> as a general term for "fiction" or "story-telling" of any kind.\n</p><p>The Greek term <i>mytholog&#237;a</i> was then borrowed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> <i>mythologia</i>, which occurs in the title of Latin author <a href="/wiki/Fabius_Planciades_Fulgentius" title="Fabius Planciades Fulgentius">Fulgentius</a>\' fifth-century <i>Mythologi&#230;</i>, denoted the explication of Greek and Roman stories about their gods, which we now call <a href="/wiki/Classical_mythology" title="Classical mythology">classical mythology</a>. Fulgentius\'s <i>Mythologi&#230;</i> explicitly treated its subject matter as <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegories</a> requiring interpretation and not as true events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Latin term was then adopted in <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> as <i>mythologie</i>. Whether from French or Latin usage, English adopted the word "mythology" in the fifteenth century, at first in the sense \'the exposition of a myth or myths; the interpretation of fables; a book of such expositions\'. The word is first attested in <a href="/wiki/John_Lydgate" title="John Lydgate">John Lydgate</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Troy_Book" title="Troy Book">Troy Book</a></i> of c. 1425.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From Lydgate until the seventeenth or eighteenth-century, <i>mythology</i> was used to mean a <a href="/wiki/Moral" title="Moral">moral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Parable" title="Parable">parable</a>, or collection of traditional stories,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> understood to be false. It came eventually to be applied to similar bodies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traditional_stories" title="Traditional stories">traditional stories</a> among other <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheistic</a> cultures around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oedmlogy_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-oedmlogy-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thus the word <i>mythology</i> entered the English language before the word "myth"; <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Johnson</a>\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johnson%27s_Dictionary" title="Johnson\'s Dictionary">Dictionary</a></i>, for example, has an entry for <i>mythology</i>, but not for <i>myth</i>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Indeed, the Greek loanword <i>mythos</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> (pl. <i>mythoi</i>) and Latinate <i>mythus</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> (pl. <i>mythi</i>) both appeared in English before the first example of <i>myth</i> in 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meanings_in_Ancient_Greece">Meanings in Ancient Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Meanings in Ancient Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term &#956;&#8166;&#952;&#959;&#962; (<i>mythos</i>) appears in the works of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> and other poets of Homer\'s era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-59">[59]</a></sup> In these works, the term had several meanings: conversation, narrative, speech, story, tale, and word.\n</p><p>Like the related term &#955;&#972;&#947;&#959;&#962; (<i><a href="/wiki/Logos" title="Logos">logos</a></i>), <i>mythos</i> expresses whatever can be delivered in the form of words; these can be contrasted with  &#7956;&#961;&#947;&#959;&#957; (<i>ergon</i>), a Greek term for action, deed, and work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-59">[59]</a></sup> The term <i>mythos</i> lacks an explicit distinction between true or false narratives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-59">[59]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the context of the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_ancient_Greece" title="Theatre of ancient Greece">theatre of ancient Greece</a>, the term <i>mythos</i> referred to the myth, the narrative, the plot, and the story of a play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup> According to David Wiles, the Greek term <i>mythos</i> in this era covered an entire spectrum of different meanings, from undeniable falsehoods to stories with religious and symbolic significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> (384&#8211;322 BCE), the spirit of a theatrical play was its <i>mythos</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup> The term <i>mythos</i> was also used for the source material of <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">Greek tragedy</a>. The tragedians of the era could draw inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, a body of "traditional storylines" which concerned <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">gods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hero" title="Hero">heroes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup> David Wiles observes that modern conceptions about Greek tragedy can be misleading. It is commonly thought that the ancient audience members were already familiar with the <i>mythos</i> behind a play, and could predict the outcome of the play. However, the Greek dramatists were not expected to faithfully reproduce traditional myths when <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_adaptation" title="Theatrical adaptation">adapting them for the stage</a>. They were instead recreating the myths and producing new versions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup> Storytellers like <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a> (c. 480&#8211;406 BCE) relied on <a href="/wiki/Suspense" title="Suspense">suspense</a> to excite their audiences. In one of his works, <a href="/wiki/Merope_(Messenia)" title="Merope (Messenia)">Merope</a> attempts to kill her son\'s murderer with an axe, unaware that the man in question is actually her son. According to an ancient description of audience reactions to this work, the audience members were genuinely unsure of whether she would commit <a href="/wiki/Filicide" title="Filicide">filicide</a> or she will be stopped in time. They rose to their feet in terror and caused an uproar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles_60-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p><p>David Wiles points that the traditional <i>mythos</i> of Ancient Greece, was primarily a part of its <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> of this era were a literate culture, but produced no <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">sacred texts</a>. There were no definitive or authoritative versions of myths recorded in texts and preserved forever in an unchanging form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles2_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2-61">[61]</a></sup> Instead multiple variants of myths were in circulation. These variants were adapted into songs, dances, poetry, and visual art. Performers of myths could freely reshape their source material for a new work, adapting it to the needs of a new audience or in response to a new situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles2_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Children in Ancient Greece were familiar with traditional myths from an early age. According to the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> (c. 428&#8211;347 BCE), mothers and <a href="/wiki/Nursemaid" title="Nursemaid">nursemaids</a> narrated myths and stories to the children in their charge: David Wiles describes them as a repository of mythological lore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiles2_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wiles2-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lincoln" title="Bruce Lincoln">Bruce Lincoln</a> has called attention to the apparent meaning of the terms <i>mythos</i> and <i>logos</i> in the works of <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>. In <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i>, Hesiod attributes to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muse" title="Muse">Muses</a> the ability to both proclaim truths and narrate plausible falsehoods (falsehoods which seem like real things).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup> The verb used for narrating the falsehoods in the text is <i>legein</i>, which is etymologically associated with <i>logos</i>. There are two variants in the manuscript tradition for the verb used to proclaim truths. One variant uses <i>gerusasthai</i>, the other <i>mythesasthai</i>. The latter is a form of the verb <i>mytheomai</i> (to speak, to tell), which is etymologically associated with <i>mythos</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup> In the <i><a href="/wiki/Works_and_Days" title="Works and Days">Works and Days</a></i>, Hesiod describes his dispute with his brother Perses. He also announces to his readers his intention to tell true things to his brother. The verb he uses for telling the truth is <i>mythesaimen</i>, another form of <i>mytheomai</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lincoln draws the conclusion that Hesiod associated the "speech of <i>mythos</i>" (as Lincoln calls it) with telling the <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a>. While he associated the "speech of <i>logos</i>" with telling <a href="/wiki/Lie" title="Lie">lies</a>, and hiding one\'s true thoughts (dissimulation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup> This conclusion is strengthened by the use of the plural term <i>logoi</i> (the plural form of <i>logos</i>) elsewhere in Hesiod\'s works. Three times the term is associated with the term "<a href="/wiki/Seduction" title="Seduction">seductive</a>" and three times with the term "falsehoods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup> In his genealogy of the gods, Hesiod lists <i>logoi</i> among the children of <a href="/wiki/Eris_(mythology)" title="Eris (mythology)">Eris</a>, the goddess personifying strife. Eris\' children are ominous figures, which personify various physical and verbal forms of conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincoln_62-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lincoln-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpreting_myths">Interpreting myths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Interpreting myths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_mythology">Comparative mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Comparative mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">Comparative mythology</a></div>\n<p>Comparative mythology is the systematic comparison of myths from different cultures. It seeks to discover underlying themes that are common to the myths of multiple cultures. In some cases, comparative mythologists use the similarities between separate mythologies to argue that those mythologies have a common source. This source may inspire myths or provide a common "protomythology" that diverged into the mythologies of each culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELittleton197332_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELittleton197332-63">[63]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functionalism">Functionalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Functionalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of commentators have argued that myths function to form and shape society and social behaviour. <a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Eliade</a> argued that one of the foremost functions of myth is to establish models for behavior<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade19988_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade19988-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198451_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198451-65">[65]</a></sup> and that myths may provide a religious experience. By telling or reenacting myths, members of traditional societies detach themselves from the present, returning to the mythical age, thereby coming closer to the divine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199823_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199823-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198451_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198451-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEliade199819_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEliade199819-66">[66]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lauri_Honko" title="Lauri Honko">Honko</a> asserted that, in some cases, a society reenacts a myth in an attempt to reproduce the conditions of the mythical age. For example, it might reenact the healing performed by a god at the beginning of time in order to heal someone in the present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HONKO-1984_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HONKO-1984-8">[8]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">Barthes</a> argued that modern culture explores religious experience. Since it is not the job of science to define human morality, a religious experience is an attempt to connect with a perceived moral past, which is in contrast with the technological present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarthes1972_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarthes1972-67">[67]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Devdutt_Pattanaik" title="Devdutt Pattanaik">Pattanaik</a> defines mythology as "a subjective truth of people that is communicated through stories, symbols and rituals". He adds, "unlike fantasy that is nobody&#8217;s truth, and history that seeks to be everybody&#8217;s truth, mythology is somebody&#8217;s truth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PD2016_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-PD2016-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euhemerism">Euhemerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Euhemerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">Euhemerism</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a></div>\n<p>One theory claims that myths are distorted accounts of historical events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup> According to this theory, storytellers repeatedly elaborate upon historical accounts until the figures in those accounts gain the status of gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup> For example, the myth of the wind-god <a href="/wiki/Aeolus" title="Aeolus">Aeolus</a> may have evolved from a historical account of a king who taught his people to use sails and interpret the winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194_69-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004194-69">[69]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> (fifth-century BCE) and <a href="/wiki/Prodicus" title="Prodicus">Prodicus</a> made claims of this kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup> This theory is named <i><a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">euhemerism</a></i> after mythologist <a href="/wiki/Euhemerus" title="Euhemerus">Euhemerus</a> (c. 320 BCE), who suggested that Greek gods developed from legends about human beings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegory">Allegory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Allegory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some theories propose that myths began as allegories for natural phenomena: <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> represents the sun, <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> represents water, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup> According to another theory, myths began as allegories for philosophical or spiritual concepts: <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> represents wise judgment, <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a> desire, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHonko198445_70-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHonko198445-70">[70]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max M&#252;ller">M&#252;ller</a> supported an allegorical theory of myth. He believed myths began as allegorical descriptions of nature and gradually came to be interpreted literally. For example, a poetic description of the sea as "raging" was eventually taken literally and the sea was then thought of as a raging god.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201520_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201520-72">[72]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personification">Personification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Personification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeic_thought" title="Mythopoeic thought">Mythopoeic thought</a></div>\n<p>Some thinkers claimed that myths result from the <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">personification</a> of objects and forces. According to these thinkers, the ancients worshiped natural phenomena, such as fire and air, gradually deifying them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004195_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBulfinch2004195-73">[73]</a></sup> For example, according to this theory, ancients tended to view things as gods, not as mere objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen20134_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen20134-74">[74]</a></sup> Thus, they described natural events as acts of personal gods, giving rise to myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen201315_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrankfortFrankfortWilsonJacobsen201315-75">[75]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth-ritual_theory">Myth-ritual theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Myth-ritual theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Myth_and_ritual" title="Myth and ritual">Myth and ritual</a></div>\n<p>According to the myth-ritual theory, myth is tied to ritual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201561_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201561-76">[76]</a></sup> In its most extreme form, this theory claims myths arose to explain rituals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraf199640_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraf199640-77">[77]</a></sup> This claim was first put forward by <a href="/wiki/William_Robertson_Smith" title="William Robertson Smith">Smith</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeletinsky201419&#8211;20_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeletinsky201419&#8211;20-78">[78]</a></sup> who argued that people begin performing rituals for reasons not related to myth. Forgetting the original reason for a ritual, they account for it by inventing a myth and claiming the ritual commemorates the events described in that myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201563_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201563-79">[79]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Frazer" title="James Frazer">Frazer</a> claimed that humans started out with a belief in magical rituals; later, they began to lose faith in magic and invented myths about gods, reinterpreting their rituals as religious rituals intended to appease the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711-80">[80]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_academic_discipline">History of the academic discipline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History of the academic discipline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically, important approaches to the study of mythology have included those of <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">Vico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Joseph_Schelling" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling">Schelling</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schiller" title="Schiller">Schiller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jung</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freud" title="Freud">Freud</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9vy-Bruhl" title="L&#233;vy-Bruhl">L&#233;vy-Bruhl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="L&#233;vi-Strauss">L&#233;vi-Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Frye" title="Northrop Frye">Frye</a>, the Soviet school, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_and_Ritual_School" title="Myth and Ritual School">Myth and Ritual School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greece">Ancient Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ancient Greece">edit</a><span critical interpretation of myth began with the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Socratic_philosophy" title="Pre-Socratic philosophy">Presocratics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20151_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20151-82">[82]</a></sup> Euhemerus was one of the most important pre-modern mythologists. He interpreted myths as accounts of actual historical events &#8211; distorted over many retellings. Sallustius<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> divided myths into five categories &#8211; theological, physical (or concerning natural laws), animistic (or concerning soul), material, and mixed. Mixed concerns myths that show the interaction between two or more of the previous categories and are particularly used in initiations.\n</p><p>Plato famously condemned poetic myth when discussing education in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Republic_(Plato)" title="The Republic (Plato)">Republic</a>.</i> His critique was primarily on the grounds that the uneducated might take the stories of gods and heroes literally. Nevertheless, he constantly referred to myths throughout his writings. As <a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonism</a> developed in the phases commonly called <a href="/wiki/Middle_Platonism" title="Middle Platonism">Middle Platonism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoplatonism" title="Neoplatonism">neoplatonism</a>, writers such as <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher)" title="Porphyry (philosopher)">Porphyry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proclus" title="Proclus">Proclus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympiodorus_the_younger" title="Olympiodorus the younger">Olympiodorus,</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damascius" title="Damascius">Damascius</a> wrote explicitly about the symbolic interpretation of traditional and Orphic myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mythological themes were consciously employed in literature, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>. The resulting work may expressly refer to a mythological background without itself becoming part of a body of myths (<a href="/wiki/Cupid_and_Psyche" title="Cupid and Psyche">Cupid and Psyche</a>). Medieval romance in particular plays with this process of turning myth into literature. <i>Euhemerism</i>, as stated earlier, refers to the rationalization of myths, putting themes formerly imbued with mythological qualities into pragmatic contexts. An example of this would be following a cultural or religious paradigm shift (notably the re-interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> mythology following <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianization</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Renaissance">European Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: European Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bartolomeo_di_Giovanni_-_The_Myth_of_Io_-_Walters_37421.jpg"><img alt="The ancient Roman poet Ovid, in his &quot;The Metamorphoses,&quot; told the story of the nymph Io who was seduced by Jupiter, the king of the gods. This movement drew European scholars\' attention not only to Classical myths, but also material now associated with <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_mythology" title="Finnish mythology">Finnish mythology</a>, and so forth. Western theories were also partly driven by Europeans\' efforts to comprehend and control the cultures, stories and religions they were encountering through <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>. These encounters included both extremely old texts such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigveda</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>, and current oral narratives such as <a href="/wiki/Mythologies_of_the_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Mythologies of the indigenous peoples of the Americas">mythologies of the indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> or stories told in <a href="/wiki/Traditional_African_religions" title="Traditional African religions">traditional African religions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shippey_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-shippey-87">[87]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The intellectual context for nineteenth-century scholars was profoundly shaped by emerging ideas about <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>. These ideas included the recognition that many Eurasian languages&#8212;and therefore, conceivably, stories&#8212;were all descended from a lost common ancestor (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language" title="Indo-European language">Indo-European language</a>) which could rationally be reconstructed through the comparison of its descendant languages. They also included the idea that <a href="/wiki/Cultural_evolution" title="Cultural evolution">cultures might evolve</a> in ways comparable to species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shippey_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-shippey-87">[87]</a></sup> In general, nineteenth-century theories framed myth as a failed or obsolete mode of thought, often by interpreting myth as the primitive counterpart of modern science within a <a href="/wiki/Unilineal_evolution" title="Unilineal evolution">unilineal</a> framework that imagined that human cultures are travelling, at different speeds, along a linear path of cultural development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153&#8211;4_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153&#8211;4-88">[88]</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the dominant mythological theories of the later nineteenth century was "nature mythology", whose foremost exponents included <a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max M&#252;ller">Max M&#252;ller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burnett_Tylor" title="Edward Burnett Tylor">Edward Burnett Tylor</a>. This theory posited that "primitive man" was primarily concerned with the natural world. It tended to interpret myths that seemed distasteful European Victorians&#8212;for example tales about sex, incest, or cannibalism&#8212;as being metaphors for natural phenomena like agricultural fertility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Unable to conceive impersonal natural laws, early humans tried to explain natural phenomena by attributing souls to inanimate objects, giving rise to <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animism</a>. According to Tylor, human thought evolved through stages, starting with mythological ideas and gradually progressing to scientific ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20154_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20154-90">[90]</a></sup> M&#252;ller also saw myth arising from language, even calling myth a "disease of language". He speculated that myths arose due to the lack of abstract nouns and neuter gender in ancient languages. Anthropomorphic figures of speech, necessary in such languages, were eventually taken literally, leading to the idea that natural phenomena were in actuality conscious beings or gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201520_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201520-72">[72]</a></sup> Not all scholars, not even all nineteenth-century scholars, accepted this view. <a href="/wiki/Lucien_L%C3%A9vy-Bruhl" title="Lucien L&#233;vy-Bruhl">Lucien L&#233;vy-Bruhl</a> claimed "the primitive mentality is a condition of the human mind, and not a stage in its historical development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Recent scholarship, noting the fundamental lack of evidence for "nature mythology" interpretations among people who actually circulated myths, has likewise abandoned the key ideas of "nature mythology".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_George_Frazer" title="James George Frazer">James George Frazer</a> saw myths as a misinterpretation of magical rituals, which were themselves based on a mistaken idea of natural law: this idea was central to the "<a href="/wiki/Myth_and_ritual" title="Myth and ritual">myth and ritual</a>" school of thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal201567&#8211;68_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal201567&#8211;68-94">[94]</a></sup> According to Frazer, humans begin with an unfounded belief in impersonal magical laws. When they realize applications of these laws do not work, they give up their belief in natural law in favor of a belief in personal gods controlling nature, thus giving rise to religious myths. Meanwhile, humans continue practicing formerly magical rituals through force of habit, reinterpreting them as reenactments of mythical events. Finally humans come to realize nature follows natural laws, and they discover their true nature through science. Here again, science makes myth obsolete as humans progress "from magic through religion to science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrazer1913711-80">[80]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Segal asserted that by pitting mythical thought against modern scientific thought, such theories imply modern humans must abandon myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153-95">[95]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3101" data-file-width="1843" decoding="async" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg/170px-Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg/255px-Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg/340px-Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prometheus_by_Gustave_Moreau.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a></i> (1868) by <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Moreau" title="Gustave Moreau">Gustave Moreau</a>. In the mythos of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hesiodus" title="Hesiodus">Hesiodus</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_mythos" title="Greek mythos">Greek</a> trilogy <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Bound" title="Prometheus Bound">Prometheus Bound</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Unbound_(Aeschylus)" title="Prometheus Unbound (Aeschylus)">Prometheus Unbound</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prometheus_Pyrphoros" title="Prometheus Pyrphoros">Prometheus Pyrphoros</a></i>), Prometheus is bound and tortured for giving fire to humanity</div></div></div>\n<p>The earlier twentieth century saw major work developing <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalytical</a> approaches to interpreting myth, led by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>, who, drawing inspiration from Classical myth, began developing the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Oedipus_complex" title="Oedipus complex">Oedipus complex</a> in his 1899 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Interpretation_of_Dreams" title="The Interpretation of Dreams">The Interpretation of Dreams</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jung</a> likwise tried to understand the psychology behind world myths. Jung asserted that all humans share certain innate unconscious psychological forces, which he called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archetypes" title="Archetypes">archetypes</a></i>. He believed similarities between the myths of different cultures reveals the existence of these universal archetypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mid-twentieth century saw the influential development of a <a href="/wiki/Structuralist_theory_of_mythology" title="Structuralist theory of mythology">structuralist theory of mythology</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude L&#233;vi-Strauss">L&#233;vi-Strauss</a>. Strauss argued that myths reflect patterns in the mind and interpreted those patterns more as fixed mental structures, specifically pairs of opposites (good/evil, compassionate/callous), rather than unconscious feelings or urges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal2015113_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal2015113-97">[97]</a></sup> Meanwhile, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronislaw_Malinowski" title="Bronislaw Malinowski">Bronislaw Malinowski</a> developed analyses of myths focusing on their social functions in the real world. He is associated with the idea that myths such as <a href="/wiki/Origin_myth" title="Origin myth">origin stories</a> might provide a "mythic charter"&#8212;a legitimisation&#8212;for cultural norms and social institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Thus, following the Structuralist Era (roughly the 1960s to 1980s), the predominant anthropological and sociological approaches to myth increasingly treated myth as a form of narrative that can be studied, interpreted and analyzed like ideology, history and culture. In other words, myth is a form of understanding and telling stories that is connected to power, political structures, and political and economic interests. These approaches contrast with approaches such as those of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Campbell" title="Joseph Campbell">Joseph Campbell</a> and Eliade that hold that myth has some type of essential connection to ultimate sacred meanings that transcend cultural specifics. In particular, myth was studied in relation to history from diverse social sciences. Most of these studies share the assumption that history and myth are not distinct in the sense that history is factual, real, accurate, and truth, while myth is the opposite.\n</p><p>In the 1950s, Barthes published a series of essays examining modern myths and the process of their creation in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Mythologies_(book)" title="Mythologies (book)">Mythologies</a></i>, which stood as an early work in the emerging <a href="/wiki/Post-structuralism" title="Post-structuralism">post-structuralist</a> approach to mythology, which recognised myths\' existence in the modern world and in <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The twentieth century saw rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secularisation" title="Secularisation">secularisation</a> in Western culture. This made Western scholars more willing to analyse narratives in the <a href="/wiki/Abrahamic_religions" title="Abrahamic religions">Abrahamic religions</a> as myths; theologians such as <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Bultmann" title="Rudolf Bultmann">Rudolf Bultmann</a> argued that a modern Christianity needed to <a href="/wiki/Demythologization" title="Demythologization">demythologize</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> and other religious scholars embraced the idea that the mythical status of Abrahamic narratives was a legitimate feature of their importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESegal20153_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESegal20153-95">[95]</a></sup> This, in his appendix to <i>Myths, Dreams and Mysteries</i>, and in <i>The Myth of the Eternal Return</i>, <a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Eliade</a> attributed modern humans&#8217; anxieties to their rejection of myths and the sense of the sacred.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Christian theologian <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Hyers" title="Conrad Hyers">Conrad Hyers</a> wrote that\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...myth today has come to have negative connotations which are the complete opposite of its meaning in a religious context... In a religious context, however, myths are storied vehicles of supreme truth, the most basic and important truths of all. By them people regulate and interpret their lives and find worth and purpose in their existence. Myths put one in touch with sacred realities, the fundamental sources of being, power, and truth. They are seen not only as being the opposite of error but also as being clearly distinguishable from stories told for entertainment and from the workaday, domestic, practical language of a people. They provide answers to the mysteries of being and becoming, mysteries which, as mysteries, are hidden, yet mysteries which are revealed through story and ritual. Myths deal not only with truth but with ultimate truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHyers1984107_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHyers1984107-101">[101]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twenty-first_century">Twenty-first century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Twenty-first century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Both in nineteenth-century research that tended to see existing records of stories and folklore as imperfect fragments of partially lost myths, and in twentieth-century structuralist work that sought to identify underlying patterns and structures in often diverse versions of a given myth, there had been a tendency to synthesise sources to attempt to reconstruct what scholars supposed to be more perfect or underlying forms of myths. From the late twentieth century, however, researchers influenced by <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a> tended instead to argue that each account of a given myth has its own cultural significance and meaning, and argued that rather than representing degradation from a once more perfect form, myths are inherently plastic and variable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> There is, consequently, no such thing as the \'original version\' or \'original form\' of a myth. One prominent example of this movement was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.K._Ramanujan" title="A.K. Ramanujan">A.K. Ramanujan</a>\'s essay <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Hundred_Ramayanas" title="Three Hundred Ramayanas">Three Hundred Ramayanas</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Correspondingly, scholars challenged the precedence that had once been given to texts as a medium for mythology, arguing that other media, such as the visual arts or even landscape and place-naming, could be as or more important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_mythology">Modern mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Modern mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:10,000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1566" data-file-width="2634" decoding="async" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/220px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/330px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg/440px-10%2C000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:10,000_Belgian_francs_of_1929_edited.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1929 <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">banknote</a>, depicting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceres_(Roman_mythology)" title="Ceres (Roman mythology)">Ceres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neptune_(mythology)" title="Neptune (mythology)">Neptune</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">caduceus</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In modern society, myth is often regarded as a collection of stories. Scholars in the field of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> research how myth has worked itself into modern discourses. Mythological discourse can reach greater audiences than ever before via digital media. Various mythic elements appear in <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">cinema</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although myth was traditionally transmitted through the oral tradition on a small scale, the film industry has enabled filmmakers to transmit myths to large audiences via film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Jungian</a> psychology myths are the expression of a culture or society&#8217;s goals, fears, ambitions and dreams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The basis of modern visual storytelling is rooted in the mythological tradition. Many contemporary films rely on ancient myths to construct narratives. <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> is well-known among cultural study scholars for "reinventing" traditional childhood myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> While many films are not as obvious as Disney fairy tales, the plots of many films are based on the rough structure of myths. Mythological archetypes, such as the cautionary tale regarding the abuse of technology, battles between gods and creation stories, are often the subject of major film productions. These films are often created under the guise of <a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk" title="Cyberpunk">cyberpunk</a> <a href="/wiki/Action_film" title="Action film">action films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_literature" title="Apocalyptic literature">apocalyptic</a> tales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorner199947&#8211;59_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorner199947&#8211;59-109">[109]</a></sup>\n</p><p>21st-century films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Clash_of_the_Titans_(2010_film)" title="Clash of the Titans (2010 film)">Clash of the Titans</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Immortals_(2011_film)" title="Immortals (2011 film)">Immortals</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Thor_(film)" title="Thor (film)">Thor</a></i> continue the trend of mining traditional mythology to frame modern plots.
