searchaux" style="display:none">A cosmic ray particle with a kinetic energy greater than 1&#215;10<sup>18</sup> eV</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astroparticle_physics" title="Astroparticle physics">astroparticle physics</a>, an <b>ultra-high-energy cosmic ray</b> (<b>UHECR</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> with an energy greater than 1&#160;EeV (10<sup>18</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronvolts" title="Electronvolts">electronvolts</a>, approximately 0.16 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a>), far beyond both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> and energies typical of other cosmic ray particles.\n</p><p>An <b>extreme-energy cosmic ray</b> (<b>EECR</b>) is an UHECR with energy exceeding <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000801088243500000&#9824;</span>5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>19</sup>&#160;eV</span>  (about 8&#160;<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>), the so-called  <a href="/wiki/Greisen%E2%80%93Zatsepin%E2%80%93Kuzmin_limit" title="Greisen&#8211;Zatsepin&#8211;Kuzmin limit">Greisen&#8211;Zatsepin&#8211;Kuzmin limit</a> (GZK limit).  This limit should be the maximum energy of cosmic ray protons that have traveled long distances (about 160 million light years), since higher-energy protons would have lost energy over that distance due to scattering from photons in the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> (CMB). It follows that EECR could not be survivors from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_universe" title="Early universe">early universe</a>, but are cosmologically "young", emitted somewhere in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_Supercluster" title="Local Supercluster">Local Supercluster</a> by some unknown physical process. If an EECR is not a proton, but a nucleus with <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle A}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>A</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle A}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="A" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.743ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span> nucleons, then the GZK limit applies to its nucleons, which carry only a fraction <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle 1/A}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>/</mo>\n</mrow>\n<mi>A</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle 1/A}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="1/A" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cd62775834c956066187d9cd8b6af20170fc509" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:4.068ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span> of the total energy of the nucleus. For an iron nucleus, the corresponding limit would be <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002448609416360000&#9824;</span>2.8<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>21</sup>&#160;eV</span>. However, nuclear physics processes lead to limits for iron nuclei similar to that of protons. Other abundant nuclei have even much lower limits.\n</p><p>These particles are extremely rare; between 2004 and 2007, the initial runs of the <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a> (PAO) detected 27 events with estimated arrival energies above <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000913240597589999&#9824;</span>5.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>19</sup>&#160;eV</span>, i.e., about one such event every four weeks in the 3000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> area surveyed by the observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is evidence that these highest-energy cosmic rays might be <b>iron nuclei</b>, rather than the protons that make up most cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosmic-ray_theory_unravels_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cosmic-ray_theory_unravels-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The postulated (hypothetical) sources of EECR are known as <b>Zevatrons</b>, named in analogy to <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Bevatron" title="Bevatron">Bevatron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fermilab" title="Fermilab">Fermilab</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Tevatron" title="Tevatron">Tevatron</a>, and therefore capable of accelerating particles to 1 ZeV (10<sup>21</sup> eV, zetta-electronvolt).  In 2004 there was a consideration of the possibility of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_jet" title="Galactic jet">galactic jets</a> acting as Zevatrons, due to diffusive acceleration of particles caused by shock waves inside the jets. In particular, models suggested that shock waves from the nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy_M87" title="Elliptical galaxy M87">M87</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_jet" title="Galactic jet">galactic jet</a> could accelerate an iron nucleus to ZeV ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2007, the Pierre Auge Observatory oberved a correlation of EECR with extragalactic supermassive black holes at the center of nearby galaxies called <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nuclei (AGN)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PAO2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PAO2007-4">[4]</a></sup> \nHowever, the strength of the correlation became weaker while continuing observations. \nExtremely high energies might be explained also by the <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_mechanism_of_acceleration" title="Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration">Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in the magnetospheres of <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">AGN</a>. Although newer results indicate that fewer than 40% of these cosmic rays seemed to be coming from the AGN, a much weaker correlation than previously reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosmic-ray_theory_unravels_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cosmic-ray_theory_unravels-2">[2]</a></sup> A more speculative suggestion by Grib and Pavlov (2007, 2008) envisages the decay of superheavy <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>&#160;by means of the <a href="/wiki/Penrose_process" title="Penrose process">Penrose process</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Observational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_observatory" title="Cosmic ray observatory">Cosmic ray observatory</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle" title="Oh-My-God particle">Oh-My-God particle</a></div>\n<p>The first observation of a cosmic ray particle with an energy exceeding <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001160217648700000&#9824;</span>1.0<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>20</sup>&#160;eV</span> (16&#160;J) was made by Dr <a href="/wiki/John_Linsley" title="John Linsley">John D Linsley</a> and Livio Scarsi at the <a href="/wiki/Volcano_Ranch_experiment" title="Volcano Ranch experiment">Volcano Ranch experiment</a> in New Mexico in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cosmic ray particles with even higher energies have since been observed. Among them was the <a href="/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle" title="Oh-My-God particle">Oh-My-God particle</a> observed by the University of Utah\'s Fly\'s Eye experiment on the evening of 15 October 1991 over <a href="/wiki/Dugway_Proving_Ground" title="Dugway Proving Ground">Dugway Proving Ground</a>, Utah. Its observation was a shock to <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysicists</a>, who estimated its energy to be approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001512696475840000&#9824;</span>3.2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>20</sup>&#160;eV</span> (50&#160;J)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>&#8212;in other words, an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a> with kinetic energy equal to that of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_(object)" title="Baseball (object)">baseball</a> (5 ounces or 142 grams) traveling at about 100 kilometers per hour (60&#160;mph).\n</p><p>The energy of this particle is some 40 million times that of the highest energy protons that have been produced in any terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a>. However, only a small fraction of this energy would be available for an interaction with a proton or neutron on Earth, with most of the energy remaining in the form of kinetic energy of the products of the interaction (see <a href="/wiki/Collider#Explanation" title="Collider">Collider#Explanation</a>). The effective energy available for such a collision is the square root of double the product of the particle\'s energy and the mass energy of the proton, which for this particle gives <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996120163236525000&#9824;</span>7.5<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>14</sup>&#160;eV</span>, roughly 50 times the collision energy of the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a>.\n</p><p>Since the first observation, by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">University of Utah</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Fly%27s_Eye_Cosmic_Ray_Detector" title="High Resolution Fly\'s Eye Cosmic Ray Detector">Fly\'s Eye Cosmic Ray Detector</a>, at least fifteen similar events have been recorded, confirming the phenomenon. These very high energy cosmic ray particles are very rare; the energy of most cosmic ray particles is between 10&#160;MeV and 10&#160;GeV.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray_observatories">Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray observatories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray observatories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:High_energy_particle_telescopes" title="Category:High energy particle telescopes">Category:High energy particle telescopes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic-ray_observatory" title="Cosmic-ray observatory">Cosmic-ray observatory</a></div>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AGASA" title="AGASA">AGASA</a> &#8211; Akeno Giant Air Shower Array in Japan</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Impulse_Transient_Antenna" title="Antarctic Impulse Transient Antenna">Antarctic Impulse Transient Antenna</a> (ANITA) detects ultra-high-energy cosmic <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> believed to be caused by ultra-high-energy cosmic ray particles</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_Universe_Space_Observatory" title="Extreme Universe Space Observatory">Extreme Universe Space Observatory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/GRAPES-3" title="GRAPES-3">GRAPES-3</a> (Gamma Ray Astronomy PeV EnergieS 3rd establishment) is a project for cosmic ray study with air shower detector array and large area muon detectors at Ooty in southern India.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Fly%27s_Eye_Cosmic_Ray_Detector" title="High Resolution Fly\'s Eye Cosmic Ray Detector">High Resolution Fly\'s Eye Cosmic Ray Detector</a> (HiRes)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/MARIACHI" title="MARIACHI">MARIACHI</a> &#8211; Mixed Apparatus for Radar Investigation of Cosmic-rays of High Ionization located on Long Island, USA.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Telescope_Array_Project" title="Telescope Array Project">Telescope Array Project</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakutsk_Extensive_Air_Shower_Array" title="Yakutsk Extensive Air Shower Array">Yakutsk Extensive Air Shower Array</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tunka_experiment" title="Tunka experiment">Tunka experiment</a></li>\n<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=COSMICi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="COSMICi (page does not exist)">COSMICi</a> project at <a href="/wiki/Florida_A%26M_University" title="Florida A&amp;M University">Florida A&amp;M University</a> is developing technology for a distributed network of low-cost detectors for UHECR showers in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/MARIACHI" title="MARIACHI">MARIACHI</a>.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pierre_Auger_Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pierre Auger Observatory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Observatory</a></div>\n<p>Pierre Auger Observatory is an international cosmic ray observatory designed to detect ultra-high-energy cosmic ray particles (with energies beyond 10<sup>20</sup> eV). These high-energy particles have an estimated arrival rate of just 1 per square kilometer per century, therefore, in order to record a large number of these events, the Auger Observatory has created a detection area of 3,000&#160;km&#178; (the size of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, USA) in <a href="/wiki/Mendoza_Province" title="Mendoza Province">Mendoza Province</a>, western <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.\nThe Pierre Auger Observatory, in addition to obtaining directional information from the cluster of water tanks used to observe the cosmic-ray-shower components, also has four telescopes trained on the night sky to observe <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> molecules as the shower particles traverse the sky, giving further directional information on the original cosmic ray particle.\n</p><p>In September 2017, data from 12 years of observations from PAO supported an extragalactic source (e.g. outside of Earth\'s galaxy) for the origin of extremely high energy cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggested_explanations">Suggested explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Suggested explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutron_stars">Neutron stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neutron stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One suggested source of UHECR particles is their origination from <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a>.  In young neutron stars with spin periods of &lt;10ms, the <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) forces</a> from the quasi-neutral fluid of superconducting protons and electrons existing in a neutron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfluid" title="Superfluid">superfluid</a> accelerate iron nuclei to UHECR velocities.  The magnetic field produced by the neutron superfluid in rapidly rotating stars creates a magnetic field of 10<sup>8</sup>&#8211;10<sup>11</sup> tesla, at which point the neutron star is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Magnetar" title="Magnetar">magnetar</a>.  This magnetic field is the strongest in the observed universe and creates the relativistic MHD wind believed to accelerate iron nuclei remaining from the supernova to the necessary energy.\n</p><p>Another hypothesized source of UHECRs from neutron stars is during neutron star to <a href="/wiki/Strange_star" title="Strange star">strange star</a> combustion.  This hypothesis relies on the assumption that <a href="/wiki/Strange_matter" title="Strange matter">strange matter</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> of matter which has no experimental or observational data to support it.  Due to the immense gravitational pressures from the neutron star, it is believed that small pockets of matter consisting of <i>up</i>, <i>down</i>, and <i>strange</i> quarks in equilibrium acting as a single hadron (as opposed to a number of <a href="/wiki/Sigma_baryon" title="Sigma baryon"><span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>&#931;<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> baryons</a>).  This will then combust the entire star to strange matter, at which point the neutron star becomes a strange star and its magnetic field breaks down, which occurs because the protons and neutrons in the quasi-neutral fluid have become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strangelets" title="Strangelets">strangelets</a>.  This magnetic field breakdown releases large amplitude electromagnetic waves (LAEMWs).  The LAEMWs accelerate light ion remnants from the supernova to UHECR energies.\n</p><p>Very high energy cosmic ray electrons might be explained by the <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_mechanism_of_acceleration" title="Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration">Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration</a> in the magnetospheres of the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula#Crab_Pulsar" title="Crab Nebula">Crab</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">Pulsars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_galactic_cores">Active galactic cores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Active galactic cores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Interactions with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_shift" title="Blue shift">blue-shifted</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> limit the distance that these particles can travel before losing energy; this is known as the <a href="/wiki/Greisen%E2%80%93Zatsepin%E2%80%93Kuzmin_limit" title="Greisen&#8211;Zatsepin&#8211;Kuzmin limit">Greisen&#8211;Zatsepin&#8211;Kuzmin limit</a> or GZK limit.\n</p><p>The source of such high energy particles has been a mystery for many years. Recent results from the Pierre Auger Observatory show that ultra-high-energy cosmic ray arrival directions appear to be correlated with extragalactic supermassive black holes at the center of nearby galaxies called <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galactic nuclei (AGN)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PAO2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PAO2007-4">[4]</a></sup>\nHowever, since the angular correlation scale used is fairly large (3.1 degrees) these results do not unambiguously identify the origins of such cosmic ray particles. The AGN could merely be closely associated with the actual sources, for example in galaxies or other astrophysical objects that are clumped with matter on large scales within 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaparsec" title="Megaparsec">Mpc</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Some of the <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black holes</a> in AGN are known to be rotating, as in the <a href="/wiki/Seyfert_galaxy" title="Seyfert galaxy">Seyfert galaxy</a> MCG 6-30-15<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with time-variability in their inner accretion disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Black hole spin is a potentially effective agent to drive UHECR production,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> provided ions are suitably launched to circumvent limiting factors deep within the galactic nucleus, notably curvature radiation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and inelastic scattering with radiation from the inner disk. Low-luminosity, intermittent Seyfert galaxies may meet the requirements with the formation of a linear accelerator several light years away from the nucleus, yet within their extended ion tori whose UV radiation ensures a supply of ionic contaminants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The corresponding electric fields are small, on the order of 10 V/cm, whereby the observed UHECRs are indicative for the astronomical size of the source. Improved statistics by the Pierre Auger Observatory will be instrumental in identifying the presently tentative association of UHECRs (from the Local Universe) with Seyferts and <a href="/wiki/Low-ionization_nuclear_emission-line_region" title="Low-ionization nuclear emission-line region">LINERs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_possible_sources_of_the_particles">Other possible sources of the particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other possible sources of the particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Other possible sources of the UHECR are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>radio lobes of powerful <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a></li>\n<li>intergalactic shocks created during the epoch of galaxy formation</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypernova" title="Hypernova">hypernovae</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007PhRvD..76h3009W_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-2007PhRvD..76h3009W-17">[17]</a></sup></li>\n<li>relativistic <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011NatCo...2E.175C_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011NatCo...2E.175C-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1995PhRvL..75..386W_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-1995PhRvL..75..386W-19">[19]</a></sup><sup href="/wiki/Penrose_process" title="Penrose process">Penrose effect</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Preon_star" title="Preon star">Preon stars</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phys.Lett.B616,1(2005_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phys.Lett.B616,1(2005-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_dark_matter">Relation with dark matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Relation with dark matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">Dark matter</a></div>\n<p>It is hypothesized that active galactic nuclei are capable of converting dark matter into high energy protons. Yuri Pavlov and Andrey Grib at the Alexander Friedmann Laboratory for Theoretical Physics at St. Petersburg hypothesize that dark matter particles are about 15 times heavier than protons, and that they can decay into pairs of heavier virtual particles of a type that interacts with ordinary matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Near an active galactic nucleus, one of these particles can fall into the black hole, while the other escapes, as described by the <a href="/wiki/Penrose_process" title="Penrose process">Penrose process</a>. Some of those particles will collide with incoming particles; these are very high energy collisions which, according to Pavlov, can form ordinary visible protons with very high energy. Pavlov then claims that evidence of such processes are ultra-high-energy cosmic ray particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Ultra-high energy cosmic ray particles may also be produced by the decay of super-heavy dark matter "X particles"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Holeum" title="Holeum">Holeums</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Such very energetic decay products, carrying a fraction of the mass of the X particle, are believed to be a plausible explanation for the observed ultra-high energy cosmic rays (UHECR).\n</p><p>High energy cosmic ray particles traversing intergalactic space suffer the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GZK_cutoff" title="GZK cutoff">GZK cutoff</a> above 10<sup>20</sup> eV due to interactions with cosmic background radiation if the primary cosmic ray particles are protons or nuclei. The <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory" title="Pierre Auger Observatory">Pierre Auger Project</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Fly%27s_Eye_Cosmic_Ray_Detector" title="High Resolution Fly\'s Eye Cosmic Ray Detector">HiRes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yakutsk_Extensive_Air_Shower_Array" title="Yakutsk Extensive Air Shower Array">Yakutsk Extensive Air Shower Array</a> found the GZK cutoff, while Akeno-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AGASA" title="AGASA">AGASA</a> observed the events above the cutoff (11 events in the past 10 years). The result of the Akeno-AGASA experiment is smooth near the GZK cutoff energy. If one assumes that the Akeno-AGASA result is correct and consider its implication, a possible explanation for the AGASA data on GZK cutoff violation would be a shower caused by dark matter particles. A dark matter particle is not constrained by the GZK cutoff, since it interacts weakly with cosmic background radiation. Recent measurements by the Pierre Auger Project have found a correlation between the direction of high energy cosmic ray particles and the location of AGN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Extragalactic_cosmic_ray" title="Extragalactic cosmic ray"> Extragalactic cosmic ray</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle" title="Oh-My-God particle"> Oh-My-God particle</a>&#160;&#8211; An unexpectedly ultra-high-energy cosmic ray</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">\n<cite class="citation journal">Watson, L.
