Shown for comparison is the classical <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Jeans_law" title="Rayleigh&#8211;Jeans law">Rayleigh&#8211;Jeans law</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_catastrophe" title="Ultraviolet catastrophe">ultraviolet catastrophe</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>black body</b> or <b>blackbody</b> is an idealized physical body that absorbs all incident <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, regardless of frequency or angle of incidence.  A <b>white body</b> is one with a "rough surface [that] reflects all incident rays completely and uniformly in all directions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A black body in <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">thermal equilibrium</a> (that is, at a constant temperature) emits electromagnetic radiation called <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>. The radiation is emitted according to <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck\'s law">Planck\'s law</a>, meaning that it has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">spectrum</a> that is determined by the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> alone (see figure at right), not by the body\'s shape or composition.\n</p><p>An ideal black body in thermal equilibrium has two notable properties:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Massoud_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Massoud-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>It is an ideal emitter: at every frequency, it emits as much or more thermal radiative energy as any other body at the same temperature.</li>\n<li>It is a diffuse emitter: the energy is radiated <a href="/wiki/Isotropic_radiator" title="Isotropic radiator">isotropically</a>, independent of direction.</li></ol>\n<p>An approximate realization of a black surface is a hole in the wall of a large insulated enclosure (an <a href="/wiki/Oven" title="Oven">oven</a>). Any light entering the hole is reflected or absorbed at the internal surfaces of the body and is unlikely to re-emerge, making the hole a nearly perfect absorber. When the radiation confined in such an enclosure is in thermal equilibrium, the radiation emitted from the hole will be as great as from any body at that equilibrium temperature.\n</p><p>Real materials emit energy at a fraction&#8212;called the <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a>&#8212;of black-body energy levels. By definition, a black body in thermal equilibrium has an emissivity of <span class="nowrap"><i>&#949;</i> = 1.0</span>. A source with lower emissivity independent of frequency often is referred to as a <b>gray body</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emissivity_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-emissivity-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gray_body_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gray_body-4">[4]</a></sup>\nConstruction of black bodies with emissivity as close to one as possible remains a topic of current interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chun_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chun-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the radiation from <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> is sometimes characterized in terms of an <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a>, the temperature of a black body that would emit the same total flux of electromagnetic energy.\n</p>\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The idea of a black body originally was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> in 1860 as follows:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...the supposition that bodies can be imagined which, for infinitely small thicknesses, completely absorb all incident rays, and neither reflect nor transmit any. I shall call such bodies <i>perfectly black</i>, or, more briefly, <i>black bodies</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirchhoff_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirchhoff-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>A more modern definition drops the reference to "infinitely small thicknesses":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-infinitesimal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-infinitesimal-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>An ideal body is now defined, called a <i>blackbody</i>. A <i>blackbody</i> allows <i>all</i> incident radiation to pass into it (no reflected energy) and internally absorbs <i>all</i> the incident radiation (no energy transmitted through the body). This is true for radiation of all wavelengths and for all angles of incidence. Hence the blackbody is <i>a perfect absorber for all incident radiation.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Idealizations">Idealizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Idealizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This section describes some concepts developed in connection with black bodies.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_body_realization.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="437" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Black_body_realization.svg/220px-Black_body_realization.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Black_body_realization.svg/330px-Black_body_realization.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Black_body_realization.svg/440px-Black_body_realization.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_body_realization.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An approximate realization of a black body as a tiny hole in an insulated enclosure</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cavity_with_a_hole">Cavity with a hole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cavity with a hole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A widely used model of a black surface is a small hole in a cavity with walls that are opaque to radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel-8">[8]</a></sup>  Radiation incident on the hole will pass into the cavity, and is very unlikely to be re-emitted if the cavity is large.  The hole is not quite a perfect black surface &#8212; in particular, if the wavelength of the incident radiation is longer than the diameter of the hole, part will be reflected.  Similarly, even in perfect thermal equilibrium, the radiation inside a finite-sized cavity will not have an ideal Planck spectrum for wavelengths comparable to or larger than the size of the cavity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zee_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zee-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Suppose the cavity is held at a fixed temperature <i>T</i> and the radiation trapped inside the enclosure is at <a href="/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium" title="Thermal equilibrium">thermal equilibrium</a> with the enclosure. The hole in the enclosure will allow some radiation to escape. If the hole is small, radiation passing in and out of the hole has negligible effect upon the equilibrium of the radiation inside the cavity. This escaping radiation will approximate <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a> that exhibits a distribution in energy characteristic of the temperature <i>T</i> and does not depend upon the properties of the cavity or the hole, at least for wavelengths smaller than the size of the hole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zee_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zee-9">[9]</a></sup> See the figure in the Introduction for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum#Light" title="Frequency spectrum">spectrum</a> as a function of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">frequency</a> of the radiation, which is related to the energy of the radiation by the equation <i>E</i>=<i>hf</i>, with <i>E</i> = energy, <i>h</i> = <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck\'s constant</a>, <i>f</i> = frequency.\n</p><p>At any given time the radiation in the cavity may not be in thermal equilibrium, but <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">the second law of thermodynamics</a> states that if left undisturbed it will eventually reach equilibrium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adkins_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adkins-10">[10]</a></sup> although the time it takes to do so may be very long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batrouni_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batrouni-11">[11]</a></sup> Typically, equilibrium is reached by continual absorption and emission of radiation by material in the cavity or its walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landsberg_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landsberg-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planck_1914_44_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planck_1914_44-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Radiation entering the cavity will be "<a href="/wiki/H-theorem" title="H-theorem">thermalized</a>"; by this mechanism: the energy will be redistributed until the ensemble of photons achieves a <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck\'s law">Planck distribution</a>. The time taken for thermalization is much faster with condensed matter present than with rarefied matter such as a dilute gas.  At temperatures below billions of Kelvin, direct <a href="/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Heisenberg_Lagrangian" title="Euler&#8211;Heisenberg Lagrangian">photon&#8211;photon interactions</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karplus_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karplus-16">[16]</a></sup> are usually negligible compared to interactions with matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bergmann_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bergmann-17">[17]</a></sup> Photons are an example of an interacting <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">boson</a> gas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boson_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-boson-18">[18]</a></sup> and as described by the <a href="/wiki/H-theorem" title="H-theorem">H-theorem</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tolman_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tolman-19">[19]</a></sup> under very general conditions any interacting boson gas will approach thermal equilibrium.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Transmission.2C_absorption.2C_and_reflection"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission,_absorption,_and_reflection">Transmission, absorption, and reflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transmission, absorption, and reflection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A body\'s behavior with regard to thermal radiation is characterized by its transmission &#964;, absorption &#945;, and reflection &#961;.\n</p><p>The boundary of a body forms an interface with its surroundings, and this interface may be rough or smooth. A nonreflecting interface separating regions with different refractive indices must be rough, because the laws of reflection and refraction governed by the <a href="/wiki/Fresnel_equations" title="Fresnel equations">Fresnel equations</a> for a smooth interface require a reflected ray when the refractive indices of the material and its surroundings differ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tipler_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tipler-20">[20]</a></sup> A few idealized types of behavior are given particular names:\n</p><p>An opaque body is one that transmits none of the radiation that reaches it, although some may be reflected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaviany_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaviany-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venkanna_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venkanna-22">[22]</a></sup> That is, &#964;=0 and &#945;+&#961;=1\n</p><p>A transparent body is one that transmits all the radiation that reaches it. That is, &#964;=1 and &#945;=&#961;=0.\n</p><p>A gray body is one where &#945;, &#961; and &#964; are uniform for all wavelengths. This term also is used to mean a body for which &#945; is temperature and wavelength independent.\n</p><p>A white body is one for which all incident radiation is reflected uniformly in all directions: &#964;=0, &#945;=0, and &#961;=1.\n</p><p>For a black body, &#964;=0, &#945;=1, and &#961;=0. Planck offers a theoretical model for perfectly black bodies, which he noted do not exist in nature: besides their opaque interior, they have interfaces that are perfectly transmitting and non-reflective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Kirchhoff.27s_perfect_black_bodies"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kirchhoff\'s_perfect_black_bodies">Kirchhoff\'s perfect black bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kirchhoff\'s perfect black bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kirchhoff in 1860 introduced the theoretical concept of a perfect black body with a completely absorbing surface layer of infinitely small thickness, but Planck noted some severe restrictions upon this idea. Planck noted three requirements upon a black body: the body must (i) allow radiation to enter but not reflect; (ii) possess a minimum thickness adequate to absorb the incident radiation and prevent its re-emission; (iii) satisfy severe limitations upon <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a> to prevent radiation from entering and bouncing back out. As a consequence, Kirchhoff\'s perfect black bodies that absorb all the radiation that falls on them cannot be realized in an infinitely thin surface layer, and impose conditions upon scattering of the light within the black body that are difficult to satisfy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirchhoff_1860c_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirchhoff_1860c-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Realizations">Realizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Realizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A realization of a black body is a real world, physical embodiment. Here are a few.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cavity_with_a_hole_2">Cavity with a hole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cavity with a hole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1898, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lummer" title="Otto Lummer">Otto Lummer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Kurlbaum" title="Ferdinand Kurlbaum">Ferdinand Kurlbaum</a> published an account of their cavity radiation source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Their design has been used largely unchanged for radiation measurements to the present day. It was a hole in the wall of a platinum box, divided by diaphragms, with its interior blackened with iron oxide. It was an important ingredient for the progressively improved measurements that led to the discovery of Planck\'s law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rechenberg_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rechenberg-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A version described in 1901 had its interior blackened with a mixture of chromium, nickel, and cobalt oxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Hohlraum" title="Hohlraum">Hohlraum</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Near-black_materials">Near-black materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Near-black materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There is interest in blackbody-like materials for <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar-absorbent_material" title="Radar-absorbent material">radar-absorbent materials</a> for radar invisibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quinn_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quinn-31">[31]</a></sup> They also have application as solar energy collectors, and infrared thermal detectors. As a perfect emitter of radiation, a hot material with black body behavior would create an efficient infrared heater, particularly in space or in a vacuum where convective heating is unavailable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mizuno_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mizuno-32">[32]</a></sup> They are also useful in telescopes and cameras as anti-reflection surfaces to reduce stray light, and to gather information about objects in high-contrast areas (for example, observation of planets in orbit around their stars), where blackbody-like materials absorb light that comes from the wrong sources.\n</p><p>It has long been known that a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamp-black" title="Lamp-black">lamp-black</a> coating will make a body nearly black. An improvement on lamp-black is found in manufactured <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotubes</a>. Nano-porous materials can achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refractive_indices" title="Refractive indices">refractive indices</a> nearly that of vacuum, in one case obtaining average reflectance of 0.045%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chun_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chun-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yang_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yang-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2009, a team of Japanese scientists created a material called nanoblack which is close to an ideal black body, based on vertically aligned single-walled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotubes" title="Carbon nanotubes">carbon nanotubes</a>. This absorbs between 98% and 99% of the incoming light in the spectral range from the ultra-violet to the far-infrared regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mizuno_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mizuno-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other examples of nearly perfect black materials are <a href="/wiki/Super_black" title="Super black">super black</a>, prepared by chemically etching a <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>&#8211;<a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-34">[34]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Vantablack" title="Vantablack">vantablack</a> made of carbon nanotubes; both absorb 99.9% of light or more.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stars_and_planets">Stars and planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stars and planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For more about the UBV color index, see <a href="/wiki/Photometric_system" title="Photometric system">Photometric system</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Idealized_photosphere.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="333" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Idealized_photosphere.png/220px-Idealized_photosphere.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Idealized_photosphere.png/330px-Idealized_photosphere.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Idealized_photosphere.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Idealized_photosphere.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An idealized view of the cross-section of a star. The <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> contains <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> of light nearly in thermal equilibrium, and some escape into space as near-black-body radiation.</div></div></div>\n<p>A star or planet often is modeled as a black body, and electromagnetic radiation emitted from these bodies as <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>. The figure shows a highly schematic cross-section to illustrate the idea. The <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> of the star, where the emitted light is generated, is idealized as a layer within which the photons of light interact with the material in the photosphere and achieve a common temperature <i>T</i> that is maintained over a long period of time. Some photons escape and are emitted into space, but the energy they carry away is replaced by energy from within the star, so that the temperature of the photosphere is nearly steady. Changes in the core lead to changes in the supply of energy to the photosphere, but such changes are slow on the time scale of interest here. Assuming these circumstances can be realized, the outer layer of the star is somewhat analogous to the example of an enclosure with a small hole in it, with the hole replaced by the limited transmission into space at the outside of the photosphere. With all these assumptions in place, the star emits black-body radiation at the temperature of the photosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="499" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png/220px-Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png/330px-Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png/440px-Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Effective_temperature_and_color_index.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Effective temperature of a black body compared with the B-V and U-B color index of main sequence and super giant stars in what is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color-color_diagram" title="Color-color diagram">color-color diagram</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UBV_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-UBV-36">[36]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Using this model the <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> of stars is estimated, defined as the temperature of a black body that yields the same surface flux of energy as the star. If a star were a black body, the same effective temperature would result from any region of the spectrum. For example, comparisons in the <i>B</i> (blue) or <i>V</i> (visible) range lead to the so-called <i>B-V</i> <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">color index</a>, which increases the redder the star,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelley_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelley-37">[37]</a></sup> with the Sun having an index of +0.648 &#177; 0.006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-38">[38]</a></sup> Combining the <i>U</i> (ultraviolet) and the <i>B</i> indices leads to the <i>U-B</i> index, which becomes more negative the hotter the star and the more the UV radiation. Assuming the Sun is a type G2 V star, its <i>U-B</i> index is +0.12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golay_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golay-39">[39]</a></sup> The two indices for two types of most common star sequences are compared in the figure (diagram) with the effective surface temperature of the stars if they were perfect black bodies. There is a rough correlation. For example, for a given <i>B-V</i> index measurement, the curves of both most common sequences of star (the main sequence and the supergiants) lie below the corresponding black-body <i>U-B</i> index that includes the ultraviolet spectrum, showing that both groupings of star emit less ultraviolet light than a black body with the same <i>B-V</i> index. It is perhaps surprising that they fit a black body curve as well as they do, considering that stars have greatly different temperatures at different depths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aller_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aller-40">[40]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> has an effective temperature of 5780 K,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lang_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-41">[41]</a></sup> which can be compared to the temperature of its <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">photosphere</a> (the region generating the light), which ranges from about 5000 K at its outer boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromosphere</a> to about 9500 K at its inner boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">convection zone</a> approximately 500&#160;km (310&#160;mi) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bertotti_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bertotti-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_holes">Black holes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Black holes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hawking_radiation" title="Hawking radiation">Hawking radiation</a></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> is a region of <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> from which nothing escapes. Around a black hole there is a mathematically defined surface called an <a href="/wiki/Event_horizon" title="Event horizon">event horizon</a> that marks the point of no return. It is called "black" because it absorbs all the light that hits the horizon, reflecting nothing, making it almost an ideal black body<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schutz_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schutz-43">[43]</a></sup> (radiation with a wavelength equal to or larger than the radius of the hole may not be absorbed, so black holes are not perfect black bodies).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-44">[44]</a></sup> Physicists believe that to an outside observer, black holes have a non-zero temperature and emit radiation with a nearly perfect <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body</a> spectrum, ultimately <a href="/wiki/Black_hole#Evaporation" title="Black hole">evaporating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wald_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wald-45">[45]</a></sup> The mechanism for this emission is related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuum_fluctuations" title="Vacuum fluctuations">vacuum fluctuations</a> in which a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtual_particles" title="Virtual particles">virtual pair</a> of particles is separated by the gravity of the hole, one member being sucked into the hole, and the other being emitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carr_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carr-46">[46]</a></sup> The energy distribution of emission is described by <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck\'s law">Planck\'s law</a> with a temperature <i>T</i>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle T={\\frac {\\hbar c^{3}}{8\\pi Gk_{B}M}}\\ ,}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>T</mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mrow>\n<mi class="MJX-variant">&#8463;<!-- &#8463; According to theory, the Universe approximately a second after its formation was a near-ideal black body in thermal equilibrium at a temperature above 10<sup>10</sup> K. The temperature decreased as the Universe expanded and the matter and radiation in it cooled.  The cosmic microwave background radiation observed today is "the most perfect black body ever measured in nature".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-White-49">[49]</a></sup> It has a nearly ideal Planck spectrum at a temperature of about 2.7&#8201;K. 5.67 &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;8</sup> W/(m<sup>2</sup>K<sup>4</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-50">[50]</a></sup></span> To remain in thermal equilibrium at constant temperature <i>T</i>, the black body must absorb or internally generate this amount of <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> P over the given area A.\n</p><p>The cooling of a body due to thermal radiation is often approximated using the Stefan&#8211;Boltzmann law supplemented with a "gray body" <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> <span class="nowrap"><i>&#949; &#8804; 1</i></span> <span class="nowrap">(<i>P/A</i> = <i>&#949;&#963;T</i><sup>4</sup>).</span>  The rate of decrease of the temperature of the emitting body can be estimated from the power radiated and the body\'s <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson-51">[51]</a></sup> This approach is a simplification that ignores details of the mechanisms behind heat redistribution (which may include changing composition, <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transitions</a> or restructuring of the body) that occur within the body while it cools, and assumes that at each moment in time the body is characterized by a single temperature. It also ignores other possible complications, such as changes in the emissivity with temperature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vollmer_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vollmer-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Champion_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Champion-53">[53]</a></sup> and the role of other accompanying forms of energy emission, for example, emission of particles like neutrinos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shifman_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shifman-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p><p>If a hot emitting body is assumed to follow the Stefan&#8211;Boltzmann law and its power emission <i>P</i> and temperature <i>T</i> are known, this law can be used to estimate the dimensions of the emitting object, because the total emitted power is proportional to the area of the emitting surface. In this way it was found that X-ray bursts observed by astronomers originated in neutron stars with a radius of about 10&#160;km, rather than black holes as originally conjectured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewin_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewin-55">[55]</a></sup> It should be noted that an accurate estimate of size requires some knowledge of the emissivity, particularly its
