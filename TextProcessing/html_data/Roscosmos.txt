Corporation for Space Activities</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1043;&#1086;&#1089;&#1091;&#1076;&#1072;&#1088;&#1089;&#1090;&#1074;&#1077;&#1085;&#1085;&#1072;&#1103; &#1082;&#1086;&#1088;&#1087;&#1086;&#1088;&#1072;&#1094;&#1080;&#1103; &#1087;&#1086; &#1082;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1080;&#1095;&#1077;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1081; &#1076;&#1077;&#1103;&#1090;&#1077;&#1083;&#1100;&#1085;&#1086;&#1089;&#1090;&#1080; &#171;&#1056;&#1086;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1086;&#1089;&#187;</span>, <i>Gosudarstvyennaya korporaciya po kosmicheskoy dyeyatyel\'nosti "Roskosmos"</i>), commonly known as <b>Roscosmos</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1056;&#1086;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1086;&#1089;</span>), is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_corporation" title="State corporation">state corporation</a> responsible for the wide range and types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">space flights</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_space_agencies" title="List of space agencies">cosmonautics programs</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>. \n</p><p>Originally being a part of the Federal Space Agency (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1060;&#1077;&#1076;&#1077;&#1088;&#1072;&#1083;&#1100;&#1085;&#1086;&#1077; &#1082;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1080;&#1095;&#1077;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1077; &#1072;&#1075;&#1077;&#1085;&#1090;&#1089;&#1090;&#1074;&#1086;</span>, <i><i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">Federal\'noye kosmicheskoye agentstvo</i></i>), the corporation evolved and consolidated itself to the national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_corporation" title="State corporation">state corporation</a> on 28 December 2015 through a presidential decree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Before, since 1992, Roscosmos was known as the Russian Aviation and Space Agency (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1056;&#1086;&#1089;&#1089;&#1080;&#1081;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1077; &#1072;&#1074;&#1080;&#1072;&#1094;&#1080;&#1086;&#1085;&#1085;&#1086;-&#1082;&#1086;&#1089;&#1084;&#1080;&#1095;&#1077;&#1089;&#1082;&#1086;&#1077; &#1072;&#1075;&#1077;&#1085;&#1090;&#1089;&#1090;&#1074;&#1086;</span>, <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian language text">Rossiyskoe aviatsionno-kosmicheskoe agentstvo</i>, commonly known as <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian language text">Rosaviakosmos</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The headquarters of Roscosmos are located in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, while the main <a href="/wiki/RKA_Mission_Control_Center" title="RKA Mission Control Center">Mission Control space center</a> is located in the nearby city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korolyov_(city)" title="Korolyov (city)">Korolev</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin_Cosmonaut_Training_Center" title="Yuri Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Center">Yuri Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Center</a> is in <a href="/wiki/Star_City,_Russia" title="Star City, Russia">Star City</a>, also in <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Oblast" title="Moscow Oblast">Moscow Oblast</a>. The launch facilities used are <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> (with most launches taking place there, both manned and unmanned), and <a href="/wiki/Vostochny_Cosmodrome" title="Vostochny Cosmodrome">Vostochny Cosmodrome</a> being built in the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Far_East" title="Russian Far East">Russian Far East</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amur_Oblast" title="Amur Oblast">Amur Oblast</a>.\n</p><p>The current <a href="/wiki/List_of_Heads_of_Roscosmos" title="List of Heads of Roscosmos">director</a> since May 2018 is <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Rogozin" title="Dmitry Rogozin">Dmitry Rogozin</a>. In 2015 the Russian government merged Roscosmos with the <a href="/wiki/United_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation" title="United Rocket and Space Corporation">United Rocket and Space Corporation</a>, the re-nationalized <a href="/wiki/Space_industry_of_Russia" title="Space industry of Russia">Russian space industry</a>, to create the Roscosmos State Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div Instead, its organizational architecture was multi-centered; it was the design bureaus and the council of designers that had the most say, not the political leadership. The creation of a central agency after the separation of Russia from the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> was therefore a new development. The Russian Space Agency was formed on February 25, 1992, by a decree of President <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Yeltsin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Koptev" title="Yuri Koptev">Yuri Koptev</a>, who had previously worked with designing Mars landers at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Lavochkin" title="NPO Lavochkin">NPO Lavochkin</a>, became the agency\'s first director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvey-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the early years, the agency suffered from lack of <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> as the powerful design bureaus fought to protect their own spheres of operation and to survive. For example, the decision to keep Mir in operation beyond 1999 was not taken by the agency; instead, it was made by the private shareholder board of the Energia design bureau. Another example is that the decision to develop the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_rocket" title="Angara rocket">Angara rocket</a> was rather a function of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khrunichev" title="Khrunichev">Khrunichev</a>\'s ability to attract resources than a conscious long-term decision by the agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-harvey-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crisis_years">Crisis years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crisis years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 1990s saw serious financial problems because of decreased cash flow, which encouraged Roscosmos to improvise and seek other ways to keep space programs running. This resulted in Roscosmos\' leading role in commercial satellite launches and <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While scientific missions, such as interplanetary probes or astronomy missions during these years played a very small role, although Roscosmos has connections with Russian aerospace forces, its budget is not part of the defense budget of the country, Roscosmos managed to operate the space station <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> well past its planned lifespan, contribute to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, and continue to fly additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">Progress</a> missions.\n</p><p>In March 2004,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> director <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Koptev" title="Yuri Koptev">Yuri Koptev</a> was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Perminov" title="Anatoly Perminov">Anatoly Perminov</a>, who had previously served as the first commander of the Space Forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-harvey-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Improved_situation_in_2005.E2.80.932006"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Improved_situation_in_2005&#8211;2006">Improved situation in 2005&#8211;2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Improved situation in 2005&#8211;2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_economy" title="Russian economy">Russian economy</a> boomed throughout 2005 from high prices for exports, such as oil and gas, the outlook for future funding in 2006 appeared more favorable. Under the current 10-year budget approved, the budget of the Space Agency shall increase 5&#8211;10% per year, providing the space agency with a constant influx of money. In addition to the budget, Roscosmos plans to have over 130 billion rubles flowing into its budget by other means, such as industry investments and commercial space launches. Some observers have pointed out that this has a detrimental effect on other aspects of space exploration, and that the other space powers spend much lesser proportions of their overall budgets on maintaining human presence in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observer_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-observer-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the considerably improved budget,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> attention of legislative and executive authorities, positive media coverage and broad support among the population, the Russian space program continues to face several problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdb_industry_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-mdb_industry-15">[15]</a></sup> Wages in the space industry are low; the average age of employees is high (46 years in 2007),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mdb_industry_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-mdb_industry-15">[15]</a></sup> and much of the equipment is obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kislyakov_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-kislyakov-16">[16]</a></sup> On the positive side, many companies in the sector have been able to profit from contracts and partnerships with foreign companies; several new systems such as new rocket upper stages have been developed in recent years; investments have been made to production lines, and companies have started to pay more attention to educating a new generation of engineers and technicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvey_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-harvey-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kislyakov_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-kislyakov-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011:_New_director">2011: New director</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2011: New director">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 29 April 2011, Perminov was replaced with <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Popovkin" title="Vladimir Popovkin">Vladimir Popovkin</a> as the director of Roscosmos. The 65-year-old Perminov was over the legal age for state officials, and had received some criticism after a failed <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a> launch in December 2010. Popovkin is a former commander of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Forces" title="Russian Space Forces">Russian Space Forces</a> and First Deputy Defense Minister of Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rian_popovkin_appointment_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rian_popovkin_appointment-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mt_popovkin_appointment_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-mt_popovkin_appointment-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013-2015_reorganization_of_the_Russian_space_sector">2013-2015 reorganization of the Russian space sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2013-2015 reorganization of the Russian space sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As a result of a series of reliability problems, and proximate to the failure of a July 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_M" title="Proton M">Proton M</a> launch, a major reorganization of the Russian space industry was undertaken.  The <a href="/wiki/United_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation" title="United Rocket and Space Corporation">United Rocket and Space Corporation</a> was formed as a <a href="/wiki/Joint-stock_company" title="Joint-stock company">joint-stock</a> corporation by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">government</a> in August 2013 to consolidate the Russian <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">space</a> sector.  Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Rogozin" title="Dmitry Rogozin">Dmitry Rogozin</a> said "the failure-prone space sector is so troubled that it needs state supervision to overcome its problems."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20130830_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-pa20130830-19">[19]</a></sup> \nThree days following the Proton M launch failure, the Russian government had announced that "extremely harsh measures" would be taken "and spell the end of the [Russian] space industry as we know it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbth20130703_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbth20130703-20">[20]</a></sup>\nInformation indicated then that the government intended to reorganize in such a way as to "preserve and enhance the Roscosmos space agency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20130830_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-pa20130830-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>More detailed plans released in October 2013 called for a <a href="/wiki/Nationalization" title="Nationalization">re-nationalization</a> of the "troubled space industry," with sweeping reforms including a new "unified command structure and reducing redundant capabilities, acts that could lead to tens of thousands of layoffs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20131009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pa20131009-21">[21]</a></sup>  According to Rogozin, the Russian space sector employs about 250,000 people, while the United States needs only 70,000 to achieve similar results. He said: "Russian space productivity is eight times lower than America&#8217;s, with companies duplicating one another\'s work and operating at about 40 percent efficiency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20131009_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-pa20131009-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the 2013 plan, Roscosmos was to "act as a federal executive body and contracting authority for programs to be implemented by the industry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pa20130830_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-pa20130830-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_agency" title="State agency">state agency</a> was dissolved and the Roscosmos brand moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_corporation" title="State corporation">state corporation</a>, which had been created in 2013 as the <a href="/wiki/United_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation" title="United Rocket and Space Corporation">United Rocket and Space Corporation</a>, with the specific mission to renationalize the Russian space sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ti20151228_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ti20151228-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Russian</a> President Vladimir Putin said "it \'is necessary to drastically improve the quality and reliability of space and launch vehicles\' ... to preserve Russia&#8217;s increasingly threatened leadership in space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In November 2018 <a href="/wiki/Alexei_Kudrin" title="Alexei Kudrin">Alexei Kudrin</a>, head of Russian financial audit agency, named Roscosmos as the public enterprise with "the highest losses" due to "irrational spending" and outright <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">theft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_Russia" title="Corruption in Russia">corruption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_programs">Current programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orbital_Segment" title="Russian Orbital Segment">Russian Orbital Segment</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zarya_from_STS-88.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1984" data-file-width="1792" decoding="async" href="/wiki/File:Zarya_from_STS-88.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> module was the first module of the ISS, launched in 1998.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Russian Space Agency is one of the partners in the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS) program; it contributed the core space modules <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">Zvezda</a>, which were both launched by <a class="mw-redirect" The <a href="/wiki/Rassvet_(ISS_module)" title="Rassvet (ISS module)">Rassvet module</a> was launched aboard <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis" title="Space Shuttle Atlantis"><i>Atlantis</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and will be primarily used for cargo storage and as a docking port for visiting spacecraft. The <a href="/wiki/Nauka_(ISS_module)" title="Nauka (ISS module)">Nauka module</a> is the last component of the ISS, due to be launched in November 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russian_Launch_Manifest_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russian_Launch_Manifest-26">[26]</a></sup> Roscosmos is furthermore responsible for expedition crew launches by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz-TMA</a> spacecraft and resupplies the space station with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">Progress</a> space transporters. After the initial ISS contract with NASA expired, RKA and NASA, with the approval of the US government, entered into a space contract running until 2011, according to which Roscosmos will sell NASA spots on Soyuz spacecraft for approximately $21 million per person each way (thus $42 million to and back from the ISS per person) as well as provide Progress transport flights ($50 million per Progress as outlined in the <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Systems_Architecture_Study" title="Exploration Systems Architecture Study">Exploration Systems Architecture Study</a> study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>). RKA has announced that according to this arrangement, manned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz</a> flights will be doubled to 4 per year and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_spacecraft" title="Progress spacecraft">Progress</a> flights also doubled to 8 per year beginning in 2008.\n</p><p>RKA also provides <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a> for fare-paying passengers to ISS through the <a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures" title="Space Adventures">Space Adventures</a> company. As of 2009, six space tourists have contracted with Roscosmos and have flown into space, each for an estimated fee of at least $20 million (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_programs">Science programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Science programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_research_on_the_ISS" title="Scientific research on the ISS">Scientific research on the ISS</a></div>\n<p>RKA operates a number of programs for earth science, communication, and scientific research. Future projects include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz</a> successor, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prospective_Piloted_Transport_System" title="Prospective Piloted Transport System">Prospective Piloted Transport System</a>, scientific robotic missions to one of the <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> moons as well as an increase in Lunar orbit research satellites.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Luna-Glob" title="Luna-Glob">Luna-Glob</a> Moon orbiter with penetrators, planned in 2025</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Venera-D" title="Venera-D">Venera-D</a> Venus lander, planned in 2025</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">Fobos-Grunt</a> Mars mission, lost in low Earth orbit in 2012</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rockets">Rockets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rockets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Roscosmos uses a family of several launch rockets, the most famous of them being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-7_family" title="R-7 family">R-7</a>, commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz</a> rocket that is capable of launching about 7.5 tons into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit (LEO)</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton rocket</a> (or UR-500K) has a lift capacity of over 20 tons to LEO. Smaller rockets include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmos-3M" title="Cosmos-3M">Cosmos-3M</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rokot" title="Rokot">Rokot</a>, and other Stations.\n</p><p>Currently rocket development encompasses both a new rocket system, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_(rocket)" title="Angara (rocket)">Angara</a>, as well as enhancements of the Soyuz rocket, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz-2</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz-2-3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz-2-3 (page does not exist)">Soyuz-2-3</a>. Two modifications of the Soyuz, the Soyuz-2.1a and Soyuz-2.1b have already been successfully tested, enhancing the launch capacity to 8.5 tons to LEO.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_piloted_spacecraft">New piloted spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New piloted spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One of RKA\'s projects that was widely covered in the media in 2005 was <a href="/wiki/Kliper" title="Kliper">Kliper</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a> reusable spacecraft. While Roscosmos had reached out to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> and <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a> as well as others to share development costs of the project, it also stated that it will go forward with the project even without the support of other space agencies. This statement was backed by the approval of its budget for 2006&#8211;2015, which includes the necessary funding of <a href="/wiki/Kliper" title="Kliper">Kliper</a>. However, the Kliper program was cancelled in July 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flightglobal_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal-28">[28]</a></sup> and has been replaced by the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federatsiya_(spacecraft)" title="Federatsiya (spacecraft)">Federatsiya (Prospective Piloted Transport System)</a> project. As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> no crafts were launched.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_systems">Space systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Space systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>"Resurs-P"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> is a series of Russian commercial <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a> capable of acquiring high-resolution imagery (resolution up to 1.0 m). The spacecraft is operated by Roscosmos as a replacement of the <a href="/wiki/Resurs-DK_No.1" title="Resurs-DK No.1">Resurs-DK No.1</a> satellite.\n</p><p>Create HEO space system "Arctic" to address the hydrological and meteorological problems in the Arctic region and the northern areas of the Earth, with the help of two spacecraft "Arktika-M" and in the future within the system can create a communications satellite "Arktika-MS" and radar satellites "Arktika-R."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The launch of two satellites "Obzor-R" (Review-R) Remote Sensing of the Earth, with the AESA radar and four spacecraft "Obzor-O" (Review-O) to capture the Earth\'s surface in normal and infrared light in a broad swath of 80&#160;km with a resolution of 10 meters. The first two satellites of the projects planned for launch in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gonets" title="Gonets">Gonets</a>: Civilian low Earth orbit communication satellite system. On 2016, the system consists of 13 satellites (12 Gonets-M and 1 Gonets-D1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gecko_Mating_Experiment">Gecko Mating Experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gecko Mating Experiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Russian Federal Space Agency Roscosmos launched on 19 July 2014 the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Foton-M4_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Foton-M4 satellite (page does not exist)">Foton-M4 satellite</a> containing among other animals and plants, a group of five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geckos" title="Geckos">geckos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The five geckos, four females and one male, were used as a part of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gecko-F4&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gecko-F4 (page does not exist)">Gecko-F4</a> research program aimed at measuring the effects of weightlessness on the lizards&#8217; ability to procreate and develop in the harsh environment. However, soon after the spacecraft exited the atmosphere, mission control lost contact with the vessel which led to an attempt to reestablish communication that was only achieved later in the mission. When the satellite returned to earth after its planned two-month mission was cut short to 44 days, the geckos were reported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_agency" title="Space agency">space agency</a> researchers to have all perished during the course of their travels.\n</p><p>The exact cause that led to the deaths of the geckos was declared unknown by the scientific team in charge of the project. Reports from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_of_Medical_and_Biological_Problems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute of Medical and Biological Problems (page does not exist)">Institute of Medical and Biological Problems</a> in Russia have indicated that the lizards had been dead for at least a week prior to their return to earth. A number of those connected to the mission have theorized that a failure in the vessel&#8217;s heating system may have caused the cold blooded reptiles to freeze to death.\n</p><p>Included in the mission were a number of fruit flies, plants, and mushrooms which all survived the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_control">Launch control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roscosmos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Launch control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The military counterpart of the RKA is the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Aerospace_Defence_Forces" title="Russian Aerospace Defence Forces">Military Space Forces</a> (VKO). The RKA and VKO also share control of the <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin_Cosmonaut_Training_Center" title="Yuri Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Center">Yuri Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Center</a>. It has been announced that Russia is to build another spaceport in <a href="/wiki/Tsiolkovsky,_Amur_Oblast" title="Tsiolkovsky, Amur Oblast">Tsiolkovsky, Amur Oblast</a>.<sup class="reference"
