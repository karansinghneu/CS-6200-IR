class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Space Complex Alpha" redirects here. For the NASA space station design that evolved into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">ISS</a>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Station_Alpha" title="Space Station Alpha">Space Station Alpha</a>.</div>\n<p>The <b>Bigelow Next-Generation Commercial Space Station</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private</a> <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">orbital</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> currently under development by <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Aerospace" title="Bigelow Aerospace">Bigelow Aerospace</a>.  Previous concepts of the space station had included multiple modules such as two <a href="/wiki/B330" title="B330">B330</a> expandable spacecraft modules as well as a central <a href="/wiki/Docking_Compartment" title="Docking Compartment">docking node</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar arrays</a>, and attached <a href="/wiki/Space_capsule" title="Space capsule">crew capsules</a>. However it now appears that each B330 can operate as an independent space station. Attaching a B330 to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> or flying a B330 alone have been suggested by Robert Bigelow.\n</p><p>On 8 April 2016, NASA launched a <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Expandable_Activity_Module" title="Bigelow Expandable Activity Module">Bigelow inflatable module</a> and attached it to the ISS, where it has been tested for over two years. Any independent Bigelow Commercial space station will have to await the development of commercially available human rated orbital spacecraft. The first of these is expected to be the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> <a href="/wiki/Dragon_2" title="Dragon 2">Dragon 2</a> in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Two B330 are expected to be ready by 2020 and a launch contract for one in 2021 followed by move to low lunar orbit in 2022 has been signed.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Early work at Bigelow Aerospace on expandable space habitats, with plans to eventually assemble them into on-orbit space stations, began in the early years after the company was formed in 1999.  By 2004, plans made public included assembly of multiple modules "into a manned space facility in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> for both privately- and publicly-funded research and for <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr20040719_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sr20040719-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two more formal concepts have since been made public.  By 2005, Bigelow space station plans had been further conceptualized into <i>Commercial Space Station Skywalker</i>, or CSS Skywalker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20050301_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ps20050301-3">[3]</a></sup>  In mid-2010, Bigelow announced their <i>Next-Generation Commercial Space Station</i>&#8212;later named "Space Complex Alpha".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsr20101101_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsr20101101-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baocc20100715_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-baocc20100715-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The initial dates for the Alpha complex were not achieved. In January 2013, the Alpha complex was specified to be an in-space assemblage of only two <a href="/wiki/B330" title="B330">B330</a> modules, with the first module to be launched no earlier than 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CSS_Skywalker">CSS Skywalker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CSS Skywalker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><caption>CSS Skywalker</caption><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" (53,000&#160;cu&#160;ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ea20100706_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-ea20100706-7">[7]</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>CSS Skywalker</b> (<b>Commercial Space Station Skywalker</b>) was a 2005 concept for the first "space hotel" by Bigelow Aerospace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20050301_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ps20050301-3">[3]</a></sup>\nThe Skywalker was designed to be composed of multiple Nautilus (B330) habitat modules, which would be inflated and connected upon reaching orbit. An MDPM (Multi-Directional Propulsion Module) would allow the Skywalker to be moved into interplanetary or lunar trajectories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ea20100706_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-ea20100706-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In short, CSS Skywalker was "an effort to build the planet\'s first orbiting space hotel, [with a projected] room rate of USD$1 million per night", and a hoped-for launch date for the first Nautilus module of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20050301_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ps20050301-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_challenges">Company challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Company challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Early assessments of the probability of success of the technology development and challenges of a commercial space station pointed to the importance of factors largely beyond Bigelow\'s control.  For example, in 2005, John M. Logsdon, director of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a>\'s Space Policy Institute said "I have little doubt that the basic technology is likely to work ... The issue is whether there\'s a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_transport" title="Space transport">transportation</a> system that can get people or things, or both, up there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20050301_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ps20050301-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In practice, orbital launch plans were significantly delayed. First, after the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">Space Shuttle <i>Columbia</i> disaster</a> in 2003, Bigelow had to compete with <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> for rides on the <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> three-person rocket &#8212; "a distinctly untenable position."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20050301_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ps20050301-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc200041108_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdc200041108-8">[8]</a></sup> In mid-2009, Bigelow announced they were continuing to develop a variety of space habitat architectures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps20090814_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ps20090814-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_transport">Space transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Space transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2008, Bigelow initially began talks with <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> to potentially contract launch services on its <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>-401 vehicle for both crew and cargo launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ba20080205_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ba20080205-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ns20080207_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ns20080207-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By mid-2010, Bigelow was actively pursuing launch options for its space station modules and crew capsules from two launch systems: the <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CST-100" title="CST-100">CST-100</a> capsule on a <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">ULA</a> <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> launcher and also the <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">SpaceX Dragon</a> / <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> capsule/launcher combination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20100506_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw20100506-12">[12]</a></sup> "Bigelow offers Boeing, SpaceX, and other vehicle developers ... the promise of a sustained, large market for space transportation services."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsr20101101_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tsr20101101-4">[4]</a></sup>\nWith the initial Space Complex Alpha, Bigelow "would need six flights a year; with the launch of a second, larger station, that number would grow to 24, or two a month."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsr20101101_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-tsr20101101-4">[4]</a></sup>  After 2010, no further concrete plans have been announced for transport with Atlas V launch vehicles.\n</p><p>In May 2012, almost simultaneously with the successful mission of SpaceX\'s Dragon capsule, launched by SpaceX\'s Falcon 9 vehicle, to the International Space Station, Bigelow and SpaceX jointly announced that they were teaming to offer private crewed missions to space, promoting the Bigelow space station and SpaceX transport systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2014, plans called for transport of humans and resupply cargo to the station to be via a SpaceX <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_V2" title="Dragon V2">Dragon V2</a>, with a round-trip seat priced at <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$26.5 million</span>.  Lease of the on-orbit stations was priced at <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$25 million</span> to rent one-third of a B330 module for 60 days.  The B330 modules and any of several <a href="/wiki/Space_tug" title="Space tug">tugs</a> were planned for launch aboard a <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heavy" title="Falcon Heavy">Falcon Heavy</a> launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20140207_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20140207-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_Complex_Alpha">Space Complex module&#8212; of the first Bigelow station as <b>Space Complex Alpha</b> in October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsr20101101_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-tsr20101101-4">[4]</a></sup> If the entire station is leased out, it could mean up to 25 launches per year for crew and cargo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  In early 2013, Bigelow Aerospace started referring to Alpha as consisting of two B330 modules instead of two Sundancer and one B330.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2010, Bigelow announced that it has agreements with six <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign nations</a> to utilize the on-orbit facilities of the commercial space station:  <a href="/wiki/UK_Astronomy_Technology_Centre" title="UK Astronomy Technology Centre">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore" title="Government of Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_International_Trade_and_Industry" title="Ministry of International Trade and Industry">Japan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_National_Space_Board" title="Swedish National Space Board">Sweden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pm20101028_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pm20101028-18">[18]</a></sup> A seventh country signed on in February 2011: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirate" title="United Arab Emirate">United Arab Emirate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20110204_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw20110204-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2015, Michael Gold stated that the timetable for the first B330 deployment is uncertain at the moment, since it is tied to the development of private astronaut taxis that can get people to orbit. With this projected to be 2017 or later Bigelow expects to be "ready when they are".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2016, the two B330s attached together was also questioned by suggesting that the first B330 might ideally be attached to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> or that each B330 could operate on its own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2020_Wall_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-2020_Wall-21">[21]</a></sup> The first liftoff was targeted for 2020.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_complex_construction">Orbital complex construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orbital complex construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2010, Bigelow Aerospace began building a large production facility in <a href="/wiki/North_Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="North Las Vegas, Nevada">North Las Vegas, Nevada</a> to produce the space modules. The 16,800&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (181,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) facility will include three <a href="/wiki/Production_line" title="Production line">production lines</a> for three distinct spacecraft, doubling the amount of floor space at Bigelow and transitioning the focus from <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">production</a>.  Bigelow expects to hire approximately 1200 new employees to staff the plant, with production commencing in early 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8nn20110204_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-8nn20110204-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ba20110207_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ba20110207-23">[23]</a></sup> Construction would require three medium lift launches and one heavy lift launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In October 2011 <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> reported that Bigelow had, "pared its 115-member workforce to 51 [...] because of delays developing space taxis needed to fly people to the outposts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters20111019_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters20111019-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, on-orbit assembly of the <i>Bigelow Next-Generation Commercial Space Station</i> components was projected to begin in 2014.  As of  July&#160;2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, construction of the orbital complex was projected to occur in seven principal steps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baocc20100715_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-baocc20100715-5">[5]</a></sup> based on an operations concept that included the on-orbit addition of two Sundancer modules and one B330 module.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baocc20100715_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-baocc20100715-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Unit 1:  <a href="/wiki/Sundancer" title="Sundancer">Sundancer-one</a> module, with a pressurized volume of 180 cubic meters (m<sup>3</sup>), (unoccupied)</li>\n<li>Unit 2:  Commercial crew capsule arrives with Bigelow Aerospace astronauts to set up Sundancer-one and carry additional supplies</li>\n<li>Unit 3:  Supplemental power bus and docking node</li>\n<li>Unit 4:  <a href="/wiki/Sundancer" title="Sundancer">Sundancer-two</a></li>\n<li>Unit 5:  Second commercial crew capsule brings additional crew and supplies, and provides a redundant method for crew return to Earth.</li>\n<li>Unit 6:  <a href="/wiki/B330" title="B330">B330</a>, larger-volume module (330 m<sup>3</sup>)</li>\n<li>Unit 7:  Third commercial crew capsule brings additional supplies and provides a double-redundant, robust solution for astronaut re-entry.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_leasing">Commercial leasing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In January 2013, Bigelow announced that they would sell <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> to the dual-<a href="/wiki/B330" title="B330">B330</a>-module Alpha complex for US$25 million per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2014, Bigelow announced that prices for human access to the space station were expected to be US$26.25 million aboard a <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">Dragon</a>, or US$36.75 million aboard a Boeing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CST-100" title="CST-100">CST-100</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The price for a two-month <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lease</a> of one-third of a module (approximately 110 cubic metres (3,900 cubic feet; 110,000 litres)) was provisionally set at US$25 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" Docking system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As of  2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kurs_(docking_system)" title="Kurs (docking system)">Soyuz-style docking system</a> on one end and a  NASA-standard <a class="mw-redirect" id="Test_program">Test program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Test program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Expandable_Activity_Module" title="Bigelow Expandable Activity Module">Bigelow Expandable Activity Module</a></div>\n<p>The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-in-the-Loop" title="Human-in-the-Loop">human-in-the-loop</a> testing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Control_and_Life_Support_System" title="Environmental Control and Life Support System">environmental control and life support system (ECLSS)</a>" for <i>Sundancer</i> began in October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-av20101022_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-av20101022-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By January 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Expandable_Activity_Module" title="Bigelow Expandable Activity Module">Bigelow Expandable Activity Module</a> (BEAM) <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station#Pressurised_modules" title="International Space Station">pressurised module</a> was under development by Bigelow Aerospace, being purchased by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> for <a href="/wiki/Docking_and_berthing_of_spacecraft" title="Docking and berthing of spacecraft">attachment</a> to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup> The BEAM arrived at the ISS on April 10, 2016, was berthed to the station on April 16, and was expanded and pressurized on May 28, 2016. The initial plan was to test the expandable habitat technology for at least two years. During its flight mission, NASA has been testing and monitoring the module\'s structural integrity, leak rate, radiation dosage and temperature changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ns20130116_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ns20130116-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20130116_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa20130116-29">[29]</a></sup> The module has been performing well, and in October 2017, it was announced that the module would stay attached to the ISS until 2020, with options for two further one-year extensions. The module is being used to store up to 130 cargo transfer bags in an effort to free up additional space aboard the station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstech20171003_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstech20171003-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_planning">Launch planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Launch planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Potential launch options are in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparison_of_mid-heavy_lift_launch_systems" title="Comparison of mid-heavy lift launch systems">mid-heavy lift launch system</a></i> class of launch vehicles, where Bigelow has now negotiated arrangements with two commercial launch providers. As of  January&#160;2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, both <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>&#8212;using the <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a>/<a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">Dragon</a>&#8212; and <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a>/<a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>&#8212;using the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CST-100_Starliner" title="CST-100 Starliner">CST-100</a>&#8212;have signed to deliver launch services to Bigelow Space Station Alpha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsw20130117_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-nsw20130117-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2011, Bigelow announced that it would begin launching its unmanned space station modules in 2014 from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> using Atlas V launch vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssn20110203_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssn20110203-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the Atlas launches for the expandable modules, Bigelow had reserved a single 2014 launch on the SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sxlm20100720_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-sxlm20100720-32">[32]</a></sup> but that launch had not taken place as of early 2016. As of  August&#160;2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, press reports indicate that Bigelow will launch at least some of their crews to the station on the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V#Atlas_V_CTS_(Crew_Transportation_System)" title="Atlas V">human-rated Atlas V</a> utilizing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_CST-100" title="Boeing CST-100">Boeing CST-100</a> seven-person <a href="/wiki/Space_capsule" title="Space capsule">space capsule</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20110804_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20110804-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2016, Bigelow signed an agreement with United Launch Alliance to launch the first B330 module in 2020 using an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2020_Wall_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-2020_Wall-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2017, Bigelow Aerospace and United Launch Alliance (ULA) announced they are working together to launch a B330 expandable module on ULA\'s <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(rocket)" title="Vulcan (rocket)">Vulcan</a> launch vehicle.  The launch would place a B330 module in Earth orbit, and after outfitting it would be boosted to low <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit</a> by two further Vulcan ACES launches by the end of 2022 to serve as a lunar depot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As this announcement stated that only a <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(rocket)" title="Vulcan (rocket)">Vulcan</a> had the performance and fairing capacity needed to launch a B330, it appears that any Atlas V launches would be for crew rather than B330 modules. The timeline may be \'aspirational\' as ULA have indicated that the Vulcan will transition to using the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Cryogenic_Evolved_Stage" title="Advanced Cryogenic Evolved Stage">ACES</a> upper stage around 2024.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seligman2016_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seligman2016-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
