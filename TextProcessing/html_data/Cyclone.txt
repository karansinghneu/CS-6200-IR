title="Air mass">air mass</a> that rotates around a strong center of low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMSCcDef_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMSCcDef-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMSCycDef_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMSCycDef-2">[2]</a></sup> Cyclones are characterized by inward <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">spiraling</a> <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> that rotate about a zone of <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCCycDef_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCCycDef-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCARCycDef_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCARCycDef-4">[4]</a></sup> The largest low-pressure systems are <a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">polar vortices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> of the largest scale (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a>). Warm-core cyclones such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a> also lie within the synoptic scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">Mesocyclones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">dust devils</a> lie within smaller <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Upper level cyclones can exist without the presence of a surface low, and can pinch off from the base of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_upper_tropospheric_trough" title="Tropical upper tropospheric trough">tropical upper tropospheric trough</a> during the summer months in the Northern Hemisphere. Cyclones have also been seen on extraterrestrial planets, such as <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brand-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIZ_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIZ-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">Cyclogenesis</a> is the process of cyclone formation and intensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arc_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arc-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclones" title="Extratropical cyclones">Extratropical cyclones</a> begin as waves in large regions of enhanced mid-latitude temperature contrasts called <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic zones</a>. These zones contract and form <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather fronts</a> as the cyclonic circulation closes and intensifies. Later in their life cycle, extratropical cyclones <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occlude</a> as cold air masses undercut the warmer air and become cold core systems. A cyclone\'s track is guided over the course of its 2 to 6 day life cycle by the steering flow of the subtropical <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.\n</p><p>Weather fronts mark the boundary between two masses of air of different <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">densities</a>, and are associated with the most prominent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_phenomenon" title="Meteorological phenomenon">meteorological phenomena</a>. Strong cold fronts typically feature narrow bands of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a>, and may on occasion be preceded by <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dry_line" title="Dry line">dry lines</a>. Such fronts form west of the circulation center and generally move from west to east; <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a> form east of the cyclone center and are usually preceded by <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>. Warm fronts move <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poleward</a> ahead of the cyclone path. Occluded fronts form late in the cyclone life cycle near the center of the cyclone and often wrap around the storm center.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a> describes the process of development of tropical cyclones. Tropical cyclones form due to latent heat driven by significant thunderstorm activity, and are warm core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-10">[10]</a></sup> Cyclones can transition between extratropical, subtropical, and tropical phases. Mesocyclones form as warm core cyclones over land, and can lead to <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FoN_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FoN-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspouts</a> can also form from mesocyclones, but more often develop from environments of high instability and low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWS-12">[12]</a></sup> In the Atlantic and the northeastern Pacific oceans, a tropical cyclone is generally referred to as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> (from the name of the ancient Central American deity of wind, <a href="/wiki/Huracan" title="Huracan">Huracan</a>), in the Indian and south Pacific oceans it is called a cyclone, and in the northwestern Pacific it is called a <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\nThe growth of instability in the vortices is not universal. For example, the size, intensity, moist-convection, surface evaporation, the value of potential temperature at each potential height can affect the nonlinear evolution of a vortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_Piddington" title="Henry Piddington">Henry Piddington</a> published  40 papers dealing with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storms" title="Tropical storms">tropical storms</a> from Calcutta between 1836 and 1855 in <i>The Journal of the Asiatic Society</i>. He also coined the term <i>cyclone</i>, meaning the coil of a snake. In 1842, he published his landmark thesis, <i>Laws of the Storms</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imd-mm_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-imd-mm-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5160" data-file-width="5160" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png/220px-Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png/330px-Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png/440px-Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post-Tropical_Cyclone_Nuri_and_Typhoon_Haiyan_surface_analysis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison between extratropical and tropical cyclones on surface analysis</div></div></div>\n<p>There are a number of structural characteristics common to all cyclones.  A cyclone is a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-17">[17]</a></sup> A cyclone\'s center (often known in a mature tropical cyclone as the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>), is the area of lowest atmospheric pressure in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-17">[17]</a></sup> Near the center, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a> (from the pressure in the center of the cyclone compared to the pressure outside the cyclone) and the <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> must be in an approximate balance, or the cyclone would collapse on itself as a result of the difference in pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>, the wind flow around a large cyclone is counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the Northern Hemisphere, the fastest winds relative to the surface of the  Earth therefore occur on the eastern side of a northward-moving cyclone and on the northern side of a westward-moving one; the opposite occurs in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  In contrast to low pressure systems, the wind flow around high pressure systems are clockwise (<a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a>) in the northern hemisphere, and counterclockwise in the southern hemisphere.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="431" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png/220px-Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png/330px-Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1a/Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baroclinicleafphasecyclogenesiscropped.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The initial extratropical low-pressure area forms at the location of the red dot on the image. It is usually perpendicular (at a right angle to) the leaf-like cloud formation seen on satellite during the early stage of cyclogenesis. The location of the axis of the upper level <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> is in light blue.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_profile.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="689" data-file-width="1367" decoding="async" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/220px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/330px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/440px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_profile.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tropical cyclones form when the energy released by the condensation of moisture in rising air causes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_feedback_loop" title="Positive feedback loop">positive feedback loop</a> over warm ocean waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">Cyclogenesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>\n<p>Cyclogenesis is the development or strengthening of cyclonic circulation in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arc_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arc-9">[9]</a></sup> Cyclogenesis is an umbrella term for several different processes that all result in the development of some sort of cyclone. It can occur at various scales, from the microscale to the synoptic scale.\n</p><p>Extratropical cyclones begin as waves along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a> before occluding later in their life cycle as cold-core systems. However, some intense extratropical cyclones can become warm-core systems when a  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm_seclusion" title="Warm seclusion">warm seclusion</a> occurs.\n</p><p>Tropical cyclones form as a result of significant convective activity, and are warm core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-10">[10]</a></sup> Mesocyclones form as warm core cyclones over land, and can lead to tornado formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FoN_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FoN-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspouts</a> can also form from mesocyclones, but more often develop from environments of high instability and low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NWS-12">[12]</a></sup> Cyclolysis is the opposite of cyclogenesis, and is the high-pressure system equivalent, which deals with the formation of <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure areas</a>&#8212;<a href="/wiki/Anticyclogenesis" title="Anticyclogenesis">Anticyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CyclogenesisDef_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-CyclogenesisDef-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A surface low can form in a variety of ways. Topography can create a surface low. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" title="Mesoscale Convective System">Mesoscale convective systems</a> can spawn surface lows that are initially warm core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The disturbance can grow into a wave-like formation along the <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">front</a> and the low is positioned at the crest. Around the low, the flow becomes cyclonic. This rotational flow moves polar air towards the equator on the west side of the low, while warm air move towards the pole on the east side. A cold front appears on the west side, while a warm front forms on the east side. Usually, the cold front moves at a quicker pace than the warm front and "catches up" with it due to the slow erosion of higher density air mass out ahead of the cyclone. In addition, the higher density air mass sweeping in behind the cyclone strengthens the higher pressure, denser cold air mass. The cold front over takes the warm front, and reduces the length of the warm front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> At this point an <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded front</a> forms where the warm air mass is pushed upwards into a trough of warm air aloft, which is also known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trowal" title="Trowal">trowal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tropical cyclogenesis is the development and strengthening of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CYCDEF_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CYCDEF-26">[26]</a></sup> The mechanisms by which tropical cyclogenesis occurs are distinctly different from those that produce mid-<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> cyclones. Tropical cyclogenesis, the development of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Mechanics" title="Tropical cyclone">warm-core</a> cyclone, begins with significant <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> in a favorable atmospheric environment. There are six main requirements for tropical cyclogenesis: \n</p>\n<ol><li>sufficiently warm sea surface temperatures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>\n<li>atmospheric instability,</li>\n<li>high <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> in the lower to middle levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a></li>\n<li>enough <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> to develop a low-pressure center</li>\n<li>a preexisting low-level focus or disturbance</li>\n<li>low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A15_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-A15-28">[28]</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>An average of 86 tropical cyclones of tropical storm intensity form annually worldwide, with 47 reaching hurricane/typhoon strength, and 20 becoming intense tropical cyclones (at least Category&#160;3 intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir&#8211;Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir&#8211;Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_scale">Synoptic scale</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UK-Cyclone.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="507" identifiable in synoptic charts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface-based_types">Surface-based types</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">Low-pressure area</a></div>\n<p>There are three main types of surface-based cyclones: <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</a></div>\n<p>An <b>extratropical cyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> of <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure</a> weather system that does not have <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> characteristics, as it is connected with <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">fronts</a> and horizontal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gradients" title="Gradients">gradients</a> (rather than vertical) in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> otherwise known as "baroclinic zones".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>"Extratropical" is applied to cyclones outside the tropics, in the middle latitudes. These systems may also be described as "mid-latitude cyclones" due to their area of formation, or "post-tropical cyclones" when a tropical cyclone has moved (<a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone#Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical transition</a>) beyond the tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtraLessonMillUni_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ExtraLessonMillUni-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtratropicalPhases_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ExtratropicalPhases-31">[31]</a></sup> They are often described as "depressions" or "lows" by weather forecasters and the general public. These are the everyday phenomena that, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-cyclone" title="Anti-cyclone">anti-cyclones</a>, drive weather over much of the Earth.\n</p><p>Although extratropical cyclones are almost always classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroclinic" title="Baroclinic">baroclinic</a> since they form along zones of temperature and dewpoint gradient within the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>, they can sometimes become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a> late in their life cycle when the temperature distribution around the cyclone becomes fairly uniform with radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> An extratropical cyclone can transform into a subtropical storm, and from there into a tropical cyclone, if it dwells over warm waters sufficient to warm its core, and as a result develops central convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7-2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-2-33">[33]</a></sup> A particularly intense type of extratropical cyclone that strikes during winter is known colloquially as a <i><a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">nor\'easter</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_low">Polar low</span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">Polar low</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="2400" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg/220px-Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg/330px-Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg/440px-Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_of_Japan_polar_low_2009-12-20_0213Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A polar low over the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a> in December 2009</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>polar low</b> is a small-scale, short-lived atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure system</a> (depression) that is found over the ocean areas poleward of the main <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">polar front</a> in both the Northern and Southern Hemispheres. Polar lows were first identified on the meteorological satellite imagery that became available in the 1960s, which revealed many small-scale cloud vortices at high latitudes. The most active polar lows are found over certain ice-free maritime areas in or near the Arctic during the winter, such as the Norwegian Sea, Barents Sea, Labrador Sea and Gulf of Alaska. Polar lows dissipate rapidly when they make landfall. Antarctic systems tend to be weaker than their northern counterparts since the air-sea temperature differences around the continent are generally smaller<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="As far as I know, the Arctic and Antarctic oceans are both warmer than the air above, at about the freezing point, and the air over the ocean is generally colder in the Antarctic due to the katabatic winds sweeping off the continent (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. However, vigorous polar lows can be found over the Southern Ocean. During winter, when cold-core lows with temperatures in the mid-levels of the troposphere reach &#8722;45&#160;&#176;C (&#8722;49&#160;&#176;F) move over open waters, deep convection forms, which allows <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar low</a> development to become possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The systems usually have a horizontal length scale of less than 1,000 kilometres (620&#160;mi) and exist for no more than a couple of days. They are part of the larger class of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> weather systems. Polar lows can be difficult to detect using conventional weather reports and are a hazard to high-latitude operations, such as shipping and gas and oil platforms. Polar lows have been referred to by many other terms, such as polar mesoscale vortex, Arctic hurricane, Arctic low, and cold air depression. Today the term is usually reserved for the more vigorous systems that have near-surface winds of at least 17&#160;m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtropical">Subtropical</span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6400" data-file-width="5000" decoding="async" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg/220px-Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg/330px-Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg/440px-Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alex_2016-01-13_1530Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Subtropical Storm Alex</a> in the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in January <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclone</a></div>\n<p>A <b>subtropical cyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> system that has some characteristics of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> and some characteristics of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. They can form between the equator and the 50th parallel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A6_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-A6-36">[36]</a></sup> As early as the 1950s, meteorologists were unclear whether they should be characterized as tropical cyclones or extratropical cyclones, and used terms such as quasi-tropical and semi-tropical to describe the cyclone hybrids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> By 1972, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> officially recognized this cyclone category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Subtropical cyclones began to receive names off the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">official tropical cyclone list</a> in the Atlantic Basin in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A6_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-A6-36">[36]</a></sup> They have broad wind patterns with maximum sustained winds located farther from the center than typical tropical cyclones, and exist in areas of weak to moderate temperature gradient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A6_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-A6-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since they form from extratropical cyclones, which have colder temperatures aloft than normally found in the tropics, the sea surface temperatures required is around 23&#160;degrees Celsius (73&#160;&#176;F) for their formation, which is  three degrees Celsius (5&#160;&#176;F) lower than for tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistSubTropCyclones_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistSubTropCyclones-39">[39]</a></sup> This means that subtropical cyclones are more likely to form outside the traditional bounds of the hurricane season. Although subtropical storms rarely have hurricane-force winds, they may become tropical in nature as their cores warm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A6_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A6-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical">Tropical</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2472" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png/220px-2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png/330px-2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png/440px-2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season_summary_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017 Atlantic hurricane season</a> summary map</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>tropical cyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm system</a> characterized by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-pressure_system" title="Low-pressure system">low-pressure</a> center and numerous <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> that produce strong winds and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>. A tropical cyclone feeds on heat released when moist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> rises, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> contained in the moist air. They are fueled by a different heat mechanism than other cyclonic windstorms such as <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">nor\'easters</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_windstorm" title="European windstorm">European windstorms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>, leading to their classification as "warm core" storm systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2004" data-file-width="3032" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/275px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/413px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/550px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</a>, a rare <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> viewed from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> on March 26, 2004</div></div></div>\n<p>The term "tropical" refers to both the geographic origin of these systems, which form almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> regions of the globe, and their dependence on <a href="/wiki/Air_mass#Classification_and_notation" title="Air mass">Maritime Tropical air masses</a> for their formation. The term "cyclone" refers to the storms\' cyclonic nature, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clockwise_and_counterclockwise" title="Clockwise and counterclockwise">counterclockwise</a> rotation in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and clockwise rotation in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. Depending on their location and strength, tropical cyclones are referred to by other names, such as hurricane, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a>, tropical storm, cyclonic storm, tropical depression, or simply as a cyclone.\n</p><p>While tropical cyclones can produce extremely powerful winds and torrential <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, they are also able to produce high waves and a damaging <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-41">[41]</a></sup> Their winds increase the wave size, and in so doing they draw more heat and moisture into their system, thereby increasing their strength. They develop over large bodies of warm water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-42">[42]</a></sup> and hence lose their strength if they move over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This is the reason coastal regions can receive significant damage from a tropical cyclone, while inland regions are relatively safe from strong winds. Heavy rains, however, can produce significant flooding inland. Storm surges are rises in sea level caused by the reduced pressure of the core that in effect "sucks" the water upward and from winds that in effect "pile" the water up. Storm surges can produce extensive coastal <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> up to 40 kilometres (25&#160;mi) from the coastline. Although their effects on human populations can be devastating, tropical cyclones can also relieve <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005_EPac_outlook_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005_EPac_outlook-44">[44]</a></sup> They also carry heat and energy away from the tropics and transport it toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">latitudes</a>, which makes them an important part of the global <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a> mechanism. As a result, tropical cyclones help to maintain equilibrium in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>.\n</p><p>Many tropical cyclones <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">develop</a> when the atmospheric conditions around a weak disturbance in the atmosphere are favorable. Others form when <a href="#Related_cyclone_types">other types of cyclones</a> acquire tropical characteristics. Tropical systems are then moved by <a href="#Steering_winds">steering winds</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>; if the conditions remain favorable, the tropical disturbance intensifies, and can even develop an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>. On the other end of the spectrum, if the conditions around the system deteriorate or the tropical cyclone makes landfall, the system weakens and eventually dissipates. A tropical cyclone can become extratropical as it moves toward higher latitudes if its energy source changes from heat released by condensation to differences in temperature between air masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-10">[10]</a></sup> A tropical cyclone is usually not considered to become subtropical during its extratropical transition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PadgetDecember2000_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-PadgetDecember2000-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n<li types</span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_cyclone">Polar cyclone</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cyclone" title="Polar cyclone">Polar cyclone</a></div>\n<p>A <b>polar</b>, <b>sub-polar</b>, or <b>Arctic cyclone</b> (also known as a <a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">polar vortex</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossvortex_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-glossvortex-46">[46]</a></sup> is a vast area of low pressure that strengthens in the winter and weakens in the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> A polar cyclone is a low-pressure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_system" title="Weather system">weather system</a>, usually spanning 1,000 kilometres (620&#160;mi) to 2,000 kilometres (1,200&#160;mi), in which the air circulates in a counterclockwise direction in the northern hemisphere, and a clockwise direction in the southern hemisphere. The Coriolis acceleration acting on  the air masses moving poleward at high altitude, causes a counterclockwise circulation at high altitude. The poleward movement of air originates from the air circulation of the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation#Latitudinal_circulation_features" title="Atmospheric circulation">Polar cell</a>. The polar low is not driven by convection as are tropical cyclones, nor the cold and warm air mass interactions as are extratropical cyclones, but is an artifact of the global air movement of the Polar cell. The base of the polar low is in the mid to upper troposphere. In the Northern Hemisphere, the polar cyclone has two centers on average. One center lies near Baffin Island and the other over northeast Siberia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossvortex_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-glossvortex-46">[46]</a></sup> In the southern hemisphere, it tends to be located near the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ross_ice_shelf" title="Ross ice shelf">Ross ice shelf</a> near 160 west longitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> When the polar vortex is strong, its effect can be felt at the surface as a westerly wind (toward the east). When the polar cyclone is weak, significant cold outbreaks occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="TUTT_cell">TUTT cell</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Upper_tropospheric_cyclonic_vortex" title="Upper tropospheric cyclonic vortex">Upper tropospheric cyclonic vortex</a></div>\n<p>Under specific circumstances, upper level cold lows can break off from the base of the Tropical Upper Tropospheric Trough (TUTT), which is located mid-ocean in the Northern Hemisphere during the summer months. These upper tropospheric cyclonic vortices, also known as TUTT cells or TUTT lows, usually move slowly from east-northeast to west-southwest, and their bases generally do not extend below 20,000 feet (6,100&#160;m) in altitude. A weak inverted surface trough within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade wind</a> is generally found underneath them, and they may also be associated with broad areas of high-level clouds. Downward development results in an increase of <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus clouds</a> and the appearance of a surface vortex. In rare cases, they become warm-core <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. Upper cyclones and the upper troughs that trail tropical cyclones can cause additional outflow channels and aid in their intensification. Developing tropical disturbances can help create or deepen upper troughs or upper lows in their wake due to the outflow jet emanating from the developing tropical disturbance/cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLARK_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLARK-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMSPAPER_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMSPAPER-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale">Mesoscale</span></h2>\n<p>The following types of cyclones are not identifiable in synoptic charts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesocyclone">Mesocyclone</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">Mesocyclone</a></div>\n<p>A <b>mesocyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a> of air, 2.0 kilometres (1.2&#160;mi) to 10 kilometres (6.2&#160;mi) in diameter (the <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale of meteorology</a>), within a <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MesocyloneDef_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-MesocyloneDef-52">[52]</a></sup> Air rises and rotates around a vertical axis, usually in the same direction as low-pressure systems in both northern and southern hemisphere. They are most often cyclonic, that is, associated with a localized low-pressure region within a <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Such storms can feature strong surface winds and severe <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>. Mesocyclones often occur together with updrafts in <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> may form. About 1,700 mesocyclones form annually across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, but only half produce tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FoN_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-FoN-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado">Tornado</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>\n<p>A tornado is a violently rotating column of air that is in contact with both the surface of the earth and a cumulonimbus cloud or, in rare cases, the base of a cumulus cloud. Also referred to as twisters, a colloquial term in America, or cyclones, although the word cyclone is used in meteorology, in a wider sense, to name any closed low-pressure circulation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_devil">Dust devil</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">Dust devil</a></div>\n<p>A dust devil is a strong, well-formed, and relatively long-lived whirlwind, ranging from small (half a metre wide and a few metres tall) to large (more than 10 metres wide and more than 1000 metres tall). The primary vertical motion is upward. Dust devils are usually harmless, but can on rare occasions grow large enough to pose a threat to both people and property.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterspout">Waterspout</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspout</a></div>\n<p>A waterspout is a columnar vortex forming over water that is, in its most common form, a non-<a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> over water that is connected to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumuliform_cloud" title="Cumuliform cloud">cumuliform cloud</a>. While it is often weaker than most of its land counterparts, stronger versions spawned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesocyclones" title="Mesocyclones">mesocyclones</a> do occur.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_devil">Steam devil</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Steam_devil" title="Steam devil">Steam devil</a></div>\n<p>A gentle vortex over calm water or wet land made visible by rising water vapour.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_whirl">Fire whirl</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fire_whirl" title="Fire whirl">Fire whirl</a></div>\n<p>A fire whirl &#8211; also colloquially known as a fire devil, fire tornado, firenado, or fire twister &#8211; is a whirlwind induced by a fire and often made up of flame or ash.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span></h2>\n<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" decoding="async" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2016</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes_and_climate_change" title="Hurricanes and climate change">Hurricanes and climate change</a></div>\n<p>Scientists warn that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a> could increase the intensity of typhoons as climate change projections show that the difference in temperature between the ocean &#8211; the heat source for cyclones &#8211; and the storm tops &#8211; the cold parts of cyclones &#8211; are likely to increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Climate change is predicted to increase the frequency of high-intensity storms in selected ocean basins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> While the effect changing climate is having on tropical storms remains largely unresolved<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> scientists and president of <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_Lonsdale" title="Baldwin Lonsdale">Baldwin Lonsdale</a> say the devastation caused by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Pam" title="Cyclone Pam">Pam</a>, was aggravated by climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_planets">Other planets</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars_cyclone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="256" data-file-width="420" decoding="async" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Mars_cyclone.jpg/350px-Mars_cyclone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Mars_cyclone.jpg 1.5x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars_cyclone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cyclone on Mars, imaged by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Cyclones are not unique to Earth. Cyclonic storms are common on <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">Jovian planets</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Small_Dark_Spot" title="Small Dark Spot">Small Dark Spot</a> on <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. It is about one third the diameter of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot" title="Great Dark Spot">Great Dark Spot</a> and received the nickname "Wizard\'s Eye" because it looks like an eye. This appearance is caused by a white cloud in the middle of the Wizard\'s Eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIZ_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIZ-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> has also exhibited cyclonic storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brand-7">[7]</a></sup> Jovian storms like the <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> are usually mistakenly named as giant hurricanes or cyclonic storms. However, this is inaccurate, as the Great Red Spot is, in fact, the inverse phenomenon, an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HaydPlan_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-HaydPlan-59">[59]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Australian_region_tropical_cyclone" title="Australian region tropical cyclone">Australian region tropical cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">Storm</a></li></ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-AMSCcDef-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-AMSCcDef_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Glossary of Meteorology (June 2000). <a class="external text" href="http://glossary.ametsoc.org/wiki/Cyclonic_circulation" rel="nofollow">"Cyclonic  circulation"</a>. <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a><span class="reference-accessdate">.
