"&#1057;&#1077;&#1084;&#1105;&#1088;&#1082;&#1072;"</span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> missile developed during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The R-7 made 28 launches between 1957 and 1961, but was never deployed operationally. A derivative, the <a href="/wiki/R-7A_Semyorka" title="R-7A Semyorka">R-7A</a>, was deployed from 1959 to 1968. To the West it was known by the <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> <b>SS-6 Sapwood</b> and within the Soviet Union by the <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> index <b>8K71</b>. In modified form, it launched <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, the first artificial satellite, into orbit, and became the basis for the <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">R-7 family</a> which includes <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_(rocket)" title="Sputnik (rocket)">Sputnik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luna_(rocket)" title="Luna (rocket)">Luna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(rocket_family)" title="Vostok (rocket family)">Vostok</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_(rocket)" title="Voskhod (rocket)">Voskhod</a> <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">space launchers</a>, as well as later <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz</a> variants.\n</p><p>The widely used nickname for the R-7 launcher, "Semyorka", means "the 7" in Russian.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-7_Semyorka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The R-7 was 34&#160;m (112&#160;ft) long, 3.02&#160;m (9.9&#160;ft) in diameter and weighed 280 metric tons (280 long tons; 310 short tons); it had two stages, powered by <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a> using <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> and capable of delivering its payload up to 8,800&#160;km (5,500&#160;mi), with an accuracy (<a href="/wiki/Circular_error_probable" title="Circular error probable">CEP</a>) of around 5&#160;km (3.1&#160;mi). A single <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear warhead</a> was carried with a nominal yield of 3 <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megatons of TNT</a>. The initial launch was boosted by four strap-on <a href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket_booster" title="Liquid rocket booster">liquid rocket boosters</a> making up the first stage, with a central \'sustainer\' motor powering through both the first and the second stage. Each strap-on booster included two <a href="/wiki/Vernier_thruster" title="Vernier thruster">vernier thrusters</a> and the core stage included four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The guidance system was inertial with radio control of the vernier thrusters.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-7_Semyorka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Refimprove_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=R-7_Semyorka&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.<br/><small><span class="plainlinks"><i>Find sources:</i>&#160;<a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?as_eq=wikipedia&amp;q=%22R-7+Semyorka%22" rel="nofollow">"R-7 Semyorka"</a>&#160;&#8211;&#160;<a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbm=nws&amp;q=%22R-7+Semyorka%22+-wikipedia" rel="nofollow">news</a>&#160;<b>&#183;</b> <a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?&amp;q=%22R-7+Semyorka%22+site:news.google.com/newspapers&amp;source=newspapers" rel="nofollow">newspapers</a>&#160;<b>&#183;</b> <a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbs=bks:1&amp;q=%22R-7+Semyorka%22+-wikipedia" rel="nofollow">books</a>&#160;<b>&#183;</b> <a class="external text" href="//scholar.google.com/scholar?q=%22R-7+Semyorka%22" rel="nofollow">scholar</a>&#160;<b>&#183;</b> <a class="external text" href="https://www.jstor.org/action/doBasicSearch?Query=%22R-7+Semyorka%22&amp;acc=on&amp;wc=on" rel="nofollow">JSTOR</a></span></small></span> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2016</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Design work began in 1953 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">OKB-1</a> in Kaliningrad in Moscow Oblast (presently <a href="/wiki/Korolyov,_Moscow_Oblast" title="Korolyov, Moscow Oblast">Korolyov, Moscow Oblast</a>) and other divisions with the requirement for a two-stage missile of 170 metric tons (170 long tons; 190 short tons) with a range of 8,000&#160;km (5,000&#160;mi) and the maximum speed of 20 mach<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> carrying a 3,000&#160;kg (6,600&#160;lb) warhead. Following first ground tests in late 1953 the initial design was heavily reworked and the final design was not approved until May 1954 and Korolev reportedly reviewed more than 100 design proposals. In 1954, the draft project was completed. For the first time the development of the project was created a separate <a class="external text" href="http://novosti-kosmonavtiki.ru/forum/forum9/topic10797/?PAGEN_1=10" rel="nofollow">volume &#8470; 14</a> dedicated to the testing of missile technology. This volume was developed under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Arkady_Ostashev" title="Arkady Ostashev">Arcady Ilyich Ostashev</a>. \nContrary to claims<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the R-7 was based largely on experience and assistance of German scientists, the missile is noteworthy for looking beyond past achievements that had used German ideas. For example, instead of using jet vanes for control, which increased resistance generated at the engine nozzle exhaust outlet, the R-7 used special control engines. These same engines served as the last stage\'s <a href="/wiki/Vernier_thruster" title="Vernier thruster">vernier thrusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChertokVol2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChertokVol2-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of clustered design, each booster had its own propellant tanks. The design team had to develop a system to regulate the propellant component consumption ratio and to synchronize the consumption between the boosters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChertokVol2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChertokVol2-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Starting from the <a href="/wiki/R-1_(missile)" title="R-1 (missile)">R-1</a>, which was a copy of the German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a>, a free-standing missile was launched from a horizontal pad. It turned out that assembling a cluster of a central core and four boosters on the pad is almost impossible without it falling apart. Also, a wind gust could knock the missile off of the pad. The solution was to eliminate the pad and to suspend the entire rocket in the <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">trusses</a> that bear both vertical weight load as well as horizontal wind forces. The launch system simulated flight conditions with strap-on boosters pushing the central core forward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChertokVol2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ChertokVol2-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The new missile\'s <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> index was 8K71. The first flight-ready vehicle was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> on 1 May 1957, and flown on 15 May. A fire broke out in the Blok D strap-on almost immediately at liftoff. It broke away from the booster at T+88 seconds, which crashed 400&#160;km (248 miles) downrange. The next attempt on 11 June (notable the same day the United States conducted its first test launch of an ICBM), an electrical short caused the missile to start rolling uncontrollably and disintegrate 33 seconds after liftoff. The first successful long flight, of 6,000&#160;km (3,700&#160;mi), was made on 21 August 1957. The dummy warhead impacted in the Pacific Ocean and five days later, TASS announced that the Soviet Union had "successfully tested a multi-stage intercontinental ballistic missile". A modified version of the missile (8K71PS) placed <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> in orbit from Baikonur on 4 October 1957 and <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">Sputnik 2</a> on 3 November 1957.\n</p><p>The next ICBM test took place on 30 January 1958, but the strap-ons failed to separate cleanly and damaged plumbing in the core stage, which lost thrust and impacted far off target. These early flights revealed assorted design flaws in the R-7 which necessitated multiple modifications to the missile. Testing continued through December 1959, and the last original 8K71 flew on 27 February 1961. The additional development resulted in the 8K74 (also known as R-7A), which was lighter, had better navigation systems, more powerful engines, extended its range to 12,000&#160;km by carrying more fuel, and increased payload to 5,370&#160;kg (11,840&#160;lb). In addition, the missile was designed to be easier to take apart and service. The warhead was tested on <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> in October 1957 and again in 1958, yielding an estimated 2.9 Mt.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Aside from the initial Sputnik launches, the 8K71 formed the basis of the 8K72 booster used for the first generation Luna probes. However, six out of nine Luna probes launched on the 8K72 failed. Combined with the failed Sputnik launch on 27 April 1958, this brought the booster\'s total space launch record to 6 successes in 13 attempts. The improved 8K74 would then form the basis for the later Vostok and Molniya boosters, greatly increasing reliability.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg" title="Some R-7 variants"><img alt="Some R-7 variants" data-file-height="784" data-file-width="1123" decoding="async" height="489" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg/700px-Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg/1050px-Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg/1400px-Roket_Launcher_R-7.svg.png 2x" width="700"/></a></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=R-7_Semyorka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first strategic-missile unit became operational on 9 February 1959 at <a href="/wiki/Plesetsk_Cosmodrome" title="Plesetsk Cosmodrome">Plesetsk</a> in north-west Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On 15 December 1959 the R-7 missile was tested at Plesetsk for the first time. The missiles were fully deployed by 1962.\n</p><p>Total service was limited to no more than ten nuclear-armed missiles active at any time.  A single launch pad was operational at <a href="/wiki/Baikonur" title="Baikonur">Baikonur</a> and from six to eight were in operation at Plesetsk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The costs of the system were high, mostly due to the difficulty of constructing in remote areas the large launch sites required.  At one point, each launch site was projected to cost 5% of the total Soviet defense budget.\n</p><p>Besides the cost, the missile system faced other operational challenges.  With the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a> overflights, the huge R-7 launch complexes could not be hidden and therefore could be expected to be destroyed quickly in any nuclear war.  Also, the R-7 took almost twenty hours to prepare for launching, and it could not be left on alert for more than a day due to its <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic fuel</a> system.  Therefore, the Soviet force could not be kept on permanent alert and could have been subject to an air strike before launching. Additionally, the huge payload for which it was designed, adapted to early heavy H-bombs, became irrelevant with the coming of lighter bomb technology.\n</p><p>The limitations of the R-7 pushed the Soviet Union into rapidly developing second-generation missiles which would be more viable weapons systems. The R-7 was phased out of military service by 1968.\n</p><p>While the R-7 turned out to be impractical as a weapon, it became the basis for a series of Soviet <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable space launch vehicles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz family of launchers.</a> As of 2018, in modified versions (<a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">Soyuz-U</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">Soyuz-FG</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-2" title="Soyuz-2">Soyuz-2</a> (including the boosterless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2.1v" title="Soyuz-2.1v">2.1v variant</a>), the vehicle is still in service, having launched over 1840 times.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
