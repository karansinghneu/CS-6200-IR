The blue fiber structures represent the matter (primarily dark matter) and the empty regions in between represent the cosmic voids.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Cosmic voids</b> are vast spaces between <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_filament" title="Galaxy filament">filaments</a> (the largest-scale structures in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>), which contain <a href="/wiki/Void_galaxy" title="Void galaxy">very few</a> or no <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>. Voids typically have a diameter of 10 to 100 <a href="/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs" title="Parsec">megaparsecs</a>; particularly large voids, defined by the absence of rich <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a>, are sometimes called <b>supervoids</b>. They have less than one tenth of the average density of matter abundance that is considered typical for the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>. They were first discovered in 1978 in a pioneering study by Stephen Gregory and <a href="/wiki/Laird_A._Thompson" title="Laird A. Thompson">Laird A. Thompson</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kitt_Peak_National_Observatory" title="Kitt Peak National Observatory">Kitt Peak National Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Voids are believed to have been formed by <a href="/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations" title="Baryon acoustic oscillations">baryon acoustic oscillations</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, collapses of mass followed by implosions of the compressed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryonic_matter" title="Baryonic matter">baryonic matter</a>. Starting from initially small <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropies</a> from <a href="/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation" title="Quantum fluctuation">quantum fluctuations</a> in the early universe, the anisotropies grew larger in scale over time. Regions of higher density collapsed more rapidly under gravity, eventually resulting in the large-scale, foam-like structure or "cosmic web" of voids and galaxy filaments seen today. Voids located in high-density environments are smaller than voids situated in low-density spaces of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Voids appear to correlate with the observed temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> (CMB) because of the <a href="/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect" title="Sachs&#8211;Wolfe effect">Sachs&#8211;Wolfe effect</a>. Colder regions correlate with voids and hotter regions correlate with filaments because of <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_redshift" title="Gravitational redshift">gravitational redshifting</a>. As the Sachs&#8211;Wolfe effect is only significant if the universe is dominated by <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, the existence of voids is significant in providing physical evidence for dark energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Large-scale_structure">Large-scale structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Large-scale structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The structure of our Universe can be broken down into components that can help describe the characteristics of individual regions of the cosmos. These are the main structural components of the cosmic web:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Voids &#8211; vast, largely spherical<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> regions with very low cosmic mean densities, up to 100 <a href="/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs" title="Parsec">megaparsecs</a> (Mpc) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">Walls</a> &#8211; the regions that contain the typical cosmic mean density of matter abundance. Walls can be further broken down into two smaller structural features:\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">Clusters</a> &#8211; highly concentrated zones where walls meet and intersect, adding to the effective size of the local wall.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_filament" title="Galaxy filament">Filaments</a> &#8211; the branching arms of walls that can stretch for tens of megaparsecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pan2011_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pan2011-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>Voids have a mean density less than a tenth of the average density of the universe. This serves as a working definition even though there is no single agreed-upon definition of what constitutes a void. The matter density value used for describing the cosmic mean density is usually based on a ratio of the number of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> per unit volume rather than the total mass of the matter contained in a unit volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neyrinck2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neyrinck2008-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_discovery">History and discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cosmic voids as a topic of study in <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> began in the mid-1970s when <a href="/wiki/Redshift_survey" title="Redshift survey">redshift surveys</a> became more popular and led two separate teams of astrophysicists in 1978 to identifying superclusters and voids in the distribution of galaxies and <a href="/wiki/Abell_catalogue" title="Abell catalogue">Abell clusters</a> in a large region of space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory1978_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory1978-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-J&#245;eveer_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-J&#245;eveer-10">[10]</a></sup> The new redshift surveys revolutionized the field of astronomy by adding depth to the two-dimensional maps of cosmological structure, which were often densely packed and overlapping,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> allowing for the first three-dimensional mapping of the universe. In the redshift surveys, the depth was calculated from the individual <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifts</a> of the galaxies due to the <a href="/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe" title="Expansion of the universe">expansion of the universe</a> according to <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RexBennett1998_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RexBennett1998-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A summarized timeline of important events in the field of cosmic voids from its beginning to recent times is listed below:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1961 &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">Large-scale structural</a> features such as "second-order clusters", a specific type of <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">supercluster</a>, were brought to the astronomical community\'s attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abell1961_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abell1961-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1978 &#8211; The first two papers on the topic of voids in the large-scale structure were published referencing voids found in the foreground of the Coma/A1367 clusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory1978_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory1978-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1981 &#8211; Discovery of a large void in the <a href="/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes" title="Bo&#246;tes">Bo&#246;tes</a> region of the sky that was nearly 50 <i>h</i><sup>&#8722;1</sup> Mpc in diameter (which was later recalculated to be about 34 <i>h</i><sup>&#8722;1</sup> Mpc).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirshner1981_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirshner1981-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirshner1987_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirshner1987-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1983 &#8211; Computer simulations sophisticated enough to provide relatively reliable results of growth and evolution of the large-scale structure emerged and yielded insight on key features of the large-scale galaxy distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merlott1983_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merlott1983-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frenk1983_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frenk1983-17">[17]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1985 &#8211; Details of the supercluster and void structure of the <a href="/wiki/Perseus-Pisces_Supercluster" title="Perseus-Pisces Supercluster">Perseus-Pisces region</a> were surveyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giovanelli1985_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giovanelli1985-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1989 &#8211; The Center for Astrophysics Redshift Survey revealed that large voids, sharp filaments, and the walls that surround them dominate the large-scale structure of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geller1989_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geller1989-19">[19]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1991 &#8211; The Las Campanas Redshift Survey confirmed the abundance of voids in the large-scale structure of the universe (Kirshner et al. 1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>\n<li>1995 &#8211; Comparisons of optically selected galaxy surveys indicate that the same voids are found regardless of the sample selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>\n<li>2001 &#8211; The completed two-degree Field Galaxy Redshift Survey adds a significantly large amount of voids to the database of all known cosmic voids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>\n<li>2009 &#8211; The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) data combined with previous large-scale surveys now provide the most complete view of the detailed structure of cosmic voids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_for_finding">Methods for finding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methods for finding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There exist a number of ways for finding voids with the results of large-scale surveys of the universe. Of the many different algorithms, virtually all fall into one of three general categories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_2966_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Definition_2966-26">[26]</a></sup> The first class consists of void finders that try to find empty regions of space based on local galaxy density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The second class are those which try to find voids via the geometrical structures in the dark matter distribution as suggested by the galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The third class is made up of those finders which identify structures dynamically by using gravitationally unstable points in the distribution of dark matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The three most popular methods through the study of cosmic voids are listed below:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VoidFinder_algorithm">VoidFinder algorithm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: VoidFinder algorithm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This first-class method uses each galaxy in a catalog as its target and then uses the Nearest Neighbor Approximation to calculate the cosmic density in the region contained in a spherical radius determined by the distance to the third-closest galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup> El Ad &amp; Piran introduced this method in 1997 to allow a quick and effective method for standardizing the cataloging of voids. Once the spherical cells are mined from all of the structure data, each cell is expanded until the underdensity returns to average expected wall density values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> One of the helpful features of void regions is that their boundaries are very distinct and defined, with a cosmic mean density that starts at 10% in the body and quickly rises to 20% at the edge and then to 100% in the walls directly outside the edges. The remaining walls and overlapping void regions are then gridded into, respectively, distinct and intertwining zones of filaments, clusters, and near-empty voids. Any overlap of more than 10% with already known voids are considered to be subregions within those known voids. All voids admitted to the catalog had a minimum radius of 10 Mpc in order to ensure all identified voids were not accidentally cataloged due to sampling errors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Zone_bordering_on_voidness_.28ZOBOV.29_algorithm"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Zone_bordering_on_voidness_(ZOBOV)_algorithm">Zone bordering on voidness (ZOBOV) algorithm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zone bordering on voidness (ZOBOV) algorithm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This particular second-class algorithm uses a Voronoi tessellation technique and mock border particles in order to categorize regions based on a high-density contrasting border with a very low amount of bias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\nNeyrinck introduced this algorithm in 2008 with the purpose of introducing a method that did not contain free parameters or presumed shape tessellations. Therefore, this technique can create more accurately shaped and sized void regions. Although this algorithm has some advantages in shape and size, it has been criticized often for sometimes providing loosely defined results. Since it has no free parameters, it mostly finds small and trivial voids, although the algorithm places a statistical significance on each void it finds. A physical significance parameter can be applied in order to reduce the number of trivial voids by including a minimum density to average density ratio of at least 1:5. Subvoids are also identified using this process which raises more philosophical questions on what qualifies as a void.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Void finders such as VIDE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> are based on ZOBOV.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Dynamical_void_analysis_.28DIVA.29_algorithm"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamical_void_analysis_(DIVA)_algorithm">Dynamical void analysis (DIVA) algorithm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dynamical void analysis (DIVA) algorithm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This third-class method is drastically different from the previous two algorithms listed. The most striking aspect is that it requires a different definition of what it means to be a void. Instead of the general notion that a void is a region of space with a low cosmic mean density; a hole in the distribution of galaxies, it defines voids to be regions in which matter is escaping; which corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> equation of state, <i>w</i>. Void centers are then considered to be the maximal source of the displacement field denoted as <i>S</i><sub>&#968;</sub>. The purpose for this change in definitions was presented by Lavaux and Wandelt in 2009 as a way to yield cosmic voids such that exact analytical calculations can be made on their dynamical and geometrical properties. This allows DIVA to heavily explore the <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">ellipticity</a> of voids and how they evolve in the large-scale structure, subsequently leading to the classification of three distinct types of voids. These three morphological classes are True voids, Pancake voids, and Filament voids. Another notable quality is that even though DIVA also contains selection function bias just as first-class methods do, DIVA is devised such that this bias can be precisely calibrated, leading to much more reliable results. Multiple shortfalls of this Lagrangian-Eulerian hybrid approach exist. One example is that the resulting voids from this method are intrinsically different than those found by other methods, which makes an all-data points inclusive comparison between results of differing algorithms very difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_2966_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Definition_2966-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robustness_testing">Robustness testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Robustness testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Once an algorithm is presented to find what it deems to be cosmic voids, it is crucial that its findings approximately match what is expected by the current simulations and models of large-scale structure. In order to perform this, the number, size, and proportion as well as other features of voids found by the algorithm are then checked by placing mock data through a Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamic Halo simulation, &#923;<i>CDM</i> model, or other reliable simulator. An algorithm is much more robust if its data is in concordance with the results of these simulations for a range of input criterion (Pan et al. 2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Voids have contributed significantly to the modern understanding of the cosmos, with applications ranging from shedding light on the current understanding of <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, to refining and constraining <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology#Formation_and_evolution_of_large-scale_structure" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological evolution</a> models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> Some popular applications are mentioned in detail below.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_energy">Dark energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dark energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The simultaneous existence of the largest-known voids and galaxy clusters requires about 70% dark energy in the universe today, consistent with the latest data from the cosmic microwave background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> Voids act as bubbles in the universe that are sensitive to background cosmological changes. This means that the evolution of a void\'s shape is in part the result of the expansion of the universe. Since this acceleration is believed to be caused by dark energy, studying the changes of a void\'s shape over a period of time can be used to constrain the standard <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">&#923;<i>CDM</i></a> model<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>, or further refine the <a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">Quintessence</a> + Cold Dark Matter (<i>QCDM</i>) model and provide a more accurate dark energy <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology)" title="Equation of state (cosmology)">equation of state</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Additionally the abundance of voids is a promising way to constrain the dark energy equation of state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galactic_formation_and_evolution_models">Galactic formation and evolution models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Galactic formation and evolution models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:176px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Large-scale_structure_formation.gif"><img alt="Large-scale structure formation" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="144" data-file-width="174" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/Large-scale_structure_formation.gif" width="174"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Large-scale_structure_formation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 43&#215;43&#215;43-megaparsec cube shows the evolution of the large-scale structure over a logarithmic period starting from a <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of 30 and ending at redshift 0. The model makes it clear to see how the matter-dense regions contract under the collective gravitational force while simultaneously aiding in the expansion of cosmic voids as the matter flees to the walls and filaments.</div></div></div>\n<p>Cosmic voids contain a mix of galaxies and matter that is slightly different than other regions in the universe. This unique mix supports the biased galaxy formation picture predicted in Gaussian adiabatic cold dark matter models. This phenomenon provides an opportunity to modify the morphology-density correlation that holds discrepancies with these voids. Such observations like the morphology-density correlation can help uncover new facets about how galaxies form and evolve on the large scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> On a more local scale, galaxies that reside in voids have differing morphological and spectral properties than those that are located in the walls. One feature that has been found is that voids have been shown to contain a significantly higher fraction of <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">starburst galaxies</a> of young, hot stars when compared to samples of galaxies in walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalies_in_anisotropies">Anomalies in anisotropies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Anomalies in anisotropies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cold spots in the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/CMB_cold_spot" title="CMB cold spot">WMAP cold spot</a> found by <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe</a>, could possibly be explained by an extremely large cosmic void that has a radius of ~120 Mpc, as long as the late integrated <a href="/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect" title="Sachs&#8211;Wolfe effect">Sachs&#8211;Wolfe effect</a> was accounted for in the possible solution. Anomalies in CMB screenings are now being potentially explained through the existence of large voids located down the line-of-sight in which the cold spots lie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WMAP_white.png"><img alt="Cosmic Microwave Background screening of Universe." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1050" data-file-width="2075" decoding="async" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/WMAP_white.png/220px-WMAP_white.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/WMAP_white.png/330px-WMAP_white.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/WMAP_white.png/440px-WMAP_white.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WMAP_white.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CMB screening of the universe.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe">Accelerating expansion of the universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Accelerating expansion of the universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> is currently the most popular explanation for the <a href="/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe" title="Accelerating expansion of the universe">acceleration in the expansion of the universe</a>, another theory elaborates on the possibility of our galaxy being part of a very large, not-so-underdense, cosmic void. According to this theory, such an environment could naively lead to the demand for dark energy to solve the problem with the observed acceleration. As more data has been released on this topic the chances of it being a realistic solution in place of the current <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">&#923;<i>CDM</i></a> interpretation has been largely diminished but not all together abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravitational_theories">Gravitational theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gravitational theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The abundance of voids, particularly when combined with the abundance of clusters of galaxies, is a promising method for precision tests of deviations from <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> on large scales and in low-density regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The insides of voids often seem to adhere to cosmological parameters which differ from those of the known universe. It is because of this unique feature that cosmic voids make for great laboratories to study the effects that gravitational clustering and growth rates have on local galaxies and structure when the cosmological parameters have different values from the outside universe. Due to the observation that larger voids predominantly remain in a linear regime, with most structures within exhibiting spherical symmetry in the underdense environment; that is, the underdensity leads to near-negligible particle-particle gravitational interactions that would otherwise occur in a region of normal galactic density. Testing models for voids can be performed with very high accuracy. The cosmological parameters that differ in these voids are &#937;<sub>m</sub>, &#937;<sub>&#923;</sub>, and <i>H</i><sub>0</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_voids" title="List of voids">List of voids</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">Observable universe</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes_void" title="Bo&#246;tes void">Bo&#246;tes void</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/KBC_Void" title="KBC Void">KBC Void</a> (Largest void known in the observable universe)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">Galaxy</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Freedman, R.A., &amp; Kaufmann III, W.J.
