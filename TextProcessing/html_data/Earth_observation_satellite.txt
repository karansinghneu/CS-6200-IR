style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A-Train_w-Time2013_Web.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1351" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="203" constellation)">A-train</a> satellite constellation as of 2014.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Earth observation satellites</b> or <b>Earth remote sensing satellites</b> are <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> specifically designed for <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation" title="Earth observation">Earth observation</a> from <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">spy satellites</a> but intended for non-military uses such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> monitoring, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Map_making" title="Map making">map making</a> etc.  \nThe first occurrence of satellite remote sensing can be dated to the launch of the first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, by the Soviet Union on October 4, 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatem_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tatem-1">[1]</a></sup> Sputnik 1 sent back radio signals, which scientists used to study the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuznetsov_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuznetsov-2">[2]</a></sup>\nNASA launched the first American satellite, <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a>, in January 31, 1958. The information sent back from its radiation detector led to the discovery of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen-3">[3]</a></sup>\nThe <a href="/wiki/TIROS-1" title="TIROS-1">TIROS-1</a> spacecraft, launched on April 1, 1960 as part of NASA\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TIROS" title="TIROS">TIROS</a> (Television Infrared Observation Satellite) Program, sent back the first television footage of weather patterns to be taken from space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatem_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tatem-1">[1]</a></sup>\nAs of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earth_observation_satellite&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, more than 150 Earth observation satellites were in orbit, recording data with both passive and active sensors and acquiring more than 10 terabits of data daily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tatem_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tatem-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most Earth observation satellites carry instruments that should be operated at a relatively low altitude. Altitudes below 500-600 kilometers are in general avoided, though, because of the significant <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">air-drag</a> at such low altitudes making frequent orbit <a href="/wiki/Reboost" title="Reboost">reboost</a> maneuvres necessary.  The Earth observation satellites <a href="/wiki/European_Remote-Sensing_Satellite" title="European Remote-Sensing Satellite">ERS-1, ERS-2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Envisat" title="Envisat">Envisat</a> of <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/MetOp" title="MetOp">MetOp</a> spacecraft of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">EUMETSAT</a> are all operated at altitudes of about 800&#160;km. The <a href="/wiki/PROBA" title="PROBA">Proba-1</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proba-2" title="Proba-2">Proba-2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_Moisture_and_Ocean_Salinity_satellite" title="Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity satellite">SMOS</a> spacecraft of European Space Agency are observing the Earth from an altitude of about 700&#160;km. The Earth observation satellites of UAE, <a href="/wiki/DubaiSat-1" title="DubaiSat-1">DubaiSat-1</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/DubaiSat-2" title="DubaiSat-2">DubaiSat-2</a> are also placed in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth Orbits (LEO)</a> orbits and providing <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> of various parts of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>To get (nearly) global coverage with a low orbit it must be a <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbit</a> or nearly so.  A low orbit will have an orbital period of roughly 100 minutes and the Earth will rotate around its polar axis with about 25 deg between successive orbits, with the result that the <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">ground track</a> is shifted towards west with these 25 deg in longitude. Most are in <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous orbits</a>.\n</p><p>Spacecraft carrying instruments for which an altitude of 36000&#160;km is suitable sometimes use a <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a>.  Such an orbit allows uninterrupted coverage of more than 1/3 of the Earth. Three geostationary spacecraft at longitudes separated with 120 deg can cover the whole Earth except the extreme polar regions. This type of orbit is mainly used for <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">meteorological satellites</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_observation_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Herman_Poto%C4%8Dnik" title="Herman Poto&#269;nik">Herman Poto&#269;nik</a> explored the idea of using orbiting spacecraft for detailed peaceful and military observation of the ground in his 1928 book, <i>The Problem of Space Travel</i>. He described how the special conditions of space could be useful for scientific experiments. The book described <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">geostationary</a> satellites (first put forward by <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a>) and discussed communication between them and the ground using radio, but fell short of the idea of using satellites for mass broadcasting and as telecommunications relays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">Weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span States</a> weather satellite.</div></div></div>\n<p>A weather satellite is a type of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> that is primarily used to monitor the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NESDIS_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NESDIS-7">[7]</a></sup>  These meteorological satellites, however, see more than <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and cloud systems.  City lights, <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fires</a>, effects of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auroral_light" title="Auroral light">auroras</a>, sand and dust storms, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> cover, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> mapping, boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> flows, etc., are other types of environmental information collected using weather satellites.\n</p><p>Weather satellite images helped in monitoring the volcanic ash cloud from <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">Mount St. Helens</a> and activity from other volcanoes such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Smoke from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" href="/wiki/Environmental_monitoring" title="Environmental monitoring">environmental monitoring</a> by detecting changes in the Earth\'s vegetation, atmospheric trace gas content, sea state, ocean color, and ice fields.  By monitoring vegetation changes over time, droughts can be monitored by comparing the current vegetation state to its long term average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  For example, the 2002 oil spill off the northwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> was watched carefully by the European ENVISAT, which, though not a weather satellite, flies an instrument (ASAR) which can see changes in the sea surface. Anthropogenic emissions can be monitored by evaluating data of tropospheric NO2 and SO2.\n</p><p>These types of satellites are almost always in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_synchronous_orbit" title="Sun synchronous orbit">Sun synchronous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">"frozen"</a> orbits.  The Sun synchronous orbit is in general sufficiently close to polar to get the desired global coverage while the relatively constant geometry to the Sun mostly is an advantage for the instruments.  The <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">"frozen"</a> orbit is selected as this is the closest to a circular orbit that is possible in the <a href="/wiki/Geopotential_model" title="Geopotential model">gravitational field</a> of the Earth.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mapping">Mapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_observation_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Terrain can be mapped from space with the use of satellites, such as <a href="/wiki/Radarsat-1" title="Radarsat-1">Radarsat-1</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/TerraSAR-X" title="TerraSAR-X">TerraSAR-X</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_observation_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Earth_observation" title="Earth observation">Earth observation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellites_transmission_frequencies" title="Earth observation satellites transmission frequencies">Earth observation satellites transmission frequencies</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_Observing_System" title="Earth Observing System">Earth Observing System</a> -
