thought to be <i>B. subtilis</i> but is since formally recognized as <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_atrophaeus" title="Bacillus atrophaeus">Bacillus atrophaeus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" subtilis</b></i>, known also as the <b>hay bacillus</b> or <b>grass bacillus</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">Gram-positive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalase" title="Catalase">catalase</a>-positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>, found in soil and the gastrointestinal tract of <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> and humans. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus" title="Bacillus">Bacillus</a></i>, <i>B. subtilis</i> is rod-shaped, and can form a tough, protective <a href="/wiki/Endospore" title="Endospore">endospore</a>, allowing it to tolerate extreme environmental conditions. <i>B. subtilis</i> has historically been classified as an <a href="/wiki/Obligate_aerobe" title="Obligate aerobe">obligate aerobe</a>, though evidence exists that it is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobe" title="Facultative anaerobe">facultative anaerobe</a>. <i>B. subtilis</i> is considered the best studied Gram-positive bacterium and a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> to study bacterial chromosome replication and cell differentiation. It is one of the bacterial champions in secreted enzyme production and used on an industrial scale by biotechnology companies.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacillus_subtilis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Bacillus subtilis</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">Gram-positive</a> bacterium,  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rod-shaped" title="Rod-shaped">rod-shaped</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catalase" title="Catalase">catalase</a>-positive. It was originally named <i>Vibrio subtilis</i> by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Gottfried_Ehrenberg" title="Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg">Christian Gottfried Ehrenberg</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrenberg_1835_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrenberg_1835-3">[3]</a></sup> and renamed <i>Bacillus subtilis</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Cohn" title="Ferdinand Cohn">Ferdinand Cohn</a> in 1872<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohn_1872_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohn_1872-4">[4]</a></sup> (subtilis being the Latin for \'fine\'). <i>B. subtilis</i> cells are typically rod-shaped, and are about 4-10 micrometers (&#956;m) long and 0.25&#8211;1.0 &#956;m in diameter, with a cell volume of about 4.6 fL at stationary phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yu_2013_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yu_2013-5">[5]</a></sup> As with other members of the <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus" title="Bacillus">Bacillus</a></i>, it can form an <a href="/wiki/Endospore" title="Endospore">endospore</a>, to survive  extreme environmental conditions of temperature and desiccation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brock-6">[6]</a></sup>\n<i>B. subtilis</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobe" title="Facultative anaerobe">facultative anaerobe</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nakano_1998_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nakano_1998-7">[7]</a></sup> and had been considered as an <a href="/wiki/Obligate_aerobe" title="Obligate aerobe">obligate aerobe</a> until 1998. <i>B. subtilis</i> is heavily <a href="/wiki/Flagellate" title="Flagellate">flagellated</a>, which gives it the ability to move quickly in liquids.  \n<i>B. subtilis</i> has proven highly amenable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_manipulation" title="Genetic manipulation">genetic manipulation</a>, and has become widely adopted as a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> for laboratory studies, especially of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sporulation" title="Sporulation">sporulation</a>, which is a simplified example of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">cellular differentiation</a>. In terms of popularity as a laboratory model organism, <i>B. subtilis</i> is often considered as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">Gram-positive</a> equivalent of <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, an extensively studied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-negative" title="Gram-negative">Gram-negative</a> bacterium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacillus_subtilis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This species is commonly found in the upper layers of the soil, and evidence exists that <i>B. subtilis</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">normal gut commensal</a> in humans. A 2009 study compared the density of spores found in soil (about 10<sup>6</sup> spores per gram) to that found in human feces (about 10<sup>4</sup> spores per gram). The number of spores found in the human gut was too high to be attributed solely to consumption through food contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>B. subtilis</i> has been linked to grow in higher elevations and act as an identifier for both eco-adaptability and honey bee health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Daughter cell">daughter cells</a> (binary fission), or asymmetrically, producing a single <a href="/wiki/Endospore" title="Endospore">endospore</a> that can remain viable for decades and is resistant to unfavourable environmental conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>, extreme pH, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>. The endospore is formed at times of nutritional stress and through the use of hydrolysis, allowing the organism to persist in the environment until conditions become favourable. Prior to the process of sporulation the cells might become <a href="/wiki/Motility" title="Motility">motile</a> by producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flagella" title="Flagella">flagella</a>, take up DNA from the environment, or produce <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>. These responses are viewed as attempts to seek out nutrients by seeking a more favourable environment, enabling the cell to make use of new beneficial genetic material or simply by killing off competition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Under stressful conditions, such as nutrient deprivation, <i>B. subtilis</i> undergoes the process of <a href="/wiki/Sporulation_in_Bacillus_subtilis" title="Sporulation in Bacillus subtilis">sporulation</a>. This process has been very well studied and has served as a model organism for studying sporulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chromosomal_replication">Chromosomal replication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacillus_subtilis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chromosomal replication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>B. subtilis</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> used to study bacterial chromosome replication. Replication of the single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circular_chromosome" title="Circular chromosome">circular chromosome</a> initiates at a single locus, the origin (<i><a href="/wiki/Origin_of_replication" title="Origin of replication">oriC</a></i>). Replication proceeds bidirectionally and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Replication_fork" title="Replication fork">replication forks</a> progress in clockwise and counterclockwise directions along the chromosome. Chromosome replication is completed when the forks reach the terminus region, which is positioned opposite to the origin on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromosome_map" title="Chromosome map">chromosome map</a>. The terminus region contains several short DNA sequences (<i>Ter</i> sites) that promote replication arrest. Specific proteins mediate all the steps in DNA replication. Comparison between the proteins involved in chromosomal DNA replication in <i>B. subtilis</i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> reveals similarities and differences. Although the basic components promoting initiation, elongation, and termination of replication are well-<a href="/wiki/Conserved_sequence" title="Conserved sequence">conserved</a>, some important differences can be found (such as one bacterium missing proteins essential in the other). These differences underline the diversity in the mechanisms and strategies that various bacterial species have adopted to carry out the duplication of their genomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graumann_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graumann-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genome">Genome</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacillus_subtilis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Genome">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>B. subtilis</i> has about 4,100 genes. Of these, only 192 were shown to be indispensable; another 79 were predicted to be essential, as well. A vast majority of essential genes were categorized in relatively few domains of cell metabolism, with about half involved in information processing, one-fifth involved in the synthesis of cell envelope and the determination of cell shape and division, and one-tenth related to cell energetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The complete genome sequence of <i>B. subtilis</i> sub-strain QB928 has 4,146,839 DNA base pairs and 4,292 genes. The QB928 strain is widely used in genetic studies due to the presence of various markers [aroI(aroK)906 purE1 dal(alrA)1 trpC2].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several noncoding RNAs have been characterized in the <i>B. subtilis</i> genome in 2009, including <a href="/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis_BSR_sRNAs" title="Bacillus subtilis BSR sRNAs">Bsr RNAs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\nMicroarray-based comparative genomic analyses have revealed that <i>B. subtilis</i> members show considerable genomic diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Earl_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Earl-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transformation">Transformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bacillus_subtilis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)" title="Transformation (genetics)">Natural bacterial transformation</a> involves the transfer of DNA from one bacterium to another through the surrounding medium. In <i>B. subtilis</i> the length of transferred DNA is greater than 1271kb (more than 1 million bases).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The transferred DNA is likely double-stranded DNA and is often more than a third of the total chromosome length of 4215 kb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It appears that about 7-9% of the recipient cells take up an entire chromosome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In order for a recipient bacterium to bind, take up exogenous DNA from another bacterium of the same species and recombine it into its chromosome, it must enter a special physiological state called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competence_(biology)" title="Competence (biology)">competence</a>.\nCompetence in <i>B. subtilis</i> is induced toward the end of logarithmic growth, especially under conditions of amino-acid limitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Under these stressful conditions of semistarvation, cells typically have just one copy of their chromosome and likely have increased DNA damage. To test whether transformation is an adaptive function for <i>B. subtilis</i> to repair its DNA damage, experiments were conducted using UV light as the damaging agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoelzer1991_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoelzer1991-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  These experiments led to the conclusion that competence, with uptake of DNA, is specifically induced by DNA-damaging conditions, and that Some <i>B. subtilis</i> strains are responsible for causing ropiness&#160;&#8212; a sticky, stringy consistency caused by bacterial production of long-chain <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>&#160;&#8212; in spoiled bread dough. For a long time, bread ropiness was associated uniquely with <i>B. subtilis</i> species by biochemical tests.  Molecular assays (randomly amplified polymorphic DNA PCR assay, denaturing gradient gel electrophoresis analysis, and sequencing of the V3 region of 16S ribosomal DNA) revealed greater <i>Bacillus</i> species variety in ropy breads, which all seems to have a positive amylase activity and high heat resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <i>B. subtilis</i> CU1 (2 &#215; 10<sup>9</sup> spores per day) was evaluated in a 16-week study (10 days administration of probiotic, followed by 18 days wash-out period per each month; repeated same procedure for total 4 months) to healthy subjects. <i>B. subtilis</i> CU1 was found to be safe and well-tolerated in the subjects without any side effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>B. subtilis</i> and substances derived from it has been evaluated by different authoritative bodies for their safe and beneficial use in food. In the United States, an opinion letter issued in the early 1960s by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) recognized some substances derived from microorganisms as <a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a> (GRAS), including carbohydrase and protease enzymes from <i>B. subtilis</i>. The opinions were predicated on the use of nonpathogenic and nontoxicogenic strains of the respective organisms and on the use of current good manufacturing practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The FDA stated the enzymes derived from the <i>B. subtilis</i> strain were in common use in food prior to January 1, 1958, and that nontoxigenic and nonpathogenic strains of <i>B. subtilis</i> are widely available and have been safely used in a variety of food applications. This includes consumption of Japanese fermented soy bean, in the form of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natto" title="Natto">Natto</a></i>, which is commonly consumed in Japan, and contains as many as 10<sup>8</sup> viable cells per gram. The fermented beans are recognized for their contribution to a healthy gut flora and vitamin K<sub>2</sub> intake; during this long history of widespread use, <i>natto</i> has not been implicated in adverse events potentially attributable to the presence of <i>B. subtilis</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The natto product and the <i>B. subtilis</i> natto as its principal component are FOSHU (Foods for Specified Health Use) approved by the Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health,_Labour,_and_Welfare" title="Ministry of Health, Labour, and Welfare">Ministry of Health, Labour, and Welfare</a> as effective for preservation of health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>B. subtilis</i> has been granted "Qualified Presumption of Safety" status by the <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a
