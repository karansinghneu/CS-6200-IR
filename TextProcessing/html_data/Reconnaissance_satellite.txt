(satellite)">Rhyolite</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>reconnaissance satellite</b> or <b>intelligence satellite</b> (commonly, although unofficially, referred to as a <b>spy satellite</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellite</a> deployed for <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">military</a> or <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">intelligence</a> applications.\n</p><p>The first generation type (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Corona_(satellite)" title="Corona (satellite)">Corona</a>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRO-Corona_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRO-Corona-1">[1]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-Corona_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-Corona-2">[2]</a></sup>\nand <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(satellite)" title="Zenit (satellite)">Zenit</a>) took photographs, then ejected canisters of <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> which would descend to earth. Corona capsules were <a href="/wiki/Mid-air_retrieval" title="Mid-air retrieval">retrieved in mid-air</a> as they floated down on <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachutes</a>. Later, spacecraft had digital imaging systems and downloaded the images via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encrypted" title="Encrypted">encrypted</a> radio links.\n</p><p>In the United States, most information available is on programs that existed up to 1972, as this information has been <a href="/wiki/Declassification" title="Declassification">declassified</a> due to its age. Some information about programs prior to that time is still <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">classified</a>, and a small amount of information is available on subsequent missions.\n</p><p>A few up-to-date reconnaissance satellite images have been declassified on occasion, or leaked, as in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KH-11" title="KH-11">KH-11</a> photographs which were sent to <i><a href="/wiki/Jane%27s_Defence_Weekly" title="Jane\'s Defence Weekly">Jane\'s Defence Weekly</a></i> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-1985-Morison_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-1985-Morison-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Reconnaissance_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 16 March 1955, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> officially ordered the development of an advanced reconnaissance satellite to provide continuous surveillance of "preselected areas of the Earth" in order "to determine the status of a potential enemy&#8217;s war-making capability".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reconnaissance_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are several major types of reconnaissance satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Missile Early warning</dt></dl>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Defense_Support_Program" title="Defense Support Program">Defense Support Program</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space-Based_Infrared_System" title="Space-Based Infrared System">Space-Based Infrared System</a></div>\n<dl><dd>Provides warning of an attack by detecting <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missile</a> launches. Earliest known are <a href="/wiki/Missile_Defense_Alarm_System" title="Missile Defense Alarm System">Missile Defense Alarm System</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Nuclear explosion detection</dt>\n<dd>Identifies and characterizes <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_explosion" title="Nuclear explosion">nuclear explosions</a> in space. <a href="/wiki/Vela_(satellite)" title="Vela (satellite)">Vela (satellite)</a> is the earliest known.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Photo surveillance</dt>\n<dd>Provides imaging of earth from space. Images can be a survey or close-look <a href="/wiki/Telephoto_lens" title="Telephoto lens">telephoto</a>. <a href="/wiki/Corona_(satellite)" title="Corona (satellite)">Corona (satellite)</a> is the earliest known. <a href="/wiki/Spectral_imaging" title="Spectral imaging">Spectral imaging</a> is commonplace.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Electronic-reconnaissance</dt>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Signals_intelligence" title="Signals intelligence">Signals intelligence</a>, intercepts stray <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> waves. <a href="/wiki/Samos-F" title="Samos-F">Samos-F</a> is the earliest known.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Radar imaging</dt>\n<dd>Most <a href="/wiki/Space-based_radar" title="Space-based radar">space-based radars</a> use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a>. Can be used at night or through <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud cover</a>. Earliest known are the Soviet <a href="/wiki/US-A" title="US-A">US-A</a> series.</dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reconnaissance_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Examples of reconnaissance satellite missions:\n</p>\n<ul><li>High resolution photography (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IMINT" title="IMINT">IMINT</a>)</li>\n<li>Measurement and Signature Intelligence (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MASINT" title="MASINT">MASINT</a>)</li>\n<li>Communications eavesdropping (<a href="/wiki/Signals_intelligence" title="Signals intelligence">SIGINT</a>)</li>\n<li>Covert communications</li>\n<li>Monitoring of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty">nuclear test ban</a> compliance (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Technical_Means" title="National Technical Means">National Technical Means</a>)</li>\n<li>Detection of missile launches</li></ul>\n<p>On 28 August 2013, it was thought that "a $1-billion high-powered spy satellite capable of snapping pictures detailed enough to distinguish the make and model of an automobile hundreds of miles below"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes-Rocket-2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-Rocket-2013-6">[6]</a></sup> was launched  from California\'s Vandenberg Air Force Base using a Delta IV Heavy launcher, America\'s highest-payload space launch vehicle.\n</p><p>On 17 February 2014, a Russian Kosmos-1220 originally launched in 1980 and used for naval missile targeting until 1982, made an uncontrolled <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">atmospheric entry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reconnaissance_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Reconnaissance satellites have been used to enforce human rights, through the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Sentinel_Project" title="Satellite Sentinel Project">Satellite Sentinel Project</a>, which monitors atrocities in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Sudan" title="South Sudan">South Sudan</a>.\n</p><p>During his <a href="/wiki/1980_State_of_the_Union_Address" title="1980 State of the Union Address">1980 State of the Union Address</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> explained how all of humanity benefited from the presence of American spy satellites:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...photo-reconnaissance satellites, for example, are enormously important in stabilizing world affairs and thereby make a significant contribution to the security of all nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Additionally, companies such as <a href="/wiki/GeoEye" title="GeoEye">GeoEye</a> and <a href="/wiki/DigitalGlobe" title="DigitalGlobe">DigitalGlobe</a> have provided commercial satellite imagery in support of natural disaster response and humanitarian missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1950s, a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> hoax had led to American fears of a <a href="/wiki/Bomber_gap" title="Bomber gap">bomber gap</a>. In 1968, after gaining satellite photography, the United States\' intelligence agencies were able to state with certainty that "No new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBM</a> complexes have been established in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> during the past year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heppenheimer1998_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-heppenheimer1998-10">[10]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> told a gathering in 1967:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I wouldn\'t want to be quoted on this ... We\'ve spent $35 or $40 billion on the space program. And if nothing else had come out of it except the knowledge that we gained from space photography, it would be worth ten times what the whole program has cost. Because tonight we know how many missiles the enemy has and, it turned out, <a href="/wiki/Missile_gap" title="Missile gap">our guesses were way off</a>. We were doing things we didn\'t need to do. We were building things we didn\'t need to build. We were harboring fears we didn\'t need to harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heppenheimer1998_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-heppenheimer1998-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reconnaissance_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Spy satellites are commonly seen in <a href="/wiki/Spy_fiction" title="Spy fiction">spy fiction</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_fiction" title="Military fiction">military fiction</a>.
