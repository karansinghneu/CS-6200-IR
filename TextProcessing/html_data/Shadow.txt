class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Point and non-point light sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_umbra,_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png/220px-Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png/330px-Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png/440px-Diagram_of_umbra%2C_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diagram_of_umbra,_penumbra_%26_antumbra.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra" title="Umbra, penumbra and antumbra">Umbra, penumbra and antumbra</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Point_source" title="Point source">point source</a> of light casts only a simple shadow, called an "<b>umbra</b>". For a non-point or "extended" source of light, the shadow is divided into the <a href="/wiki/Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra" title="Umbra, penumbra and antumbra">umbra, penumbra and antumbra</a>. The wider the light source, the more blurred the shadow becomes.  If two penumbras overlap, the shadows appear to attract and merge.  This is known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Shadow_blister_effect" title="Shadow blister effect">Shadow blister effect</a></b>.\n</p><p>The outlines of the shadow zones can be found by tracing the rays of light emitted by the outermost regions of the extended light source. The umbra region does not receive any direct light from any part of the light source, and is the darkest. A viewer located in the umbra region cannot directly see any part of the light source.\n</p><p>By contrast, the penumbra is illuminated by some parts of the light source, giving it an intermediate level of light intensity. A viewer located in the penumbra region will see the light source, but it is partially blocked by the object casting the shadow.\n</p><p>If there is more than one light source, there will be several shadows, with the overlapping parts darker, and various combinations of brightnesses or even colors. The more <a href="/wiki/Diffuser_(optics)" title="Diffuser (optics)">diffuse</a> the lighting is, the softer and more indistinct the shadow outlines become, until they disappear. The lighting of an overcast sky produces few visible shadows.\n</p><p>The absence of diffusing atmospheric effects in the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> produces shadows that are stark and sharply delineated by high-contrast boundaries between light and dark.\n</p><p>For a person or object touching the surface where the shadow is projected (e.g. a person standing on the ground, or a pole in the ground) the shadows converge at the point of contact.\n</p><p>A shadow shows, apart from distortion, the same image as the silhouette when looking at the object from the sun-side, hence the <a href="/wiki/Mirror_image" title="Mirror image">mirror image</a> of the silhouette seen from the other side.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Three_moons_and_their_shadows_parade_across_Jupiter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1282" shadows parade across <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The names <a href="/wiki/Umbra,_penumbra_and_antumbra" title="Umbra, penumbra and antumbra">umbra, penumbra and antumbra</a> are often used for the shadows cast by <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a>, though they are sometimes used to describe levels of darkness, such as in sunspots.  An astronomical object casts human-visible shadows when its <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> is equal or lower than -4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Currently the only astronomical objects able to produce visible shadows on Earth are the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a> and, in the right conditions, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A shadow cast by the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a>. Conversely, a shadow cast by the Moon on the Earth is a <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Daytime_variation">Daytime variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Daytime variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The sun casts shadows which change dramatically through the day. The length of a shadow cast on the ground is proportional to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotangent" title="Cotangent">cotangent</a> of the sun\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_(astronomy)#Elevation_angle" title="Altitude (astronomy)">elevation angle</a>&#8212;its angle <i>&#952;</i> relative to the horizon. Near sunrise and sunset, when <i>&#952;</i> = 0&#176; and <i>cot(&#952;)</i> = &#8734;, shadows can be extremely long. If the sun passes directly overhead, then <i>&#952;</i> = 90&#176;, <i>cot(&#952;)</i> = 0, and shadows are cast directly underneath objects.\n</p><p>Such variations have long aided travellers during their travels, especially in barren regions such as the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Desert" title="Arabian Desert">Arabian Desert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation_speed">Propagation speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Crepuscular rays">Crepuscular rays</a> are also visible.</div></div></div>\n<p>The farther the distance from the object blocking the light to the surface of projection, the larger the silhouette (they are considered <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">proportional</a>). Also, if the object is moving, the shadow cast by the object will project an image with dimensions (length) expanding proportionally faster than the object\'s own rate of movement. The increase of size and movement is also true if the distance between the object of interference and the light source are closer. This, however, does not mean the shadow may move faster than light, even when projected at vast distances, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a>. The loss of light, which projects the shadow, will move towards the surface of projection at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_speed" title="Light speed">light speed</a>.\n</p><p>Although the edge of a shadow appears to "move" along a wall, in actuality the increase of a shadow\'s length is part of a new projection which propagates at the speed of light from the object of interference. Since there is no actual communication between points in a shadow (except for reflection or interference of light, at the speed of light), a shadow that projects over a surface of large distances (light years) <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity#Causality_and_prohibition_of_motion_faster_than_light" title="Special relativity">cannot convey information</a> between those distances with the shadow\'s edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Color">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Visual artists are usually very aware of colored light emitted or reflected from several sources, which can generate complex multicolored shadows. <a href="/wiki/Chiaroscuro" title="Chiaroscuro">Chiaroscuro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sfumato" title="Sfumato">sfumato</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Silhouette" title="Silhouette">silhouette</a> are examples of artistic techniques which make deliberate use of shadow effects.\n</p><p>During the daytime, a shadow cast by an opaque object illuminated by sunlight has a bluish tinge. This happens because of <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>, the same property that causes the sky to appear blue. The opaque object is able to block the light of the sun, but not the ambient light of the sky which is blue as the atmosphere molecules scatter blue light more effectively. As a result, the shadow appears bluish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-dimensional_shadows">Three-dimensional shadows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Three-dimensional shadows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1097" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg/220px-Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg/330px-Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg/440px-Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fog_shadow_of_GGB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fog shadow of the south tower of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a></div></div></div>\n<p>A shadow occupies a three-dimensional volume of space, but this is usually not visible until it projects onto a reflective surface. A light <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, mist, or dust cloud can reveal the 3D presence of volumetric patterns in light and shadow.\n</p><p>Fog shadows may look odd to viewers who are not used to seeing shadows in three dimensions. A thin fog is just dense enough to be illuminated by the light that passes through the gaps in a structure or in a tree.  As a result, the path of an object\'s shadow through the fog becomes visible as a darkened volume. In a sense, these shadow lanes are the inverse of <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular_rays" title="Crepuscular rays">crepuscular rays</a> caused by beams of light, but caused by the shadows of solid objects.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theatrical_fog" title="Theatrical fog">Theatrical fog</a> and strong beams of light are sometimes used by lighting designers and visual artists who seek to highlight three-dimensional aspects of their work. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shadow_inversions">Shadow inversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shadow inversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Oftentimes shadows of chain-linked fences and other such objects become inverted (light and dark areas are swapped) as they get farther from the object. A chain-link fence shadow will start with light diamonds and shadow outlines when it is touching the fence, but it will gradually blur. Eventually, if the fence is tall enough, the light pattern will go to shadow diamonds and light outlines.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">Photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moonlight_shadow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4364" data-file-width="3283" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Moonlight_shadow.jpg/170px-Moonlight_shadow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Moonlight_shadow.jpg/255px-Moonlight_shadow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Moonlight_shadow.jpg/340px-Moonlight_shadow.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moonlight_shadow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">Moonlight</a> shadow of a photographer</div></div></div>\n<p>In photography, which is essentially recording patterns of light, shade, and color, "highlights" and "shadows" are the brightest and darkest parts, respectively, of a scene or image. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photographic_exposure" title="Photographic exposure">Photographic exposure</a> must be adjusted (unless special effects are wanted) to allow the <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">film</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensor</a>, which has limited <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a>, to record detail in the highlights without them being washed out, and in the shadows without their becoming undifferentiated black areas.\n</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_photograph" title="Aerial photograph">aerial photographs</a>, taken vertically, tall buildings can be recognized as such by their long shadows (if the photographs are not taken in the tropics around noon), while these also show more of the shape of these buildings.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogous_concepts">Analogous concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Analogous concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Shadow</i> as a term is often used for any occlusion or blockage, not just those with respect to light.  For example, a <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> is a dry area, which with respect to the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> direction, is beyond a <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a>; the elevated terrain impedes rainclouds from entering the dry zone. An <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_shadow" title="Acoustic shadow">acoustic shadow</a> occurs when direct sound has been blocked or diverted around a given area.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_aspects">Cultural aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shadow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An unattended <a href="/wiki/Shade_(mythology)" title="Shade (mythology)">shade</a> was thought by some cultures to be similar to that of a ghost. The name for the fear of shadows is "sciophobia" or "sciaphobia".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chhaya" title="Chhaya">Chhaya</a> is the Hindu goddess of shadows.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a>, when a charge is supposedly shown "in shadow" (the appearance is of the charge merely being outlined in a neutral tint rather than being of one or more <a href="/wiki/Tincture_(heraldry)" title="Tincture (heraldry)">tinctures</a> different from the field on which it is placed), it is technnically described as "umbrated".
