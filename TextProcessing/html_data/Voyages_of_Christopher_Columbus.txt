href="/wiki/Transatlantic_crossing" title="Transatlantic crossing">transatlantic</a> maritime expedition led by Italian explorer <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> encountered the Americas, continents which were largely unknown in Europe and were outside the <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> political and economic system. The four voyages of Columbus began the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization of the Americas</a>.\n</p><p>For a long time it was generally believed that Columbus and his crew had been the first Europeans to make landfall in the Americas. In fact they were not the first explorers from Europe to reach the Americas, having been preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Viking</a> expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a> in the <a href="/wiki/11th_century" title="11th century">11th century</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> however, Columbus\'s voyages were the ones that led to ongoing European contact with the Americas, inaugurating a period of <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">exploration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conquest_(military)" title="Conquest (military)">conquest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_colonialism" title="History of colonialism">colonization</a> whose effects and consequences persist to the present.\n</p><p>Columbus was an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigator</a> sailing for the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a> (Spain) in search of a westward route to Asia, to access the sources of <a href="/wiki/Spice_trade" title="Spice trade">spices</a> and other oriental goods. This failed when he encountered the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> between Europe and Asia. Columbus made a total of four voyages to the Americas between 1492 and 1502, setting the stage for the European exploration and colonization of the Americas, ultimately leading to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbian_Exchange" title="Columbian Exchange">Columbian Exchange</a>.\n</p><p>At the time of the Columbus voyages, the Americas were inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous Americans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Genetic_history_of_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Genetic history of indigenous peoples of the Americas">descendants</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a> who <a href="/wiki/Settlement_of_the_Americas" title="Settlement of the Americas">crossed</a> <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> to <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> beginning around 20,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Columbus\'s voyages led to the widespread knowledge that a continent existed west of Europe and east of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. This breakthrough in geographical science led to the exploration and colonization of the New World by <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a> and other European sea powers, and is sometimes cited as the start of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_era" title="Modern era">modern era</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portugal</a>, and other European kingdoms sent expeditions and <a href="/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="European colonization of the Americas">established colonies</a> throughout the New World, converted the native inhabitants to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and built large <a href="/wiki/Columbian_exchange" title="Columbian exchange">trade networks</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, which introduced new plants, animals, and food crops to both continents. The search for a westward route to Asia continued in 1513 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasco_Nu%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Nu&#241;ez de Balboa">Vasco Nu&#241;ez de Balboa</a> crossed the narrow <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> to become the first European to sight the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. The search was completed in 1521, when the Castilian (<i>Spanish</i>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Magellan%27s_circumnavigation" title="Timeline of Magellan\'s circumnavigation">Magellan expedition</a> sailed across the Pacific and reached <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="#cite_note-Phillips-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> had been the main European power interested in pursuing trade routes overseas. Their next-door neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a> (predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>) had been somewhat slower to begin exploring the Atlantic because of the bigger land area it had to re-conquer (the <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i>) from the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>. It was not until the late 15th century, following the <a href="/wiki/Dynastic_union" title="Dynastic union">dynastic union</a> of the Crowns of <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Aragon</a> and the completion of the <i>Reconquista</i>, that the unified crowns of what would become Spain (although countries still legally existing) emerged and became fully committed to looking for new trade routes and colonies overseas. In 1492 the joint rulers conquered the <a href="/wiki/Granada#Nasrid_Kingdom_of_Granada" title="Granada">Moorish kingdom of Granada</a>, which had been providing Castile with African goods through <a href="/wiki/Tribute" title="Tribute">tribute</a>. Columbus had previously failed to convince King <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">John II of Portugal</a> to fund his exploration of a western route, but the new king and queen of the re-conquered Spain decided to fund Columbus\'s expedition in hopes of bypassing Portugal\'s lock on Africa and the Indian Ocean, reaching Asia by traveling west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_campaign">Funding campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Funding campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>He proposed the king equip three sturdy ships and grant Columbus one year\'s time to sail out west into the Atlantic, search for a western route to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and return. Columbus also requested he be made "Great Admiral of the Ocean Sea" (Atlantic Ocean), appointed governor of any and all lands he discovered, and be given one-tenth of all revenue from those lands. The king submitted the proposal to his experts, who rejected it after several years. It was their considered opinion that Columbus\'s estimation of a travel distance of 2,400 miles (3,900&#160;km) was, in fact, far too short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison-Admiral_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morison-Admiral-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1488 Columbus appealed to the court of Portugal, receiving a new invitation for an audience with King John II. This also proved unsuccessful, in part because not long afterwards <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a> returned to Portugal following a successful rounding of the southern tip of Africa. With an eastern sea route now under its control, Portugal was no longer interested in trailblazing a western trade route to Asia crossing unknown seas. Columbus traveled from Portugal to Castile to convince the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> of Castile and Aragon to finance the expedition.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_procurement">Spanish procurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanish procurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<table class="box-Refimprove_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. They were known jointly as the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a>, and ruled their kingdoms independently, but had common internal and foreign policies.\n</p><p>Columbus was granted an audience with them; on May 1, 1489, he presented his plans to Queen Isabella, who referred them to a committee. They pronounced the idea impractical, and advised the monarchs not to support the proposed venture.\n</p><p>However, to expand the Spanish empire and Catholicism in the name of Spanish Kings, and to assure a better market position in trading, the Queen gave Columbus an annual allowance of 12,000 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maravedis" title="Maravedis">maravedis</a></i> and part of the newly conquered lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_(Madrid)_02b.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1835" data-file-width="2111" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg/220px-Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg/330px-Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg/440px-Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_%28Madrid%29_02b.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Col%C3%B3n_(Madrid)_02b.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbus and Queen Isabella. Detail of the Columbus monument in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> (1885).</div></div></div>\n<p>After continually lobbying at the royal court and enduring two years of negotiations, Columbus finally succeeded in January 1492. Queen Isabella\'s forces had just conquered the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a>, the last Muslim stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Al-Andalus</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a>, for Castile. Isabella and Ferdinand received Columbus in the <i><a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar_de_los_Reyes_Cristianos" title="Alc&#225;zar de los Reyes Cristianos">Alc&#225;zar</a></i> (castle) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_(Spain)" title="C&#243;rdoba (Spain)">C&#243;rdoba</a> to support his plans.\n</p><p>The monarchs left it to the royal treasurer to shift funds among various royal accounts on behalf of the enterprise. Columbus was to be made "Admiral of the Seas" and would receive a portion of all profits. The terms were unusually generous but, as his son later wrote, the monarchs were not confident of his return.\n</p><p>According to Columbus\'s contract made for the expedition commission by Queen Isabella for Castile, if Columbus claimed any new islands or mainland for the Crown, he would receive many high rewards. In terms of power, he would be given the rank of Admiral of the Ocean Sea and appointed <a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">Viceroy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Governor#Other_colonial_empires" title="Governor">Governor</a> of the newly colonised lands. He had the right to nominate three people, from whom the sovereigns would choose one, for any office in the new lands. He would be entitled to ten per cent of all the revenues from the new lands in perpetuity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation_plans">Navigation plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Navigation plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Europe had long enjoyed a safe land passage to China and India&#8212;sources of valued goods such as <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spices" title="Spices">spices</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opiates</a>&#8212;under the <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol Empire</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Pax_Mongolica" title="Pax Mongolica">Pax Mongolica</a>, or <i>Mongol peace</i>). With the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">Fall of Constantinople</a> to the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> in 1453, the land route to Asia became more difficult. In response to this the <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Columbus" title="Bartholomew Columbus">Columbus brothers</a> had, by the 1480s, developed a plan to travel to the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">Indies</a>, then construed roughly as all of southern and eastern Asia, by sailing directly west across what was believed to be the singular "<a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">Ocean Sea</a>," the Atlantic Ocean.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">Washington Irving</a>\'s 1828 biography of Columbus popularized the idea that Columbus had difficulty obtaining support for his plan because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">Europeans</a> thought <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_earth" title="Flat earth">the Earth was flat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-book3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-book3-10">[10]</a></sup> In fact, the primitive maritime navigation of the time relied on the stars and the curvature of the <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">spherical Earth</a>. The knowledge that the Earth was spherical was widespread, and the means of calculating its diameter using an <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a> was known to both scholars and navigators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diameter_of_Earth_and_travel_distance_estimates">Diameter of Earth and travel distance estimates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diameter of Earth and travel distance estimates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="635" data-file-width="1022" decoding="async" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg/400px-Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg/600px-Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg/800px-Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Conceptions_Colomb_map-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbus\'s geographical conceptions</div></div></div>\n<p>A spherical Earth had been the general opinion of Ancient Greek science, and this view continued through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a> mentions it in <i>The Reckoning of Time</i>). In fact <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a> had measured the diameter of the Earth with good precision in the 2nd century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan-12">[12]</a></sup> Where Columbus did differ from the generally accepted view of his time was in his incorrect arguments that assumed a significantly smaller diameter for the Earth, claiming that Asia could be easily reached by sailing west across the Atlantic. Most scholars accepted <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s correct assessment that the terrestrial landmass (for Europeans of the time, comprising Eurasia and Africa) occupied 180 <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">degrees</a> of the terrestrial sphere, and dismissed Columbus\'s claim that the Earth was much smaller, and that Asia was only a few thousand nautical miles to the west of Europe. Columbus\'s error was attributed to his insufficient experience in navigation at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison-Admiral_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morison-Admiral-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ColombusMap.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1260" data-file-width="1970" decoding="async" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ColombusMap.jpg/220px-ColombusMap.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ColombusMap.jpg/330px-ColombusMap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ColombusMap.jpg/440px-ColombusMap.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ColombusMap.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "Columbus map" was drawn circa 1490 in the workshop of Bartolomeo and Christopher Columbus in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ColombusNotesToMarcoPolo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="968" Polo">Marco Polo</a>\'s <i>Le livre des merveilles</i>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Columbus believed the incorrect calculations of <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">Marinus of Tyre</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-14">[14]</a></sup> putting the landmass at 225 degrees, leaving only 135 degrees of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Moreover, Columbus believed that one degree represented a shorter distance on the Earth\'s surface than was actually the case &#8211; he read maps as if the distances were calculated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_mile" title="Italian mile">Italian miles</a> (about 1,480 meters). Accepting the length of a degree to be 56&#8532; miles, from the writings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfraganus" title="Alfraganus">Alfraganus</a>, he therefore calculated the circumference of the Earth as 25,255&#160;km (13,637 nautical miles; 15,693 miles) at most, and the distance from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> to Japan as 3,000 Italian miles (3,700&#160;km [2,000 nautical miles; 2,300 miles]). Columbus did not realize Alfraganus used the much longer Arabic mile (about 1,830 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The true circumference of the Earth is about 40,000&#160;km (22,000 nautical miles; 25,000 miles), a figure first established approximately by <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a> in the 2nd century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan-12">[12]</a></sup> and the distance from the Canary Islands to Japan 19,600&#160;km (10,600 nautical miles; 12,200 miles). No ship that was readily available in the 15th century could carry enough food and fresh water for such a journey. Most European sailors and navigators concluded, probably correctly, that sailors undertaking a westward voyage from Europe to Asia non-stop would die of thirst, <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a> or starvation long before reaching their destination. Spain, however, having just completed the expensive <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, was desperate for a competitive edge over other European countries in trade with the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>. Columbus promised such an advantage.\n</p><p>Europeans generally assumed that the aquatic expanse between Europe and Asia was uninterrupted. While hints of North America, <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, were already surfacing in Europe, historians agree that Columbus calculated too short a distance from the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> to Japan by the standards of his peers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_winds">Trade winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trade winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>There was a further element of key importance in the plans of Columbus, a closely held fact discovered by or otherwise learned by Columbus: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_Winds" title="Trade Winds">Trade Winds</a>. A brisk westward wind from the east, commonly called an "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easterlies" title="Easterlies">easterly</a>", propelled <i><a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_(ship)" title="Santa Mar&#237;a (ship)">Santa Mar&#237;a</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1a" title="Ni&#241;a">La Ni&#241;a</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Pinta_(ship)" title="Pinta (ship)">La Pinta</a></i> for five weeks from the Canary Islands off Africa. To return to Spain eastward against this prevailing wind would have required several months of an arduous sailing technique upwind, called <a href=\'/wiki/Sailing#Close_Hauled_or_"Beating"\' title="Sailing">beating</a>, during which food and drinkable water would have been utterly exhausted. Columbus returned home by following prevailing winds northeastward from the southern zone of the North Atlantic to the middle latitudes of the North Atlantic, where prevailing winds are eastward (westerly) to the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, where the winds curve southward towards the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. So he used the North Atlantic\'s great circular wind pattern, clockwise in direction, in both legs of his voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voyages_and_events">Voyages and events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Voyages and events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage">First voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: First voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At 8:00 on the morning of 3 August 1492, Columbus departed from Castilian <a href="/wiki/Palos_de_la_Frontera" title="Palos de la Frontera">Palos de la Frontera</a> (on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rio_Saltes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rio Saltes (page does not exist)">river Saltes</a>, at the confluence of the rivers <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">Rio Tinto</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rio_Odiel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rio Odiel (page does not exist)">Rio Odiel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tharoor_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-tharoor-18">[18]</a></sup> Columbus and his crew embarked on a voyage to find a shorter route to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">Orient</a> with three medium-sized ships, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1a" title="Ni&#241;a">Ni&#241;a</a></i> (real name <i>Santa Clara</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Pinta_(ship)" title="Pinta (ship)"><i>Pinta</i></a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_(ship)" title="Santa Mar&#237;a (ship)"><i>Santa Maria</i></a>. The ships were the property of <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_la_Cosa" title="Juan de la Cosa">Juan de la Cosa</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pinz%C3%B3n_Brothers" title="The Pinz&#243;n Brothers">Pinz&#243;n brothers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Alonso_Pinz%C3%B3n" title="Mart&#237;n Alonso Pinz&#243;n">Mart&#237;n Alonso Pinz&#243;n</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Y%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Pinz%C3%B3n" title="Vicente Y&#225;&#241;ez Pinz&#243;n">Vicente Y&#225;&#241;ez Pinz&#243;n</a>), but the monarchs forced the <a href="/wiki/Palos_de_la_Frontera" title="Palos de la Frontera">Palos de la Frontera</a> inhabitants to contribute to the expedition as well.\n</p>\n<table align="center" class="toccolours" width="515px">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_(ship)" title="Santa Mar&#237;a (ship)">Santa Mar&#237;a</a></i>, was always referred to by Columbus as <i>La Capitana</i> ("The Captain"). The two smaller caravels, <i>La <a href="/wiki/Pinta_(ship)" title="Pinta (ship)">Pinta</a></i> ("The Painted") and <i>La <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1a" title="Ni&#241;a">Ni&#241;a</a></i> (<small>lit.</small> "The Girl", but actually named after her owners, the <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1o_brothers" title="Ni&#241;o brothers">Ni&#241;o brothers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moguer" title="Moguer">Moguer</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> are today better known by their nicknames: the real name of the <i>Pinta</i> has been lost; the <i>Ni&#241;a</i> was actually named <i>Santa Clara</i>, after the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of Moguer.\n</p>\n<div class="hatnote Piloty</div></div></div>\n<p>Three days into the journey, on 6 August 1492, the rudder of the <i>Pinta</i> broke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The owners of the ship, Gomez Rascon and Christoval Quintero, were suspected of sabotage, as they and their ship had been pressed into service against their will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The crew was able to secure the rudder with ropes until they could reach the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, where they arrived on 9 August 1492.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m20_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-m20-22">[22]</a></sup> Here the <i>Pinta</i> was repaired and the <i>Ni&#241;a</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Lateen" title="Lateen">lateen</a> sails were re-rigged to standard square sails. While securing provisions from the island of <a href="/wiki/La_Gomera" title="La Gomera">La Gomera</a>, Columbus received word that three Portuguese caravels had been seen hovering near the island of <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">El Hierro</a> with the supposed intention of capturing him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, on September 6, 1492 the westward voyage began without incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-m22_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-m22-24">[24]</a></sup> The ships departed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_de_la_Gomera" title="San Sebasti&#225;n de la Gomera">San Sebasti&#225;n de la Gomera</a> for what turned out to be a six-week long voyage across the Atlantic.\n</p><p>As described in the abstract of his log made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartolome_de_Las_Casas" title="Bartolome de Las Casas">Bartolome de Las Casas</a>, on the outward bound voyage Columbus recorded two sets of distances. Las Casas originally interpreted that he reported the shorter distances to his crew so they would not worry about sailing too far from Spain. However, according to Oliver Dunn and James Kelley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> this was a misunderstanding by Las Casas. Columbus did report two distances each day but one was in measurements he normally used, the other in the Portuguese maritime leagues used by his crew.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_declination">Magnetic declination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Magnetic declination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On 13 September 1492, Columbus observed that <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">the needle of his compass no longer pointed to the North Star</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The needle instead had varied a half point to the northwest, and continued to vary further as the journey progressed. Columbus at first made no mention of this, knowing his crew to be prone to panic with their destination unknown, but after several days his pilots took notice with much anxiety. Allegedly the crew grew so homesick and fearful that they threatened to sail back to Spain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus reasoned that the needle did not point to the North Star, but to <a href="/wiki/North_Magnetic_Pole" title="North Magnetic Pole">some invisible point on the Earth</a>. His reputation as an astronomer held weight with the crew, and his theory alleviated their alarm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was once believed that Columbus had discovered <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">magnetic declination</a>, but it was later shown that the phenomenon was already known, both in Europe and in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="European_discovery_and_exploration">European discovery and exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: European discovery and exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_first_voyage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="1001" decoding="async" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Columbus_first_voyage.jpg/400px-Columbus_first_voyage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Columbus_first_voyage.jpg/600px-Columbus_first_voyage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Columbus_first_voyage.jpg/800px-Columbus_first_voyage.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_first_voyage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First voyage. Modern place names in black, Columbus\'s place names in blue</div></div></div>\n<p>After twenty-nine days out of sight of land, on 7 October 1492, the crew spotted "[i]mmense flocks of birds", some of which his sailors trapped and determined to be "field" birds (probably <a href="/wiki/Eskimo_curlew" title="Eskimo curlew">Eskimo curlews</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_golden_plover" title="American golden plover">American golden plovers</a>). Columbus changed course to follow their flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Land was first sighted at 2 <a href="/wiki/12-hour_clock" title="12-hour clock">a.m.</a> on October 12, 1492, by a sailor named <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Triana" title="Rodrigo de Triana">Rodrigo de Triana</a> (also known as Juan Rodriguez Bermejo) aboard <i>La Pinta</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Columbus would later assert that he had first seen the land and, thus, earned the reward of 10,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_maraved%C3%AD" title="Spanish maraved&#237;">maraved&#237;s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Columbus called the island San Salvador, in the present-day <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">Bahamas</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos" title="Turks and Caicos">Turks and Caicos</a>; the indigenous residents had named it <a href="/wiki/Guanahani" title="Guanahani">Guanahani</a>. Exactly which island in the Bahamas or Turks and Caicos this corresponds to is an unresolved topic; prime candidates are <a href="/wiki/Samana_Cay" title="Samana Cay">Samana Cay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plana_Cays" title="Plana Cays">Plana Cays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cat_Island,_Bahamas" title="Cat Island, Bahamas">Cat Island</a> or <a href="/wiki/San_Salvador_Island" title="San Salvador Island">San Salvador Island</a> (<i>Watlings Island</i> named San Salvador in 1925 in the belief that it was Columbus\'s San Salvador).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Landing_of_Columbus_(2).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1971" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg/220px-Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg/330px-Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg/440px-Landing_of_Columbus_%282%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Landing_of_Columbus_(2).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of Columbus claiming possession of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravels</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1a" title="Ni&#241;a">Ni&#241;a</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Pinta_(ship)" title="Pinta (ship)">Pinta</a></i>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous people</a> he encountered in their homelands were peaceful and friendly. At the time of the European discovery of most of the islands of the Caribbean, three major indigenous peoples lived on the islands: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Ta&#237;no people">Ta&#237;no</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, the Bahamas, and the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Island Caribs</a> (Kalina) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galibi" title="Galibi">Galibi</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Ciboney" title="Ciboney">Ciboney</a> (a Ta&#237;no people) and <a href="/wiki/Guanahatabey" title="Guanahatabey">Guanahatabey</a> of central and western Cuba, respectively. The Ta&#237;nos are subdivided into Classic Ta&#237;nos, who occupied Hispaniola and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>; Western Ta&#237;nos, who occupied Cuba, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamian_archipelago" title="Bahamian archipelago">Bahamian archipelago</a>; and the Eastern Ta&#237;nos, who occupied the Leeward Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> was inhabited by both <a href="/wiki/Carib_language" title="Carib language">Carib-speaking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arawak_language" title="Arawak language">Arawak-speaking</a> groups. Most of modern <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerican</a> civilization. The <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Amerindian</a> societies of Mesoamerica occupied the land ranging from central <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in the north to <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> in the south. The cultures of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> traded with both Mesoamerica and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and can be considered transitional between those two cultural areas.\n</p><p>Columbus proceeded to observe the people and their cultural lifestyle. He also explored the northeast coast of Cuba, landing on 28 October 1492, and the north-western coast of Hispaniola, present day <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, by 5 December 1492. Here, the <i>Santa Maria</i> ran aground on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>, December 25, 1492, and had to be abandoned. Columbus was received by the native <a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">cacique</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guacanagari" title="Guacanagari">Guacanagari</a>, who gave him permission to leave some of his men behind. Columbus founded the settlement, <i><a href="/wiki/La_Navidad" title="La Navidad">La Navidad</a></i>, leaving behind 39 men.\n</p><p>On 15 January 1493, he set sail for home aboard the <i>Ni&#241;a</i>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_return">First return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: First return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>While returning to Spain, the <i>Ni&#241;a</i> and <i>Pinta</i> encountered the roughest storm of their journey, and, on the night of 13 February, lost contact with each other. All hands on the <i>Ni&#241;a</i> vowed, if they were spared, to make a pilgrimage to the nearest church of Our Lady wherever they first made land. On the morning of February 15, land was spotted. Columbus believed they were approaching the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azore_Islands" title="Azore Islands">Azore Islands</a>, but other members of the crew felt that they were considerably north of the islands. Columbus turned out to be right. On the night of February 17, the <i>Ni&#241;a</i> laid anchor at <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Island" title="Santa Maria Island">Santa Maria Island</a>, but the cable broke on sharp rocks, forcing Columbus to stay offshore until the morning, when a safer location was found to drop anchor nearby. A few sailors took a boat to the island, where they were told by several islanders of a still safer place to land, so the Ni&#241;a moved once again. At this spot, Columbus took on board several islanders who had gathered onshore with food, and told them that his crew wished to come ashore to fulfill their vow. The islanders told him that a small shrine dedicated to Our Lady was nearby. Columbus sent half of the crew members to the island to fulfill their vow, but he and the rest of the crew stayed on the Ni&#241;a, planning to send the other half to the island upon the return of the first crew members. While the first crew members were saying their prayers at the shrine, they were taken prisoner by the islanders, under orders from the island\'s captain, Jo&#227;o de Castanheira, ostensibly out of fear that the men were pirates. The boat that the crew members had taken to the island was then commandeered by Castanheira, which he took with several armed men to the <i>Ni&#241;a</i>, in an attempt to arrest Columbus. During a verbal battle across the bows of both craft, during which Columbus did not grant permission for him to come aboard, Castanheira announced that he did not believe or care who Columbus said that he was, especially if he was indeed from Spain. Castanheira returned to the island. However, after another two days, Castanheira released the prisoners, having been unable to get confessions from them, and having been unable to capture his real target, Columbus. There are later claims that Columbus was also captured, but this is not backed up by Columbus\'s log book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leaving the island of Santa Maria in the Azores on 23 February, Columbus headed for Castilian Spain, but another storm forced him into <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>. He anchored next to the king\'s harbor patrol ship on March 4, 1493, where he was told a fleet of 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caravels" title="Caravels">caravels</a> had been lost in the storm. Astoundingly, both the <i>Ni&#241;a</i> and the <i>Pinta</i> had been spared. Not finding <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">King John II</a> of Portugal in Lisbon, Columbus wrote a letter to him and waited for the king\'s reply. After receiving the letter, the king agreed to meet with Columbus in <i>Vale do Para&#237;so</i> despite poor relations between Portugal and Castile at the time. Upon learning of Columbus\'s discoveries, the Portuguese king informed him that he believed the voyage to be in violation of the 1479 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Alc%C3%A1%C3%A7ovas" title="Treaty of Alc&#225;&#231;ovas">Treaty of Alc&#225;&#231;ovas</a>. After spending more than a week in Portugal, Columbus set sail for Spain. Word of his finding new lands rapidly spread throughout Europe. After the voyage, Columbus met with Spanish monarchs <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Ferdinand and Isabella</a> in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> on 15 March 1493 to report his findings. The <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Monument,_Barcelona" title="Columbus Monument, Barcelona"><i>Monument a Colom</i></a> in that city commemorates the event.\n</p><p>Columbus and his remaining crew came home to a hero\'s welcome when they returned to Spain. He showed off what he had brought back from his voyage to the monarchs, including a few small samples of gold, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearls" title="Pearls">pearls</a>, gold jewelry stolen from natives, a few natives he had kidnapped, flowers, and a hammock. He gave the monarchs a few of the gold nuggets, gold jewelry, and pearls, as well as the previously unknown tobacco plant, the pineapple fruit, the turkey, and the hammock. The monarchs invited Columbus to dine with them. A taster even tasted the food from each of his dishes before he ate to "make sure it was not poisoned." He was given his own footmen to open doors for him and to serve him at the table. Columbus was even rewarded with his own coat of arms. He did not bring any of the coveted <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a> spices, such as the exceedingly expensive black pepper, ginger or cloves. In his log, he wrote "there is also plenty of \'aj&#237;\', which is their pepper, which is more valuable than black pepper, and all the people eat nothing else, it being very wholesome".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The word "aj&#237;" is still used in South American Spanish for chili peppers.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Columbus%27s_letter_on_the_first_voyage" title="Columbus\'s letter on the first voyage">Columbus\'s letter on the first voyage</a> to the royal court in Madrid was extravagant. He insisted he had reached Asia (it was Cuba) and an island off the coast of China (Hispaniola). His descriptions were part fact, part fiction: "Hispaniola is a miracle. Mountains and hills, plains and pastures, are both fertile and beautiful ... the harbors are unbelievably good and there are many wide rivers of which the majority contain gold. ... There are many spices, and great mines of gold and other metals..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zinn_2009_3_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zinn_2009_3-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Papal_decree">Papal decree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Papal decree">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization of the Americas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Portuguese colonization of the Americas">Portuguese colonization of the Americas</a></div>\n<p>After Columbus\'s return, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_VI" title="Pope Alexander VI">Pope Alexander VI</a> <a href="/wiki/Inter_caetera" title="Inter caetera">issued a bull</a> which divided the "newly discovered" lands outside Europe between <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Portuguese colonization of the Americas">Portugal</a> along a north&#8211;south meridian 958 leagues west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands (off the coast of Africa in the mid-Atlantic), thus granting Spain all land discovered by Columbus. This division was never accepted by the rulers of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a>. (See also the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a> that followed the papal decree.)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_voyage">Second voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Second voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_second_voyage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="563" data-file-width="1001" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Columbus_second_voyage.jpg/400px-Columbus_second_voyage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Columbus_second_voyage.jpg/600px-Columbus_second_voyage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Columbus_second_voyage.jpg/800px-Columbus_second_voyage.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_second_voyage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbus\'s second voyage</div></div></div>\n<p>Before he left Spain on his second voyage, Columbus had been directed by Ferdinand and Isabella to maintain friendly, even loving, relations with the natives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He set sail from <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a>, Spain, on September 24, 1493.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fleet for the second voyage was much larger: two <a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">naos</a> and 15 <a href="/wiki/Caravel" title="Caravel">caravels</a>. The two naos were the flagship <i>Marigalante</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and the <i>Gallega</i>; the caravels were the <i>Fraila</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <i>San Juan</i>, <i>Colina</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <i>Gallarda</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <i>Gutierre</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <i>Bonial</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> <i>Rodriga</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <i>Triana</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <i>Vieja</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <i>Prieta</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <i>Gorda</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <i>Cardera</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and <i>Quintera</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The <i>Ni&#241;a</i> returned for this expedition, which also included a ship named <i>Pinta</i> probably identical to that from the first expedition. In addition, the expedition saw the construction of the first ship in the Americas, the <i>Santa Cruz</i> or <i>India</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ships_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ships-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_exploration">Caribbean exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Caribbean exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On November 3, 1493, Christopher Columbus landed on a rugged shore on an island that he named <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>. On the same day, he landed at <a href="/wiki/Marie-Galante" title="Marie-Galante">Marie-Galante</a>, which he named Santa Mar&#237;a la Galante. After sailing past Les Saintes (Todos los Santos), he arrived at <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> (Santa Mar&#237;a de Guadalupe), which he explored between November 4 and November 10, 1493. The exact course of his voyage through the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> is debated, but it seems likely that he turned north, sighting and naming many islands including Santa Mar&#237;a de Montserrat (<a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>), Santa Mar&#237;a la Antigua (<a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>), Santa Mar&#237;a la Redonda (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Martin" title="Saint Martin">Saint Martin</a>), and Santa Cruz (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix" title="Saint Croix">Saint Croix</a>). He also sighted and named the island chain of the Santa &#218;rsula y las Once Mil V&#237;rgenes (the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>), and named the islands of Virgen Gorda. He continued to the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, and landed on the island of San Juan Bautista, present day <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, on November 19, 1493. His men rescued two boys who had just been castrated by their captors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hispaniola_and_Jamaica">Hispaniola and Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hispaniola and Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On November 22, he returned to Hispaniola, where he found his men at <a href="/wiki/La_Navidad" title="La Navidad">La Navidad</a> had fallen into dispute with natives in the interior and had been killed, but he did not accuse Chief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guacanagari" title="Guacanagari">Guacanagari</a>, his ally, of any wrongdoing. Another Chief, named Caonabo in <a href="/wiki/Jaragua,_Hispaniola" title="Jaragua, Hispaniola">Jaragua</a>, was charged. Columbus established a new settlement at <a href="/wiki/La_Isabela" title="La Isabela">La Isabela</a>, on the north coast of Hispaniola, where gold had first been found, but it was a poor location and the settlement was short-lived. He spent some time exploring the interior of the island for gold. Finding some, he established a small fort in the interior.\n</p><p>He left Hispaniola on April 24, 1494, and arrived at the island of Juana (<a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>) (which he had discovered and named during his first voyage) on April 30 and <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Bay,_Jamaica" title="Discovery Bay, Jamaica">Discovery Bay, Jamaica</a> on May 5. He explored the south coast of Juana, which he believed to be a peninsula of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> rather than an island, and several nearby islands including La Evangelista (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Youth" title="Isle of Youth">Isle of Youth</a>), before returning to Hispaniola on August 20. After staying for a time on the western end of present-day <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> he finally returned to Spain.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Slavery.2C_settlers.2C_and_tribute"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery,_settlers,_and_tribute">Slavery, settlers, and tribute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Slavery, settlers, and tribute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>During the second voyage,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus sent a letter to the monarchs proposing to enslave some of the Americas\' people, specifically from the <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Carib</a> tribe, on the grounds of their independence-minded aggressiveness and their status as enemies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Ta&#237;no people">Ta&#237;no</a> tribe. Although his petition was refused by the Crown, in February 1495, Columbus disobeyed the Queen and took 1,600 people from the <a href="/wiki/Arawak" title="Arawak">Arawak</a> tribe who were then taken by the Carib as captives and slaves. No room was available for about 400 of the kidnapped Arawak leading to their release. The long-term consequence for the Arawaks of contact with Europeans was that thousands of people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Dubious" title="Talk:Voyages of Christopher Columbus">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>  were almost entirely exterminated by disease, infighting and economic despair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The many voyages of discovery did not pay for themselves, and there was no funding for pure science in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus had planned for Queen Isabella to set up trading posts with the cities of the Far East made famous by <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>, but whose Silk Road and eastern maritime routes had been blockaded to her crown\'s trade. Of course, Columbus would never find <a href="/wiki/Cathay" title="Cathay">Cathay</a> (<a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zipangu" title="Zipangu">Zipangu</a> (<a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>), and there was no longer any Great <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a> for trade treaties.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">Slavery</a> was practiced widely at that time amongst many peoples of the world, including some Native Americans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For the Portuguese&#8212;from whom Columbus received most of his maritime training&#8212;the profits from enslaving people had resulted in the first "financial return" on a 75-year <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Columbus enslaved five hundred and sixty people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The slaves were shipped to Spain; 200 died during the route back to Spain, and half of the remainder were ill when they arrived. After legal proceedings in the <i>Cortes</i>, some survivors were ordered released and to be returned to their <i>las Americas</i> homeland, whereas others were used by Queen Isabella as galley slaves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus, desperate to repay his investors, failed to realize that Isabella and Ferdinand did not plan to follow or allow Portuguese slavery policy<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2013)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> in this respect. Rounding up the slaves led to the first major battle between the Spanish and the native peoples in their homeland, called by Europeans "the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Columbus was eager to pay back dividends to those who had invested in his promise to fill his ships with gold. And since so many of the slaves died in captivity, he developed a plan while in the Province of Cicao on Hispaniola. Columbus imposed a tribute system, similar to that of the still-unknown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztec#Tribute_and_trade" title="Aztec">Aztec Empire tribute</a> on the mainland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> All Cicaoan indigenous residents above 14 years of age were required to find and deliver a specific quota of gold every three months. Upon their doing so, they would receive copper tokens that they wore around their necks. Any Indian found without a copper token had their hands cut off and subsequently bled to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zinn_2009_3_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zinn_2009_3-36">[36]</a></sup> Since there were no gold mines on the island, the Indians had no chance of meeting Columbus\' quota and thousands are reported to have committed suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite or because of such extreme enforcement, Columbus did not obtain much gold, and many new foreign "settlers" were unhappy with the climate and disillusioned about their chances of getting rich quickly. A classic <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rush</a> had been set off that would have tragic consequences for the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>\'s indigenous people and cultures. Anthropologists have shown there was more intermarriage and assimilation than previously believed (see the <a href="/wiki/Black_Legend" title="Black Legend">Black Legend</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="9 May 2010 (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus allowed settlers to return to their homeland with any Indian women with whom they had started families, or to Queen Isabella\'s fury, had kidnapped and owned as slaves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_voyage">Third voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Third voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_third_voyage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="638" data-file-width="901" decoding="async" height="283" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Columbus_third_voyage.jpg/800px-Columbus_third_voyage.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_third_voyage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Third voyage</div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AndalusAndMorocco.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="180" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/AndalusAndMorocco.png" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AndalusAndMorocco.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of <a href="/wiki/Sanl%C3%BAcar_de_Barrameda" title="Sanl&#250;car de Barrameda">Sanl&#250;car de Barrameda</a>, the starting point for Columbus\'s third journey.</div></div></div>\n<p>According to the abstract of Columbus\'s journal made by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_de_Las_Casas" title="Bartolom&#233; de Las Casas">Bartolom&#233; de Las Casas</a>, the objective of the third voyage was to verify the existence of a continent that King John II of Portugal suggested was located to the southwest of the Cape Verde Islands. King John reportedly knew of the existence of such a mainland because "canoes had been found which set out from the coast of Guinea [West Africa] and sailed to the west with merchandise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 30, 1498, Columbus left the port of <a href="/wiki/Sanl%C3%BAcar_de_Barrameda" title="Sanl&#250;car de Barrameda">Sanl&#250;car</a>, Spain with a fleet of six ships,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ships_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ships-51">[51]</a></sup> sending three directly to the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> while leading the other three: the <i>Santa Mar&#237;a de Gu&#237;a</i>, the <i>Vaque&#241;os</i>, and the <i>Correo</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> &#8211; to the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Porto_Santo_Island" title="Porto Santo Island">Porto Santo Island</a>, his wife\'s native homeland. He then sailed to the island of <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and spent some time there with the Portuguese captain Jo&#227;o Gon&#231;alves da C&#226;mara before sailing to the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. The ships found King John\'s hypothesized continent, which is <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, when they sighted the land of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> on 31 July approaching from the southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The fleet sailed along the southern coast and entered <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Channel" title="Columbus Channel">Dragon\'s Mouth</a>, anchoring near <a href="/wiki/Soldado_Rock" title="Soldado Rock">Soldado Rock</a> where they made contact with a group of native <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Amerindians</a> in canoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Columbus then landed on Trinidad at <a href="/wiki/Icacos_Point" title="Icacos Point">Icacos Point</a> (which he named <i>Punta de Arenal</i>) on 2 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From August 4 through August 12, 1498, he explored the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Paria" title="Gulf of Paria">Gulf of Paria</a> which separates Trinidad from mainland <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>. He then explored the mainland of South America, including the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> River. He also sailed to the islands of <a href="/wiki/Chacachacare" title="Chacachacare">Chacachacare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Margarita_Island" title="Margarita Island">Margarita Island</a> and sighted and named islands Bella Forma (<a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>) and Concepcion (<a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>). He described the new lands as belonging to a previously unknown new continent, but he pictured it hanging from China, bulging out to make the earth pear-shaped.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (August 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Dubious" title="Talk:Voyages of Christopher Columbus">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governorship_issues">Governorship issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Governorship issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_Bobadilla">Governor Bobadilla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Governor Bobadilla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>During Columbus\'s term as Viceroy and <a href="/wiki/List_of_viceroys_of_New_Spain#The_Indies" title="List of viceroys of New Spain">Governor of the Indies</a>, he had been accused of governing tyrannically, called "the tyrant of the Caribbean".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Columbus was physically and mentally exhausted; his body was wracked by <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> and his eyes by <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmia" title="Ophthalmia">ophthalmia</a>. In October 1499, he sent two ships to Spain, asking the <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">Cortes Generales</a> of Castile to appoint a royal commissioner to help him govern.\n</p><p>The Cortes appointed <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Bobadilla" title="Francisco de Bobadilla">Francisco de Bobadilla</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Calatrava" title="Order of Calatrava">Order of Calatrava</a>; however, his authority stretched far beyond what Columbus had requested. Bobadilla was given complete control as governor from 1500 until he was killed in a hurricane in 1502. Arriving in Santo Domingo while Columbus was away, Bobadilla immediately received many serious complaints about all three Columbus brothers: Christopher, Bartolom&#233;, and Diego. The testimonies of 23 people who had seen or heard about the treatment meted out by Columbus and his brothers&#8212;had originally been lost for centuries, but was rediscovered in 2005 in the Spanish archives in <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a>. It contained an account of Columbus\'s seven-year reign as the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_viceroys_of_New_Spain#The_Indies" title="List of viceroys of New Spain">Governor of the Indies</a> Consuelo Varela, a Spanish historian, states: "Even those who loved him [Columbus] had to admit the atrocities that had taken place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newspaper1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-newspaper1-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a result of these testimonies and without being allowed a word in his own defense, Columbus upon his return, had manacles placed on his wrists and chains placed on his ankles and was cast into prison to await return to Spain. He was 49 years old.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrest_of_Governor_Columbus">Arrest of Governor Columbus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Arrest of Governor Columbus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Columbus was arrested in 1500 and supplanted from his posts. A number of returned settlers and friars lobbied against Columbus at the Spanish court, accusing him of mismanagement. <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Bobadilla" title="Francisco de Bobadilla">Francisco de Bobadilla</a> arrived on August 23, 1500 and detained Columbus and his brothers and had them shipped home. On October 1, 1500, Columbus and his two brothers, likewise in chains, were sent back to Spanish Aragon. Once in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a>, a grieving Columbus wrote to a friend at court:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>It is now seventeen years since I came to serve these princes with the Enterprise of the Indies. They made me pass eight of them in discussion, and at the end rejected it as a thing of jest. Nevertheless I persisted therein... Over there I have placed under their sovereignty more land than there is in Africa and Europe, and more than 1,700 islands... In seven years I, by the divine will, made that conquest. At a time when I was entitled to expect rewards and retirement, I was incontinently arrested and sent home loaded with chains... The accusation was brought out of malice on the basis of charges made by civilians who had revolted and wished to take possession on the land...\n</p><p>I beg your graces, with the zeal of faithful Christians in whom their Highnesses have confidence, to read all my papers, and to consider how I, who came from so far to serve these princes... now at the end of my days have been despoiled of my honor and my property without cause, wherein is neither justice nor mercy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<div class="thumb before the busy King Ferdinand ordered them released. Not long thereafter, the king and queen summoned the Columbus brothers to their presence at the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a> palace in <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>. There the royal couple heard the brothers\' pleas; restored their freedom and their wealth; and, after much persuasion, agreed to fund Columbus\'s fourth voyage. But the door was firmly shut on Christopher Columbus\'s role as governor. From that point forward, <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando_y_C%C3%A1ceres" title="Nicol&#225;s de Ovando y C&#225;ceres">Nicol&#225;s de Ovando y C&#225;ceres</a> was to be the new <a href="/wiki/List_of_viceroys_of_New_Spain#The_Indies" title="List of viceroys of New Spain">Governor of the Indies</a>.\n</p><p>Although he regained his freedom, he did not regain his prestige and lost all his titles including the governorship. As an added insult, the Portuguese had won the race to the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>: <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> returned in September 1499 from a trip to India, having sailed east around Africa.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourth_voyage">Fourth voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Fourth voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="630" data-file-width="1001" decoding="async" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg/400px-Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg/600px-Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg/800px-Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Columbus_fourth_voyage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbus\'s fourth voyage</div></div></div>\n<p>On March 14, 1502 Columbus made a fourth voyage with strict orders from the king and queen which instructed him not to stop at Hispaniola, but only to search for a westward passage to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> mainland. Accompanied by his stepbrother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bartolomeo_Columbus" title="Bartolomeo Columbus">Bartolomeo</a>, Diego Mendez, and his son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernando_Col%C3%B3n" title="Fernando Col&#243;n">Fernando</a>, he left <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a>, Spain on May 12, 1502, with his flagship, <i>Capitana</i>, as well as the <i>Gallega</i>, <i>Vizca&#237;na,</i> and <i>Santiago de Palos</i>. He sailed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arzila" title="Arzila">Arzila</a> on the Moroccan coast to rescue the Portuguese soldiers who he heard were under siege by the Moors. On June 15, 1502, they landed at Carbet on the island of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> (Martinica). A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> was brewing, so he continued on, hoping to find shelter on Hispaniola. He arrived at <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a> on June 29, 1502, but was denied port, and the new governor refused to listen to his storm prediction. Instead, while Columbus\'s ships sheltered at the mouth of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jaina_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jaina River (page does not exist)">Jaina River</a>, the first Spanish treasure fleet sailed into the hurricane. The only ship to reach Spain had Columbus\'s money and belongings on it, and all of his former enemies (and a few friends) had drowned, including Francisco de Bobadilla, who had sent Columbus back to Spain in chains two years earlier.\n</p><p>After a brief stop at Jamaica, he sailed to <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, arriving at Guanaja (Isla de Pinos) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islas_de_la_Bah%C3%ADa_department" title="Islas de la Bah&#237;a department">Bay Islands</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> on July 30, 1502. Here Bartolomeo found native merchants and a large canoe, which was described as "long as a galley" and was filled with cargo. On August 14, 1502, he landed on the mainland of the Americas at Puerto Castilla, near <a href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Honduras" title="Trujillo, Honduras">Trujillo, Honduras</a>. He spent two months exploring the coasts of Honduras, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, before arriving in Almirante Bay, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> on October 16, 1502.\n</p><p>In Panam&#225;, he learned from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ngobe" title="Ngobe">Ngobe</a> of gold and a strait to another ocean. After some exploration, he established a garrison at the mouth of <a href="/wiki/Bel%C3%A9n_River" title="Bel&#233;n River">Bel&#233;n River</a> in January 1503.\n</p><p>By April 6, the garrison he had established captured the local leader <a href="/wiki/El_Quib%C3%ADan" title="El Quib&#237;an">El Quib&#237;an</a>, who had demanded they not go down<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (January 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Dubious" title="Talk:Voyages of Christopher Columbus">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> the Bel&#233;n River. El Quib&#237;an escaped, and returned with an army to attack and repel the Spanish, damaging some of the ships so that one vessel had to be abandoned. Columbus left for Hispaniola on April 16, but sustained more damage in a storm off the coast of Cuba. Unable to travel any farther, the ships were beached in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Ann_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Saint Ann Parish, Jamaica">St. Ann\'s Bay, Jamaica</a>, on June 25, 1503.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1302" data-file-width="1602" decoding="async" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/220px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/330px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg/440px-Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_Christophe_Colomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Columbus fills the natives with fear and awe by predicting the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a></div></div></div>\n<p>For a year Columbus and his men remained stranded on Jamaica. A Spaniard, Diego Mendez, and some natives paddled a canoe to get help from <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. The island\'s governor, <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando_y_C%C3%A1ceres" title="Nicol&#225;s de Ovando y C&#225;ceres">Nicol&#225;s de Ovando y C&#225;ceres</a>, detested Columbus and obstructed all efforts to rescue him and his men. In the meantime, Columbus mesmerized the natives by correctly predicting a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">lunar eclipse</a> for February 29, 1504, using the <i><a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">Ephemeris</a></i> of the German astronomer <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus" title="Regiomontanus">Regiomontanus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Help finally arrived, from the governor, on June 29, 1504, and Columbus and his men arrived in <a href="/wiki/Sanl%C3%BAcar_de_Barrameda" title="Sanl&#250;car de Barrameda">Sanl&#250;car de Barrameda</a>, Castile, on November 7, 1504. Columbus died in Valladolid, Spain, on May 20, 1506, at around the age of 54, probably of the effects of chronic <a href="/wiki/Reactive_arthritis" title="Reactive arthritis">reactive arthritis</a> perhaps acquired secondarily to food poisoning.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After his death, Columbus\'s sons, Diego and Fernando, took legal action to enforce their father\'s contract. Many of the allegations against Columbus and his tyrannical governorship were initiated by the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown</a> during these lengthy court cases, known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleitos_Colombinos" title="Pleitos Colombinos">Pleitos Colombinos</a></i>. The family had some success in their first litigation, as a judgment of 1511 confirmed Diego\'s position as Viceroy, but reduced his powers. Diego resumed litigation in 1512 lasting until 1536, and further disputes continued until 1790.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The success of Columbus\'s first voyage touched off a series of westward explorations by European seafaring states. These states sought to exploit the New World\'s riches; build trade networks and colonies; and built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_reductions" title="Indian reductions">Indian reductions</a> (settlements) to relocate, use the labor of, and attempt Christian conversions of the native people.\n</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a> starting in the 15th century, Europeans explored the world by ocean, searching for particular trade goods, humans to enslave, and trading locations and ports. The most desired trading goods were gold, silver and spices. Columbus did not reach Asia but rather found what was to the Europeans a New World, the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. For the Catholic monarchies of Spain and Portugal, a division of influence became necessary to avoid conflict. This was resolved by Papal intervention in 1494 when the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tordesillas" title="Treaty of Tordesillas">Treaty of Tordesillas</a> purported to divide the world between the two powers. The Portuguese were to receive everything outside of Europe east of a line that ran 270 leagues west of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a>, thought to include the continents of Africa and Asia, but none of the New World.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Spanish received everything west of this line, territory that was still almost completely unknown, and proved to be primarily the vast majority of the continents of the Americas and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Islands of the Pacific Ocean</a>. This arrangement was somewhat subverted in 1500,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when the Portuguese navigator <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro &#193;lvares Cabral">Pedro &#193;lvares Cabral</a> arrived at a point on the eastern coast of South America, and realized that it was on the Portuguese side of the dividing line between the two empires. This would lead to the Portuguese colonization of what is now Brazil.\n</p><p>Columbus and other Iberian explorers were initially disappointed with their discoveries&#8212;unlike Africa or Asia, the Caribbean islanders had little to trade with the Castillo ships. The islands thus became the focus of colonization efforts. It was not until the continent itself was explored that Spain found the wealth it had sought in the form of abundant gold. In the Americas the Spanish found a number of empires that were as large and populous as those in Europe. However, small bodies of Spanish conquistadors, with large armies of indigenous groups, managed to conquer these states. The most notable amongst the conquered states were the <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Empire" title="Aztec Empire">Aztec Empire</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Aztec_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Aztec Empire">conquered in 1521</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_the_Inca_Empire" title="Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire">conquered in 1532</a>). During this time, pandemics of European disease such as smallpox devastated the indigenous populations. Once Spanish sovereignty was established, the Spanish focused on the extraction and export of gold and silver.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
