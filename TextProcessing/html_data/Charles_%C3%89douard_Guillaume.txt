colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Charles &#201;douard Guillaume</b> (15 February 1861, <a href="/wiki/Fleurier" title="Fleurier">Fleurier</a>, Switzerland &#8211; 13 May 1938, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A8vres" title="S&#232;vres">S&#232;vres</a>, France) was a Swiss physicist who received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> in 1920 in recognition of the service he had rendered to precision measurements in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> by his discovery of anomalies in nickel steel alloys. In 1919, he gave the fifth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faraday_Medal_and_Prize" title="Faraday Medal and Prize">Guthrie Lecture</a> at the Institute of Physics in London with the title "The Anomaly of the Nickel-Steels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p><br/>\nGuillaume is known for his discovery of nickel-steel alloys he named <a href="/wiki/Invar" title="Invar">invar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elinvar" title="Elinvar">elinvar</a>.  Invar has a near-zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a>, making it useful in constructing precision instruments whose dimensions need to remain constant in spite of varying temperature.  Elinvar has a near-zero thermal coefficient of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modulus_of_elasticity" title="Modulus of elasticity">modulus of elasticity</a>, making it useful in constructing instruments with springs that need to be unaffected by varying temperature, such as the <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">marine chronometer</a>.  Elinvar is also non-magnetic, which is a secondary useful property for <a href="/wiki/Antimagnetic_watch" title="Antimagnetic watch">antimagnetic watches</a>. \n</p><p>Guillaume is also known for the  earliest  estimation  of  the "radiation of the stars&#8221; in his 1896 paper "La Temp&#233;rature de L\'Espace (The Temperature of Space)". This is the first known reference to the concept that would later be known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Microwave_Background" title="Cosmic Microwave Background">Cosmic Microwave Background</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the son of a Swiss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horologist" title="Horologist">horologist</a> Guillaume took an interest in marine chronometers.  For use as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compensation_balance" title="Compensation balance">compensation balance</a> he developed a slight variation of the invar alloy which had a negative quadratic coefficient of expansion.  The purpose of doing this was to eliminate the "middle temperature" error of the balance wheel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gould_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gould-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Guillaume was head of the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gould_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gould-3">[3]</a></sup>  He worked with <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a>, serving at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_de_Paris" title="Observatoire de Paris">Observatoire de Paris</a>&#8212;Section de Meudon. He conducted several <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> with <a href="/wiki/Thermostatic" title="Thermostatic">thermostatic</a> measurements at the <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a>.  He was the first to determine accurately the temperature of space.\n</p><p>Guillaume was married in 1888 to A.M. Taufflieb, with whom he had three children.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div
