Technologies Commercial Space Station</b> is a proposed <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">orbital</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">station</a> intended for commercial clients.  The station was initially proposed in 2010 by Orbital Technologies, a <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> aerospace firm (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a>), who is collaborating to develop the station with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">Rocket and Space Corporation Energia</a> (RSC Energia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-0"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As initially proposed in 2010, the station was to consist of a single module of approximately 3 metres (9.8&#160;ft) diameter with a usable volume of about 20&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (710&#160;cu&#160;ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20101112-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of  November 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the company was "looking to launch in the next five years or so," ... "in 2015 or 2016."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20101112-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (January 2013)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>At that time, several customers were "under contract from the commercial space industry and the scientific community interested in areas such as medical research, protein crystallization, and materials processing, as well as from the geographic imaging and remote-sensing industry. Media projects have also been proposed [as have] <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">tourism</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20101112-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of August 2011, the station was planned to be launched in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (January 2013)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>As of April 2013, Orbital Technologies appointed Stiphan Beher as Chief Operating Officer, taking the firm in a global direction, joining CEO Sergey Kostenko, a former Cosmonaut; and Sergey Chernikov, former Deputy Head of Manned Space Missions for the Russian Space Agency.  Orbital Technologies was co-founded by Eric Anderson, Chairman of Space Adventures.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_sponsors">Financial sponsors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Financial sponsors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the station was receiving support from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">Russian Federal Space Agency</a>, who was encouraging private participation.  It is hoped the station "will attract private investment for the Russian space industry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20101112-1">[1]</a></sup>\nNo <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private financing</a> has yet been announced.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Servicing">Servicing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_Technologies_Commercial_Space_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Servicing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The initial 2010 plan indicated that the station would be "serviced by Russian <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">Progress</a> spacecraft and potentially other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_spacecraft#See_also" title="Dragon spacecraft">commercially available vehicles</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdc20101112_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-sdc20101112-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
