href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span><span title="/i&#720;/: \'ee\' in \'fleece\'">i&#720;</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="\'d\' in \'dye\'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Aeneis</i> <small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Latin" title="Help:IPA/Latin">[ae&#815;&#712;ne&#720;&#618;s]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic poem</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a> between 29 and 19 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> that tells the <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legendary</a> story of <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Trojan</a> who travelled to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, where he became the ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>. It comprises 9,896 lines in <a href="/wiki/Dactylic_hexameter" title="Dactylic hexameter">dactylic hexameter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The first six of the poem\'s twelve books tell the story of Aeneas\'s wanderings from Troy to Italy, and the poem\'s second half tells of the Trojans\' ultimately victorious war upon the <a href="/wiki/Latins_(Italic_tribe)" title="Latins (Italic tribe)">Latins</a>, under whose name Aeneas and his Trojan followers are destined to be subsumed.\n</p><p>The hero Aeneas was already known to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">Greco-Roman</a> legend and myth, having been a character in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>. Virgil took the disconnected tales of Aeneas\'s wanderings, his vague association with the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a> and his description as a personage of no fixed characteristics other than a scrupulous <i><a href="/wiki/Pietas" title="Pietas">pietas</a></i>, and fashioned the <i>Aeneid</i> into a compelling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_myth" title="Founding myth">founding myth</a> or <a href="/wiki/National_epic" title="National epic">national epic</a> that tied Rome to the legends of Troy, explained the <a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">Punic Wars</a>, glorified traditional Roman virtues, and legitimized the <a href="/wiki/Julio-Claudian_dynasty" title="Julio-Claudian dynasty">Julio-Claudian dynasty</a> as descendants of the founders, heroes, and gods of Rome and Troy.\n</p><p>The <i>Aeneid</i> is widely regarded as Virgil\'s masterpiece<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and one of the greatest works of Latin literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a class="toclevel-1 tocsection-28"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">12</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-29"><a <span class="toctext">Text</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-31"><a href="#Sequels"><span class="tocnumber">12.3</span> <span class="toctext">Sequels</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-32"><a href="#Illustrations"><span class="tocnumber">12.4</span> <span class="toctext">Illustrations</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-33"><a href="#Commentary"><span class="tocnumber">12.5</span> <span class="toctext">Commentary</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Story">Story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Characters_in_the_Aeneid" title="Category:Characters in the Aeneid">Characters in the Aeneid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parallels_between_Virgil%27s_Aeneid_and_Homer%27s_Iliad_and_Odyssey" title="Parallels between Virgil\'s Aeneid and Homer\'s Iliad and Odyssey">Parallels between the Aeneid and Homer\'s Iliad and Odyssey</a></div>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i> can be divided into two halves based on the disparate subject matter of Books 1&#8211;6 (Aeneas\'s journey to Latium in Italy) and Books 7&#8211;12 (the war in Latium). These two halves are commonly regarded as reflecting Virgil\'s ambition to rival <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> by treating both the <i>Odyssey\'s</i> wandering theme and the <i>Iliad\'</i>s warfare themes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This is, however, a rough correspondence, the limitations of which should be borne in mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Journey_to_Italy_.28books_1.E2.80.936.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Journey_to_Italy_(books_1&#8211;6)">Journey to Italy (books 1&#8211;6)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Journey to Italy (books 1&#8211;6)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Theme">Theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Virgil begins his poem with a statement of his theme (<i>Arma virumque cano&#160;...</i>, "Of arms and the man I sing&#160;...") and an invocation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muse" title="Muse">Muse</a>, falling some seven lines after the poem\'s inception (<i>Musa, mihi causas memora&#160;...</i>, "O Muse, recount to me the causes&#160;..."). He then explains the reason for the principal conflict in the story: the resentment held by the goddess <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Trojan</a> people. This is consistent with her role throughout the <a href="/wiki/Homer#Epics" title="Homer">Homeric epics</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_and_Refuge">Storm and Refuge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Storm and Refuge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Also in the manner of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, the story proper begins <i><a href="/wiki/In_medias_res" title="In medias res">in medias res</a></i> (into the middle of things), with the Trojan fleet in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>, heading in the direction of Italy. The fleet, led by <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>, is on a voyage to find a second home. It has been foretold that in Italy he will give rise to a race both noble and courageous, a race which will become known to all nations. Juno is wrathful, because she had not been chosen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judgment_of_Paris" title="Judgment of Paris">judgment of Paris</a>, and because her favorite city, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a>, will be destroyed by Aeneas\'s descendants. Also, <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(mythology)" title="Ganymede (mythology)">Ganymede</a>, a Trojan prince, was chosen to be the cupbearer to her husband, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>&#8212;replacing Juno\'s daughter, <a href="/wiki/Hebe_(mythology)" title="Hebe (mythology)">Hebe</a>. Juno proceeds to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeolus_(son_of_Hippotes)" title="Aeolus (son of Hippotes)">Aeolus</a>, King of the Winds, and asks that he release the winds to stir up a storm in exchange for a bribe (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nereids" title="Nereids">Deiopea</a>, the loveliest of all her sea nymphs, as a wife). Aeolus does not accept the bribe, but agrees to carry out Juno\'s orders (line 77, "My task is / To fulfill your commands"); the storm then devastates the fleet.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1875,_C%C3%A9zanne,_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1295" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg/220px-1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg/330px-1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg/440px-1875%2C_C%C3%A9zanne%2C_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1875,_C%C3%A9zanne,_Aeneas_Meeting_Dido_at_Carthage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Paul_C%C3%A9zanne" title="Paul C&#233;zanne">Paul C&#233;zanne</a>, <i>Aeneas Meeting Dido at Carthage</i>, ca. 1875, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University_Art_Museum" title="Princeton University Art Museum">Princeton University Art Museum</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Neptune_(mythology)" title="Neptune (mythology)">Neptune</a> takes notice: although he himself is no friend of the Trojans, he is infuriated by Juno\'s intrusion into his domain, and stills the winds and calms the waters, after making sure that the winds would not bother the Trojans again, lest they be punished more harshly than they were this time. The fleet takes shelter on the coast of Africa, where Aeneas rouses the spirits of his men, reassuring them that they have been through worse situations before. There, Aeneas\'s mother, Venus, in the form of a huntress very similar to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">Diana</a>, encourages him and recounts to him the history of Carthage. Eventually, Aeneas ventures into the city, and in the temple of Juno he seeks and gains the favor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dido_(Queen_of_Carthage)" title="Dido (Queen of Carthage)">Dido</a>, queen of the city. The city has only recently been founded by refugees from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyre_(Lebanon)" title="Tyre (Lebanon)">Tyre</a> and will later become a great imperial rival and enemy to Rome.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> has her own plans. She goes to her son, Aeneas\'s half-brother <a href="/wiki/Cupid" title="Cupid">Cupid</a>, and tells him to imitate <a href="/wiki/Ascanius" title="Ascanius">Ascanius</a> (the son of Aeneas and his first wife Creusa). Disguised as such, Cupid goes to Dido and offers the gifts expected from a guest. With Dido\'s motherly love revived as she cradles the boy during a banquet given in honour of the <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Trojans</a>, Cupid secretly weakens her sworn fidelity to the soul of her late husband, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sychaeus" title="Sychaeus">Sychaeus</a>, who had been murdered by her brother, <a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_of_Tyre" title="Pygmalion of Tyre">Pygmalion</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trojan_Horse">Trojan Horse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trojan Horse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Aeneas sadly recounts the events that occasioned the Trojans\' arrival. He begins the tale shortly after the war described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>. Cunning <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Ulysses</a> devised a way for <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> warriors to gain entry into the walled city of Troy by hiding in a large <a href="/wiki/Trojan_Horse" title="Trojan Horse">wooden horse</a>. The Greeks pretended to sail away, leaving a warrior, <a href="/wiki/Sinon" title="Sinon">Sinon</a>, to inform the Trojans that the horse was an offering and that if it were taken into the city, the Trojans would be able to conquer Greece. The Trojan priest <a href="/wiki/Laoco%C3%B6n" title="Laoco&#246;n">Laoco&#246;n</a> saw through the Greek plot and urged the horse\'s destruction, but his protests fell on deaf ears, so he hurled his spear at the horse. Then, in what would be seen by the Trojans as punishment from the gods, two serpents emerged from the sea and devoured Laoco&#246;n, along with his two sons. The Trojans then took the horse inside the fortified walls, and after nightfall the armed Greeks emerged from it, opening the city\'s gates to allow the returned Greek army to slaughter the Trojans.\n</p><p>In a dream, <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>, the fallen Trojan prince, advised Aeneas to flee with his family. Aeneas awoke and saw with horror what was happening to his beloved city. At first he tried to fight the enemy, but soon he lost his comrades and was left alone to fend off the Greeks. He witnessed the murder of <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">Priam</a> by Achilles\' son <a href="/wiki/Neoptolemus" title="Neoptolemus">Pyrrhus</a>. His mother, Venus, appeared to him and led him back to his house. Aeneas tells of his escape with his son, <a href="/wiki/Ascanius" title="Ascanius">Ascanius</a>, and father, <a href="/wiki/Anchises" title="Anchises">Anchises</a>, after the occurrence of various omens (Ascanius\' head catching fire without his being harmed, a clap of thunder and a shooting star). After fleeing Troy, he goes back for his wife, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creusa_(wife_of_Aeneas)" title="Creusa (wife of Aeneas)">Creusa</a>, but she has been killed. Her ghost tells him that his destiny is to found a new city in the West.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wanderings">Wanderings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wanderings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>He tells of how, rallying the other survivors, he built a fleet of ships and made landfall at various locations in the Mediterranean: <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, where they find the last remains of a fellow Trojan, <a href="/wiki/Polydorus_(son_of_Priam)" title="Polydorus (son of Priam)">Polydorus</a>; <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, which they believe to be the land where they are to build their city, which they name <a href="/wiki/Pergamea" title="Pergamea">Pergamea</a> (but they are set straight by <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>); the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strophades" title="Strophades">Strophades</a>, where they encounter the Harpy <a href="/wiki/Celaeno" title="Celaeno">Celaeno</a>, who tells them to leave her island and to look for Italy; and <a href="/wiki/Buthrotum" title="Buthrotum">Buthrotum</a>. This last city had been built in an attempt to replicate Troy. In Buthrotum, Aeneas meets <a href="/wiki/Andromache" title="Andromache">Andromache</a>, the widow of <a href="/wiki/Hector" title="Hector">Hector</a>. She is still lamenting the loss of her valiant husband and beloved child. There, too, Aeneas sees and meets Helenus, one of <a href="/wiki/Priam" title="Priam">Priam</a>\'s sons, who has the gift of prophecy. Through him, Aeneas learns the destiny laid out for him: he is divinely advised to seek out the land of Italy (also known as <i>Ausonia</i> or <i>Hesperia</i>), where his descendants will not only prosper, but in time rule the entire known world. In addition, Helenus also bids him go to the <a href="/wiki/Sibyl" title="Sibyl">Sibyl</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cumae" title="Cumae">Cumae</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="2667" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg/220px-Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg/330px-Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg/440px-Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Death_Dido_Cayot_Louvre_MR1780.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The suicide of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dido_(Queen_of_Carthage)" title="Dido (Queen of Carthage)">Queen Dido</a> (book 4), sculpture by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Claude-Augustin_Cayot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Claude-Augustin Cayot (page does not exist)">Claude-Augustin Cayot</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;[<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude-Augustin_Cayot" title="fr:Claude-Augustin Cayot">fr</a>]</span> (1667&#8211;1722)</div></div></div>\n<p>Heading into the open sea, Aeneas leaves Buthrotum, rounds the south eastern tip of Italy and makes his way towards <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> (Trinacria). There, they are caught in the whirlpool of <a href="/wiki/Charybdis" title="Charybdis">Charybdis</a> and driven out to sea. Soon they come ashore at the land of the <a href="/wiki/Cyclops" title="Cyclops">Cyclopes</a>. There they meet a Greek, <a href="/wiki/Achaemenides" title="Achaemenides">Achaemenides</a>, one of Ulysses\' men, who has been left behind when his comrades escaped the cave of <a href="/wiki/Polyphemus" title="Polyphemus">Polyphemus</a>. They take Achaemenides on board and narrowly escape Polyphemus. Shortly after, Anchises dies peacefully of old age, and Aeneas sails to Carthage.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate_of_Queen_Dido">Fate of Queen Dido</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fate of Queen Dido">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Aeneas finishes his story, and Dido realizes that she has fallen in love with Aeneas. Juno seizes upon this opportunity to make a deal with Venus, Aeneas\'s mother, with the intention of distracting Aeneas from his destiny of founding a city in Italy. Aeneas is inclined to return Dido\'s love, and during a hunting expedition, a storm drives them into a small covered grove in which Aeneas and Dido presumably made love, an event that Dido takes to indicate a marriage between them. But when <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> sends <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> to remind Aeneas of his duty, he has no choice but to part. Her heart broken, Dido commits suicide by stabbing herself upon a <a href="/wiki/Pyre" title="Pyre">pyre</a> with Aeneas\'s sword. Before dying, she predicts eternal strife between Aeneas\'s people and hers; "rise up from my bones, avenging spirit" (4.625, trans. Fitzgerald) is a possible invocation to <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Looking back from the deck of his ship, Aeneas sees the smoke of Dido\'s funeral pyre and knows its meaning only too clearly. Nevertheless, destiny calls, and the Trojan fleet sails on to Italy.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sicily">Sicily</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sicily">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2007" data-file-width="1872" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg/220px-Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg/330px-Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg/440px-Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mosaic_boxers_Getty_Villa_71.AH.106.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boxing scene from the <i>Aeneid</i> (book 5), mosaic floor from a Gallo-Roman villa in <a href="/wiki/Villelaure" title="Villelaure">Villelaure</a> (France), ca. 175 AD, <a href="/wiki/Getty_Villa" title="Getty Villa">Getty Villa</a> (71.AH.106)</div></div></div>\n<p>Book 5 takes place on <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and centers on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funeral_games_(antiquity)" title="Funeral games (antiquity)">funeral games</a> that Aeneas organizes for the anniversary of his father\'s death. Aeneas and his men have left Carthage for Sicily, where Aeneas organizes celebratory games&#8212;a boat race, a foot race, a boxing match, and an archery contest. In all those contests, Aeneas is careful to reward winners and losers, showing his leadership qualities by not allowing antagonism even after foul play. Each of these contests comments on past events or prefigures future events: the boxing match, for instance, is "a preview of the final encounter of Aeneas and Turnus", and the dove, the target during the archery contest, is connected to the deaths of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polites_(Prince_of_Troy)" title="Polites (Prince of Troy)">Polites</a> and King Priam in Book 2 and that of Camilla in Book 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Afterwards, Ascanius leads the boys in a military parade and mock battle, a tradition he will teach the Latins while building the walls of Alba Longa.\n</p><p>During these events (in which only men participate), Juno incites the womenfolk to burn the fleet and prevent the Trojans from ever reaching Italy, but her plan is thwarted when Ascanius and Aeneas intervene. Aeneas prays to Jupiter to quench the fires, which the god does with a torrential rainstorm. An anxious Aeneas is comforted by a vision of his father, who tells him to go to the underworld to receive a vision of his and Rome\'s future. In return for safe passage to Italy, the gods, by order of Jupiter, will receive one of Aeneas\'s men as a sacrifice: <a href="/wiki/Palinurus" title="Palinurus">Palinurus</a>, who steers Aeneas\'s ship by night, falls overboard.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Underworld">Underworld</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Underworld">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough_(mythology)" title="The Golden Bough (mythology)">The Golden Bough (mythology)</a></div>\n<p>In Book 6, Aeneas, with the guidance of the <a href="/wiki/Cumaean_Sibyl" title="Cumaean Sibyl">Cumaean Sibyl</a>, descends into the underworld. They pass by crowds of the dead by the banks of the river <a href="/wiki/Acheron" title="Acheron">Acheron</a> and are ferried across by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charon_(mythology)" title="Charon (mythology)">Charon</a> before passing by <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">Cerberus</a>, the three-headed guardian of the underworld. Then Aeneas is shown the fates of the wicked in <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a> and is warned by the Sibyl to bow to the justice of the gods. He is then brought to green fields of <a href="/wiki/Elysium" title="Elysium">Elysium</a>. There he speaks with the spirit of his father and is offered a prophetic vision of the destiny of Rome.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="War_in_Italy_.28books_7.E2.80.9312.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="War_in_Italy_(books_7&#8211;12)">War in Italy (books 7&#8211;12)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: War in Italy (books 7&#8211;12)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2672" data-file-width="4026" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg/220px-Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg/330px-Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg/440px-Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aeneas_Latium_BM_GR1927.12-12.1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman bas-relief, 2nd century: Aeneas lands in <a href="/wiki/Latium" title="Latium">Latium</a>, leading <a href="/wiki/Ascanius" title="Ascanius">Ascanius</a>; the sow identifies the place to found his city (book 8).</div></div></div>\n<p>Upon returning to the land of the living, Aeneas leads the Trojans to settle in <a href="/wiki/Latium" title="Latium">Latium</a>, where he courts <a href="/wiki/Lavinia" title="Lavinia">Lavinia</a>, the daughter of King <a href="/wiki/Latinus" title="Latinus">Latinus</a>. Although Aeneas wished to avoid a war, hostilities break out. Juno is heavily involved in bringing about this war&#8212;she has persuaded the Queen of Latium to demand that Lavinia be married to <a href="/wiki/Turnus" title="Turnus">Turnus</a>, the ruler of another local people, the <a href="/wiki/Rutuli" title="Rutuli">Rutuli</a>. Juno continues to stir up trouble, even summoning the <a href="/wiki/Erinyes" title="Erinyes">fury</a> <a href="/wiki/Alecto" title="Alecto">Alecto</a> to ensure that a war takes place.\n</p><p>Seeing the masses of warriors that Turnus has brought against him, Aeneas seeks help from the Tuscans, enemies of the Rutuli. He meets King Evander of <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a>, whose son <a href="/wiki/Pallas_(son_of_Evander)" title="Pallas (son of Evander)">Pallas</a> agrees to lead troops against the Rutuli. Meanwhile, in book 9, the Trojan camp is attacked, and a midnight raid leads to the deaths of <a href="/wiki/Nisus_and_Euryalus" title="Nisus and Euryalus">Nisus and his companion, Euryalus</a>. The gates, however, are defended until Aeneas returns with his Tuscan and Arcadian reinforcements.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg/220px-Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg/330px-Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg/440px-Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aeneas_and_Turnus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aeneas\'s defeat of Turnus (book 12), painting by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Giordano" title="Luca Giordano">Luca Giordano</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the battling that follows, many are slain&#8212;notably Pallas (a close friend of Aeneas), who is killed by Turnus, and <a href="/wiki/Mezentius" title="Mezentius">Mezentius</a>, Turnus\'s close associate. Mezentius, who has allowed his son to be killed while he himself fled, reproaches himself and faces Aeneas in <a href="/wiki/Single_combat" title="Single combat">single combat</a>&#8212;an honourable but essentially futile endeavour. In book 11, another notable, <a href="/wiki/Camilla_(mythology)" title="Camilla (mythology)">Camilla</a>, a sort of <a href="/wiki/Amazons" title="Amazons">Amazon</a> character, fights bravely but is killed. She has been a virgin devoted to <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">Diana</a> and to her nation; Arruns, the man who kills her, is struck dead by Diana\'s sentinel, <a href="/wiki/Nereid" title="Nereid">Opis</a>.\n</p><p>Single combat is then proposed between Aeneas and Turnus, but Aeneas is so obviously superior to Turnus that the Rutuli, urged on by Turnus\'s divine sister, <a href="/wiki/Juturna" title="Juturna">Juturna</a>, break the truce. Aeneas is injured, but returns to the battle. Turnus and Aeneas dominate the battle on opposite wings, but when Aeneas makes a daring attack at the city of Latium (causing the queen of Latium to hang herself in despair), he forces Turnus into single combat once more. Turnus\'s strength deserts him as he tries to hurl a rock, and Aeneas\'s spear goes through his thigh. As Turnus is on his knees,  begging for his life, the epic ends with Aeneas first tempted to obey pleas to spare Turnus\'s life, but killing him in rage when he sees that Turnus is wearing his friend Pallas\'s belt over a shoulder as a trophy.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Critics of the <i>Aeneid</i> focus on a variety of issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The tone of the poem as a whole is a particular matter of debate; some see the poem as ultimately pessimistic and politically subversive to the <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustan</a> regime, while others view it as a celebration of the new imperial dynasty. Virgil makes use of the symbolism of the Augustan regime, and some scholars see strong associations between Augustus and Aeneas, the one as founder and the other as re-founder of Rome. A strong <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleology</a>, or drive towards a climax, has been detected in the poem. The <i>Aeneid</i> is full of prophecies about the future of Rome, the deeds of Augustus, his ancestors, and famous Romans, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthaginian_Wars" title="Carthaginian Wars">Carthaginian Wars</a>; the shield of Aeneas even depicts Augustus\' victory at Actium in 31 BC. A further focus of study is the character of Aeneas. As the protagonist of the poem, Aeneas seems to constantly waver between his emotions and commitment to his prophetic duty to found Rome; critics note the breakdown of Aeneas\'s emotional control in the last sections of the poem where the "pious" and "righteous" Aeneas mercilessly slaughters the Latin warrior Turnus.\n</p><p>The <i>Aeneid</i> appears to have been a great success. Virgil is said to have recited Books 2, 4 and 6 to Augustus;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the mention of her son, Marcellus, in book 6 apparently caused Augustus\' sister <a href="/wiki/Octavia_the_Younger" title="Octavia the Younger">Octavia</a> to faint. The poem was unfinished when Virgil died in 19 BC.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Virgil.27s_death_and_editing"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Virgil\'s_death_and_editing">Virgil\'s death and editing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Virgil\'s death and editing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1197" data-file-width="1597" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG/220px-A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG/330px-A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG/440px-A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_mosaic_BardoMuseum.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Virgil, holding a manuscript of the <i>Aeneid</i>, flanked by the muses <a href="/wiki/Clio" title="Clio">Clio</a> (history) and <a href="/wiki/Melpomene" title="Melpomene">Melpomene</a> (tragedy). <a href="/wiki/Roman_mosaic" title="Roman mosaic">Roman mosaic</a>, third century AD, from <a href="/wiki/Hadrumetum" title="Hadrumetum">Hadrumetum</a>, now in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bardo_Museum" title="Bardo Museum">Bardo Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>According to tradition, Virgil traveled to Greece around 19 BC to revise the <i>Aeneid</i>. After meeting Augustus in Athens and deciding to return home, Virgil caught a fever while visiting a town near <a href="/wiki/Megara" title="Megara">Megara</a>. Virgil crossed to Italy by ship, weakened with disease, and died in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brundisium" title="Brundisium">Brundisium</a> harbour on 21 September&#160;19&#160;BC, leaving a wish that the manuscript of the <i>Aeneid</i> was <a href="/wiki/Book_burning" title="Book burning">to be burned</a>. Augustus ordered Virgil\'s literary executors, <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Varius_Rufus" title="Lucius Varius Rufus">Lucius Varius Rufus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plotius_Tucca" title="Plotius Tucca">Plotius Tucca</a>, to disregard that wish, instead ordering the <i>Aeneid</i> to be published with as few editorial changes as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> As a result, the existing text of the <i>Aeneid</i> may contain faults which Virgil was planning to correct before publication. However, the only obvious imperfections are a few lines of verse that are metrically unfinished (i.e., not a complete line of <a href="/wiki/Dactylic_hexameter" title="Dactylic hexameter">dactylic hexameter</a>). Other alleged "imperfections" are subject to scholarly debate.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="666" decoding="async" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg/220px-VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg/330px-VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg/440px-VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VaticanVergilFol22rFlightFromTroy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Folio 22 from the <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_Vaticanus" title="Vergilius Vaticanus">Vergilius Vaticanus</a>&#8212;flight from Troy</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i> was written in a time of major political and social change in Rome, with the fall of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_republic" title="Roman republic">Republic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Final_War_of_the_Roman_Republic" title="Final War of the Roman Republic">Final War of the Roman Republic</a> having torn through society and many Romans\' faith in the "Greatness of Rome" severely faltering. However, the new emperor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus Caesar</a>, began to institute a new era of prosperity and peace, specifically through the re-introduction of traditional Roman moral values. The <i>Aeneid</i> was seen as reflecting this aim, by depicting the heroic Aeneas as a man devoted and loyal to his country and its prominence, rather than his own personal gains. In addition, the <i>Aeneid</i> gives mythic legitimization to the rule of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> and, by extension, to his adopted son Augustus, by immortalizing the tradition that renamed Aeneas\'s son, Ascanius (called Ilus from <i>Ilium</i>, meaning Troy), <i>Iulus</i>, thus making him an ancestor of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gens_Julia" title="Gens Julia">gens Julia</a></i>, the family of Julius Caesar, and many other great imperial descendants as part of the prophecy given to him in the Underworld. (The meter shows that the name "Iulus" is pronounced as 3 syllables, not as "Julus".)\n</p><p>Despite the polished and complex nature of the <i>Aeneid</i> (legend stating that Virgil wrote only three lines of the poem each day), the number of half-complete lines and the abrupt ending are generally seen as evidence that Virgil died before he could finish the work. Because this poem was composed and preserved in writing rather than orally, the <i>Aeneid</i> is more complete than most classical epics. Furthermore, it is possible to debate whether Virgil intended to rewrite and add to such lines. Some of them would be difficult to complete, and in some instances, the brevity of a line increases its dramatic impact (some arguing the violent ending as a typically Virgilian comment on the darker, vengeful side of humanity). However, these arguments may be anachronistic&#8212;half-finished lines might equally, to Roman readers, have been a clear indication of an unfinished poem and have added nothing whatsoever to the dramatic effect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The perceived deficiency of any account of Aeneas\'s marriage to Lavinia or his founding of the Roman race led some writers, such as the 15th-century Italian poet <a href="/wiki/Maffeo_Vegio" title="Maffeo Vegio">Maffeo Vegio</a> (through his <i>Mapheus Vegius</i> widely printed in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>), <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Candido_Decembrio" title="Pietro Candido Decembrio">Pier Candido Decembrio</a> (whose attempt was never completed), Claudio Salvucci (in his 1994 epic poem <i>The Laviniad</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Ursula_K._Le_Guin" title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula K. Le Guin</a> (in her 2008 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Lavinia_(novel)" title="Lavinia (novel)">Lavinia</a></i>) to compose their own supplements.\n</p><p>Some legends state that Virgil, fearing that he would die before he had properly revised the poem, gave instructions to friends (including the current emperor, <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>) that the <i>Aeneid</i> should be burned upon his death, owing to its unfinished state and because he had come to dislike one of the sequences in Book VIII, in which <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a> made love, for its nonconformity to Roman moral virtues. The friends did not comply with Virgil\'s wishes and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus</a> himself ordered that they be disregarded. After minor modifications, the <i>Aeneid</i> was published.\n</p><p>The first full and faithful rendering of the poem in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_languages" title="English languages">Anglic language</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Scots</a> translation by <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Douglas" title="Gavin Douglas">Gavin Douglas</a>&#8212;his <i><a href="/wiki/Eneados" title="Eneados">Eneados</a></i>, completed in 1513, which also included Maffeo Vegio\'s supplement. Even in the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> considered this still to be the best <i>Aeneid</i> translation, praising the "richness and fervour" of its language and its hallmark fidelity to the original.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The English translation by the 17th-century poet <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a> is another important version. Most classic translations, including both Douglas and Dryden, employ a rhyme scheme; most more modern attempts do not.\n</p><p>Recent English verse translations include those by British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Day-Lewis" title="Cecil Day-Lewis">Cecil Day-Lewis</a> (1963) which strove to render Virgil\'s original <a href="/wiki/Hexameter" title="Hexameter">hexameter</a> line, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Mandelbaum" title="Allen Mandelbaum">Allen Mandelbaum</a> (honoured by a 1973 <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">National Book Award</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_Consultant_in_Poetry_to_the_Library_of_Congress" title="Poet Laureate Consultant in Poetry to the Library of Congress">Library of Congress Poet Laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzgerald" title="Robert Fitzgerald">Robert Fitzgerald</a> (1981), <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Lombardo" title="Stanley Lombardo">Stanley Lombardo</a> (2005), <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fagles" title="Robert Fagles">Robert Fagles</a> (2006), <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Ruden" title="Sarah Ruden">Sarah Ruden</a> (2008), and <a href="/wiki/Barry_B._Powell" title="Barry B. Powell">Barry B. Powell</a> (2015).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As with other classical Latin poetry, the meter is based on the length of syllables rather than the stress, though the interplay of meter and stress is also important. Virgil also incorporated such poetic devices as <a href="/wiki/Alliteration" title="Alliteration">alliteration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">synecdoche</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Assonance" title="Assonance">assonance</a>. Furthermore, he uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a> in his work, usually to add drama and tension to the scene. An example of a simile can be found in book II when Aeneas is compared to a shepherd who stood on the high top of a rock unaware of what is going on around him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It can be seen that just as the shepherd is a protector of his sheep, so too is Aeneas to his people.\n</p><p>As was the rule in classical antiquity, an author\'s style was seen as an expression of his personality and character. Virgil\'s Latin has been praised for its evenness, subtlety and dignity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i>, like other classical epics, is written in <a href="/wiki/Dactylic_hexameter" title="Dactylic hexameter">dactylic hexameters</a>: each line consists of six metrical feet made up of <a href="/wiki/Dactyl_(poetry)" title="Dactyl (poetry)">dactyls</a> (one long syllable followed by two short syllables) and <a href="/wiki/Spondee" title="Spondee">spondees</a> (two long syllables). This epic consists of twelve books, and the narrative is broken up into three sections of four books each, respectively addressing Dido; the Trojans\' arrival in Italy; and the war with the Latins. Each book has about 1,000 lines. The <i>Aeneid</i> comes to an abrupt ending, and scholars have speculated that Virgil died before he could finish the poem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pietas"><i>Pietas</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pietas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Roman ideal of <i><a href="/wiki/Pietas" title="Pietas">pietas</a></i> ("piety, dutiful respect"), which can be loosely translated from the Latin as a selfless sense of duty toward one\'s\nfilial, religious, and societal obligations, was a crux of ancient Roman morality. Throughout the <i>Aeneid</i>, Aeneas serves as the embodiment of\n<i>pietas</i>, with the phrase "pious Aeneas" occurring 20 times throughout the poem,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> thereby fulfilling his capacity as the father of the Roman people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\nFor instance, in Book 2 Aeneas describes how he carried his father Anchises from the burning city of Troy:  "No help/ Or hope of help existed./\nSo I resigned myself, picked up my father,/ And turned my face toward the mountain range."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  Furthermore, Aeneas ventures into the underworld, thereby fulfilling Anchises\'\nwishes. His father\'s gratitude is presented in the text by the following lines:  "Have you at last come, has that loyalty/ Your father counted on conquered the journey?\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, Aeneas\'s <i>pietas</i> extends beyond his devotion to his father: we also see several examples of his religious fervour. Aeneas is consistently subservient to the gods, even in actions opposed to his own desires, as he responds to one such divine command, "I sail to Italy not of my own free will."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to his religious and familial <i>pietas</i>, Aeneas also displays fervent patriotism and devotion to his people, particularly in a military capacity. For instance, as he and his followers leave Troy, Aeneas swears that he will "take up/ The combat once again. We shall not all/ Die this day unavenged."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Aeneas is a symbol of <i>pietas</i> in all of its forms, serving as a moral paragon to whom a Roman should aspire.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divine_intervention">Divine intervention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Divine intervention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One of the most recurring themes in the <i>Aeneid</i> is that of <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">divine intervention</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Throughout the poem, the gods are constantly influencing the main characters and trying to change and impact the outcome, regardless of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fate" title="Fate">fate</a> that they all know will occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> For example, Juno comes down and acts as a phantom Aeneas to drive Turnus away from the real Aeneas and all of his rage from the death of Pallas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Even though Juno knows in the end that Aeneas will triumph over Turnus, she does all she can to delay and avoid this outcome.\n</p><p>Divine intervention occurs multiple times, in Book 4 especially. Aeneas falls in love with Dido, delaying his ultimate fate of traveling to Italy. However, it is actually the gods who inspired the love, as Juno plots:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>Dido and the Trojan captain [will come]<br/>\nTo one same cavern. I shall be on hand,<br/>\nAnd if I can be certain you are willing,<br/>\nThere I shall marry them and call her his.<br/>\nA wedding, this will be.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Juno is speaking to Venus, making an agreement and influencing the lives and emotions of both Dido and Aeneas. Later in the same book, Jupiter steps in and restores what is the true fate and path for Aeneas, sending Mercury down to Aeneas\'s dreams, telling him that he must travel to Italy and leave his new-found lover. As Aeneas later pleads with Dido:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The gods\' interpreter, sent by Jove himself &#8211;<br/>\nI swear it by your head and mine &#8211; has brought<br/>\nCommands down through the racing winds!...<br/>\nI sail for Italy not of my own free will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Several of the gods try to intervene against the powers of fate, even though they know what the eventual outcome will be. The interventions are really just distractions to continue the conflict and postpone the inevitable. If the gods represent humans, just as the human characters engage in conflicts and power struggles, so too do the gods.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate">Fate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Fate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fate" title="Fate">Fate</a>, described as a preordained destiny that men and gods have to follow, is a major theme in the <i>Aeneid</i>. One example is when Aeneas is reminded of his fate through Jupiter and Mercury while he is falling in love with Dido. Mercury urges, "Think of your expectations of your heir,/ Iulus, to whom the whole Italian realm, the land/ Of Rome, are due."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Mercury is referring to Aeneas\'s preordained fate to found Rome, as well as Rome\'s preordained fate to rule the world:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>He was to be ruler of Italy,<br/>\nPotential empire, armorer of war;<br/>\nTo father men from Teucer\'s noble blood<br/>\nAnd bring the whole world under law\'s dominion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>It is important to recognize that there is a marked difference between fate and divine intervention, as even though the gods might remind mortals of their eventual fate, the gods themselves are not in control of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> For example, the opening lines of the poem specify that Aeneas "came to Italy by destiny", but is also harassed by the separate force of "baleful Juno in her sleepless rage".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Even though Juno might intervene, Aeneas\'s fate is set in stone and cannot be changed.\n</p><p>Later in Book 6, when Aeneas visits the underworld, his father Anchises introduces him to the larger fate of the Roman people, as contrasted against his own personal fate to found Rome:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>So raptly, everywhere, father and son<br/>\nWandered the airy plain and viewed it all.<br/>\nAfter Anchises had conducted him<br/>\nTo every region and had fired his love<br/>\nOf glory in the years to come, he spoke<br/>\nOf wars that he might fight, of Laurentines,<br/>\nAnd of Latinus\' city, then of how<br/>\nHe might avoid or bear each toil to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Violence_and_conflict">Violence and conflict</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Violence and conflict">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From the very beginning of the <i>Aeneid</i>, violence and conflict are used as a means of survival and conquest. Aeneas\'s voyage is caused by the Trojan War and the destruction of\nTroy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  Aeneas describes to Dido in Book 2 the massive amount of destruction that occurs after the Greeks sneak into Troy. He recalls that he asks his men to "defend/ A city\nlost in flames. Come, let us die,/ We\'ll make a rush into the thick of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\nThis is one of the first examples of how violence begets violence: even though the Trojans know they have lost the battle, they continue to fight for their country.\n</p><p>This violence continues as Aeneas makes his journey. Dido kills herself in an excessively violent way over a pyre in order to end and escape her worldly problem: being heartbroken\nover the departure of her "husband" Aeneas. Queen Dido\'s suicide is a double edged sword. While releasing herself from the burden of her pain through violence, her last words\nimplore her people to view Aeneas\'s people with hate for all eternity:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>This is my last cry, as my last blood flows.<br/>\nThen, O my Tyrians, besiege with hate<br/>\nHis progeny and all his race to come:<br/>\nMake this your offering to my dust. No love,<br/>\nNo pact must be between our peoples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Furthermore, her people, hearing of their queen\'s death, have only one avenue on which to direct the blame: the already-departed Trojans. Thus, Dido\'s request of her people\nand her people\'s only recourse for closure align in their mutual hate for Aeneas and his Trojans. In effect, Dido\'s violent suicide leads to the violent nature of the\n<a href="/wiki/Punic_Wars" title="Punic Wars">later relationship between Carthage and Rome.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Finally, when Aeneas arrives in Latium, conflict inevitably arises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  Juno sends <a href="/wiki/Alecto" title="Alecto">Alecto</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Erinyes" title="Erinyes">Furies</a>, to cause Turnus to go against Aeneas. In the ensuing battles, Turnus\nkills Pallas, who is supposed to be under Aeneas\'s protection. This act of violence causes Aeneas to be consumed with fury. Although Turnus asks for mercy in their final encounter,\nwhen Aeneas sees that Turnus has taken Pallas\' sword belt, Aeneas proclaims:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>You in your plunder, torn from one of mine,<br/>\nShall I be robbed of you?  This wound will come<br/>\nFrom Pallas: Pallas makes this offering<br/>\nAnd from your criminal blood exacts his due.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>This final act of violence shows how Turnus\' violence&#8212;the act of killing Pallas&#8212;inevitably leads to more violence and his own death.\n</p><p>It is possible that the recurring theme of violence in the <i>Aeneid</i> is a subtle commentary on the bloody violence contemporary readers would have just experienced during the Late Republican\n<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_civil_wars" title="Roman civil wars">civil wars</a>.  The <i>Aeneid</i> potentially explores whether the violence of the civil wars was necessary to establish a lasting peace under Augustus, or whether it would just lead to more violence in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propaganda">Propaganda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Propaganda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Political_commentary_of_the_Aeneid" title="Political commentary of the Aeneid">Political commentary of the Aeneid</a></div>\n<p>Written during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, the <i>Aeneid</i> presents the hero Aeneas as a strong and powerful leader. The favorable representation of Aeneas parallels\nAugustus in that it portrays his reign in a progressive and admirable light, and allows Augustus to be positively associated with the portrayal of Aeneas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\nAlthough Virgil\'s patron <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Maecenas" title="Gaius Maecenas">Maecenas</a> was obviously not Augustus himself, he was still a high figure within Augustus\' administration and could have personally\nbenefitted from representing Aeneas in a positive light.\n</p><p>In the <i>Aeneid</i>, Aeneas is portrayed as the singular hope for the rebirth of the Trojan people. Charged with the preservation of his people by divine authority, Aeneas is symbolic of Augustus\' own accomplishments in establishing order after the long period of chaos of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_civil_wars" title="Roman civil wars">Roman civil wars</a>. Augustus as the light of savior and the last\nhope of the Roman people is a parallel to Aeneas as the savior of the Trojans. This parallel functions as <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> in support of Augustus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> as it depicts the Trojan people,\nfuture Romans themselves, as uniting behind a single leader who will lead them out of ruin:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>New refugees in a great crowd: men and women<br/>\nGathered for exile, young-pitiful people<br/>\nComing from every quarter, minds made up,<br/>\nWith their belongings, for whatever lands<br/>\nI\'d lead them to by sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Later in Book 6, Aeneas travels to the underworld where he sees his father Anchises, who tells him of his own destiny as well as that of\nthe Roman people. Anchises describes how Aeneas\'s descendant <a href="/wiki/Romulus" title="Romulus">Romulus</a> will found the great city of Rome,\nwhich will eventually be ruled by Caesar Augustus:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>Turn your two eyes<br/>\nThis way and see this people, your own Romans.<br/>\nHere is Caesar, and all the line of Iulus,<br/>\nAll who shall one day pass under the dome<br/>\nOf the great sky: this is the man, this one,<br/>\nOf whom so often you have heard the promise,<br/>\nCaesar Augustus, son of the deified,<br/>\nWho shall bring once again an Age of Gold<br/>\nTo Latium, to the land where Saturn reigned<br/>\nIn early times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Virgil writes about the fated future of <a href="/wiki/Lavinium" title="Lavinium">Lavinium</a>, the city that Aeneas will found, which will in turn lead directly to the\ngolden reign of Augustus. Virgil is using a form of literary propaganda to demonstrate the Augustan\nregime\'s destiny to bring glory and peace to Rome. Rather than use Aeneas indirectly as a positive parallel\nto Augustus as in other parts of the poem, Virgil outright praises the emperor in Book 6, referring to Augustus as a harbinger for the glory\nof Rome and new levels of prosperity.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegory">Allegory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Allegory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The poem abounds with smaller and greater allegories. Two of the debated allegorical sections pertain to the exit from the underworld and to Pallas\'s belt.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>There are two gates of Sleep, one said to be of horn, whereby the true shades pass with ease, the other <i>all white ivory agleam without a flaw, and yet false dreams are sent</i> through this one by the ghost to the upper world. Anchises now, his last instructions given, took son and Sibyl and let them <i>go by the Ivory Gate</i>.</dd>\n<dd>&#8212;Book VI, lines 1211&#8211;1218, Fitzgerald trans. (emphasis added)</dd></dl>\n<p>Aeneas\'s leaving the underworld through the gate of false dreams has been variously interpreted: One suggestion is that the passage simply refers to the time of day at which Aeneas returned to the world of the living; another is that it implies that all of Aeneas\'s actions in the remainder of the poem are somehow "false". In an extension of the latter interpretation, it has been suggested that Virgil is conveying that the history of the world since the foundation of Rome is but a lie. Other scholars claim that Virgil is establishing that the theological implications of the preceding scene (an apparent system of <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a>) are not to be taken as literal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The second section in question is\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Then to his glance appeared the accurst swordbelt surmounting Turnus\' shoulder, shining with its familiar studs&#8212;the strap Young Pallas wore when Turnus wounded him and left him dead upon the field; now Turnus bore that enemy token on his shoulder&#8212;enemy still. For when the sight came home to him, Aeneas <i>raged</i> at the relic of his anguish worn by this man as trophy. <i>Blazing up and terrible in his anger</i>, he called out: "You in your plunder, torn from one of mine, shall I be robbed of you? This wound will come from Pallas: Pallas makes this offering, and from your criminal blood exacts his due." <i>He sank his blade in fury</i> in Turnus\' chest&#160;...</dd>\n<dd>&#8212;Book XII, lines 1281&#8211;1295, Fitzgerald trans. (emphasis added)</dd></dl>\n<p>This section has been interpreted to mean that for the entire passage of the poem, Aeneas who symbolizes <i>pietas</i> (reason) in a moment becomes <i>furor</i> (fury), thus destroying what is essentially the primary theme of the poem itself. Many have argued over these two sections. Some claim that Virgil meant to change them before he died, while others find that the location of the two passages, at the very end of the so-called Volume I (Books 1&#8211;6, the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>), and Volume II (Books 7&#8211;12, the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>), and their short length, which contrasts with the lengthy nature of the poem, are evidence that Virgil placed them purposefully there.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VirgilAeneidVI.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4231" data-file-width="4613" decoding="async" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/VirgilAeneidVI.jpg/220px-VirgilAeneidVI.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/VirgilAeneidVI.jpg/330px-VirgilAeneidVI.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/VirgilAeneidVI.jpg/440px-VirgilAeneidVI.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VirgilAeneidVI.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Virgil Reading the Aeneid to Augustus and Octavia</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Joseph_Taillasson" title="Jean-Joseph Taillasson">Jean-Joseph Taillasson</a>, 1787, an early <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassical</a> painting (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Gallery_(London)" title="National Gallery (London)">National Gallery</a>, London)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i> is a cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/Western_canon" title="Western canon">Western canon</a>, and early (at least by the 2nd century AD) became one of the essential elements of a Latin education,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> usually required to be memorized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Even after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">decline of the Roman Empire</a>, it "remained central to a Latin education".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In Latin-Christian culture, the <i>Aeneid</i> was one of the canonical texts, subjected to <a href="/wiki/Commentary_(philology)" title="Commentary (philology)">commentary</a> as a philological and educational study,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> with the most complete commentary having been written by the 4th-century grammarian <a href="/wiki/Maurus_Servius_Honoratus" title="Maurus Servius Honoratus">Maurus Servius Honoratus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> It was widely held to be the pinnacle of Latin literature, much in the same way that the <i>Iliad</i> was seen to be supreme in Greek literature.\n</p><p>The strong influence of the <i>Aeneid</i> has been identified in the development of European vernacular literatures&#8212;some English works that show its influence being <i><a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Layamon%27s_Brut" title="Layamon\'s Brut">Layamon\'s <i>Brut</i></a> (through the source text <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faerie_Queene" title="The Faerie Queene">The Faerie Queene</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">Milton</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost" title="Paradise Lost">Paradise Lost</a></i>. The Italian poet <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> was himself profoundly influenced by the <i>Aeneid</i>, so much so that his magnum opus <i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">The Divine Comedy</a></i>, itself widely considered central to the western canon, includes a number of quotations from and allusions to the <i>Aeneid</i> and features the author Virgil as a major character &#8211; the guide of Dante through the realms of the Inferno and Purgatorio. Another continental work displaying the influence of the <i>Aeneid</i> is the 16th-century Portuguese epic <i><a href="/wiki/Os_Lus%C3%ADadas" title="Os Lus&#237;adas">Os Lus&#237;adas</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Lu&#237;s de Cam&#245;es">Lu&#237;s de Cam&#245;es</a> and dealing with <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>\'s voyage to India.\n</p><p>The importance of Latin education itself was paramount in Western culture: "from 1600 to 1900, the Latin school was at the center of European education, wherever it was found"; within that Latin school, Virgil was taught at the advanced level and, in 19th-century England, special editions of Virgil were awarded to students who distinguished themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the United States, Virgil and specifically the <i>Aeneid</i> were taught in the fourth year of a Latin sequence, at least until the 1960s;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> the current (2011) <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Placement" title="Advanced Placement">Advanced Placement</a> curriculum in Latin continues to assign a central position to the poem: "The AP Latin: Virgil Exam is designed to test the student\'s ability to read, translate, understand, analyze, and interpret the lines of the <i>Aeneid</i> that appear on the course syllabus in Latin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many phrases from this poem entered the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> language, much as passages from <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a> have entered the English language. One example is from Aeneas\'s reaction to a painting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sack_of_Troy" title="Sack of Troy">sack of Troy</a>: <i>Sunt lacrimae rerum et mentem mortalia tangunt</i>&#8212;"These are the tears of things, and our mortality cuts to the heart" (<i>Aeneid</i> I, 462). The influence is also visible in very modern work: <a href="/wiki/Brian_Friel" title="Brian Friel">Brian Friel</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Translations" title="Translations">Translations</a></i> (a play written in the 1980s, set in 19th-century Ireland), makes references to the classics throughout and ends with a passage from the <i>Aeneid</i>:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p><i>Urbs antiqua fuit</i>&#8212;there was an ancient city which, \'tis said, <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> loved above all the lands. And it was the goddess\'s aim and cherished hope that here should be the capital of all nations&#8212;should the fates perchance allow that. Yet in truth she discovered that a race was springing from Trojan blood to overthrow some day these Tyrian towers&#8212;a people <i>late regem belloque superbum</i>&#8212;kings of broad realms and proud in war who would come forth for Libya\'s downfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i> was the basis for the 1962 Italian film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Avenger_(1962_film)" title="The Avenger (1962 film)">The Avenger</a></i>.\n</p><p>In the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Spring_Awakening_(musical)" title="Spring Awakening (musical)">Spring Awakening</a></i>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Spring_Awakening_(play)" title="Spring Awakening (play)">play of the same title</a> by Frank Wedekind, schoolboys study the Latin text, and the first verse of Book 1 is incorporated into the number "All That\'s Known".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ursula_K._Le_Guin" title="Ursula K. Le Guin">Ursula Le Guin</a>\'s 2008 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Lavinia_(novel)" title="Lavinia (novel)">Lavinia</a></i> is a free prose retelling of the last six books of the <i>Aeneid</i> narrated by and centered on Aeneas\' Latin wife <a href="/wiki/Lavinia" title="Lavinia">Lavinia</a>, a minor character in the epic poem. It carries the action forward to the crowning of Aeneas\' younger son <a href="/wiki/Silvius_(mythology)" title="Silvius (mythology)">Silvius</a> as king of Latium.\n</p><p>A seventeenth-century popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadside_(music)" title="Broadside (music)">broadside</a> <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> also appears to recount events from books 1&#8211;4 of the <i>Aeneid,</i> focusing mostly on the relationship between Aeneas and Dido. The ballad, "<a href="/wiki/The_Wandering_Prince_of_Troy" title="The Wandering Prince of Troy">The Wandering Prince of Troy</a>", presents many similar elements as Virgil\'s epic, but alters Dido\'s final sentiments toward Aeneas, as well as presents an interesting end for Aeneas himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies_and_travesties">Parodies and travesties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeneid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Parodies and travesties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>A number of <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parodies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burlesque" title="Burlesque">travesties</a> of the <i>Aeneid</i> have been made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> One of the earliest was written in Italian by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giovanni_Batista_Lalli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giovanni Batista Lalli (page does not exist)">Giovanni Batista Lalli</a> in 1635, titled <i>L\'Eneide travestita del Signor Gio</i>.</li>\n<li>A French parody by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scarron" title="Paul Scarron">Paul Scarron</a> became famous in France in the mid-17th century, and spread rapidly through Europe, accompanying the growing French influence. Its influence was especially strong in Russia.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_Cotton" title="Charles Cotton">Charles Cotton</a>\'s work <i>Scarronides</i> included a travestied <i>Aeneid</i>.</li>\n<li>In 1791 the Russian poet <a href="/wiki/N._P._Osipov" title="N.
