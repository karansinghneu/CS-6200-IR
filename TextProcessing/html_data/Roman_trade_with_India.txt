Trade through the overland caravan routes via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, though at a relative trickle compared to later times, antedated the southern trade route via the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and monsoons which started around the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">Common Era</a> (CE) following the reign of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%86gyptus" title="&#198;gyptus">his conquest</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Egypt</a> in 30 BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The southern route so helped enhance trade between the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> and the Indian subcontinent, that Roman politicians and historians are on record decrying the loss of silver and gold to buy silk to pamper Roman wives, and the southern route grew to eclipse and then totally supplant the overland trade route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young1-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Roman and Greek traders frequented the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Tamil_country" title="Ancient Tamil country">ancient Tamil country</a>, present day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_India" title="Southern India">Southern India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, securing trade with the seafaring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamil_people" title="Tamil people">Tamil</a> states of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandyan_Empire" title="Pandyan Empire">Pandyan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chola_Empire" title="Chola Empire">Chola</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chera_Empire" title="Chera Empire">Chera</a> dynasties and <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_ancient_Tamil_country#Foreign_trade" title="Economy of ancient Tamil country">establishing trading settlements</a> which secured trade with the Indian Subcontinent by the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a> since the time of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindsay1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindsay1-3">[3]</a></sup> a few decades before the start of the <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">Common Era</a> and remained long after the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curtin1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curtin1-4">[4]</a></sup> As recorded by <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Augustus" title="Emperor Augustus">Emperor Augustus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> received at <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> an ambassador from a South Indian king called <b>Pandyan of Dramira</b>. The country of the Pandyas, Pandi Mandala, was described as <i>Pandyan Mediterranea</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">Periplus</a></i> and <i>Modura Regia Pandyan</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_cyclop&#230;dia_of_India_and_of_Eastern_and_Southern_Asia_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_cyclop&#230;dia_of_India_and_of_Eastern_and_Southern_Asia-5">[5]</a></sup> They also outlasted <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>\'s loss of the ports of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holl1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holl1-6">[6]</a></sup> (c. 639&#8211;645 CE) under the pressure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">Muslim conquests</a>. Sometime after the sundering of communications between the Axum and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> in the 7th century, the Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Axum" title="Kingdom of Axum">kingdom of Axum</a> fell into a slow decline, fading into obscurity in western sources. It survived, despite pressure from Islamic forces, until the 11th century, when it was reconfigured in a dynastic squabble. Communications were reinstated after the Muslim forces retreated.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" The dramatic increase in the importance of Indian ports, however, did not occur until the opening of the Red Sea by the Greeks and the Romans\' attainment concerning the region&#8217;s seasonal monsoons. The first two centuries of the Common Era indicate a marked increase in trade between western India and the Roman east by sea. The expansion of trade was made possible by the stability brought to the region by the Roman Empire from the time of Augustus (r. 27 BCE&#8211;14 CE) which allowed for new explorations and the creation of a sound silver and gold coinage. .\n</p><p>\nThe west coast of present-day India is mentioned frequently in literature, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periplus_Maris_Erythraei" title="Periplus Maris Erythraei">Periplus Maris Erythraei</a>. The area was noted for its strong tidal currents, turbulent waves and rocky sea-beds were dangerous for shipping experience. The anchors of ships would be caught by the waves and quickly detach to capsize the vessel or cause a shipwreck. Stone anchors have been observed near <a href="/wiki/Bet_Dwarka" title="Bet Dwarka">Bet Dwarka</a>, an island situated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Kachchh" title="Gulf of Kachchh">Gulf of Kachchh</a>, from ship lost at sea. Onshore and offshore explorations have been carried out around Bet Dwarka Island since 1983. The finds discovered include lead and stone objects buried in sediment and considered to be anchors due to their axial holes. Though it is unlikely that the remains of the shipwreck&#8217;s hull survived, offshore explorations in 2000 and 2001 have yielded seven differently-sized amphoras, two lead anchors, forty-two stone anchors of different types, a supply of potsherds, and a circular lead ingot. The remains of the seven amphoras were of a thick, coarse fabric with a rough surface, which was used for exporting wine and olive oil from the Roman Empire. Archeologists have concluded that most of these were wine amphoras, since olive oil was in less demand in the subcontinent. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1319" data-file-width="1289" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg/220px-TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg/330px-TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg/440px-TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TrajanCoinAhinposhBuddhistMonasteryAfghanistan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A coin of <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">Trajan</a>, found together with coins of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushan" title="Kushan">Kushan</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Kanishka" title="Kanishka">Kanishka</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Ahin_Posh" title="Ahin Posh">Ahin Posh</a> Buddhist Monastery, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Since the discoveries at Bet Dwarka are significant for the maritime history of the region, archeologists have researched the resources in India.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Despite the unfavorable conditions the island is situated in, the following items have made Bet Dwarka as well as the rest of western India an important place for trade. From Latin literature, Rome imported Indian tigers, rhinoceros, elephants, and serpents to use for circus shows &#8211; a method employed as entertainment to prevent riots in Rome. It has been noted in the <a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">Periplus</a> that Roman women also wore <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> pearls and used a supply of herbs, spices, pepper, lyceum, costus, sesame oil and sugar for food. Indigo was used as a color while cotton cloth was used as articles of clothing. Furthermore, the subcontinent exported ebony for fashioned furniture in Rome. The Roman Empire also imported Indian lime, peach, and various other fruits for medicine. Western India, as a result, was the recipient of large amounts of Roman gold during this time.\n</p><p>Since one must sail against the narrow gulfs of western India, special large boats were used and ship development was demanded. At the entrance of the gulf, large ships called trappaga and cotymba helped guide foreign vessels safely to the harbor. These ships were capable of relatively long coastal cruises, and several seals have depicted this type of ship. In each seal, parallel bands were suggested to represent the beams of the ship. In the center of the vessel is a single mast with a tripod base.\n</p><p>Apart from the recent explorations, close trade relations, as well as the development of ship building, were supported by the discovery of several Roman coins. On these coins were depictions of two strongly constructed masted ships. Thus, these depictions of Indian ships, originating from both coins and literature (Pliny and Pluriplus), indicate Indian development in seafaring due to the increase in Indo-Roman commerce. In addition, the silver Roman coins discovered in western India primarily come from the 1st, 2nd, and 5th centuries. These Roman coins also suggest that the Indian peninsula possessed a stable seaborne trade with Rome during 1st and 2nd century AD. Land routes, during the time of Augustus, were also used for Indian embassies to reach Rome.\n</p><p>The discoveries found on Bet Dwarka and on other areas on the western coast of India strongly indicate that there were strong Indo-Roman trade relations during the first two centuries of the Common Era. The 3rd century, however, was the demise of the Indo-Roman trade. The sea-route between Rome and India was shut down, and as a result, the trading reverted to the time prior to Roman expansion and exploration.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trade of exotic animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="618" data-file-width="1167" decoding="async" height="117" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg/330px-Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg/440px-Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sri_Lankan_imitations_of_4th_century_Roman_coins_4th_to_8th_century_CE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lankan</a> imitations of 4th-century Roman coins, 4th&#8211;8th century CE.</div></div></div>\n<p>There is evidence of animal trade between Indian Ocean harbours and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>. This can be seen in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosaics" title="Mosaics">mosaics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frescoes" title="Frescoes">frescoes</a> of the remains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_villas" title="Roman villas">Roman villas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Romana_del_Casale" title="Villa Romana del Casale">Villa del Casale</a> has mosaics depicting the capture of animals in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The intercontinental trade of animals was one of the sources of wealth for the owners of the villa. In the <a class="external text" href="http://www.villadelcasale.it/ambulacro-grande-caccia.html?lang=en" rel="nofollow">Ambulacro della Grande Caccia</a>, the hunting and capture of animals is represented in such detail that it is possible to identify the species. There is a scene that shows a technique to distract a mother <a href="/wiki/Tiger" title="Tiger">tiger</a> with a shimmering ball of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirror</a> in order to take her cubs. Tiger hunting with red ribbons serving as a distraction is also shown. In the mosaic there are also numerous other animals such as <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceros</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Indian_elephant" title="Indian elephant">Indian elephant</a> (recognized from the ears) with his Indian conductor, and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_peafowl" title="Indian peafowl">Indian peafowl</a>, and other exotic birds. There are also numerous animals from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Tigers, <a href="/wiki/Leopard" title="Leopard">leopards</a> and Asian and African <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> were used in the <a href="/wiki/Arena" title="Arena">arenas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circuses</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_lion" title="European lion">European lion</a> was already extinct at that time. Probably the last lived in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a> and were hunted to stock arenas. The birds and monkeys entertained the guests of many villas. Also in the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Romana_del_Tellaro" title="Villa Romana del Tellaro">Villa Romana del Tellaro</a> there is a mosaic with a tiger in the jungle attacking a man with Roman clothes, probably a careless hunter. The animals were transported in cages by ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ports">Ports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_ports">Roman ports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman ports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The three main Roman ports involved with eastern trade were <a href="/wiki/Arsinoe_(Gulf_of_Suez)" title="Arsinoe (Gulf of Suez)">Arsinoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berenice_Troglodytica" title="Berenice Troglodytica">Berenice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Myos_Hormos" title="Myos Hormos">Myos Hormos</a>. Arsinoe was one of the early trading centers but was soon overshadowed by the more easily accessible Myos Hormos and Berenice.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsinoe">Arsinoe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arsinoe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egypt-region-map-cities-2.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="373" data-file-width="310" decoding="async" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Egypt-region-map-cities-2.gif/260px-Egypt-region-map-cities-2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Egypt-region-map-cities-2.gif 1.5x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egypt-region-map-cities-2.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sites of Egyptian Red Sea ports, including Alexandria and Berenice.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Ptolemaic dynasty exploited the strategic position of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> to secure trade with the subcontinent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindsay1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lindsay1-3">[3]</a></sup> The course of trade with the east then seems to have been first through the harbor of Arsinoe, the present day <a href="/wiki/Suez" title="Suez">Suez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindsay1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lindsay1-3">[3]</a></sup> The goods from the <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East African</a> trade were landed at one of the three main Roman ports, Arsinoe, Berenice or Myos Hormos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Romans repaired and cleared out the silted up canal from the Nile to harbor center of Arsinoe on the Red Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fayle1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fayle1-19">[19]</a></sup> This was one of the many efforts the Roman administration had to undertake to divert as much of the trade to the maritime routes as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fayle1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fayle1-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Arsinoe was eventually overshadowed by the rising prominence of Myos Hormos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fayle1_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fayle1-19">[19]</a></sup> The navigation to the northern ports, such as Arsinoe-Clysma, became difficult in comparison to Myos Hormos due to the northern winds in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Suez" title="Gulf of Suez">Gulf of Suez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman1-20">[20]</a></sup> Venturing to these northern ports presented additional difficulties such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoals" title="Shoals">shoals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a> and treacherous <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">currents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman1-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Myos_Hormos_and_Berenice">Myos Hormos and Berenice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Myos Hormos and Berenice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Myos Hormos and Berenice appear to have been important ancient trading ports, possibly used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharaonic" title="Pharaonic">Pharaonic</a> traders of ancient Egypt and the Ptolemaic dynasty before falling into Roman control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The site of Berenice, since its discovery by Belzoni (1818), has been equated  with the ruins near <a href="/wiki/Ras_Banas" title="Ras Banas">Ras Banas</a> in Southern Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the precise location of Myos Hormos is disputed with the latitude and longitude given in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographia_(Ptolemy)" title="Geographia (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> favoring Abu Sha\'ar and the accounts given in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_literature" title="Classical literature">classical literature</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_images" title="Satellite images">satellite images</a> indicating a probable identification with Quseir el-Quadim at the end of a fortified road from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koptos" title="Koptos">Koptos</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-1">[1]</a></sup> The Quseir el-Quadim site has further been associated with Myos Hormos following the excavations at el-Zerqa, halfway along the route, which have revealed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostraca" title="Ostraca">ostraca</a> leading to the conclusion that the port at the end of this road may have been Myos Hormos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_regional_ports">Major regional ports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wari-Bateshwar" title="Wari-Bateshwar">Sounagoura</a> (central <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>), <a href="/wiki/Bharuch" href="#cite_note-Periplus1-21">[21]</a></sup> In Barygaza, they would buy wheat, rice, sesame oil, cotton and cloth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus1-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Barigaza">Barigaza</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Barigaza, under the control of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Scythian" title="Indo-Scythian">Indo-Scythian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Satrap" title="Western Satrap">Western Satrap</a> <a href="/wiki/Nahapana" title="Nahapana">Nahapana</a> ("Nambanus"), was especially flourishing:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus1_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus1-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There are imported into this market-town (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barigaza" title="Barigaza">Barigaza</a>), wine, Italian preferred, also Laodicean and Arabian; copper, tin, and lead; coral and topaz; thin the money of the country; and ointment, but not very costly and not much. modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranganore" title="Cranganore">Cranganore</a> (central Kerala).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">Periplus</a>, numerous Greek seamen managed an intense trade with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pondicherry_(city)" title="Pondicherry (city)">Pondicherry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huntingford1_25-0"><a saw several cultural exchanges which had a lasting effect on both the civilizations and others involved in the trade. The <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopian</a> kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aksum" title="Aksum">Aksum</a> was involved in the Indian Ocean trade network and was id="cite_ref-Lach2_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lach2-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> mentions the <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Buddha</a> in his writings and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_religion" title="Indian religion">Indian religions</a> find mentions href="/wiki/Jianzhi" title="Jianzhi">Jianzhi</a>) in northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, according to <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four and <a href="/wiki/Roman_metallurgy" title="Roman metallurgy">silverwares</a> have been found in China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">the land route</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Persia" title="Ancient Persia">ancient Persia</a> playing a secondary role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" href="#cite_note-Curtin1-4">[4]</a></sup> Large hoards of Roman coins have been found throughout India, and especially in the busy maritime trading centers of the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curtin1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Curtin1-4">[4]</a></sup> The Tamilakkam kings reissued Roman coinage in their own name after defacing the coins in order to signify their sovereignty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulke1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kulke1-38">[38]</a></sup> Mentions of the traders are recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a> of India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulke1_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kulke1-38">[38]</a></sup> One such mention reads: "The beautiful warships of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yavanas" title="Yavanas">Yavanas</a> came to the prosperous and beautiful Muchiri (<a href="/wiki/Muziris" title="Muziris">Muziris</a>) breaking the white foams of \'<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periyar_river" title="Periyar river">Chulli</a>\', the big river, and returned with \'curry\' (kari, the black pepper) paying for it in gold. (from poem no. 149 of \'Akananuru\' of Sangam Literature)"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kulke1_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kulke1-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_aftermath">Decline and aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Decline and aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_decline">Roman decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Roman decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Trade declined from the mid-3rd century during a crisis period in the Roman Empire but recovered in the 4th century until the early 7th century when <a href="/wiki/Khosrow_II" title="Khosrow II">Khosrow II</a> Shah of  <a href="/wiki/Persian_Empire" title="Persian Empire">Persian</a> occupied the Roman parts of the Fertile Crescent and Egypt beginning in 614 until defeated by the <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Eastern Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Heraclius" title="Heraclius">Heraclius</a> at the end of 627 after which the lost territories were returned to the Romans.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ravaging_of_the_Gupta_Empire_by_the_Huns">Ravaging of the Gupta Empire by the Huns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Roman_trade_relations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ravaging of the Gupta Empire by the Huns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In India, the <a href="/wiki/Alchon_Huns" title="Alchon Huns">Alchon Huns</a> invasions (496&#8211;534 CE) are said to have seriously damaged India\'s trade with <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eraly_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eraly-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">Gupta Empire</a> had been benefiting greatly from Indo-Roman trade. They had been exporting numerous luxury products such as <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, leather goods, fur, iron products, <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearl</a> or pepper from centers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasik" title="Nasik">Nashik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paithan" title="Paithan">Prathisthana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pataliputra" title="Pataliputra">Pataliputra</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benares" title="Benares">Varanasi</a> etc. The Huna invasion probably disrupted these trade relations and the tax revenues that came with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Soon after the invasions, the Gupta Empire, already weakened by these invasions and the rise of local rulers, ended as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  Following the invasions, northern India was left in disarray, with numerous smaller Indian powers emerging after the crumbling of the Guptas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" title="\'Amr ibn al-\'As">\'Amr ibn al-\'As</a>, crossed into Egypt in late 639 or early 640 CE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meri1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meri1-44">[44]</a></sup> This href="#cite_note-Lindsay1-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tamil speaking south India turned to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> for international trade where Indian culture influenced the native culture to a greater degree than the sketchy impressions made on Rome seen in the adoption of Hinduism and then Buddhism <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> However, knowledge of the Indian subcontinent and its trade was preserved in Byzantine books and it is likely that the court of the emperor still maintained some form of diplomatic relation to the region up until at least the time of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_VII" title="Constantine VII">Constantine VII</a>, seeking an ally against the rising influence of the Islamic states in the Middle East and Persia, appearing in a work on ceremonies called <a href="/wiki/De_Ceremoniis" title="De Ceremoniis">De Ceremoniis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in the 15th century (1453), marking the beginning of Turkish control over the most direct trade routes between Europe and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The Ottomans initially cut off eastern trade with Europe, leading in turn to the attempt by Europeans to find a sea route around Africa, spurring the European <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>, and the eventual rise of European <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">Mercantilism</a> and <a
