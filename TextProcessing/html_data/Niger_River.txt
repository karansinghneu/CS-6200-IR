River</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary It runs in a crescent through <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, on the border with <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a> and then through <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, discharging through a massive <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">delta</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Niger_Delta" title="Niger Delta">Niger Delta</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_Rivers" title="Oil Rivers">Oil Rivers</a>, into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.  The Niger is the third-longest river in Africa, exceeded only by the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a> (also known as the Za&#239;re River). Its main <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributary</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Benue_River" title="Benue River">Benue River</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Niger has different names in the different languages of the region:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Manding_languages" title="Manding languages">Manding</a>: <i>Jeliba</i> or <i>Joliba</i> "great river"</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Igbo_language" title="Igbo language">Igbo</a>: <i>Orimiri</i> or <i>Orimili</i> "great water"</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tuareg_languages" title="Tuareg languages">Tuareg</a>: <i>Egerew n-Igerewen</i> "river of rivers"</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Songhay_languages" title="Songhay languages">Songhay</a>: <i>Isa</i> "the river"</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Zarma_language" title="Zarma language">Zarma</a>: <i>Isa Beeri</i> "great river"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>: <i>Kwara</i></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">Yoruba</a>: <i>Oya</i></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fula_language" title="Fula language">Fula</a>: <i>Maayo Jaaliba</i></li></ul>\n<p>The earliest use of the name "Niger" for the river is by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Africanus" title="Leo Africanus">Leo Africanus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Description_of_Africa_(1550_book)" title="Description of Africa (1550 book)">Della descrittione dell&#8217;Africa et delle cose notabili che iui sono</a></i> published in Italian in 1550. The name may come from <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber</a> phrase <i>ger-n-ger</i> meaning "river of rivers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> was the southern end of the principal <a href="/wiki/Trans-Saharan_trade" title="Trans-Saharan trade">Trans-Saharan trade</a> route to the western Mediterranean, it was the source of most European knowledge of the region.\n</p><p>Medieval <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a> applied the name <i>Niger</i> to the middle reaches of the river, in modern Mali, but <i>Quorra (Kworra)</i> to the lower reaches in modern Nigeria, as these were not recognized at the time as being the same river. When European colonial powers began to send ships along the west coast of Africa in the 16th and 17th centuries, the <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a> was often postulated to be the seaward end of the Niger. The <a href="/wiki/Niger_Delta" title="Niger Delta">Niger Delta</a>, pouring into the Atlantic through mangrove swamps and thousands of <a href="/wiki/Distributary" title="Distributary">distributaries</a> along more than 160 kilometres (100 miles), was thought to be no more than coastal wetlands. It was only with the 18th-century visits of <a href="/wiki/Mungo_Park_(explorer)" title="Mungo Park (explorer)">Mungo Park</a>, who travelled down the Niger River and visited the great <a href="/wiki/Sahelian_kingdoms" title="Sahelian kingdoms">Sahelian empires</a> of his day, that Europeans correctly identified the course of the Niger and extended the name to its entire course.\n</p><p>The modern nations of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> take their names from the river, marking contesting national claims by colonial powers of the "Upper", "Lower" and "Middle" Niger river basin during the <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">Scramble for Africa</a> at the end of the 19th century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg/220px-Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg/330px-Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg/440px-Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mali.A2001291.1045.250m.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The great bend of the Niger River, seen from space, creates a green arc through the brown of the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> and Savanna.  The green mass on the left is the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Niger_Delta" title="Inner Niger Delta">Inner Niger Delta</a>, and on the far left are tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg/220px-Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg/330px-Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg/440px-Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niger_River_Center_Island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mud houses on the center island at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Debo" title="Lake Debo">Lake Debo</a>, a wide section of the Niger River.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Niger River is a relatively "clear" river, carrying only a tenth as much sediment as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile</a> because the Niger\'s headwaters lie in ancient rocks that provide little <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReader2001191_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReader2001191-7">[7]</a></sup> Like the Nile, the Niger floods yearly; this begins in September, peaks in November, and finishes by May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReader2001191_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReader2001191-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>An unusual feature of the river is the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Niger_Delta" title="Inner Niger Delta">Inner Niger Delta</a>, which forms where its <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> suddenly decreases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReader2001191_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReader2001191-7">[7]</a></sup> The result is a region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Braided_stream" title="Braided stream">braided streams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshes</a>, and lakes the size of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>; the seasonal floods make the Delta extremely productive for both <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReader2001191&#8211;192_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReader2001191&#8211;192-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The river loses nearly two-thirds of its potential flow in the Inner Delta between <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9gou" title="S&#233;gou">S&#233;gou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> to seepage and evaporation. All the water from the <a href="/wiki/Bani_River" title="Bani River">Bani River</a>, which flows into the Delta at <a href="/wiki/Mopti" title="Mopti">Mopti</a>, does not compensate for the \'losses\'. The average \'loss\' is estimated at 31&#160;km<sup>3</sup>/year, but varies considerably between years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-9">[9]</a></sup> The river is then joined by various tributaries, but also loses more water to evaporation. The quantity of water entering Nigeria measured in Yola was estimated at 25&#160;km<sup>3</sup>/year before the 1980s and at 13.5&#160;km<sup>3</sup>/year during the 1980s. The most important tributary of the Niger in Nigeria is the <a href="/wiki/Benue_River" title="Benue River">Benue River</a> which merges with the river at Lokoja in Nigeria. The total volume of tributaries in Nigeria is six times higher than the inflow into Nigeria, with a flow near the mouth of the river standing at 177.0&#160;km<sup>3</sup>/year before the 1980s and 147.3&#160;km<sup>3</sup>/year during the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unusual_route">Unusual route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unusual route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Niger takes one of the most unusual routes of any major river, a <a href="/wiki/Boomerang" title="Boomerang">boomerang</a> shape that baffled geographers for two centuries. Its source is just 240&#160;km (150&#160;mi) inland from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, but the river runs directly away from the sea into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, then takes a sharp right turn near the ancient city of <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> (Tombouctou) and heads southeast to the Gulf of Guinea.\n</p><p>This strange geography apparently came about because the Niger River is two ancient rivers joined together. The upper Niger, from the source west of Timbuktu to the bend in the current river near Timbuktu, once emptied into a now dry lake to the east northeast of Timbuktu, while the lower Niger started to the south of Timbuktu and flowed south into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>. Over time upstream erosion by the lower Niger resulted in <a href="/wiki/Stream_capture" title="Stream capture">stream capture</a> of the upper Niger by the lower Niger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PG462_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PG462-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The northern part of the river, known as the <i>Niger bend</i>, is an important area because it is the major river and source of water in that part of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert. This made it the focal point of trade across the western Sahara, and the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Sahelian_kingdoms" title="Sahelian kingdoms">Sahelian kingdoms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mali_Empire" title="Mali Empire">Mali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gao" title="Gao">Gao</a>.\n</p><p>The surrounding Niger River Basin is one of the distinct physiographic sections of the Sudan province, which in turn is part of the larger <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=African_massive&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="African massive (page does not exist)">African massive</a> physiographic division.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1744" decoding="async" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg/220px-Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg/330px-Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg/440px-Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ravenna_Cosmography_1889_Africa_crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A reconstruction of the <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a> placed on a <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_world_map" title="Ptolemy\'s world map">Ptolemaic map</a>.  The <b>River Ger</b> is visible at bottom.  Note it is placed, following <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, as just south of the land of the <a href="/wiki/Garamantes" title="Garamantes">Garamantes</a>, in modern <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, constricting the continent to the land from the central <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> north.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="921" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG/220px-1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG/330px-1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG/440px-1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1561_map_of_West_Africa_by_Girolamo_Ruscelli.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1561 map of West Africa by Girolamo Ruscelli, from Italian translation of Ptolemy\'s Atlas "La Geograpfia Di Claudio Tolomeo Alessandrino, Nouvamente Tradatta Di Greco in Italiano".  The writer was attempting to square information gleaned from Portuguese trade along the coast with <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_world_map" title="Ptolemy\'s world map">Ptolemy\'s world map</a>.  The mouths of the <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gambia_River" title="Gambia River">Gambia River</a> are postulated to flow into a lake, which also feeds the "Ger"/<b>Niger River</b>, which in turn feeds the "Nile Lake" and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The origin of the river\'s name remains unclear.  What is clear is that "Niger" was an appellation applied in the Mediterranean world from at least the Classical era, when knowledge of the area by Europeans was slightly better than fable.   A careful study of Classical writings on the interior of the Sahara begins with <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, who mentions two rivers in the desert: the "Gir" (&#915;&#949;&#953;&#961;)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and farther south, the "Nigir" (&#925;&#953;&#947;&#949;&#953;&#961;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meek1960_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-meek1960-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  The first has been since identified as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wadi_Ghir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wadi Ghir (page does not exist)">Wadi Ghir</a> on the north western edge of the <a href="/wiki/Tuat" title="Tuat">Tuat</a>, along the borders of modern <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meek1960_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-meek1960-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  This would likely have been as far as Ptolemy would have had consistent records.  The Ni-Ger was likely speculation, although the name stuck as that of a river south of the Mediterranean\'s "known world".  <a href="/wiki/Suetonius" title="Suetonius">Suetonius</a> reports Romans traveling to the "Ger", although in reporting any river\'s name derived from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">Berber language</a>, in which "gher" means "watercourse", confusion could easily arise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson1948258&#8211;259_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1948258&#8211;259-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> connected these two rivers  as one long watercourse which flowed (via lakes and underground sections) into the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThomson1948258_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1948258-16">[16]</a></sup> a notion which persisted in the Arab and European worlds &#8211; and further added the <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a> as the "Ger" &#8211;  until the 19th century.  The connection to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">Nile River</a> was made not simply because this was then known as the great river of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aethiopia_(Classical_Greek_term)" title="Aethiopia (Classical Greek term)">Aethiopia</a>" (by which all lands south of the desert were called by Classical writers), but because the Nile flooded every summer.  In Europe and Western Asia, floods are expected in the Spring, following snow melt.  Classical authors explained the summer flood by calculating the time it took for flood waters to move down a river, and calculating how long the Nile must have been for the waters to travel from a mountain range in the spring.  However the cycle of the Nile is influenced by tropical rain patterns instead of by melting snow, a characteristic unknown to the Classical Mediterranean world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Through the descriptions of <a href="/wiki/Leo_Africanus" title="Leo Africanus">Leo Africanus</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a> &#8211; despite his visit to the river &#8211; the myth connecting the Niger to the Nile persisted.\n</p><p>While the true course of the Niger was presumably known to locals, it was a mystery to the outside world until the late 18th century. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" title="Ancient Romans">Ancient Romans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> (N.H. 5.10) thought that the river near <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a> was part of the Nile River, a belief also held by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a>, while early European explorers thought that it flowed west and joined the <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a>.\n</p><p>Many European expeditions to plot the river were unsuccessful. In 1788 the <a href="/wiki/African_Association" title="African Association">African Association</a> was formed in England to promote the exploration of Africa in the hopes of locating the Niger, and in June 1796 the Scottish explorer <a href="/wiki/Mungo_Park_(explorer)" title="Mungo Park (explorer)">Mungo Park</a> was the first European to lay eyes on the middle portion of the river since antiquity (and perhaps ever). The true course was established in his  book <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Travels_in_the_Interior_of_Africa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Travels in the Interior of Africa (page does not exist)">Travels in the Interior of Africa</a>,</i> which appeared in 1799.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  The African Association failed in assaults from the north (Tripoli), the east (Cairo), and the west (Gambia).  The membership now proposed that an effort be made from the south. The site chosen in 1804 from which to strike inland was a British <a href="/wiki/Trading_post" title="Trading post">trading post</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>.  Unbeknownst to him, the river mouth that emptied into the Gulf, whence Henry Nicholls was to set out in search of the Niger, was precisely the end of the Niger itself&#8212;only the Europeans did not know it yet. The starting point of the expedition was in fact its destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On October 24, 1946 three Frenchmen, Jean Sauvy, Pierre Ponty and movie maker <a href="/wiki/Jean_Rouch" title="Jean Rouch">Jean Rouch</a>, former civil servants in the African <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French colonies</a>, set out to travel the entire length of the river, as no one else seemed to have done previously. They travelled from the very beginning of the river near <a href="/wiki/Kissidougou" title="Kissidougou">Kissidougou</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">Guinea</a>, walking at first till a <a href="/wiki/Raft" title="Raft">raft</a> could be used, then changing to various local crafts as the river broadened and changed. Two of them reached the ocean on March 25, 1947, with Pierre Ponty having had to leave the expedition at <a href="/wiki/Niamey" title="Niamey">Niamey</a>, somewhat past the halfway mark. They carried a <a href="/wiki/16_mm_film" title="16 mm film">16mm movie camera</a>, the resulting footage giving Jean Rouch his first two ethnographic documentaries: "Au pays des mages noirs", and "La chasse &#224; l&#8217;hippopotame". A camera was used to illustrate Jean Rouch\'s subsequent book "Le Niger En Pirogue" (Fernand Nathan, 1954), as well as Jean Sauvy\'s &#8220;Descente du Niger&#8221; (L\'Harmattan 2001). A <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewriter</a> was brought as well, on which Pierre Ponty produced newspaper articles he mailed out whenever possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>More recently, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> adventurer Helge Hjelland made another journey through the entire length of the Niger River starting in Guinea-Bissau in 2005. The trip was filmed by the adventurer himself and made into a documentary titled "The Cruellest Journey".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_and_development">Management and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Management and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The water in the Niger River basin is partially regulated through dams. In Mali the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9lingu%C3%A9_Dam" title="S&#233;lingu&#233; Dam">S&#233;lingu&#233; Dam</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Sankarani_River" title="Sankarani River">Sankarani River</a> is mainly used for hydropower, but also permits irrigation. Two diversion dams, one at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sotuba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sotuba (page does not exist)">Sotuba</a> just downstream of <a href="/wiki/Bamako" title="Bamako">Bamako</a>, and one at <a href="/wiki/Markala" title="Markala">Markala</a>, just downstream of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9gou" title="S&#233;gou">S&#233;gou</a>, are used to irrigate about 54,000 hectares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-9">[9]</a></sup> In Nigeria the <a href="/wiki/Kainji_Dam" title="Kainji Dam">Kainji Dam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jebba_Hydroelectric_Power_Station" title="Jebba Hydroelectric Power Station">Jebba dam</a> are used to generate hydropower.\n</p><p>The water resources of the Niger River are under pressure due to increased water abstraction for irrigation and due to the impact of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The construction of dams for hydropower generation is underway or envisaged in order to alleviate chronic power shortages in the countries of the Niger basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> estimates the irrigation potential of all countries in the Niger river basin at 2.8 million hectares. Only 0.93m hectares (ha) were under irrigation in the late 1980s. The irrigation potential was estimated at 1.68m ha in Nigeria 0.56m ha in Mali, and the actual irrigated area was 0.67m ha and 0.19m ha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niger_Basin_Charta_and_Investment_Plan">Niger Basin Charta and Investment Plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Niger Basin Charta and Investment Plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In order to further coordinate their efforts, in April 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Riparian_zone" title="Riparian zone">riparian</a> countries which form the <a href="/wiki/Niger_Basin_Authority" title="Niger Basin Authority">Niger Basin Authority</a> adopted a Niger Basin Water Charta, a basin-wide 30-year investment plan and a 5-year priority investment plan. The Charta promotes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Water_Resources_Management" title="Integrated Water Resources Management">Integrated Water Resources Management</a>,  defines procedures for the examination and approval of new projects, provides a framework for the allocation of water resources between sectors, commits to maintain the integrity of aquatic ecosystems and defines mechanisms for the settlement of disputes between countries and for user participation. Investments include the expansion of irrigated agriculture to improve food security, the construction of the Taoussa (or Tossaye) dam in Mali and the <a href="/wiki/Kandadji_Dam" title="Kandadji Dam">Kandadji Dam</a> in Niger (the latter has been under construction since August 2008), as well as the rehabilitation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kainji_dam" title="Kainji dam">Kainji dam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jebba_Power_Station" title="Jebba Power Station">Jebba dam</a> in Nigeria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[a]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most of the investments are funded or are expected to be funded through aid. For example, the Kandadji Dam is financed by the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Development_Bank" title="Islamic Development Bank">Islamic Development Bank</a>, the <a href="/wiki/African_Development_Bank" title="African Development Bank">African Development Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/OPEC" title="OPEC">OPEC</a> Development Fund. The World Bank approved a US$500 million soft loan in July 2007 to finance projects in the basin over a 12-year period. Funding will be awarded in two phases. The initial $185 million credit will go to Nigeria, Guinea, Benin, Mali and Niger. The second, $315 million investment, is slated for Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Chad and Ivory Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Besides financing the rehabilitation of dams in Nigeria, the loan will also fund the "sustainable management of selected degraded ecosystems and rehabilitation of small water infrastructure" and capacity building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="River_transport_and_dredging">River transport and dredging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niger_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: River transport and dredging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In September 2009, the Nigerian government commenced a 36 billion <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_naira" title="Nigerian naira">naira</a> <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredging</a> of the Niger River from <a href="/wiki/Baro_(Nigeria)" title="Baro (Nigeria)">Baro</a> to <a href="/wiki/Warri" title="Warri">Warri</a>, a move which will see <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> removed from several hundred kilometres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge-28">[27]</a></sup> The dredging is intended to make it easier for goods to be transported to isolated settlements located deep within from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge-28">[27]</a></sup> Estimated to be completed within six to eight months, it had first been proposed and then postponed for 43 years previously by the then government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger-29">[28]</a></sup> Speaking in <a href="/wiki/Lokoja" title="Lokoja">Lokoja</a>, Nigerian <a href="/wiki/President_of_Nigeria" title="President of Nigeria">President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umaru_Yar%27Adua" title="Umaru Yar\'Adua">Umaru Yar\'Adua</a> stated that the project would lead to "all-year-round navigability" on the River Niger and that he hoped that, by 2020, Nigeria would have become one of the twenty most industrialised nations in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger-29">[28]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alhaji_Ibrahim_Bio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alhaji Ibrahim Bio (page does not exist)">Alhaji Ibrahim Bio</a>, the Nigerian Minister of Transport, said his ministry would work to make certain the project would be completed within its designated timeframe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Yar&#8217;Adua_Flags_off_Dredging_of_River_Niger-29">[28]</a></sup> Some activists have, however, opposed the project in the past, claiming it may have negative effects on waterside villagers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nigeria_begins_vast_river_dredge-28">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In late March 2010 the dredging project was 50% complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
