Solid lines denote rings; dashed lines denote orbits of moons.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>rings of Uranus</b> are a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">rings</a> around the planet <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, intermediate in complexity between the more extensive set around <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">Saturn</a> and the simpler systems around <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Neptune" title="Rings of Neptune">Neptune</a>. The rings of Uranus were discovered on March 10, 1977, by <a href="/wiki/James_L._Elliot" title="James L. Elliot">James L. Elliot</a>, Edward W. Dunham, and <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Mink" title="Jessica Mink">Jessica Mink</a>.  <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> had also reported observing rings in 1789; modern astronomers are divided on whether he could have seen them, as they are very dark and faint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCN-Rincon_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCN-Rincon-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1978, nine distinct rings were identified. Two additional rings were discovered in 1986 in images taken by the <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> spacecraft, and two outer rings were found in 2003&#8211;2005 in <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> photos. In the order of increasing distance from the planet the 13 known rings are designated 1986U2R/<a href="/wiki/Zeta" title="Zeta">&#950;</a>, 6, 5, 4, <a href="/wiki/Alpha" title="Alpha">&#945;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beta" title="Beta">&#946;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eta" title="Eta">&#951;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gamma" title="Gamma">&#947;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_(letter)" title="Delta (letter)">&#948;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">&#955;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epsilon" title="Epsilon">&#949;</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nu_(letter)" title="Nu (letter)">&#957;</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mu_(letter)" title="Mu (letter)">&#956;</a>. Their radii range from about 38,000&#160;km for the 1986U2R/&#950; ring to about 98,000&#160;km for the &#956; ring. Additional faint dust bands and incomplete arcs may exist between the main rings. The rings are extremely dark&#8212;the <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">Bond albedo</a> of the rings\' particles does not exceed 2%. They are probably composed of water ice with the addition of some dark radiation-processed <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organics</a>.\n</p><p>The majority of Uranus\'s rings are opaque and only a few kilometers wide. The ring system contains little dust overall; it consists mostly of large bodies 0.2&#8211;20&#160;m in diameter. Some rings are optically thin: the broad and faint 1986U2R/&#950;, &#956; and &#957; rings are made of small dust particles, while the narrow and faint &#955; ring also contains larger bodies. The relative lack of dust in the ring system may be due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a> from the extended Uranian <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a>.\n</p><p>The rings of Uranus are thought to be relatively young, and not more than 600&#160;million years old. The Uranian ring system probably originated from the collisional fragmentation of several moons that once existed around the planet. After colliding, the moons probably broke up into many particles, which survived as narrow and optically dense rings only in strictly confined zones of maximum stability.\n</p><p>The mechanism that confines the narrow rings is not well understood. Initially it was assumed that every narrow ring had a pair of nearby <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_moon" title="Shepherd moon">shepherd moons</a> corralling them into shape. In 1986 <i>Voyager 2</i> discovered only one such shepherd pair (<a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(moon)" title="Cordelia (moon)">Cordelia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ophelia_(moon)" title="Ophelia (moon)">Ophelia</a>) around the brightest ring (&#949;).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first mention of a Uranian ring system comes from William Herschel\'s notes detailing his observations of Uranus in the 18th century, which include the following passage: "February 22, 1789: A ring was suspected".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCN-Rincon_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCN-Rincon-1">[1]</a></sup> Herschel drew a small diagram of the ring and noted that it was "a little inclined to the red". The <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">Keck Telescope</a> in Hawaii has since confirmed this to be the case, at least for the &#957; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Herschel\'s notes were published in a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> journal in 1797. In the two centuries between 1797 and 1977 the rings are rarely mentioned, if at all. This casts serious doubt on whether Herschel could have seen anything of the sort while hundreds of other astronomers saw nothing. It has been claimed that Herschel gave accurate descriptions of the &#949; ring\'s size relative to Uranus, its changes as Uranus travelled around the Sun, and its color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The definitive discovery of the Uranian rings was made by astronomers <a href="/wiki/James_L._Elliot" title="James L. Elliot">James L. Elliot</a>, Edward W. Dunham, and <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Mink" title="Jessica Mink">Jessica Mink</a> on March 10, 1977, using the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_Airborne_Observatory" title="Kuiper Airborne Observatory">Kuiper Airborne Observatory</a>, and was <a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">serendipitous</a>. They planned to use the <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultation</a> of the star SAO 158687 by Uranus to study the planet\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. When their observations were analyzed, they found that the star disappeared briefly from view five times both before and after it was eclipsed by the planet. They deduced that a system of narrow rings was present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliot1977_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot1977-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliot1977b_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot1977b-5">[5]</a></sup> The five occultation events they observed were denoted by the Greek letters &#945;, &#946;, &#947;, &#948; and &#949; in their papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliot1977_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot1977-4">[4]</a></sup> These designations have been used as the rings\' names since then. Later they found four additional rings: one between the &#946; and &#947; rings and three inside the &#945; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson1978_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicholson1978-6">[6]</a></sup> The former was named the &#951; ring. The latter were dubbed rings 4, 5 and 6&#8212;according to the numbering of the occultation events in one paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millis1978_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millis1978-7">[7]</a></sup> Uranus\'s ring system was the second to be discovered in the Solar System, after that of <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rings were directly imaged when the <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> spacecraft flew through the Uranian system in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Two more faint rings were revealed, bringing the total to eleven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> detected an additional pair of previously unseen rings in 2003&#8211;2005, bringing the total number known to 13. The discovery of these outer rings doubled the known radius of the ring system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> Hubble also imaged two small satellites for the first time, one of which, <a href="/wiki/Mab_(moon)" title="Mab (moon)">Mab</a>, shares its orbit with the outermost newly discovered &#956; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2005_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2005-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_properties">General properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="754" decoding="async" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg/220px-Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg/330px-Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg/440px-Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranian_rings_PIA01977.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Uranus\'s inner rings. The bright outer ring is the epsilon ring; eight other rings are visible</div></div></div>\n<p>As currently understood, the ring system of Uranus comprises thirteen distinct rings. In order of increasing distance from the planet they are: 1986U2R/&#950;, 6, 5, 4, &#945;, &#946;, &#951;, &#947;, &#948;, &#955;, &#949;, &#957;, &#956; rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> They can be divided into three groups: nine narrow main rings (6, 5, 4, &#945;, &#946;, &#951;, &#947;, &#948;, &#949;),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> two dusty rings (1986U2R/&#950;, &#955;)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> and two outer rings (&#957;, &#956;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2008b_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2008b-13">[13]</a></sup> The rings of Uranus   consist mainly of macroscopic particles and  little <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> although dust is known to be present in 1986U2R/&#950;, &#951;, &#948;, &#955;, &#957; and &#956; rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition to these well-known rings, there may be numerous optically thin dust bands and faint rings between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> These faint rings and dust bands may exist only temporarily or consist of a number of separate arcs, which are sometimes detected during <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> Some of them became visible during a series of ring plane-crossing events in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> A number of dust bands between the rings were observed in forward-scattering<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[a]</a></sup> geometry by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup>  All rings of Uranus show <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuthal</a> brightness variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rings are made of an extremely dark material. The <a href="/wiki/Geometric_albedo" title="Geometric albedo">geometric albedo</a> of the ring particles does not exceed 5&#8211;6%, while the <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">Bond albedo</a> is even lower&#8212;about 2%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka1997_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka1997-18">[17]</a></sup> The rings particles demonstrate a steep opposition surge&#8212;an increase of the albedo when the <a href="/wiki/Phase_angle" title="Phase angle">phase angle</a> is close to zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> This means that their albedo is much lower when they are observed slightly off the opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[b]</a></sup> The rings are slightly red in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> and visible parts of the <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> and grey in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-infrared" title="Near-infrared">near-infrared</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baines1998_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baines1998-20">[18]</a></sup> They exhibit no identifiable <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectral features</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a> of the ring particles is not known. They cannot be made of pure water ice like the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">rings of Saturn</a> because they are too dark, darker than the <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">inner moons of Uranus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baines1998_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baines1998-20">[18]</a></sup> This indicates that they are probably composed of a mixture of the ice and a dark material. The nature of this material is not clear, but it may be <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> considerably darkened by the <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particle</a> irradiation from the Uranian <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. The rings\' particles may consist of a heavily processed material which was initially similar to that of the inner moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baines1998_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baines1998-20">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a whole, the ring system of Uranus is unlike either the faint dusty <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Jupiter" title="Rings of Jupiter">rings of Jupiter</a> or the broad and complex <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">rings of Saturn</a>, some of which are composed of very bright material&#8212;water ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> There are similarities with some parts of the latter ring system; the Saturnian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F_ring" title="F ring">F ring</a> and the Uranian &#949; ring are both narrow, relatively dark and are shepherded by a pair of moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> The newly discovered outer &#957; and &#956; rings of Uranus are similar to the outer G and E rings of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006b_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006b-21">[19]</a></sup> Narrow ringlets existing in the broad Saturnian rings also resemble the narrow rings of Uranus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> In addition, dust bands observed between the main rings of Uranus may be similar to the rings of Jupiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> In contrast, the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Neptune" title="Rings of Neptune">Neptunian ring</a> system is quite similar to that of Uranus, although it is less complex, darker and contains more dust; the Neptunian rings are also positioned further from the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrow_main_rings">Narrow main rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Narrow main rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id=".CE.B5_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#949;_ring">&#949; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: &#949; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="528" data-file-width="878" decoding="async" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg/220px-Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg/330px-Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg/440px-Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epsilon_ring_of_Uranus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A close-up view of the &#949; ring of Uranus</div></div></div>\n<p>The &#949; ring is the brightest and densest part of the Uranian ring system, and is responsible for about two-thirds of the light reflected by the rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baines1998_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baines1998-20">[18]</a></sup>  While it is the most <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentric</a> of the Uranian rings, it has negligible <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">orbital inclination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> The ring\'s eccentricity causes its brightness to vary over the course of its orbit. The radially integrated brightness of the &#949; ring is highest near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">apoapsis</a> and lowest near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The maximum/minimum brightness ratio is about 2.5&#8211;3.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> These variations are connected with the variations of the ring width, which is 19.7&#160;km at the periapsis and 96.4&#160;km at the apoapsis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> As the ring becomes wider, the amount of shadowing between particles decreases and more of them come into view, leading to higher integrated brightness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka1997_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka1997-18">[17]</a></sup> The width variations were measured directly from <i>Voyager 2</i> images, as the &#949; ring was one of only two rings resolved by Voyager&#8217;s cameras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Such behavior indicates that the ring is not optically thin. Indeed, occultation observations conducted from the ground and the spacecraft showed that its normal <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical depth</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-footnoteC_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-footnoteC-24">[c]</a></sup> varies between 0.5 and 2.5,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1986Tyler_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-1986Tyler-25">[22]</a></sup> being highest near the periapsis. The equivalent depth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-footnoteD_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-footnoteD-26">[d]</a></sup> of the &#949; ring is around 47&#160;km and is invariant around the orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rings_of_Uranus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="996" data-file-width="996" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Rings_of_Uranus.jpg/250px-Rings_of_Uranus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Rings_of_Uranus.jpg/375px-Rings_of_Uranus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Rings_of_Uranus.jpg/500px-Rings_of_Uranus.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rings_of_Uranus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A close-up view of the (from top to bottom) &#948;, &#947;, &#951;, &#946; and &#945; rings of Uranus. The resolved &#951; ring demonstrates the optically thin broad component.</div></div></div>\n<p>The geometric thickness of the &#949; ring is not precisely known, although the ring is certainly very thin&#8212;by some estimates as thin as 150&#160;m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> Despite such infinitesimal thickness, it consists of several layers of particles. The &#949; ring is a rather crowded place with a <a href="/wiki/Filling_factor" title="Filling factor">filling factor</a> near the apoapsis estimated by different sources at from 0.008 to 0.06.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The mean size of the ring particles is 0.2&#8211;20.0&#160;m,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> and the mean separation is around 4.5 times their radius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The ring is almost devoid of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a>, possibly due to the aerodynamic drag from Uranus\'s extended atmospheric corona.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to its razor-thin nature the &#949; ring is invisible when viewed edge-on. This happened in 2007 when a ring plane-crossing was observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Voyager 2</i> spacecraft observed a strange signal from the &#949; ring during the <a href="/wiki/Radio_occultation" title="Radio occultation">radio occultation</a> experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1986Tyler_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-1986Tyler-25">[22]</a></sup> The signal looked like a strong enhancement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_scattering" title="Forward scattering">forward-scattering</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> 3.6&#160;cm near ring&#8217;s apoapsis. Such strong scattering requires the existence of a coherent structure. That the &#949; ring does have such a fine structure has been confirmed by many occultation observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> The &#949; ring seems to consist of a number of narrow and optically dense ringlets, some of which may have incomplete arcs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The &#949; ring is known to have interior and exterior <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_moon" title="Shepherd moon">shepherd moons</a>&#8212;<a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(moon)" title="Cordelia (moon)">Cordelia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ophelia_(moon)" title="Ophelia (moon)">Ophelia</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> The inner edge of the ring is in 24:25 resonance with Cordelia, and the outer edge is in 14:13 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">resonance</a> with Ophelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> The masses of the moons need to be at least three times the mass of the ring to confine it effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> The mass of the &#949; ring is estimated to be about 10<sup>16</sup>&#160;kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".CE.B4_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#948;_ring">&#948; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: &#948; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1517" data-file-width="801" decoding="async" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png/170px-Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png/255px-Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png/340px-Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forward_Back_Uranus_Rings.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of the Uranian rings in <a href="/wiki/Forward_scatter" title="Forward scatter">forward-scattered</a> and <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">back-scattered</a> light (images obtained by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> in 1986)</div></div></div>\n<p>The &#948; ring is circular and slightly inclined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> It shows significant unexplained azimuthal variations in normal optical depth and width.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> One possible explanation is that the ring has an azimuthal wave-like structure, excited by a small moonlet just inside it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horn1988_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horn1988-28">[24]</a></sup> The sharp outer edge of the &#948; ring is in 23:22 resonance with Cordelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco1987_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Porco1987-29">[25]</a></sup> The &#948; ring consists of two components: a narrow optically dense component and a broad inward shoulder with low optical depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> The width of the narrow component is 4.1&#8211;6.1&#160;km and the equivalent depth is about 2.2&#160;km, which corresponds to a normal optical depth of about 0.3&#8211;0.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The ring\'s broad component is about 10&#8211;12&#160;km wide and its equivalent depth is close to 0.3&#160;km, indicating a low normal optical depth of 3&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>&#8722;2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-7"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holberg1987_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holberg1987-30">[26]</a></sup> This is known only from occultation data because <i>Voyager 2\'s</i> imaging experiment failed to resolve the &#948; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holberg1987_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holberg1987-30">[26]</a></sup> When observed in forward-scattering geometry by <i>Voyager 2</i>, the &#948; ring appeared relatively bright, which is compatible with the presence of dust in its broad component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The broad component is geometrically thicker than the narrow component. This is supported by the observations of a ring plane-crossing event in 2007, when the &#948; ring remained visible, which is consistent with the behavior of a simultaneously geometrically thick and optically thin ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".CE.B3_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#947;_ring">&#947; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: &#947; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The &#947; ring is narrow, optically dense and slightly eccentric. Its orbital inclination is almost zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> The width of the ring varies in the range 3.6&#8211;4.7&#160;km, although equivalent optical depth is constant at 3.3&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-8"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The normal optical depth of the &#947; ring is 0.7&#8211;0.9. During a ring plane-crossing event in 2007 the &#947; ring disappeared, which means it is geometrically thin like the &#949; ring<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-8"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> and devoid of dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> The width and normal optical depth of the &#947; ring show significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azimuthal" title="Azimuthal">azimuthal</a> variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-9"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> The mechanism of confinement of such a narrow ring is not known, but it has been noticed that the sharp inner edge of the &#947; ring is in a 6:5 resonance with Ophelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco1987_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Porco1987-29">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French1988_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-French1988-31">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".CE.B7_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#951;_ring">&#951; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &#951; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The &#951; ring has zero orbital eccentricity and inclination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> Like the &#948; ring, it consists of two components: a narrow optically dense component and a broad outward shoulder with low optical depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The width of the narrow component is 1.9&#8211;2.7&#160;km and the equivalent depth is about 0.42&#160;km, which corresponds to the normal optical depth of about 0.16&#8211;0.25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-9"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The broad component is about 40&#160;km wide and its equivalent depth is close to 0.85&#160;km, indicating a low normal optical depth of 2&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>&#8722;2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-10"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> It was resolved in <i>Voyager 2</i> images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> In forward-scattered light, the &#951; ring looked bright, which indicated the presence of a considerable amount of dust in this ring, probably in the broad component.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The broad component is much thicker (geometrically) than the narrow one. This conclusion is supported by the observations of a ring plane-crossing event in 2007, when the &#951; ring demonstrated increased brightness, becoming the second brightest feature in the ring system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> This is consistent with the behavior of a geometrically thick but simultaneously optically thin ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> Like the majority of other rings, the &#951; ring shows significant azimuthal variations in the normal optical depth and width. The narrow component even vanishes in some places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-10"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id=".CE.B1_and_.CE.B2_rings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#945;_and_&#946;_rings">&#945; and &#946; rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: &#945; and &#946; rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After the &#949; ring, the &#945; and &#946; rings are the brightest of Uranus\'s rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> Like the &#949; ring, they exhibit regular variations in brightness and width.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> They are brightest and widest 30&#176; from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">apoapsis</a> and dimmest and narrowest 30&#176; from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibbard2005_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbard2005-32">[28]</a></sup> The &#945; and &#946; rings have sizable orbital eccentricity and non-negligible inclination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> The widths of these rings are 4.8&#8211;10&#160;km and 6.1&#8211;11.4&#160;km, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-11"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> The equivalent optical depths are 3.29&#160;km and 2.14&#160;km, resulting in normal optical depths of 0.3&#8211;0.7 and 0.2&#8211;0.35, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-12"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> During a ring plane-crossing event in 2007 the rings disappeared, which means they are geometrically thin like the &#949; ring and devoid of dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> The same event revealed a thick and optically thin dust band just outside the &#946; ring, which was also observed earlier by <i>Voyager 2</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The masses of the &#945; and &#946; rings are estimated to be about 5&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>15</sup>&#160;kg (each)&#8212;half the mass of the &#949; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chiang2003_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chiang2003-33">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Rings_6.2C_5_and_4"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rings_6,_5_and_4">Rings 6, 5 and 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rings 6, 5 and 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rings 6, 5 and 4 are the innermost and dimmest of Uranus\'s narrow rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> They are the most inclined rings, and their orbital eccentricities are the largest excluding the &#949; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> In fact, their inclinations (0.06&#176;, 0.05&#176; and 0.03&#176;) were large enough for <i>Voyager 2</i> to observe their elevations above the Uranian equatorial plane, which were 24&#8211;46&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Rings 6, 5 and 4 are also the narrowest rings of Uranus, measuring 1.6&#8211;2.2&#160;km, 1.9&#8211;4.9&#160;km and 2.4&#8211;4.4&#160;km wide, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-14"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-13"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> Their equivalent depths are 0.41&#160;km, 0.91 and 0.71&#160;km resulting in normal optical depth 0.18&#8211;0.25, 0.18&#8211;0.48 and 0.16&#8211;0.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoshka2001b_23-14"><a href="#cite_note-Karkoshka2001b-23">[21]</a></sup> They  were not visible during a ring plane-crossing event in 2007 due to their narrowness and lack of dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dusty_rings">Dusty rings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dusty rings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id=".CE.BB_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#955;_ring">&#955; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: &#955; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="638" data-file-width="720" decoding="async" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png/220px-FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png/330px-FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png/440px-FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FDS_26852.19_Rings_of_Uranus.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A long-exposure, high <a href="/wiki/Phase_angle_(astronomy)" title="Phase angle (astronomy)">phase angle</a> (172.5&#176;)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> image of Uranus\'s inner rings. In <a href="/wiki/Forward_scatter" title="Forward scatter">forward-scattered</a> light, dust bands not visible in other images can be seen, as well as the recognized rings.</div></div></div>\n<p>The &#955; ring was one of two rings discovered by <i>Voyager 2</i> in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> It is a narrow, faint ring located just inside the &#949; ring, between it and the shepherd moon <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(moon)" title="Cordelia (moon)">Cordelia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-15"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> This moon clears a dark lane just inside the &#955; ring. When viewed in <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">back-scattered</a> light,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[e]</a></sup> the &#955; ring is extremely narrow&#8212;about 1&#8211;2&#160;km&#8212;and has the equivalent optical depth 0.1&#8211;0.2&#160;km at the wavelength 2.2&#160;&#956;m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> The normal optical depth is 0.1&#8211;0.2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-16"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holberg1987_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holberg1987-30">[26]</a></sup> The optical depth of the &#955; ring shows strong wavelength dependence, which is atypical for the Uranian ring system. The equivalent depth is as high as 0.36&#160;km in the ultraviolet part of the spectrum, which explains why &#955; ring was initially detected only in UV stellar <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a> by <i>Voyager 2</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holberg1987_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Holberg1987-30">[26]</a></sup> The detection during a stellar occultation at the wavelength 2.2&#160;&#956;m was only announced in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The appearance of the &#955; ring changed dramatically when it was observed in forward-scattered light in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-17"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> In this geometry the ring became the brightest feature of the Uranian ring system, outshining the &#949; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> This observation, together with the wavelength dependence of the optical depth, indicates that the &#955; ring contains significant amount of <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometer</a>-sized dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> The normal optical depth of this dust is 10<sup>&#8722;4</sup>&#8211;10<sup>&#8722;3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> Observations in 2007 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescopes" title="Keck telescopes">Keck telescope</a> during the ring plane-crossing event confirmed this conclusion, because the &#955; ring became one of the brightest features in the Uranian ring system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Detailed analysis of the <i>Voyager 2</i> images revealed azimuthal variations in the brightness of the &#955; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> The variations appear to be periodic, resembling a <a href="/wiki/Standing_wave" title="Standing wave">standing wave</a>. The origin of this fine structure in the &#955; ring remains a mystery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1986U2R.2F.CE.B6_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1986U2R/&#950;_ring">1986U2R/&#950; ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1986U2R/&#950; ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="810" data-file-width="1234" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg/220px-Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg/330px-Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg/440px-Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranus%27_rings_dim.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Discovery_image" title="Discovery image">discovery image</a> of the 1986U2R ring</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1986 <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> detected a broad and faint sheet of material inward of ring 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-18"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> This ring was given the temporary designation 1986U2R. It had a normal optical depth of 10<sup>&#8722;3</sup> or less and was extremely faint. It was visible only in a single <i>Voyager 2</i> image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-19"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The ring was located between 37,000 and 39,500&#160;km from the centre of Uranus, or only about 12,000&#160;km above the clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> It was not observed again until 2003&#8211;2004, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keck_telescopes" title="Keck telescopes">Keck telescope</a> found a broad and faint sheet of material just inside ring 6. This ring was dubbed the &#950; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> The position of the recovered &#950; ring differs significantly from that observed in 1986. Now it is situated between 37,850 and 41,350&#160;km from the centre of the planet. There is an inward gradually fading extension reaching to at least 32,600&#160;km,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> or possibly even to 27,000&#160;km&#8212;to the atmosphere of Uranus. These extensions are labelled as the &#950;<sub>c</sub> and &#950;<sub>cc</sub> rings respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn2010_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn2010-35">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The &#950; ring was observed again during the ring plane-crossing event in 2007 when it became the brightest feature of the ring system, outshining all other rings combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-9"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> The equivalent optical depth of this ring is near 1&#160;km (0.6&#160;km for the inward extension), while the normal optical depth is again less than 10<sup>&#8722;3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup> Rather different appearances of the 1986U2R and &#950; rings may be caused by different viewing geometries: back-scattering geometry in 2003&#8211;2007 and side-scattering geometry in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-10"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> Changes during the past 20 years in the distribution of dust, which is thought to predominate in the ring, cannot be ruled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-11"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_dust_bands">Other dust bands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other dust bands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In addition to the 1986U2R/&#950; and &#955; rings, there are other extremely faint dust bands in the Uranian ring system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-20"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> They are invisible during occultations because they have negligible optical depth, though they are bright in forward-scattered light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Voyager 2\'</i>s images of forward-scattered light revealed the existence of bright dust bands between the &#955; and &#948; rings, between the &#951; and &#946; rings, and between the &#945; ring and ring 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-21"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Many of these bands were detected again in 2003&#8211;2004 by the <a href="/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory" title="W. M. Keck Observatory">Keck Telescope</a> and during the 2007 ring-plane crossing event in backscattered light, but their precise locations and relative brightnesses were different from during the <i>Voyager</i> observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2007_16-12"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2007-16">[16]</a></sup> The normal optical depth of the dust bands is about 10<sup>&#8722;5</sup> or less. The dust particle size distribution is thought to obey a <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">power law</a> with the index <i>p</i>&#160;=&#160;2.5&#160;&#177;&#160;0.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-11"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to separate dust bands the system of Uranian rings appears to be immersed into wide and faint sheet of dust with the normal optical depth not exceeding  10<sup>&#8722;3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn2010_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn2010-35">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_ring_system">Outer ring system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Outer ring system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="736" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg/220px-Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg/330px-Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg/440px-Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Outer_Uranian_rings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The &#956; and &#957; rings of Uranus (R/2003&#160;U1&#160;and&#160;U2) in <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> images from 2005</div></div></div>\n<p>In 2003&#8211;2005, the Hubble Space Telescope detected a pair of previously unknown rings, now called the outer ring system, which brought the number of known Uranian rings to 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> These rings were subsequently named the &#956; and &#957; rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2008b_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2008b-13">[13]</a></sup> The &#956; ring is the outermost of the pair, and is twice the distance from the planet as the bright &#951; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> The outer rings differ from the inner narrow rings in a number of respects. They are broad, 17,000&#160;and 3,800&#160;km wide, respectively, and very faint. Their peak normal optical depths are 8.5&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>&#8722;6</sup> and 5.4&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>&#8722;6</sup>, respectively. The resulting equivalent optical depths are 0.14&#160;km and 0.012&#160;km. The rings have triangular radial brightness profiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The peak brightness of the &#956; ring lies almost exactly on the orbit of the small Uranian moon <a href="/wiki/Mab_(moon)" title="Mab (moon)">Mab</a>, which is probably the source of the ring&#8217;s particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2005_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2005-11">[11]</a></sup> The &#957; ring is positioned between <a href="/wiki/Portia_(moon)" title="Portia (moon)">Portia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_(moon)" title="Rosalind (moon)">Rosalind</a> and does not contain any moons inside it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> A reanalysis of the <i>Voyager 2</i> images of forward-scattered light clearly reveals the &#956; and &#957; rings. In this geometry the rings are much brighter, which indicates that they contain much micrometer-sized dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup> The outer rings of Uranus may be similar to the <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#G_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">G and E rings of Saturn</a> as E ring is extremely broad and receives dust from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">Enceladus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter2006_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-Showalter2006-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2005_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA2005-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The &#956; ring may consist entirely of dust, without any large particles at all. This hypothesis is supported by observations performed by the Keck telescope, which failed to detect  the &#956; ring in the near infrared at 2.2&#160;&#956;m, but detected the &#957; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006b_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006b-21">[19]</a></sup> This failure means that the &#956; ring is blue in color, which in turn indicates that very small (submicrometer) dust predominates within it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006b_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006b-21">[19]</a></sup> The dust may be made of water ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[31]</a></sup> In contrast, the &#957; ring is slightly red in color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dePater2006b_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-dePater2006b-21">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics_and_origin">Dynamics and origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Dynamics and origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranus_rings.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="399" decoding="async" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Uranus_rings.png/170px-Uranus_rings.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Uranus_rings.png/255px-Uranus_rings.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Uranus_rings.png/340px-Uranus_rings.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranus_rings.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An enhanced-color schematic of the inner rings derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> images</div></div></div>\n<p>An outstanding problem concerning the physics governing the narrow Uranian rings is their confinement. Without some mechanism to hold their particles together, the rings would quickly spread out radially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> The lifetime of the Uranian rings without such a mechanism cannot be more than 1&#160;million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> The most widely cited model for such confinement, proposed initially by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Goldreich" title="Peter Goldreich">Goldreich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Tremaine" title="Scott Tremaine">Tremaine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[33]</a></sup> is that a pair of nearby moons, outer and inner shepherds, interact gravitationally with a ring and act like sinks and donors, respectively, for excessive and insufficient angular momentum (or equivalently, energy). The shepherds thus keep ring particles in place, but gradually move away from the ring themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> To be effective, the masses of the shepherds should exceed the mass of the ring by at least a factor of two to three. This mechanism is known to be at work in the case of the &#949; ring, where <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(moon)" title="Cordelia (moon)">Cordelia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ophelia_(moon)" title="Ophelia (moon)">Ophelia</a> serve as shepherds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco1987_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Porco1987-29">[25]</a></sup> Cordelia is also the outer shepherd of the &#948; ring, and Ophelia is the outer shepherd of the &#947; ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porco1987_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Porco1987-29">[25]</a></sup> No moon larger than 10&#160;km is known in the vicinity of other rings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-22"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The current distance of Cordelia and Ophelia from the &#949; ring can be used to estimate the ring&#8217;s age. The calculations show that the &#949; ring cannot be older than 600 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the rings of Uranus appear to be young, they must be continuously renewed by the collisional fragmentation of larger bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> The estimates show that the lifetime against collisional disruption of a moon with the size like that of <a href="/wiki/Puck_(moon)" title="Puck (moon)">Puck</a> is a few billion years. The lifetime of a smaller satellite is much shorter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> Therefore, all current inner moons and rings can be products of disruption of several Puck-sized satellites during the last four and half billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> Every such disruption would have started a collisional cascade that quickly ground almost all large bodies into much smaller particles, including dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito2002_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito2002-8">[8]</a></sup> Eventually the majority of mass was lost, and particles survived only in positions that were stabilized by mutual resonances and shepherding. The end product of such a disruptive evolution would be a system of narrow rings. A few <a href="/wiki/Moonlet" title="Moonlet">moonlets</a> must still be embedded within the rings at present. The maximum size of such moonlets is probably around 10&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The origin of the <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> bands is less problematic. The dust has a very short lifetime, 100&#8211;1000 years, and should be continuously replenished by collisions between larger ring particles, moonlets and <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a> from outside the Uranian system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2001_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2001-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> The belts of the parent moonlets and particles are themselves invisible due to their low optical depth, while the dust reveals itself in forward-scattered light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-7"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> The narrow main rings and the moonlet belts that create dust bands are expected to differ in particle size distribution. The main rings have more centimeter to meter-sized bodies. Such a distribution increases the surface area of the material in the rings, leading to high optical density in back-scattered light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-8"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup> In contrast, the dust bands have relatively few large particles, which results in low optical depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esposito1989_27-9"><a href="#cite_note-Esposito1989-27">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The rings were thoroughly investigated by the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> spacecraft in January 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone1986_22-7"><a href="#cite_note-Stone1986-22">[20]</a></sup> Two new faint rings&#8212;&#955; and 1986U2R&#8212;were discovered bringing the total number then known to eleven. Rings were studied by analyzing results of radio,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1986Tyler_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-1986Tyler-25">[22]</a></sup> ultraviolet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holberg1987_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Holberg1987-30">[26]</a></sup> and optical occultations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lane1986_15-11"><a href="#cite_note-Lane1986-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Voyager 2</i> observed the rings in different geometries relative to the sun, producing images with back-scattered, forward-scattered and side-scattered light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-23"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Analysis of these images allowed derivation of the complete phase function, geometrical and Bond albedo of ring particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ockert1987_14-12"><a href="#cite_note-Ockert1987-14">[14]</a></sup> Two rings&#8212;&#949; and &#951;&#8212;were resolved in the images revealing a complicated fine structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-24"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup> Analysis of Voyager\'s images also led to discovery of eleven inner <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">moons of Uranus</a>, including the two shepherd moons of the &#949; ring&#8212;Cordelia and Ophelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_9-25"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_properties">List of properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rings_of_Uranus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: List of properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This table summarizes the properties of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">planetary ring</a> system of <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable sortable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th><b>Ring name</b></th>\n<th data-sort-type="number"><b>Radius (km)</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-footnoteF_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-footnoteF-39">[f]</a></sup></th>\n<th data-sort-type="number"><b>Width (km)</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-footnoteF_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-footnoteF-39">[f]</a></sup></th>\n<th data-sort-type="number"><b>Eq. depth (km)</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-footnoteD_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-footnoteD-26">[d]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[g]</a></sup></th>\n<th data-sort-type="number"><b>N. style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span
