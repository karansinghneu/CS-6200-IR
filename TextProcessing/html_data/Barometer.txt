navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a broader coverage of this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement" title="Pressure measurement">Pressure measurement</a>.</div>\n<p>A <b>barometer</b> is a scientific instrument used to measure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pressure" title="Air pressure">air pressure</a>. Pressure tendency can forecast short term changes in the weather. Many measurements of air pressure are used within <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface weather analysis</a> to help find surface <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">troughs</a>, high pressure systems and frontal boundaries.\n</p><p>Barometers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_altimeter" title="Pressure altimeter">pressure altimeters</a> (the most basic and common type of altimeter) are essentially the same instrument, but used for different purposes. An altimeter is intended to be used at different levels matching the corresponding atmospheric pressure to the <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>, while a barometer is kept at the same level and measures subtle pressure changes caused by weather.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/barometer" title="wikt:barometer">barometer</a></i> is derived from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AC%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:&#946;&#940;&#961;&#959;&#962;">&#946;&#940;&#961;&#959;&#962;</a></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#160;</small>\'weight\', and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-meter" title="wikt:-meter">-meter</a></i> from Ancient Greek: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD" title="wikt:&#956;&#941;&#964;&#961;&#959;&#957;">&#956;&#941;&#964;&#961;&#959;&#957;</a> ("measure").\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli" title="Evangelista Torricelli">Evangelista Torricelli</a> is universally credited with inventing the barometer in 1643,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.barometerfair.com/history_of_the_barometer.htm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.barometerfair.com/history_of_the_barometer.htm-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.juliantrubin.com/bigten/torricellibarometer.html_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.juliantrubin.com/bigten/torricellibarometer.html-3">[3]</a></sup> historical documentation also suggests <a href="/wiki/Gasparo_Berti" title="Gasparo Berti">Gasparo Berti</a>, an Italian mathematician and astronomer, unintentionally built a water barometer sometime between 1640 and 1643.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> French scientist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren&#233; Descartes">Ren&#233; Descartes</a> described the design of an experiment to determine atmospheric pressure as early as 1631, but there is no evidence that he built a working barometer at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On July 27, 1630, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Baliani" title="Giovanni Battista Baliani">Giovanni Battista Baliani</a> wrote a letter to <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> explaining an experiment he had made in which a <a href="/wiki/Siphon" title="Siphon">siphon</a>, led over a hill about twenty-one meters high, failed to work. Galileo responded with an explanation of the phenomenon: he proposed that it was the power of a vacuum that held the water up, and at a certain height  the amount of water simply became too much and the force could not hold any more, like a cord that can support only so much weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shea2003_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shea2003-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup> This was a restatement of the theory of <i><a href="/wiki/Horror_vacui_(physics)" title="Horror vacui (physics)">horror vacui</a></i> ("nature abhors a vacuum"), which dates to <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, and which Galileo restated as <i>resistenza del vacuo</i>.\n</p><p>Galileo\'s ideas reached Rome in December 1638 in his <i>Discorsi</i>. Raffaele Magiotti and <a href="/wiki/Gasparo_Berti" title="Gasparo Berti">Gasparo Berti</a> were excited by these ideas, and decided to seek a better way to attempt to produce a vacuum other than with a siphon. Magiotti devised such an experiment, and sometime between 1639 and 1641, Berti (with Magiotti, <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">Athanasius Kircher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Zucchi" title="Niccol&#242; Zucchi">Niccol&#242; Zucchi</a> present) carried it out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Four accounts of Berti\'s experiment exist, but a simple model of his experiment consisted of filling with water a long tube that had both ends plugged, then standing the tube in a basin already full of water. The bottom end of the tube was opened, and water that had been inside of it poured out into the basin. However, only part of the water in the tube flowed out, and the level of the water inside the tube stayed at an exact level, which happened to be 10.3&#160;m (34&#160;ft),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the same height Baliani and Galileo had observed that was limited by the siphon. What was most important about this experiment was that the lowering water had left a space above it in the tube which had no intermediate contact with air to fill it up. This seemed to suggest the possibility of a vacuum existing in the space above the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Torricelli, a friend and student of Galileo, interpreted the results of the experiments in a novel way. He proposed that the weight of the atmosphere, not an attracting force of the vacuum, held the water in the tube. In a letter to <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Ricci" title="Michelangelo Ricci">Michelangelo Ricci</a> in 1644 concerning the experiments, he wrote:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Many have said that a vacuum does not exist, others that it does exist in spite of the repugnance of nature and with difficulty; I know of no one who has said that it exists without difficulty and without a resistance from nature. I argued thus: If there can be found a manifest cause from which the resistance can be derived which is felt if we try to make a vacuum, it seems to me foolish to try to attribute to vacuum those operations which follow evidently from some other cause; and so by making some very easy calculations, I found that the cause assigned by me (that is, the weight of the atmosphere) ought by itself alone to offer a greater resistance than it does when we try to produce a vacuum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>It was traditionally thought (especially by the Aristotelians) that the air did not have weight: that is, that the kilometers of air above the surface did not exert any weight on the bodies below it. Even Galileo had accepted the weightlessness of air as a simple truth. Torricelli questioned that assumption, and instead proposed that air had weight and that it was the latter (not the attracting force of the vacuum) which held (or rather, pushed) up the column of water. He thought that the level the water stayed at (c. 10.3&#160;m) was reflective of the force of the air\'s weight pushing on it (specifically, pushing on the water in the basin and thus limiting how much water can fall from the tube into it). In other words, he viewed the barometer as a balance, an instrument for measurement (as opposed to merely being an instrument to create a vacuum), and because he was the first to view it this way, he is traditionally considered the inventor of the barometer (in the sense in which we now use the term).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because of rumors circulating in Torricelli\'s gossipy Italian neighborhood, which included that he was engaged in some form of sorcery or witchcraft, Torricelli realized he had to keep his experiment secret to avoid  the risk of being arrested. He needed to use a liquid that was heavier than water, and from his previous association and suggestions by Galileo, he deduced by using <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, a shorter tube could be used. With mercury, which is about 14 times denser than water, a tube only 80&#160;cm was now needed, not 10.5&#160;m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1646, <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Petit_(engineer)" title="Pierre Petit (engineer)">Pierre Petit</a>, had repeated and perfected Torricelli\'s experiment after hearing about it from <a href="/wiki/Marin_Mersenne" title="Marin Mersenne">Marin Mersenne</a>, who himself had been shown the experiment by Torricelli toward the end of 1644. Pascal further devised an experiment to test the Aristotelian proposition that it was vapors from the liquid that filled the space in a barometer. His experiment compared water with wine, and since the latter was considered more "spiritous", the Aristotelians expected the wine to stand lower (since more vapors would mean more pushing down on the liquid column). Pascal performed the experiment publicly, inviting the Aristotelians to predict the outcome beforehand. The Aristotelians predicted the wine would stand lower. It did not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, Pascal went even further to test the mechanical theory. If, as suspected by mechanical philosophers like Torricelli and Pascal, air had weight, the pressure would be less at higher altitudes. Therefore, Pascal wrote to his brother-in-law, Florin Perier, who lived near a mountain called the <a href="/wiki/Puy_de_D%C3%B4me" title="Puy de D&#244;me">Puy de Dome</a>, asking him to perform a crucial experiment. Perier was to take a barometer up the Puy de Dome and make measurements along the way of the height of the column of mercury. He was then to compare it to measurements taken at the foot of the mountain to see if those measurements taken higher up were in fact smaller. In September 1648, Perier carefully and meticulously carried out the experiment, and found that Pascal\'s predictions had been correct. The mercury barometer stood lower the higher one went.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Barometer_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Barometer-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water-based_barometers">Water-based barometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Water-based barometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barometer_Goethe_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="292" data-file-width="125" decoding="async" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Barometer_Goethe_01.jpg" width="125"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barometer_Goethe_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Goethe\'s device</div></div></div>\n<p>The concept that decreasing atmospheric pressure predicts stormy weather, postulated by <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Vidi" title="Lucien Vidi">Lucien Vidi</a>, provides the theoretical basis for a weather prediction device called a "weather glass" or a "Goethe barometer" (named for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_Von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang Von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang Von Goethe</a>, the renowned German writer and <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a> who developed a simple but effective weather ball barometer using the principles developed by <a href="/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli" title="Evangelista Torricelli">Torricelli</a>).  The <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> name, <i>le barom&#232;tre Li&#232;geois</i>, is used by some English speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner-9">[9]</a></sup>  This name reflects the origins of many early weather glasses &#8211; the glass blowers of <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Li&#232;ge">Li&#232;ge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-turner-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zittle_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-zittle-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The weather ball barometer consists of a glass container with a sealed body, half filled with water.  A narrow spout connects to the body below the water level and rises above the water level. The narrow spout is open to the atmosphere. When the air pressure is lower than it was at the time the body was sealed, the water level in the spout will rise above the water level in the body; when the air pressure is higher, the water level in the spout will drop below the water level in the body.  A variation of this type of barometer can be easily made at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JET_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-JET-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury_barometers">Mercury barometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mercury barometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> barometer has a vertical glass tube closed at the top sitting in an open mercury-filled basin at the bottom <a class="external free" href="http://mercurypolicy.scripts.mit.edu/blog/?p=352" rel="nofollow">http://mercurypolicy.scripts.mit.edu/blog/?p=352</a>.  The weight of the mercury creates a vacuum at the top of the tube known as Torricellian vacuum. Mercury in the tube adjusts until the weight of the mercury column balances the atmospheric force exerted on the reservoir.  High atmospheric pressure places more force on the reservoir, forcing mercury higher in the column.  Low pressure allows the mercury to drop to a lower level in the column by lowering the force placed on the reservoir. Since higher temperature levels around the instrument will reduce the density of the mercury, the scale for reading the height of the mercury is adjusted to compensate for this effect.  The tube has to be at least as long as the amount dipping in the mercury + head space + the maximum length of the column.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MercuryBarometer.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="860" data-file-width="650" decoding="async" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/MercuryBarometer.svg/220px-MercuryBarometer.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/MercuryBarometer.svg/330px-MercuryBarometer.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/MercuryBarometer.svg/440px-MercuryBarometer.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MercuryBarometer.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic drawing of a simple mercury barometer with vertical <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> column and reservoir at  base</div></div></div>\n<p>Torricelli documented that the height of the mercury in a barometer changed slightly each day and concluded that this was due to the changing pressure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.islandnet.com/~see/weather/history/barometerhistory1.htm-1">[1]</a></sup>  He wrote: "We live submerged at the bottom of an ocean of elementary air, which is known by incontestable experiments to have weight".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Inspired by Torricelli, <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Guericke" title="Otto von Guericke">Otto von Guericke</a> on 5 December 1660 found that air pressure was unusually low and predicted a storm, which occurred the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196606_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196606-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baro_3.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1594" data-file-width="748" decoding="async" height="362" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Baro_3.png/170px-Baro_3.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Baro_3.png/255px-Baro_3.png 1.5x, title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fortin barometer</div></div></div>\n<p>The mercury barometer\'s design gives rise to the expression of atmospheric pressure in <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inches</a> or <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">millimeters</a> of mercury (mmHg).  A <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a> was originally defined as 1 mmHg.  The pressure is quoted as the level of the mercury\'s height in the vertical column.  Typically, atmospheric pressure is measured between 26.5 inches (670&#160;mm) and 31.5 inches (800&#160;mm) of Hg. One atmosphere (1 atm) is equivalent to 29.92 inches (760&#160;mm) of mercury.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baro_2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="462" decoding="async" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Baro_2.png/170px-Baro_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Baro_2.png/255px-Baro_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Baro_2.png/340px-Baro_2.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baro_2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reservoir of a Fortin barometer</div></div></div>\n<p>Design changes to make the instrument more sensitive, simpler to read, and easier to transport resulted in variations such as the basin, siphon, wheel, cistern, Fortin, multiple folded, stereometric, and balance barometers. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzroy" title="Robert Fitzroy"><i>Fitzroy</i></a> barometers combine the standard mercury barometer with a thermometer, as well as a guide of how to interpret pressure changes.  <a href="/wiki/Jean_Nicolas_Fortin" title="Jean Nicolas Fortin">Fortin</a> barometers use a variable displacement mercury cistern, usually constructed with a thumbscrew pressing on a leather diaphragm bottom (V in the diagram).  This compensates for displacement of mercury in the column with varying pressure.  To use a Fortin barometer, the level of mercury is set to zero by using the thumbscrew to make an ivory pointer (O in the diagram) just touch the surface of the mercury.  The pressure is then read on the column by adjusting the <a href="/wiki/Vernier_scale" title="Vernier scale">vernier scale</a> so that the mercury just touches the sightline at Z..  Some models also employ a valve for closing the cistern, enabling the mercury column to be forced to the top of the column for transport.  This prevents water-hammer damage to the column in transit.\n</p><p>On June 5, 2007, a <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> directive was enacted to restrict the sale of mercury, thus effectively ending the production of new mercury barometers in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eu_reuters_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-eu_reuters-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vacuum_pump_oil_barometer">Vacuum pump oil barometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vacuum pump oil barometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Using vacuum pump oil  the working fluid in a barometer has led to the creation of the new "World\'s Tallest Barometer" in February 2013. The barometer at Portland State University (PSU) uses doubly distilled vacuum pump oil and has a nominal height of about 12.4&#160;m for the oil column height; expected excursions are in the range of &#177;0.4&#160;m over the course of a year. Vacuum pump oil has very low vapor pressure and it is available in a range of densities; the lowest density vacuum oil was chosen for the PSU barometer to maximize the oil column height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aneroid_barometers">Aneroid barometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aneroid_barometer.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="875" href="/wiki/File:Aneroid_barometer.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old aneroid barometer</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Modern_Aneroid_Barometer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern aneroid barometer</div></div></div>\n<p>An <b>aneroid barometer</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Measuring_instrument" title="Measuring instrument">instrument</a> used for measuring <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> as a method that does not involve <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>. Invented in 1844 by French scientist <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Vidi" title="Lucien Vidi">Lucien Vidi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-r1-16">[16]</a></sup> the aneroid barometer uses a small, flexible metal box called an aneroid cell (capsule), which is made from an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. The evacuated capsule (or usually several capsules, stacked to add up their movements) is prevented from collapsing by a strong spring. Small changes in external air pressure cause the cell to expand or contract.  This expansion and contraction drives mechanical levers such that the tiny movements of the capsule are amplified and displayed on the face of the aneroid barometer.  Many models include a manually set needle which is used to mark the current measurement so a change can be seen. This type of barometer is common in homes and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recreational_boat" title="Recreational boat">recreational boats</a>. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Barograph" title="Barograph">barographs</a> and as a pressure instrument in <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosondes</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barographs">Barographs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Barographs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Barograph" title="Barograph">Barograph</a></div>\n<p>A barograph records a graph of atmospheric pressure.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MEMS_Barometers">MEMS Barometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: MEMS Barometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro_Electro_Mechanical_Systems" title="Micro Electro Mechanical Systems">Micro Electro Mechanical Systems</a> (or MEMS) barometers are extremely small devices between 1 and 100 micrometres in size (0.001 to 0.1&#160;mm). id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> When used in combination with wind observations, reasonably accurate short-term forecasts can be made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Simultaneous barometric readings from across a network of weather stations allow maps of air pressure to be produced, which were the first form of the modern <a atmospheric pressure acts as a barrier to approaching weather systems, diverting their course. Atmospheric lift caused by low-level wind convergence into the surface brings clouds and sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convection_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-convection-28">[28]</a></sup> The larger the change in pressure, especially if more than 3.5&#160;hPa (0.1 inHg), the greater the change in weather that can be expected. If the pressure drop is rapid, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure system</a> is approaching, and there is a greater chance of rain. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">Rapid pressure rises</a>, such as in the wake of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, are associated with improving weather conditions, such as clearing skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With falling air pressure, gases trapped within the coal in deep mines can escape more freely. Thus low pressure increases the risk of <a href="/wiki/Firedamp" title="Firedamp">firedamp</a> accumulating. Collieries therefore keep track of the pressure. In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Trimdon_Grange#Colliery_Disaster" title="Trimdon Grange">Trimdon Grange colliery disaster</a> of 1882 the mines inspector drew attention to the records and in the report stated "the conditions of atmosphere and temperature may be taken to have reached a dangerous point".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Aneroid barometers are used in <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Submersible_pressure_gauge_(SPG)" title="Diving regulator">submersible pressure gauge</a> is used to keep track of the contents of the diver\'s air tank. Another gauge is used to measure the hydrostatic pressure, usually expressed as a depth of sea water.  Either or both gauges may be replaced with electronic variants or a dive computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compensations">Compensations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Compensations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The density of mercury will change with increase or decrease in temperature, so a reading must be adjusted for the temperature of the instrument. For this purpose a mercury <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> is usually mounted on the instrument. Temperature compensation of an aneroid barometer is accomplished by including a bi-metal element in the mechanical linkages.  Aneroid barometers sold for domestic use typically have no compensation under the assumption that they will be used within a controlled room temperature range.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altitude">Altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg/220px-NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg/330px-NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg/440px-NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NWS_Key_West_office_barometer_as_Hurricane_Irma_approached.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A digital barometer with altimeter setting (for correction) displayed</div></div></div>\n<p>As the air pressure decreases at <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> above sea level (and increases below sea level) the uncorrected reading of the barometer will depend on its location. The reading is then adjusted to an equivalent sea-level pressure for purposes of reporting. For example, if a barometer located at sea level and under fair weather conditions is moved to an altitude of 1,000 feet (305&#160;m), about 1 inch of mercury (~35 hPa) must be added on to the reading. The barometer readings at the two locations should be the same if there are negligible changes in time, horizontal distance, and temperature. If this were not done, there would be a false indication of an approaching storm at the higher elevation.\n</p><p>Aneroid barometers have a mechanical adjustment that allows the equivalent sea level pressure to be read directly and without further adjustment if the instrument is not moved to a different altitude. Setting an aneroid barometer is similar to resetting an <a href="/wiki/Clock_face" title="Clock face">analog clock</a> that is not at the correct time. Its dial is rotated so that the current atmospheric pressure from a known accurate and nearby barometer (such as the local <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather station</a>) is displayed. No calculation is needed, as the source barometer reading has already been converted to equivalent sea-level pressure, and this is transferred to the barometer being set&#8212;regardless of its altitude. Though somewhat rare, a few aneroid barometers intended for monitoring the weather are calibrated to manually adjust for altitude. In this case, knowing <i>either</i> the altitude or the current atmospheric pressure would be sufficient for future accurate readings.\n</p><p>The table below shows examples for three locations in the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. In the mid-19th century, this method was used by explorers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equation">Equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When atmospheric pressure is measured by a barometer, the pressure is also referred to as the "barometric pressure". Assume a barometer with a cross-sectional area <i>A</i>, a height <i>h</i>, filled with mercury from the bottom at Point B to the top at Point C. The pressure at the bottom of the barometer, Point B, is equal to the atmospheric pressure. The pressure at the very top, Point C, can be taken as zero because there is only mercury vapor above this point and its pressure is very low relative to the atmospheric pressure. Therefore, one can find the atmospheric pressure using the barometer and this equation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>P<sub>atm</sub> = &#961;gh</dd></dl>\n<p>where &#961; is the density of mercury, g is the gravitational acceleration, and h is the height of the mercury column above the free surface area. The physical dimensions (length of tube and cross-sectional area of the tube) of the barometer itself have no effect on the height of the fluid column in the tube.\n</p><p>In thermodynamic calculations, a commonly used pressure unit is the "standard atmosphere". This is the pressure resulting from a column of mercury of 760&#160;mm in height at 0&#160;&#176;C. For the density of mercury, use &#961;<sub>Hg</sub> = 13,595&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup> and for gravitational acceleration use g = 9.807&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup>.\n</p><p>If water were used (instead of mercury) to meet the standard atmospheric pressure, a water column of roughly 10.3&#160;m (33.8&#160;ft) would be needed.\n</p><p>Standard <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric
