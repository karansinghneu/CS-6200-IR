(Image not to scale)</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>hydrogen atom</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electrically</a> neutral atom contains a single positively charged <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and a single negatively charged <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> bound to the nucleus by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coulomb_force" title="Coulomb force">Coulomb force</a>. <b>Atomic hydrogen</b> constitutes <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">about 75%</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryonic</a> mass of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In everyday life on Earth, isolated hydrogen atoms (called "atomic hydrogen") are extremely rare. Instead, hydrogen tends to combine with other atoms in compounds, or with itself to form ordinary (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a>) hydrogen gas, H<sub>2</sub>. "Atomic hydrogen" and "hydrogen atom" in ordinary English use have overlapping, yet distinct, meanings. For example, a water molecule contains two hydrogen atoms, but does not contain atomic hydrogen (which would refer to isolated hydrogen atoms).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">Atomic spectroscopy</a> shows that there is a discrete infinite set of states in which a hydrogen (or any) atom can exist, contrary to the predictions of <a href="/wiki/Classical_physics" title="Classical physics">classical physics</a>. Attempts to develop a theoretical understanding of the states of the hydrogen atom have been important to the <a href="/wiki/History_of_quantum_mechanics" title="History of quantum mechanics">history of quantum mechanics</a>, since all other atoms can be roughly understood by knowing in detail about this simplest atomic structure.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_hydrogen" title="Isotopes of hydrogen">Isotopes of hydrogen</a></div>\n<p>The most <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">abundant</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <b>hydrogen-1</b>, <b>protium</b>, or <b>light hydrogen</b>, contains no <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> and is simply a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. Protium is <a href="/wiki/Proton_decay" title="Proton decay">stable</a> and makes up 99.985% of naturally occurring hydrogen atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housecroft_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housecroft-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">Deuterium</a> contains one neutron and one proton. Deuterium is stable and makes up 0.0156% of naturally occurring hydrogen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housecroft_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Housecroft-2">[2]</a></sup> and is used in industrial processes like <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deuterium_NMR" title="Deuterium NMR">Nuclear Magnetic Resonance</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">Tritium</a> contains two neutrons and one proton and is not stable, decaying with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 12.32 years. Because of the short half life, tritium does not exist in nature except in trace amounts.\n</p><p>Higher isotopes of hydrogen are only created in artificial <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">accelerators</a> and reactors and have half lives around the order of 10<sup>&#8722;22</sup> second (0.0000000000000000000001 second).\n</p><p>The formulas below are valid for all three isotopes of hydrogen, but slightly different values of the <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_constant" title="Rydberg constant">Rydberg constant</a> (correction formula given below) must be used for each hydrogen isotope.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_ion">Hydrogen ion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hydrogen ion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">Hydron (chemistry)</a></div>\n<p>Hydrogen is not found without its electron in ordinary chemistry (room temperatures and pressures), as ionized hydrogen is highly chemically reactive. When ionized hydrogen is written as "H<sup>+</sup>" as in the solvation of classical acids such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydronium_ion" title="Hydronium ion">hydronium ion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sub>3</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sup>+</sup>, is meant, not a literal ionized single hydrogen atom. In that case, the acid transfers the proton to H<sub>2</sub>O to form H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>.\n</p><p>Ionized hydrogen without its electron, or free protons, are common in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_analysis">Theoretical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The hydrogen atom has special significance in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a> as a simple <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">two-body problem</a> physical system which has yielded many simple <a href="/wiki/Closed-form_expression" title="Closed-form expression">analytical</a> solutions in closed-form.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_classical_description">Failed classical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Failed classical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Rutherford_model" title="Rutherford model">Experiments</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> in 1909 showed the structure of the atom to be a dense, positive nucleus with a tenuous negative charge cloud around it. Sommerfeld introduced two additional degrees of freedom, allowing an electron to move on an elliptical orbit characterized by its <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> with respect to a chosen axis. This introduced two additional quantum numbers, which correspond to the orbital <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> and its projection on the chosen axis. Thus the correct multiplicity of states (except for the factor 2 accounting for the yet unknown electron spin) was found. Further, by applying <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> to the elliptic orbits, Sommerfeld succeeded in deriving the correct expression for the fine structure of hydrogen spectra (which happens to be exactly the same as in the most elaborate Dirac theory). However, some observed phenomena, such as the anomalous <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">Zeeman effect</a>, remained unexplained. These issues were resolved with the full development of quantum mechanics and the <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a>. It is often alleged that the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schr&#246;dinger equation">Schr&#246;dinger equation</a> is superior to the Bohr-Sommerfeld theory in describing hydrogen atom. This is not the case, as most of the results of both approaches coincide or are very close (a remarkable exception is the problem of hydrogen atom in crossed electric and magnetic fields, which cannot be self-consistently solved in the framework of the Bohr-Sommerfeld theory), and in both theories the main shortcomings result from the absence of the electron spin. --></mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>/</mo>\n</mrow>\n<msubsup>\n<mi>a</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>0</mn>\n</mrow>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>&#8727;<!-- &#8727; --></mo>\n</mrow>\n</msubsup>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\hbar /a_{0}^{*}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\displaystyle \\hbar /a_{0}^{*}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f1d3b3e502e57e8d28529f10833b2de96cc85a2" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:4.753ex; height:3.009ex;"/></span>.\n</p><p>The solutions to the Schr&#246;dinger equation for hydrogen are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytical_expression" title="Analytical expression">analytical</a>, giving a simple expression for the hydrogen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_levels" title="Energy levels">energy levels</a> and thus the frequencies of the hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral lines</a> and fully reproduced the Bohr model and went beyond it. It also yields two other quantum numbers and the shape of the electron\'s wave function ("orbital") for the various possible quantum-mechanical states, thus explaining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anisotropic" title="Anisotropic">anisotropic</a> character of atomic bonds.\n</p><p>The Schr&#246;dinger equation also applies to more complicated atoms and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>. When there is more than one electron or nucleus the solution is not analytical and either computer calculations are necessary or simplifying assumptions must be made.\n</p><p>Since the Schr&#246;dinger equation is only valid for non-relativistic quantum mechanics, the solutions it yields for the hydrogen atom are not entirely correct. The <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a> of relativistic quantum theory improves these solutions (see below).\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Results_of_Schr.C3.B6dinger_equation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_of_Schr&#246;dinger_equation">Results of Schr&#246;dinger equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Results of Schr&#246;dinger equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The solution of the Schr&#246;dinger equation (wave equation) for the hydrogen atom uses the fact that the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb\'s law">Coulomb potential</a> produced by the nucleus is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> (it is radially symmetric in space and only depends on the distance to the nucleus). Although the resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_eigenfunctions" title="Energy eigenfunctions">energy eigenfunctions</a> (the <i>orbitals</i>) are not necessarily isotropic themselves, their dependence on the <a href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system" title="Spherical coordinate system">angular coordinates</a> follows completely generally from this isotropy of the underlying potential: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenstates" title="Eigenstates">eigenstates</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)">Hamiltonian</a> (that is, the energy eigenstates) can be chosen as simultaneous eigenstates of the <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator" title="Angular momentum operator">angular momentum operator</a>. This corresponds to the fact that angular momentum is conserved in the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_motion_(quantum)" title="Orbital motion (quantum)">orbital motion</a> of the electron around the nucleus. Therefore, the energy eigenstates may be classified by two angular momentum <a href="/wiki/Quantum_number" title="Quantum number">quantum numbers</a>, <i>&#8467;</i> and <i>m</i> (both are integers). The angular momentum quantum number <span class="nowrap"><i>&#8467;</i> = 0, 1, 2, ...</span> determines the magnitude of the angular momentum. The magnetic quantum number <span class="nowrap"><i>m</i> = &#8722;<i>&#8467;</i>, ..., +<i>&#8467;</i></span> determines the projection of the angular momentum on the (arbitrarily chosen) <i>z</i>-axis.\n</p><p>In addition to mathematical expressions for total angular momentum and angular momentum projection of wavefunctions, an expression for the radial dependence of the wave functions must be found. It is only here that the details of the 1/<i>r</i> Coulomb potential enter (leading to <a href="/wiki/Laguerre_polynomials" title="Laguerre polynomials">Laguerre polynomials</a> in <i>r</i>). This leads to a third quantum number, the principal quantum number <span class="nowrap"><i>n</i> = 1, 2, 3, ...</span>. The principal quantum number in hydrogen is related to the atom\'s total energy.\n</p><p>Note that the maximum value of the angular momentum quantum number is limited by the principal quantum number: it can run only up to <i>n</i> &#8722; 1, i.e. <span class="nowrap"><i>&#8467;</i> = 0, 1, ..., <i>n</i> &#8722; 1</span>.\n</p><p>Due to angular momentum conservation, states of the same <i>&#8467;</i> but different <i>m</i> have the same energy (this holds for all problems with <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">rotational symmetry</a>). In addition, for the hydrogen atom, states of the same <i>n</i> but different <i>&#8467;</i> are also <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels" title="Degenerate energy levels">degenerate</a> (i.e. they have the same energy). However, this is a specific property of hydrogen and is no longer true for more complicated atoms which have an (effective) potential differing from the form 1/<i>r</i> (due to the presence of the inner electrons shielding the nucleus potential).\n</p><p>Taking into account the <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> of the electron adds a last quantum number, the projection of the electron\'s spin angular momentum along the <i>z</i>-axis, which can take on two values. Therefore, any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenstate" title="Eigenstate">eigenstate</a> of the electron in the hydrogen atom is described fully by four quantum numbers. According to the usual rules of quantum mechanics, the actual state of the electron may be any <a href="/wiki/Quantum_superposition" title="Quantum superposition">superposition</a> of these states. This explains also why the choice of <i>z</i>-axis for the directional <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(physics)" title="Quantization (physics)">quantization</a> of the angular momentum vector is immaterial: an orbital of given <i>&#8467;</i> and <i>m</i>&#8242; obtained for another preferred axis <i>z</i>&#8242; can always be represented as a suitable superposition of the various states of different <i>m</i> (but same <i>l</i>) that have been obtained for <i>z</i>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_summary_of_eigenstates_of_hydrogen_atom">Mathematical summary of eigenstates of hydrogen atom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mathematical summary of eigenstates of hydrogen atom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen-like_atom" title="Hydrogen-like atom">Hydrogen-like atom</a></div>\n<p>In 1928, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a> found <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">an equation</a> that was fully compatible with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Relativity" title="Special Relativity">Special Relativity</a>, and (as a consequence) made the wave function a 4-component "<a href="/wiki/Dirac_spinor" title="Dirac spinor">Dirac spinor</a>" including "up" and "down" spin components, with both positive and "negative" energy (or matter and antimatter). width:8.931ex; height:2.343ex;"/></span> (the remaining non-angular nodes) are radial nodes.</li></ul>\n<h4><span id="Features_going_beyond_the_Schr.C3.B6dinger_solution"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Features_going_beyond_the_Schr&#246;dinger_solution">Features going beyond the Schr&#246;dinger solution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Features going beyond the Schr&#246;dinger solution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There are several important effects that are neglected by the Schr&#246;dinger equation and which are responsible for certain small but measurable deviations of the real spectral lines from the predicted ones:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Although the mean speed of the electron in hydrogen is only 1/137th of the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, many modern experiments are sufficiently precise that a complete theoretical explanation requires a fully relativistic treatment of the problem. A relativistic treatment results in a momentum increase of about 1 part in 37,000 for the electron. Since the electron\'s wavelength is determined by its momentum, orbitals containing higher speed electrons show contraction due to smaller wavelengths.</li>\n<li>Even when there is no external <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_frame" title="Inertial frame">inertial frame</a> of the moving electron, the electromagnetic field of the nucleus has a magnetic component. The spin of the electron has an associated <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_moment" title="Magnetic moment">magnetic moment</a> which interacts with this magnetic field. This effect is also explained by special relativity, and it leads to the so-called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spin-orbit_coupling" title="Spin-orbit coupling">spin-orbit coupling</a></i>, i.e., an interaction between the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbital_motion_(quantum)" title="Orbital motion (quantum)">orbital motion</a> around the nucleus, and its <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Both of these features (and more) are incorporated in the relativistic <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a>, with predictions that come still closer to experiment. Again the Dirac equation may be solved analytically in the special case of a two-body system, such as the hydrogen atom. The resulting solution quantum states now must be classified by the <a href="/wiki/Total_angular_momentum_quantum_number" title="Total angular momentum quantum number">total angular momentum number</a> <i>j</i> (arising through the coupling between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_spin" title="Electron spin">electron spin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator" title="Angular momentum operator">orbital angular momentum</a>). States of the same <i>j</i> and the same <i>n</i> are still degenerate. Thus, direct analytical solution of <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">Dirac equation</a> predicts 2S(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>) and 2P(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>) levels of Hydrogen to have exactly the same energy, which is in a contradiction with observations (<a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">Lamb-Retherford experiment</a>).\n</p>\n<ul><li>There are always <a href="/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation" title="Quantum fluctuation">vacuum fluctuations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic field</a>, according to quantum mechanics. Due to such fluctuations degeneracy between states of the same j but different l is lifted, giving them slightly different energies. This has been demonstrated in the famous <a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">Lamb-Retherford experiment</a> and was the starting point for the development of the theory of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">Quantum electrodynamics</a> (which is able to deal with these vacuum fluctuations and employs the famous <a href="/wiki/Feynman_diagram" title="Feynman diagram">Feynman diagrams</a> for approximations using <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)">perturbation theory</a>). This effect is now called <a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">Lamb shift</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>For these developments, it was essential that the solution of the Dirac equation for the hydrogen atom could be worked out exactly, such that any experimentally observed deviation had to be taken seriously as a signal of failure of the theory.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Alternatives_to_the_Schr.C3.B6dinger_theory"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_the_Schr&#246;dinger_theory">Alternatives to the Schr&#246;dinger theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alternatives to the Schr&#246;dinger theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the language of <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Heisenberg\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Matrix_mechanics" title="Matrix mechanics">matrix mechanics</a>, the hydrogen  atom was first solved by <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli" title="Wolfgang Pauli">Wolfgang Pauli</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pauli_1926_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pauli_1926-14">[14]</a></sup> using a <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">rotational symmetry</a> in four dimensions [O(4)-symmetry] generated by the angular momentum \nand the <a href="/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector" title="Laplace&#8211;Runge&#8211;Lenz vector">Laplace&#8211;Runge&#8211;Lenz vector</a>. By extending the symmetry group O(4) to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamical_group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dynamical group (page does not exist)">dynamical group</a> O(4,2),\nthe entire spectrum and all transitions were embedded in a single irreducible group representation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1979 the (non relativistic) hydrogen atom was solved for the first time within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.P._Feynman" title="R.P. Feynman">Feynman\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Path_integral_formulation" title="Path integral formulation">path integral formulation</a>\nof <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  This work greatly extended the range of applicability of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R.P._Feynman" title="R.P. Feynman">Feynman\'s</a> method.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="metadata mbox-small" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="30" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Office-book.svg/30px-Office-book.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Office-book.svg/45px-Office-book.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Office-book.svg/60px-Office-book.svg.png 2x" width="30"/></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist"><ul style="font-weight: bold">\n<li><a href="/wiki/Book:Hydrogen" title="Book:Hydrogen">Book: Hydrogen</a></li>\n</ul></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<div>\n<table class="multicol" role="presentation" style="border-collapse: collapse;
