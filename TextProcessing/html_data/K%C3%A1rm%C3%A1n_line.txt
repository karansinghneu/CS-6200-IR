For the boundary of the universe, see <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmosphere_layers-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2688" data-file-width="457" decoding="async" height="1000" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Atmosphere_layers-en.svg/170px-Atmosphere_layers-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Atmosphere_layers-en.svg/255px-Atmosphere_layers-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Atmosphere_layers-en.svg/340px-Atmosphere_layers-en.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmosphere_layers-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The K&#225;rm&#225;n line lies within the lower <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>. The layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> are shown out of scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>K&#225;rm&#225;n line</b>, or <b>Karman line</b>, is an attempt to define a boundary between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This is important for legal and regulatory measures; aircraft and spacecraft fall under different jurisdictions and are subject to different treaties. \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale">F&#233;d&#233;ration a&#233;ronautique internationale</a> (FAI; English: World Air Sports Federation), an international standard-setting and record-keeping body for <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>, defines the K&#225;rm&#225;n line as the <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> of 100 kilometres (62 miles; 330,000 feet) above <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. Other organizations do not use this definition. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">US Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> define the limit to be 50 miles (80 kilometres) above sea level for purposes of awarding personnel with outer space badges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There is no international law defining the edge of space, and therefore the limit of national airspace, and the US is resisting regulatory movement on this front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The line is named after <a href="/wiki/Theodore_von_K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n" title="Theodore von K&#225;rm&#225;n">Theodore von K&#225;rm&#225;n</a> (1881&#8211;1963), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_American" title="Hungarian American">Hungarian American</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, who was active primarily in <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>. He was the first person to calculate at which altitude the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> becomes too thin to support aeronautical flight and arrived at 83.6&#160;km (51.9&#160;mi) himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The reason is that a vehicle at this altitude would have to travel faster than <a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">orbital velocity</a> to derive sufficient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_lift" title="Aerodynamic lift">aerodynamic lift</a> to support itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The line is approximately at the <a href="/wiki/Turbopause" title="Turbopause">turbopause</a>, above which atmospheric gasses are not well-mixed.  The <a href="/wiki/Mesopause" title="Mesopause">mesopause</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_temperature" title="Atmospheric temperature">atmospheric temperature</a> minimum has been measured to vary from 85 to 100&#160;km, which places the line at or near the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input id="K&#225;rm&#225;n\'s_comments">K&#225;rm&#225;n\'s comments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: K&#225;rm&#225;n\'s comments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the final chapter of his autobiography K&#225;rm&#225;n addresses the issue of the edge of <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Where space begins&#8230; can actually be determined by the speed of the space vehicle and its altitude above the earth. Consider, for instance, the record flight of Captain <a href="/wiki/Iven_Carl_Kincheloe_Jr." title="Iven Carl Kincheloe Jr.">Iven Carl Kincheloe Jr.</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-2" title="Bell X-2">X-2 rocket plane</a>. Kincheloe flew 2000 miles per hour (3,200 km/h) at 126,000 feet (38,500 m), or 24 miles up. At this altitude and speed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_lift" title="Aerodynamic lift">aerodynamic lift</a> still carries 98 per cent of the weight of the plane, and only two per cent is carried by <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">centrifugal force</a>, or Kepler Force, as space scientists call it. But at 300,000 feet (91,440 m) or 57 miles up, this relationship is reversed because there is no longer any air to contribute lift: only centrifugal force prevails. This is certainly a physical boundary, where <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> stops and <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a> begins, and so I thought why should it not also be a jurisdictional boundary? <a href="/wiki/Andrew_G._Haley" title="Andrew G. Haley">Haley</a> has kindly called it the K&#225;rm&#225;n Jurisdictional Line. Below this line space belongs to each country. Above this level there would be free space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An atmosphere does not abruptly end at any given height but becomes progressively thinner with altitude. Also, depending on how the various layers that make up the space around the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> are defined (and depending on whether these layers are considered part of the actual atmosphere), the definition of the edge of space could vary considerably: If one were to consider the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> part of the atmosphere and not of space, one might have to extend the boundary to space to at least 10,000&#160;km (6,200&#160;mi) above sea level. The K&#225;rm&#225;n line thus is an arbitrary definition based on the following considerations:\n</p><p>An aircraft can only stay aloft by constantly traveling forward relative to the air (rather than the ground), so that the wings can generate lift. The thinner the air, the faster the plane must go to generate enough lift to stay up. All other factors remaining unchanged, true airspeed (<i>v</i>) must increase to compensate for less air density (<i>&#961;</i>) at higher altitudes.\n</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbiting</a> spacecraft only stays in the sky if the centrifugal component of its movement around the Earth is enough to balance the downward pull of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. If it goes slower, the pull of gravity gradually makes its altitude decrease. The required speed is called <i><a href="/wiki/Orbital_speed" title="Orbital speed">orbital velocity</a>,</i> and it varies with the height of the orbit. For the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, the orbital velocity is about 27,000&#160;km per hour (17,000 miles per hour).\n</p><p>For an airplane flying higher and higher, the increasingly thin air provides less and less <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>, requiring increasingly higher speed to create enough lift to hold the airplane up. It eventually reaches an altitude where it must fly so fast to generate lift that it reaches orbital velocity. The K&#225;rm&#225;n line is the altitude where the speed necessary to aerodynamically support the airplane\'s full weight equals orbital velocity (assuming wing loading of a typical airplane). In practice, supporting full weight wouldn\'t be necessary to maintain altitude because the curvature of the Earth adds centrifugal lift as the airplane reaches orbital speed. However, the K&#225;rm&#225;n line definition ignores this effect because orbital velocity is implicitly sufficient to maintain any altitude regardless of atmospheric density. The K&#225;rm&#225;n line is therefore the highest altitude at which orbital speed provides sufficient aerodynamic lift to fly in a straight line that doesn\'t follow the curvature of the Earth\'s surface.\n</p><p>Above 100 kilometers the air density is about 1/2,200,000 the density on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-squire2000_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-squire2000-11">[11]</a></sup> At the K&#225;rm&#225;n line, the air density &#961; --></mi>\n<msubsup>\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>0</mn>\n</mrow>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msubsup>\n<mi>S</mi>\n<msub>\n<mi>C</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>L</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mi>m</mi>\n<mi>g</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle L={\\tfrac {1}{2}}\\rho v_{0}^{2}SC_{L}=mg}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="L={\\tfrac  12}\\rho v_{0}^{2}SC_{L}=mg" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/660945dedd89c630807415c89ac15f901a1cdfc3" style="vertical-align: -1.171ex; width:20.491ex; height:3.509ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>v</i><sub>0</sub> is the speed of a circular orbit at the same altitude in vacuum</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd><i>m</i> is the mass of the aircraft</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd><i>g</i> is the acceleration due to gravity.</dd></dl>\n<p>Although the calculated altitude was not exactly 100 km, K&#225;rm&#225;n proposed that 100 km be the designated boundary to space, because the round number is more memorable, and the calculated altitude varies minutely as certain parameters are varied. An international committee recommended the 100 km line to the FAI, and upon adoption, it became widely accepted as the boundary to space for many purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> However, there is still no international legal definition of the demarcation between a country\'s air space and outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another hurdle to strictly defining the boundary to space is the dynamic nature of Earth\'s atmosphere. For example, at an altitude of 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi), the atmosphere\'s density can vary by a factor of five, depending on the time of day, time of year, <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere#Solar_flux" title="Ionosphere">AP magnetic index</a>, and recent <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere#Solar_flux" title="Ionosphere">solar flux</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The FAI uses the K&#225;rm&#225;n line to define the boundary between aeronautics and astronautics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">Aeronautics</a> &#8212; For FAI purposes, aerial activity, including all air sports, within 100 kilometers of Earth\'s surface.<i></i></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">Astronautics</a> &#8212; For FAI purposes, activity more than 100 kilometers above Earth\'s surface.</li></ul>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_of_the_definition">Interpretations of the definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpretations of the definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The expression "<b>edge of space</b>", is often used (by, for instance, the FAI<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in some of their publications) to refer to a region below the conventional 100&#160;km boundary to space, which is often meant to include substantially lower regions as well. Thus, certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">balloon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane</a> flights might be described as "reaching the edge of space". In such statements, "reaching the edge of space" merely refers to going higher than average aeronautical vehicles commonly would.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wings_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-wings-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1963 <a href="/wiki/Andrew_G._Haley" title="Andrew G. Haley">Andrew G. Haley</a> discussed the K&#225;rm&#225;n line in his book <i>Space Law and Government</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGH_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AGH-17">[17]</a></sup> In a chapter on the limits of national <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>, he made a survey of major writers&#8217; views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGH_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-AGH-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>82&#8211;96</span></sup> He indicated the inherent imprecision of the Line:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>The line represents a <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> measurement. It is comparable to such measures used in the law as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meander</a> line, <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a> line; but it is more complex than these. In arriving at the von K&#225;rm&#225;n jurisdictional line, myriad factors must be considered &#8211; other than the factor of aerodynamic lift. These factors have been discussed in a very large body of literature and by a score or more of commentators. They include the physical constitution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>; the biological and physiological viability; and still other factors which logically join to establish a point at which air no longer exists and at which <a href="/wiki/Airspace" title="Airspace">airspace</a> ends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AGH_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-AGH-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>78,9</span></sup></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_the_definition">Alternatives to the definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternatives to the definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2010" data-file-width="3027" decoding="async" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/370px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/555px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/740px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg 2x" width="370"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric gases scatter blue wavelengths of visible light more than other wavelengths, giving the Earth&#8217;s visible edge a blue halo. The <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is seen behind the halo. At higher and higher altitudes, the atmosphere becomes so thin that it essentially ceases to exist. Gradually, the atmospheric halo fades into the blackness of space.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> definition of an <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> is a person who has flown higher than 50 miles (80 kilometres) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a>, approximately the line between the <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> formerly used the FAI\'s 100-kilometer (62&#160;mi) figure, though this was changed in 2005, to eliminate any inconsistency between military personnel and civilians flying in the same vehicle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> when three veteran NASA <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">X-15</a> pilots (<a href="/wiki/John_B._McKay" title="John B. McKay">John B. McKay</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_H._Dana" title="William H. Dana">William H. Dana</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Albert_Walker" title="Joseph Albert Walker">Joseph Albert Walker</a>) were retroactively (two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posthumous_award" title="Posthumous award">posthumously</a>) awarded their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Badge" title="Astronaut Badge">astronaut wings</a>, as they had flown between 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) and 108&#160;km (67&#160;mi) in the 1960s, but at the time had not been recognized as astronauts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wings_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-wings-15">[15]</a></sup> The latter altitude exceeds the modern international definition of the boundary of space.\n</p><p>Two recent papers (J.C. McDowell, Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and T. Gangale, JSD, University of Nebraska-Lincoln<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>)\nadvocate that the demarcation of space should be at 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi; 260,000&#160;ft), citing as evidence von K&#225;rm&#225;n\'s original notes and calculations (which concluded the boundary should be 270,000&#160;ft), plus functional, cultural, physical, technological, mathematical, and historical factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These findings have prompted the FAI to propose holding a joint conference with the International Astronautical Federation (IAF) in 2019 to "fully explore" the issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another definition proposed in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> discussions defines the lower boundary of space as the lowest perigee attainable by an orbiting space vehicle, but does not specify an altitude.  Due to atmospheric drag, the lowest altitude at which an object in a circular orbit can complete at least one full revolution without propulsion is approximately 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi), whereas an object can maintain an elliptical orbit with perigee as low as about 130&#160;km (81&#160;mi) without propulsion. Above altitudes of approximately 160&#160;km (100&#160;mi) the sky is completely black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Army_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Army-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">Exosphere</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Atmosphere of Earth</a>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">Thermosphere</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">Mesophere</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">Stratosphere</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">Troposphere</a></li></ul></li>\n<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MW_18014&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MW 18014 (page does not exist)">MW 18014</a> &#8211;
