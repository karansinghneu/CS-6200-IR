Uncrewed spacecraft may have varying levels of autonomy from human input; they may be <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_vehicle" title="Remotely operated vehicle">remote controlled</a>, remote guided or even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_vehicle" title="Autonomous vehicle">autonomous</a>, meaning they have a pre-programmed list of operations, which they will execute unless otherwise instructed. Many habitable spacecraft also have varying levels of robotic features. For example, the space stations <a href="/wiki/Salyut_7" title="Salyut 7">Salyut 7</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a> module <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> were capable of remote guided station-keeping, and docking maneuvers with both resupply craft and new modules. The most common uncrewed spacecraft categories are <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncrewed_resupply_spacecraft" title="Uncrewed resupply spacecraft">uncrewed resupply spacecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">space observatories</a>. Not every uncrewed spacecraft is a robotic spacecraft; for example, a reflector ball is a non-robotic uncrewed spacecraft.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox"
