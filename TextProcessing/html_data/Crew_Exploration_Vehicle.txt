after the initial plans for the Crew Exploration Vehicle led to development of the Orion.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Crew Exploration Vehicle</b> (<b>CEV</b>) was the conceptual component of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">Vision for Space Exploration</a> that later became known as the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> spacecraft. The Orion CEV was part of NASA\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constellation_Program" title="Constellation Program">Constellation Program</a> to send human explorers back to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and then onward to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and other destinations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>.\n</p><p>Official planning for the vehicle began in 2004, with the final <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFP" title="RFP">Request For Proposal</a> issued on March 1, 2005, to begin a design competition for the vehicle. For the later design and construction phases, see  <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion (spacecraft)</a>. NASA has posted project status notes at the NASA.gov website, under the name "Orion Crew Exploration Vehicle" (see External links, below).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Competition"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Competition</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Proposals"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Proposals</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-3"><a href="#Original_designs"><span class="tocnumber">2.1</span> <span class="toctext">Original designs</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-4"><a href="#Changes_to_original_bids"><span class="tocnumber">2.2</span> <span class="toctext">Changes to original bids</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crew_Exploration_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The concept for the vehicle was officially announced in a speech given by <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> at <a href="/wiki/NASA_Headquarters" title="NASA Headquarters">NASA Headquarters</a> on January 14, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The Draft Statement of Work for the CEV was issued by NASA on December 9, 2004, and slightly more than one month later, on January 21, 2005, NASA issued a Draft <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFP" title="RFP">Request For Proposal</a> (RFP). The Final RFP was issued on March 1, 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with the potential bidders being asked to answer by May 2, 2005. \n</p><p>NASA had planned to have a suborbital or an Earth orbit fly-off called <i>Flight Application of Spacecraft Technologies</i> (FAST) between two teams\' CEV designs before September 1, 2008. However, in order to permit an earlier date for the start of CEV operations, Administrator <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">Michael D. Griffin</a> had indicated that NASA would select one contractor for the CEV in 2006. From his perspective, this would both help eliminate the currently planned four-year gap between the retirement of the Shuttle in 2010 and the first manned flight of the CEV in 2014 (by allowing the CEV to fly earlier), and save over $1 billion for use in CEV development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On June 13, 2005, NASA announced the selection of two consortia, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> Corp. and the team of <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> Corp. and The <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> Co. for further CEV development work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Each team had received a US$28 million contract to come up with a complete design for the CEV and its launch vehicle until August 2006, when NASA would award one of them the task of building the CEV<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  The teams would also have to develop a plan for their CEV to take part in the assembly of a lunar expedition, either with an <a href="/wiki/Earth_orbit_rendezvous" title="Earth orbit rendezvous">Earth orbit rendezvous</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit_rendezvous" title="Lunar orbit rendezvous">lunar orbit rendezvous</a>, or with a <a href="/wiki/Direct_ascent" title="Direct ascent">direct ascent</a>. The two teams were composed of:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Northrop Grumman associated with Boeing as subcontractor for the Spiral One, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcatel_Alenia_Space" title="Alcatel Alenia Space">Alenia Spazio</a>, ARES Corporation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Stark_Draper_Laboratory" title="Charles Stark Draper Laboratory">Draper Laboratory</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Space_Alliance" title="United Space Alliance">United Space Alliance</a></li>\n<li>Lockheed Martin associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EADS_SPACE_Transportation" title="EADS SPACE Transportation">EADS SPACE Transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Space_Alliance" title="United Space Alliance">United Space Alliance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerojet" title="Aerojet">Aerojet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">Honeywell</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences" title="Orbital Sciences">Orbital Sciences</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Technologies_Corporation" title="United Technologies Corporation">Hamilton Sundstrand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wyle_Laboratories" title="Wyle Laboratories">Wyle Laboratories</a> (awarded the contract August 31, 2006).</li></ul>\n<p>Each contractor-led team included subcontractors that provided the lunar expedition astronauts with equipment, life support, rocket engines, and onboard navigation systems.  The planned orbital or suborbital fly-offs under FAST would have seen the competition of a CEV built by each team, or of a technology demonstrator incorporating CEV technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Under FAST, NASA would have chosen the winner to build the final CEV after actual demonstration of this hardware.  Fly-offs are often used by the U.S. Air Force to select military aircraft; NASA has never used this approach in awarding contracts.  However, as Administrator Griffin had indicated he would abandon the FAST approach, NASA pursued the more traditional approach of selecting a vehicle based on the contractors\' proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schneider_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schneider-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On August 31, 2006, NASA announced that the contract to design and develop the Orion was awarded to Lockheed Martin Corp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_News" title="Bloomberg News">Bloomberg News</a>, five analysts it surveyed prior to the award announcement tipped the Northrop team to win.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lococo20060831_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lococo20060831-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_Caceres&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marco Caceres (page does not exist)">Marco Caceres</a>, a space industry analyst with Teal Group, had projected that Lockheed would lose, partly because of Lockheed Martin\'s earlier failure on the $912 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-33" title="X-33">X-33</a> shuttle replacement program; after the contract award he suggested that Lockheed Martin\'s work on the X-33 gave it more recent research and development experience in propulsion and materials, which may have helped it win the contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lococo20060831_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-lococo20060831-8">[8]</a></sup> According to an Aerospace Daily &amp; Defense Report summary of a NASA document explaining the rationale for the contract award, the Lockheed Martin proposal won on the basis of a superior technical approach, lower and more realistic cost estimates, and exceptional performance on Phase I of the CEV program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lockheed Martin plans to manufacture the manned spacecraft at facilities in Texas, Louisiana, and Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposals">Proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crew_Exploration_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_designs">Original designs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crew_Exploration_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Original designs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lockheed\'s proposed craft was a small <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> shaped <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a> design big enough for six astronauts and their equipment. Its airplane-shaped design made it easier to navigate during high-speed returns to Earth than the capsule-shaped vehicles of the past, according to Lockheed Martin. According to the French daily <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Figaro" title="Le Figaro">Le Figaro</a></i> and the publication <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviation_Week_and_Space_Technology" title="Aviation Week and Space Technology">Aviation Week and Space Technology</a></i>, EADS SPACE Transportation would be in charge of the design and construction of the associated <i>Mission Module</i> (MM). The head of the Lockheed team was <a href="/wiki/Cleon_Lacefield" title="Cleon Lacefield">Cleon Lacefield</a>. \n</p><p>The Lockheed Martin CEV design included several modules in the LEO (low earth orbit) and manned lunar versions of the spacecraft, plus an abort system. The abort system was an escape tower like that used in the Mercury, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a>, Soyuz, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shenzhou_spacecraft" title="Shenzhou spacecraft">Shenzhou</a> craft (Gemini, along with the Space Shuttles <i>Enterprise</i> and <i>Columbia</i> [until <a href="/wiki/STS-4" title="STS-4">STS-4</a>] used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ejection_seats" title="Ejection seats">ejection seats</a>). It would be capable of an abort during any part of the ascent phase of the mission. The crew would sit in the Rescue Module (RM) during launch. According to the publication <i>Aviation Week and Space Technology</i>, the RM would have an outer heat shield of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reinforced_carbon-carbon" title="Reinforced carbon-carbon">reinforced carbon-carbon</a> and a redundant layer of felt reusable surface insulation underneath in case of RCC failure. The RM comprised the top half of the Crew Module (CM), which comprised the RM and the rest of the lifting-body structure. The CM included living space for four crew members. In an emergency the RM separates from the rest of the CM. The RM would seat up to six crew members, with two to a row, and the CM has living space and provisions for four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronauts" title="Astronauts">astronauts</a> for 5&#8211;7 days. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">Extra-Vehicular Activities</a> (EVAs) could be conducted from the CM, which could land on land or water and could be reused 5&#8211;10 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mission module would be added to the bottom of the CEV for a lunar mission, and would be able to hold extra consumables and provide extra space for a mission of lunar duration. It would also provide extra power and communications capabilities, and include a docking port for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Surface_Access_Module" title="Lunar Surface Access Module">Lunar Surface Access Module</a> (LSAM). On the bottom of the lunar CEV stack would be the Propulsion or Trans-Earth Injection Module (TEIM) which would provide for return to the Earth from the Moon. It would probably incorporate (according to Aviation Week) 2 Pratt &amp; Whitney RL-10 engines. Together, the RM/CM, MM, and TEIM made up the Lockheed Martin lunar stack. The original idea was to launch the CM, MM, and TEIM on three separate <a href="/wiki/Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle" title="Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle">Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicles</a> (EELVs), with one component in each launch. This vehicle would need additional modules to reach lunar orbit and to land on the Moon. However, this plan was to be altered according to the CFI (Call for Improvements), described below. \n</p><p>Unlike the well-publicized Lockheed Martin CEV design, virtually no information was publicly available on the Boeing/Northrop Grumman CEV design. However, it is instructive to note that most publicly released Boeing designs for the canceled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_Space_Plane" title="Orbital Space Plane">Orbital Space Plane</a> (OSP)  resembled the Apollo capsule.
