href="/wiki/File:WHAM_survey.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The distribution of <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">ionized hydrogen</a> (known by astronomers as H&#160;II from href="#CITEREFHaffnerReynoldsTufteMadsen2003">Haffner et al. 2003</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the <b>interstellar medium</b> (<b>ISM</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> and radiation that exists in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Star_system" title="Star system">star systems</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>. This matter includes <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular" title="Molecular">molecular</a> form, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>. It fills <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">interstellar space</a> and blends smoothly into the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Intergalactic_space" title="Outer space">intergalactic space</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> that occupies the same volume, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, is the <b>interstellar radiation field</b>.\n</p><p>The interstellar medium is composed of multiple phases, distinguished by whether matter is ionic, atomic, or molecular, and the temperature and density of the matter. The interstellar medium is composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> with trace amounts of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> comparatively to hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The thermal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressures" title="Pressures">pressures</a> of these phases are in rough equilibrium with one another. <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">Magnetic fields</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbulent" title="Turbulent">turbulent</a> motions also provide pressure in the ISM, and are typically more important <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamically</a> than the thermal pressure is.\n</p><p>In all phases, the interstellar medium is extremely tenuous by terrestrial standards. In cool, dense regions of the ISM, matter is primarily in molecular form, and reaches number <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">densities</a> of 10<sup>6</sup> molecules per cm<sup>3</sup> (1 million molecules per cm<sup>3</sup>). In hot, diffuse regions of the ISM, matter is primarily ionized, and the density may be as low as 10<sup>&#8722;4</sup> ions per cm<sup>3</sup>. Compare this with a number density of roughly 10<sup>19</sup> molecules per cm<sup>3</sup> for air at sea level, and 10<sup>10</sup> molecules per cm<sup>3</sup> (10 billion molecules per cm<sup>3</sup>) for a laboratory high-vacuum chamber. By <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, 99% of the ISM is gas in any form, and 1% is dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boulanger_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boulanger-2">[2]</a></sup> Of the gas in the ISM, by number 91% of atoms are <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and 8.9% are <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, with 0.1% being atoms of elements heavier than hydrogen or helium,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferriere2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferriere2001-3">[3]</a></sup> known as "<a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metals</a>" in astronomical parlance. By mass this amounts to 70% hydrogen, 28% helium, and 1.5% heavier elements. The hydrogen and helium are primarily a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_nucleosynthesis" title="Primordial nucleosynthesis">primordial nucleosynthesis</a>, while the heavier elements in the ISM are mostly a result of enrichment in the process of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a>.\n</p><p>The ISM plays a crucial role in <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> precisely because of its intermediate role between stellar and galactic scales. Stars form within the densest regions of the ISM, which ultimately contributes to <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular clouds</a> and replenishes the ISM with matter and energy through <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebulae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">stellar winds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>. This interplay between stars and the ISM helps determine the rate at which a galaxy depletes its gaseous content, and therefore its lifespan of active star formation.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> reached the ISM on August 25, 2012, making it the first artificial object from Earth to do so. Interstellar plasma and dust will be studied until the mission\'s end in 2025. Its twin, <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> entered the ISM in November 2018.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The three-phase model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="#CITEREFFieldGoldsmithHabing1969">Field, Goldsmith &amp; Habing (1969)</a> put forward the static two <i>phase</i> equilibrium model to explain the observed properties of the ISM. Their modeled ISM consisted of a cold dense phase (<i>T</i>&#160;&lt;&#160;300&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>), consisting of clouds of neutral and molecular hydrogen, and a warm intercloud phase (<i>T</i>&#160;~&#160;10<sup>4</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>), consisting of rarefied neutral and ionized gas. <a href="#CITEREFMcKeeOstriker1977">McKee &amp; Ostriker (1977)</a> added a dynamic third phase that represented the very hot (<i>T</i>&#160;~&#160;10<sup>6</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) gas which had been shock heated by <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> and constituted most of the volume of the ISM.\nThese phases are the temperatures where heating and cooling can reach a stable equilibrium. Their paper formed the basis for further study over the past three decades. However, the relative proportions of the phases and their subdivisions are still not well known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferriere2001_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ferriere2001-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_atomic_hydrogen_model">The atomic hydrogen model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The atomic hydrogen model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This model takes into account only atomic hydrogen&#160;: Temperature larger than 3000 K breaks molecules, lower than 50 000 K leaves atoms in their ground state. It is assumed that influence of other atoms (He ...) is negligible.  Pressure is assumed very low, so that durations of free paths of atoms are larger than the ~ 1 nanosecond duration of light pulses which make ordinary, temporally incoherent light.\n</p><p>In this collisionless gas, Einstein&#8217;s theory of coherent light-matter interactions applies, all gas-light interactions are spatially coherent. \nSuppose that a monochromatic light is pulsed, then scattered by molecules having a quadrupole (Raman) resonance frequency. If &#8220;length of light pulses is shorter than all involved time constants&#8221; (Lamb (1971)), an &#8220;impulsive stimulated Raman scattering (ISRS) &#8221; (Yan, Gamble &amp; Nelson (1985)) works: While light generated by incoherent Raman at a shifted frequency has a phase independent on phase of exciting light, thus generates a new spectral line, coherence between incident and scattered light allows their interference into a single frequency, thus shifts incident frequency.\nAssume that a star radiates a continuous light spectrum up to X rays. Lyman frequencies are absorbed in this light and pump atoms mainly to first excited state. In this state, hyperfine periods are longer than 1 ns, so that an ISRS &#8220;may&#8221; redshift light frequency, populating high hyperfine levels. An other ISRS &#8220;may&#8221; transfer energy from hyperfine levels to thermal electromagnetic waves, so that redshift is permanent. Temperature of a light beam is defined from frequency and spectral radiance by Planck&#8217;s formula. As entropy must increase, &#8220;may&#8221; becomes &#8220;does&#8221;. \nHowever, where a previously absorbed line (first Lyman beta, ...) reaches Lyman alpha frequency, redshifting process stops and all hydrogen lines are strongly absorbed. But the stop is not perfect if there is energy at frequency shifted to Lyman beta frequency, which produces a slow redshift. Successive redshifts separated by Lyman absorptions generate many absorption lines, frequencies of which, deduced from absorption process, obey a law more dependable than Karlsson&#8217;s formula.\n</p><p>The previous process excites more and more atoms because a de-excitation obeys Einstein&#8217;s law of coherent interactions: Variation dI of radiance I of a light beam along a path dx is dI=BIdx,  where B is Einstein amplification coefficient which depends on medium.  I is the modulus of Poynting vector of field, absorption occurs for an opposed vector, which corresponds to a change of sign of B. Factor I in this formula shows that intense rays are more amplified than weak ones (competition of modes). Emission of a flare requires a sufficient radiance I provided by random zero point field. After emission of a flare, weak B increases by pumping while I remains close to zero: De-excitation by a coherent emission involves stochastic parameters of zero point field, as observed close to quasars (and in polar auroras).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structures">Structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2700" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg/220px-Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg/330px-Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg/440px-Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Three-dim-pillars-creation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three-dimensional structure in <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Creation" title="Pillars of Creation">Pillars of Creation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The ISM is <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent</a> and therefore full of structure on all spatial scales. <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">Stars are born</a> deep inside large complexes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_clouds" title="Molecular clouds">molecular clouds</a>, typically a few <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> in size. During their lives and deaths, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> interact physically with the ISM.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">Stellar winds</a> from young clusters of stars (often with giant or supergiant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HII_region" title="HII region">HII regions</a> surrounding them) and <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> inject enormous amounts of energy into their surroundings, which leads to hypersonic turbulence. The resultant structures &#8211; of varying sizes &#8211; can be observed, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_wind_bubble" title="Stellar wind bubble">stellar wind bubbles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superbubble" title="Superbubble">superbubbles</a> of hot gas, seen by X-ray satellite telescopes or turbulent flows observed in <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> maps.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is currently traveling through the <a href="/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud" title="Local Interstellar Cloud">Local Interstellar Cloud</a>, a denser region in the low-density <a href="/wiki/Local_Bubble" title="Local Bubble">Local Bubble</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" title="Interplanetary medium">interplanetary medium</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> ends. The <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> slows to <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">subsonic</a> velocities at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Termination_shock" title="Termination shock">termination shock</a>, 90&#8212;100 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. In the region beyond the termination shock, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliosheath" title="Heliosheath">heliosheath</a>, interstellar matter interacts with the solar wind. <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a>, the farthest human-made object from the Earth (after 1998<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>), crossed the termination shock December 16, 2004 and later entered interstellar space when it crossed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a> on August 25, 2012, providing the first direct probe of conditions in the ISM (<a href="#CITEREFStoneCummingsMcDonaldHeikkila2005">Stone et al. 2005</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstellar_extinction">Interstellar extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interstellar extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ISM is also responsible for <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">extinction</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_reddening" title="Interstellar reddening">reddening</a>, the decreasing <a href="/wiki/Radiance" title="Radiance">light intensity</a> and shift in the dominant observable <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of light from a star. These effects are caused by scattering and absorption of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> and allow the ISM to be observed with the naked eye in a dark sky. The apparent rifts that can be seen in the band of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> &#8211; a uniform disk of stars &#8211; are caused by absorption of background starlight by molecular clouds within a few thousand light years from Earth.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_ultraviolet" title="Far ultraviolet">Far ultraviolet light</a> is absorbed effectively by the neutral components of the ISM. For example, a typical absorption wavelength of atomic <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> lies at about 121.5 nanometers, the <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman-alpha</a> transition. Therefore, it is nearly impossible to see light emitted at that wavelength from a star farther than a few hundred light years from Earth, because most of it is absorbed during the trip to Earth by intervening neutral hydrogen.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_and_cooling">Heating and cooling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heating and cooling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The ISM is usually far from <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a>. Collisions establish a <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell&#8211;Boltzmann distribution">Maxwell&#8211;Boltzmann distribution</a> of velocities, and the \'temperature\' normally used to describe interstellar gas is the \'kinetic temperature\', which describes the temperature at which the particles would have the observed Maxwell&#8211;Boltzmann velocity distribution in thermodynamic equilibrium. However, the interstellar radiation field is typically much weaker than a medium in thermodynamic equilibrium; it is most often roughly that of an <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification#Class_A" title="Stellar classification">A star</a> (surface temperature of ~10,000 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) highly diluted. Therefore, <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">bound levels</a> within an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> in the ISM are rarely populated according to the Boltzmann formula (<a href="#CITEREFSpitzer1978">Spitzer 1978</a>, &#167; 2.4).\n</p><p>Depending on the temperature, density, and ionization state of a portion of the ISM, different heating and cooling mechanisms determine the temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_mechanisms">Heating mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heating mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dt>Heating by low-energy <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a></dt>\n<dd>The first mechanism proposed for heating the ISM was heating by low-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic rays</a> are an efficient heating source able to penetrate in the depths of molecular clouds. <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">Cosmic rays</a> transfer energy to <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> through both ionization and excitation and to free <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> through <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">Coulomb</a> interactions. Low-energy <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> (a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MeV" title="MeV">MeV</a>) are more important because they are far more numerous than high-energy <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Photoelectric heating by grains</dt>\n<dd>The <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation emitted by hot <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> can remove <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> from dust grains. The <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> is absorbed by the dust grain, and some of its energy is used to overcome the potential energy barrier and remove the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> from the grain. This potential barrier is due to the binding energy of the electron (the <a href="/wiki/Work_function" title="Work function">work function</a>) and the charge of the grain. The remainder of the photon\'s energy gives the ejected <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> which heats the gas through collisions with other particles. A typical size distribution of dust grains is <i>n</i>(<i>r</i>)&#160;&#8733;&#160;<i>r</i><sup>&#8722;3.5</sup>, where <i>r</i> is the radius of the dust particle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. Assuming this, the projected grain surface area distribution is <i>&#960;r</i><sup>2</sup><i>n</i>(<i>r</i>)&#160;&#8733;&#160;<i>r</i><sup>&#8722;1.5</sup>. This indicates that the smallest dust grains dominate this method of heating<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Photoionization</dt>\n<dd>When an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> is freed from an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> (typically from absorption of a UV <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>) it carries kinetic energy away of the order <i>E</i><sub>photon</sub>&#160;&#8722;&#160;<i>E</i><sub>ionization</sub>. This heating mechanism dominates in H&#160;II regions, but is negligible in the diffuse ISM due to the relative lack of neutral <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> heating</dt>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> remove <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> from <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, and those photoelectrons can provoke secondary ionizations. As the intensity is often low, this heating is only efficient in warm, less dense atomic medium (as the column density is small). For example, in molecular clouds only hard <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-rays</a> can penetrate and <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-ray</a> heating can be ignored. This is assuming the region is not near an <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-ray</a> source such as a <a href="/wiki/Supernova_remnant" title="Supernova remnant">supernova remnant</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Chemical heating</dt>\n<dd>Molecular <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) can be formed on the surface of dust grains when two <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a> atoms (which can travel over the grain) meet. This process yields 4.48&#160;eV of energy distributed over the rotational and vibrational modes, kinetic energy of the H<sub>2</sub> molecule, as well as heating the dust grain. This kinetic energy, as well as the energy transferred from de-excitation of the hydrogen molecule through collisions, heats the gas.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Grain-gas heating</dt>\n<dd>Collisions at high densities between gas atoms and molecules with dust grains can transfer thermal energy. This is not important in HII regions because UV radiation is more important. It is also not important in diffuse ionized medium due to the low density. In the neutral diffuse medium grains are always colder, but do not effectively cool the gas due to the low densities.</dd></dl>\n<p>Grain heating by thermal exchange is very important in supernova remnants where densities and temperatures are very high.\n</p><p>Gas heating via grain-gas collisions is dominant deep in giant molecular clouds (especially at high densities). Far <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation penetrates deeply due to the low optical depth. Dust grains are heated via this radiation and can transfer thermal energy during collisions with the gas. A measure of efficiency in the heating is given by the accommodation coefficient:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\alpha ={\\frac {T_{2}-T}{T_{d}-T}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>&#945;<!-- &#945; --></mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mrow>\n<msub>\n<mi>T</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mi>T</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mrow>\n<msub>\n<mi>T</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>d</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mi>T</mi>\n</mrow>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\alpha ={\\frac {T_{2}-T}{T_{d}-T}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\alpha = \\frac{T_2 - T}{T_d - T}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c25bc203de2eccdc32ac9d7190071f8f5dd76c01" style="vertical-align: -2.171ex; width:12.348ex; height:5.509ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where <i>T</i> is the gas temperature, <i>T<sub>d</sub></i> the dust temperature, and <i>T</i><sub>2</sub> the post-collision temperature of the gas atom or molecule. This coefficient was measured by (<a href="#CITEREFBurkeHollenbach1983">Burke &amp; Hollenbach 1983</a>) as <i>&#945;</i>&#160;=&#160;0.35.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Other heating mechanisms</dt>\n<dd>A variety of macroscopic heating mechanisms are present including:\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">Gravitational collapse</a> of a cloud</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">Supernova</a> explosions</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">Stellar winds</a></li>\n<li>Expansion of <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">H&#160;II regions</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic" title="Magnetohydrodynamic">Magnetohydrodynamic</a> waves created by supernova remnants</li></ul></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooling_mechanisms">Cooling mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cooling mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dt>Fine structure cooling</dt>\n<dd>The process of fine structure cooling is dominant in most regions of the Interstellar Medium, except regions of hot <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> and regions deep in molecular clouds. It occurs most efficiently with abundant <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> having fine structure levels close to the fundamental level such as: C&#160;II and O&#160;I in the neutral medium and O&#160;II, O&#160;III, N&#160;II, N&#160;III, Ne&#160;II and Ne&#160;III in H&#160;II regions. Collisions will excite these atoms to higher levels, and they will eventually de-excite through photon emission, which will carry the energy out of the region.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>Cooling by permitted lines</dt>\n<dd>At lower temperatures, more levels than fine structure levels can be populated via collisions. For example, collisional excitation of the <i>n</i>&#160;=&#160;2 level of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> will release a Ly-&#945; photon upon de-excitation. In molecular clouds, excitation of rotational lines of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a> is important. Once a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> is excited, it eventually returns to a lower energy state, emitting a photon which can leave the region, cooling the cloud.</dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiowave_propagation">Radiowave propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Radiowave propagation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Micrwavattrp.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="695" decoding="async" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Micrwavattrp.png/400px-Micrwavattrp.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Micrwavattrp.png/600px-Micrwavattrp.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Micrwavattrp.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Micrwavattrp.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric attenuation in <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a>/km as a function of frequency over the EHF band. Peaks in absorption at specific frequencies are a problem, due to atmosphere constituents such as water vapor (H<sub>2</sub>O) and carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>).</div></div></div>\n<p>Radio waves from &#8776;10&#160;kHz (<a href="/wiki/Very_low_frequency" title="Very low frequency">very low frequency</a>) to &#8776;300&#160;GHz (<a href="/wiki/Extremely_high_frequency" title="Extremely high frequency">extremely high frequency</a>) propagate differently in interstellar space than on the Earth\'s surface. There are many sources of interference and signal distortion that do not exist on Earth. A great deal of <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> depends on compensating for the different propagation effects to uncover the desired signal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_knowledge_of_interstellar_space">History of knowledge of interstellar space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History of knowledge of interstellar space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Herbig-Haro_110_(captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope).tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3052" data-file-width="3883" decoding="async" height="173" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Herbig-Haro_110_%28captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope%29.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Herbig-Haro_110_%28captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope%29.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Herbig-Haro_110_(captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope).tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Herbig%E2%80%93Haro_110&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Herbig&#8211;Haro 110 (page does not exist)">Herbig&#8211;Haro 110</a> object ejects gas through interstellar space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The nature of the interstellar medium has received the attention of astronomers and scientists over the centuries, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_the_interstellar_and_intergalactic_medium" title="Timeline of knowledge about the interstellar and intergalactic medium">understanding of the ISM has developed</a>. However, they first had to acknowledge the basic concept of "interstellar" space. The term appears to have been first used in print by <a href="#CITEREFBacon1626">Bacon (1626</a>, &#167; 354&#8211;5): "The Interstellar Skie.. hath .. so much Affinity with the Starre, that there is a Rotation of that, as well as of the Starre." Later, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_philosopher" title="Natural philosopher">natural philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a>&#160;(<a href="#CITEREFBoyle1674">1674</a>) discussed "The inter-stellar part of heaven, which several of the modern <a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureans</a> would have to be empty."\n</p><p>Before modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_theory" title="Electromagnetic theory">electromagnetic theory</a>, early <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> postulated that an invisible <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a> existed as a medium to carry lightwaves. It was assumed that this aether extended into interstellar space, as <a href="#CITEREFPatterson1862">Patterson (1862)</a> wrote, "this efflux occasions a thrill, or vibratory motion, in the <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">ether</a> which fills the interstellar spaces."\n</p><p>The advent of deep photographic imaging allowed <a href="/wiki/Edward_Emerson_Barnard" title="Edward Emerson Barnard">Edward Barnard</a> to produce the first images of <a href="/wiki/Dark_nebula" title="Dark nebula">dark nebulae</a> silhouetted against the background star field of the galaxy, while the first actual detection of cold diffuse matter in interstellar space was made by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Franz_Hartmann" title="Johannes Franz Hartmann">Johannes Hartmann</a> in 1904<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> through the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_spectrum" title="Absorption spectrum">absorption line spectroscopy</a>. In his historic study of the spectrum and orbit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_Orionis" title="Delta Orionis">Delta Orionis</a>, Hartmann observed the light coming from this star and realized that some of this light was being absorbed before it reached the Earth. Hartmann reported that absorption from the "K" line of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> appeared "extraordinarily weak, but almost perfectly sharp" and also reported the "quite surprising result that the calcium line at 393.4&#160;nanometres does not share in the periodic displacements of the lines caused by the orbital motion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic binary</a> star". The stationary nature of the line led Hartmann to conclude that the gas responsible for the absorption was not present in the atmosphere of Delta Orionis, but was instead located within an isolated cloud of matter residing somewhere along the line-of-sight to this star. This discovery launched the study of the Interstellar Medium.\n</p><p>In the series of investigations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viktor_Ambartsumian" title="Viktor Ambartsumian">Viktor Ambartsumian</a> introduced the now commonly accepted notion that interstellar matter occurs in the form of clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following Hartmann\'s identification of interstellar calcium absorption, interstellar <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> was detected by <a href="#CITEREFHeger1919">Heger (1919)</a> through the observation of stationary absorption from the atom\'s "D" lines at 589.0 and 589.6 nanometres towards Delta Orionis and <a href="/wiki/Beta_Scorpii" title="Beta Scorpii">Beta Scorpii</a>.\n</p><p>Subsequent observations of the "H" and "K" lines of calcium by <a href="#CITEREFBeals1936">Beals (1936)</a> revealed double and asymmetric profiles in the spectra of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epsilon_Orionis" title="Epsilon Orionis">Epsilon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeta_Orionis" title="Zeta Orionis">Zeta Orionis</a>. These were the first steps in the study of the very complex interstellar sightline towards <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>. Asymmetric absorption line profiles are the result of the superposition of multiple absorption lines, each corresponding to the same atomic transition (for example the "K" line of calcium), but occurring in interstellar clouds with different <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocities</a>. Because each cloud has a different velocity (either towards or away from the observer/Earth) the absorption lines occurring within each cloud are either <a href="/wiki/Blueshift" title="Blueshift">Blue-shifted</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift" title="Red shift">Red-shifted</a> (respectively) from the lines\' rest wavelength, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_Effect" title="Doppler Effect">Doppler Effect</a>. These observations confirming that matter is not distributed homogeneously were the first evidence of multiple discrete clouds within the ISM.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="824" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="142" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg/330px-Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg/440px-Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_sees_a_cosmic_caterpillar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This light-year-long knot of interstellar gas and dust resembles a <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The growing evidence for interstellar material led <a href="#CITEREFPickering1912">Pickering (1912)</a> to comment that "While the interstellar absorbing medium may be simply the ether, yet the character of its selective absorption, as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Kapteyn" title="Jacobus Kapteyn">Kapteyn</a>, is characteristic of a gas, and free gaseous <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> are certainly there, since they are probably constantly being expelled by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>."\n</p><p>The same year <a href="/wiki/Victor_Francis_Hess" title="Victor Francis Hess">Victor Hess</a>\'s discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, highly energetic charged particles that rain onto the Earth from space, led others to speculate whether they also pervaded interstellar space. The following year the Norwegian explorer and physicist <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a> wrote: "It seems to be a natural consequence of our points of view to assume that the whole of space is filled with electrons and flying electric <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> of all kinds. We have assumed that each stellar system in evolutions throws off electric corpuscles into space. It does not seem unreasonable therefore to think that the greater part of the material masses in the universe is found, not in the solar systems or <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>, but in \'empty\' space" (<a href="#CITEREFBirkeland1913">Birkeland 1913</a>).\n</p><p><a href="#CITEREFThorndike1930">Thorndike (1930)</a> noted that "it could scarcely have been believed that the enormous gaps between the stars are completely void. Terrestrial aurorae are not improbably excited by charged particles emitted by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. If the millions of other <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> are also ejecting <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, as is undoubtedly true, no absolute vacuum can exist within the galaxy."\n</p><p>In September 2012, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA scientists</a> reported that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)</a>, subjected to <i>interstellar medium (ISM)</i> conditions, are transformed, through <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygenate" title="Oxygenate">oxygenation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylation" title="Hydroxylation">hydroxylation</a>, to more complex <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organics</a> &#8211; "a step along the path toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleotides" title="Nucleotides">nucleotides</a>, the raw materials of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, respectively".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20120920_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space-20120920-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJL-20120901_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AJL-20120901-15">[15]</a></sup> Further, as a result of these transformations, the PAHs lose their <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic signature</a> which could be one of the reasons "for the lack of PAH detection in <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_ice" title="Interstellar ice">interstellar ice</a> <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust#Dust_grain_formation" title="Cosmic dust">grains</a>, particularly the outer regions of cold, dense clouds or the upper molecular layers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disks" title="Protoplanetary disks">protoplanetary disks</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20120920_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Space-20120920-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AJL-20120901_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-AJL-20120901-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2014, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> announced a <a class="external text" href="http://www.astrochem.org/pahdb/" rel="nofollow">greatly upgraded database</a> for tracking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs) in the universe. According to scientists, more than 20% of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> in the universe may be associated with PAHs, possible <a href="/wiki/PAH_world_hypothesis" title="PAH world hypothesis">starting materials</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis#PAH_world_hypothesis" title="Abiogenesis">formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Life#Extraterrestrial_life" title="Life">life</a>. PAHs seem to have been formed shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, are widespread throughout the universe, and are associated with <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">new stars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exoplanets" title="Exoplanets">exoplanets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140221_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140221-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
