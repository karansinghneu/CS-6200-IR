Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Beam-powered propulsion</b>, also known as directed energy propulsion, is a class of <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft propulsion</a> that uses energy beamed to the spacecraft from a remote power plant to provide energy. The beam is typically either a <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> beam and it is either pulsed or continuous.  A continuous beam lends itself to <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">thermal rockets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photonic_laser_thruster" title="Photonic laser thruster">photonic thrusters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_sail" title="Light sail">light sails</a>, whereas a pulsed beam lends itself to ablative thrusters and <a href="/wiki/Pulse_detonation_engine" title="Pulse detonation engine">pulse detonation engines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rule of thumb that is usually quoted is that it takes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MegaWatt" title="MegaWatt">megawatt</a> of power beamed to a vehicle per kg of payload while it is being accelerated to permit it to reach low earth orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other than launching to orbit, applications for moving around the world quickly have also been proposed.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Rockets are <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> machines; they use mass ejected from the rocket to provide momentum to the rocket. Momentum is the product of mass and velocity, so rockets generally attempt to put as much velocity into their <a href="/wiki/Working_mass" title="Working mass">working mass</a> as possible, thereby minimizing the amount of working mass that is needed. In order to accelerate the working mass, <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> is required. In a conventional rocket, the fuel is chemically combined to provide the energy, and the resulting fuel products, the ash or exhaust, are used as the working mass.\n</p><p>There is no particular reason why the same fuel has to be used for both energy and momentum. In the <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a>, for instance, the fuel is used only to produce energy, the working mass is provided from the air that the jet aircraft flies through. In modern jet engines, the amount of air propelled is much greater than the amount of air used for energy. This is not a solution for the rocket, however, as they quickly climb to altitudes where the air is too thin to be useful as a source of working mass.\n</p><p>Rockets can, however, carry their working mass and use some other source of energy. The problem is finding an energy source with a <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">power-to-weight ratio</a> that competes with chemical fuels. Small <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> can compete in this regard, and considerable work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_thermal_propulsion" title="Nuclear thermal propulsion">nuclear thermal propulsion</a> was carried out in the 1960s, but environmental concerns and rising costs led to the ending of most of these programs.\n</p><p>A further improvement can be made by removing the energy creation from the spacecraft. If the nuclear reactor is left on the ground and its energy transmitted to the spacecraft, the weight of the reactor is removed as well. The issue then is to get the energy into the spacecraft. This is the idea behind beamed power.\n</p><p>With beamed propulsion one can leave the power-source stationary on the ground, and directly (or via a heat exchanger) heat propellant on the spacecraft with a <a href="/wiki/Maser" title="Maser">maser</a> or a laser beam from a fixed installation.  This permits the spacecraft to leave its power-source at home, saving significant amounts of mass, greatly improving performance.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laser_propulsion">Laser propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Laser propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laser_propulsion" title="Laser propulsion">Laser propulsion</a></div>\n<p>Since a laser can heat propellant to extremely high temperatures, this potentially greatly improves the efficiency of a rocket, as exhaust velocity is proportional to the square root of the temperature. Normal chemical rockets have an exhaust speed limited by the fixed amount of energy in the propellants, but beamed propulsion systems have no particular theoretical limit (although in practice there are temperature limits).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_propulsion">Microwave propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Microwave propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In microwave thermal propulsion, an external microwave beam is used to heat a refractory heat exchanger to &gt;1,500 K, in turn heating a propellant such as hydrogen, methane or ammonia. This improves the specific impulse and thrust/weight ratio of the propulsion system relative to conventional rocket propulsion. For example, hydrogen can provide a specific impulse of 700&#8211;900 seconds and a thrust/weight ratio of 50-150.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-parkin-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A variation, developed by brothers <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Benford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Benford (page does not exist)">James Benford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Benford" title="Gregory Benford">Gregory Benford</a>, is to use thermal <a href="/wiki/Desorption" title="Desorption">desorption</a> of propellant trapped in the material of a very large <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">microwave sail</a>. This produces a very high acceleration compared to microwave pushed sails alone.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_propulsion">Electric propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electric propulsion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some proposed spacecraft propulsion mechanisms use power in the form of electricity.  Usually these schemes assume either solar panels, or an on-board reactor. However, both power sources are heavy.\n</p><p>Beamed propulsion in the form of laser can be used to send power to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_panel" title="Photovoltaic panel">photovoltaic panel</a>, for <i>Laser electric propulsion.</i>  In this system, careful design of the panels is necessary as the extra power tends to cause a fall-off of the conversion efficiency due to heating effects.\n</p><p>A microwave beam could be used to send power to a <a href="/wiki/Rectenna" title="Rectenna">rectenna</a>, for <i>microwave electric propulsion</i>.  <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">Microwave</a> broadcast power has been practically demonstrated several times (e.g. Goldstone, California in 1974), rectennas are potentially lightweight and can handle high power at high conversion efficiency.  However, rectennas tend to need to be very large for a significant amount of power to be captured.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_impulse">Direct impulse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Direct impulse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A beam could also be used to provide impulse by directly "pushing" on the sail.\n</p><p>One example of this would be using a <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">solar sail</a> to reflect a laser beam.  This concept, called a <i>laser-pushed lightsail,</i> was initially proposed by Marx<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but first analyzed in detail, and elaborated on, by physicist <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Forward" title="Robert L. Forward">Robert L. Forward</a> in 1989<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forward1989_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forward1989-5">[5]</a></sup> as a method of <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">Interstellar travel</a> that would avoid extremely high <a href="/wiki/Mass_ratio" title="Mass ratio">mass ratios</a> by not carrying fuel.  Further analysis of the concept was done by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_A._Landis" title="Geoffrey A. Landis">Landis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1989_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-landis1989-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis_1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis_1997-7">[7]</a></sup> Mallove and Matloff,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dana_Andrews" title="Dana Andrews">Andrews</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and others.\n</p><p>In a later paper, Forward proposed pushing a sail with a microwave beam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forward1985_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-forward1985-10">[10]</a></sup>  This has the advantage that the sail need not be a continuous surface.  Forward tagged his proposal for an ultralight sail "<a href="/wiki/Starwisp" title="Starwisp">Starwisp</a>".  A later analysis by Landis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> suggested that the Starwisp concept as originally proposed by Forward would not work, but variations on the proposal might be implemented.\n</p><p>The beam has to have a large diameter so that only a small portion of the beam misses the sail due to <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> and the laser or microwave antenna has to have a good pointing stability so that the craft can tilt its sails fast enough to follow the center of the beam. This gets more important when going from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_travel" title="Interplanetary travel">interplanetary travel</a> to <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_travel" title="Interstellar travel">interstellar travel</a>, and when going from a fly-by mission, to a landing mission, to a return mission. The laser or the microwave sender would probably be a large <a href="/wiki/Phased_array" title="Phased array">phased array</a> of small devices, which get their energy directly from solar radiation. The size of the array negates the need for a lens or mirror.\n</p><p>Another beam-pushed concept would be to use a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_sail" title="Magnetic sail">magnetic sail</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-magnetospheric_plasma_propulsion" title="Mini-magnetospheric plasma propulsion">MMPP sail</a> to divert a beam of charged particles from a <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerator</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  Landis proposed a particle beam pushed sail in 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1989_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-landis1989-6">[6]</a></sup> and analyzed in more detail in a 2004 paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jordin_Kare" title="Jordin Kare">Jordin Kare</a> has proposed a variant to this whereby a "beam" of small laser accelerated light sails would transfer momentum to a magsail vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another beam-pushed concept uses pellets or projectiles of ordinary matter. A stream of pellets from a stationary mass-driver is "reflected" by the spacecraft, cf. <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver#Hybrid_mass_drivers" title="Mass driver">mass driver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The spacecraft neither needs energy nor reaction mass for propulsion of its own.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_systems">Proposed systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Proposed systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lightcraft">Lightcraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lightcraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lightcraft" title="Lightcraft">lightcraft</a></div>\n<p>A <b>lightcraft</b> is a vehicle currently under development that uses an external pulsed source of laser or maser energy to provide power for producing thrust.\n</p><p>The laser shines on a parabolic reflector on the underside of the vehicle that concentrates the light to produce a region of extremely high temperature. The air in this region is heated and expands violently, producing thrust with each pulse of laser light. In space, a lightcraft would need to provide this gas itself from onboard tanks or from an ablative solid. By leaving the vehicle\'s power source on the ground and by using ambient atmosphere as reaction mass for much of its ascent, a lightcraft would be capable of delivering a very large percentage of its launch mass to orbit. It could also potentially be very cheap to manufacture.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Early in the morning of 2 October 2000 at the High Energy Laser Systems Test Facility (HELSTF), Lightcraft Technologies, Inc. (LTI) with the help of Franklin B. Mead of the U.S. Air Force Research Laboratory and <a href="/wiki/Leik_Myrabo" title="Leik Myrabo">Leik Myrabo</a> set a new world\'s altitude record of 233 feet (71 m) for its 4.8&#160;inch (12.2&#160;cm) diameter, 1.8-ounce (51&#160;g), laser-boosted rocket in a flight lasting 12.7 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Although much of the 8:35 am flight was spent hovering at 230+ feet, the Lightcraft earned a world record for the longest ever laser-powered free flight and the greatest "air time" (i.e., launch-to-landing/recovery) from a light-propelled object. This is comparable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Goddard_(scientist)" title="Robert Goddard (scientist)">Robert Goddard</a>\'s first test flight of his rocket design. Increasing the laser power to 100 kilowatts will enable flights up to a 30-kilometer altitude. Their goal is to accelerate a one-kilogram microsatellite into <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> using a custom-built, one megawatt ground-based laser. Such a system would use just about 20 dollars\' worth of electricity, placing launch costs per kilogram to many times less than current launch costs (which are measured in thousands of dollars).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Myrabo\'s "<a href="/wiki/Lightcraft" title="Lightcraft">lightcraft</a>" design is a reflective funnel-shaped craft that channels heat from the laser, towards the center, using a reflective parabolic surface causing the laser to literally explode the air underneath it, generating lift. Reflective surfaces in the craft focus the beam into a ring, where it heats air to a temperature nearly five times hotter than the surface of the sun, causing the air to expand explosively for thrust.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laser_thermal_rocket">Laser thermal rocket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Laser thermal rocket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">Thermal rocket</a></div>\n<p>A laser thermal rocket is a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">thermal rocket</a> in which the propellant is heated by energy provided by an external laser beam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  \nIn 1992, the late <a href="/wiki/Jordin_Kare" title="Jordin Kare">Jordin Kare</a> proposed a simpler, nearer term concept which has a rocket containing liquid hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The propellant is heated in a heat exchanger that the laser beam shines on before leaving the vehicle via a conventional nozzle. This concept can use continuous beam lasers, and the semiconductor lasers are now cost effective for this application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_thermal_rocket">Microwave thermal rocket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Microwave thermal rocket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">Thermal rocket</a></div>\n<p>In 2002, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kevin_L._Parkin" title="Kevin L. Parkin">Kevin L.G. Parkin</a> proposed a similar system using microwaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkin_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-parkin-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  In May 2012, the DARPA/NASA Millimeter-wave Thermal Launch System (MTLS) Project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> began the first steps toward implementing this idea.  The MTLS Project was the first to demonstrate a millimeter-wave absorbent refractory heat exchanger, subsequently integrating it into the propulsion system of a small rocket to produce the first millimeter-wave thermal rocket. Simultaneously, it developed the first high power cooperative target millimeter-wave beam director and used it to attempt the first millimeter-wave thermal rocket launch.  Several launches were attempted but problems with the beam director could not be resolved before funding ran out in March 2014.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>\nMotivation to develop beam-powered propulsion systems comes from the economic advantages that would be gained as a result of improved propulsion performance.  In the case of beam-powered launch vehicles, better propulsion performance enables some combination of increased payload fraction, increased structural margins and fewer stages.  <a href="/wiki/JASON_(advisory_group)" title="JASON (advisory group)">JASON</a>\'s 1977 study of laser propulsion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> authored by <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a>, succinctly articulates the promise of beam-powered launch: </p><blockquote><p><i>"Laser propulsion as an idea that may produce a revolution in space technology.  A single laser facility on the ground can in theory launch single-stage vehicles into low or high earth orbit.  The payload can be 20% or 30% of the vehicle take-off weight.  It is far more economical in the use of mass and energy than chemical propulsion, and it is far more flexible in putting identical vehicles into a variety of orbits."</i></p></blockquote><p>This promise was quantified in a 1978 Lockheed Study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> conducted for NASA:</p><blockquote><p><i>"The results of the study showed that, with advanced technology, laser rocket system with either a space- or ground-based laser transmitter could reduce the national budget allocated to space transportation by 10 to 345 billion dollars over a 10-year life cycle when compared to advanced chemical propulsion systems (LO<sub>2</sub>-LH<sub>2</sub>) of equal capability."</i></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beam_director_cost">Beam director cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Beam director cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 1970s-era studies and others since have cited beam director cost as a possible impediment to beam-powered launch systems.  A recent cost-benefit analysis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> estimates that microwave (or laser) thermal rockets would be economical once beam director cost falls below 20 $/Watt.   The current cost of suitable lasers is &lt;100 $/Watt and the current cost of suitable microwave sources is &lt;$5/Watt.  Mass production has lowered the production cost of microwave oven magnetrons to &lt;0.01 $/Watt and some medical lasers to &lt;10 $/Watt, though these are thought to be unsuitable for use in beam directors.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-spacecraft_applications">Non-spacecraft applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beam-powered_propulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Non-spacecraft applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1964 <a href="/wiki/William_C._Brown" title="William C. Brown">William C. Brown</a> demonstrated a miniature <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> equipped with a combination <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antenna_(electronics)" title="Antenna (electronics)">antenna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">rectifier</a> device called a <a href="/wiki/Rectenna" title="Rectenna">rectenna</a>. The rectenna converted microwave power into electricity, allowing the helicopter to fly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2002 a Japanese group propelled a tiny aluminium airplane by using a laser to vaporize a water droplet clinging to it, and in 2003 NASA researchers flew an 11-ounce (312 g) model airplane with a propeller powered with solar panels illuminated by a laser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It is possible that such beam-powered propulsion could be useful for long-duration high altitude unmanned aircraft or balloons, perhaps designed to serve &#8211; like satellites do today &#8211; as communication relays, science platforms, or surveillance platforms.\n</p><p>A "<a href="/wiki/Laser_broom" title="Laser broom">laser broom</a>" has been proposed to sweep <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> from Earth orbit. This is another proposed use of beam-powered propulsion, used on objects that were not designed to be propelled by it, for example small pieces of scrap knocked off ("spalled") satellites. The technique works since the laser power ablates one side of the object, giving an impulse that changes the eccentricity of the object\'s orbit. The orbit would then intersect the atmosphere and burn up.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
