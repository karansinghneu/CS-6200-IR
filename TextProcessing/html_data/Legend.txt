href="/wiki/Folklore_genre" title="Folklore genre">genre of folklore</a> that consists of a <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> featuring human actions perceived or believed both by teller and listeners to have taken place within human history. Narratives in this genre may demonstrate human values, and possess certain qualities that give the tale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verisimilitude_(literature)" title="Verisimilitude (literature)">verisimilitude</a>. Legend, for its active and passive participants, includes no happenings that are outside the realm of "possibility," but may include <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a>. Legends may be transformed over time, in order to keep them fresh, vital, and <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realistic</a>. Many legends operate within the realm of uncertainty, never being entirely believed by the participants, but also never being resolutely doubted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Brothers Grimm</a> defined legend as <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folktale</a> historically grounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A modern <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklorist</a>\'s professional definition of <i>legend</i> was proposed by Timothy R. Tangherlini in 1990:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Legend, typically, is a short (mono-) episodic, traditional, highly ecotypified<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> historicized narrative performed in a conversational mode, reflecting on a psychological level a symbolic representation of folk belief and collective experiences and serving as a reaffirmation of commonly held values of the group to whose tradition it belongs.</p></blockquote>\n<div class="toc" tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Holger_danske.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="433" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Holger_danske.jpg/170px-Holger_danske.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Holger_danske.jpg/255px-Holger_danske.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Holger_danske.jpg/340px-Holger_danske.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Holger_danske.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holger_Danske" title="Holger Danske">Holger Danske</a>, a legendary character</div></div></div>\n<p><i>Legend</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a> from <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> that entered English usage circa 1340. The Old French noun <i>legende</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>legenda</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In its early English-language usage, the word indicated a narrative of an event. The word <i>legendary</i> was originally a noun (introduced in the 1510s) meaning a collection or corpus of legends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etymonline_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This word changed to <i>legendry</i>, and <i>legendary</i> became the adjectival form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etymonline_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 1613, English-speaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestants</a> began to use the word when they wished to imply that an event (especially the story of any <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> not acknowledged in <a href="/wiki/John_Foxe" title="John Foxe">John Foxe</a>\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actes_and_Monuments" title="Actes and Monuments">Actes and Monuments</a></i>) was fictitious. Thus, <i>legend</i> gained its modern connotations of "undocumented" and "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/spurious" title="wikt:spurious">spurious</a>", which distinguish it from the meaning of <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">chronicle</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1866, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Grimm" title="Jacob Grimm">Jacob Grimm</a> described the <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tale</a> as "poetic, legend historic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Early scholars such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karl_Wehrhan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karl Wehrhan (page does not exist)">Karl Wehrhan</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;[<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Wehrhan" title="de:Karl Wehrhan">de</a>]</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ranke" title="Friedrich Ranke">Friedrich Ranke</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Will_Erich_Peuckert" title="Will Erich Peuckert">Will Erich Peuckert</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> followed Grimm\'s example in focussing solely on the literary narrative, an approach that was enriched particularly after the 1960s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> by addressing questions of performance and the anthropological and psychological insights provided in considering legends\' social context. Questions of categorising legends, in hopes of compiling a content-based series of categories on the line of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aarne%E2%80%93Thompson" title="Aarne&#8211;Thompson">Aarne&#8211;Thompson</a> folktale index, provoked a search for a broader new synthesis.\n</p><p>In an early attempt at defining some basic questions operative in examining folk tales, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Ranke" title="Friedrich Ranke">Friedrich Ranke</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;[<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Ranke" title="de:Friedrich Ranke">de</a>]</span> in 1925<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> characterised the folk legend as "a popular narrative with an objectively untrue imaginary content"  a dismissive position that was subsequently largely abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Compared to the highly structured folktale, legend is comparatively amorphous, <a href="/wiki/Helmut_de_Boor" title="Helmut de Boor">Helmut de Boor</a> noted in 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The narrative content of legend is in realistic mode, rather than the wry <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a> of folktale;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Wilhelm Heiske<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> remarked on the similarity of <a href="/wiki/Motif_(folkloristics)" title="Motif (folkloristics)">motifs</a> in legend and folktale and concluded that, in spite of its realistic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_mode" title="Literary mode">mode</a>, legend is not more historical than folktale.\n</p><p>In <i>Einleitung in der Geschichtswissenschaft</i> (1928), <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Bernheim" title="Ernst Bernheim">Ernst Bernheim</a> asserted that a legend is simply a longstanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rumour" title="Rumour">rumour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Allport" title="Gordon Allport">Gordon Allport</a> credited the staying-power of some rumours to the persistent cultural state-of-mind that they embody and capsulise;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> thus "<a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">Urban legends</a>" are a feature of rumour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> When Willian Jansen suggested that legends that disappear quickly were "short-term legends" and the persistent ones be termed "long-term legends", the distinction between legend and rumour was effectively obliterated, Tangherlini concluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_legenda">Christian <i>legenda</i></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legendary_material_in_Christian_hagiography" title="Legendary material in Christian hagiography">Legendary material in Christian hagiography</a></div>\n<p>In the narrow Christian sense, <i>legenda</i> ("things to be read [on a certain day, in church]") were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hagiographical" title="Hagiographical">hagiographical</a> accounts, often collected in a legendary. Because saints\' lives are often included in many miracle stories, <i>legend</i>, in a wider sense, came to refer to any story that is set in a historical context but that contains <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>, divine or fantastic elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Delehaye">Hippolyte Delehaye</a> distinguished legend from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myth</a>: "The <i>legend</i>, on the other hand, has, of necessity, some historical or topographical connection. It refers imaginary events to some real personage, or it localizes romantic stories in some definite spot."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the moment a legend is retold as fiction, its authentic legendary qualities begin to fade and recede: in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Sleepy_Hollow" title="The Legend of Sleepy Hollow">The Legend of Sleepy Hollow</a></i>,  <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">Washington Irving</a> transformed a local Hudson River Valley legend into a literary anecdote with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_novel" title="Gothic novel">"Gothic" overtones</a>, which actually tended to diminish its character as genuine legend.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Stories that exceed the boundaries of "<a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">realism</a>" are called "<a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fables</a>". For example, the <a href="/wiki/Talking_animal" title="Talking animal">talking animal</a> formula of <a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">Aesop</a> identifies his brief stories as fables, not legends. The parable of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prodigal_Son" title="Prodigal Son">Prodigal Son</a> would be a legend if it were told as having actually happened to a specific son of a historical father. If it included a <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkey</a> that gave sage advice to the Prodigal Son it would be a fable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Legend may be transmitted orally, passed on person-to-person, or, in the original sense, through written text. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacob_de_Voragine" title="Jacob de Voragine">Jacob de Voragine</a>\'s <i>Legenda Aurea</i> or "The Golden Legend" comprises a series of <i>vitae</i> or instructive  biographical narratives, tied to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liturgical_calendar" title="Liturgical calendar">liturgical calendar</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. They are presented as lives of the saints, but the profusion of miraculous happenings and above all their uncritical context are characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a>. The <i>Legenda</i> was intended to inspire extemporized homilies and sermons appropriate to the <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> of the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_legend">Urban legend</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Union_Graveyard_II.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4912" data-file-width="7360" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Union_Graveyard_II.jpg/220px-Union_Graveyard_II.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Union_Graveyard_II.jpg/330px-Union_Graveyard_II.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Union_Graveyard_II.jpg/440px-Union_Graveyard_II.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Union_Graveyard_II.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The tale of the White Lady who haunts Union Cemetery is a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_hitchhiker" title="Vanishing hitchhiker">Vanishing hitchhiker</a> legend.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">Urban legend</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vanishing_hitchhiker" title="Vanishing hitchhiker">vanishing hitchhiker</a> is the best-known <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a> in America, traceable as far back as 1870, but it is found around the world including in Korea and Russia. In the legend, a young girl in a white dress picked up alongside of the road by a passerby. The unknown girl in white remains silent for the duration of her ride, thanks the driver, and quietly gets out at her destination. When the driver turns to look back, the girl has vanished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langlois,_Janet_L._27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langlois,_Janet_L.-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Georges_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Georges-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fine,_Gary_Alan_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fine,_Gary_Alan-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1942, Beardsley and Hankey collected 79 written accounts of the legend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beardsley_1942_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beardsley_1942-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beardsley_1943_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beardsley_1943-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lists_of_legendary_creatures" title="Lists of legendary creatures">Lists of legendary creatures</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Georges, Robert; Owens, Michael (1995). <i>Folkloristics</i>.
