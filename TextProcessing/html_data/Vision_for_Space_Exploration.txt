Policy, 2004</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Vision for Space Exploration</b> (<b>VSE</b>) was a plan for space exploration announced on January 14, 2004 by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>. It was conceived as a response to the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">Space Shuttle <i>Columbia</i> disaster</a>, the state of <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and as a way to regain public enthusiasm for <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>. It was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Barack_Obama_administration" title="Space policy of the Barack Obama administration">space policy of the Barack Obama administration</a> in June 2010.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline">Outline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vision_for_Space_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Outline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Vision for Space Exploration sought to implement a sustained and affordable human and robotic program to explore the solar system and beyond; extend human presence across the solar system, starting with a human return to the Moon by the year 2020, in preparation for human exploration of Mars and other destinations; develop the innovative technologies, knowledge, and infrastructures both to explore and to support decisions about the destinations for human exploration; and to promote international and commercial participation in exploration to further U.S. scientific, security, and economic interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAdocument_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdocument-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In pursuit of these goals, the Vision called for the space program to complete the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> by 2010; retire the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle</a> by 2010; develop a new <a href="/wiki/Crew_Exploration_Vehicle" title="Crew Exploration Vehicle">Crew Exploration Vehicle</a> (later renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion</a>) by 2008, and conduct its first human spaceflight mission by 2014; explore the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> with <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> missions by 2008 and crewed missions by 2020, and use lunar exploration to develop and test new approaches and technologies useful for supporting sustained exploration of Mars and beyond; explore <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and other destinations with robotic and crewed missions; pursue commercial transportation to support the International Space Station and missions beyond low Earth orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAdocument_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdocument-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAaddress_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAaddress-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Outlining some of the advantages, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">U.S. President</a> George W. Bush addressed the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAaddress_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAaddress-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Establishing an extended human presence on the moon could vastly reduce the costs of further space exploration, making possible ever more ambitious missions. Lifting heavy spacecraft and fuel out of the Earth\'s gravity is expensive. Spacecraft assembled and provisioned on the moon could escape its far lower gravity using far less energy, and thus, far less cost. Also, the moon is home to abundant resources. Its soil contains raw materials that might be harvested and processed into rocket fuel or breathable air. We can use our time on the moon to develop and test new approaches and technologies and systems that will allow us to function in other, more challenging environments.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>One of the stated goals for the <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a> is to gain significant experience in operating away from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s environment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> as the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> contended, to embody a "sustainable course of long-term exploration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpaceBush_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceBush-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ares_(rocket)" title="Ares (rocket)">Ares boosters</a> are a cost-effective approach<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowCostMoon_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LowCostMoon-5">[5]</a></sup> &#8212; entailing the <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a>\'s enormous, unprecedented cargo-carrying capacity<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> &#8212; transporting future space exploration resources to the Moon\'s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowCostMoon_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-LowCostMoon-5">[5]</a></sup> weaker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravity_field" title="Gravity field">gravity field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-7">[7]</a></sup> While simultaneously serving as a proving ground for a wide range of space operations and processes, the Moon may serve as a cost-effective construction, launching and fueling site for future <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-base_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-base-8">[8]</a></sup> For example, future Ares V missions could cost-effectively<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowCostMoon_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-LowCostMoon-5">[5]</a></sup> deliver <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw materials</a> for future <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> and missions to a Moon-based<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowCostMoon_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-LowCostMoon-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Space_dock" title="Space dock">space dock</a> positioned as a <a href="/wiki/Counterweight" title="Counterweight">counterweight</a> to a Moon-based <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator#Extraterrestrial_elevators" title="Space elevator">space elevator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SDLV_rockets.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="790" data-file-width="681" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/SDLV_rockets.jpg/220px-SDLV_rockets.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/SDLV_rockets.jpg/330px-SDLV_rockets.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/SDLV_rockets.jpg/440px-SDLV_rockets.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SDLV_rockets.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two planned configurations for a return to the Moon, heavy lift (left) and crew (right)</div></div></div>\n<p>NASA has also outlined plans for manned missions to the <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">far side of the Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> All of the Apollo missions have landed on the near side. Unique products may be producible in the nearly limitless extreme <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> of the lunar surface, and the Moon\'s remoteness is the ultimate isolation for <a href="/wiki/Biological_hazard" title="Biological hazard">biologically hazardous</a> experiments. The Moon would become a proving ground also toward the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization" title="In-Situ Resource Utilization">In-Situ Resource Utilization</a>, or "living off the land" (i.e., self-sufficiency) for permanent human outposts.\n</p><p>In a position paper issued by the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">National Space Society</a> (NSS), a return to the Moon should be considered a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_program" title="Space program">space program</a> priority, to begin development of the knowledge and identification of the industries unique to the Moon. The NSS believes that the Moon may be a repository of the history and possible future of our planet, and that the six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> landings only scratched the surface of that \'treasure\'. According to NSS, the Moon\'s far side, permanently shielded from the noisy Earth, is an ideal site for future <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> (for example, signals in the 1-10&#160;MHz range cannot be detected on Earth because of <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>)\n</p><p>When the Vision was announced in January 2004, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> and the scientific community gave it a mix of positive and negative reviews. For example, Rep. <a href="/wiki/Dave_Weldon" title="Dave Weldon">Dave Weldon</a> (R-Fla.) said, "I think this is the best thing that has happened to the space program in decades." Though physicist and outspoken <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_versus_robotic_spaceflight" title="Human versus robotic spaceflight">manned spaceflight opponent</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Park" title="Robert L. Park">Robert L. Park</a> stated that robotic spacecraft "are doing so well it\'s going to be hard to justify sending a human,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpaceBush_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceBush-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">vision</a> announced by the President states that <i>"robotic missions will serve as trailblazers &#8212; the advanced guard to the unknown."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAaddress_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASAaddress-2">[2]</a></sup> Others, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Society" title="Mars Society">Mars Society</a>, have argued that it makes more sense to avoid going back to the Moon and instead focus on going to Mars first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vsfe_ship.jpg"><img alt="Vsfe ship.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Vsfe_ship.jpg/220px-Vsfe_ship.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Vsfe_ship.jpg/330px-Vsfe_ship.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Vsfe_ship.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vsfe_ship.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>\n<p>Throughout much of 2004, it was unclear whether the U.S. Congress would be willing to approve and fund the Vision for Space Exploration. However, in November 2004, Congress passed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" title="Bill (proposed law)">spending bill</a> which gave NASA the $16.2 billion that President Bush had sought to kick-start the Vision. According to then-NASA chief <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Keefe" title="Sean O\'Keefe">Sean O\'Keefe</a>, that spending bill "was as strong an endorsement of the space exploration vision, as any of us could have imagined."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2005, Congress passed S.1281, the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Authorization_Act_of_2005" title="NASA Authorization Act of 2005">NASA Authorization Act of 2005</a>, which explicitly endorses the Vision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Former NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Griffin" title="Michael D. Griffin">Michael Griffin</a> is a big supporter of the Vision, but modified it somewhat, saying that he wants to reduce the four-year gap between the retirement of the Space Shuttle and the first manned mission of the Crew Exploration Vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_Architecture">Lunar Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vision_for_Space_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lunar Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>NASA\'s "Lunar Architecture" forms a key part of its Global Exploration Strategy, also known as the Vision for Space Exploration. The first part of the Lunar Architecture is the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter</a>, which launched in June 2009 on board an <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>. The preliminary design review was completed in February 2006 and the critical design review was completed in November 2006. An important function of the orbiter will be to look for further evidence that the increased concentrations of hydrogen discovered at the moon\'s poles is in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_ice" title="Lunar ice">lunar ice</a>. After this the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Constellation#Lunar_Flights" title="Project Constellation">lunar flights</a> will make use of the new <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> and Ares V rockets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_perspectives">Critical perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vision_for_Space_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="673" data-file-width="898" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg/220px-NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg/330px-NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg/440px-NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA-Main_budget_chart_14-01-2004.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NASA\'s 2004 budget projections for the Vision for Space Exploration</div></div></div>\n<p>In December 2003, <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> voiced criticism for NASA\'s vision and objectives, stating that the goal of sending astronauts back to the moon was "more like reaching for past glory than striving for new triumphs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fly_Me_To_L1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fly_Me_To_L1-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2009, the Aerospace Technology Working Group released an in-depth report asserting that the Vision had several fundamental problems with regard to politics, financing, and general space policy issues and that the initiative should be rectified or replaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another concern noted is that funding for VSE could instead be harnessed to advance science and technology, such as in aeronautics, commercial spacecraft and launch vehicle technology, environmental monitoring, and biomedical sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Practical_Benefits_for_America_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Practical_Benefits_for_America-19">[19]</a></sup> However, VSE itself is poised to propel a host of beneficial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_science" title="Moon science">Moon science</a> activities, including lunar telescopes, selenological studies and solar energy beams.\n</p><p>With or without VSE, human spaceflight will be made sustainable. However, without VSE, more funds could be directed toward reducing human spaceflight costs sufficiently for the betterment of <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> research, business, and tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Practical_Benefits_for_America_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Practical_Benefits_for_America-19">[19]</a></sup> Alternatively, VSE could afford advances in other scientific research (<a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selenology" title="Selenology">selenology</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ" title="In-situ">in-situ</a> lunar business industries, and lunar-space tourism.\n</p><p>The VSE budget required termination the Space Shuttle by 2010 and of any US role in the International Space Station by 2017. This would have required, even in the most optimistic plans, in a period of years without human spaceflight capability from the US. Termination of the Space Shuttle program, without any planned alternatives, in 2011 ended virtually all US capability for reusable launch vehicles.  This severely limited any future of low earth orbit or deep space exploration.  Ultimately, the lack of proper funding caused the VSE to fall short of its original goals, leaving many projects behind schedule as President George W. Bush\'s term in office ended.\n</p><p>"The damage done to America and the rest of the world by unsustainable deficits is real, and any lack of zeal in facing this problem would be a mistake. In that context, this would be a good time for Congress to look again at Bush\'s plans for NASA to re-establish a human presence in deep space. The outgoing Republican Congress gave its Republican president too much benefit of the doubt on this undertaking. The new Congress must, at the very least, articulate more convincing reasons than have yet been heard for such a colossal expenditure."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>"A large portion of the scientific community" concurs that NASA is not "expanding our scientific understanding of the universe" in "the most effective or cost-efficient way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p8_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-p8-21">[Tumlinson 1]</a></sup> Proponents for VSE argue that a permanent settlement on the moon would drastically reduce costs for further space exploration missions. President George W. Bush voiced this sentiment when the vision was first announced (see quote above), and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> has re-entered testimony<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p8_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-p8-21">[Tumlinson 1]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Space_Frontier_Foundation" title="Space Frontier Foundation">Space Frontier Foundation</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Rick_Tumlinson" title="Rick Tumlinson">Rick Tumlinson</a> offered previously to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Commerce,_Science_and_Transportation" title="United States Senate Committee on Commerce, Science and Transportation">United States Senate Committee on Commerce, Science and Transportation</a> advocating this particular perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p8_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-p8-21">[Tumlinson 1]</a></sup> The reason that the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">National Space Society</a> regards a return to the Moon as a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_program" title="Space program">space program</a> priority is to begin development of the knowledge and identification of the industries unique to the Moon, because "such industries can provide economic leverage and support for NASA activities, saving the government millions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[Tumlinson 2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As Tumlinson additionally notes, the goal is to "open space ... to human settlement ... to create ways to harvest the resources ... not only saving this precious planet, but also ... assuring our survival."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[Tumlinson 3]</a></sup> Regarding "the Moon, NASA should support early exploration now. ... "<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[Tumlinson 4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_vision">Mars vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
