For its usage in expert witnessing, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_evidence_(law)" title="Scientific evidence (law)">Scientific evidence (law)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Scientific evidence</b> is <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a> which serves to either support or counter a <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">theory</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a>. Such evidence is expected to be <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a> and interpretation in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>. Standards for scientific evidence vary according to the field of inquiry, but the strength of scientific evidence is generally based on the results of <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical analysis</a> and the strength of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_control" title="Scientific control">scientific controls</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_of_inference">Principles of inference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principles of inference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A person\'s assumptions or beliefs about the relationship between observations and a hypothesis will affect whether that person takes the observations as evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philsci_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-philsci-1">[1]</a></sup> These assumptions or beliefs will also affect how a person utilizes the observations as evidence. For example, the Earth\'s apparent lack of motion may be taken as evidence for a geocentric cosmology. However, after sufficient evidence is presented for heliocentric cosmology and the apparent lack of motion is explained, the initial observation is strongly discounted as evidence.\n</p><p>When rational observers have different background beliefs, they may draw different conclusions from the same scientific evidence. For example, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Priestley</a>, working with <a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">phlogiston theory</a>, explained his observations about the decomposition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercuric_oxide" title="Mercuric oxide">mercuric oxide</a> using phlogiston. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Lavoisier</a>, developing the theory of elements, explained the same observations with reference to oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuhn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuhn-2">[2]</a></sup> Note that a causal relationship between the observations and hypothesis does not exist to cause the observation to be taken as evidence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-philsci_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-philsci-1">[1]</a></sup> but rather the causal relationship is provided by the person seeking to establish observations as evidence.\n</p><p>A more formal method to characterize the effect of background beliefs is <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_inference" title="Bayesian inference">Bayesian inference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Bayesian inference, beliefs are expressed as percentages indicating one\'s confidence in them. One starts from an initial probability (a <a href="/wiki/Prior_probability" title="Prior probability">prior</a>), and then updates that probability using <a href="/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem" title="Bayes\' theorem">Bayes\' theorem</a> after observing evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result, two independent observers of the same event will rationally arrive at different conclusions if their priors (previous observations that are also relevant to the conclusion) differ. However, if they are allowed to communicate with each other, they will end in agreement (per <a href="/wiki/Aumann%27s_agreement_theorem" title="Aumann\'s agreement theorem">Aumann\'s agreement theorem</a>).\n</p><p>The importance of background beliefs in the determination of what observations are evidence can be illustrated using <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">deductive reasoning</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Syllogism" title="Syllogism">syllogisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> If either of the propositions is not accepted as true, the conclusion will not be accepted either.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_of_scientific_evidence">Utility of scientific evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Utility of scientific evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">Falsifiability</a></div>\n<p>Philosophers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_R._Popper" title="Karl R. Popper">Karl R. Popper</a>, have provided influential theories of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> within which scientific evidence plays a central role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In summary, Popper provides that a scientist creatively develops a theory which may be falsified by testing the theory against evidence or known facts. Popper\'s theory presents an asymmetry in that evidence can prove a theory wrong, by establishing facts that are inconsistent with the theory. In contrast, evidence cannot prove a theory correct because other evidence, yet to be discovered, may exist that is inconsistent with the theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophical_versus_scientific_views">Philosophical versus scientific views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Philosophical versus scientific views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The philosophical community has investigated the logical requirements for scientific evidence by examination of the relationship between evidence and hypotheses, in contrast to scientific approaches which focus on the candidate facts and their context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Bechtel" title="William Bechtel">Bechtel</a>, as an example of a scientific approach, provides factors (clarity of the data, replication by others, consistency with results arrived at by alternative methods and consistency with plausible theories) useful for determination of whether observations may be considered scientific evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are a variety of philosophical approaches to decide whether an observation may be considered evidence; many of these focus on the relationship between the evidence and the hypothesis. Carnap recommends distinguishing such approaches into three categories: classificatory (whether the evidence confirms the hypothesis), comparative (whether the evidence supports a first hypothesis more than an alternative hypothesis) or quantitative (the degree to which the evidence supports a hypothesis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Achinstein" title="Peter Achinstein">Achinstein</a> provides a concise presentation by prominent philosophers on evidence, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Hempel" title="Carl Hempel">Carl Hempel</a> (Confirmation), <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Goodman" title="Nelson Goodman">Nelson Goodman</a> (of grue fame), <a href="/wiki/R._B._Braithwaite" title="R. B. Braithwaite">R. B. Braithwaite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwood_Russell_Hanson" title="Norwood Russell Hanson">Norwood Russell Hanson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wesley_C._Salmon" title="Wesley C. Salmon">Wesley C. Salmon</a>, Clark Glymour and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Carnap" title="Rudolf Carnap">Rudolf Carnap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Based on the philosophical assumption of the <a href="/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis#Philosophical_implications" title="Church&#8211;Turing thesis">Strong Church-Turing Universe Thesis</a>, a mathematical criterion for evaluation of evidence has been conjectured, with the criterion having a resemblance to the idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occam%27s_Razor" title="Occam\'s Razor">Occam\'s Razor</a> that the simplest comprehensive description of the evidence is most likely correct. It states formally, "The ideal principle states that the prior probability associated with the hypothesis should be given by the algorithmic universal probability, and the sum of the log universal probability of the model plus the log of the probability of the data given the model should be minimized."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the posted curriculum for an <a class="external text" href="http://undsci.berkeley.edu/article/0_0_0/howscienceworks_06" rel="nofollow">"Understanding Science 101"</a> course taught at University of California - Berkeley: "Testing hypotheses and theories is at the core of the process of science." This philosophical belief in  "hypothesis testing" as the essence of science is prevalent among both scientists and philosophers. It is important to note that this <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> does not take into account all of the activities or scientific objectives of all scientists. When Geiger and Marsden <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93Marsden_experiment" title="Geiger&#8211;Marsden experiment"> scattered alpha particles through thin gold foil</a> for example, the resulting data enabled their experimental adviser, Ernest Rutherford, to very accurately calculate the mass and size of an atomic nucleus for the first time. No hypothesis was required. It may be that a more general view of science is offered by physicist, Lawrence Krauss, who consistently writes in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/lawrence-m-krauss/the-big-unanswered-questi_1_b_7151666.html" rel="nofollow">media</a> about scientists answering questions by measuring physical properties and processes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept_of_scientific_proof">Concept of scientific proof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_evidence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Concept of scientific proof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While the phrase "scientific proof" is often used in the popular media,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> many scientists have argued that there is really no such thing. For example, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a> once wrote that "In the empirical sciences, which alone can furnish us with information about the world we live in, proofs do not occur, if we mean by \'proof\' an argument which establishes once and for ever the truth of a theory".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Albert Einstein said:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The scientific theorist is not to be envied.  For Nature, or more precisely experiment, is an inexorable and not very friendly judge of his work.  It never says "Yes" to a theory.  In the most favorable cases it says "Maybe," and in the great majority of cases simply "No." If an experiment agrees with a theory it means for the latter "Maybe," and if it does not agree it means "No."  Probably every theory will someday experience its "No" - most theories, soon after conception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
