style="text-align:center">&#160;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Mars 3</b> was an unmanned <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_program" title="Mars program">Mars program</a> which spanned the years between 1960 and 1973. Mars 3 was launched May 28, 1971, nine days after its twin spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Mars_2" title="Mars 2">Mars 2</a>. The probes were identical <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> launched by <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a> rockets with a <a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">Blok D</a> upper stage, each consisting of an <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a> and an attached <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lander</a>. After the Mars 2 lander crashed on the martian surface, Mars 3 lander became the first spacecraft to attain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a> on Mars on 2 December 1971.  It failed shortly after landing.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Launch Date/Time:\n<ul><li>Mars 3: May 28, 1971 at 15:26:30 UTC</li></ul></li>\n<li>Launch mass (including fuel):\n<ul><li>Combined: 4,650&#160;kg (10,250&#160;lb)</li>\n<li>Orbiter: 3,440&#160;kg (7,580&#160;lb)</li>\n<li>Lander: 1,210&#160;kg (2,670&#160;lb)</li></ul></li>\n<li>On-orbit dry mass: 2,265&#160;kg (4,993&#160;lb)</li>\n<li>Dimensions: 4.1&#160;m (13.5&#160;ft) tall, 2&#160;m (6.6&#160;ft) across (5.9&#160;m (19.4&#160;ft) across with solar panels deployed)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbiter">Orbiter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbiter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The type of Orbiter was the Variant M-71 of the Bus/Obiter 4M-V. The primary purpose of the orbiter was to study the topography of the surface; analyze its soil composition; measure various properties of the atmosphere; monitor "solar radiation, the solar wind, and the interplanetary and martian magnetic fields".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In addition, it served as a "communications relay to send signals from the lander to Earth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The orbiter suffered from a partial loss of fuel and did not have enough to put itself into a planned 25-hour orbit. The engine instead performed a truncated burn to put the spacecraft into a highly-elliptical long-period (12 day, 19 hours) orbit about Mars.\n</p><p>By coincidence, a particularly large <a href="/wiki/Storm#Extraterrestrial_storms" title="Storm">dust storm</a> on Mars adversely affected the mission.  When <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a> arrived and successfully orbited Mars on 14 November 1971, just two weeks prior to Mars 2 and Mars 3, <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary scientists</a> were surprised to find the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body%27s_atmosphere" title="Celestial body\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> was thick with "a planet-wide robe of <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, the largest storm ever observed".  The surface was totally obscured.  Unable to reprogram the mission computers, both Mars 2 and Mars 3 dispatched their landers immediately, and the orbiters used up a significant portion of their available data resources in snapping images of the featureless dust clouds below, rather than the surface mapping intended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Mars 3 orbiter sent back data covering the period from December 1971 to March 1972, although transmissions continued through August. It was announced that Mars 3 had completed their mission by 22 August 1972, after 20 orbits. The probe, combined with Mars 2, sent back a total of 60 pictures. The images and data revealed mountains as high as 22&#160;km, atomic hydrogen and oxygen in the upper atmosphere, surface temperatures ranging from &#8722;110&#160;&#176;C to +13&#160;&#176;C, surface pressures of 5.5 to 6 mb, water vapor concentrations 5000 times less than in Earth\'s atmosphere, the base of the ionosphere starting at 80 to 110&#160;km altitude, and grains from dust storms as high as 7&#160;km in the atmosphere. The images and data enabled creation of surface relief maps,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perminov1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perminov1-5">[5]</a></sup> and gave information on the Martian <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lander">Lander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="449" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg/220px-Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg/330px-Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg/440px-Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars3_lander_vsm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mars 3 Lander model at the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Museum_of_Cosmonautics" title="Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics">Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics</a> in Moscow</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lander_spacecraft_system">Lander spacecraft system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lander spacecraft system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Mars 3 descent module was mounted on the bus/orbiter opposite the propulsion system. It consisted of a spherical 1.2 m diameter landing capsule, a 2.9 m diameter conical aerodynamic braking shield, a parachute system and retro-rockets.\n</p><p>The entire descent module had a fueled mass of 1210&#160;kg, the spherical landing capsule accounted for 358&#160;kg of this. An automatic control system consisting of gas micro-engines and pressurized nitrogen containers provided attitude control. Four solid-fuel motors were mounted to the outer edge of the cone to control pitch and yaw.\n</p><p>The main and auxiliary parachutes, the engine to initiate the landing, and the radar altimeter were mounted on the top section of the lander. Foam was used to absorb shock within the descent module. The landing capsule had four triangular petals which would open after landing, righting the spacecraft and exposing the instrumentation.\n</p><p>The lander was equipped with two television cameras with a 360 degree view of the surface as well as a mass spectrometer to study atmospheric composition; temperature, pressure, and wind sensors; and devices to measure mechanical and chemical properties of the surface, including a mechanical scoop to search for organic materials and signs of life. It also contained a pennant with the Soviet coat of arms.\n</p><p>Four aerials protruded from the top of the sphere to provide communications with the orbiter via an onboard radio system. The equipment was powered by batteries which were charged by the orbiter prior to separation. Temperature control was maintained through thermal insulation and a system of radiators. The landing capsule was sterilized before launch to prevent contamination of the martian environment.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Prop-M_Rover">Prop-M Rover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prop-M Rover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars_propm_rover.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="519" data-file-width="660" decoding="async" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Mars_propm_rover.jpg/250px-Mars_propm_rover.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Mars_propm_rover.jpg/375px-Mars_propm_rover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Mars_propm_rover.jpg/500px-Mars_propm_rover.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars_propm_rover.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prop-M Rover</div></div></div>\n<p>Mars 3 lander had a small 4.5&#160;kg (9.9 Lb) \'<a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">Mars rover</a>\' on board, which was planned to move across the surface on skis while connected to the lander with a 15-meter <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical cable</a>. Two small metal rods were used for autonomous obstacle avoidance, as radio signals from Earth would take too long to drive the rovers using remote control. The rover carried a dynamic <a href="/wiki/Penetrometer" title="Penetrometer">penetrometer</a> and a radiation <a href="/wiki/Densitometer" title="Densitometer">densitometer</a>.\n</p><p>The main PROP-M frame was a squat box with a small protrusion at the center. The frame was supported on two wide flat skis, one extending down from each side elevating the frame slightly above the surface. At the front of the box were obstacle detection bars. The rover was planned to be placed on the surface after landing by a manipulator arm and to move in the field of view of the television cameras and stop to make measurements every 1.5 meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The traces of movement in the Martian soil would also be recorded to determine material properties.\n</p><p>Because of the demise of the lander, the rover was not deployed.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Entry.2C_descent.2C_landing.2C_and_transmission_failure"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry,_descent,_landing,_and_transmission_failure">Entry, descent, landing, and transmission failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Entry, descent, landing, and transmission failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mars 3\'s descent module was released at 09:14 UT on December 2, 1971, 4 hours 35 minutes before reaching Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-M3-7">[7]</a></sup> The descent module <a href="/wiki/Mars_atmospheric_entry" title="Mars atmospheric entry">entered the Martian atmosphere</a> at roughly 5.7&#160;km/s. Through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_braking" title="Aerodynamic braking">aerodynamic braking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachutes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retrorocket" title="Retrorocket">retrorockets</a>, the lander achieved a soft landing at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_3&amp;params=45_S_202_E_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">202&#176;E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45&#176;S 202&#176;E</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-45; 202</span></span></span></a></span><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_3&amp;params=45_S_202_E_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">202&#176;E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45&#176;S 202&#176;E</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-45; 202</span></span></span></a></span></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20130411_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20130411-1">[1]</a></sup> and began operations.\n</p><p>The lander began transmitting to the Mars 3 orbiter 90 seconds after landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-M3-7">[7]</a></sup> After 20 seconds, transmission stopped for unknown reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M3_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-M3-7">[7]</a></sup> It is not known whether the fault originated with the lander or the communications relay on the orbiter. The cause of the failure may have been related to the extremely powerful martian dust storm taking place at the time which may have induced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_discharge" title="Coronal discharge">coronal discharge</a>, damaging the communications system. The dust storm would also explain the poor image lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perminov1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Perminov1-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A partial image (70 lines) was transmitted. Although this image appears to show the horizon and dark sky, the photograph was taken with a cycloramic camera. This means that to correctly view the photograph it should be turned 90 degrees clockwise, assuming the rover did land upright when it landed at the time of the global Martian dust storm. According to the Soviet Academy of Sciences there is nothing, horizon or otherwise, identifiable in the photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perminov1_5-2"><a
