For the 2009 film, see <a href="/wiki/Space_Tourists" title="Space Tourists">Space Tourists</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1356" decoding="async" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg/220px-Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg/330px-Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg/440px-Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mark_Shuttleworth_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Space tourist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Shuttleworth" title="Mark Shuttleworth">Mark Shuttleworth</a></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Space tourism</b> is <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">space travel</a> for recreational, leisure or business<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Why would \'business travel\' be lumped with \'tourism\' here; needs a source (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> purposes. There are several different types of space tourism, including orbital, suborbital and lunar space tourism. To date, orbital space tourism has been performed only by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Agency" title="Russian Space Agency">Russian Space Agency</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Think it was done by a company called Space Adventures who contracted for a bulk purchase of so many non-NASA and non-Roscosmos astronauts to go to the space station privately. From previous reading, I believe it was Space Adventures that hatched the biz plan for that, and they approached the Russian government to negotiate the bulk buy of Soyuz seats at a price that the Russian government preferred over sending one more Russian cosmonaut to the ISS on various occasions. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>   Work also continues towards developing suborbital space tourism vehicles. This is being done by aerospace companies like <a href="/wiki/Blue_Origin" title="Blue Origin">Blue Origin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Galactic" title="Virgin Galactic">Virgin Galactic</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> (an aerospace manufacturer) announced in 2018 that it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceX_Lunar_Tourism_Mission" title="SpaceX Lunar Tourism Mission">planning on sending two space tourists</a> on a free-return trajectory around the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> on the upper stage of SpaceX\'s <a href="/wiki/BFR_(rocket)" title="BFR (rocket)">BFR</a> rocket, known as the Big Falcon Spaceship (BFS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teslarati_moonflight_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-teslarati_moonflight-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grush_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-grush-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the period from 2001 to 2009, the publicized price for flights brokered by <a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures" title="Space Adventures">Space Adventures</a> to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> aboard a Russian <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz spacecraft</a> was in the range of US$20&#8211;40 million. 7 space tourists made 8 space flights during this time. Some space tourists have signed contracts with third parties to conduct certain research activities while in orbit. By 2007, space tourism was thought to be one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">markets</a> that would <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emerge</a> for commercial spaceflight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfiore2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-belfiore2007-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup> Space Adventures is the only company that has sent paying passengers to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSN_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSN-5">[5]</a></sup> In conjunction with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Space_Agency_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Federal Space Agency of the Russian Federation">Federal Space Agency of the Russian Federation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">Rocket and Space Corporation Energia</a>, Space Adventures facilitated the flights for all of the world\'s first private space explorers. The first three participants paid in excess of $20 million (USD) each for their 10-day visit to the ISS.\n</p><p>Russia halted orbital space tourism in 2010 due to the increase in the International Space Station crew size, using the seats for expedition crews that would previously have been sold to paying spaceflight participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomorespacetourism_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nomorespacetourism-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interfaxtourism_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-interfaxtourism-7">[7]</a></sup> Orbital tourist flights were set to resume in 2015 but the one planned was postponed indefinitely and none have occurred since 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceadventures.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-spaceadventures.com-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As an alternative term to "tourism", some organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Spaceflight_Federation" title="Commercial Spaceflight Federation">Commercial Spaceflight Federation</a> use the term "personal spaceflight". The Citizens in Space project uses the term "citizen space exploration".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many private space travelers have objected to the term "space tourist", often pointing out that their role went beyond that of an observer, since they also carried out scientific experiments in the course of their journey. Richard Garriott additionally emphasized that his training was identical to the requirements of non-Russian Soyuz crew members, and that teachers and other non-professional astronauts chosen to fly with NASA are called astronauts. He has said that if the distinction has to be made, he would rather be called "private astronaut" than "tourist".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garriott_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garriott-10">[10]</a></sup> Dennis Tito has asked to be known as an "independent researcher",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and Mark Shuttleworth described himself as a "pioneer of commercial space travel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Gregory Olsen prefers "private researcher",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and Anousheh Ansari prefers the term "private space explorer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anousheh_Ansari_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anousheh_Ansari-13">[13]</a></sup> Other space enthusiasts object to the term on similar grounds. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Tumlinson" title="Rick Tumlinson">Rick Tumlinson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Frontier_Foundation" title="Space Frontier Foundation">Space Frontier Foundation</a>, for example, has said: "I hate the word tourist, and I always will ... \'Tourist\' is somebody in a flowered shirt with three cameras around his neck."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Russian cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Maksim_Surayev" title="Maksim Surayev">Maksim Surayev</a> told the press in 2009 not to describe <a href="/wiki/Guy_Lalibert%C3%A9" title="Guy Lalibert&#233;">Guy Lalibert&#233;</a> as a tourist: "It\'s become fashionable to speak of space tourists. He is not a tourist but a participant in the mission."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaceflight_participant" title="Spaceflight participant">Spaceflight participant</a>" is the official term used by NASA and the Russian Federal Space Agency to distinguish between private space travelers and career astronauts. Tito, Shuttleworth, Olsen, Ansari, and Simonyi were designated as such during their respective space flights. NASA also lists <a href="/wiki/Christa_McAuliffe" title="Christa McAuliffe">Christa McAuliffe</a> as a spaceflight participant (although she did not pay a fee), apparently due to her non-technical duties aboard the <a href="/wiki/STS-51-L" title="STS-51-L">STS-51-L</a> flight.\n</p><p>The US Federal Aviation Administration awards the title of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_Astronaut" title="Commercial Astronaut">Commercial Astronaut</a>" to trained crew members of privately funded spacecraft. The only people currently holding this title are <a href="/wiki/Mike_Melvill" title="Mike Melvill">Mike Melvill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Binnie" title="Brian Binnie">Brian Binnie</a>, the pilots of <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">Precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a></div>\n<p>The Soviet space program was aggressive in broadening the pool of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonauts" title="Cosmonauts">cosmonauts</a>. The Soviet <a href="/wiki/Interkosmos" title="Interkosmos">Intercosmos</a> program included cosmonauts selected from <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> member countries (Czechoslovakia, Poland, East Germany, Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania) and later from allies of the USSR (Cuba, Mongolia, Vietnam) and <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">non-aligned</a> countries (India, Syria, Afghanistan). Most of these cosmonauts received full training for their missions and were treated as equals, but were generally given shorter flights than Soviet cosmonauts. The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA) also took advantage of the program.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The US space shuttle program included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_Specialist" title="Payload Specialist">payload specialist</a> positions which were usually filled by representatives of companies or institutions managing a specific payload on that mission. These payload specialists did not receive the same training as professional <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> astronauts and were not employed by NASA. In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Ulf_Merbold" title="Ulf Merbold">Ulf Merbold</a> from ESA and <a href="/wiki/Byron_K._Lichtenberg" title="Byron K. Lichtenberg">Byron Lichtenberg</a> from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> (engineer and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> fighter pilot) were the first payload specialists to fly on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, on mission <a href="/wiki/STS-9" title="STS-9">STS-9</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1984, <a href="/wiki/Charles_D._Walker" title="Charles D. Walker">Charles D. Walker</a> became the first non-government astronaut to fly, with his employer <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> paying US$40,000 (equivalent to $96,464 in 2018) for his flight. NASA was also eager to prove its capability to Congressional sponsors. During the 1970s, Shuttle prime contractor <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a> studied a $200&#8211;300 million removable cabin that could fit into the Shuttle\'s cargo bay. The cabin could carry up to 74 passengers into orbit for up to three days. Space Habitation Design Associates proposed, in 1983, a cabin for 72 passengers in the bay. Passengers were located in six sections, each with windows and its own loading ramp, and with seats in different configurations for launch and landing. Another proposal was based on the Spacelab habitation modules, which provided 32 seats in the payload bay in addition to those in the cockpit area. A 1985 presentation to the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">National Space Society</a> stated that, although flying tourists in the cabin would cost $1 to 1.5 million per passenger without government subsidy, within 15 years 30,000 people a year would pay US$25,000 (equivalent to $58,238 in 2018) each to fly in space on new spacecraft. The presentation also forecast flights to <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit</a> within 30 years and visits to the lunar surface within 50 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-l5198504_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-l5198504-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the shuttle program expanded in the early 1980s, NASA began a Space Flight Participant program to allow citizens without scientific or governmental roles to fly. <a href="/wiki/Christa_McAuliffe" title="Christa McAuliffe">Christa McAuliffe</a> was chosen as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teacher_in_Space" title="Teacher in Space">Teacher in Space</a> in July 1985 from 11,400 applicants. 1,700 applied for the Journalist in Space program. An Artist in Space program was considered, and NASA expected that after McAuliffe\'s flight two to three civilians a year would fly on the shuttle. After McAuliffe was killed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenger_disaster" title="Challenger disaster">Challenger disaster</a> in January 1986, the programs were canceled. McAuliffe\'s backup, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Morgan" title="Barbara Morgan">Barbara Morgan</a>, eventually got hired in 1998 as a professional astronaut and flew on <a href="/wiki/STS-118" title="STS-118">STS-118</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Mission_specialist" title="Mission specialist">mission specialist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unl2011_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-unl2011-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>84&#8211;85</span></sup> A second journalist-in-space program, in which NASA green-lighted <a href="/wiki/Miles_O%27Brien_(journalist)" title="Miles O\'Brien (journalist)">Miles O\'Brien</a> to fly on the space shuttle, was scheduled to be announced in 2003. That program was canceled in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_disaster" title="Columbia disaster">Columbia disaster</a> on <a href="/wiki/STS-107" title="STS-107">STS-107</a> and subsequent emphasis on finishing the International Space Station before retiring the space shuttle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>\nInitially, senior figures at NASA strongly opposed space tourism on principle; from the beginning of the ISS expeditions, NASA stated it wasn\'t interested in accommodating paying guests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NoTourists_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NoTourists-20">[20]</a></sup> The Subcommittee on Space and Aeronautics Committee On Science of the House of Representatives held in June 2001 revealed the shifting attitude of NASA towards paying space tourists wanting to travel to the ISS in its statement on the hearing\'s purpose: </p><blockquote><p>"Review the issues and opportunities for flying nonprofessional astronauts in space, the appropriate government role for supporting the nascent space tourism industry, use of the Shuttle and Space Station for Tourism, safety and training criteria for space tourists, and the potential commercial market for space tourism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> The subcommittee report was interested in evaluating Dennis Tito\'s extensive training and his experience in space as a nonprofessional astronaut.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>With the realities of the post-<a href="/wiki/Perestroika" title="Perestroika">Perestroika</a> economy in Russia, its space industry was especially starved for cash. The <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Broadcasting_System" title="Tokyo Broadcasting System">Tokyo Broadcasting System</a> (TBS) offered to pay for one of its reporters to fly on a mission. <a href="/wiki/Toyohiro_Akiyama" title="Toyohiro Akiyama">Toyohiro Akiyama</a> was flown in 1990 to <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> with the eighth crew and returned a week later with the seventh crew. Cost estimates vary from $10 million up to $37 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Akiyama gave a daily TV broadcast from orbit and also performed scientific experiments for Russian and Japanese companies. However, since the cost of the flight was paid by his employer, Akiyama could be considered a business traveler rather than a tourist.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In 1991, British chemist <a href="/wiki/Helen_Sharman" title="Helen Sharman">Helen Sharman</a> was selected from a pool of 13,000 applicants to be the first Briton in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FirstBriton_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FirstBriton-24">[24]</a></sup> The program was known as <a href="/wiki/Project_Juno" title="Project Juno">Project Juno</a> and was a cooperative arrangement between the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and a group of British companies. The Project Juno consortium failed to raise the funds required, and the program was almost canceled. Reportedly <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> ordered it to proceed under Soviet expense in the interests of international relations, but in the absence of Western underwriting, less expensive experiments were substituted for those in the original plans. Sharman flew aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-12" title="Soyuz TM-12">Soyuz TM-12</a> to Mir and returned aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-11" title="Soyuz TM-11">Soyuz TM-11</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-orbital_space_tourism">Sub-orbital space tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sub-orbital space tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">Sub-orbital spaceflight</a></div>\n<p>As of 2018, no suborbital space tourism has yet occurred, but since it is projected to be more affordable, many companies view it as a money-making proposition. Most are proposing vehicles that make suborbital flights peaking at an altitude of 100&#8211;160&#160;km (62&#8211;99&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotland2040_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland2040-25">[25]</a></sup> Passengers would experience three to six minutes of weightlessness, a view of a twinkle-free starfield, and a vista of the curved Earth below. Projected costs are expected to be about $200,000 per passenger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Successful_projects">Successful projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Successful projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites" title="Scaled Composites">Scaled Composites</a> won the $10 million <a href="/wiki/Ansari_X_Prize" title="Ansari X Prize">X Prize</a> in October 2004 with <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>, as the first private company to reach and surpass an altitude of 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) twice within two weeks. In September 2006, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_American" title="Iranian American">Iranian American</a> businesswoman named <a href="/wiki/Anousheh_Ansari" title="Anousheh Ansari">Anousheh Ansari</a> became the fourth space tourist (<a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-9" title="Soyuz TMA-9">Soyuz TMA-9</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anousheh_Ansari_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anousheh_Ansari-13">[13]</a></sup>) In April 2007, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Simonyi" title="Charles Simonyi">Charles Simonyi</a>, an American businessman of Hungarian descent, joined their ranks (<a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-10" title="Soyuz TMA-10">Soyuz TMA-10</a>). href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tourism beyond Earth orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_on_the_Moon" title="Tourism on the Moon">Tourism on the Moon</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ongoing_projects_3">Ongoing projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ongoing projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>In February 2017, <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> announced that substantial deposits from two individuals had been received by <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> for a Moon loop flight using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">free return trajectory</a> and that this could happen as soon as late 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Musk said that the cost of the mission would be "comparable" to that of sending an astronaut to the International Space Station, about $70 million US dollars in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In February 2018, Elon Musk announced the Falcon Heavy rocket would not be used for crewed missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spacenews_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spacenews-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The proposal changed in 2018 to use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceX_lunar_tourism_mission" title="SpaceX lunar tourism mission">BFR system</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spacenews_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spacenews-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grush_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-grush-2">[2]</a></sup>In September 2018, <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> revealed the passenger for the trip, <a href="/wiki/Yusaku_Maezawa" title="Yusaku Maezawa">Yusaku Maezawa</a> during a livestream. Yusaku Maezawa described the plan for his trip in further detail, dubbed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DearMoon_Project" title="DearMoon Project">DearMoon Project</a>, intending to take 6-8 artists with him on the journey to inspire the artists to create new art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Elon Musk said he hopes BFR will be ready for an unpiloted trip to Mars in 2022. The crewed flight will follow in 2024<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures" title="Space Adventures">Space Adventures</a> Ltd. have announced that they are working on <a href="/wiki/DSE-Alpha" title="DSE-Alpha">DSE-Alpha</a>, a circumlunar mission to the moon, with the price per passenger being $100,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancelled_projects_3">Cancelled projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cancelled projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Excalibur_Almaz" title="Excalibur Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</a> proposed to take three tourists in a flyby around the Moon, using modified <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> space station modules, in a low-energy trajectory flyby around the Moon. The trip would last around 6 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist1_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist1-55">[55]</a></sup> However, their equipment was never launched and is to be converted into an educational exhibit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Golden_Spike_Company" title="Golden Spike Company">Golden Spike Company</a> was an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_transport" title="Space transport">space transport</a> startup active from 2010&#8211;2013. The company held the objective to offer private commercial space transportation services to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_landing" title="Lunar landing">surface of the Moon</a>. The company\'s website was quietly taken offline in September 2015.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Inspiration_Mars_Foundation" title="Inspiration Mars Foundation">Inspiration Mars Foundation</a> is an American <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">nonprofit organization</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Tito" title="Dennis Tito">Dennis Tito</a> that proposed to launch a crewed mission to <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">flyby</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in January 2018,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borenstein_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Borenstein-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boucher_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boucher-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sonnenberg_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnenberg-59">[59]</a></sup> or 2021 if they missed the first deadline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parabolicarc.com_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-parabolicarc.com-60">[60]</a></sup> Their website became defunct by late 2015 but it is archived by the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Archive" title="Internet Archive">Internet Archive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web.archive.org_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-web.archive.org-61">[61]</a></sup> The Foundation\'s future plans are unclear.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legality">Legality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> signed in 1967, the launch operator\'s nationality and the launch site\'s location determine which country is responsible for any damages occurred from a launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After valuable resources were detected on the Moon, private companies began to formulate methods to extract the resources. Article II of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a> dictates that "outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, is not subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> However, countries have the right to freely explore the Moon and any resources collected are property of that country when they return.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In December 2005, the US government released a set of proposed rules for space tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> These included screening procedures and training for emergency situations, but not health requirements.\n</p><p>Under current US law, any company proposing to launch paying passengers from American soil on a suborbital rocket must receive a license from the Federal Aviation Administration\'s <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Commercial_Space_Transportation" title="Office of Commercial Space Transportation">Office of Commercial Space Transportation</a> (FAA/AST). The licensing process focuses on public safety and safety of property, and the details can be found in the Code of Federal Regulations, Title 14, Chapter III.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAAReg_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAAReg-65">[65]</a></sup> This is in accordance with the Commercial Space Launch Amendments Act passed by Congress in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpaceTourismCongress_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceTourismCongress-66">[66]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2010, the New Mexico legislature passed the Spaceflight Informed Consent Act. The SICA gives legal protection to companies who provide private space flights in the case of accidental harm or death to individuals. Participants sign an Informed Consent waiver, dictating that spaceflight operators cannot be held liable in the "death of a participant resulting from the inherent risks of space flight activities". Operators are however not covered in the case of gross negligence or willful misconduct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitudes_toward_space_tourism">Attitudes toward space tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Attitudes toward space tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A web-based survey suggested that over 70% of those surveyed wanted less than or equal to 2 weeks in space; in addition, 88% wanted to spacewalk (only 14% of these would do it for a 50% premium), and 21% wanted a hotel or space station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.comSurvey_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space.comSurvey-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The concept has met with some criticism from some, including politicians, notably <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Verheugen" title="G&#252;nter Verheugen">G&#252;nter Verheugen</a>, vice-president of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>, who said of the EADS Astrium Space Tourism Project: "It\'s only for the super-rich, which is against my social convictions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 2010 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Research_Letters" title="Geophysical Research Letters">Geophysical Research Letters</a></i> raised concerns that the growing commercial spaceflight industry could accelerate global warming. The study, funded by NASA and The Aerospace Corporation, simulated the impact of 1,000 suborbital launches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_rocket" title="Hybrid rocket">hybrid rockets</a> from a single location, calculating that this would release a total of 600 tonnes of black carbon into the stratosphere. They found that the resultant layer of soot particles remained relatively localized, with only 20% of the carbon straying into the southern hemisphere, thus creating a strong hemispherical asymmetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRL_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRL-70">[70]</a></sup> This unbalance would cause the temperature to decrease by about 0.4&#160;&#176;C (0.72&#160;&#176;F) in the tropics and subtropics, whereas the temperature at the poles would increase by between 0.2 and 1&#160;&#176;C (0.36 and 1.80&#160;&#176;F). The ozone layer would also be affected, with the tropics losing up to 1.7% of ozone cover, and the polar regions gaining 5&#8211;6%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The researchers stressed that these results should not be taken as "a precise forecast of the climate response to a specific launch rate of a specific rocket type", but as a demonstration of the sensitivity of the atmosphere to the large-scale disruption that commercial space tourism could bring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRL_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-GRL-70">[70]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_advocacy">Education and advocacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Education and advocacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Several organizations have been formed to promote the space tourism industry, including the Space Tourism Society, Space Future, and HobbySpace. <i>UniGalactic Space Travel Magazine</i> is a bi-monthly educational publication covering space tourism and space exploration developments in companies like SpaceX, Orbital Sciences, Virgin Galactic and organizations like NASA.\n</p><p>Classes in space tourism are currently taught at the <a href="/wiki/Rochester_Institute_of_Technology" title="Rochester Institute of Technology">Rochester Institute of Technology</a> in New York,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_potential">Economic potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economic potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 2010 report from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>, titled "The Economic Impact of Commercial Space Transportation on the U. S Economy in 2009", cites studies done by Futron, an aerospace and technology-consulting firm, which predict that space tourism could become a billion-dollar market within 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In addition, in the nearly two decades since Dennis Tito journeyed to the International Space Station, eight private citizens have paid the $20 million fee to travel to space. Space Adventures suggests that this number could increase fifteen-fold by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> These figures do not include other private space agencies such as Virgin Galactic, which as of 2014 has sold approximately 700 tickets priced at $200,000 or $250,000 dollars each and has accepted more than $80 million in deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
