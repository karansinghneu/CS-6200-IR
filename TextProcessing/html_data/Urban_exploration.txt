Photography and historical interest/documentation are heavily featured in the hobby and, although it may sometimes involve trespassing onto private property, this is not always the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGATE_062008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFGATE_062008-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Urban exploration may also be referred to as <b>draining</b> (an alternate form of urban exploring where drains are explored), <b>urban spelunking</b>, <b>urban rock climbing</b>, <b>urban caving</b>, or <b>building hacking</b>.\n</p><p>The nature of this activity presents various risks, including both physical danger and, if done illegally and/or without permission, the possibility of arrest and punishment. Some activities associated with urban exploration violate local or regional laws and certain broadly interpreted <a href="/wiki/Anti-terrorism_legislation" title="Anti-terrorism legislation">anti-terrorism laws</a>, or can be considered <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespassing</a> or invasion of privacy.<sup class="reference" At times, sites are entered first by locals and may suffer from large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> and other acts of <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>, while other locations may be better preserved. Although targets of exploration vary from one country to another, high-profile abandonments include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_abandoned_amusement_parks" title="List of abandoned amusement parks">amusement parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grain_elevator" title="Grain elevator">grain elevators</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factories" title="Factories">factories</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">power plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">missile silos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" hospital">asylums</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schools" title="Schools">schools</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poor_houses" title="Poor houses">poor houses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanatoriums</a>.\n</p><p>In Japan, abandoned infrastructure is known as <i>haikyo</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">&#24259;&#34394;</span>)</span> (literally "ruins"), but the term is synonymous with the practice of urban exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Haikyo</i> <span style="font-weight:normal"></span> are particularly common in Japan because of its rapid industrialization (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hashima_Island" title="Hashima Island">Hashima Island</a>), damage during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_asset_price_bubble" title="Japanese asset price bubble">1980s real estate bubble</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 T&#333;hoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 T&#333;hoku earthquake and tsunami</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many explorers find decay of uninhabited space to be profoundly beautiful, and some are also proficient <a href="/wiki/Photographer" title="Photographer">freelance photographers</a> who document what they see, as is the case with those who document some of the <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> of the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Abandoned sites are also popular among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historians" title="Historians">historians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Historic_preservation" title="Historic preservation">preservationists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Architects" title="Architects">architects</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologists" title="Archaeologists">archaeologists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_Archaeology" title="Industrial Archaeology">industrial archaeologists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ghost_hunting" title="Ghost hunting">ghost hunters</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_buildings">Active buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Active buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another aspect of urban exploration is the practice of exploring active or <i>in use</i> buildings which includes gaining access, by various means, to secured or "member-only" areas, mechanical rooms, roofs, elevator rooms, abandoned floors, and other normally unseen parts of working buildings. The term "infiltration" is often associated with the exploration of active structures. People entering restricted areas may be committing <a href="/wiki/Trespass" title="Trespass">trespass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosecution" title="Prosecution">prosecution</a> may result.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catacombs">Catacombs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Catacombs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg/220px-Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg/330px-Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg/440px-Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catacombes_de_Paris.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Catacombs (France)</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Catacombs" title="Catacombs">Catacombs</a> such as those found in <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Paris" title="Catacombs of Paris">Paris</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Rome" title="Catacombs of Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odessa_Catacombs" title="Odessa Catacombs">Odessa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_San_Gennaro" title="Catacombs of San Gennaro">Naples</a> have been investigated by urban explorers. The <a href="/wiki/Mines_of_Paris" title="Mines of Paris">Mines of Paris</a>, comprising much of the underground tunnels that are not open to public tourism like the catacombs, have been considered the "<a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>" by some due to their extensive nature and history. Explorers of these spaces are known as <a href="/wiki/Cataphile" title="Cataphile">cataphiles</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sewers_and_storm_drains">Sewers and storm drains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sewers and storm drains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="533" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg/160px-St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg/240px-St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg/320px-St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:St_Paul_storm_drain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Storm drain outfall in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">Saint Paul, Minnesota</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Entry into <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a>, or "draining", is another common form of urban exploration. Groups devoted to the task have arisen, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cave_Clan" title="Cave Clan">Cave Clan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Draining has a specialized set of guidelines, the foremost of which is "When it rains, no drains!" The dangers of becoming entrapped, washed away, or killed increase dramatically during a heavy rainfall.\n</p><p>A small subset of explorers enter <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary sewers</a>. Sometimes they are the only connection to caves or other subterranean features. Sewers are among the most dangerous locations to explore owing to risk of poisoning by buildups of toxic gas (commonly <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transit_tunnels">Transit tunnels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transit tunnels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="604" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg/220px-DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg/330px-DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg/440px-DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DPS-01_and_AS1A_in_D6_tunnel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diesel trains in a tunnel of <a href="/wiki/Metro-2" title="Metro-2">Metro-2</a> D6 line in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Exploring active and abandoned subway and underground railway tunnels, bores, and <a href="/wiki/Ghost_station" title="Ghost station">stations</a> is often considered to be trespassing and can result in civil prosecution, due to security concerns. As a result, this type of exploration is rarely publicized. An important exception to this is the abandoned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rochester_Industrial_and_Rapid_Transit_Railway" title="Rochester Industrial and Rapid Transit Railway">subway of Rochester, New York</a>, the only American city to have an abandoned, formerly used, subway system. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati_subway" title="Cincinnati subway">Cincinnati subway</a> is also abandoned, but was never completed and placed into service. In <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, there are a number of stations on the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> network that have been closed over the years, with <a href="/wiki/Aldwych_tube_station" title="Aldwych tube station">Aldwych tube station</a> being a popular location for explorers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_tunnels">Utility tunnels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Utility tunnels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Utility_tunnel" title="Utility tunnel">Utility tunnel</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2370" data-file-width="3194" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg/220px-Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg/330px-Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg/440px-Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schiffbau_tunnel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Utility_tunnel" title="Utility tunnel">Utility tunnel</a> in the center of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/University" title="University">Universities</a>, and other large institutions such as hospitals, often distribute hazardous <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated steam</a> for heating or cooling buildings from a <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">central heating plant</a>. These pipes are generally run through utility tunnels, which are often intended to be accessible solely for the purposes of maintenance. Nevertheless, many of these steam tunnels, especially those on college campuses, often also have a tradition of exploration by students. This practice was once called "vadding" at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, though students there now refer to it as <a href="/wiki/Roof_and_tunnel_hacking" title="Roof and tunnel hacking">roof and tunnel hacking</a>.\n</p><p>Some steam tunnels have dirt floors, poor lighting and temperatures upwards of 45&#160;&#176;C (113&#160;&#176;F). Others have concrete floors, bright light, and more moderate temperatures. Most steam tunnels have large intake fans to bring in fresh air and push the hot air out the back, and these fans may start without warning.  Most active steam tunnels do not contain airborne <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>, but proper breathing protection may be required for other respiratory hazards. Experienced explorers are very cautious inside active utility tunnels, since pipes can spew boiling hot water or steam from leaky valves or pressure relief blowoffs.  Frequently there are puddles of muddy water on the floor, making slips and falls a special concern near hot pipes.\n</p><p>Steam tunnels have generally been secured more heavily in recent years, due to their frequent use for carrying communications network backbone cables,  increased safety and liability concerns, and perceived risk of their use in terrorist activities.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The rise in the popularity of urban exploration can be attributed to its increased media attention. Recent television shows, such as <i>Urban Explorers</i> on the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Channel" title="Discovery Channel">Discovery Channel</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV%27s_Fear" title="MTV\'s Fear">MTV\'s Fear</a></i>, and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghost_Hunting" title="Ghost Hunting">Ghost Hunting</a></i> exploits of <a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic_Paranormal_Society" title="The Atlantic Paranormal Society">The Atlantic Paranormal Society</a> have packaged the hobby for a popular audience.  The fictional film <i><a href="/wiki/After..._(film)" title="After... (film)">After...</a></i> (2006), a hallucinatory thriller set in Moscow\'s underground subways, features urban explorers caught up in extreme situations. Talks and exhibits on urban exploration have appeared at the fifth and sixth <a href="/wiki/Hackers_on_Planet_Earth" title="Hackers on Planet Earth">Hackers on Planet Earth</a> Conference, complementing numerous newspaper articles and interviews.\n</p><p>Another source of popular information is <i><a href="/wiki/Cities_of_the_Underworld" title="Cities of the Underworld">Cities of the Underworld</a></i>, a documentary series which ran for three seasons on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="History (U.S. TV channel)">History Channel</a>, starting in 2007.  This series roamed around the world, showing little-known underground structures in remote locales, as well as right under the feet of densely packed city-dwellers.\n</p><p>With the rise in the relative popularity of the hobby due to this increased focus, there has been increasing discussion on whether the extra attention has been beneficial to urban exploration as a whole. The unspoken rule of urban exploring is "take nothing but photographs, leave nothing but footprints", but because of the rising popularity, many individuals who may have other intentions are creating a concern among many property owners.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_legality">Safety and legality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Safety and legality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1228" data-file-width="2203" decoding="async" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg/220px-Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg/330px-Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg/440px-Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hill_60_illowra_battery_port_kembla.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Illowra_Battery" title="Illowra Battery">Hill 60 bunker</a>. On the right is a corridor leading to the bunker complex and on the left is the "mushroom tunnel".</div></div></div>\n<p>Urban exploration is a hobby that comes with a number of inherent dangers. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_drains" title="Storm drains">storm drains</a> are not designed with human access as their primary use. They can be subject to <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a> and bad air. There have been a number of deaths in storm water drains, but these are usually during floods, and the victims are normally not urban explorers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many old abandoned structures feature hazards such as unstable structures, unsafe floors, broken glass, the presence of unknown chemicals and other harmful substances (most notably <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>), <a href="/wiki/Stray_voltage" title="Stray voltage">stray voltage</a>, and entrapment hazards.  Other risks include freely roaming <a href="/wiki/Guard_dog" title="Guard dog">guard dogs</a> and hostile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squatter" title="Squatter">squatters</a>. Some abandoned locations may be heavily guarded with <a href="/wiki/Motion_detector" title="Motion detector">motion detectors</a> and active security patrols, while others are more easily accessible and carry less risk of discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">Asbestos</a> is a long-term health risk for urban explorers, along with breathing in contaminants from dried <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">bird feces</a>, which can cause a condition known as <a href="/wiki/Bird_fancier%27s_lung" title="Bird fancier\'s lung">pigeon-breeder\'s lung</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Hypersensitivity_pneumonitis" title="Hypersensitivity pneumonitis">hypersensitivity pneumonitis</a>. Urban explorers may use dust masks and <a href="/wiki/Respirator" title="Respirator">respirators</a> to alleviate this danger. Some sites are occasionally used by substance abusers for either recreation or waste disposal, and there may be used or infected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypodermic_syringe" title="Hypodermic syringe">syringe needles</a> en route, such as those commonly used with <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>.\n</p><p>The growing popularity of the activity has resulted not just in increased attention from explorers, but also from vandals and law enforcement. The illicit aspects of urban exploring, which may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trespassing" title="Trespassing">trespassing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">breaking and entering</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> have brought along with them critical articles in mainstream newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_062008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PPG_062008-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the website of the Sydney <a href="/wiki/Cave_Clan" title="Cave Clan">Cave Clan</a> was shut down by lawyers for the Roads and Traffic Authority of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, after they raised concerns that the portal could "risk human safety and threaten the security of its infrastructure".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_062008_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-PPG_062008-10">[10]</a></sup> Another website belonging to the Bangor Explorers Guild was criticized by the <a href="/wiki/Maine_State_Police" title="Maine State Police">Maine State Police</a> for potentially encouraging behavior that "could get someone hurt or killed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_062008_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-PPG_062008-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Transit_Commission" title="Toronto Transit Commission">Toronto Transit Commission</a> has used the Internet to crimp subway tunnel explorations, going as far as to send investigators to various explorers\' homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_062008_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-PPG_062008-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ninjalicious" title="Ninjalicious">Jeff Chapman</a>, who authored <i>Infiltration</i>, stated that genuine urban explorers "never vandalize, steal or damage anything". The thrill comes from that of "discovery and a few nice pictures".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPG_062008_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-PPG_062008-10">[10]</a></sup> Some explorers will also request permission for entry in advance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-legal-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rooftopping">Rooftopping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Photographic Leave nothing but footprints."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TQP_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TQP-14">[14]</a></sup> Some are photographers who specialize in documenting urban ruins and scenes of industrial decay. Moore. Other well-known photographers, such as <a href="/wiki/Jan_Saudek" title="Jan Saudek">Jan Saudek</a>, use the interiors of abandoned buildings as backdrops for their figurative and portrait works.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_and_technology">Methods and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Methods and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some urban explorers use head-cams such as <a href="/wiki/GoPro" title="GoPro">GoPro</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Helmet_camera" title="Helmet camera">helmet cameras</a> for videos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some also use <a href="/wiki/Quadcopter" title="Quadcopter">quadcopter</a> <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">drones</a> for exploration and recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scmp1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-scmp1-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Location-based_game" title="Location-based game">location-based games</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingress_(game)" title="Ingress (game)">Ingress</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and the following <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Go" title="Pok&#233;mon Go">Pok&#233;mon Go</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> based on the former have urban exploration elements. While some are concerned with keeping certain sites secret from the public at large, mainly to prevent vandalism, several apps dedicated to urban exploration exist.<sup class="reference"
