href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>, the <b>age of the universe</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_time" title="Cosmological time">time</a> elapsed since the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)#2015_data_release" title="Planck (spacecraft)">current measurement</a> of the age of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001137990000000000&#9824;</span>13.799<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>0.021</span> billion (10<sup>9</sup>) years within the <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">Lambda-CDM concordance model</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planck_2015_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planck_2015-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_on_Planck_2015_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_on_Planck_2015-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Measurement_uncertainty" title="Measurement uncertainty">uncertainty</a> has been narrowed down to 21 million years, based on a number of projects that all give extremely close figures for the age. These include studies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_background_radiation" title="Microwave background radiation">microwave background radiation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurements</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)"><i>Planck</i> spacecraft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe</a> and other probes. Measurements of the cosmic background radiation give the cooling time of the universe since the Big Bang,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv-20121220_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv-20121220-3">[3]</a></sup> and measurements of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift#Extragalactic_observations" title="Red shift">expansion rate</a> of the universe can be used to calculate its approximate age by extrapolating backwards in time.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Age_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">Lambda-CDM concordance model</a> describes the evolution of the universe from a very uniform, hot, dense primordial state to its present state over a span of about 13.8 billion years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_time" title="Cosmological time">cosmological time</a>. This model is well understood theoretically and strongly supported by recent high-precision <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_observation" title="Astronomical observation">astronomical observations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">WMAP</a>. In contrast, theories of the origin of the primordial state remain very speculative. If one extrapolates the Lambda-CDM model backward from the earliest well-understood state, it quickly (within a small fraction of a second) reaches a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">singularity</a>. This is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Initial_singularity" title="Initial singularity">initial singularity</a>" or the "<a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> singularity". This singularity is not understood as having a physical significance in the usual sense, but it is convenient to quote times measured "since the Big Bang" even though they do not correspond to a physically measurable time. For example, "10<sup>&#8722;6</sup> seconds after the Big Bang" is a well-defined era in the universe\'s evolution. If one referred to the same era as "13.8 billion years minus 10<sup>&#8722;6</sup> seconds ago", the precision of the meaning would be lost because the minuscule latter time interval is eclipsed by uncertainty in the former.\n</p><p>Though the universe might in theory have a longer history, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> presently use "age of the universe" to mean the duration of the Lambda-CDM expansion, or equivalently the elapsed time since the Big Bang in the current <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_limits">Observational limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Age_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observational limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the universe must be at least as old as the oldest things in it, there are a number of observations which put a lower limit on the age of the universe; these include the temperature of the coolest <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarfs</a>, which gradually cool as they age, and the dimmest <a href="/wiki/Turnoff_point" title="Turnoff point">turnoff point</a> of <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stars" title="Stars">stars</a> in clusters (lower-mass stars spend a greater amount of time on the main sequence, so the lowest-mass stars that have evolved off of the main sequence set a minimum age).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmological_parameters">Cosmological parameters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Age_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cosmological parameters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg/400px-Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg/600px-Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg/800px-Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mplwp_universe_scale_evolution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The age of the universe can be determined by measuring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a> today and extrapolating back in time with the observed value of density parameters (&#937;). Before the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a>, it was believed that the universe was matter-dominated (<a href="/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93de_Sitter_universe" title="Einstein&#8211;de Sitter universe">Einstein&#8211;de Sitter universe</a>, green curve). Note that the <a href="/wiki/De_Sitter_universe" title="De Sitter universe">de Sitter universe</a> has infinite age, while the <a href="/wiki/Big_Crunch" title="Big Crunch">closed</a> universe has the least age.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="858" data-file-width="930" decoding="async" height="369" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png/400px-Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png/600px-Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png/800px-Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Age_Universe_Planck_2013.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The value of the age correction factor, <i>F</i>, is shown as a function of two <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmological parameters</a>: the current fractional matter density &#937;<sub><i>m</i></sub> and cosmological constant density &#937;<sub><i>&#923;</i></sub>.  The <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">best-fit values</a> of these parameters are shown by the box in the upper left; the matter-dominated universe is shown by the star in the lower right.</div></div></div>\n<p>The problem of determining the age of the universe is closely tied to the problem of determining the values of the cosmological parameters. Today this is largely carried out in the context of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda_CDM_model" title="Lambda CDM model">&#923;CDM</a> model, where the universe is assumed to contain normal (baryonic) matter, cold <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>, radiation (including both <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>), and a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>.  The fractional contribution of each to the current energy density of the universe is given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Density_parameter" title="Density parameter">density parameters</a> &#937;<sub><i>m</i></sub>, &#937;<sub><i>r</i></sub>, and &#937;<sub>&#923;</sub>.  The full &#923;CDM model is described by a number of other parameters, but for the purpose of computing its age these three, along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble parameter</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle H_{0}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>H</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>0</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle H_{0}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="H_{0}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43910602a221b7a4c373791f94793e3008622070" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.985ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>, are the most important.\n</p><p>If one has accurate measurements of these parameters, then the age of the universe can be determined by using the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann_equations" title="Friedmann equations">Friedmann equation</a>.  This equation relates the rate of change in the <a href="/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology)" title="Scale factor (cosmology)">scale factor</a> <i>a</i>(<i>t</i>) to the matter content of the universe.  Turning this relation around, we can calculate the change in time per change in scale factor and thus calculate the total age of the universe by <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integrating</a> this formula. For the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda_CDM_model" title="Lambda CDM model">Planck values</a> (&#937;<sub><i>m</i></sub>, &#937;<sub><i>&#923;</i></sub>) = (0.3086, 0.6914), shown by the box in the upper left corner of the figure, this correction factor is about <i>F</i> = 0.956. For a flat universe without any cosmological constant, shown by the star in the lower right corner, <i>F</i> = &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> is much smaller and thus the universe is younger for a fixed value of the Hubble parameter. To make this figure, &#937;<sub><i>r</i></sub> is held constant (roughly equivalent to holding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_Microwave_Background" title="Cosmic Microwave Background">CMB</a> temperature constant) and the curvature density parameter is fixed by the value of the other three.\n</p><p>Apart from the Planck satellite, the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMAP" title="WMAP">WMAP</a>) was instrumental in establishing an accurate age of the universe, though other measurements must be folded in to gain an accurate number.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMB" title="CMB">CMB</a> measurements are very good at constraining the matter content &#937;<sub><i>m</i></sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and curvature parameter &#937;<sub><i>k</i></sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anim3_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-anim3-8">[8]</a></sup>  It is not as sensitive to &#937;<sub>&#923;</sub> directly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anim3_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-anim3-8">[8]</a></sup> partly because the cosmological constant becomes important only at low redshift.  The most accurate determinations of the Hubble parameter <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> come from <a href="/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova" title="Type Ia supernova">Type Ia supernovae</a>.  Combining these measurements leads to the generally accepted value for the age of the universe quoted above.\n</p><p>The cosmological constant makes the universe "older" for fixed values of the other parameters. This is significant, since before the cosmological constant became generally accepted, the Big Bang model had difficulty explaining why <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular clusters</a> in the Milky Way appeared to be far older than the age of the universe as calculated from the Hubble parameter and a matter-only universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Introducing the cosmological constant allows the universe to be older than these clusters, as well as explaining other features that the matter-only cosmological model could not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="WMAP">WMAP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a id="Assumption_of_strong_priors">Assumption of strong priors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Age_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Assumption of strong priors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Calculating the age of the universe is accurate only if the assumptions built into the models being used to estimate it are also accurate. This is referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_priors" title="Strong priors">strong priors</a> and essentially involves stripping the potential errors in other parts of the model to render the accuracy of actual observational data directly into the concluded result.  Although this is not a valid procedure in all contexts (as noted in the accompanying caveat: "based on the fact we have assumed the underlying model we used is correct"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), the age given is thus accurate to the specified error (since this error represents the error in the instrument used to gather the raw data input into the model).\n</p><p>The age of the universe based on the best fit to <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)#2015_data_release" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck 2015 data</a> alone is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001138130000000000&#9824;</span>13.813<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>0.038</span> billion years (the estimate of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001137990000000000&#9824;</span>13.799<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>0.021</span> billion years uses Gaussian <a href="/wiki/Prior_probability" title="Prior probability">priors</a> based on earlier estimates from other studies to determine the combined uncertainty). This number represents an accurate "direct" measurement of the age of the universe (other methods typically involve <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a> and the age of the oldest stars in globular clusters, etc.). It is possible to use different methods for determining the same parameter (in this case &#8211; the age of the universe) and arrive at different answers with no overlap in the "errors". To best avoid the problem, it is common to show two sets of uncertainties; one related to the actual measurement and the other related to the systematic errors of the model being used.\n</p><p>An important component to the analysis of data used to determine the age of the universe (e.g. from <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck</a>) therefore is to use a <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_statistics" title="Bayesian statistics">Bayesian statistical</a> analysis, which normalizes the results based upon the priors (i.e. the model).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmap_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmap-13">[13]</a></sup> This quantifies any uncertainty in the accuracy of a measurement due to a particular model used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Age_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="toccolours searchaux" id="Container" style="text-align: left; clear:both;"></div><div class="toccolours" id="Caption" style="margin:0 0.5em; float:left; padding:0 auto; border-style:none; clear:both; text-align:center; width:20.5em"><div style="float: left;"><small><a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#One_dimension" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Axis scale</a>: <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">billion years</a></small></div><br/><span style="float:right;margin-right:0.5em"><img alt="The image above contains clickable links" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="133" decoding="async" height="27" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Interactive_icon.svg/18px-Interactive_icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Interactive_icon.svg/27px-Interactive_icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Interactive_icon.svg/36px-Interactive_icon.svg.png 2x" title="The image above contains clickable links" width="18"/></span><div style="float: left;"><small>Also see: <i><a href="/wiki/Template:Human_timeline" title="Template:Human timeline">Human timeline</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Template:Life_timeline" title="Template:Life timeline">Life timeline</a></i></small></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_age_problem" title="Cosmic age problem">Cosmic age problem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">Cosmic microwave background</a></div>\n<p>In the 18th century, the concept that the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">age of the Earth</a> was millions, if not billions, of years began to appear. However, most scientists throughout the 19th century and into the first decades of the 20th century presumed that the universe itself was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_State_theory" title="Steady State theory">Steady State</a> and eternal, with maybe stars coming and going but no changes occurring at the largest scale known at the time.\n</p><p>The first scientific theories indicating that the age of the universe might be finite were the studies of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, formalized in the mid-19th century. The concept of <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> dictates that if the universe (or any other closed system) were infinitely old, then everything inside would be at the same temperature, and thus there would be no stars and no life. No scientific explanation for this contradiction was put forth at the time.\n</p><p>In 1915 <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> published the theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and in 1917 constructed the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmological_model" title="Cosmological model">cosmological model</a> based on his theory. In order to remain consistent with a steady state universe, Einstein added what was later called a <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> to his equations. However, already in 1922, also using  Einstein\'s theory, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann" title="Alexander Friedmann">Alexander Friedmann</a>, and independently five years later <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema&#238;tre">Georges Lema&#238;tre</a>, showed that the universe cannot be static and must be either expanding or contracting. Einstein\'s model of a static universe was in addition proved unstable by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a>.\n</p><p>The first direct observational hint that the universe has a finite age came from the observations of \'<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recession_velocity" title="Recession velocity">recession velocities</a>\', mostly by <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a>, combined with distances to the \'<a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>\' (<a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>) by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> in a  work published in 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Earlier in the 20th century, Hubble and others resolved individual stars within certain nebulae, thus determining that they were galaxies, similar to, but external to, our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a>. In addition, these galaxies were very large and very far away. <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">Spectra</a> taken of these distant galaxies showed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift" title="Red shift">red shift</a> in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines" title="Spectral lines">spectral lines</a> presumably caused by the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler effect</a>, thus indicating that these galaxies were moving away from the Earth. In addition, the farther away these galaxies seemed to be (the dimmer they appeared to us) the greater was their redshift, and thus the faster they seemed to be moving away. This was the first direct evidence that the universe is not static but expanding. The first estimate of the age of the universe came from the calculation of when all of the objects must have started speeding out from the same point. Hubble\'s initial value for the universe\'s age was very low, as the galaxies were assumed to be much closer than later observations found them to be.\n</p><p>The first reasonably accurate measurement of the rate of expansion of the universe, a numerical value now known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a>, was made in 1958 by astronomer <a href="/wiki/Allan_Sandage" title="Allan Sandage">Allan Sandage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His measured value for the Hubble constant came very close to the value range generally accepted today.\n</p><p>However Sandage, like Einstein, did not believe his own results at the time of discovery. His value for the age of the universe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What was his value for the age of the universe? (February 2014)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was too short to reconcile with the 25-billion-year age estimated at that time for the oldest known <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Sandage and other astronomers repeated these measurements numerous times, attempting to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a> and thus increase the resulting age for the universe. Sandage even proposed new theories of <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogony</a> to explain this discrepancy. This issue was finally resolved by improvements in the theoretical models used for estimating the ages of stars. As of 2013, using the latest models for stellar evolution, the estimated age of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oldest_star" title="Oldest star">oldest known star</a> is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001144600000000000&#9824;</span>14.46<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>0.8</span> billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-arxiv-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The discovery of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">microwave cosmic background radiation</a> announced in 1965<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj142:419_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj142:419-20">[20]</a></sup> finally brought an effective end to the remaining scientific uncertainty over the expanding universe. It was a chance result from work by two teams less than 60 miles apart.  In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Arno_Allan_Penzias" title="Arno Allan Penzias">Arno Penzias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson" title="Robert Woodrow Wilson">Robert Wilson</a> were trying to detect <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio wave</a> <a href="/wiki/Echo" title="Echo">echoes</a> with a supersensitive antenna. The antenna persistently detected a low, steady, mysterious <a href="/wiki/Noise_(electronics)" title="Noise (electronics)">noise</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_radiation" title="Microwave radiation">microwave region</a> that was evenly spread over the sky, and was present day and night. After testing, they became certain that the signal did not come from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">our galaxy</a>, but from outside our own galaxy, but could not explain it. At the same time another team, <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke" title="Robert H. Dicke">Robert H. Dicke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P._J._E._Peebles" title="P. J. E. Peebles">Jim Peebles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Todd_Wilkinson" title="David Todd Wilkinson">David Wilkinson</a>, were attempting to detect low level noise which might be left over from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> and could prove whether the Big Bang theory was correct. The two teams realized that the detected noise was in fact radiation left over from the Big Bang, and that this was strong evidence that the theory was correct. Since then, a great deal of other evidence has strengthened and confirmed this conclusion, and refined the estimated age of the universe to its current figure.\n</p><p>The space probes WMAP, launched in 2001, and <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck</a>, launched in 2009, produced data that determines the Hubble constant and the age of the universe independent of galaxy distances, removing the largest source of error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmap_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-wmap-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
