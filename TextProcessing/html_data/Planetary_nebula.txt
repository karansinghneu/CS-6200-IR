star</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>planetary nebula</b>, abbreviated as PN or plural PNe, is a type of <a href="/wiki/Emission_nebula" title="Emission nebula">emission nebula</a> consisting of an expanding, glowing shell of <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionized</a> gas ejected from <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> late in their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankowskietal2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term "planetary nebula" is arguably a <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">misnomer</a>, since this phenomenon is not associated with observations of actual <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, and perhaps was derived from the planet-like round shape of these <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a> as observed by astronomers through early <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_telescope" title="History of the telescope">telescopes</a>.  The term may have originated in the 1780s with the English astronomer <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> who described these nebulae as resembling planets; however, as early as January 1779, the French astronomer <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Darquier_de_Pellepoix" title="Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix">Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix</a> described in his observations of the <a href="/wiki/Ring_Nebula" title="Ring Nebula">Ring Nebula</a>, "...a very dim but perfectly outlined; it is as large as Jupiter and resembles a fading planet"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darquier_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Darquier-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_4-0"><a href="#cite_note--4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinicke2018_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steinicke2018-5">[5]</a></sup>. Whatever the true origin of the term, the label "planetary nebula" became ingrained in the terminology used by astronomers to categorize these types of nebulae, and is still used by astronomers today. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seds2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-seds2013-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubbleSite1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hubbleSite1997-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most planetary nebulae form at the end of the star\'s life, and are a relatively short-lived phenomenon, lasting perhaps a few tens of thousands of years, compared to a typical stellar lifetime spanning several billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FrewParker2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FrewParker2010-8">[8]</a></sup> Once most of the red giant\'s atmosphere is dissipated,  <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> from the hot luminous core, called a planetary nebula nucleus (PNN), ionizes the ejected material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankowskietal2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-2">[2]</a></sup> Absorbed <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light then energises the shell of nebulous gas around the central star, causing it to appear as a brightly coloured planetary nebula.\n</p><p>Planetary nebulae likely play a crucial role in the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">evolution of the Milky Way</a> by expelling elements to the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> from stars where those <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">elements</a> were created. Planetary nebulae are observed in more distant <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>, yielding useful information about their chemical abundances.\n</p><p>Starting from the 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> images revealed that many planetary nebulae have extremely complex and varied morphologies. About one-fifth are roughly spherical, but the majority are not spherically symmetric. The mechanisms that produce such a wide variety of shapes and features are not yet well understood, but <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary central stars</a>, stellar winds and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> may play a role.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" It was observed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Messier" title="Charles Messier">Charles Messier</a> in 1764 and listed as M27 in his <a href="/wiki/Messier_object" title="Messier object">catalogue</a> of nebulous objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup> To early observers with low-resolution telescopes, M27 and subsequently discovered planetary nebulae resembled the giant planets like <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a>, discoverer of Uranus, perhaps coined the term "planetary nebula".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2007_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2007-10">[10]</a></sup> However, in as early as January 1779, the French astronomer <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Darquier_de_Pellepoix" title="Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix">Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix</a> described in his observations of the <a href="/wiki/Ring_Nebula" title="Ring Nebula">Ring Nebula</a>, "a very dull nebula, but perfectly outlined; as large as Jupiter and looks like a fading planet.""<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Darquier_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Darquier-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_4-1"><a href="#cite_note--4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinicke2018_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Steinicke2018-5">[5]</a></sup> Whatever the true origin of the term, the label "planetary nebula" became ingrained in the terminology used by astronomers to categorize these types of nebulae, and is still in use by astronomers today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seds2013_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-seds2013-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hubbleSite1997_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-hubbleSite1997-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The true nature of these objects was uncertain, and Herschel first thought the objects were stars surrounded by material that was condensing into planets rather than what is now known to be evidence of dead stars that have incinerated any orbiting planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1782<span class="noprint"> (237&#160;years ago)</span><span style="display:none">&#160;(<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1782</span>)</span>, <a "Saturn Nebula"), upon which he used the term "planetary nebula".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" I have already found four that have a visible diameter of between 15 and 30 seconds. These bodies appear to have a disk that is rather like a planet, that is to say, of equal brightness all over, round or somewhat oval, and about as well defined in outline as the disk of the planets, of a light strong enough to be visible with an ordinary telescope of only one foot, yet they have only the appearance of a star of about ninth magnitude."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Herschel assigned these to Class IV of his catalogue of "nebulae", eventually listing 78 "planetary nebulae", most of which are in fact galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectra">Spectra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spectra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The nature of planetary nebulae remained unknown until the first <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> observations were made in the mid-19th century. Using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prism</a> to disperse their light, <a href="/wiki/William_Huggins" title="William Huggins">William Huggins</a> was one of the earliest astronomers to study the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_spectrum" title="Optical spectrum">optical spectra</a> of astronomical objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2007_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2007-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On August 29, 1864, Huggins was the first to analyze the spectrum of a planetary nebula when he observed <a href="/wiki/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula" title="Cat\'s Eye Nebula">Cat\'s Eye Nebula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup> His observations of stars had shown that their spectra consisted of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuum_(theory)" title="Continuum (theory)">continuum</a> of radiation with many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_line" title="Absorption line">dark lines</a> superimposed. He found that many nebulous objects such as the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Nebula</a> (as it was then known) had spectra that were quite similar. However, when Huggins looked at the <a href="/wiki/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula" title="Cat\'s Eye Nebula">Cat\'s Eye Nebula</a>, he found a very different spectrum. Rather than a strong continuum with absorption lines superimposed, the Cat\'s Eye Nebula and other similar objects showed a number of <a href="/wiki/Emission_spectrum" title="Emission spectrum">emission lines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore2007_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Moore2007-10">[10]</a></sup> Brightest of these was at a wavelength of 500.7&#160;<a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a>, which did not correspond with a line of any known element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huggins1864_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huggins1864-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At first, it was hypothesized that the line might be due to an unknown element, which was named <a href="/wiki/Nebulium" title="Nebulium">nebulium</a>. A similar idea had led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> through analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>\'s spectrum in 1868.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup> While helium was isolated on Earth soon after its discovery in the spectrum of the Sun, "nebulium" was not. In the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell" title="Henry Norris Russell">Henry Norris Russell</a> proposed that, rather than being a new element, the line at 500.7&#160;nm was due to a familiar element in unfamiliar conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Physicists showed in the 1920s that in gas at extremely low densities, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> can occupy <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited</a> <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy levels</a> in atoms and ions that would otherwise be de-excited by collisions that would occur at higher densities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen1927_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen1927-16">[16]</a></sup> Electron transitions from these levels in <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> ions (<span class="nowrap">O<sup>+</sup></span>, <a href="/wiki/Doubly_ionized_oxygen" title="Doubly ionized oxygen"><span class="nowrap">O<sup>2+</sup></span></a> (a.k.a. O&#160;<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872446130">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps">iii</span>), and <span class="nowrap">N<sup>+</sup></span>) give rise to the 500.7&#160;nm emission line and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup> These spectral lines, which can only be seen in very low density gases, are called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forbidden_line" title="Forbidden line">forbidden lines</a></i>. Spectroscopic observations thus showed that nebulae were made of extremely rarefied gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurzadyan_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurzadyan-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Potw1550a.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="846" data-file-width="846" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Potw1550a.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Potw1550a.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Potw1550a.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Potw1550a.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Potw1550a.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Potw1550a.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Potw1550a.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Planetary nebula <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_3699&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NGC 3699 (page does not exist)">NGC 3699</a> is distinguished by an irregular mottled appearance and a dark rift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_stars">Central stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Central stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The central stars of planetary nebulae are very hot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankowskietal2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-2">[2]</a></sup> Only when a star has exhausted most of its nuclear fuel can it collapse to a small size. Planetary nebulae came to be understood as a final stage of <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a>. Spectroscopic observations show that all planetary nebulae are expanding. This led to the idea that planetary nebulae were caused by a star\'s outer layers being thrown into space at the end of its life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_observations">Modern observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Towards the end of the 20th century, technological improvements helped to further the study of planetary nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">Space telescopes</a> allowed astronomers to study light wavelengths outside those that the Earth\'s atmosphere transmits. <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a> and ultraviolet studies of planetary nebulae allowed much more accurate determinations of nebular <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">densities</a> and elemental abundances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hora2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hora2004-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwoketal2006_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwoketal2006-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">Charge-coupled device</a> technology allowed much fainter spectral lines to be measured accurately than had previously been possible. The Hubble Space Telescope also showed that while many nebulae appear to have simple and regular structures when observed from the ground, the very high <a href="/wiki/Optical_resolution" title="Optical resolution">optical resolution</a> achievable by telescopes above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> reveals extremely complex structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed1999_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reed1999-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alleretal2003_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alleretal2003-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgan-Keenan_spectral_classification" title="Morgan-Keenan spectral classification">Morgan-Keenan spectral classification</a> scheme, planetary nebulae are classified as <i>Type-<b>P</b></i>, although this notation is seldom used in practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krause_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krause-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg"><img alt="Central star has elongated S shaped curve of white emanating in opposite directions to the edge. A butterfly-like area surrounds the S shape with the S shape corresponding to the body of the butterfly." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="980" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg/220px-Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg/330px-Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg/440px-Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Computer simulation of the formation of a planetary nebula from a star with a warped disk, showing the complexity which can result from a small initial asymmetry.<br/><center><i>Credit: Vincent Icke</i></center></div></div></div>\n<p>Stars greater than 8&#160;<a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a> (M<sub>&#8857;</sub>) will likely end their lives in dramatic <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> explosions, while planetary nebulae seemingly only occur at the end of the lives of intermediate and low mass stars between 0.8&#160;M<sub>&#8857;</sub> to 8.0&#160;M<sub>&#8857;</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macieletal2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macieletal2009-25">[25]</a></sup> Progenitor stars that form planetary nebulae will spend most of their lifetimes converting their <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> into <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> in the star\'s core by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> at about 15&#160;million <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>. This generated energy creates outward pressure from fusion reactions in the core, balancing the crushing inward pressures of the star\'s gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harpaz4_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harpaz4-26">[26]</a></sup> This state of equilibrium is known as the <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a>, which can last for tens of millions to billions of years, depending on the mass.\n</p><p>When the hydrogen source in the core starts to diminish, gravity starts compressing the core, causing a rise in temperature to about 100 million K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harpaz6_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harpaz6-27">[27]</a></sup> Such higher core temperatures then make the star\'s cooler outer layers expand to create much larger red giant stars. This end phase causes a dramatic rise in stellar luminosity, where the released energy is distributed over a much larger surface area, even though the average surface temperature is lower. In <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a> terms, stars undergoing such increases in luminosity are known as <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch stars</a> (AGB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harpaz6_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harpaz6-27">[27]</a></sup> During this phase, the star can lose 50 to 70% of its total mass from its <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-wood-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the more massive asymptotic giant branch stars that form planetary nebulae, whose progenitors exceed about 3M<sub>&#8857;</sub>, their cores will continue to contract. When temperatures reach about 100&#160;million K, the available <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium nuclei</a> fuse into <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, so that the star again resumes radiating energy, temporarily stopping the core\'s contraction. This new helium burning phase (fusion of helium nuclei) forms a growing inner core of inert carbon and oxygen. Above it is a thin helium-burning shell, surrounded in turn by a hydrogen-burning shell. However, this new phase lasts only 20,000 years or so, a short period compared to the entire lifetime of the star.\n</p><p>In either scenario, the venting of atmosphere continues unabated into interstellar space, but when the outer surface of the exposed core reaches temperatures exceeding about 30,000&#160;K, there are enough emitted <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionisation" title="Ionisation">ionize</a> the ejected atmosphere, causing the gas to shine as a planetary nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harpaz6_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harpaz6-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifetime">Lifetime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lifetime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Necklace_Nebula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2871" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Necklace_Nebula.jpg/220px-Necklace_Nebula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Necklace_Nebula.jpg/330px-Necklace_Nebula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Necklace_Nebula.jpg/440px-Necklace_Nebula.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Necklace_Nebula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Necklace_Nebula" title="Necklace Nebula">Necklace Nebula</a> consists of a bright ring, measuring about two light-years across, dotted with dense, bright knots of gas that resemble diamonds in a necklace. The knots glow brightly due to absorption of ultraviolet light from the central stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>After a star passes through the <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a> (AGB) phase, the short planetary nebula phase of stellar evolution begins<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> as gases blow away from the central star at speeds of a few kilometers per second. The central star is the remnant of its AGB progenitor, an electron-degenerate carbon-oxygen core that has lost most of its hydrogen envelope due to mass loss on the AGB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> As the gases expand, the central star undergoes a two-stage evolution, first growing hotter as it continues to contract and hydrogen fusion reactions occur in the shell around the core and then slowly cooling when the hydrogen shell is exhausted through fusion and mass loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> In the second phase, it radiates away its energy and fusion reactions cease, as the central star is not heavy enough to generate the core temperatures required for carbon and oxygen to fuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> During the first phase, the central star maintains constant luminosity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> while at the same time it grows ever hotter, eventually reaching temperatures around 100,000&#160;K. In the second phase, it cools so much that it does not give off enough ultraviolet radiation to ionize the increasingly distant gas cloud. The star becomes a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a>, and the expanding gas cloud becomes invisible to us, ending the planetary nebula phase of evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> For a typical planetary nebula, about 10,000&#160;years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwokJun2005_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-KwokJun2005-19">[19]</a></sup> passes between its formation and recombination of the resulting <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok1_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok1-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_galactic_enrichment">Role in galactic enrichment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Role in galactic enrichment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Planetary nebulae may play a very important role in galactic evolution. Newly born stars consist almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> but as stars evolve through the <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a> phase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> they create heavier elements via <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> which are eventually expelled by strong <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar winds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Planetary nebulae usually contain larger proportions of elements such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and these are recycled into the interstellar medium via these powerful winds. In turn, planetary nebulae greatly enrich the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a> with these heavier elements &#8211; collectively known by astronomers as <i>metals</i> and specifically referred to by the <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity parameter</a> <i>Z</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok19_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok19-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Subsequent generations of stars formed from such nebulae also tend to have higher metallicities. Although these metals are present in stars in relatively tiny amounts, they have marked effects on <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">stellar evolution</a> and fusion reactions. When stars formed earlier in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> they theoretically contained smaller quantities of heavier elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopIII_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopIII-34">[34]</a></sup> Known examples are the metal poor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">Population II</a> stars. (See <a href="/wiki/Stellar_population" title="Stellar population">Stellar population</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marochnik_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marochnik-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gregory_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory-36">[36]</a></sup>\nIdentification of stellar metallicity content is found by <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M57_The_Ring_Nebula.JPG"><img alt="Elliptical shell with fine red outer edge surrounding region of yellow and then pink around a nearly circular blue area with the central star at its center. nebula">Lemon slice nebula</a> (IC 3568).</div></div></div>\n<p>A typical planetary nebula is roughly one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light year</a> across, and consists of extremely rarefied gas, with a density generally from 100 to 10,000 particles <span class="nowrap">per cm<sup>3</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterbrock1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterbrock1-37">[37]</a></sup> (The Earth\'s atmosphere, by comparison, contains 2.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">19</span></sup> particles <span class="nowrap">per cm<sup>3</sup></span>.) Young planetary nebulae have the highest densities, sometimes as high as 10<sup>6</sup> particles <span class="nowrap">per cm<sup>3</sup></span>. As nebulae age, their expansion causes their density to decrease. The masses of planetary nebulae range from 0.1 to 1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterbrock1_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osterbrock1-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Radiation from the central star heats the gases to temperatures of about 10,000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurzadyan2_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurzadyan2-38">[38]</a></sup> The gas temperature in central regions is usually much higher than at the periphery reaching 16,000&#8211;25,000&#160;K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurzadyan3_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurzadyan3-39">[39]</a></sup> The volume in the vicinity of the central star is often filled with a very hot (coronal) gas having the temperature of about 1,000,000&#160;K. This gas originates from the surface of the central star in the form of the fast stellar wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterbrock_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterbrock-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nebulae may be described as <i>matter bounded</i> or <i>radiation bounded</i>. In the former case, there is not enough matter in the nebula to absorb all the UV photons emitted by the star, and the visible nebula is fully ionized. In the latter case, there are not enough UV photons being emitted by the central star to ionize all the surrounding gas, and an ionization front propagates outward into the circumstellar envelope of neutral atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterbrock2_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterbrock2-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers_and_distribution">Numbers and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Numbers and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>About 3000 planetary nebulae are now known to exist in our galaxy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parkeretal2006_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parkeretal2006-42">[42]</a></sup> out of 200 billion stars. Their very short lifetime compared to total stellar lifetime accounts for their rarity. They are found mostly near the plane of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, with the greatest concentration near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">galactic center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2007_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2007-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:300px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:854px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Artist%E2%80%99s_view_of_how_a_planetary_nebula%E2%80%99s_wobbling_jets_are_sculpted.ogv/854px--Artist%E2%80%99s_view_of_how_a_planetary_nebula%E2%80%99s_wobbling_jets_are_sculpted.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:854px;height:480px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="50.155102040816" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Artist&#8217;s_view_of_how_a_planetary_nebula&#8217;s_wobbling_jets_are_sculpted.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/3/3e/Artist%E2%80%99s_view_of_how_a_planetary_nebula%E2%80%99s_wobbling_jets_are_sculpted.ogv/Artist%E2%80%99s_view_of_how_a_planetary_nebula%E2%80%99s_wobbling_jets_are_sculpted.ogv.480p.vp9.webm" type="video/webm; Planetary nebulae are classified by different authors into: stellar, disk, ring, irregular, helical, <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_nebula" title="Bipolar nebula">bipolar</a>, quadrupolar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KwoketalDec2005_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-KwoketalDec2005-45">[45]</a></sup> and other types,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok8_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok8-46">[46]</a></sup> although the majority of them belong to just three types: spherical, elliptical and bipolar. Bipolar nebulae are concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_plane" title="Galactic plane">galactic plane</a>, likely produced by relatively young massive progenitor stars; and bipolars in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_bulge" title="Galactic bulge">galactic bulge</a> appear to prefer orienting their orbital axes parallel to the galactic plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On the other hand, spherical nebulae are likely produced by the old stars similar to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterbrock_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osterbrock-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The huge variety of the shapes is partially the projection effect&#8212;the same nebula when viewed under different angles will appear different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the reason for the huge variety of physical shapes is not fully understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok8_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok8-46">[46]</a></sup> Gravitational interactions with companion stars if the central stars are <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary stars</a> may be one cause. Another possibility is that planets disrupt the flow of material away from the star as the nebula forms. It has been determined that the more massive stars produce more irregularly shaped nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-49">[49]</a></sup> In January 2005, astronomers announced the first detection of magnetic fields around the central stars of two planetary nebulae, and hypothesized that the fields might be partly or wholly responsible for their remarkable shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordanetal2005_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jordanetal2005-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership_in_clusters">Membership in clusters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Membership in clusters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abell_78.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1240" data-file-width="1753" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Abell_78.jpg/220px-Abell_78.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Abell_78.jpg/330px-Abell_78.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Abell_78.jpg/440px-Abell_78.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abell_78.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Abell 78, 24 inch telescope on Mt. Lemmon, AZ. Courtesy of Joseph D. Schulman.</div></div></div>\n<p>Planetary nebulae have been detected as members in four Galactic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">globular clusters</a>: <a href="/wiki/Messier_15" title="Messier 15">Messier 15</a>, <a href="/wiki/Messier_22" title="Messier 22">Messier 22</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGC_6441" title="NGC 6441">NGC 6441</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palomar_6" title="Palomar 6">Palomar 6</a>. Evidence also points to the potential discovery of planetary nebulae in globular clusters in the galaxy <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">M31</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ja2013_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ja2013-52">[52]</a></sup>  However, there is currently only one case of a planetary nebula discovered in an <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">open cluster</a> that is agreed upon by independent researchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fr2008_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-fr2008-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker2011_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-parker2011-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ma2014_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-ma2014-55">[55]</a></sup> That case pertains to the planetary nebula PHR 1315-6555 and the open cluster Andrews-Lindsay 1.  Indeed, through cluster membership, PHR 1315-6555 possesses among the most precise distances established for a planetary nebula (i.e., a 4% distance solution). The cases of <a href="/wiki/NGC_2818" title="NGC 2818">NGC 2818</a> and NGC 2348 in <a href="/wiki/Messier_46" title="Messier 46">Messier 46</a>, exhibit mismatched velocities between the planetary nebulae and the clusters, which indicates they are line-of-sight coincidences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2007_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2007-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiss2008_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiss2008-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mermilliod2001_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-mermilliod2001-57">[57]</a></sup>  A subsample of <i>tentative</i> cases that may potentially be cluster/PN pairs includes Abell 8 and Bica 6,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bo2008_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-bo2008-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tu2011_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-tu2011-59">[59]</a></sup> and He 2-86 and NGC 4463.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mo2014_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-mo2014-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Theoretical models predict that planetary nebulae can form from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-sequence" title="Main-sequence">main-sequence</a> stars of between one and eight solar masses, which puts the progenitor star\'s age at greater than 40 million years. Although there are a few hundred known open clusters within that age range, a variety of reasons limit the chances of finding a planetary nebula within.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-majaess2007_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-majaess2007-43">[43]</a></sup> For one reason, the planetary nebula phase for more massive stars is on the order of thousands of years, which is a blink of the eye in cosmic terms.  Also, partly because of their small total mass, open clusters have relatively poor gravitational cohesion and tend to disperse after a relatively short time, typically from 100 to 600 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allison_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allison-61">[61]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_issues_in_planetary_nebula_studies">Current 6886">NGC 6886</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The distances to planetary nebulae are generally poorly determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> It is possible to determine distances to the nearest planetary nebula by measuring their expansion rates. High resolution observations taken several years apart will show the expansion of the nebula perpendicular to the line of sight, while spectroscopic observations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">Doppler shift</a> will reveal the velocity of expansion in the line of sight. Comparing the angular expansion with the derived velocity of expansion will reveal the distance to the nebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reed1999_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reed1999-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The issue of how such a diverse range of nebular shapes can be produced is a debatable topic. It is theorised that interactions between material moving away from the star at different speeds gives rise to most observed shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kwok8_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kwok8-46">[46]</a></sup> However, some astronomers postulate that close binary central stars might be responsible for the more complex and extreme planetary nebulae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soker2002_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soker2002-64">[64]</a></sup> Several have been shown to exhibit strong magnetic fields,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurzadyan4_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurzadyan4-65">[65]</a></sup> and their interactions with ionized gas could explain some planetary nebulae shapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jordanetal2005_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jordanetal2005-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are two main methods of determining <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metal abundances</a> in nebulae. These rely on recombination lines and collisionally excited lines. Large discrepancies are sometimes seen between the results derived from the two methods. This may be explained by the presence of small temperature fluctuations within planetary nebulae. The discrepancies may be too large to be caused by temperature effects, and some hypothesize the existence of cold knots containing very little hydrogen to explain the observations. However, such knots have yet to be observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liuetal2000_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liuetal2000-66">[66]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
