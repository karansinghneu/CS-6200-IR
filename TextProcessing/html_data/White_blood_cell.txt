(also called <b>leukocytes</b> or <b>leucocytes</b> and abbreviated as WBCs) are the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a> that are involved in protecting the body against both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious disease</a> and foreign invaders. All white blood cells are produced and derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multipotent" title="Multipotent">multipotent</a> cells in the <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Hematopoietic_stem_cell" title="Hematopoietic stem cell">hematopoietic stem cells</a>. Leukocytes are found throughout the body, including the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>All white blood cells have <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nuclei</a>, which distinguishes them from the other <a href="/wiki/Blood_cell" title="Blood cell">blood cells</a>, the anucleated <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a> (RBCs) and <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelets</a>. Types of white blood cells can be classified in standard ways. Two pairs of broadest categories classify them either by structure (<a href="/wiki/Granulocyte" title="Granulocyte">granulocytes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agranulocyte" title="Agranulocyte">agranulocytes</a>) or by <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell</a> lineage (myeloid cells or lymphoid cells). These broadest categories can be further divided into the five main types: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrophil_granulocyte" title="Neutrophil granulocyte">neutrophils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eosinophil_granulocyte" title="Eosinophil granulocyte">eosinophils</a> (acidophiles), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basophil_granulocyte" title="Basophil granulocyte">basophils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">lymphocytes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monocyte" title="Monocyte">monocytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-0-323-04950-4_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-0-323-04950-4-2">[2]</a></sup> These types are distinguished by their physical and functional characteristics. Monocytes and neutrophils are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phagocytic" title="Phagocytic">phagocytic</a>. Further subtypes can be classified; for example, among lymphocytes, there are <a href="/wiki/B_cell" title="B cell">B cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_killer_cell" title="Natural killer cell">NK cells</a>.\n</p><p>The number of leukocytes in the blood is often an indicator of <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>, and thus the <i>white blood cell count</i> is an important subset of the <a href="/wiki/Complete_blood_count" title="Complete blood count">complete blood count</a>. The normal white cell count <a href="/wiki/Reference_ranges_for_blood_tests" title="Reference ranges for blood tests">is usually</a> between 4 &#215; 10<sup>9</sup>/L and 1.1 &#215; 10<sup>10</sup>/L. In the US, this is usually expressed as 4,000 to 11,000 white blood cells per microliter of blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> White blood cells make up approximately 1% of the total blood volume in a healthy adult,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_table_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-alberts_table-4">[4]</a></sup> making them substantially less numerous than the <a href="/wiki/Hematocrit" title="Hematocrit">red blood cells at 40% to 45%</a>. However, this 1% of the blood makes a large difference to health, because <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a> depends on it. An increase in the number of leukocytes over the <a href="/wiki/Reference_ranges_for_blood_tests#White_blood_cells_2" title="Reference ranges for blood tests">upper limits</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Leukocytosis" title="Leukocytosis">leukocytosis</a>. It is normal when it is part of healthy immune responses, which happen frequently. It is occasionally abnormal, when it is <a href="/wiki/Neoplasm" title="Neoplasm">neoplastic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Autoimmunity" title="Autoimmunity">autoimmune</a> in origin. A decrease below the lower limit is called <a href="/wiki/Leukopenia" title="Leukopenia">leukopenia</a>. This indicates a weakened immune system.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div tocsection-20"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>\n<p>The name "white blood cell" derives from the physical appearance of a blood sample after <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a>. White cells are found in the <a href="/wiki/Buffy_coat" title="Buffy coat"><i>buffy</i> <i>coat</i></a>, a thin, typically white layer of nucleated cells between the sedimented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. The scientific term <i>leukocyte</i> directly reflects its description. It is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> roots <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Greek_and_Latin_roots#L" title="List of Greek and Latin roots">leuk-</a></i> meaning "white" and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Greek_and_Latin_roots#C" title="List of Greek and Latin roots">cyt-</a></i> meaning "cell". The <a href="/wiki/Buffy_coat" title="Buffy coat">buffy coat</a> may sometimes be green if there are large amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrophil_granulocyte" title="Neutrophil granulocyte">neutrophils</a> in the sample, due to the <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a>-containing enzyme <a href="/wiki/Myeloperoxidase" title="Myeloperoxidase">myeloperoxidase</a> that they produce.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png/220px-Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png/330px-Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png/440px-Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0909_WhiteBloodCells.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3D rendering of various types of white blood cells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>All white blood cells are nucleated, which distinguishes them from the anucleated red blood cells and platelets. Types of leukocytes can be classified in standard ways. Two pairs of broadest categories classify them either by structure (<a href="/wiki/Granulocyte" title="Granulocyte">granulocytes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agranulocyte" title="Agranulocyte">agranulocytes</a>) or by cell lineage (myeloid cells or lymphoid cells). These broadest categories can be further divided into the five main types: <a href="/wiki/Neutrophil" title="Neutrophil">neutrophils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eosinophil" title="Eosinophil">eosinophils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basophil" title="Basophil">basophils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">lymphocytes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Monocyte" title="Monocyte">monocytes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn978-0-323-04950-4_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn978-0-323-04950-4-2">[2]</a></sup> These types are distinguished by their physical and functional characteristics. Monocytes and neutrophils are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phagocytic" title="Phagocytic">phagocytic</a>. Further subtypes can be classified.\n</p><p>Granulocytes are distinguished from agranulocytes by their nucleus shape (lobed versus round, that is, polymorphonuclear versus mononuclear) and by their cytoplasm <a href="/wiki/Granule_(cell_biology)" title="Granule (cell biology)">granules</a> (present or absent, or more precisely, visible on light microscopy or not thus visible). The other dichotomy is by lineage: Myeloid cells (neutrophils, monocytes, eosinophils and basophils) are distinguished from lymphoid cells (lymphocytes) by <a href="/wiki/Haematopoiesis" title="Haematopoiesis">hematopoietic</a> lineage (<a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">cellular differentiation</a> lineage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Lymphocytes can be further classified as T cells, B cells, and natural killer cells. \n</p>\n<table href="/wiki/Neutrophil" title="Neutrophil">Neutrophil</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2403" data-file-width="2304" decoding="async" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg/210px-Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg/315px-Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg/420px-Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neutrophil_with_anthrax_copy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neutrophil engulfing <a href="/wiki/Bacillus_anthracis" title="Bacillus anthracis">anthrax bacteria</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Neutrophils are the most abundant white blood cell, constituting 60-70% of the circulating leukocytes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alberts_table_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-alberts_table-4">[4]</a></sup> and including two functionally unequal <a href="/wiki/Neutrophil#Subpopulations" title="Neutrophil">subpopulations</a>: neutrophil-killers and neutrophil-cagers. They defend against <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungal</a> infection. They are usually first responders to microbial infection; their activity and death in large numbers form <a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">pus</a>. They are commonly referred to as polymorphonuclear (PMN) leukocytes, although, in the technical sense, PMN refers to all granulocytes. They have a multi-lobed nucleus, which consists of three to five lobes connected by slender strands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saladin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saladin-9">[9]</a></sup> This gives the neutrophils the appearance of having multiple nuclei, hence the name polymorphonuclear leukocyte. The cytoplasm may look transparent because of fine granules that are pale lilac when stained. Neutrophils are active in phagocytosing bacteria and are present in large amount in the pus of wounds. These cells are not able to renew their <a href="/wiki/Lysosome" title="Lysosome">lysosomes</a> (used in digesting microbes) and die after having phagocytosed a few pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Neutrophils are the most common cell type seen in the early stages of acute inflammation. The life span of a circulating human neutrophil is about 5.4 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eosinophil">Eosinophil</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eosinophil" title="Eosinophil">Eosinophil</a></div>\n<p>Eosinophils compose about 2-4% of the WBC total. This count fluctuates throughout the day, seasonally, and during <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstruation</a>. It rises in response to allergies, parasitic infections, collagen diseases, and disease of the spleen and central nervous system. They are rare in the blood, but numerous in the mucous membranes of the respiratory, digestive, and lower urinary tracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saladin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saladin-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>They primarily deal with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasitic" title="Parasitic">parasitic</a> infections. Eosinophils are also the predominant inflammatory cells in allergic reactions. The most important causes of eosinophilia include allergies such as asthma, hay fever, and hives; and also parasitic infections. They secrete chemicals that destroy these large parasites, such as hook worms and tapeworms, that are too big for any one WBC to phagocytize. In general, their nucleus is bi-lobed. The lobes are connected by a thin strand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saladin_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Saladin-9">[9]</a></sup> The cytoplasm is full of granules that assume a characteristic pink-orange color with <a href="/wiki/Eosin" title="Eosin">eosin</a> staining.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basophil">Basophil</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Basophil" title="Basophil">Basophil</a></div>\n<p>Basophils are chiefly responsible for <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigen</a> response by releasing the chemical <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a> causing the <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">dilation of blood vessels</a>. Because they are the rarest of the white blood cells (less than 0.5% of the total count) and share physicochemical properties with other blood cells, they are difficult to study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They can be recognized by several coarse, dark violet granules, giving them a blue hue. The nucleus is bi- or tri-lobed, but it is hard to see because of the number of coarse granules that hide it.\n</p><p>They excrete two chemicals that aid in the body\'s defenses: <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heparin" title="Heparin">heparin</a>. Histamine is responsible for widening blood vessels and increasing the flow of blood to injured tissue. It also makes blood vessels more permeable so neutrophils and clotting proteins can get into connective tissue more easily. Heparin is an anticoagulant that inhibits blood clotting and promotes the movement of white blood cells into an area. Basophils can also release chemical signals that attract eosinophils and neutrophils to an infection site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saladin_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Saladin-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lymphocyte">Lymphocyte</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lymphocyte" title="Lymphocyte">Lymphocyte</a></div>\n<p>Lymphocytes are much more common in the lymphatic system than in blood. Lymphocytes are distinguished by having a deeply staining nucleus that may be eccentric in location, and a relatively small amount of cytoplasm. Lymphocytes include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/B_cell" title="B cell">B cells</a> make <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibodies</a> that can bind to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathogens" title="Pathogens">pathogens</a>, block pathogen invasion, activate the <a href="/wiki/Complement_system" title="Complement system">complement system</a>, and enhance pathogen destruction.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cells</a>:\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/CD4" title="CD4">CD4</a>+ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helper_T_cells" title="Helper T cells">helper T cells</a>: T cells displaying <a href="/wiki/Co-receptor" title="Co-receptor">co-receptor</a> <a href="/wiki/CD4" title="CD4">CD4</a> are known as CD4+ T cells. These cells have <a href="/wiki/T-cell_receptor" title="T-cell receptor">T-cell receptors</a> and CD4 molecules that, in combination, bind <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigens" title="Antigens">antigenic peptides</a> presented on <a href="/wiki/MHC_class_II" title="MHC class II">major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II</a> molecules on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigen-presenting_cells" title="Antigen-presenting cells">antigen-presenting cells</a>. Helper T cells make <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> and perform other functions that help coordinate the <a href="/wiki/Immune_response" title="Immune response">immune response</a>. In <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> infection, these T cells are the main index to identify the individual\'s immune system integrity.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/CD8" title="CD8">CD8</a>+ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytotoxic_T_cells" title="Cytotoxic T cells">cytotoxic T cells</a>: T cells displaying co-receptor <a href="/wiki/CD8" title="CD8">CD8</a> are known as CD8+ T cells. These cells bind antigens presented on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHC_I" title="MHC I">MHC I</a> complex of virus-infected or tumour cells and kill them. Nearly all nucleated cells display MHC I.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma/delta_T_cells" title="Gamma/delta T cells">&#947;&#948; T cells</a> possess an alternative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_cell_receptor" title="T cell receptor">T cell receptor</a> (different from the &#945;&#946; TCR found on conventional CD4+ and CD8+ T cells). Found in tissue more commonly than in blood, &#947;&#948; T cells share characteristics of helper T cells, cytotoxic T cells, and natural killer cells.</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_killer_cell" title="Natural killer cell">Natural killer cells</a> are able to kill cells of the body that do not display <a href="/wiki/MHC_class_I" title="MHC class I">MHC class I</a> molecules, or display stress markers such as <a href="/wiki/MHC_class_I_polypeptide-related_sequence_A" title="MHC class I polypeptide-related sequence A">MHC class I polypeptide-related sequence A</a> (MIC-A). Decreased expression of MHC class I and up-regulation of MIC-A can happen when cells are infected by a virus or become cancerous.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monocyte">Monocyte</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monocyte" title="Monocyte">Monocyte</a></div>\n<p>Monocytes, the largest type of WBCs, share the "vacuum cleaner" (<a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a>) function of neutrophils, but are much longer lived as they have an extra role: they present pieces of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> to T cells so that the pathogens may be recognized again and killed. This causes an antibody response to be mounted. Monocytes eventually leave the bloodstream and become tissue <a href="/wiki/Macrophage" title="Macrophage">macrophages</a>, which remove dead cell debris as well as attack microorganisms. Neither dead cell debris nor attacking microorganisms can be dealt with effectively by the neutrophils. Unlike neutrophils, monocytes are able to replace their <a href="/wiki/Lysosome" title="Lysosome">lysosomal</a> contents and are thought to have a much longer active life. They have the kidney shaped nucleus and are typically agranulated. They also possess abundant cytoplasm.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_leucocytes">Fixed leucocytes</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a residence at that location rather than remaining in the blood. Often these cells have specific names depending upon which tissue they settle in, such as fixed macrophages in the liver, which become known as <a href="/wiki/Kupffer_cell" title="Kupffer cell">Kupffer cells</a>. These cells still serve a role in the immune system.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Histiocyte" title="Histiocyte">Histiocytes</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dendritic_cell" title="Dendritic cell">Dendritic cells</a> (Although these will often migrate to local lymph nodes upon ingesting antigens)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mast_cell" title="Mast cell">Mast cells</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Microglia" title="Microglia">Microglia</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disorders">Disorders</span></h2>\n<p>The two commonly used categories of white blood cell disorders divide them <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quantitative#Adjective" title="wikt:quantitative">quantitatively</a> into those causing excessive numbers (<a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">proliferative</a> disorders) and those causing insufficient numbers (<a href="/wiki/Leukopenia" title="Leukopenia">leukopenias</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leukocytosis" title="Leukocytosis">Leukocytosis</a> is usually healthy (e.g., fighting an <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a>), but it also may be dysfunctionally proliferative. WBC proliferative disorders can be classed as <a href="/wiki/Myeloproliferative_neoplasm" title="Myeloproliferative neoplasm">myeloproliferative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymphoproliferative_disorders" title="Lymphoproliferative disorders">lymphoproliferative</a>. Some are <a href="/wiki/Autoimmunity" title="Autoimmunity">autoimmune</a>, but many are <a href="/wiki/Neoplasm" title="Neoplasm">neoplastic</a>.\n</p><p>Another way to categorize disorders of white blood cells is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/qualitative#Adjective" title="wikt:qualitative">qualitatively</a>. There are various disorders in which the number of white blood cells is normal but the cells do not function normally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Neoplasia of WBCs can be <a href="/wiki/Benign_tumor" title="Benign tumor">benign</a> but is often <a href="/wiki/Malignancy" title="Malignancy">malignant</a>. Of the various <a href="/wiki/Tumors_of_the_hematopoietic_and_lymphoid_tissues" title="Tumors of the hematopoietic and lymphoid tissues">tumors of the blood and lymph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancers</a> of WBCs can be broadly classified as <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma" title="Lymphoma">lymphomas</a>, although those categories overlap and are often grouped as a pair.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leucopenias">Leucopenias</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leucopenia" title="Leucopenia">Leucopenia</a></div>\n<p>A range of disorders can cause decreases in white blood cells. This type of white blood cell decreased is usually the neutrophil. In this case the decrease may be called <a href="/wiki/Neutropenia" title="Neutropenia">neutropenia</a> or granulocytopenia. Less commonly, a decrease in lymphocytes (called <a href="/wiki/Lymphocytopenia" title="Lymphocytopenia">lymphocytopenia</a> or lymphopenia) may be seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutropenia">Neutropenia</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neutropenia" title="Neutropenia">Neutropenia</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Neutropenia" title="Neutropenia">Neutropenia</a> can be <a href="/wiki/Acquired_characteristic" title="Acquired characteristic">acquired</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" title="Congenital disorder">intrinsic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A decrease in levels of neutrophils on lab tests is due to either decreased production of neutrophils or increased removal from the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup> The following list of causes is not complete.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Medications - <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>, sulfas or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a>, phenothiazenes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzodiazepines" title="Benzodiazepines">benzodiazepines</a>, antithyroids, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticonvulsants" title="Anticonvulsants">anticonvulsants</a>, quinine, quinidine, indomethacin, procainamide, thiazides</li>\n<li>Radiation</li>\n<li>Toxins - <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>, benzenes</li>\n<li>Intrinsic disorders - <a href="/wiki/Fanconi_syndrome" title="Fanconi syndrome">Fanconi\'s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kostmann_syndrome" title="Kostmann syndrome">Kostmann\'s</a>, cyclic neutropenia, <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A9diak%E2%80%93Higashi_syndrome" title="Ch&#233;diak&#8211;Higashi syndrome">Ch&#233;diak&#8211;Higashi</a></li>\n<li>Immune dysfunction - disorders of collagen, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a></li>\n<li>Blood cell dysfunction - <a href="/wiki/Megaloblastic_anemia" title="Megaloblastic anemia">megaloblastic anemia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myelodysplasia" title="Myelodysplasia">myelodysplasia</a>, marrow failure, marrow replacement, acute <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a></li>\n<li>Any major infection</li>\n<li>Miscellaneous - <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersplenism" title="Hypersplenism">hypersplenism</a></li></ul>\n<p>Symptoms of neutropenia are associated with the underlying cause of the decrease in neutrophils. For example, the most common cause of acquired neutropenia is drug-induced, so an individual may have symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Drug_overdose" title="Drug overdose">medication overdose</a> or toxicity.\nTreatment is also aimed at the underlying cause of the neutropenia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schafer_a_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schafer_a-16">[16]</a></sup> One severe consequence of neutropenia is that it can increase the risk of infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lymphocytopenia">Lymphocytopenia</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lymphocytopenia" title="Lymphocytopenia">Lymphocytopenia</a></div>\n<p>Defined as total lymphocyte count below 1.0x10<sup>9</sup>/L, the cells most commonly affected are CD4+ T cells. Like neutropenia, lymphocytopenia may be acquired or intrinsic and there are many causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup> This is not a complete list.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Inherited immune deficiency - <a href="/wiki/Severe_combined_immunodeficiency" title="Severe combined immunodeficiency">severe combined immunodeficiency</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_variable_immune_deficiency" title="Common variable immune deficiency">common variable immune deficiency</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ataxia-telangiectasia" title="Ataxia-telangiectasia">ataxia-telangiectasia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wiskott-Aldrich_syndrome" title="Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome">Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome</a>, immunodeficiency with short-limbed dwarfism, immunodeficiency with thymoma, <a href="/wiki/Purine_nucleoside_phosphorylase_deficiency" title="Purine nucleoside phosphorylase deficiency">purine nucleoside phosphorylase deficiency</a>, genetic polymorphism</li>\n<li>Blood cell dysfunction - <a href="/wiki/Aplastic_anemia" title="Aplastic anemia">aplastic anemia</a></li>\n<li>Infectious diseases - <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viral</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SARS" title="SARS">SARS</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Nile_virus" title="West Nile virus">West Nile encephalitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herpes_simplex_virus" title="Herpes simplex virus">herpes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a>, others), <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">TB</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rickettsiosis" title="Rickettsiosis">rickettsiosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ehrlichiosis" title="Ehrlichiosis">ehrlichiosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasitic</a> (acute phase of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>)</li>\n<li>Medications - chemotherapy (antilymphocyte globulin therapy, <a href="/wiki/Alemtuzumab" title="Alemtuzumab">alemtuzumab</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucocorticoids" title="Glucocorticoids">glucocorticoids</a>)</li>\n<li>Radiation</li>\n<li>Major surgery</li>\n<li>Miscellaneous - <a href="/wiki/Extracorporeal_membrane_oxygenation" title="Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation">ECMO</a>, kidney or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_marrow_transplant" title="Bone marrow transplant">bone marrow transplant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemodialysis" title="Hemodialysis">hemodialysis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">kidney failure</a>, severe burn, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a>, severe acute <a href="/wiki/Pancreatitis" title="Pancreatitis">pancreatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarcoidosis" title="Sarcoidosis">sarcoidosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein-losing_enteropathy" title="Protein-losing enteropathy">protein-losing enteropathy</a>, strenuous exercise, <a href="/wiki/Carcinoma" title="Carcinoma">carcinoma</a></li>\n<li>Immune dysfunction - <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">systemic lupus erythematosus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sj%C3%B6gren_syndrome" title="Sj&#246;gren syndrome">Sj&#246;gren syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myasthenia_gravis" title="Myasthenia gravis">myasthenia gravis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_vasculitis" title="Systemic vasculitis">systemic vasculitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behcet_syndrome" title="Behcet syndrome">Behcet</a>-like syndrome, <a href="/wiki/Dermatomyositis" title="Dermatomyositis">dermatomyositis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Granulomatosis_with_polyangiitis" title="Granulomatosis with polyangiitis">granulomatosis with polyangiitis</a></li>\n<li>Nutritional/Dietary - <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_abuse" title="Alcohol abuse">alcohol abuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc_deficiency" title="Zinc deficiency">zinc deficiency</a></li></ul>\n<p>Like neutropenia, symptoms and treatment of lymphocytopenia are directed at the underlying cause of the change in cell counts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proliferative_disorders">Proliferative disorders</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leukocytosis" title="Leukocytosis">Leukocytosis</a></div>\n<p>An increase in the number of white blood cells in <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulation</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Leukocytosis" title="Leukocytosis">leukocytosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup> This increase is most commonly caused by <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup> There are four major causes: increase of production in bone marrow, increased release from storage in bone marrow, decreased attachment to veins and arteries, decreased uptake by tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Al2010_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Al2010-13">[13]</a></sup> Leukocytosis may affect one or more cell lines and can be neutrophilic, eosinophilic, basophilic, monocytosis, or lymphocytosis.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutrophilia">Neutrophilia</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neutrophilia" title="Neutrophilia">Neutrophilia</a></div>\n<p>Neutrophilia is an increase in the absolute neutrophil count in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">peripheral circulation</a>. Normal blood values vary by age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Neutrophilia can be caused by a direct problem with blood cells (primary disease). It can also occur as a consequence of an underlying disease (secondary). Most cases of neutrophilia are secondary to inflammation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schafer_a_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schafer_a-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Primary causes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schafer_a_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schafer_a-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Conditions with normally functioning neutrophils &#8211; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hereditary" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a> neutrophilia, chronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiopathic" title="Idiopathic">idiopathic</a> neutrophilia</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pelger%E2%80%93Huet_anomaly" title="Pelger&#8211;Huet anomaly">Pelger&#8211;Huet anomaly</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Leukocyte_adhesion_deficiency" title="Leukocyte adhesion deficiency">Leukocyte adhesion deficiency</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Familial_cold_urticaria" title="Familial cold urticaria">Familial cold urticaria</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">Leukemia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chronic_myelogenous_leukemia" title="Chronic myelogenous leukemia">chronic myelogenous (CML))</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myeloproliferative_disorders" title="Myeloproliferative disorders">myeloproliferative disorders</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Splenectomy" title="Splenectomy">Surgical removal of spleen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Secondary causes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schafer_a_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schafer_a-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">Infection</a></li>\n<li>Chronic <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> &#8211; especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juvenile_rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Juvenile rheumatoid arthritis">juvenile rheumatoid arthritis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adult-onset_Still%27s_disease" title="Adult-onset Still\'s disease">Still\'s disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn\'s disease">Crohn\'s disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis" title="Ulcerative colitis">ulcerative colitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granulomatous" title="Granulomatous">granulomatous</a> infections (for example, <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>), and chronic <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cigarette_smoking" title="Cigarette smoking">Cigarette smoking</a> &#8211; occurs in 25&#8211;50% of chronic smokers and can last up to 5 years after quitting</li>\n<li>Stress &#8211; exercise, surgery, general stress</li>\n<li>Medication induced &#8211; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucocorticoids" title="Glucocorticoids">corticosteroids</a> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Prednisone" title="Prednisone">prednisone</a>, &#946;-agonists, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>)</li>\n<li>Cancer &#8211; either by <a href="/wiki/Growth_factor" title="Growth factor">growth factors</a> secreted by the tumor or invasion of bone marrow by the cancer</li>\n<li>Increased destruction of cells in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">peripheral circulation</a> can stimulate bone marrow. This can occur in <a href="/wiki/Hemolytic_anemia" title="Hemolytic anemia">hemolytic anemia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiopathic_thrombocytopenic_purpura" title="Idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura">idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura</a></li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eosinophilia">Eosinophilia</span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eosinophilia" title="Eosinophilia">Eosinophilia</a></div>\n<p>A normal eosinophil count is considered to be less than 0.65<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">9</span></sup>/L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Eosinophil counts are higher in newborns and vary with age, time (lower in the morning and higher at night), exercise, environment, and exposure to allergens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup> Eosinophilia is never a normal lab finding. Efforts should always be made to discover the underlying cause, though the cause may not always be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamshematology2010_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Williamshematology2010-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counting_and_reference_ranges">Counting and reference ranges</span></h2>\n<p>The <a
