(April 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is the hypothesis that most scientific discoveries and inventions are made independently and more or less simultaneously by multiple scientists and inventors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LambEaston84_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LambEaston84-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. class="mw-headline" id="Multiples">Multiples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_discovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Multiples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates#Laureates" title="List of Nobel laureates">Nobel laureates</a> are announced annually&#8212;especially in physics, chemistry, physiology, medicine, and economics&#8212;increasingly, in the given field, rather than just a single laureate, there are two, or the maximally permissible three, who often have independently made the same discovery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\nHistorians and sociologists have remarked on the occurrence, in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, of "multiple independent discovery". <a href="/wiki/Robert_K._Merton" title="Robert K. Merton">Robert K. Merton</a> defined such "multiples" as instances in which similar <a href="/wiki/Discovery_(observation)" title="Discovery (observation)">discoveries</a> are made by scientists working independently of each other. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MertonSociology73_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MertonSociology73-4">[4]</a></sup> Merton contrasted a "multiple" with a "singleton"&#8212;a discovery that has been made uniquely by a single scientist or group of scientists working together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MertonSocStruct96_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MertonSocStruct96-5">[5]</a></sup> As Merton said, "Sometimes the discoveries are simultaneous or almost so; sometimes a scientist will make a new discovery which, unknown to him, somebody else has made years before."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MertonSociology73_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MertonSociology73-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (April 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Commonly cited examples of multiple independent discovery are the 17th-century independent formulation of <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a> by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> and others, described by A. Rupert Hall;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (April 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the 18th-century discovery of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> and others;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_evolution" title="Theory of evolution">theory of evolution</a> of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, independently advanced in the 19th century by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">Alfred Russel Wallace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeve09_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reeve09-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (April 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> What holds for discoveries, also goes for <a href="/wiki/Invention" title="Invention">inventions</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> (invented independently in China, Europe and Africa),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbow</a> (invented independently in China, Greece, Africa, northern Canada, and the Baltic countries),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a> (discovered independently in Greece, China, and India).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Multiple independent discovery, however, is not limited to only a few historic instances involving giants of scientific research. Merton believed that it is multiple discoveries, rather than unique ones, that represent the <i>common</i> pattern in science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a Darwinian manner).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant-DNA</a>-inspired "<a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a> of paradigms" has been posited, that describes a mechanism of "recombinant conceptualization."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparek94_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparek94-10">[10]</a></sup> This paradigm predicates that a new <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">concept</a> arises through the crossing of pre-existing concepts and <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparek94_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparek94-10">[10]</a></sup>  This is what is meant when one says that a scientist or artist has been "influenced by" another&#8212;<a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymologically</a>, that a concept of the latter\'s has "flowed into" the mind of the former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparek94_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparek94-10">[10]</a></sup>  Not every new concept so formed will be viable: adapting <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinist</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a>\'s phrase, only the fittest concepts survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparek94_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparek94-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Multiple independent discovery and invention, like discovery and invention generally, have been fostered by the evolution of means of <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>: <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sailing_ship" title="Sailing ship">sailing vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a>, institutions of <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, reliable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postal_service" title="Postal service">postal services</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraphy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Gutenberg</a>\'s invention of printing (which itself involved a number of discrete inventions) substantially facilitated the transition from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modern_history" title="Modern history">modern times</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> All these communication developments have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyze" title="Catalyze">catalyzed</a> and accelerated the process of recombinant conceptualization,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and thus also of multiple independent discovery.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/List_of_multiple_discoveries" title="List of multiple discoveries">Multiple independent discoveries</a> show an increased incidence beginning in the 17th century.  This may accord with the thesis of British philosopher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A.C._Grayling" title="A.C. Grayling">A.C. Grayling</a> that the 17th century was crucial in the creation of the modern  <a href="/wiki/World_view" title="World view">world view</a>, freed from the shackles of religion, the occult, and uncritical faith in the authority of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>.  Grayling speculates that Europe\'s <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years\' War">Thirty Years\' War</a> (1618-48), with the concomitant breakdown of authority, made freedom of thought and open debate possible, so that modern science rests on the heads of millions of dead.  He also notes the importance of the development of a reliable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postal_service" title="Postal service">postal service</a> in enabling savants to be in scholarly communication; the cooperative approach, first recommended by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>, was essential to making science open to <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer review</a> and public verification, and not just a matter of the lone individual issuing idiosyncratic pronouncements."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humanities">Humanities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_discovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Humanities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a> of recombinant conceptualization (see above)&#8212;more broadly, of recombinant occurrences&#8212;that explains multiple discovery in science and the arts, also elucidates the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Historic_recurrence" title="Historic recurrence">historic recurrence</a>, wherein similar events are noted in the <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">histories</a> of countries widely separated in time and geography. It is the recurrence of <a href="/wiki/Pattern" title="Pattern">patterns</a> that lends a degree of <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">prognostic</a> power&#8212;and, thus, additional scientific validity&#8212;to the findings of <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trompf79_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trompf79-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (April 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_arts">The arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Multiple_discovery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lamb and Easton, and others, have argued that science and <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> are similar with regard to multiple discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LambEaston84_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LambEaston84-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (April 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparek94_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparek94-10">[10]</a></sup> When two scientists independently make the same discovery, their papers are not word-for-word identical, but the core ideas in the papers are the same; likewise, two novelists may independently write novels with the same core themes, though their novels are not identical word-for-word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LambEaston84_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-LambEaston84-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (April 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civility">Civility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  When it transpires that a discovery has multiple originators, they may either agree to share the credit or insist on their own exclusive primacy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a> had exchanged information on their respective systems of <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a> in the 1670s, Newton in the first edition of his <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophi&#230; Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Principia</a></i> (1687), in a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholium" title="Scholium">scholium</a></i>, apparently accepted Leibniz\'s independent discovery of calculus.  In 1699, however, a Swiss mathematician suggested to Britain\'s <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> that Leibniz had borrowed his calculus from Newton.  In 1705 Leibniz, in an anonymous review of Newton\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Opticks" title="Opticks">Opticks</a></i>, implied that Newton\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_notation" title="Newton\'s notation">fluxions</a></i> (Newton\'s term for <a href="/wiki/Differential_calculus" title="Differential calculus">differential calculus</a>) were an adaptation of Leibniz\'s calculus.  In 1712 the Royal Society appointed a committee to examine the documents in question; the same year, the Society published a report, written by Newton himself, asserting his priority.  Soon after Leibniz died in 1716, Newton denied that his own 1687 <i>Principia</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholium" title="Scholium">scholium</a> "allowed [Leibniz] the invention of the <i>calculus differentialis</i> independently of my own"; and the third edition of Newton\'s <i>Principia</i> (1726) omitted the tell-tale scholium.  It is now accepted that Newton and Leibniz discovered calculus independently of each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In another classic case of multiple discovery, the two discoverers showed more <a href="/wiki/Civility" title="Civility">civility</a>.  By June 1858 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> had completed over two-thirds of his <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Origin_of_Species" title="On the Origin of Species">On the Origin of Species</a></i> when he received a startling letter from a naturalist, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">Alfred Russel Wallace</a>, 13 years his junior, with whom he had corresponded.  The letter summarized Wallace\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_natural_selection" title="Theory of natural selection">theory of natural selection</a>, with conclusions identical to Darwin\'s own.  Darwin turned for advice to his friend <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a>, the foremost geologist of the day.  Lyell proposed that Darwin and Wallace prepare a joint communication to the scientific community.  Darwin being preoccupied with his mortally ill youngest son, Lyell enlisted Darwin\'s closest friend, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dalton_Hooker" title="Joseph Dalton Hooker">Joseph Hooker</a>, director of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Botanic_Gardens,_Kew" title="Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew">Kew Gardens</a>, and together on 1 July 1858 they presented to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linnean_Society" title="Linnean Society">Linnean Society</a> a joint paper that brought together Wallace\'s abstract with extracts from Darwin\'s earlier, 1844 essay on the subject.  The paper was also published that year in the Society\'s journal.  Neither the public reading of the joint paper nor its publication attracted the least interest; but Wallace, "admirably free from envy or jealousy," had been content to remain in Darwin\'s shadow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeve09_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reeve09-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (April 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
