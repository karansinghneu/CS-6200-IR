As with some of the Soviet Union\'s other planetary probes, the later versions were launched in pairs with a second vehicle launched soon after the first.\n</p><p>Ten probes from the Venera series successfully landed on Venus and transmitted data from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_of_Venus" title="Surface of Venus">surface of Venus</a>, including the two <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">Vega program</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">Venera-Halley probes</a>. In addition, thirteen Venera probes successfully transmitted data from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">atmosphere of Venus</a>.\n</p><p>Among other results, probes of the series became the first human-made devices to enter the atmosphere of another planet (<a href="/wiki/Venera_4" title="Venera 4">Venera 4</a> on October 18, 1967), to make a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a> on another planet (<a href="/wiki/Venera_7" title="Venera 7">Venera 7</a> on December 15, 1970), to return images from the planetary surface (<a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a> on June 8, 1975), and to perform high-resolution <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_mapping" title="Radar mapping">radar mapping</a> studies of Venus (<a href="/wiki/Venera_15" title="Venera 15">Venera 15</a> on June 2, 1983). The latter probes in the Venera series successfully carried out their mission, providing the first direct observations of the surface of Venus. Since the surface conditions on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> are extreme, the probes only survived on the surface for durations varying between 23 minutes (initial probes) up to about 2 hours (final probes).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" Cosmonautics</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The first Soviet attempt at a flyby probe to Venus was launched on February 4, 1961, but failed to leave <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> orbit. In keeping with the Soviet policy at that time of not announcing details of failed missions, the launch was announced under the name <a href="/wiki/Tyazhely_Sputnik" title="Tyazhely Sputnik">Tyazhely Sputnik</a> ("Heavy Satellite").  It is also known as Venera 1VA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-1VA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA-1VA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Venera_1" title="Venera 1">Venera 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_2" title="Venera 2">Venera 2</a> were intended to fly past Venus without entering orbit. Venera 1 was launched on February 12, 1961.  Telemetry on the probe failed seven days after launch. It is believed to have passed within 100,000&#160;km (62,000&#160;mi) of Venus and remains in heliocentric orbit. Venera 2 launched on November 12, 1965, but also suffered a telemetry failure after leaving Earth orbit.\n</p><p>Several other failed attempts at Venus flyby probes were launched by the Soviet Union in the early 1960s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chronology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chronology-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but were not announced as planetary missions at the time, and hence did not officially receive the "Venera" designation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_3_to_6">Venera 3 to 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Venera 3 to 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Venera_3" title="Venera 3">Venera 3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venera_4" title="Venera 4">Venera 4</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venera_5" title="Venera 5">Venera 5</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venera_6" title="Venera 6">Venera 6</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_(2004).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2544" data-file-width="2008" decoding="async" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg/180px-Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg/270px-Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg/360px-Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_%282004%29.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars-Venera_station_liquid-based_engine_(2004).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Venera station liquid based engine.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Venera 3 to 6 probes were similar. Weighing approximately one ton, and launched by the <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya</a>-type booster rocket, they included a cruise "bus" and a spherical atmospheric entry probe. The probes were optimised for atmospheric measurements, but not equipped with any special landing apparatus. Although it was hoped they would reach the surface still functioning, the first probes failed almost immediately, thereby disabling data transmission to Earth.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Venera_3" title="Venera 3">Venera 3</a> became the first human-made object to impact another planet\'s surface as it crash-landed on March 1, 1966. However, as the spacecraft\'s dataprobes had failed upon atmospheric penetration, no data from within the Venusian boundary were retrieved from the mission.\n</p><p>On 18 October 1967, <a href="/wiki/Venera_4" title="Venera 4">Venera 4</a> became the first spacecraft to measure the atmosphere of another planet. While the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> initially claimed the craft reached the surface intact, re-analysis including atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultation</a> data from the American <a href="/wiki/Mariner_5" title="Mariner 5">Mariner 5</a> spacecraft that flew by Venus the day after its arrival demonstrated that Venus\'s surface pressure was 75-100 atmospheres, much higher than Venera 4\'s 25 atm hull strength, and the claim was retracted.\n</p><p>Realizing the ships would be crushed before reaching the surface, the Soviets launched <a href="/wiki/Venera_5" title="Venera 5">Venera 5</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_6" title="Venera 6">Venera 6</a> as atmospheric probes. Designed to jettison nearly half their payload prior to entering the planet\'s atmosphere, these craft recorded 53 and 51 minutes of data, respectively, while slowly descending by parachute before their batteries failed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_7">Venera 7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Venera 7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Venera_7" title="Venera 7">Venera 7</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Venera_7" title="Venera 7">Venera 7</a> probe was the first one designed to survive Venus surface conditions and to make a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a>. Massively overbuilt to ensure survival, it had few experiments on board, and scientific output from the mission was further limited due to an internal switchboard failure which stuck in the "transmit temperature" position. Still, the control scientists succeeded in extrapolating the pressure (90 atm) from the temperature data with 465&#160;&#176;C (869&#160;&#176;F), which resulted from the first direct surface measurements. The Doppler measurements of the Venera 4 to 7 probes were the first evidence of the existence of high-speed zonal winds (up to 100 metres per second (330&#160;ft/s) or 362 kilometres per hour (225&#160;mph)) in the Venusian atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus#Circulation" title="Atmosphere of Venus">super rotation</a>).\n</p><p>Venera 7\'s parachute failed shortly before landing very close to the surface. It impacted at 17 metres per second (56&#160;ft/s) and toppled over, but survived. Due to the resultant antenna misalignment, the radio signal was very weak, but was detected (with temperature telemetry) for 23 more minutes before its batteries expired. Thus, it became, on 15 December 1970, the first human-made probe to transmit data from the surface of Venus.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_8">Venera 8</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Venera 8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Venera_8" title="Venera 8">Venera 8</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Venera_8" title="Venera 8">Venera 8</a> was equipped with an extended set of scientific instruments for studying the surface (gamma-spectrometer etc.). The cruise bus of Venera 7 and 8 was similar to that of earlier ones, with the design ascending to the <a href="/wiki/Zond_3" title="Zond 3">Zond 3</a> mission. The lander transmitted data during the descent and landed in sunlight. It measured the light level but had no camera. It transmitted data for almost an hour.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_9_to_12">Venera 9 to 12</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Venera 9 to 12">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">Venera 10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venera_11" title="Venera 11">Venera 11</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venera_12" title="Venera 12">Venera 12</a></div>\n<p>The Venera 9 to 12 probes were of a different design. They weighed approximately five tons and were launched by the powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton</a> booster. They included a transfer and relay bus that had engines to brake into Venus orbit (<a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venera_15" title="Venera 15">15</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_16" title="Venera 16">16</a>) and to serve as receiver and relay for the entry probe\'s transmissions. The entry probe was attached to the top of the bus in a spherical heat shield. The probes were optimized for surface operations with an unusual looking design that included a spherical compartment to protect the electronics from atmospheric pressure and heat for as long as possible. Beneath this was a shock absorbing "crush ring" for landing. Above the pressure sphere was a cylindrical antenna structure and a wide dish shaped structure that resembled an antenna but was actually an aerobrake. They were designed to operate on the surface for a minimum of 30 minutes. Instruments varied on different missions, but included cameras and atmospheric and soil analysis equipment. All four landers had problems with some or all of their camera lens caps not releasing.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a> lander operated for at least 53 minutes and took pictures with one of two cameras; the other lens cap did not release.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">Venera 10</a> lander operated for at least 65 minutes and took pictures with one of two cameras; the other lens cap did not release.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Venera_11" title="Venera 11">Venera 11</a> lander operated for at least 95 minutes but neither cameras\' lens caps released.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Venera_12" title="Venera 12">Venera 12</a> lander operated for at least 110 minutes but neither cameras\' lens caps released.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_13_and_14">Venera 13 and 14</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Venera 13 and 14">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Venera_13" title="Venera 13">Venera 13</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_14" title="Venera 14">Venera 14</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cut-away_model_of_a_Soviet_communications_satellite.JPEG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2810" data-file-width="1890" decoding="async" height="297" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cut-away_model_of_a_Soviet_communications_satellite.JPEG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Model of a lander Venera</div></div></div>\n<p>The descent craft/lander contained most of the instrumentation and electronics, and was topped by an antenna. The design was similar to the earlier Venera 9&#8211;12 landers. They carried instruments to take scientific measurements of the ground and atmosphere once landed, including cameras, a microphone, a drill and surface sampler, and a seismometer. They also had instruments to record electric discharges during its descent phase through the Venusian atmosphere.\n</p><p>The two descent craft landed about 950&#160;km (590&#160;mi) apart, just east of the eastern extension of an elevated region known as <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_Regio" title="Phoebe Regio">Phoebe Regio</a>. The Venera 13 lander survived for 127 minutes, and the Venera 14 lander for 57 minutes, where the planned design life was only 32 minutes. The Venera 14 craft had the misfortune of ejecting the camera lens cap directly under the surface compressibility tester arm, and returned information for the compressibility of the lens cap rather than the surface. The descent vehicles transmitted data to the buses, which acted as data relays as they flew by Venus.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_15_and_16">Venera 15 and 16</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Venera 15 and 16">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Venera_15" title="Venera 15">Venera 15</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_16" title="Venera 16">Venera 16</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VenusTopoVenera.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1060" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/VenusTopoVenera.jpg/220px-VenusTopoVenera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/VenusTopoVenera.jpg/330px-VenusTopoVenera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/VenusTopoVenera.jpg/440px-VenusTopoVenera.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VenusTopoVenera.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Radar topography obtained by Venera 15/16</div></div></div><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_15_and_16" title="Venera 15 and 16">Venera 15 and 16</a> were similar to previous probes, but the entry probes were replaced with surface imaging radar equipment. Radar imaging was necessary to penetrate the dense cloud of Venus.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VeGa_probes">VeGa probes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: VeGa probes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">Vega program</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">VeGa</a> (Cyrillic: &#1042;&#1077;&#1043;&#1072;) probes to Venus and <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">comet 1/P Halley</a> launched in 1985 also used this basic Venera design, including landers but also atmospheric balloons which relayed data for about two days. "VeGa" is an agglutination of the words "Venera" (<a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> in Russian) and "Gallei" (<a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">Halley</a> in Russian).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera-D">Venera-D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Venera-D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Venera-D" title="Venera-D">Venera-D</a></div>\n<p>Venera-D is a proposed mission to Venus that would include a highly capable orbiter and a lander. From the standpoint of total mass delivered to Venus, the best launch opportunities occur in 2026 and 2031.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2018_status_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-2018_status-4">[4]</a></sup> Venera-D could incorporate some NASA components, including balloons, a subsatellite for <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> measurements, or a long-lived (24 hours) surface station on the lander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joint_proposal_2017_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joint_proposal_2017-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Science_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Science-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Report_2017_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Report_2017-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_findings">Scientific findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scientific findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There were many scientific findings about Venus from the data retrieved by the Venera probes. For example, after analyzing the radar images returned from Venera 15 and 16, it was concluded that the ridges and grooves on the surface of Venus were the result of <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> deformations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Venera_camera_successes_and_failures">Venera camera successes and failures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Venera camera successes and failures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Venera 9 and 10 landers had two cameras each. Only one functioned because the lens covers failed to separate from the second camera on each lander. The design was changed for Venera 11 and 12, but this change made the problem worse and all cameras failed on those missions. Venera 13 and 14 were the only landers on which all cameras worked properly; although unfortunately, the titanium lens cap on Venera 14 landed precisely on the area which was targeted by the soil compression probe.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_Venera_probes">Types of Venera probes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
