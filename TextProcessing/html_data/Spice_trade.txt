id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silk_route.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1866" data-file-width="2868" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Silk_route.jpg/250px-Silk_route.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Silk_route.jpg/375px-Silk_route.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Silk_route.jpg/500px-Silk_route.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silk_route.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The economically important <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> (red) and spice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_routes" title="Trade routes">trade routes</a> (blue) blocked by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> c. 1453 with the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>, spurring exploration motivated initially by the finding of a sea route around Africa and triggering the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>spice trade</b>\nrefers to the <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> between historical civilizations in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Northeast Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Spices such as <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinnamomum_aromaticum" title="Cinnamomum aromaticum">cassia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardamom" title="Cardamom">cardamom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">pepper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric</a> were known and <a href="/wiki/Spice_use_in_Antiquity" title="Spice use in Antiquity">used in antiquity</a> for commerce in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_World" title="Eastern World">Eastern World</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> These spices found their way into the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> before the beginning of the Christian era, where the true sources of these spices were withheld by the traders and associated with fantastic tales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> Early writings and stone age carvings of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> age obtained indicates that <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>\'s southwest coastal port <a href="/wiki/Muziris" title="Muziris">Muziris</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a>, had established itself as a major spice trade centre from as early as 3000 BC, which marked the beginning of the spice trade. Kerala, referred to as the land of spices or as the "Spice Garden of India", was the place traders and explorers wanted to reach, including <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco-Roman world</a> followed by trading along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incense_route" title="Incense route">Incense route</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_trade_with_India" title="Roman trade with India">Roman-India routes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fage_1975:_164_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fage_1975:_164-3">[3]</a></sup> During the first millennium, the sea routes to <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> (the Roman &#8211; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taprobane" title="Taprobane">Taprobane</a>) and India were controlled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethiopian" title="Ethiopian">Ethiopians</a> who became the maritime trading power of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and the Indians. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aksumite_Empire" title="Aksumite Empire">Kingdom of Axum</a> (c. 5th-century BC&#8211;AD 11th century) had pioneered the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> route before the 1st century AD. By mid-7th century AD after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rise_of_Islam" title="Rise of Islam">rise of Islam</a>, Arab traders started dominating the maritime routes.\n</p><p>Arab traders eventually took over conveying goods via the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian</a> merchants to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> until the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">Ottoman Turks</a> cut the route again by 1453. Overland routes helped the spice trade initially, but maritime <a href="/wiki/Trade_route" title="Trade route">trade routes</a> led to tremendous growth in commercial activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">high</a> and <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">late</a> medieval periods Muslim traders dominated maritime spice trading routes throughout the Indian Ocean, tapping source regions in East Asia and shipping spices from trading emporiums in India westward to the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and the Red Sea, from which overland routes led to Europe.\n</p><p>The trade was changed by the European <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin-4">[4]</a></sup> during which the spice trade, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, became an influential activity for European traders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CornPrologue_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CornPrologue-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cape_Route" title="Cape Route">Cape Route</a> from Europe to the Indian Ocean via the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> was pioneered by the Portuguese explorer navigator Vasco da Gama in 1498, resulting in new maritime routes for trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia1-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This trade, which drove the world economy from the end of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> well into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern" title="Early Modern">modern times</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CornPrologue_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CornPrologue-5">[5]</a></sup> ushered in an age of European domination in the East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia1-6">[6]</a></sup> Channels, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>, served as bridges for cultural and commercial exchanges between diverse cultures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin-4">[4]</a></sup> as nations struggled to gain control of the trade along the many spice routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> European dominance was slow to develop. The Portuguese trade routes were mainly restricted and limited by the use of ancient routes, ports, and nations that were difficult to dominate. The Dutch were later able to bypass many of these problems by pioneering a direct ocean route from the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptians</a> had traded in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, spices from the "<a href="/wiki/Land_of_Punt" title="Land of Punt">Land of Punt</a>" and from <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">Luxury goods</a> traded along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incense_Route" title="Incense Route">Incense Route</a> included Indian spices, <a href="/wiki/Ebony" title="Ebony">ebony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> and fine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a>. The spice trade was associated with overland routes early on but maritime routes proved to be the factor which helped the trade grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a> had developed trade with India using the Red Sea ports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw1-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>People from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period traded in <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_shells" title="Sea shells">sea shells</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precious_stones" title="Precious stones">precious stones</a> and other high-value materials as early as the 10th millennium BC. The first to mention the trade in historical periods are the Egyptians. In the 3rd millennium BC, they traded with the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Punt" title="Land of Punt">Land of Punt</a>, which is believed to have been situated in an area encompassing northern <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the first millennium BC the <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arabs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">Phoenicians</a>, and Indians were engaged in sea and land trade in luxury goods such as spices, gold, precious stones, leather of rare animals, ebony and pearls. The sea trade was in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. The sea route in the Red Sea was from <a href="/wiki/Bab-el-Mandeb" title="Bab-el-Mandeb">Bab-el-Mandeb</a> to Berenike and from there by land to the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> and then by boats to <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>. The land trade was in deserts of Western Arabia using camels. The <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Indonesia" title="Ethnic groups in Indonesia">Indonesians</a> were trading in spices (mainly <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassia_bark" title="Cassia bark">cassia</a>) with <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> using <a href="/wiki/Catamaran" title="Catamaran">Catamaran</a> boats and sailing with the help of the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a> in the Indian Ocean.\n</p><p>In the second half of the first millennium BC the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_tribes" title="Arab tribes">Arab tribes</a> of South and West Arabia took control over the land trade of spices from <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">South Arabia</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. The tribes were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%27ain" title="M\'ain">M\'ain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qataban" title="Qataban">Qataban</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hadhramaut" title="Hadhramaut">Hadhramaut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">Saba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himyarite" title="Himyarite">Himyarite</a>. In the north the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nabateans" title="Nabateans">Nabateans</a> took control of the trade route that crossed the <a href="/wiki/Negev" title="Negev">Negev</a> from <a href="/wiki/Petra" title="Petra">Petra</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gaza_City" title="Gaza City">Gaza</a>. The trade made the Arab tribes very rich. The <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">South Arabia</a> region was called <a href="/wiki/Arabia_Felix" title="Arabia Felix">Eudaemon Arabia</a> (the elated Arabia) by the Greeks and was on the agenda of conquests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_of_Macedonia" title="Alexander of Macedonia">Alexander of Macedonia</a> before he died. The Indians and the Arabs had control over the sea trade with India. In the late second century BC, the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> learned from the Indians how to sail directly from <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> to the West coast of India using the monsoon winds (<a href="/wiki/Hippalus" title="Hippalus">Hippalus</a>) and took control over the sea trade.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_trade_and_medieval_Europe">Arab trade and medieval Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arab trade and medieval Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Italy_to_India_Route.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="606" decoding="async" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Italy_to_India_Route.svg/220px-Italy_to_India_Route.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Italy_to_India_Route.svg/330px-Italy_to_India_Route.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Italy_to_India_Route.svg/440px-Italy_to_India_Route.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Italy_to_India_Route.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trade route in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> linking Italy to south-west India</div></div></div>\n<p>Rome played a part in the spice trade during the 5th century, but this role, unlike the Arabian one, did not last through the Middle Ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> The rise of Islam brought a significant change to the trade as <a href="/wiki/Radhanite" title="Radhanite">Radhanite</a> Jewish and Arab merchants particularly from <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> eventually took over conveying goods via the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.\n</p><p>The Spice trade had brought great riches to the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a>, and even inspired famous legends such as that of <a href="/wiki/Sinbad_the_Sailor" title="Sinbad the Sailor">Sinbad the Sailor</a>. These early sailors and merchants would often set sail from the port city of <a href="/wiki/Basra" title="Basra">Basra</a> and eventually after many voyages they would return to sell their goods including spices in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>. The fame of many spices such as <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cinnamon" title="Cinnamon">cinnamon</a> are attributed to these early Spice merchants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (March 2012)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The Indian commercial connection with South East Asia proved vital to the merchants of Arabia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persia</a> during the 7th and 8th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin1-11">[11]</a></sup> Arab traders &#8211; mainly descendants of sailors from <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> &#8211; dominated maritime routes throughout the Indian Ocean, tapping source regions in the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> &#8211; linking to the secret "spice islands" (<a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Maluku Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda Islands</a>). The islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molucca" title="Molucca">Molucca</a> also find mention in several records: a Javanese chronicle (1365) mentions the Moluccas and <i>Maloko</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin6_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin6-12">[12]</a></sup> and navigational works of the 14th and 15th centuries contain the first unequivocal Arab reference to Moluccas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin6_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin6-12">[12]</a></sup> Sulaima al-Mahr writes: "East of <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a> [where <a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">sandalwood</a> is found] are the islands of <i>Bandam</i> and they are the islands where nutmeg and mace are found. The islands of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloves" title="Cloves">cloves</a> are called <i>Maluku</i> ....."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin6_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin6-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Moluccan products were then shipped to trading emporiums in India, passing through ports like <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Kozhikode</a>, and through <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin4-13">[13]</a></sup> from there they were shipped westward across the ports of Arabia to the Near East, to <a href="/wiki/Ormus" title="Ormus">Ormus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and sometimes shipped to <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, where they would be used for many purposes, including burial rites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin4_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin4-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasids" title="Abbasids">Abbasids</a> used Alexandria, <a href="/wiki/Damietta" title="Damietta">Damietta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siraf" title="Siraf">Siraf</a> as entry ports to India and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin3-14">[14]</a></sup> Merchants arriving from India in the port city of Aden paid tribute in form of <a href="/wiki/Musk" title="Musk">musk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camphor" title="Camphor">camphor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambergris" title="Ambergris">ambergris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">sandalwood</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Ziyad" title="Ibn Ziyad">Ibn Ziyad</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin3-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Indian spice exports find mention in the works of Ibn Khurdadhbeh (850), al-Ghafiqi (1150), Ishak bin Imaran (907) and Al Kalkashandi (14th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin4_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin4-13">[13]</a></sup> Chinese traveler <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> mentions the town of <a href="/wiki/Puri" title="Puri">Puri</a> where "merchants depart for distant countries."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin2-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From there, overland routes led to the Mediterranean coasts. From the 8th until the 15th century, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a> and neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Republics" title="Maritime Republics">maritime republics</a> held the monopoly of European trade with the Middle East. The silk and spice trade, involving <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incense" title="Incense">incense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drugs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, made these Mediterranean city-states phenomenally rich. Spices were among the most expensive and in-demand products of the Middle Ages, used in <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>. They were all imported from Asia and Africa. Venetian merchants distributed then the goods through Europe until the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, which eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">fall of Constantinople</a> in 1453, barring Europeans from important combined land-sea routes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_European_Discovery:_finding_a_new_route_and_a_New_World">Age of European Discovery: finding a new route and a New World</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Age of European Discovery: finding a new route and a New World">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/16th_century_Portuguese_Spanish_trade_routes.png/440px-16th_century_Portuguese_Spanish_trade_routes.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div 1572.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a> had become a formidable power, and a key player in the Eastern spice trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\nOther powers, in an attempt to break the Venetian hold on spice trade, began to build up maritime capability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-EB1-1">[1]</a></sup> Until the mid-15th century, trade with the east was achieved through the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, with the Byzantine Empire and the <a href="/wiki/Italian_city-states" title="Italian city-states">Italian city-states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a> acting as a middle man.\n</p><p>In 1453, however, the Ottomans took <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and so the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> was no more. Now in control of the sole spice trade route that existed at the time, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> was in a favorable position to charge hefty taxes on merchandise bound for the west. The Western Europeans, not wanting to be dependent on an expansionist, non-Christian power for the lucrative commerce with the east, set out to find an alternate sea route around <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.\n</p><p>The first country to attempt to circumnavigate Africa was Portugal, which had, since the early 15th century, begun to explore northern Africa under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_the_Navigator" title="Henry the Navigator">Henry the Navigator</a>. Emboldened by these early successes and eyeing a lucrative monopoly on a possible sea route to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indies" title="Indies">Indies</a> the Portuguese first crossed the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> in 1488 on an expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Just nine years later in 1497 on the orders of <a href="/wiki/Manuel_I_of_Portugal" title="Manuel I of Portugal">Manuel I of Portugal</a>, four vessels under the command of navigator <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> rounded the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, continuing to the eastern coast of Africa to <a href="/wiki/Malindi" title="Malindi">Malindi</a> to sail across the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to <a href="/wiki/History_of_Kozhikode" title="History of Kozhikode">Calicut</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Malabar_Coast" title="Malabar Coast">Malabar Coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia1-6">[6]</a></sup> in south India &#8211; the capital of the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zamorin" title="Zamorin">Zamorin</a> rulers. The wealth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indies" title="Indies">Indies</a> was now open for the Europeans to explore; the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese Empire</a> was the earliest European seaborne empire to grow from the spice trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia1-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay,_1762.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1241" data-file-width="1752" decoding="async" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg/240px-Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg/360px-Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg/480px-Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay%2C_1762.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anonymous_The_Noord-Nieuwland_in_Table_Bay,_1762.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dutch ships in <a href="/wiki/Table_Bay" title="Table Bay">Table Bay</a> docking at the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> at the Cape of Good Hope, 1762.</div></div></div>\n<p>It was during this time that Italian, Spanish and Portuguese explorers first set foot on the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> was the first to do so in 1492 while sailing westward across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> on an expedition to the indies. Instead of reaching Asia, Colombus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discovery_of_the_Americas" title="Discovery of the Americas">discovered the Americas</a>, landing on an island in what is now <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>. Believing to have in fact reached India, the crew named the natives "Indians", a name which has continued in use to this day, to describe the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Just eight years later in 1500, the Portuguese navigator, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_%C3%81lvares_Cabral" title="Pedro &#193;lvares Cabral">Pedro &#193;lvares Cabral</a> while attempting to reproduce Vasco da Gama&#8217;s Atlantic route to the Cape and India was blown westwards to what is today <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. After taking possession of the new land, Cabral resumed his voyage across the Atlantic to the southern tip of Africa and India, finally arriving there in September 1500&#160;&#8211; opening for the first time a route from the New World to Asia&#160;&#8211; and returning to Portugal by 1501.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1511, <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> for Portugal, then the center of Asian trade. East of Malacca, Albuquerque sent several diplomatic and exploratory missions, including to the Moluccas. Getting to know the secret location of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spice_Islands" title="Spice Islands">Spice Islands</a></i>, mainly the Banda Islands, then the world source of nutmeg and cloves, he sent an expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Abreu" title="Ant&#243;nio de Abreu">Ant&#243;nio de Abreu</a> to Banda, where they were the first Europeans to arrive in early 1512.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Abreu`s expedition reached <a href="/wiki/Buru" title="Buru">Buru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Ambon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seram_Island" title="Seram Island">Seram</a> Islands, and then Banda. Later, after a forced separation and a shipwreck, his vice-captain, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Serr%C3%A3o" title="Francisco Serr&#227;o">Francisco Serr&#227;o</a> went again to the north, to Ambon, and reached <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a>, where he obtained a license to build a Portuguese fortress-factory: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forte_de_S%C3%A3o_Jo%C3%A3o_Baptista_de_Ternate" title="Forte de S&#227;o Jo&#227;o Baptista de Ternate">Forte de S&#227;o Jo&#227;o Baptista de Ternate</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;[<a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forte_de_S%C3%A3o_Jo%C3%A3o_Baptista_de_Ternate" title="pt:Forte de S&#227;o Jo&#227;o Baptista de Ternate">pt</a>]</span>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="956" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg/350px-Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg/525px-Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg/700px-Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iberian_mare_clausum_claims.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portugal claimed the Indian Ocean as its <i><a href="/wiki/Mare_clausum" title="Mare clausum">mare clausum</a></i> during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>From 1507&#8211;1515 Albuquerque tried to completely block Arab and other traditional routes that stretched from the shores of Western Pacific to the Mediterranean sea, through the conquest of strategic bases in the Persian Gulf and at the entry of the Red Sea. By the early 16th century the Portuguese had complete control of the African sea route, which since 1512, through a long network of routes that linked three oceans, extended from the Moluccas (the Spice Islands), in the Pacific Ocean limits, through Malacca, India and Sri Lanka (linked years later to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> in Portugal (Europe), via the Indian and the Atlantic Oceans.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a> organized the expedition of Christopher Columbus to compete with Portugal for the spice trade with Asia, but instead, landed in a New World. The search for a route to Asia was resumed a few years later, after explorer <a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco N&#250;&#241;ez de Balboa">Vasco N&#250;&#241;ez de Balboa</a> crossed the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> in 1513 and became the first European to sight the Pacific Ocean from the New World, confirming that the Americas were separate continents. The Spanish crown then prepared a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Magellan%27s_circumnavigation" title="Timeline of Magellan\'s circumnavigation">great westward voyage</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>, in order to reach Asia from Spain across the Atlantic, and then Pacific Oceans. On October 21, 1520, his expedition crossed <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">the strait that bears his name</a> in the southern tip of South America, opening the Pacific to European exploration. On March 16, 1521, the ships reached the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and soon after the Spice Islands, ultimately resulting in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manila_Galleons" title="Manila Galleons">Manila Galleon</a> trade, the first westward spice trade route to Asia.\n</p><p>After Magellan\'s death in the Philippines, navigator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Sebastian_Elcano" title="Juan Sebastian Elcano">Juan Sebastian Elcano</a> took command of the expedition and drove it across the Indian Ocean and back to Spain, where they arrived in 1522 aboard the last remaining ship: the <i>Victoria</i>. These explorers became the first men to <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigate</a> the globe. For the next two and half centuries, Spain controlled a vast trade network that linked three continents: Asia, the Americas and Europe. A global spice route had been created: from <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> in the Philippines (Asia) to <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> in Spain (Europe), via <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> (North America).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_diffusion">Cultural diffusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spice_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural diffusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> religious establishments of Southeast Asia came to be associated with economic activity and commerce as patrons entrusted large funds which would later be used to benefit local economy by estate management, craftsmanship promotion of trading activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin67_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin67-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, in particular, traveled alongside the maritime trade, promoting coinage, art and literacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin69_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin69-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> spread throughout the East, reaching <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Southeast_Asia" title="Maritime Southeast Asia">Maritime Southeast Asia</a> in the 10th century; Muslim merchants played a crucial part in the trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corn0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corn0-23">[23]</a></sup> Christian missionaries, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Francis_Xavier" title="Saint Francis Xavier">Saint Francis Xavier</a>, were instrumental in the spread of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> in the East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corn0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Corn0-23">[23]</a></sup> Christianity competed with Islam to become the dominant religion of the Moluccas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corn0_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Corn0-23">[23]</a></sup> However, the natives of the Spice Islands accommodated aspects of both religions easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Portuguese colonial settlements saw traders such as the Gujarati <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanika" title="Vanika">banias</a>, South Indian Chettis, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syriac_Christians" title="Syriac Christians">Syrian Christians</a>, Chinese from <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> province, and Arabs from <a href="/wiki/Aden" title="Aden">Aden</a> involved in the spice trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collingham56_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collingham56-25">[25]</a></sup> Epics, languages, and cultural customs were borrowed by Southeast Asia from India, and later China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donkin_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Donkin-4">[4]</a></sup> Knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese language</a> became essential for merchants involved in the trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Colonial pepper trade drastically changed the experience of modernity in Europe and in Kerala and it brought, along with colonialism, early capitalism to India\'s Malabar Coast, changing cultures of work and caste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Indian merchants involved in spice trade took <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">Indian cuisine</a> to Southeast Asia, notably present day <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, where spice mixtures and <a href="/wiki/Curry" title="Curry">curries</a> became popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collingham245_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collingham245-28">[28]</a></sup> European people intermarried with the Indians, and popularized valuable culinary skills, such as <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a>, in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collingham61_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collingham61-29">[29]</a></sup> The Portuguese also introduced vinegar to India, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan priests</a> manufactured it from coconut toddy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collingham69_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collingham69-30">[30]</a></sup> Indian food, adapted to the European palate, became visible in England by 1811 as exclusive establishments began catering to the tastes of both the curious and those returning from India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collingham129_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collingham129-31">[31]</a></sup> Opium was a part of the spice trade and some people involved in the spice trade were driven by opium addiction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
