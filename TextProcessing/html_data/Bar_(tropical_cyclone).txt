mw-body-content">\n</div>\n<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bar (tropical cyclone)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>bar</b> of a mature <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> is a very dark gray-black layer of cloud appearing near the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> as seen from an observer preceding the approach of the storm, and is composed of dense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a> clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRBS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IRBS-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus" title="Cumulus">Cumulus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a> clouds bearing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> follow immediately after the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall</a>-like bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurEnc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HurEnc-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altostratus" title="Altostratus">Altostratus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cirrostratus" title="Cirrostratus">cirrostratus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus</a> clouds are usually visible in ascending order above the top of the bar, while the <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a> for an observer facing toward the bar is typically from the left and slightly behind the observer.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(tropical_cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The dark layer of clouds on the horizon seen prior to a tropical cyclone\'s passage over a location was first described in 1687 and the observed phenomenon later published in 1697 by <a href="/wiki/William_Dampier" title="William Dampier">William Dampier</a> while observing a typhoon in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigation</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">pirate</a> ship. These observations led to an improved understanding of the nature of tropical cyclones. The use of "bar" as a term to describe this cloud layer first appeared in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurEnc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HurEnc-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inside_the_bar">Inside the bar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(tropical_cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inside the bar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_structure_graphic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="350" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Hurricane_structure_graphic.jpg/300px-Hurricane_structure_graphic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/Hurricane_structure_graphic.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_structure_graphic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The basic vertical structure of a mature tropical cyclone. The bar appears as the storm\'s main disk of initially the upper storm clouds followed by the lower clouds as viewed from a distance, and gaps exist in the rainbands after the bar passes over.</div></div></div>\n<p>When the bar of the storm approaches an observer, it appears stationary in <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuthal</a> position, whereas a storm moving toward the observer at an angle or perpendicular to the observer will appear to drift along the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRBS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IRBS-1">[1]</a></sup> A reddish tint sometimes appears toward the top of the bar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while the darkness often varies depending on the storm\'s intensity. Tints of indigo, green, yellow or violet may also be present depending on the time of day due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prismatic</a> effect of water droplets in the cloud. Cumulus clouds first appear at the <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">boundary</a> of the lower part of the bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurEnc_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HurEnc-2">[2]</a></sup> As the bar passes overhead, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> at the observer\'s location falls steadily. The first <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainband</a> arrives, the clouds moving from left to right roughly along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobar</a> lines, but this is often followed by short periods of relative calm. During the onset of the bar\'s arrival, winds continuously increase in intensity leading toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a>. Navigators of ships at sea often use the first appearance of a bar to steer clear of the approaching tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRBS_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IRBS-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Closer to the center of tropical cyclone, the eyewall also exhibits the appearance of a bar, and high <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> activity occurs within this central bar. After the bar passes through, the strongest winds and often the heaviest precipitation abruptly changes to calmer conditions within the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, before the eyewall passes over again and the strongest winds arrive from the opposite direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HurEnc_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HurEnc-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At sea, wind speeds typically reach level 8 on the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a> while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_wave" title="Ocean wave">waves</a> become drastically higher when the bar reaches overhead and <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> begin to arrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRBS_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-IRBS-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(tropical_cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-IRBS-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-IRBS_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-IRBS_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-IRBS_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-IRBS_1-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Bowditch, Nathaniel. <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110409152823/http://www.irbs.com/bowditch/pdf/chapt36.pdf" rel="nofollow">"Tropical Cyclones"</a> <span class="cs1-format">(PDF)</span>. <i>I\'d Rather Be Sailing</i>.
