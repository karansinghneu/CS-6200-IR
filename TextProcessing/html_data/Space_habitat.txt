called a <b>space colony</b>, <b>space settlement</b>, <b>orbital habitat</b>, <b>orbital settlement</b> or <b>orbital colony</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a>, intended as a <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">permanent settlement</a> rather than as a simple way-station or other specialized facility.  No space habitat has been constructed yet, but many design concepts, with varying degrees of realism, have come both from engineers and from science-fiction authors.\n</p><p>The term <i>space habitat</i> sometimes includes more broadly <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">colonies built on or in a body other than Earth</a> - such as the Moon, Mars or an asteroid. made for space habitats having a number of advantages:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_to_solar_energy">Access to solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Access to solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Space has an abundance of light produced from the Sun. In Earth orbit, this amounts to 1400 watts of power per square meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoppLean11_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-KoppLean11-2">[2]</a></sup> This energy can be used to produce electricity from <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">solar cells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> based power stations, process ores, provide light for plants to grow and to warm space colonies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outside_gravity_well">Outside gravity well</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Outside gravity well">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Earth-to-space habitat trade would be easier than Earth-to-planetary colony trade, as colonies orbiting Earth will not have a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_well" title="Gravity well">gravity well</a> to overcome to export to Earth, and a smaller gravity well to overcome to import from Earth.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In-situ_resource_utilization">In-situ resource utilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In-situ resource utilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Space habitats may be supplied with resources from extraterrestrial places like <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">in-situ resource utilization</a> [ISRU];<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doehring_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doehring-1">[1]</a></sup> see <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">Asteroid mining</a>). One could produce breathing oxygen, drinking water, and rocket fuel with the help of ISRU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doehring_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Doehring-1">[1]</a></sup> It may become possible to manufacture solar panels from lunar materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doehring_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Doehring-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asteroids_and_other_small_bodies">Asteroids and other small bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asteroids and other small bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most asteroids have a mixture of materials, that could be mined, and because these bodies do not have substantial gravity wells, it would require low delta-V to <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">draw materials from them</a> and haul them to a construction site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pournelle,_Jerrold_E._1977_3-0"><a They would have to provide all the material needs for hundreds or thousands of humans, in an environment out in space that is very hostile to human life.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Air <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, with normal partial pressures of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>(21%), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>(78%), is a basic requirement of any space habitat. Basically, most space colony designs concepts envision large, thin-walled pressure vessels. The required oxygen could be obtained from lunar rock. Nitrogen is most easily available from the Earth, but is also recycled nearly perfectly.  Also, nitrogen in the form of ammonia (<a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia"><span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></a>) may be obtainable from comets and the moons of outer planets.  Nitrogen may also be available in unknown quantities on certain other bodies in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_solar_system" title="Outer solar system">outer solar system</a>. The air of a colony could be recycled in a number of ways. One concept is to use <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardens</a>, possibly via <a href="/wiki/Hydroponics" title="Hydroponics">hydroponics</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">forest gardening</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, these do not remove certain industrial pollutants, such as volatile oils, and excess simple molecular gases. The standard method used on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_submarines" title="Nuclear submarines">nuclear submarines</a>, a similar form of closed environment, is to use a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> burner, which effectively decomposes most organics. Further protection might be provided by a small cryogenic distillation system which would gradually remove impurities such as <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> vapor, and noble gases that cannot be catalytically burned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_production">Food production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Food production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">Organic</a> materials for food production would also need to be provided. At first, most of these would have to be imported from Earth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After that, feces recycling should reduce the need for imports.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One proposed recycling method would start by burning the cryogenic distillate, plants, garbage and sewage with air in an electric arc, and distilling the result.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The resulting carbon dioxide and water would be immediately usable in agriculture. The nitrates and salts in the ash could be dissolved in water and separated into pure minerals. Most of the nitrates, potassium and sodium salts would recycle as fertilizers. Other minerals containing iron, nickel, and silicon could be chemically purified in batches and reused industrially. The small fraction of remaining materials, well below 0.01% by weight, could be processed into pure elements with zero-gravity <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a>, and added in appropriate amounts to the fertilizers and industrial stocks. It is likely that methods would be greatly refined as people began to actually live in space habitats.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_gravity">Artificial gravity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Artificial gravity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Artificial_gravity" title="Artificial gravity">Artificial gravity</a></div>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Most people have a continual stuffy nose or sinus problems, and a few people have dramatic, incurable motion sickness. Most colony designs would rotate in order to use <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertial</a> forces to <a href="/wiki/Artificial_gravity" title="Artificial gravity">simulate gravity</a>. NASA studies with chickens and plants have proven that this is an effective physiological substitute for gravity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Turning one\'s head rapidly in such an environment causes a "tilt" to be sensed as one\'s inner ears move at different rotational rates. Centrifuge studies show that people get motion-sick in habitats with a rotational radius of less than 100 metres, or with a rotation rate above 3 rotations per minute. However, the same studies and statistical inference indicate that almost all people should be able to live comfortably in habitats with a rotational radius larger than 500 meters and below 1 RPM. Experienced persons were not merely more resistant to motion sickness, but could also use the effect to determine "spinward" and "antispinward" directions in the centrifuges.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Smaller habitats could be shielded by stationary (nonrotating) bags of rock.  Sunlight could be admitted indirectly via mirrors in radiation-proof louvres, which would function in the same manner as a <a href="/wiki/Periscope" title="Periscope">periscope</a>.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>For instance, 4 metric tons per square meter of surface area could reduce radiation dosage to several mSv or less annually, below the rate of some populated <a href="/wiki/Background_radiation" title="Background radiation">high natural background areas</a> on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Alternative concepts based on active shielding are untested yet and more complex than such passive mass shielding, but usage of magnetic and/or electric fields to deflect particles could potentially greatly reduce mass requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacecraftshielding_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-spacecraftshielding-12">[12]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>If a space habitat is located at <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">L4 or&#160;L5</a>, then its orbit will take it outside of the protection of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> for approximately two-thirds of the time (as happens with the Moon), putting residents at risk of <a href="/wiki/Proton#Exposure" title="Proton">proton exposure</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</dd>\n<dd>See <i><a href="/wiki/Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays" title="Health threat from cosmic rays">Health threat from cosmic rays</a></i></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_rejection">Heat rejection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Heat rejection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The colony is in a vacuum, and therefore resembles a giant thermos bottle. Habitats also need a <a href="/wiki/Radiator" title="Radiator">radiator</a> to eliminate heat from absorbed sunlight. Very small habitats might have a central vane that rotates with the colony. In this design, <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> would raise hot air "up" (toward the center), and cool air would fall down into the outer habitat. Some other designs would distribute coolants, such as chilled water from a central radiator.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteoroids_and_dust">Meteoroids and dust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Meteoroids and dust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The habitat would need to withstand potential impacts from <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a>, dust,&#160;etc. Most meteoroids that strike the earth vaporize in the atmosphere. Without a thick protective atmosphere meteoroid strikes would pose a much greater risk to a space habitat.  <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a> will sweep the space around each habitat mapping the trajectory of debris and other man-made objects and allowing corrective actions to be taken to protect the habitat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\nIn some designs (O\'Neill/NASA Ames "Stanford Torus" and "Crystal palace in a Hatbox" habitat designs have a non-rotating cosmic ray shield of packed sand (~1.9m thick) or even artificial aggregate rock (1.7m ersatz concrete). Other proposals use the rock as structure and integral shielding (O\'Neill, "the High Frontier". Sheppard, "Concrete Space Colonies"; Spaceflight, journal of the B.I.S.) In any of these cases, strong meteoroid protection is implied by the external radiation shell ~4.5tonnes of rock material, per square meter.\nSuggestions have been made to layer such protections around a habitat, multiple thicknesses, up to such protection to equal an Earth city (which after all, is completely vulnerable to total obliteration at any second without notice, under the open sky. A small rock such as hit near Chelyabinsk in 2013 being too numerous and hard to detect for any mitigation scenario or advance warning.) With such a shell, mass of a colony goes up sharply, but it is only dumb structure -possibly non-rotating, and rock-dust as industrial slag is a free left-over from processing regolith for metals or volatiles extraction.\nNote that Solar Power Satellites are proposed in the multi-gW ranges, and such energies and technologies would allow constant radar mapping of nearby 3D space out-to arbitrarily far away, limited only by effort expended to do so.\nProposals are available to move even kilometer-sized NEOs to high Earth orbits, and reaction engines for such purposes would move a space colony and any arbitrarily large shield, but not in any timely or rapid manner, the thrust being very low compared to the huge mass.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitude_control">Attitude control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Attitude control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most mirror geometries require something on the habitat to be aimed at the sun and so <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">attitude control</a> is necessary. The lunar <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>4</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>5</sub></a> orbits are now thought to be too far away from the moon and Earth. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This provides quick, inexpensive access to both raw materials and the major market. Most colony designs plan to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tether_propulsion" title="Tether propulsion">electromagnetic tether propulsion</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">mass drivers</a> used as rocket motors. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conceptual_studies">Conceptual studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Conceptual studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noordung_space_station.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2686" data-file-width="1939" decoding="async" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Noordung_space_station.jpg/220px-Noordung_space_station.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Noordung_space_station.jpg/330px-Noordung_space_station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Noordung_space_station.jpg/440px-Noordung_space_station.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noordung_space_station.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Description of a rotating wheel space station in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Noordung" title="Hermann Noordung">Hermann Noordung</a>\'s <i>The Problem of Space Travel</i> (1929)</div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="O.27Neill_-_The_High_Frontier"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="O\'Neill_-_The_High_Frontier">O\'Neill - The High Frontier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: O\'Neill - The High Frontier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Around 1970, near the end of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Project Apollo</a> (1961-1972), <a href="/wiki/Gerard_K._O%27Neill" title="Gerard K. O\'Neill">Gerard K. O\'Neill</a>, an experimental physicist at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, was looking for a topic to tempt his physics students, most of them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshmen" title="Freshmen">freshmen</a> in engineering. He hit upon the  idea of assigning them feasibility calculations for large space-habitats. To his surprise, the habitats seemed feasible even in very large sizes: cylinders 8&#160;km (5&#160;mi) in diameter and 32&#160;km (20&#160;mi) long, even if made from ordinary materials such as steel and glass. Also, the students solved problems such as radiation protection from cosmic rays (almost free in the larger sizes), getting naturalistic Sun angles, provision of power, realistic pest-free farming and orbital attitude control without reaction motors. O\'Neill published an article about these colony concepts  in <i><a href="/wiki/Physics_Today" title="Physics Today">Physics Today</a></i> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cos_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-cos-5">[5]</a></sup> (See the above illustration of such a colony, a classic "O\'Neill Colony").  He expanded the article in his 1976 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_High_Frontier:_Human_Colonies_in_Space" title="The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space">The High Frontier: Human Colonies in Space</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="NASA_Ames.2FStanford_1975_Summer_Study"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_Ames/Stanford_1975_Summer_Study">NASA Ames/Stanford 1975 Summer Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: NASA Ames/Stanford 1975 Summer Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The result motivated <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> to sponsor a couple of summer workshops led by O\'Neill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Several concepts were studied, with sizes ranging from 1,000 to 10,000,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pournelle,_Jerrold_E._1977_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pournelle,_Jerrold_E._1977-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (November 2012)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> including versions of the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_torus" title="Stanford torus">Stanford torus</a>. Three concepts were presented to NASA: the Bernal Sphere, the Toroidal Colony and the Cylindrical Colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>O\'Neill\'s concepts had an example of a payback scheme: construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_satellite" title="Solar power satellite">solar power satellites</a> from lunar materials. O\'Neill did not emphasize the building of solar power satellites as such, but rather offered proof that orbital manufacturing from lunar materials could generate profits. He and other participants presumed that once such manufacturing facilities had started production, many profitable uses for them would be found, and the colony would become self-supporting and begin to build other colonies as well.\n</p><p>The concept studies generated a notable groundswell of public interest. One effect of this expansion was the founding of the <a href="/wiki/L5_Society" title="L5 Society">L5&#160;Society</a> in the&#160;U.S., a group of enthusiasts that desired to build and live in such colonies. The group was named after the space-colony orbit which was then believed to be the most profitable, a kidney-shaped orbit around either of Earth\'s lunar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_point" title="Lagrange point">Lagrange points</a> 5 or&#160;4.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_Studies_Institute">Space Studies Institute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Space Studies Institute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1977 O\'Neill founded the <a href="/wiki/Space_Studies_Institute" title="Space Studies Institute">Space Studies Institute</a>, which initially funded and constructed some prototypes of the new hardware needed for a <a href="/wiki/Space_colonization" title="Space colonization">space colonization</a> effort, as well as producing a number of feasibility studies. One of the early projects, for instance, involved a series of functional prototypes of a <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">mass driver</a>, the essential technology for moving <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> efficiently from the Moon to space colony orbits.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_concepts">NASA concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: NASA concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some NASA concept studies included:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bernal_sphere#Island_One" title="Bernal sphere">Island One</a>, a Bernal sphere habitat for about 10,000 - 20,000 people.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stanford_torus" title="Stanford torus">Stanford torus</a>: an alternative to Island One.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_cylinder" title="O\'Neill cylinder">O\'Neill cylinder</a>: "Island Three", an even larger design (3.2 km radius and 32 km long).</li>\n<li>Lewis One:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A cylinder of radius 250&#160;m with a non rotating radiation shielding. The shielding protects the micro-gravity industrial space, too. The rotating part is 450m long and has several inner cylinders. Some of them are used for agriculture.</li>\n<li>Kalpana One, revised:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kalpana_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kalpana-7">[7]</a></sup> A short cylinder with 250 m radius and 325 m length. The radiation shielding is 10 t/m<sup>2</sup> and rotates. It has several inner cylinders for agriculture and recreaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It is sized for 3,000 residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>\n<li>A bola: a spacecraft or habitat connected by a cable to a <a href="/wiki/Counterweight" title="Counterweight">counterweight</a> or other habitat. This design has been proposed as a Mars ship, initial construction shack for a space habitat, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transhab" title="Transhab">orbital hotel</a>. It has a comfortably long and slow rotational radius for a relatively small station mass. Also, if some of the equipment can form the counter-weight, the equipment dedicated to artificial gravity is just a cable, and thus has a much smaller mass-fraction than in other concepts. For a long-term habitation, however, radiation shielding must rotate with the habitat, and is extremely heavy, thus requiring a much stronger and heavier cable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minimized_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-minimized-23">[23]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Beaded habitats:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minimized_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-minimized-23">[23]</a></sup> This speculative design was also considered by the NASA studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Small habitats would be mass-produced to standards that allow the habitats to interconnect. A single habitat can operate alone as a bola. However, further habitats can be attached, to grow into a "dumbbell" then a "bow-tie", then a ring, then a cylinder of "beads", and finally a framed array of cylinders. Each stage of growth shares more radiation shielding and capital equipment, increasing redundancy and safety while reducing the cost per person. This concept was originally proposed by a professional architect because it can grow much like Earth-bound cities, with incremental individual investments, unlike those that require large start-up investments. The main disadvantage is that the smaller versions use a large structure to support the radiation shielding, which rotates with them. In large sizes, the shielding becomes economical, because it grows roughly as the square of the colony radius. The number of people, their habitats, and the radiators to cool them grow roughly as the cube of the colony radius.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_concepts">Other concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Other concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Bubbleworld: The Bubbleworld or Inside/Outside concept was originated by <a href="/wiki/Dandridge_MacFarlan_Cole" title="Dandridge MacFarlan Cole">Dandridge M. Cole</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-25">[25]</a></sup> The concept calls for drilling a tunnel through the longest axis of a large asteroid of iron or nickel-iron composition and filling it with a <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> substance, possibly water. A very large solar reflector would be constructed nearby, focusing solar heat onto the asteroid, first to weld and seal the tunnel ends, then more diffusely to slowly heat the entire outer surface. As the metal softens, the water inside expands and inflates the mass, while rotational forces help shape it into a cylindrical form. Once expanded and allowed to cool, it can be spun to produce artificial gravity by centrifugation, and the interior filled with soil, air and water. By creating a slight bulge in the middle of the cylinder, a ring-shaped lake can be made to form. Reflectors would allow sunlight to enter and to be directed where needed. This method would require a significant human and industrial presence in space to be at all feasible. The concept was popularized by <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> author <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">Larry Niven</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Known_Space" title="Known Space">Known Space</a> stories, describing such worlds as the primary habitats of the <a href="/wiki/Belter_(Niven)" title="Belter (Niven)">Belters</a>, a civilization who had <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_asteroids" title="Colonization of the asteroids">colonized the asteroid belt</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Terrarium_(space_habitat)" title="Terrarium (space habitat)">Asteroid terrarium</a>: a similar idea to the bubble world, the asteroid terrarium, appears in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/2312_(novel)" title="2312 (novel)">2312</a></i>, authored by <a href="/wiki/Hard_science_fiction" title="Hard science fiction">hard science fiction</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Kim_Stanley_Robinson" title="Kim Stanley Robinson">Kim Stanley Robinson</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bishop_Ring_(habitat)" title="Bishop Ring (habitat)">Bishop Ring</a>: a speculative design using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotubes</a>, a Bishop Ring is a torus 1000&#160;km in radius, 500&#160;km in width, and with atmosphere retention walls 200&#160;km in height. The habitat would be large enough that it could be "roofless", open to outer space on the inner rim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/McKendree_cylinder" title="McKendree cylinder">McKendree cylinder</a>: another concept that would use carbon nanotubes, a McKendree cylinder is paired cylinders in the same vein as the Island Three concept, but each 460&#160;km in radius and 4600&#160;km long (versus 3.2&#160;km radius and 32&#160;km long in the Island Three).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Current projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following projects and proposals, while not truly space habitats, incorporate aspects of what they would have and may represent stepping stones towards eventually building of space habitats.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nautilus-X" title="Nautilus-X">Nautilus-X</a> Multi-Mission Space Exploration Vehicle (MMSEV): this 2011 NASA proposal for a long-duration crewed space transport vehicle included an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_gravity" title="Artificial gravity">artificial gravity</a> compartment intended to promote crew-health for a crew of up to six persons on missions of up to two years duration.  The <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">partial-g</a> <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus-ring</a> <a href="/wiki/Centrifuge" title="Centrifuge">centrifuge</a> would utilize both standard metal-frame and <a href="/wiki/Inflatable_space_habitat" title="Inflatable space habitat">inflatable</a> spacecraft structures and would provide 0.11 to <span class="nowrap">0.69<a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">g</a></span> if built with the 40 feet (12&#160;m) diameter option.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a> <a href="/wiki/Nautilus-X#ISS_centrifuge_demonstration" title="Nautilus-X">Centrifuge Demo</a>, also proposed in 2011 as a demonstration project preparatory to the final design of the larger torus centrifuge space habitat for the Multi-Mission Space Exploration Vehicle.  The structure would have an outside diameter of 30 feet (9.1&#160;m) with a 30 inches (760&#160;mm) ring interior cross-section diameter and would provide 0.08 to <span class="nowrap">0.51g</span> partial gravity.  This test and evaluation centrifuge would have the capability to become a Sleep Module for ISS crew.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bigelow_Commercial_Space_Station" title="Bigelow Commercial Space Station">Bigelow Commercial Space Station</a> was announced in mid-2010. The initial build-out of the station is expected in 2014/2015. Bigelow has publicly shown space station design configurations with up to nine modules containing 100,000&#160;cu&#160;ft (2,800&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of habitable space. Bigelow began to publicly refer to the initial configuration as "Space Complex Alpha" in October 2010.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The idea of space habitats either in fact or fiction goes back to the second half of the 19th century. "<a href="/wiki/The_Brick_Moon" title="The Brick Moon">The Brick Moon</a>", a fictional story written in 1869 by Edward Everett Hale, is perhaps the first treatment of this idea in writing. In 1903, space pioneer <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a> speculated about rotating cylindrical space colonies, with plants fed by the sun, in <i>Beyond Planet Earth</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In the 1920s <a href="/wiki/John_Desmond_Bernal" title="John Desmond Bernal">John Desmond Bernal</a> and others speculated about giant space habitats. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandridge_M._Cole" title="Dandridge M. Cole">Dandridge M. Cole</a> in the late 1950s and 1960s speculated about hollowing out asteroids and then rotating them to use as settlements in various magazine articles and books, notably <i>Islands In Space: The Challenge Of The Planetoids</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fiction">In fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_habitat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: In fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The space habitats have inspired a large number of fictional societies in <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>. Some of the most popular and recognizable are in the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Gundam" title="Gundam">Gundam</a> universe, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Nine_(space_station)" title="Deep href="/wiki/Babylon_5_(space_station)" title="Babylon 5 (space station)">the space station</a> <a href="/wiki/Babylon_5" title="Babylon 5">Babylon 5</a>.\n</p><p>The 2013 science-fiction movie <i><a href="/wiki/Elysium_(film)" title="Elysium (film)">Elysium</a></i> takes place on both a ravaged <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and a luxurious <a href="/wiki/Rotating_wheel_space_station" title="Rotating wheel space station">rotating wheel space station</a> called Elysium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a
