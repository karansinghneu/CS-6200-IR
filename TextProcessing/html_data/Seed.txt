The formation of the seed is part of the process of <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a> in seed plants, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spermatophytes" title="Spermatophytes">spermatophytes</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperm</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperm</a> plants.\n</p><p>Seeds are the product of the ripened <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovule</a>, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilization" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> and some growth within the mother plant.  The <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a> is developed from the <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a> and the seed coat from the integuments of the ovule.\n</p><p>Seeds have been an important development in the reproduction and success of gymnosperm and angiosperm plants, relative to more primitive plants such as <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">mosses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marchantiophyta" title="Marchantiophyta">liverworts</a>, which do not have seeds and use water-dependent means to propagate themselves.  Seed plants now dominate biological <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">niches</a> on land, from <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> to <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> both in hot and cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climates" title="Climates">climates</a>.\n</p><p>The term "seed" also has a general meaning that antedates the above&#160;&#8211; anything that can be <a href="/wiki/Sowing" title="Sowing">sown</a>, e.g. "seed" <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, "seeds" of <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed" title="Sunflower seed">sunflower "seeds"</a>. In the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">sunflower</a> and corn "seeds", what is sown is the seed enclosed in a shell or <a href="/wiki/Husk" title="Husk">husk</a>, whereas the potato is a <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tuber</a>.\n</p><p>Many structures commonly referred to as "seeds" are actually dry <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>. Plants producing berries are called baccate. <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed" title="Sunflower seed">Sunflower seeds</a> are sometimes sold commercially while still enclosed within the hard wall of the fruit, which must be split open to reach the seed. Different groups of plants have other modifications, the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stone_fruits" title="Stone fruits">stone fruits</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>) have a hardened fruit layer (the endocarp) fused to and surrounding the actual seed. <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">Nuts</a> are the one-seeded, hard-shelled fruit of some plants with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indehiscent" title="Indehiscent">indehiscent</a> seed, such as an <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnut</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_production">Seed production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Seed production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Seeds are produced in several related groups of plants, and their manner of production distinguishes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperms</a> ("enclosed seeds") from the <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a> ("naked seeds").  Angiosperm seeds are produced in a hard or fleshy structure called a <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> that encloses the seeds for protection in order to secure healthy growth. Some fruits have layers of both hard and fleshy material.  In gymnosperms, no special structure develops to enclose the seeds, which begin their development "naked" on the bracts of cones. However, the seeds do become covered by the <a href="/wiki/Conifer_cone" title="Conifer cone">cone</a> scales as they develop in some species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifer</a>.\n</p><p>Seed production in natural plant populations varies widely from year to year in response to weather variables, insects and diseases, and internal cycles within the plants themselves.  Over a 20-year period, for example, forests composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loblolly_pine" title="Loblolly pine">loblolly pine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortleaf_pine" title="Shortleaf pine">shortleaf pine</a> produced from 0 to nearly 5 million sound pine seeds per hectare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Over this period, there were six bumper, five poor, and nine good seed crops, when evaluated for production of adequate <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedlings</a> for natural forest reproduction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seed_Development_Cycle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Seed_Development_Cycle.svg/220px-Seed_Development_Cycle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Seed_Development_Cycle.svg/330px-Seed_Development_Cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Seed_Development_Cycle.svg/440px-Seed_Development_Cycle.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seed_Development_Cycle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Stages of seed development</b>:  <div> <table class="multicol" role="presentation" style="border-collapse: collapse; padding: 0; border: 0; background:transparent; width:100%;"><tbody><tr> <td style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;"> <b>I</b> Zygote<br/> <b>II</b> Proembryo<br/> <b>III</b> Globular    </td> <td style="text-align: left; vertical-align: top;"> <b>IV</b>  Heart<br/> <b>V</b>  Torpedo<br/> <b>VI</b>  Mature Embryo   </td></tr></tbody></table></div> <b>Key</b>: <i>1. Endosperm 2. Zygote 3. Embryo 4. Suspensor 5. Cotyledons 6. Shoot Apical Meristem 7. Root Apical Meristem 8. Radicle 9. Hypocotyl 10. Epicotyl 11. Seed Coat</i></div></div></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">Angiosperm</a> (flowering plants) seeds consist of three genetically distinct constituents: (1) the embryo formed from the zygote, (2) the endosperm, which is normally triploid, (3) the seed coat from tissue derived from the maternal tissue of the ovule. In angiosperms, the process of seed development begins with <a href="/wiki/Double_fertilization" title="Double fertilization">double fertilization</a>, which involves the fusion of two male gametes with the egg cell and the central cell to form the primary <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a> and the zygote.  Right after fertilization, the zygote is mostly inactive, but the primary endosperm divides rapidly to form the endosperm tissue.  This tissue becomes the food the young plant will consume until the roots have developed after <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ovule">Ovule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ovule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">Ovule</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="555" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg/300px-Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg/450px-Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg/600px-Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ovule-Gymno-Angio-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plant ovules: Gymnosperm ovule on left, angiosperm ovule (inside ovary) on right</div></div></div>\n<p>After fertilization the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovules" title="Ovules">ovules</a> develop into the seeds. The ovule consists of a number of components:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <b>funicle</b> (<i>funiculus, funiculi</i>) or seed stalk which attaches the ovule to the <a href="/wiki/Placentation" title="Placentation">placenta</a> and hence ovary or fruit wall, at the <b>pericarp</b>.</li>\n<li>The <b>nucellus</b>, the remnant of the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megasporangium" title="Megasporangium">megasporangium</a> and main region of the ovule where the megagametophyte develops.</li>\n<li>The <b>micropyle</b>, a small pore or opening in the apex of the integument of the ovule where the pollen tube usually enters during the process of fertilization.</li>\n<li>The <b>chalaza</b>, the base of the ovule opposite the micropyle, where integument and nucellus are joined together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The shape of the ovules as they develop often affects the final shape of the seeds.  Plants generally produce ovules of four shapes: the most common shape is called <b>anatropous</b>, with a curved shape. <b>Orthotropous</b> ovules are straight with all the parts of the ovule lined up in a long row producing an uncurved seed. <b>Campylotropous</b> ovules have a curved megagametophyte often giving the seed a tight "C" shape. The last ovule shape is called <b>amphitropous</b>, where the ovule is partly inverted and turned back 90 degrees on its stalk (the funicle or <b>funiculus</b>).\n</p><p>In the majority of flowering plants, the zygote\'s first division is transversely oriented in regards to the long axis, and this establishes the polarity of the embryo. The upper or chalazal pole becomes the main area of growth of the embryo, while the lower or micropylar pole produces the stalk-like suspensor that attaches to the micropyle. The suspensor absorbs and manufactures nutrients from the endosperm that are used during the embryo\'s growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embryo">Embryo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Embryo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1138" data-file-width="1458" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg/220px-Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg/330px-Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg/440px-Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ginkgo_embryo_and_gametophyte.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The inside of a <i><a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">Ginkgo</a></i> seed, showing a well-developed embryo, nutritive tissue (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megagametophyte" title="Megagametophyte">megagametophyte</a>), and a bit of the surrounding seed coat</div></div></div>\n<p>The main components of the embryo are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <b>cotyledons</b>, the seed leaves, attached to the embryonic axis. There may be one (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monocotyledons" title="Monocotyledons">Monocotyledons</a>), or two (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dicotyledons" title="Dicotyledons">Dicotyledons</a>). The cotyledons are also the source of nutrients in the non-endospermic dicotyledons, in which case they replace the endosperm, and are thick and leathery. In endospermic seeds the cotyledons are thin and papery. Dicotyledons have the point of attachment opposite one another on the axis.</li>\n<li>The <b>epicotyl</b>, the  embryonic axis above the point of attachment of the cotyledon(s).</li>\n<li>The <b>plumule</b>, the tip of the epicotyl, and has a feathery appearance due to the presence of young leaf primordia at the apex, and will become the shoot upon germination.</li>\n<li>The <b>hypocotyl</b>, the embryonic axis below the point of attachment of the cotyledon(s), connecting the epicotyl and the radicle, being the stem-root transition zone.</li>\n<li>The <b>radicle</b>, the basal tip of the hypocotyl, grows into the primary root.</li></ul>\n<p>Monocotyledonous plants have two additional structures in the form of sheaths. The plumule is covered with a <b>coleoptile</b> that forms the first leaf while the radicle is covered with a <b>coleorhiza</b> that connects to the primary root and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventitious" title="Adventitious">adventitious</a> roots form from the sides. Here the hypocotyl is a rudimentary axis between radicle and plumule. The seeds of corn are constructed with these structures; pericarp, scutellum (single large cotyledon) that absorbs nutrients from the endosperm, plumule, radicle, coleoptile and coleorhiza&#160;&#8211; these last two structures are sheath-like and enclose the plumule and radicle, acting as a protective covering.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_coat"><span id="Seed_coat"></span>Seed coat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Seed coat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The maturing <a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">ovule</a> undergoes marked changes in the integuments, generally a reduction and disorganisation but occasionally a thickening. The seed coat forms from the two integuments or outer layers of cells of the ovule, which derive from tissue from the mother plant, the inner integument forms the <b>tegmen</b> and the outer forms the <b>testa</b>.  (The seed coats of some mononocotyledon plants, such as the grasses, are not distinct structures, but are fused with the fruit wall to form a <a href="/wiki/Fruit_anatomy#Anatomy_of_simple_fruits" title="Fruit anatomy">pericarp</a>.) The testae of both monocots and dicots are often marked with patterns and textured markings, or have wings or tufts of hair. When the seed coat forms from only one layer, it is also called the testa, though not all such testae are <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> from one species to the next. The funiculus abscises (detaches at fixed point&#160;&#8211; abscission zone), the scar forming an oval depression, the <b><a href="/wiki/Hilum_(biology)" title="Hilum (biology)">hilum</a></b>. Anatropous ovules have a portion of the funiculus that is adnate (fused to the seed coat), and which forms a longitudinal ridge, or <b>raphe</b>, just above the hilum. In bitegmic ovules (e.g. <i>Gossypium</i> described here) both inner and outer integuments contribute to the seed coat formation. With continuing maturation the cells enlarge in the outer integument. While the inner epidermis may remain a single layer, it may also divide to produce two to three layers and accumulates starch, and is referred to as the colourless layer. By contrast the outer epidermis becomes <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tanniferous</a>. The inner integument may consist of eight to fifteen layers. (Kozlowski 1972)\n</p><p>As the cells enlarge, and starch is deposited in the outer layers of the pigmented zone below the outer epidermis, this zone begins to lignify, while the cells of the outer epidermis enlarge radially and their walls thicken, with nucleus and cytoplasm compressed into the outer layer. these cells which are broader on their inner surface are called <b>palisade</b> cells. In the inner epidermis the cells also enlarge radially with plate like thickening of the walls. The mature inner integument has a palisade layer, a pigmented zone with 15&#8211;20 layers, while the innermost layer is known as the fringe layer. (Kozlowski 1972)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gymnosperms">Gymnosperms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gymnosperms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="box-Disputed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="System-search.svg" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="45" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/45px-System-search.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/68px-System-search.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/90px-System-search.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section\'s <b>factual accuracy is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Seed##Disputed" title="Talk:Seed">talk page</a>. This is the basis for their nomenclature&#160;&#8211; naked seeded plants. Two sperm cells transferred from the pollen do not develop the seed by double fertilization, but one sperm nucleus unites with the egg nucleus and the other sperm is not used.\n</p><p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sometimes each sperm fertilizes an egg cell and one zygote is then aborted or absorbed during early development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bot2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bot2000-5">[5]</a></sup> The seed is composed of the embryo (the result of fertilization) and tissue from the mother plant, which also form a cone around the seed in coniferous plants such as <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape_and_appearance">Shape and appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shape and appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A large number of terms are used to describe seed shapes, many of which are largely self-explanatory such as <i>Bean-shaped</i> (reniform) &#8211; resembling a kidney, with lobed ends on either side of the hilum, <i>Square</i> or <i>Oblong</i> &#8211; angular with all sides more or less equal or longer than wide, <i>Triangular</i> &#8211; three sided, broadest below middle, <i>Elliptic</i> or <i>Ovate</i> or <i>Obovate</i> &#8211; rounded at both ends, or egg shaped (ovate or obovate, broader at one end), being rounded but either symmetrical about the middle or broader below the middle or broader above the middle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other less obvious terms include <b>discoid</b> (resembling a disc or plate, having both thickness and parallel faces and with a rounded margin), <b>ellipsoid</b>, <b>globose</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical" title="Spherical">spherical</a>), or <b>subglobose</b> (Inflated, but less than spherical), <b>lenticular</b>, <b>oblong</b>, <b>ovoid</b>, <b>reniform</b> and <b>sectoroid</b>. <b>Striate</b> seeds are striped with parallel, longitudinal lines or ridges. The commonest colours are brown and black, other colours are infrequent. The surface varies from highly polished to considerably roughened. The surface may have a variety of appendages (see Seed coat). A seed coat with the consistency of <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a> is referred to as <b>suberose</b>. Other terms include <b>crustaceous</b> (hard, thin or brittle).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span dicotyledons.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_types">Seed types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Seed types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Seeds have been considered to occur in many structurally different types (Martin 1946).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> These are based on a number of criteria, of which the dominant one is the embryo-to-seed size ratio. This reflects the degree to which the developing cotyledons absorb the nutrients of the endosperm, and thus obliterate it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Six types occur amongst the monocotyledons, ten in the dicotyledons, and two in the gymnosperms (linear and spatulate).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This classification is based on three characteristics: embryo morphology, amount of endosperm and the position of the embryo relative to the endosperm.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monocot_dicot_seed.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="294" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Monocot_dicot_seed.svg/220px-Monocot_dicot_seed.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Monocot_dicot_seed.svg/330px-Monocot_dicot_seed.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Monocot_dicot_seed.svg/440px-Monocot_dicot_seed.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monocot_dicot_seed.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of a generalized dicot seed (1) versus a generalized monocot seed (2). A. Seed Coat B. Cotyledon C. Hilum D. Plumule E. Radicle F. Endosperm</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embryo_2">Embryo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Embryo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In endospermic seeds, there are two distinct regions inside the seed coat, an upper and larger endosperm and a lower smaller embryo. The <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a> is the fertilised ovule, an immature <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> from which a new plant will grow under proper conditions. The embryo has one <a href="/wiki/Cotyledon" title="Cotyledon">cotyledon</a> or seed leaf in <a href="/wiki/Monocotyledon" title="Monocotyledon">monocotyledons</a>, two cotyledons in almost all <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledons</a> and two or more in gymnosperms. In the fruit of <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">grains</a> (caryopses) the single monocotyledon is shield shaped and hence called a <b><a href="/wiki/Scutellum_(botany)" title="Scutellum (botany)">scutellum</a></b>. The scutellum is pressed closely against the endosperm from which it absorbs food, and passes it to the growing parts. Embryo descriptors include small, straight, bent, curved and curled.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_storage">Nutrient storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nutrient storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Within the seed, there usually is a store of <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedling</a> that will grow from the embryo.  The form of the stored nutrition varies depending on the kind of plant. In angiosperms, the stored food begins as a tissue called the <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a>, which is derived from the mother plant and the pollen via <a href="/wiki/Double_fertilization" title="Double fertilization">double fertilization</a>. It is usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triploid" title="Triploid">triploid</a>, and is rich in <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oil" title="Vegetable oil">oil</a> or <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>. In gymnosperms, such as <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a>, the food storage tissue (also called endosperm) is part of the female gametophyte, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> tissue. The endosperm is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Aleurone" title="Aleurone">aleurone</a> layer (peripheral endosperm), filled with proteinaceous aleurone grains.\n</p><p>Originally, by analogy with the animal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ovum" title="Ovum">ovum</a>, the outer nucellus layer (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perisperm" title="Perisperm">perisperm</a>) was referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albumen" title="Albumen">albumen</a>, and the inner endosperm layer as vitellus. Although misleading, the term began to be applied to all the nutrient matter. This terminology persists in referring to endospermic seeds as "albuminous". The nature of this material is used in both describing and classifying seeds, in addition to the embryo to endosperm size ratio. The endosperm may be considered to be farinaceous (or mealy) in which the cells are filled with <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, as for instance <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cereal_grains" title="Cereal grains">cereal grains</a>, or not (non-farinaceous). The endosperm may also be referred to as "fleshy" or "cartilaginous" with thicker soft cells such as <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a>, but may also be oily as in <i><a href="/wiki/Ricinus" title="Ricinus">Ricinus</a></i> (castor oil), <i><a href="/wiki/Croton_(plant)" title="Croton (plant)">Croton</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Poppy" title="Poppy">Poppy</a>. The endosperm is called "horny" when the cell walls are thicker such as <a href="/wiki/Date_palm" title="Date palm">date</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffee_bean" title="Coffee bean">coffee</a>, or "ruminated"  if mottled, as in <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palm_(plant)" title="Palm (plant)">palms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annonaceae" title="Annonaceae">Annonaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In most monocotyledons (such as <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">grasses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palms</a>) and some (<b>endospermic</b> or <b>albuminous</b>) dicotyledons (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castor_bean" title="Castor bean">castor beans</a>) the embryo is embedded in the endosperm (and nucellus), which the seedling will use upon <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>. In the <b>non-endospermic</b> dicotyledons the endosperm is absorbed by the embryo as the latter grows within the developing seed, and the cotyledons of the embryo become filled with stored food. At maturity, seeds of these species have no endosperm and are also referred to as <b>exalbuminous</b> seeds. The exalbuminous seeds include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legumes" title="Legumes">legumes</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pea" title="Pea">peas</a>), trees such as the <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>, vegetables such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squash_(vegetable)" title="Squash (vegetable)">squash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radish</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">sunflowers</a>. According to Bewley and Black (1978), Brazil nut storage is in hypocotyl, this place of storage is uncommon among seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> All gymnosperm seeds are albuminous.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_coat_2">Seed coat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Seed coat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The seed coat develops from the maternal tissue, the <a href="/wiki/Integument" title="Integument">integuments</a>, originally surrounding the ovule. The seed coat in the mature seed can be a paper-thin layer (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a>) or something more substantial (e.g. thick and hard in <a href="/wiki/Honey_locust" title="Honey locust">honey locust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a>), or fleshy as in the <a href="/wiki/Sarcotesta" title="Sarcotesta">sarcotesta</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a>. The seed coat helps protect the embryo from mechanical injury, predators and drying out. Depending on its development, the seed coat is either <b>bitegmic</b> or <b>unitegmic</b>. Bitegmic seeds form a testa from the outer integument and a tegmen from the inner integument while unitegmic seeds have only one integument. Usually parts of the testa or tegmen form a hard protective mechanical layer. The mechanical layer may prevent water penetration and germination. Amongst the barriers may be the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lignified" title="Lignified">lignified</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sclereids" title="Sclereids">sclereids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The outer integument has a number of layers, generally between four and eight organised into three layers: (a) outer epidermis, (b) outer pigmented zone of two to five layers containing <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a> and starch, and (c) inner epidermis.  The <b>endotegmen</b> is derived from the inner epidermis of the inner integument, the <b>exotegmen</b> from the outer surface of the inner integument. The <b>endotesta</b> is derived from the inner epidermis of the outer integument, and the outer layer of the testa from the outer surface of the outer integument is referred to as the <b>exotesta</b>. If the exotesta is also the mechanical layer, this is called an exotestal seed, but if the mechanical layer is the endotegmen, then the seed is endotestal. The exotesta may consist of one or more rows of cells that are elongated and pallisade like (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a>), hence \'palisade exotesta\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the three basic seed parts, some seeds have an appendage, an <b><a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">aril</a></b>, a fleshy outgrowth of the funicle (<a href="/wiki/Ovule" title="Ovule">funiculus</a>), (as in <a href="/wiki/Taxus" title="Taxus">yew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a>) or an oily appendage, an <b><a href="/wiki/Elaiosome" title="Elaiosome">elaiosome</a></b> (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Corydalis" title="Corydalis">Corydalis</a></i>), or hairs (trichomes). In the latter example these hairs are the source of the textile crop <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>. Other seed appendages include the raphe (a ridge), wings, <b>caruncles</b> (a soft spongy outgrowth from the outer integument in the vicinity of the micropyle), spines, or tubercles.\n</p><p>A scar also may remain on the seed coat, called the <b><a href="/wiki/Hilum_(biology)" title="Hilum (biology)">hilum</a></b>, where the seed was attached to the ovary wall by the funicle. Just below it is a small pore, representing the micropyle of the ovule.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_seed_set">Size and seed set</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Size and seed set">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seed_variety.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2460" data-file-width="3280" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Seed_variety.jpg/220px-Seed_variety.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Seed_variety.jpg/330px-Seed_variety.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Seed_variety.jpg/440px-Seed_variety.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seed_variety.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A collection of various vegetable and herb seeds</div></div></div>\n<p>Seeds are very diverse in size. The dust-like orchid seeds are the smallest, with about one million seeds per gram; they are often embryonic seeds with immature embryos and no significant energy reserves. Orchids and a few other groups of plants are <a href="/wiki/Myco-heterotrophy" title="Myco-heterotrophy">mycoheterotrophs</a> which depend on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycorrhizal_fungi" title="Mycorrhizal fungi">mycorrhizal fungi</a> for nutrition during germination and the early growth of the seedling.  Some terrestrial orchid seedlings, in fact, spend the first few years of their lives deriving energy from the fungi and do not produce green leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> At over 20&#160;kg, the largest seed is the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coco_de_mer" title="Coco de mer">coco de mer</a></i>.  Plants that produce smaller seeds can generate many more seeds per flower, while plants with larger seeds invest more resources into those seeds and normally produce fewer seeds. Small seeds are quicker to ripen and can be dispersed sooner, so fall blooming plants often have small seeds. Many annual plants produce great quantities of smaller seeds; this helps to ensure at least a few will end in a favorable place for growth.  Herbaceous perennials and woody plants often have larger seeds; they can produce seeds over many years, and larger seeds have more energy reserves for germination and seedling growth and produce larger, more established seedlings after germination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Seeds serve several functions for the plants that produce them.  Key among these functions are nourishment of the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal</a> to a new location, and <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a> during unfavorable conditions.  Seeds fundamentally are means of reproduction, and most seeds are the product of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a> which produces a remixing of genetic material and <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> variability on which <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> acts.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embryo_nourishment">Embryo nourishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Embryo nourishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Seeds protect and nourish the embryo or young plant. They usually give a seedling a faster start than a sporeling from a spore, because of the larger food reserves in the seed and the multicellularity of the enclosed embryo.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispersal">Dispersal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Dispersal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal" title="Seed dispersal">Seed dispersal</a></div>\n<p>Unlike animals, plants are limited in their ability to seek out favorable conditions for life and growth. As a result, plants have evolved many ways to <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">disperse</a> their offspring by dispersing their seeds (see also <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetative reproduction</a>). A seed must somehow "arrive" at a location and be there at a time favorable for germination and growth. When the fruits open and release their seeds in a regular way, it is called <a href="/wiki/Dehiscence_(botany)" title="Dehiscence (botany)">dehiscent</a>, which is often distinctive for related groups of plants; these fruits include capsules, follicles, legumes, silicles and siliques.  When fruits do not open and release their seeds in a regular fashion, they are called indehiscent, which include the fruits achenes, caryopsis, nuts, samaras, and utricles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="By_wind_.28anemochory.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="By_wind_(anemochory)">By wind (anemochory)</span><span wings.</li></ul>\n<h4><span id="By_water_.28hydrochory.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="By_water_(hydrochory)">By water (hydrochory)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: By water (hydrochory)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>Some plants, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Mucuna" title="Mucuna">Mucuna</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Dioclea_(plant)" title="Dioclea href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h4><span id="By_animals_.28zoochory.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="By_animals_(zoochory)">By animals (zoochory)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: By animals (zoochory)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>Seeds (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burr_(fruit)" title="Burr (fruit)">burrs</a>) with barbs or hooks (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acaena" title="Acaena">acaena</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burdock" title="Burdock">burdock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rumex" title="Rumex">dock</a>) which attach to animal fur or feathers, and then drop off later.</li>\n<li>Seeds with a fleshy covering (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juniper" title="Juniper">juniper</a>) are eaten by animals (<a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>) which then disperse these seeds in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faeces" title="Faeces">droppings</a>.</li>\n<li>Seeds (<a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>) are attractive long-term storable food resources for animals (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acorn" title="Acorn">acorns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazel" title="Hazel">hazelnut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnut</a>); the seeds are stored some distance from the parent plant, and some escape being eaten if the animal forgets them.</li></ul>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Myrmecochory" title="Myrmecochory">Myrmecochory</a></b> is the dispersal of seeds by <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ants</a>. Foraging ants disperse seeds which have appendages called <a href="/wiki/Elaiosome" title="Elaiosome">elaiosomes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodroot" title="Bloodroot">bloodroot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trillium" title="Trillium">trilliums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">acacias</a>, and many species of <a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a>).  Elaiosomes are soft, fleshy structures that contain nutrients for animals that eat them.  The ants carry such seeds back to their nest, where the elaiosomes are eaten.  The remainder of the seed, which is hard and inedible to the ants, then germinates either within the nest or at a removal site where the seed has been discarded by the ants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ricklefs_1993_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ricklefs_1993-21">[21]</a></sup>  This dispersal relationship is an example of <a href="/wiki/Mutualism_(biology)" title="Mutualism (biology)">mutualism</a>, since the plants depend upon the ants to disperse seeds, while the ants depend upon the plants seeds for food.  As a result, a drop in numbers of one partner can reduce success of the other.  In <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_ant" title="Argentine ant">Argentine ant</a> (<i>Linepithema humile</i>) has <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invaded</a> and displaced native species of ants.  Unlike the native ant species, Argentine ants do not collect the seeds of <i><a href="/wiki/Mimetes_cucullatus" title="Mimetes cucullatus">Mimetes cucullatus</a></i> or eat the elaiosomes.  In areas where these ants have invaded, the numbers of <i>Mimetes</i> seedlings have dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dormancy">Dormancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Dormancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Seed_dormancy" title="Seed dormancy">Seed dormancy</a></div>\n<p>Seed dormancy has two main functions: the first is synchronizing germination with the optimal conditions for survival of the resulting seedling; the second is spreading germination of a batch of seeds over time so a catastrophe (e.g. late frosts, drought, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivory" title="Herbivory">herbivory</a>) does not result in the death of all offspring of a plant (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bet-hedging_(biology)" title="Bet-hedging (biology)">bet-hedging</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Seed dormancy is defined as a seed failing to germinate under environmental conditions optimal for germination, normally when the environment is at a suitable temperature with proper soil moisture. This true dormancy or innate dormancy is therefore caused by conditions within the seed that prevent germination. Thus dormancy is a state of the seed, not of the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Induced dormancy, enforced dormancy or seed quiescence occurs when a seed fails to germinate because the external environmental conditions are inappropriate for germination, mostly in response to conditions being too dark or light, too cold or hot, or too dry.\n</p><p>Seed dormancy is not the same as seed persistence in the soil or on the plant, though even in scientific publications dormancy and persistence are often confused or used as synonyms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Often, seed dormancy is divided into four major categories: exogenous; endogenous; combinational; and secondary. A more recent system distinguishes five classes: morphological, physiological, morphophysiological, physical, and combinational dormancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Exogenous dormancy is caused by conditions outside the embryo, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Physical dormancy</b> or <b>hard seed coats</b> occurs when seeds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeability_(fluid)" title="Permeability (fluid)">impermeable</a> to water. At dormancy break, a specialized structure, the &#8216;water gap&#8217;, is disrupted in response to environmental cues, especially temperature, so water can enter the seed and germination can occur. Plant families where physical dormancy occurs include <a href="/wiki/Anacardiaceae" title="Anacardiaceae">Anacardiaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannaceae" title="Cannaceae">Cannaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convulvulaceae" title="Convulvulaceae">Convulvulaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malvaceae" title="Malvaceae">Malvaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Chemical dormancy</b> considers species that lack physiological dormancy, but where a chemical prevents germination. This chemical can be leached out of the seed by rainwater or snow melt or be deactivated somehow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Leaching of chemical inhibitors from the seed by rain water is often cited as an important cause of dormancy release in seeds of desert plants, but little evidence exists to support this claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Endogenous dormancy is caused by conditions within the embryo itself, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In <b>morphological dormancy</b>, germination is prevented due to morphological characteristics of the embryo. In some species, the embryo is just a mass of cells when seeds are dispersed; it is not differentiated. Before germination can take place, both differentiation and growth of the embryo have to occur. In other species, the embryo is differentiated but not fully grown (underdeveloped) at dispersal, and embryo growth up to a species specific length is required before germination can occur. Examples of plant families where morphological dormancy occurs are <a href="/wiki/Apiaceae" title="Apiaceae">Apiaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycadaceae" title="Cycadaceae">Cycadaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liliaceae" title="Liliaceae">Liliaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnoliaceae" title="Magnoliaceae">Magnoliaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ranunculaceae" title="Ranunculaceae">Ranunculaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskin,_C.C_1998_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baskin,_C.C_1998-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskin,_J.M_2004_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baskin,_J.M_2004-31">[31]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Morphophysiological dormancy</b> includes seeds with underdeveloped embryos, and also have physiological components to dormancy. These seeds, therefore, require a dormancy-breaking treatments, as well as a period of time to develop fully grown embryos. Plant families where morphophysiological dormancy occurs include <a href="/wiki/Apiaceae" title="Apiaceae">Apiaceae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquifoliaceae" title="Aquifoliaceae">Aquifoliaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liliaceae" title="Liliaceae">Liliaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnoliaceae" title="Magnoliaceae">Magnoliaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papaveraceae" title="Papaveraceae">Papaveraceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ranunculaceae" title="Ranunculaceae">Ranunculaceae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskin,_C.C_1998_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baskin,_C.C_1998-30">[30]</a></sup> Some plants with morphophysiological dormancy, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Asarum" title="Asarum">Asarum</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Trillium" title="Trillium">Trillium</a></i> species, have multiple types of dormancy, one affects radicle (root) growth, while the other affects plumule (shoot) growth. The terms "double dormancy" and "two-year seeds" are used for species whose seeds need two years to complete germination or at least two winters and one summer. Dormancy of the radicle (seedling root) is broken during the first winter after dispersal while dormancy of the shoot bud is broken during the second winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskin,_C.C_1998_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baskin,_C.C_1998-30">[30]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Physiological dormancy</b> means the embryo, due to physiological causes, cannot generate enough power to break through the seed coat, endosperm or other covering structures. Dormancy is typically broken at cool wet, warm wet or warm dry conditions. <a href="/wiki/Abscisic_acid" title="Abscisic acid">Abscisic acid</a> is usually the growth inhibitor in seeds, and its production can be affected by light.\n<ul><li><b>Drying</b>, in some plants, including a number of grasses and those from seasonally arid regions, is needed before they will germinate. The seeds are released, but need to have a lower moisture content before germination can begin. If the seeds remain moist after dispersal, germination can be delayed for many months or even years. Many herbaceous plants from temperate climate zones have physiological dormancy that disappears with drying of the seeds. Other species will germinate after dispersal only under very narrow temperature ranges, but as the seeds dry, they are able to germinate over a wider temperature range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ul></li>\n<li>In seeds with <b>combinational dormancy</b>, the seed or fruit coat is impermeable to water and the embryo has physiological dormancy. Depending on the species, physical dormancy can be broken before or after physiological dormancy is broken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baskin,_J.M_2004_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baskin,_J.M_2004-31">[31]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Secondary dormancy</b>* is caused by conditions after the seed has been dispersed and occurs in some seeds when nondormant seed is exposed to conditions that are not favorable to germination, very often high temperatures. The mechanisms of secondary dormancy are not yet fully understood, but might involve the loss of sensitivity in receptors in the plasma membrane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The following types of seed dormancy do not involve seed dormancy, strictly speaking, as lack of germination is prevented by the environment, not by characteristics of the seed itself (see <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">Germination</a>):\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Photodormancy</b> or light sensitivity affects germination of some seeds. These photoblastic seeds need a period of darkness or light to germinate.  In species with thin seed coats, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> may be able to penetrate into the dormant embryo. The presence of light or the absence of light may trigger the germination process, inhibiting germination in some seeds buried too deeply or in others not buried in the soil.</li>\n<li><b>Thermodormancy</b> is seed sensitivity to heat or cold. Some seeds, including cocklebur and amaranth, germinate only at high temperatures (30&#160;&#176;C or 86&#160;&#176;F); many plants that have seeds that germinate in early to midsummer have thermodormancy, so germinate only when the soil temperature is warm. Other seeds need cool soils to germinate, while others, such as celery, are inhibited when soil temperatures are too warm.  Often, thermodormancy requirements disappear as the seed ages or dries.</li></ul>\n<p>Not all seeds undergo a period of dormancy.  Seeds of some <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a> are viviparous; they begin to germinate while still attached to the parent. The large, heavy root allows the seed to penetrate into the ground when it falls. Many garden plant seeds will germinate readily as soon as they have water and are warm enough; though their wild ancestors may have had dormancy, these cultivated plants lack it.  After many generations of selective pressure by plant breeders and gardeners, dormancy has been selected out.\n</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annuals</a>, seeds are a way for the species to survive dry or cold seasons. Ephemeral plants are usually annuals that can go from seed to seed in as few as six weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persistence_and_seed_banks">Persistence and seed banks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Persistence and seed banks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Seed_dormancy" title="Seed dormancy">Seed dormancy</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germination">Germination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Germination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">Seedling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">Germination</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunflower_seedlings.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1828" decoding="async" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sunflower_seedlings.jpg/220px-Sunflower_seedlings.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sunflower_seedlings.jpg/330px-Sunflower_seedlings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sunflower_seedlings.jpg/440px-Sunflower_seedlings.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunflower_seedlings.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Germinating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">sunflower</a> seedlings</div></div></div>\n<p>Seed germination is a process by which a seed embryo develops into a seedling. It involves the reactivation of the metabolic pathways that lead to growth and the emergence of the radicle or seed root and plumule or shoot. The emergence of the seedling above the soil surface is the next phase of the plant\'s growth and is called seedling establishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the_encyclopedia_of_seeds_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-the_encyclopedia_of_seeds-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three fundamental conditions must exist before germination can occur. (1) The embryo must be alive, called seed viability. (2) Any dormancy requirements that prevent germination must be overcome. (3) The proper environmental conditions must exist for germination.\n</p><p>Seed viability is the ability of the embryo to germinate and is affected by a number of different conditions. Some plants do not produce seeds that have functional complete embryos, or the seed may have no embryo at all, often called empty seeds. Predators and pathogens can damage or kill the seed while it is still in the fruit or after it is dispersed. Environmental conditions like flooding or heat can kill the seed before or during germination. The age of the seed affects its health and germination ability: since the seed has a living embryo, over time cells die and cannot be replaced. Some seeds can live for a long time before germination, while others can only survive for a short period after dispersal before they die.\n</p><p><b>Seed vigor</b> is a measure of the quality of seed, and involves the viability of the seed, the germination percentage, germination rate and the strength of the seedlings produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <b>germination percentage</b> is simply the proportion of seeds that germinate from all seeds subject to the right conditions for growth. The <b>germination rate</b> is the length of time it takes for the seeds to germinate. Germination percentages and rates are affected by seed viability, dormancy and environmental effects that impact on the seed and seedling. In agriculture and horticulture quality seeds have high viability, measured by germination percentage plus the rate of germination. This is given as a percent of germination over a certain amount of time, 90% germination in 20 days, for example. \'Dormancy\' is covered above; many plants produce seeds with varying degrees of dormancy, and different seeds from the same fruit can have different degrees of dormancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It\'s possible to have seeds with no dormancy if they are dispersed right away and do not dry (if the seeds dry they go into physiological dormancy). There is great variation amongst plants and a dormant seed is still a viable seed even though the germination rate might be very low.\n</p><p>Environmental conditions affecting seed germination include; water, oxygen, temperature and light.\n</p><p>Three distinct phases of seed germination occur: water imbibition; lag phase; and <a href="/wiki/Radicle" title="Radicle">radicle</a> emergence.\n</p><p>In order for the seed coat to split, the embryo must imbibe (soak up water), which causes it to swell, splitting the seed coat. However, the nature of the seed coat determines how rapidly water can penetrate and subsequently initiate <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">germination</a>. The rate of imbibition is dependent on the permeability of the seed coat, amount of water in the environment and the area of contact the seed has to the source of water. For some seeds, imbibing too much water too quickly can kill the seed. For some seeds, once water is imbibed the germination process cannot be stopped, and drying then becomes fatal. Other seeds can imbibe and lose water a few times without causing ill effects, but drying can cause secondary dormancy.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repair_of_DNA_damage">Repair of DNA damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Repair of DNA damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During seed <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a>, often associated with unpredictable and stressful environments, <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair#DNA_damage" title="DNA repair">DNA damage</a> accumulates as the seeds age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheah_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheah-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koppen_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koppen-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16313635_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16313635-40">[40]</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> seeds, the reduction of DNA integrity due to damage is associated with loss of seed viability during storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheah_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cheah-38">[38]</a></sup>  Upon germination, seeds of <i><a href="/wiki/Vicia_faba" title="Vicia faba">Vicia faba</a></i> undergo <a href="/wiki/DNA_repair" title="DNA repair">DNA repair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koppen_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Koppen-39">[39]</a></sup>  A plant DNA <a href="/wiki/Ligase" title="Ligase">ligase</a> that is involved in repair of single- and double-strand breaks during seed germination is an important determinant of seed longevity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20584150_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20584150-41">[41]</a></sup>  Also, in <a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis" title="Arabidopsis">Arabidopsis</a> seeds, the activities of the DNA repair enzymes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poly_ADP_ribose_polymerase" title="Poly ADP ribose polymerase">Poly ADP ribose polymerases</a> (PARP) are likely needed for successful germination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17587307_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17587307-42">[42]</a></sup>  Thus DNA damages that accumulate during <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a> appear to be a problem for seed survival, and the enzymatic repair of DNA damages during germination appears to be important for seed viability.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inducing_germination">Inducing germination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Inducing germination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of different strategies are used by gardeners and horticulturists to break <a href="/wiki/Seed_dormancy" title="Seed dormancy">seed dormancy</a>.\n</p><p><b>Scarification</b> allows water and gases to penetrate into the seed; it includes methods to physically break the hard seed coats or soften them by chemicals, such as soaking in hot water or poking holes in the seed with a pin or rubbing them on sandpaper or cracking with a press or hammer.  Sometimes fruits are harvested while the seeds are still immature and the seed coat is not fully developed and sown right away before the seed coat become impermeable.  Under natural conditions, seed coats are worn down by rodents chewing on the seed, the seeds rubbing against rocks (seeds are moved by the wind or water currents), by undergoing freezing and thawing of surface water, or passing through an animal\'s digestive tract. In the latter case, the seed coat protects the seed from <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>, while often weakening the seed coat such that the embryo is ready to sprout when it is deposited, along with a bit of fecal matter that acts as fertilizer, far from the parent plant.  <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microorganisms</a> are often effective in breaking down hard seed coats and are sometimes used by people as a treatment; the seeds are stored in a moist warm sandy medium for several months under nonsterile conditions.\n</p><p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratification_(botany)" title="Stratification (botany)">Stratification</a></b>, also called moist-chilling, breaks down physiological dormancy, and involves the addition of moisture to the seeds so they absorb water, and they are then subjected to a period of moist chilling to after-ripen the embryo. Sowing in late summer and fall and allowing to overwinter under cool conditions is an effective way to stratify seeds; some seeds respond more favorably to periods of oscillating temperatures which are a part of the natural environment.\n</p><p><b>Leaching</b> or the soaking in water removes chemical inhibitors in some seeds that prevent germination. <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rain</a> and melting <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> naturally accomplish this task. For seeds planted in gardens, running water is best&#160;&#8211; if soaked in a container, 12 to 24 hours of soaking is sufficient. Soaking longer, especially in stagnant water, can result in oxygen starvation and seed death. Seeds with hard seed coats can be soaked in hot water to break open the impermeable cell layers that prevent water intake.\n</p><p>Other methods used to assist in the germination of seeds that have dormancy include prechilling, predrying, daily alternation of temperature, light exposure, potassium nitrate, the use of plant growth regulators, such as gibberellins, cytokinins, ethylene, thiourea, sodium hypochlorite, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Some seeds germinate best after a fire. For some seeds, fire cracks hard seed coats, while in others, chemical dormancy is broken in reaction to the presence of smoke. Liquid smoke is often used by gardeners to assist in the germination of these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sterile_seeds">Sterile seeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sterile seeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Seeds may be sterile for few reasons: they may have been irradiated, unpollinated, cells lived past expectancy, or bred for the purpose.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_and_origin_of_seeds">Evolution and origin of seeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Evolution and origin of seeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The origin of seed plants is a problem that still remains unsolved. However, more and more data tends to place this origin in the middle <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a>. The description in 2004 of the proto-seed <i>Runcaria heinzelinii</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Givetian" title="Givetian">Givetian</a> of <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> is an indication of that ancient origin of seed-plants. As with modern ferns, most land plants before this time reproduced by sending spores into the air, that would land and become whole new plants.\n</p><p>The first "true" seeds are described from the upper Devonian, which is probably the theater of their true first evolutionary radiation. With this radiation came an <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_seed_size" title="Evolution of seed size">evolution of seed size</a>, shape, dispersal and eventually the radiation of gymnosperms and angiosperms and <a href="/wiki/Monocotyledon" title="Monocotyledon">monocotyledons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledons</a>. The seed plants progressively became one of the major elements of nearly all ecosystems.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_importance">Economic importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Economic importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phaseolus_vulgaris_seed.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="168" data-file-width="252" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Phaseolus_vulgaris_seed.jpg/220px-Phaseolus_vulgaris_seed.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/Phaseolus_vulgaris_seed.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phaseolus_vulgaris_seed.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Phaseolus_vulgaris" title="Phaseolus vulgaris">Phaseolus vulgaris</a></i> (common bean or green bean) seeds are diverse in size, shape, and color.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edible_seeds">Edible seeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Edible seeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_edible_seeds" title="List of edible seeds">List of edible seeds</a></div>\n<p>Many seeds are edible and the majority of human calories comes from seeds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> especially from <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>. Seeds also provide most <a href="/wiki/Cooking_oil" title="Cooking oil">cooking oils</a>, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage" title="Beverage">beverages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> and some important <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additives</a>.  In different seeds the <a href="/wiki/Embryo#Embryos_of_plants_and_animals" title="Embryo">seed embryo</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Endosperm" title="Endosperm">endosperm</a> dominates and provides most of the <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>. The storage <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> of the embryo and endosperm differ in their <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> content and physical properties.  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a> of wheat, important in providing the <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a> property to bread <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">dough</a> is strictly an endosperm protein.\n</p><p>Seeds are used to propagate many crops such as cereals, legumes, <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forest trees</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turfgrass" title="Turfgrass">turfgrasses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> grasses. Particularly in developing countries, a major constraint faced is the inadequacy of the marketing channels to get the seed to poor farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Thus the use of farmer-retained seed remains quite common.\n</p><p>Seeds are also eaten by animals (<a href="/wiki/Seed_predation" title="Seed predation">seed predation</a>), and are also fed to <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> or provided as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birdseed" title="Birdseed">birdseed</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poison_and_food_safety">Poison and food safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Poison and food safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While some seeds are edible, others are harmful, poisonous or deadly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  Plants and seeds often contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a> to discourage <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seed_predation" title="Seed predation">seed predators</a>.  In some cases, these compounds simply taste bad (such as in <a href="/wiki/Mustard_(condiment)" title="Mustard (condiment)">mustard</a>), but other compounds are toxic or break down into toxic compounds within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_system" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a>.  Children, being smaller than adults, are more susceptible to poisoning by plants and seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A deadly poison, <a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">ricin</a>, comes from seeds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castor_bean" title="Castor bean">castor bean</a>.   Reported lethal doses are anywhere from two to eight seeds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>\nthough only a few deaths have been reported when castor beans have been ingested by animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, seeds containing <a href="/wiki/Amygdalin" title="Amygdalin">amygdalin</a>&#160;&#8211; <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apricot" title="Apricot">apricot</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_almond" title="Bitter almond">bitter almond</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-almond_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-almond-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plum" title="Plum">plum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quince" title="Quince">quince</a>, and others&#160;&#8211; when consumed in sufficient amounts, may cause <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_poisoning" title="Cyanide poisoning">cyanide poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-almond_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-almond-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>\nOther seeds that contain poisons include <a href="/wiki/Annona" title="Annona">annona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Custard_apple" title="Custard apple">custard apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Datura" title="Datura">datura</a>, uncooked <a href="/wiki/Durian" title="Durian">durian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_chain" title="Golden chain">golden chain</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse-chestnut_(tree)" title="Horse-chestnut (tree)">horse-chestnut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delphinium" title="Delphinium">larkspur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Locoweed" title="Locoweed">locoweed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lychee" title="Lychee">lychee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nectarine" title="Nectarine">nectarine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rambutan" title="Rambutan">rambutan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosary_pea" title="Rosary pea">rosary pea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sour_sop" title="Sour sop">sour sop</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_apple" title="Sugar apple">sugar apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisteria" title="Wisteria">wisteria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taxus" title="Taxus">yew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>  The seeds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strychnine_tree" title="Strychnine tree">strychnine tree</a> are also poisonous, containing the poison <a href="/wiki/Strychnine" title="Strychnine">strychnine</a>.\n</p><p>The seeds of many legumes, including the common bean (<i><a href="/wiki/Phaseolus_vulgaris" title="Phaseolus vulgaris">Phaseolus vulgaris</a></i>), contain proteins called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lectins" title="Lectins">lectins</a> which can cause gastric distress if the beans are eaten without <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>.  The common bean and many others, including the <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a>, also contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trypsin_inhibitors" title="Trypsin inhibitors">trypsin inhibitors</a> which interfere with the action of the digestive enzyme <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a>.  Normal cooking processes degrade lectins and trypsin inhibitors to harmless forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Please see the category <a href="/wiki/Category:Plant_toxins" title="Category:Plant toxins">plant toxins</a> for further relevant articles.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">Cotton</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> grows attached to <a href="/wiki/Gossypium" title="Gossypium">cotton plant</a> seeds. Other seed fibers are from <a href="/wiki/Kapok_tree" title="Kapok tree">kapok</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milkweed" title="Milkweed">milkweed</a>.\n</p><p>Many important nonfood oils are extracted from seeds. <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">Linseed oil</a> is used in paints. Oil from <a href="/wiki/Jojoba" title="Jojoba">jojoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crambe" title="Crambe">crambe</a> are similar to <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a>.\n</p><p>Seeds are the source of some medicines including <a href="/wiki/Castor_oil" title="Castor oil">castor oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea_tree_oil" title="Tea tree oil">tea tree oil</a> and the quack cancer drug <a href="/wiki/Amygdalin" title="Amygdalin">Laetrile</a>.\n</p><p>Many seeds have been used as <a href="/wiki/Bead" title="Bead">beads</a> in necklaces and rosaries including <a href="/wiki/Job%27s_tears" title="Job\'s tears">Job\'s tears</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinaberry" title="Chinaberry">Chinaberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abrus_precatorius" title="Abrus precatorius">rosary pea</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castor_bean" title="Castor bean">castor bean</a>. However, the latter three are also poisonous.\n</p><p>Other seed uses include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Seeds once used as weights for <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">balances</a>.</li>\n<li>Seeds used as toys by children, such as for the game <a href="/wiki/Conkers" title="Conkers">Conkers</a>.</li>\n<li>Resin from <i><a href="/wiki/Clusia" title="Clusia">Clusia</a> rosea</i> seeds used to caulk boats.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Nematicide" title="Nematicide">Nematicide</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milkweed" title="Milkweed">milkweed</a> seeds.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cottonseed_meal" title="Cottonseed meal">Cottonseed meal</a> used as animal feed and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_records">Seed records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
