<b>Vostok programme</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1042;&#1086;&#1089;&#1090;&#1086;&#769;&#1082;</span>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span Competing with the United States <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>, it succeeded in placing the first human into space, <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>, in a single orbit in <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a> on April 12, 1961. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok_spacecraft" title="Vostok spacecraft">Vostok capsule</a> was developed from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenit_spy_satellite" title="Zenit spy satellite">Zenit spy satellite</a> project, and <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(rocket_family)" title="Vostok (rocket family)">its launch rocket</a> was adapted from the existing <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a> <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a> (ICBM) design. The name "Vostok" was treated as <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">classified information</a> until Gagarin\'s flight was first publicly disclosed to the world press.\n</p><p>The programme carried out six manned spaceflights between 1961 and 1963. The longest flight lasted nearly five days, and the last four were launched in pairs, one day apart. This exceeded Project Mercury\'s demonstrated capabilities of a longest flight of just over 34 hours, and of single missions.\n</p><p>Vostok was succeeded by two <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_programme" title="Voskhod programme">Voskhod programme</a> flights in 1964 and 1965, which used three- and two-man modifications of the Vostok capsule and a <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_(rocket)" title="Voskhod (rocket)">larger launch rocket</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_race" title="Space race">Space race</a></div>\n<p>The world\'s first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, had been put into orbit by the Soviets in 1957. The next milestone in the history of space exploration would be to put a human in space, and both the Soviets and the Americans wanted to be the first.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" space</div>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>By January 1959, the Soviets had begun preparations for human spaceflight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p243_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-p243-1">[1]</a></sup> Physicians from the Soviet Air Force insisted that the potential cosmonaut candidates be qualified Air Force pilots, arguing that they would have relevant skills such as exposure to higher <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g-forces</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Ejection_seat" title="Ejection seat">ejection seat</a> experience; also the Americans had chosen the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Seven" title="Mercury Seven">Mercury Seven</a> in April 1959, all of whom had aviation backgrounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p243_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-p243-1">[1]</a></sup> The candidates had to be intelligent, comfortable in high-stress situations, and physically fit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p247_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-p247-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chief designer of the Soviet space program, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>, decided that the cosmonauts must be male, between 25 and 30 years old, no taller than 1.75 meters, and weigh no more than 72 kilograms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p244_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-p244-3">[3]</a></sup> The final specifications for cosmonauts were approved in June 1959. By September interviews with potential cosmonauts had begun. Although the pilots were not told they might be flying into space, one of the physicians in charge of the selection process believed that some pilots had deduced this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p244_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-p244-3">[3]</a></sup> Just over 200 candidates made it through the interview process, and by October a series of demanding physical tests were conducted on those remaining, such as exposure to low pressures, and a <a href="/wiki/High-G_training" title="High-G training">centrifuge test</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p245_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-p245-4">[4]</a></sup> By the end of 1959, 20 men had been selected. Korolev insisted on having a larger group than NASA\'s astronaut team of seven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p245_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-p245-4">[4]</a></sup> Of these 20, five were outside the desired age range; hence, the age requirement was relaxed. Unlike NASA\'s astronaut group, this group did not particularly consist of experienced pilots; Belyayev was the most experienced with 900 flying hours. The Soviet spacecraft were more automated than the American counterparts, so significant piloting experience was not necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p246_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-p246-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On January 11, 1960, Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Marshal" title="Chief Marshal">Chief Marshal of Aviation</a> <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Vershinin" title="Konstantin Vershinin">Konstantin Vershinin</a> approved plans to establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin_Cosmonauts_Training_Center" title="Yuri Gagarin Cosmonauts Training Center">Cosmonaut Training Center</a>, whose exclusive purpose would be to prepare the cosmonauts for their upcoming flights; initially the facility would have about 250 staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p245_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-p245-4">[4]</a></sup> Vershinin assigned the already famous aviator <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Kamanin" title="Nikolai Kamanin">Nikolai Kamanin</a> to supervise operations at the facility. By March, most of the cosmonauts had arrived at the training facility; Vershinin gave a welcome speech on March 7, and those who were present were formally inducted into the cosmonaut group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p247_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-p247-2">[2]</a></sup> By mid-June all twenty were permanently stationed at the center. In March the cosmonauts were started on a daily fitness regime, and were taught classes on topics such as rocket space systems, navigation, geophysics, and astronomy.\n</p><p>Owing to the initial facility\'s space limitations, the cosmonauts and staff were relocated to a new facility in <a href="/wiki/Star_City,_Russia" title="Star City, Russia">Star City</a> (then known as <i>Zelenyy</i>), which has been the home of Russia\'s cosmonaut training program for over fifty years. The move officially took place on June 29, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p248_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-p248-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Vanguard_Six">The Vanguard Six</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Vanguard Six">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Gromov_Flight_Research_Institute" title="Gromov Flight Research Institute">Gromov Flight Research Institute</a>, a spacecraft simulator had been built, called the <i>TDK-1</i>. Owing to the inefficiency of training all 20 cosmonauts in the simulator, it was decided they would select six men who would go through accelerated training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p249_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-p249-7">[7]</a></sup> This group, which would be known as <i>The Vanguard Six</i>, was decided on May 30, 1960, and initially consisted of Gagarin, Kartashov, Nikolayev, Popovich, Titov, and Varlamov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p249_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-p249-7">[7]</a></sup> Alexei Leonov recalls that these six were the shortest of the group of 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July, shortly after relocation to Star City, two of the six were replaced on medical grounds. Firstly, during a centrifuge test of 8&#160;<a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force"><i>g</i></a>, Kartashov experienced some internal damage, causing minor <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">hemorrhaging</a> on his back.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Despite Gagarin\'s requests for him to stay, the doctors decided to remove Kartashov from the group of six. Later in July, Varlamov was involved in a swimming accident. During a dive into a lake near the training center, he hit his head on the bottom, displacing a <a href="/wiki/Cervical_vertebrae" title="Cervical vertebrae">cervical vertebra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> So by the end of July, the Vanguard Six were: Gagarin, Bykovskiy, Nelyubov, Nikolayev, Popovich, and Titov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p249_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-p249-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By January 1961, these six had all finished parachute and recovery training, as well as three-day regimes in simulators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p261_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-p261-11">[11]</a></sup> On January 17, the six participated in their final exams, including time spent in a simulator, and a written test. Based on these results, a commission, supervised by Kamanin, recommended the use of the cosmonauts in the following order: Gagarin, Titov, Nelyubov, Nikolayev, Bykovskiy, Popovich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p261_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-p261-11">[11]</a></sup> At this stage Gagarin was the clear favorite to be the first man in space, not only based on the exams, but also among an informal peer evaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p262_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-p262-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">Vostok (spacecraft)</a></div>\n<p>The first manned spaceflight, <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a> in April 1961, was preceded by several preparatory flights. In mid-1960, the Soviets learned that the Americans could launch a <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital</a> manned spaceflight as early as January 1961. Korolev saw this as an important deadline, and was determined to launch a manned orbital mission before the Americans launched their manned suborbital mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By April 1960, designers at <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>\'s design bureau, then known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia"><i>OKB-1</i></a>, had completed a draft plan for the first Vostok spacecraft, called <i>Vostok 1K</i>. This design would be used for testing purposes; also in their plan was <i>Vostok 2K</i>, a spy satellite that would later become known as <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(satellite)" title="Zenit (satellite)">Zenit 2</a>, and <i>Vostok 3K</i>, which would be used for all six manned Vostok missions.\n</p><p>Despite the very large geographical size of the Soviet Union, there were obvious limitations to monitoring orbital spaceflights from ground stations within the country. To remedy this, the Soviets stationed about seven naval vessels, or tracking ships, around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p263_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-p263-14">[14]</a></sup> For each ground station or tracking ship, the duration of communications with an orbiting spacecraft was limited to between five and ten minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p263_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-p263-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korabl-Sputnik_1">Korabl-Sputnik 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Korabl-Sputnik 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_1" title="Korabl-Sputnik 1">Korabl-Sputnik 1</a></div>\n<p>The first <i>Vostok</i> spacecraft was a variant not designed to be recovered from orbit; the variant was also called <i>Vostok 1KP</i> (or <i>1P</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p251_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-p251-15">[15]</a></sup> At Korolev\'s suggestion, the media would call the spacecraft <i>Korabl-Sputnik</i>, ("Satellite-ship"); the name <i>Vostok</i> was still a secret codename at this point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p251_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-p251-15">[15]</a></sup> This first Vostok spacecraft was successfully sent into orbit on May 15, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p251_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-p251-15">[15]</a></sup> Owing to a system malfunction, on the spacecraft\'s 64th orbit the thrusters fired and sent it into an even higher orbit. The orbit eventually decayed, and it <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">re-entered the atmosphere</a> several years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p252_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-p252-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vostok_1K">Vostok 1K</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vostok 1K">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table border="1" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="2" style="float:right; color: black; vertical-align: middle; text-align: center;">Success\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The next six launches were all of the <i>Vostok 1K</i> design, equipped with life-support facilities, and planned to be recovered after orbit. The first spacecraft launched on July 28, 1960 carried two <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs" title="Soviet space dogs">space dogs</a> named Chayka and Lisichka. An explosion destroyed the spacecraft shortly after launch, killing both dogs, and the mission was not given a name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p252_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-p252-17">[17]</a></sup> The next mission, designated <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_2" title="Korabl-Sputnik 2">Korabl-Sputnik 2</a>, was launched on August 19, 1960, carrying two more dogs, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Belka_and_Strelka" title="Soviet space dogs">Belka and Strelka</a>, as well as a variety of other biological specimens such as mice, insects, and strips of human skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p253_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-p253-18">[18]</a></sup> This mission was successful, and Belka and Strelka became the first living beings recovered from orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p253_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-p253-18">[18]</a></sup> The spacecraft was only the second object ever to have been recovered from orbit, the first being the return capsule of the American <a href="/wiki/Corona_(satellite)" title="Corona (satellite)">Discoverer 13</a> the previous week. During the mission there was some concern for Belka and Strelka\'s health, after images of Belka vomiting had been obtained from the onboard cameras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p253_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-p253-18">[18]</a></sup> The spacecraft and dogs were recovered following the 26-hour spaceflight, and extensive physiological tests revealed that the dogs were in good health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p254_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-p254-19">[19]</a></sup> This represented a significant success for the Vostok programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p254_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-p254-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The success of Korabl-Sputnik 2 gave the designers confidence to put forward a plan leading to a manned spaceflight. A document regarding a plan for the Vostok programme, dated September 10, 1960, and declassified in 1991, was sent to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee of the Communist Party</a>, and approved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Premiers_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="List of Premiers of the Soviet Union">Premier</a> <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p254_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-p254-19">[19]</a></sup> This document had been signed by the top leaders in the Soviet defence industry at the time, the most senior being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="List of Deputies of the Soviet Union">Deputy Chairman</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dmitriy_Ustinov" title="Dmitriy Ustinov">Dmitriy Ustinov</a>; this indicated the elevated importance of the document.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p254_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-p254-19">[19]</a></sup> The plan called for one or two more <i>Vostok 1K</i> flights, followed by two unmanned <i>Vostok 3K\'</i> flights, followed by a manned flight in December 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p255_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-p255-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A major setback occurred on October 24, when a rocket explosion killed over 100 people, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Marshal" title="Chief Marshal">Chief Marshal</a> of Artillery <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitrofan_Ivanovich_Nedelin" title="Mitrofan Ivanovich Nedelin">Mitrofan Nedelin</a>, in what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Nedelin_catastrophe" title="Nedelin catastrophe">Nedelin catastrophe</a>. This was one of the worst <a href="/wiki/List_of_spaceflight-related_accidents_and_incidents" title="List of spaceflight-related accidents and incidents">disasters in the history of spaceflight</a>. It involved a rocket that was not designed by Korolev, and was not necessary for the Vostok programme; the rocket was by rival designer <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Yangel" title="Mikhail Yangel">Mikhail Yangel</a>, intended to be a new generation of <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missiles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nedelin_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nedelin-21">[21]</a></sup> It would be two weeks before work on the Vostok programme continued, and it was realised that the original target of a December manned launch was unrealistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nedelin_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nedelin-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On December 1, 1960, the next <i>Vostok 1K</i> spacecraft, called <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_3" title="Korabl-Sputnik 3">Korabl-Sputnik 3</a> by the press, was launched. It carried the two dogs <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Pchyolka_and_Mushka" title="Soviet space dogs">Pchyolka and Mushka</a>. After about 24 hours, the engines were intended to fire to begin re-entry, but they fired for less time than had been expected. This meant that the spacecraft would enter the atmosphere, but not over Soviet territory. For this reason the self-destruct system was activated, and the spacecraft and the two dogs were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p259_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-p259-22">[22]</a></sup> At the time, the press reported that an incorrect altitude caused the cabin to be destroyed upon re-entry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p259_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-p259-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The next <i>Vostok 1K</i> spacecraft was launched on December 22, 1960, but it was unnamed because it failed to reach orbit. It carried two dogs, named <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Damka_and_Krasavka" title="Soviet space dogs">Kometa and Shutka</a>. The third stage of the launch system malfunctioned, and the emergency escape system was activated. The spacecraft landed 3,500 kilometres downrange of the launch site. The resulting rescue operation took several days, in -40 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">&#176;C</a> conditions. After a few days, the dogs were both recovered alive, and the spacecraft was returned to Moscow a few weeks later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Despite Korolev\'s desire to announce this failure to the press, the State Commission vetoed the idea.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vostok_3KA">Vostok 3KA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vostok_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vostok 3KA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_4" title="Korabl-Sputnik 4">Korabl-Sputnik 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_5" title="Korabl-Sputnik 5">Korabl-Sputnik 5</a></div>\n<p>The two unmanned missions immediately preceding the first manned flight used the same spacecraft design as in the manned missions, a design called <i>Vostok 3KA</i> (or <i>3A</i>). The only differences were that they would carry a single dog into orbit, a life-size <a href="/wiki/Mannequin" title="Mannequin">mannequin</a> would be strapped into the main ejection seat, and (unlike the manned missions) they had a self-destruct system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p264_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-p264-24">[24]</a></sup> The recent failures of Vostok 1K were not encouraging, but it was decided to proceed with launches of an automated variant of <i>Vostok 3KA</i>, the spacecraft design that would conduct a manned spaceflight. The approval of a manned mission was contingent upon the success of the two automated <i>Vostok 3KA</i> missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Unlike the previous <i>Vostok 1K</i> flights, the two unmanned <i>Vostok 3KA</i> flights were planned to last only a single orbit, to imitate the plan for the first manned flight.\n</p><p>The first of these unmanned flights, <a href="/wiki/Korabl-Sputnik_4" title="Korabl-Sputnik 4">Korabl-Sputnik 4</a>, was launched on March 9, 1961. It carried the dog <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Chernushka" title="Soviet space dogs">Chernushka</a> into orbit, as well as a mannequin called <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Ivanovich_(Vostok_programme)" title="Ivan Ivanovich (Vostok programme)">Ivan Ivanovich</a>, who wore a functioning <a href="/wiki/SK-1_spacesuit" title="SK-1 spacesuit">SK-1 spacesuit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p265_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-p265-26">[26]</a></sup> The dog was contained in a small pressurized sphere, which also contained 80 mice, several <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pig" title="Guinea pig">guinea pigs</a>, and other biological specimens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p265_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-p265-26">[26]</a></sup> Additional mice, guinea pigs, and other specimens were placed within the mannequin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p265_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-p265-26">[26]</a></sup> After one orbit, the descent module successfully re-entered the atmosphere, the mannequin was safely ejected, and the dog and other specimens landed separately in the descent module by parachute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p266_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-p266-27">[27]</a></sup> The spaceflight lasted 106 minutes, and the dog was recovered alive after landing. The mission was a complete success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p266_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-p266-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On March 23, before the next mission, an accident occurred during training which led to the death of cosmonaut candidate <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Bondarenko" title="Valentin Bondarenko">Valentin Bondarenko</a>. He was burned in a fire in an oxygen-rich isolation chamber, and died in a hospital eight hours after the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p266_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-p266-27">[27]</a></sup> Bondarenko\'s death was the first known cosmonaut or astronaut fatality. It is not clear whether other cosmonauts were told of his death immediately;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p266_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-p266-27">[27]</a></sup> the media did not learn of Bondarenko\'s death &#8211; or even of his existence &#8211; until many years later, in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Unsubstantiated reports of other cosmonaut deaths created the myth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_cosmonauts" title="Lost cosmonauts">lost cosmonaut</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:33%; Like the previous <i>Vostok 3KA</i> flight, it lasted for only a single orbit, carried a mannequin and a dog, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Zvyozdochka" title="Soviet space dogs">Zvezdochka</a> ("Starlet",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p267_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-p267-30">[30]</a></sup> or "Little star"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KS5_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-KS5-31">[31]</a></sup>). This mission was also a complete success, which was the final step required to get approval for a manned mission. The re-entry module of the Korabl-Sputnik 5 spacecraft, also called <i>Vostok 3KA-2</i>, was auctioned at <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby\'s">Sotheby\'s</a> on April 12, 2011, the 50th anniversary of the first manned spaceflight, <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a>. Evgeny Yurchenko, a Russian investment banker, paid $2,882,500 for the capsule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sothebys_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-sothebys-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span
