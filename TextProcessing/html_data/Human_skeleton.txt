It is composed of around 270 <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a> at birth &#8211; this total decreases to around 206 bones by adulthood after some bones get fused together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The bone mass in the skeleton reaches maximum <a href="/wiki/Bone_density" title="Bone density">density</a> around age 21. The human skeleton can be divided into the <a href="/wiki/Axial_skeleton" title="Axial skeleton">axial skeleton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Appendicular_skeleton" title="Appendicular skeleton">appendicular skeleton</a>. The axial skeleton is formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_vertebral_column" title="Human vertebral column">vertebral column</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_rib_cage" title="Human rib cage">rib cage</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a>  and other associated bones. The appendicular skeleton, which is attached to the axial skeleton, is formed by the <a href="/wiki/Shoulder_girdle" title="Shoulder girdle">shoulder girdle</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_girdle" title="Pelvic girdle">pelvic girdle</a> and the bones of the upper and lower limbs.\n</p><p>The human skeleton performs six major functions; support, movement, protection, production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_cells" title="Blood cells">blood cells</a>, storage of minerals, and endocrine regulation.\n</p><p>The human skeleton is not as <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> as that of many other primate species, but subtle differences between sexes in the <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_bone" title="Long bone">long bones</a>, and pelvis exist. In general, female skeletal elements tend to be smaller and less robust than corresponding male elements within a given population. The human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Female_pelvis" title="Female pelvis">female pelvis</a> is also different from that of males in order to facilitate <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Unlike most primates, human males do not have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penile_bone" title="Penile bone">penile bones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patterns_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-patterns-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="mw-headline" id="Axial_skeleton">Axial skeleton</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Axial_skeleton" title="Axial skeleton">Axial skeleton</a></div>\n<p>The axial skeleton (80 bones) is formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_vertebral_column" title="Human vertebral column">vertebral column</a> (32&#8211;34 bones; the number of the vertebrae differs from human to human as the lower 2 parts, sacral and coccygeal bone may vary in length), a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_rib_cage" title="Human rib cage">rib cage</a> (12 pairs of <a href="/wiki/Rib" title="Rib">ribs</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_sternum" title="Human sternum">sternum</a>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a> (22 bones and 7 associated bones).\n</p><p>The upright posture of humans is maintained by the axial skeleton, which transmits the weight from the head, the trunk, and the upper extremities down to the lower extremities at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip_joint" title="Hip joint">hip joints</a>. The bones of the spine are supported by many ligaments. The <a href="/wiki/Erector_spinae_muscles" title="Erector spinae muscles">erector spinae muscles</a> are also supporting and are useful for balance.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appendicular_skeleton">Appendicular skeleton</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Appendicular_skeleton" title="Appendicular skeleton">Appendicular skeleton</a></div>\n<p>The appendicular skeleton (126 bones) is formed by the pectoral girdles, the upper limbs, the pelvic girdle or pelvis, and the lower limbs. Their functions are to make locomotion possible and to protect the major organs of digestion, excretion and reproduction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human-Skeleton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1510" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="369" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Human-Skeleton.jpg/220px-Human-Skeleton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Human-Skeleton.jpg/330px-Human-Skeleton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Human-Skeleton.jpg/440px-Human-Skeleton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human-Skeleton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A human skeleton on exhibit at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Osteology" title="Museum of Osteology">Museum of Osteology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City,_Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma City, Oklahoma">Oklahoma City, Oklahoma</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The skeleton serves six major functions: support, movement, protection, production of blood cells, storage of minerals and endocrine regulation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Support">Support</span></h3>\n<p>The skeleton provides the framework which supports the body and maintains its shape. The pelvis, associated ligaments and muscles provide a floor for the pelvic structures. Without the <a href="/wiki/Rib_cage" title="Rib cage">rib cages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costal_cartilage" title="Costal cartilage">costal cartilages</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Intercostal_muscle" title="Intercostal muscle">intercostal muscles</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_lungs" title="Human lungs">lungs</a> would collapse.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement">Movement</span></h3>\n<p>The joints between bones allow movement, some allowing a wider range of movement than others, e.g. the ball and socket joint allows a greater range of movement than the pivot joint at the neck. Movement is powered by <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscle" title="Skeletal muscle">skeletal muscles</a>, which are attached to the skeleton at various sites on bones. Muscles, bones, and joints provide the principal mechanics for movement, all coordinated by the nervous system.\n</p><p>It is believed that the reduction of human bone density in prehistoric times reduced the agility and dexterity of human movement. Shifting from hunting to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> has caused human bone density to reduce significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span></h3>\n<p>The skeleton helps to protect our  many vital internal <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> from being damaged.\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a> protects the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a></li>\n<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrae" title="Vertebrae">vertebrae</a> protect the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Rib_cage" title="Rib cage">rib cage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_column" title="Vertebral column">spine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sternum" title="Sternum">sternum</a> protect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> and major <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_cell_production">Blood cell production</span></h3>\n<p>The skeleton is the site of <a href="/wiki/Haematopoiesis" title="Haematopoiesis">haematopoiesis</a>, the development of blood cells that takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>. In children, haematopoiesis occurs primarily in the marrow of the long bones such as the femur and tibia.  In adults, it occurs mainly in the pelvis, cranium, vertebrae, and sternum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Osteon" title="Osteon">bone matrix</a> can store <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and is involved in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_metabolism" title="Calcium metabolism">calcium metabolism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> can store <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ferritin" title="Ferritin">ferritin</a> and is involved in <a href="/wiki/Human_iron_metabolism" title="Human iron metabolism">iron metabolism</a>. However, bones are not entirely made of calcium, but a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Chondroitin_sulfate" title="Chondroitin sulfate">chondroitin sulfate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxylapatite" title="Hydroxylapatite">hydroxyapatite</a>, the latter making up 70% of a bone. Hydroxyapatite is in turn composed of 39.8% of calcium, 41.4% of oxygen, 18.5% of phosphorus, and 0.2% of hydrogen by mass. Chondroitin sulfate is a sugar made up primarily of oxygen and carbon.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endocrine_regulation">Endocrine regulation</span></h3>\n<p>Bone cells release a hormone called <a href="/wiki/Osteocalcin" title="Osteocalcin">osteocalcin</a>, which contributes to the regulation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">blood sugar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_fat" title="Body fat">fat deposition</a>. Osteocalcin increases both the <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> secretion and sensitivity, in addition to boosting the number of <a href="/wiki/Beta_cell" title="Beta cell">insulin-producing cells</a> and reducing stores of fat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_differences">Sex differences</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1912" data-file-width="4826" decoding="async" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg/220px-Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg/330px-Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg/440px-Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Museum_-_skeleton_of_a_wealthy_woman_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>During construction of the York to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scarborough_Railway_Bridge" title="Scarborough Railway Bridge">Scarborough Railway Bridge</a> in 1901, workmen discovered a large stone coffin, close to the <a href="/wiki/River_Ouse,_Yorkshire" title="River Ouse, Yorkshire">River Ouse</a>. Inside was a skeleton, accompanied by an array of unusual and expensive objects. This chance find represents one of the most significant discoveries ever made from <a href="/wiki/Eboracum#Late_Roman_York" title="Eboracum">Roman York</a>. Study of the skeleton has revealed that it belonged to a woman.</div></div></div>\n<p>Anatomical differences between human males and females are highly pronounced in some soft tissue areas, but tend to be limited in the skeleton. The human skeleton is not as <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a> as that of many other primate species, but subtle differences between sexes in the <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">skull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentition" title="Dentition">dentition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_bone" title="Long bone">long bones</a>, and pelvis are exhibited across human populations. In general, female skeletal elements tend to be smaller and less robust than corresponding male elements within a given population. It is not known whether or to what extent those differences are genetic or environmental.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skull">Skull</span></h3>\n<p>A variety of gross morphological traits of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_skull" title="Human skull">human skull</a> demonstrate sexual dimorphism, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nuchal_lines" title="Nuchal lines">median nuchal line</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastoid_process" title="Mastoid process">mastoid processes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supraorbital_margin" title="Supraorbital margin">supraorbital margin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supraorbital_ridge" title="Supraorbital ridge">supraorbital ridge</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chin" title="Chin">chin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dentition">Dentition</span></h3>\n<p>Human inter-sex dental dimorphism centers on the <a href="/wiki/Canine_tooth" title="Canine tooth">canine teeth</a>, but it is not nearly as pronounced as in the other <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">great apes</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_bones">Long bones</span></h3>\n<p>Long bones are generally larger in males than in females within a given population. Muscle attachment sites on long bones are often more robust in males than in females, reflecting a difference in overall muscle mass and development between sexes. Sexual dimorphism in the long bones is commonly characterized by <a href="/wiki/Morphometrics" title="Morphometrics">morphometric</a> or gross morphological analyses.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pelvis">Pelvis</span></h3>\n<p>The human pelvis exhibits greater sexual dimorphism than other bones, specifically in the size and shape of the <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_cavity" title="Pelvic cavity">pelvic cavity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilium_(bone)" title="Ilium (bone)">ilia</a>, greater sciatic notches, and the sub-pubic angle. The <a href="/wiki/Phenice_method" title="Phenice method">Phenice method</a> is commonly used to determine the sex of an unidentified human skeleton by anthropologists with 96% to 100% accuracy in some populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Women\'s pelvises are wider in the pelvic inlet and are wider throughout the pelvis to allow for child birth. The <a href="/wiki/Sacrum" title="Sacrum">sacrum</a> in the women\'s pelvis is curved inwards to allow the child to have a "<a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">funnel</a>" to assist in the child\'s pathway from the uterus to the <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">birth canal</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bone_disease" title="Bone disease">Bone disease</a></div>\n<p>There are many classified skeletal disorders. One of the most common is <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">osteoporosis</a>. Also common is <a href="/wiki/Scoliosis" title="Scoliosis">scoliosis</a>, a side-to-side curve in the back or spine, often creating a pronounced "C" or "S" shape when viewed on an x-ray of the spine. This condition is most apparent during adolescence, and is most common with females.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthritis">Arthritis</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">Arthritis</a></div>\n<p>Arthritis is a <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disorder</a> of the joints. It involves inflammation of one or more joints. When affected by arthritis, the joint or joints affected may be painful to move, may move in unusual directions or may be immobile completely. The symptoms of arthritis will vary differently between types of arthritis. The most common form of arthritis: <a href="/wiki/Osteoarthritis" title="Osteoarthritis">Osteoarthritis</a> can affect both the larger and smaller joints of the human skeleton. The <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a> in the affected joints will degrade, soften and wear away. This decreases the mobility of the joints and decreases the space between <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a> where cartilage should be.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Osteoporosis">Osteoporosis</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">Osteoporosis</a></div>\n<p>Osteoporosis is a disease of bone where there is reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_mineral_density" title="Bone mineral density">bone mineral density</a>, increasing the likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">fractures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-11">[11]</a></sup>  Osteoporosis is defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> in women as a bone mineral density 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviations</a> below peak bone mass, relative to the age and sex-matched average, as measured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_energy_X-ray_absorptiometry" title="Dual energy X-ray absorptiometry">Dual energy X-ray absorptiometry</a>, with the term "established osteoporosis" including the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fragility_fracture" title="Fragility fracture">fragility fracture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO1994_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO1994-12">[12]</a></sup> Osteoporosis is most common in women after <a href="/wiki/Menopause" title="Menopause">menopause</a>, when it is called "postmenopausal osteoporosis", but may develop in men and premenopausal women in the presence of particular hormonal disorders and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_(medicine)" title="Chronic (medicine)">chronic</a> diseases or as a result of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">smoking</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medications" title="Medications">medications</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Glucocorticoid" title="Glucocorticoid">glucocorticoids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-11">[11]</a></sup> Osteoporosis usually has no symptoms until a fracture occurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-11">[11]</a></sup> For this reason, DEXA scans are often done in people with one or more risk factors, who have developed osteoporosis and be at risk of fracture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Osteoporosis treatment includes advice to stop smoking, decrease alcohol consumption, exercise regularly, and have a healthy diet. <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Calcium</a> supplements may also be advised, as may <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">Vitamin D</a>. When medication is used, it may include <a href="/wiki/Bisphosphonate" title="Bisphosphonate">bisphosphonates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strontium_ranelate" title="Strontium ranelate">Strontium ranelate</a>, and osteoporosis may be one factor considered when commencing <a href="/wiki/Hormone_replacement_therapy" title="Hormone replacement therapy">Hormone replacement therapy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>\n<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks dissected human corpses in Alexandria is credited to be the pioneer of the field. His works are lost but are often cited by notable persons in the field such as <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rufus_of_Ephesus" title="Rufus of Ephesus">Rufus of Ephesus</a>. Galen himself did little dissection though and relied on the work of others like Marinus of Alexandria,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> as well as his own observations of gladiator cadavers and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Katherine Park (page does not exist)">Katherine Park</a>, in medieval Europe dissection continued to be practiced, contrary to the popular understanding that such practices are taboo and thus completely banned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The practice of <i>holy autopsy</i>, such as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Clare_of_Montefalco" title="Clare of Montefalco">Clare of Montefalco</a> further supports the claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Alexandria continued as a center of anatomy under Islamic rule, with <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Zuhr" title="Ibn Zuhr">Ibn Zuhr</a> a notable figure. Chinese understandings are divergent, as the closest corresponding concept in the medicinal system seem to be the <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(Chinese_medicine)" title="Meridian (Chinese medicine)">meridians</a>, although given that <a href="/wiki/Hua_Tuo" title="Hua Tuo">Hua Tuo</a> regularly performs surgery, there must be some distance between medical theory and actual understanding.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Renaissance">The Renaissance</span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonardo_Da_Vinci" title="Leonardo Da Vinci">Leonardo Da Vinci</a>, among his many talents also contributed to the study of the skeleton, albeit unpublished in his time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Many artists, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonio_Pollaiuolo" title="Antonio Pollaiuolo">Antonio Pollaiuolo</a> being the first, performed dissections for better understanding of the body, although they concentrated mostly on the muscles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesalius" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a>, regarded as the founder of modern anatomy authored the book <i><a href="/wiki/De_humani_corporis_fabrica" title="De humani corporis fabrica">De humani corporis fabrica</a></i>, which contained many illustrations of the skeleton and other body parts, correcting some theories dating from Galen, such as the lower jaw being a single bone instead of two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Various other figures like <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Achillini" title="Alessandro Achillini">Alessandro Achillini</a>  also contributed to the further understanding of the skeleton.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_bones_of_the_human_skeleton" title="List of bones of the human skeleton">List
