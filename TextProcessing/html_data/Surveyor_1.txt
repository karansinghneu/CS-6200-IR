program">Surveyor</a></i></div><div style="width:100%"><div class="noprint" style="float: left; text-align:left;padding-right:0.5em;">&#8592;&#160;<i>None</i></div><div class="noprint" style="float: first <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lunar soft-lander</a> in the unmanned  <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_program" title="Surveyor program">Surveyor program</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>). This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moon" title="The Moon">lunar</a> soft-lander gathered data about the lunar surface that would be needed for the manned <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo Moon landings</a> that began in 1969. The successful soft landing of <i>Surveyor 1</i> on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_of_Storms" title="Ocean of Storms">Ocean of Storms</a></i> was the first by an American <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> on any <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">extraterrestrial body</a>, occurring on the first attempt and just four months after the first Moon landing by the Soviet Union\'s <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">Luna 9</a> probe.\n</p><p><i>Surveyor 1</i> was launched May 30, 1966, from the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral,_Florida" title="Cape Canaveral, Florida">Cape Canaveral, Florida</a>, and it landed on the Moon on June 2, 1966. <i>Surveyor 1</i> transmitted 11,237 still photos of the lunar surface to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Earth" title="The Earth">the Earth</a> by using a television camera and a sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>-<a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> system.\n</p><p>The <i>Surveyor</i> program was managed by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County, California</a>, but the Surveyor space probe was designed by Gary Mizuhara of EOS (Electrical Optical Systems, Covina, Ca.) and built by the <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a> in <a href="/wiki/El_Segundo,_California" title="El Segundo, California">El Segundo, California</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div View is 7 km wide.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Surveyor</i> series of <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probes</a> was designed to carry out the first soft landings on the Moon by any American <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>. No instrumentation was carried specifically for scientific experiments by <i>Surveyor 1</i>, but considerable scientific data were collected by its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_camera" title="Television camera">television camera</a> and then returned to Earth via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Network" title="Deep Space Network">Deep Space Network</a> from 1966 to 1967. These spacecraft carried two television cameras&#160;&#8212; one for its approach, which was not used in this case, and one for taking still pictures of the lunar surface. Over 100 engineering sensors were on board each <i>Surveyor</i>. Their television systems transmitted pictures of the spacecraft footpad and surrounding lunar terrain and surface materials. These spacecraft also acquired data on the radar reflectivity of the lunar surface, the load-bearing strength of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_surface" title="Lunar surface">lunar surface</a>, and the temperatures for use in the analysis of the lunar surface temperatures. (Later <i>Surveyor</i> space probes, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Surveyor_3" title="Surveyor 3">Surveyor 3</a></i>, carried scientific instruments to measure the composition and <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanical properties</a> of the lunar "soil".)\n</p><p><i>Surveyor 1</i> was launched May 30, 1966 and sent directly into a trajectory to the Moon without any <a href="/wiki/Parking_orbit" title="Parking orbit">parking orbit</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Retrorocket" title="Retrorocket">retrorockets</a> were turned off at a height of about 3.4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">meters</a> above the lunar surface. <i>Surveyor 1</i> fell freely to the surface from this height, and it landed on the lunar surface on June 2, 1966, on the <i>Oceanus Procellarum</i>. This location was at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Surveyor_1&amp;params=2.474_S_43.339_W_globe:moon"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">2&#176;28&#8242;26&#8243;S</span> <span class="longitude">43&#176;20&#8242;20&#8243;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">2.474&#176;S 43.339&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-2.474; -43.339</span></span></span></a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The duration of the <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> of <i>Surveyor 1</i> was about 63 hours, 30 minutes. <i>Surveyor I\'s</i> lunar launch  weight was about 995.2 kilograms (2,194&#160;lb), and its landing weight (minus expended maneuvering propellant, its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid-fueled</a> retrorocket (which had been jettisoned), and its <a href="/wiki/Radar_altimeter" title="Radar altimeter">radar altimeter</a> system) was about 294.3 kilograms (649&#160;lb).\n</p><p><i>Surveyor 1</i> transmitted <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> data from the Moon beginning shortly after its landing through July 14, 1966, but with a period of no operations during the two-week long lunar night of June 14, 1966 through July 7, 1966. Because the Moon always presents the same face to Earth, "line-of-sight" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_communication" title="Radio communication">radio communications</a> with <i>Surveyor 1</i> required only changes in ground stations as the Earth rotated. However, since it was solar-powered, <i>Surveyor 1</i> had no electricity with which to do anything at all during the two weeks of the lunar nights.\n</p><p>The return of engineering information (temperatures, etc.) from <i>Surveyor 1</i> continued through January 7, 1967, with several interruptions during the lunar nights.\n</p><p>The landing of <i>Surveyor 1</i> was carried live on some television networks, and the success of the first Surveyor landing was considered surprising, especially after the failure of a number of the Ranger spacecraft en route to the Moon. Justin Rennilson, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a>, stated, "We figured the probability of success at around 10 to 15 percent." Among hundreds of other challenges, an uninterrupted communication link for navigation and control was critical to success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_instruments">Science instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surveyor_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Science instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surveyor_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surveyor_1_Foot_Pad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="424" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7f/Surveyor_1_Foot_Pad.jpg/220px-Surveyor_1_Foot_Pad.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Surveyor_1_Foot_Pad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image from Surveyor 1 of its footpad in order to study <a href="/wiki/Soil_mechanics" title="Soil mechanics">soil mechanics</a> in preparation for the Apollo manned landings.</div></div></div>\n<p>The TV camera consisted of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidicon" title="Vidicon">vidicon</a> tube, a zoom lens operated at either end of its range resulting in 25 millimeter and 100 millimeter focal-lengths, resulting in optical fields of view of 25.3 or 6.43 degrees, a shutter, several optical filters, and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">iris</a>-system mounted along an axis inclined approximately 16 degrees from the central axis of <i>Surveyor 1</i>. The camera was mounted under a mirror that could be moved in azimuth and elevation. The rotation of the mirror in the azimuth direction, while providing azimuth coverage capability results in an image rotation proportional to the angular azimuth position of the mirror. This is because the image plane and scanning raster of the vidicon are stationary with respect to the mirror azimuth axis. The mirror drive mechanism consisting of stepper motors provided a step size of 2.48&#176; &#177;0.1&#176; in elevation and 3.0&#176; &#177;0.1&#176; in azimuth. This calibrated stepping reference allowed the creation of large composite mosaics of the lunar surface and using the data read-back from the iris and focus positioning of the lens permitted some photogrammetric measurements of various lunar features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The TV camera\'s operation was dependent on the receipt of the proper radio commands from the Earth. Frame-by-frame coverage of the lunar surface was obtained over 360 degrees in azimuth and from +40 degrees above the plane normal to the camera\'s axis to -65 degrees below this plane. Both 600-line and 200-line modes of operation were used. The 200-line mode transmitted over an omnidirectional antenna for the first 14 photos and scanned one frame every 61.8 seconds. The remaining transmissions were of 600-line pictures over a directional antenna, and each frame was scanned every 3.6 seconds. Each 200-line picture required 20 seconds for a complete video transmission and it used a <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">radio bandwidth</a> of about 1.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilohertz" title="Kilohertz">kilohertz</a>.\n</p><p>Each 600-line picture required about one second to be read from the vidicon tube, and they required a <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">radio bandwidth</a> of about 220 kilohertz. The data transmissions were converted into a standard TV signal for both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_TV" title="Closed-circuit TV">closed-circuit TV</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_TV" title="Broadcast TV">broadcast TV</a>. The television images were displayed on Earth on a slow-scan monitor coated with a long persistency phosphor. The persistency was selected to optimally match the nominal maximum frame rate. One frame of TV identification was received for each incoming TV frame, and it was displayed in real time at a rate compatible with the incoming image. These data were recorded on a video magnetic tape recorder. Over 10,000 pictures were taken by <i>Surveyor 1\'s</i> TV camera before the lunar sunset of June 14, 1966. Included in these pictures were wide-angle and narrow-angle panoramas, focus ranging surveys, photometric surveys, special area surveys, and celestial photography. <i>Surveyor 1</i> responded to commands to activate the camera on July 7, and by July 14, 1966, it had returned nearly 1000 more pictures.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width:
