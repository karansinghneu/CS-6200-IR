href="/wiki/File:EarthAtmosphereBig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth atmosphere diagram showing all the layers of the atmosphere to scale</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>thermosphere</b> is the layer in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> directly above the <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a> and below the <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a>. Within this layer of the atmosphere, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> causes <a href="/wiki/Photoionization" title="Photoionization">photoionization</a>/photodissociation of molecules, creating ions in the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. Taking its name from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> &#952;&#949;&#961;&#956;&#972;&#962; (pronounced <i>thermos</i>) meaning heat, the thermosphere begins at about 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At these high altitudes, the residual atmospheric gases sort into strata according to <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbosphere" title="Turbosphere">turbosphere</a>). Thermospheric <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a> increase with altitude due to absorption of highly energetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>. Temperatures are highly dependent on solar activity, and can rise to 1,700&#160;&#176;C (3,100&#160;&#176;F) or more. Radiation causes the atmosphere particles in this layer to become electrically charged (see <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>), enabling <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> to be refracted and thus be received beyond the horizon. In the exosphere, beginning at about 600&#160;km (375&#160;mi) above sea level, the atmosphere turns into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>, although by the criteria set for the definition of the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a>, the thermosphere itself is part of space.\n</p><p>The highly diluted gas in this layer can reach 2,500&#160;&#176;C (4,530&#160;&#176;F) during the day. Despite the high temperature, an observer or object will experience cold temperatures in the thermosphere, because the extremely low density of gas (practically a hard vacuum) is insufficient for the molecules to conduct heat. A normal <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> will read significantly below 0&#160;&#176;C (32&#160;&#176;F), at least at night, because the energy lost by thermal radiation would exceed the energy acquired from the atmospheric gas by direct contact. In the <a href="/wiki/Anacoustic_zone" title="Anacoustic zone">anacoustic zone</a> above 160 kilometres (99&#160;mi), the density is so low that molecular interactions are too infrequent to permit the transmission of sound. \n</p><p>The dynamics of the thermosphere are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_tide" title="Atmospheric tide">atmospheric tides</a>, which are driven by the very significant <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal heating</a>. Atmospheric waves dissipate above this level because of collisions between the neutral gas and the ionospheric plasma.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> orbits the Earth within the middle of the thermosphere, between 330 and 435 kilometres (205 and 270&#160;mi).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input constituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Neutral gas constituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is convenient  to separate the atmospheric regions  according to the two temperature minima at about 12&#160;km altitude (the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>) and at about 85&#160;km (the <a href="/wiki/Mesopause" title="Mesopause">mesopause</a>) (Figure 1). The thermosphere (or the upper atmosphere) is the height region above 85&#160;km, while the region between the tropopause and the mesopause is the middle atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>) where absorption of solar UV radiation generates the temperature maximum near 45&#160;km altitude and causes the <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> layer.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="980" decoding="async" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg/500px-Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg/750px-Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nomenclature_of_Thermosphere.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 1. Nomenclature of atmospheric regions based on the profiles of electric conductivity (left), temperature (middle), and electron number density in m<sup>&#8722;3</sup>(right)</div></div></div>\n<p>The density of the Earth\'s atmosphere decreases nearly exponentially with altitude. The total mass of the atmosphere  is M = &#961;<sub>A</sub> H&#160; &#8771; 1&#160;kg/cm<sup>2</sup> within a column of one square centimeter above the ground (with &#961;<sub>A</sub> = 1.29&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup> the atmospheric density on the ground at z = 0 m altitude, and H &#8771; 8&#160;km the average atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Scale_height" title="Scale height">scale height</a>). 80% of that mass is concentrated within the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. The mass of the thermosphere above about 85&#160;km is only 0.002% of the total mass. Therefore, no significant energetic feedback from the thermosphere to the lower atmospheric regions can be expected.\n</p><p>Turbulence causes the air within the lower atmospheric regions below the <a href="/wiki/Turbopause" title="Turbopause">turbopause</a> at about 110&#160;km to be a mixture of gases that does not change its composition. Its mean molecular weight is 29&#160;g/mol with molecular oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) and nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) as the two dominant constituents. Above the turbopause, however, diffusive separation of the various constituents is significant, so that each constituent follows its own barometric height structure with a scale height inversely proportional to its molecular weight. The lighter constituents atomic oxygen (O), helium (He), and hydrogen (H) successively dominate above about 200&#160;km altitude and vary with geographic location, time, and solar activity.  The ratio\nN<sub>2</sub>/O which is a measure of the electron density at the ionospheric F region is highly affected by these variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These changes follow from the diffusion of  the minor constituents through the major gas component during dynamic processes.\n</p><p>The thermosphere contains an appreciable concentration of elemental <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> located in a 10-km thick band that occurs at the edge of the mesosphere, 80 to 100&#160;km above Earth\'s surface. The sodium has an average concentration of 400,000 atoms per cubic centimeter. This band is regularly replenished by sodium sublimating from incoming meteors. Astronomers have begun utilizing this sodium band to create "<a href="/wiki/Laser_guide_star" title="Laser guide star">guide stars</a>" as part of the optical correction process in producing ultra-sharp ground-based observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_input">Energy input</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy input">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_budget">Energy budget</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy budget">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The thermospheric temperature can be determined from density observations as well as from direct satellite measurements. One estimates a total heat input of q<sub>o</sub>&#8771; 0.8 to 1.6&#160;mW/m<sup>2</sup> above z<sub>o</sub> = 120&#160;km altitude. In order to obtain equilibrium conditions, that heat input q<sub>o</sub> above z<sub>o</sub> is lost to the lower atmospheric regions by heat conduction.\n</p><p>The exospheric temperature T<sub>&#8734;</sub> is a fair measurement of the solar XUV radiation. The Covington index varies typically between 70 and 250 during a solar cycle, and never drops below about 50. Thus, T<sub>&#8734;</sub> varies between about 740 and 1350&#160;K. During very quiet magnetospheric conditions, the still continuously flowing magnetospheric energy input contributes by about 250&#160;K to the residual temperature of 500&#160;K in eq.(2). The rest of 250&#160;K in eq.(2) can be attributed to atmospheric waves generated within the troposphere and dissipated within the lower thermosphere.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_XUV_radiation">Solar XUV radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar XUV radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The solar X-ray and extreme ultraviolet radiation (XUV) at wavelengths &lt; 170&#160;nm is almost completely absorbed within the thermosphere. This radiation causes the various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionospheric_layer" title="Ionospheric layer">ionospheric layers</a> as well as a temperature increase at these heights (Figure 1).\nWhile the solar visible light (380 to 780&#160;nm) is nearly constant with a variability of not more than about 0.1% of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the solar XUV radiation is highly variable in time and space. For instance, X-ray bursts associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">solar flares</a> can dramatically increase their intensity over preflare levels by many orders of magnitude over a time span of tens of minutes. In the extreme ultraviolet, the Lyman &#945; line at 121.6&#160;nm represents an important source of <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> at ionospheric D layer heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> During quiet periods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_activity" title="Solar activity">solar activity</a>, it alone contains more energy than the rest of the XUV spectrum. Quasi-periodic changes of the order of 100% or greater, with periods of 27 days and 11 years, belong to the prominent variations of solar XUV radiation. However, irregular fluctuations over all time scales are present all the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> During low solar activity, about half of the total energy input into the thermosphere is thought to be solar XUV radiation. Evidently, that solar XUV energy input occurs only during daytime conditions, maximizing at the equator during <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_wind">Solar wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A second source of energy input into the thermosphere is <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> energy which is transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> by mechanisms that are not well understood. One possible way to transfer energy is via a hydrodynamic dynamo process. Solar wind particles penetrate into the polar regions of the magnetosphere where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_field" title="Geomagnetic field">geomagnetic field</a> lines are essentially vertically directed. An electric field is generated, directed from dawn to dusk. Along the last closed geomagnetic field lines with their footpoints within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auroral" title="Auroral">auroral</a> zones, field aligned electric currents can flow into the <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_dynamo_region" title="Ionospheric dynamo region">ionospheric dynamo region</a> where they are closed by electric <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedersen_current&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedersen current (page does not exist)">Pedersen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall_current" title="Hall current">Hall currents</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">Ohmic</a> losses of the Pedersen currents heat the lower thermosphere (see e.g., <a href="/wiki/Magnetospheric_electric_convection_field" title="Magnetospheric electric convection field">Magnetospheric electric convection field</a>). In addition, penetration of high energetic particles from the magnetosphere into the auroral regions enhance drastically the electric conductivity, further increasing the electric currents and thus <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">Joule heating</a>. During quiet magnetospheric activity, the magnetosphere contributes perhaps by a quarter to the thermosphere\'s energy budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This is about 250&#160;K of the exospheric temperature in eq.(2). During very large activity, however, this heat input can increase substantially, by a factor of four or more. That solar wind input occurs mainly in the auroral regions during both day and night.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_waves">Atmospheric waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two  kinds of large-scale atmospheric waves within the lower atmosphere exist: internal waves with finite vertical wavelengths which can transport wave energy upward; and external waves with infinitely large wavelengths which cannot transport wave energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VollandTP_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-VollandTP-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_gravity_wave" title="Atmospheric gravity wave">Atmospheric gravity waves</a> and most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_tides" title="Atmospheric tides">atmospheric tides</a> generated within the troposphere belong to the internal waves. Their density amplitudes increase exponentially with height, so that at the mesopause these waves become turbulent and their energy is dissipated (similar to breaking of ocean waves at the coast), thus contributing to the heating of the thermosphere by about 250&#160;K in eq.(2). On the other hand, the fundamental diurnal tide labelled (1, &#8722;2) which is most efficiently excited by solar irradiance is an external wave and plays only a marginal role within lower and middle atmosphere. However, at thermospheric altitudes, it becomes the predominant wave. It drives the electric Sq-current within the <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_dynamo_region" title="Ionospheric dynamo region">ionospheric dynamo region</a> between about 100 and 200&#160;km height.\n</p><p>Heating, predominately by tidal waves, occurs mainly at lower and middle latitudes. The variability of this heating depends on the meteorological conditions within troposphere and middle atmosphere, and may not exceed about 50%.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics">Dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thermospherewaves.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Thermospherewaves.jpg/500px-Thermospherewaves.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Thermospherewaves.jpg/750px-Thermospherewaves.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/Thermospherewaves.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thermospherewaves.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 2. The reaction of the thermosphere to a large magnetospheric storm is called thermospheric storm. Since the heat input into the thermosphere occurs at high latitudes (mainly into the auroral regions), the heat transport represented by the term P<sub>2</sub><sup>0</sup> in eq.(3) is reversed. In addition, due to the impulsive form of the disturbance, higher-order terms are generated which, however, possess short decay times and thus quickly disappear. The sum of these modes determines the "travel time" of the disturbance to the lower latitudes, and thus the response time of the thermosphere with respect to the magnetospheric disturbance. Important for the development of an <a href="/wiki/Ionospheric_storm" title="Ionospheric storm">ionospheric storm</a> is the increase of the ratio N<sub>2</sub>/O  during a thermospheric storm at middle and higher latitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pr&#246;lss_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pr&#246;lss-13">[13]</a></sup> An increase of  N<sub>2</sub> increases the loss process of the ionospheric plasma and causes therefore a decrease of the electron density within the ionospheric F-layer (negative ionospheric  storm).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
