Jacques Cartier was the first European to describe and map<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Saint_Lawrence" title="Gulf of Saint Lawrence">Gulf of Saint Lawrence</a> and the shores of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a>, which he <a href="/wiki/Name_of_Canada" title="Name of Canada">named "The Country of Canadas</a>", after the <a href="/wiki/Iroquoian_languages" title="Iroquoian languages">Iroquois names</a> for the two big settlements he saw at <a href="/wiki/Stadacona" title="Stadacona">Stadacona (Quebec City)</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Hochelaga_(village)" title="Hochelaga (village)">Hochelaga (Montreal Island)</a>.<sup class="reference" life</span></h2>\n<p>Jacques Cartier was born in 1491<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Malo" title="Saint-Malo">Saint-Malo</a>, the port on the north-west coast of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. Cartier, who was a respectable mariner, improved his social status in 1520 by marrying Mary Catherine des Granches, member of a leading family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His good name in Saint-Malo is recognized by its frequent appearance in baptismal registers as godfather or witness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="First_voyage.2C_1534"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage,_1534">First voyage, 1534</span></h2>\n<p>In 1534, two years after the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brittany" title="Duchy of Brittany">Duchy of Brittany</a> was formally united with France in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_between_Brittany_and_France" title="Union between Brittany and France">Edict of Union</a>, Cartier was introduced to King <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Le_Veneur" title="Jean Le Veneur">Jean Le Veneur</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_(Catholic_Church)" title="Bishop (Catholic Church)">bishop</a> of Saint-Malo and <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbot</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mont_Saint-Michel" title="Mont Saint-Michel">Mont Saint-Michel</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Manoir_de_Brion" title="Manoir de Brion">Manoir de Brion</a>. The king had previously invited (although not formally commissioned) the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_da_Verrazzano" title="Giovanni da Verrazzano">Giovanni da Verrazzano</a> to explore the eastern coast of North America on behalf of France in 1524.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\nLe Veneur cited voyages to Newfoundland and Brazil as proof of Cartier\'s ability to "lead ships to the discovery of new lands in the New World".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cartier_First_Voyage_Map_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="552" data-file-width="636" decoding="async" height="382" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cartier_First_Voyage_Map_1.png/440px-Cartier_First_Voyage_Map_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Cartier_First_Voyage_Map_1.png 1.5x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cartier_First_Voyage_Map_1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Route of Cartier\'s first voyage</div></div></div>\n<p>On April 20, 1534,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Cartier set sail under a commission from the king, hoping to discover a <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">western passage</a> to the wealthy markets of Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the words of the commission, he was to "discover certain islands and lands where it is said that a great quantity of gold and other precious things are to be found".\n</p><p>It took him twenty days to sail across the ocean. Starting on May 10 of that year, he explored parts of Newfoundland, areas that now comprise the Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_provinces" title="Atlantic provinces">Atlantic provinces</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_St._Lawrence" title="Gulf of St. Lawrence">Gulf of St. Lawrence</a>. During one stop at &#206;les aux Oiseaux (Islands of the Birds, now the Rochers-aux-Oiseaux federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_sanctuary" title="Bird sanctuary">bird sanctuary</a>, northeast of <a href="/wiki/Brion_Island" title="Brion Island">Brion Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_Islands" title="Magdalen Islands">Magdalen Islands</a>), his crew slaughtered around 1000 birds, most of them <a href="/wiki/Great_auk" title="Great auk">great auks</a> (extinct since 1852). Cartier\'s first two encounters with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">aboriginal peoples in Canada</a> on the north side of <a href="/wiki/Chaleur_Bay" title="Chaleur Bay">Chaleur Bay</a>, most likely the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mi%27kmaq_people" title="Mi\'kmaq people">Mi\'kmaq</a>, were brief; some trading occurred.\n</p><p>His third encounter took place on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Bay" title="Gasp&#233; Bay">Gasp&#233; Bay</a> with a party of <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_Iroquoians" title="St. Lawrence Iroquoians">St. Lawrence Iroquoians</a>, where on July 24, he planted a cross to claim the land for France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  The 10-meter cross bearing the words "Long Live the King of France" took possession of the territory in the name of the king. The change in mood was a clear indication that the Iroquoians understood Cartier\'s actions. Here he kidnapped the two sons of their captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Cartier wrote that they later told him this region where they were captured (Gasp&#233;) was called by them <i>Honguedo</i>. The natives\' captain at last agreed that they could be taken, under the condition that they return with European goods to trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cartier returned to France in September 1534, sure that he had reached an Asian land.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Second_voyage.2C_1535.E2.80.931536"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_voyage,_1535&#8211;1536">Second voyage, 1535&#8211;1536</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2479" data-file-width="4168" decoding="async" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg/330px-Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg/440px-Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carte_espagnole_fleuve_Saint_Laurent.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This Spanish chart of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a>, from ca. 1541, contains a legend in front of the "isla de Orliens" that says: "Here many French died of hunger"; possibly alluding to Cartier\'s second settlement in 1535&#8211;1536.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Jacques Cartier set sail for a second voyage on May 19 of the following year with three ships, 110 men, and his two Iroquoian captives. Reaching the St. Lawrence, he sailed up-river for the first time, and reached the Iroquoian capital of <a href="/wiki/Stadacona" title="Stadacona">Stadacona</a>, where Chief <a href="/wiki/Donnacona" title="Donnacona">Donnacona</a> ruled.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Cartier left his main ships in a harbour close to Stadacona, and used his smallest ship to continue on to <a href="/wiki/Hochelaga_(village)" title="Hochelaga (village)">Hochelaga</a> (now Montreal), arriving on October 2, 1535. Hochelaga was far more impressive than the small and squalid village of Stadacona, and a crowd of over a thousand came to the river edge to greet the Frenchmen. The site of their arrival has been confidently identified as the beginning of the Sainte-Marie Sault &#8211; where the <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier_Bridge" title="Jacques Cartier Bridge">bridge named after him</a> now stands. The expedition could proceed no further, as the river was blocked by rapids. So certain was Cartier that the river was the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> and that the rapids were all that was preventing him from sailing to China, that the rapids and the town that eventually grew up near them came to be named after the French word for China, <i>La Chine</i>: the <a href="/wiki/Lachine_Rapids" title="Lachine Rapids">Lachine Rapids</a> and the town of <a href="/wiki/Lachine,_Quebec" title="Lachine, Quebec">Lachine, Quebec</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chronicals_of_America_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chronicals_of_America-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After spending two days among the people of Hochelaga, Cartier returned to Stadacona on October 11. It is not known exactly when he decided to spend the winter of 1535&#8211;1536 in Stadacona, and it was by then too late to return to France. Cartier and his men prepared for the winter by strengthening their fort, stacking firewood, and <a href="/wiki/Salting_(food)" title="Salting (food)">salting down</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt-cured_meat" title="Salt-cured meat">game</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltfish" title="Saltfish">and fish</a>.\n</p><p>From mid-November 1535 to mid-April 1536, the French fleet lay frozen solid at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Saint-Charles_River" title="Saint-Charles River">St. Charles River</a>, under the Rock of Quebec. Ice was over a <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathom</a> (1.8 m) thick on the river, with snow four feet (1.2 m) deep ashore. To add to the misery, <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a> broke out &#8211; first among the Iroquoians, and then among the French. Cartier estimated the number of dead Iroquoians at 50. On a visit by Domagaya to the French fort, Cartier inquired and learned from him that a concoction made from a tree known as <a href="/wiki/Aneda" title="Aneda">annedda</a>, probably <a href="/wiki/Spruce_beer" title="Spruce beer">Spruce beer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> or <i><a href="/wiki/Thuja_occidentalis" title="Thuja occidentalis">arbor vitae,</a></i> would cure scurvy. This remedy likely saved the expedition from destruction, allowing 85 Frenchmen to survive the winter. In his journal, Cartier states that by mid-February, "out of 110 that we were, not ten were well enough to help the others, a pitiful thing to see". The Frenchmen used up the bark of an entire tree in a week on the cure, and the dramatic results prompted Cartier to proclaim it a Godsend, and a miracle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chronicals_of_America_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chronicals_of_America-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biggar_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biggar-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ready to return to France in early May 1536, Cartier decided to kidnap Chief Donnacona and take him to France,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> so that he might personally tell the tale of a country further north, called the "<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saguenay" title="Kingdom of Saguenay">Kingdom of Saguenay</a>", said to be full of gold, rubies and other treasures. After an arduous trip down the St. Lawrence and a three-week Atlantic crossing, Cartier and his men arrived in Saint-Malo on July 15, 1536, concluding the second, 14-month voyage, which was to be Cartier\'s most profitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biggar_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Biggar-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Third_voyage.2C_1541.E2.80.931542"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_voyage,_1541&#8211;1542">Third voyage, 1541&#8211;1542</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1198" decoding="async" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg/320px-Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg/480px-Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg/640px-Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dauphin_Map_of_Canada_-_circa_1543_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Dauphin Map</i> of Canada, c. 1543, showing Cartier\'s discoveries</div></div></div>\n<p>On October 17, 1540, Francis ordered the navigator Jacques Cartier to return to Canada to lend weight to a colonization project of which he would be "captain general". However, January 15, 1541, saw Cartier supplanted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_de_La_Rocque_de_Roberval" title="Jean-Fran&#231;ois de La Rocque de Roberval">Jean-Fran&#231;ois de La Rocque de Roberval</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huguenot" title="Huguenot">Huguenot</a> courtier and friend of the king named as the first <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General_of_New_France" title="Lieutenant General of New France">lieutenant general of French Canada</a>. Roberval was to lead the expedition, with Cartier as his chief navigator. While Roberval waited for artillery and supplies, he gave permission to Cartier to sail on ahead with his ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 23, 1541, Cartier departed Saint-Malo on his third voyage with five ships. This time, any thought of finding a passage to the Orient was forgotten. The goals were now to find the "Kingdom of Saguenay" and its riches, and to establish a permanent settlement along the St. Lawrence River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Journeys_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-American_Journeys-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anchoring at Stadacona, Cartier again met the <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_Iroquoians" title="St. Lawrence Iroquoians">Iroquoians</a>, but found their "show of joy" and their numbers worrisome, and decided not to build his settlement there. Sailing a few kilometres up-river to a spot he had previously observed, he decided to settle on the site of present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap-Rouge" title="Cap-Rouge">Cap-Rouge</a>, Quebec. The convicts and other colonists were landed, the cattle that had survived three months aboard ship were turned loose, earth was broken for a kitchen garden, and seeds of cabbage, turnip, and lettuce were planted. A fortified settlement was thus created and was named <a href="/wiki/Charlesbourg-Royal" title="Charlesbourg-Royal">Charlesbourg-Royal</a>. Another fort was also built on the cliff overlooking the settlement, for added protection.\n</p><p>The men also began collecting what they believed to be diamonds and gold, but which upon return to France were discovered to be merely quartz crystals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_pyrites" title="Iron pyrites">iron pyrites</a>, respectively&#160;&#8212; which gave rise to a French expression: "<i>faux comme les diamants du Canada</i>" ("As false as Canadian diamonds"). Two of the ships were sent on their journey home with some of these minerals on September 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Journeys_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-American_Journeys-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Having set tasks for everyone, Cartier left with the longboats for a reconnaissance in search of "Saguenay" on September 7. Having reached Hochelaga, he was prevented by bad weather and the numerous rapids from continuing up to the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River" title="Ottawa River">Ottawa River</a>.\n</p><p>Returning to Charlesbourg-Royal, Cartier found the situation ominous. The Iroquoians no longer made friendly visits or peddled fish and game, but prowled about in a sinister manner. No records exist about the winter of 1541&#8211;1542 and the information must be gleaned from the few details provided by returning sailors. It seems the natives attacked and killed about 35 settlers before the Frenchmen could retreat behind their fortifications. Even though scurvy was cured through the native remedy (<a href="/wiki/Thuja_occidentalis" title="Thuja occidentalis">Thuja occidentalis</a> infusion), the impression left is of a general misery, and of Cartier\'s growing conviction that he had insufficient manpower either to protect his base or to go in search of the Saguenay Kingdom.\n</p><p>Cartier left for France in early June 1542, encountering Roberval and his ships along the Newfoundland coast, at about the time Roberval marooned <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_de_La_Rocque" title="Marguerite de La Rocque">Marguerite de La Rocque</a>. Despite Roberval\'s insistence that he accompany him back to Saguenay, Cartier slipped off under the cover of darkness and continued on to France, still convinced his vessels contained a wealth of gold and diamonds. He arrived there in October, in what proved to be his last voyage. Meanwhile, Roberval took command at Charlesbourg-Royal, but it was abandoned in 1543 after disease, foul weather and hostile natives drove the would-be settlers to despair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span></h2>\n<p>Cartier spent the rest of his life in Saint-Malo and his nearby estate, where he often was useful as an interpreter in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>. He died at age 65 on September 1, 1557, during an epidemic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\npossibly of <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> though many sources list his cause of death as unknown. Cartier is interred in St. Vincent\'s Cathedral.\n</p><p>No permanent European settlements were made in Canada before 1605, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Champlain" title="Samuel Champlain">Samuel Champlain</a> founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitation_at_Port-Royal" title="Habitation at Port-Royal">Port Royal</a> in present-day Victoria Beach just outside Annapolis Royal.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>\n<table class="box-More_citations_needed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jacques_Cartier&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. already located the entrance to the St. Lawrence on his first voyage, he now opened up the greatest waterway for the European penetration of North America. He produced an intelligent estimate of the resources of Canada, both natural and human, albeit with a considerable exaggeration of its mineral wealth. While some of his actions toward the St. Lawrence Iroquoians were dishonourable, he did try at times to establish friendship with them and other native peoples living along the St. Lawrence River&#8212;an indispensable preliminary to French settlement in their lands.\n</p><p>Cartier was the first to document the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada%27s_name" title="Canada\'s name">Canada</a> to designate the territory on the shores of the St-Lawrence River. The name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Huron</a>-<a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> word "kanata", or village, which was incorrectly interpreted as the native term for the newly discovered land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Cartier used the name to describe Stadacona, the surrounding land and the river itself. And Cartier named "<i>Canadiens</i>" the inhabitants (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquoian" title="Iroquoian">Iroquoians</a>) he had seen there. Thereafter the name Canada was used to designate the small French colony on these shores, and the French colonists were called <i>Canadiens</i>, until the mid-nineteenth century, when the name started to be applied to the loyalist colonies on <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">the Great Lakes</a> and later to all of <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a>. In this way Cartier is not strictly the European discoverer of Canada as this country is understood today, a vast federation stretching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_Canada" title="Coat of Arms of Canada"><i>a mari usque ad mare</i></a> (from sea to sea). Eastern parts had previously been visited by the Norse, as well as Basque, Galician and Breton fishermen, and perhaps the <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Corte-Real_(disambiguation)" title="Corte-Real (disambiguation)">Corte-Real</a> brothers and <a href="/wiki/John_Cabot" title="John Cabot">John Cabot</a> (in addition of course to the Natives who first inhabited the territory). Cartier\'s particular contribution to the discovery of Canada is as the first European to penetrate the continent, and more precisely the interior eastern region along the St. Lawrence River. His explorations consolidated France\'s claim of the territory that would later be colonized as <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>, and his third voyage produced the first documented European attempt at settling North America since that of <a href="/wiki/Lucas_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Ayll%C3%B3n" title="Lucas V&#225;zquez de Ayll&#243;n">Lucas V&#225;zquez de Ayll&#243;n</a> in 1526&#8211;27.\n</p><p>Cartier\'s professional abilities can be easily ascertained. Considering that Cartier made three voyages of exploration in dangerous and hitherto unknown waters without losing a ship, and that he entered and departed some 50 undiscovered harbors without serious mishap, he may be considered one of the most conscientious explorers of the period.\n</p><p>Cartier was also one of the first to formally acknowledge that the New World was a separate land mass from Europe/Asia.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Rediscovery_of_Cartier.27s_first_colony"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rediscovery_of_Cartier\'s_first_colony">Rediscovery of Cartier\'s first in the Saint-Roch neighbourhood of Quebec City.</div></div></div>\n<p>On August 18, 2006, Quebec Premier <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charest" title="Jean Charest">Jean Charest</a> announced that Canadian archaeologists had discovered the precise location of Cartier\'s lost first colony of <a href="/wiki/Charlesbourg-Royal" title="Charlesbourg-Royal">Charlesbourg-Royal</a>.<sup class="reference"
