(siblings)</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Leif Erikson</b> or <b>Leif Ericson</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[note 1]</a></sup> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 970 &#8211; c. 1020) was a <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> <a href="/wiki/Viking_expansion" title="Viking expansion">explorer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vis1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-vis1-7">[6]</a></sup> He was the first known European to have set foot on continental <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> (excluding <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>), before <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Sagas_of_Icelanders" title="Sagas of Icelanders">Sagas of Icelanders</a>, he established a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">Norse settlement</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a>, tentatively identified with the Norse <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L\'Anse aux Meadows">L\'Anse aux Meadows</a> on the northern tip of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological" title="Archaeological">archaeological</a> evidence suggests that Vinland may have been the areas around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_St._Lawrence" title="Gulf of St. Lawrence">Gulf of St. Lawrence</a> and that the L\'Anse aux Meadows site was a ship repair station.\n</p><p>Leif was the son of <a href="/wiki/Erik_the_Red" title="Erik the Red">Erik the Red</a>, the founder of the first Norse settlement in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and of Thjodhild (&#222;j&#243;&#240;hildur), both of Norwegian origin. His place of birth is not known,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> but he is assumed to have been born in Iceland, which had recently been colonized by Norsemen mainly from Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vis1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-vis1-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leif_Eriksson_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leif_Eriksson-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> He grew up in the family estate <i><a href="/wiki/Brattahl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Brattahl&#237;&#240;">Brattahl&#237;&#240;</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Settlement" title="Eastern Settlement">Eastern Settlement</a> in Greenland. Leif had two known sons: Thorgils, born to noblewoman Thorgunna in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>; and Thorkell, who succeeded him as chieftain of the Greenland settlement.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" life</span></h2>\n<p>Leif was the son of <a href="/wiki/Erik_the_Red" title="Erik the Red">Erik the Red</a> and his wife Thjodhild, and the grandson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorvaldr_%C3%81svaldsson" title="&#222;orvaldr &#193;svaldsson">Thorvaldr &#193;svaldsson</a>, and distant relative of <a href="/wiki/Naddodd" title="Naddodd">Naddodd</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> who discovered Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> He was a <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Viking</a> in the early days. His year of birth is most often given as c. 970 or c. 980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sand14_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-sand14-15">[14]</a></sup> Though Leif\'s birthplace is not accounted for in the sagas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snl_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-snl-16">[15]</a></sup> it is likely he was born in <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Commonwealth" title="Icelandic Commonwealth">Iceland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vis1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-vis1-7">[6]</a></sup> where his parents met<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sand14_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-sand14-15">[14]</a></sup>&#8212;probably somewhere on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Brei%C3%B0afj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Brei&#240;afj&#246;r&#240;ur">Brei&#240;afj&#246;r&#240;ur</a>, and possibly at the farm <i>Haukadal</i> where Thj&#243;&#240;hild\'s family is said to have been based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vis1_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-vis1-7">[6]</a></sup> Leif had two brothers, whose names were <a href="/wiki/Thorstein_Eiriksson" title="Thorstein Eiriksson">Thorsteinn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thorvald_Eiriksson" title="Thorvald Eiriksson">Thorvaldr</a>, and a sister, <a href="/wiki/Freyd%C3%ADs_Eir%C3%ADksd%C3%B3ttir" title="Freyd&#237;s Eir&#237;ksd&#243;ttir">Freyd&#237;s</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ingstad_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ingstad-17">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Thorvald Asvaldsson was banished from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> for <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">manslaughter</a> and went into exile in Iceland accompanied by young Erik. When Erik was himself banished from Iceland, he travelled further west to an area he named <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, where he established the first permanent settlement in 986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snl_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-snl-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tyrker" title="Tyrker">Tyrker</a>, one of Erik\'s <a href="/wiki/Thrall" title="Thrall">thralls</a>, had been specially trusted to keep in charge of Erik\'s children, as Leif later referred to him as his "foster father".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovering_Vinland">Discovering Vinland</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1302" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg/280px-Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg/420px-Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg/560px-Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christian-krohg-leiv-eriksson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><i>Leif Eriksson Discovers America</i><br/>by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Krohg" title="Christian Krohg">Christian Krohg</a> (1893)</center></div></div></div>\n<p>Leif and his crew travelled from Greenland to Norway in 999 AD. Blown off course to the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a> and staying for much of the summer, he arrived in Norway and became a <i><a href="/wiki/Hird" title="Hird">hirdman</a></i> of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olaf_I_of_Norway" title="Olaf I of Norway">Olaf Tryggvason</a>. He also converted to Christianity and was given the mission of introducing the religion to Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snl_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-snl-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sm419_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-sm419-20">[19]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Saga_of_Erik_the_Red" title="Saga of Erik the Red">Saga of Erik the Red</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saga_of_the_Greenlanders" title="Saga of the Greenlanders">Saga of the Greenlanders</a></i>, both thought to have been written around 1200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> contain different accounts of the voyages to <i><a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sm350_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-sm350-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> The only two known strictly historical mentions of <i>Vinland</i> are found in the work of <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a> c. 1075 and in the <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dslendingab%C3%B3k" title="&#205;slendingab&#243;k">Book of Icelanders</a></i> compiled c. 1122 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ari_%C3%9Eorgilsson" title="Ari &#222;orgilsson">Ari the Wise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinhis_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-vinhis-24">[23]</a></sup> According to the <i>Saga of Erik the Red</i>, Leif apparently saw <i>Vinland</i> for the first time after being blown off course on his way to introduce Christianity to Greenland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sm419_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-sm419-20">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2832" data-file-width="2159" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg/220px-The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg/330px-The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg/440px-The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_landing_of_Vikings_on_America.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><i>The Landing of the Vikings</i><br/>by Arthur C. Michael (1919)</center></div></div></div>\n<p>According to a literal interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Einar_Haugen" title="Einar Haugen">Einar Haugen</a>\'s translation of the two sagas in the book <i>Voyages to Vinland</i>, Leif was not the first European to discover America: he had heard the story of merchant <a href="/wiki/Bjarni_Herj%C3%B3lfsson" title="Bjarni Herj&#243;lfsson">Bjarni Herj&#243;lfsson</a> who claimed to have sighted land to the west of Greenland after having been blown off course. Bjarni reportedly never made landfall there, however. Later, when travelling from Norway to Greenland, Leif was also blown off course, to a land that he did not expect to see, where he found "self-sown wheat fields and grapevines". He next rescued two men who were shipwrecked in this country and went back to Greenland (and Christianised the people there).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> Consequently, if this is to be trusted, Bjarni Herj&#243;lfsson was the first European to see America beyond Greenland, and the two unnamed shipwrecked men were the first people known to Europeans to have made landfall there.\n</p><p>Leif then approached Bjarni, purchased his ship, gathered a crew of thirty-five men, and mounted an expedition towards the land Bjarni had described.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-short34_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-short34-26">[25]</a></sup> His father Erik was set to join him but dropped out after he fell from his horse on his way to set sail, an incident he interpreted as a bad <a href="/wiki/Omen" title="Omen">omen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Leif followed Bjarni\'s route in reverse and landed first in a rocky and desolate place he named <i><a href="/wiki/Helluland" title="Helluland">Helluland</a></i> (Flat-Rock Land; possibly <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>). After venturing further by sea, he landed the second time in a forested place he named <i><a href="/wiki/Markland" title="Markland">Markland</a></i> (Forest Land; possibly <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a>). Finally, after two more days at sea, he landed in a verdant area with a mild climate and plentiful supplies of salmon. As winter approached, he decided to encamp there and broke his party into two groups &#8211; one to remain at camp and the other to explore the country. During one of these explorations, <a href="/wiki/Tyrker" title="Tyrker">Tyrker</a> discovered that the land was full of vines and grapes. Leif therefore named the land <i><a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> There, he and his crew built a small settlement, which was called <i><a href="/wiki/Leifsbudir" title="Leifsbudir">Leifsbudir</a></i> (Leif\'s Booths) by later visitors from Greenland. After having wintered over in <i>Vinland</i>, Leif returned to Greenland in the spring with a cargo of grapes and timber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-short34_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-short34-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> On the return voyage, he rescued an Icelandic castaway and his crew, earning him the nickname "Leif the Lucky".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg/220px-Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg/330px-Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg/440px-Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Authentic_Viking_recreation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern recreation of the Norse site at L\'Anse aux Meadows</div></div></div>\n<p>Research done in the early 1960s by Norwegian explorer <a href="/wiki/Helge_Ingstad" title="Helge Ingstad">Helge Ingstad</a> and his wife, archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Stine_Ingstad" title="Anne Stine Ingstad">Anne Stine Ingstad</a>, identified a Norse site<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> located at the northern tip of Newfoundland. It has been suggested that this site, known as <a href="/wiki/L%27Anse_aux_Meadows" title="L\'Anse aux Meadows">L\'Anse aux Meadows</a>, is <i>Leifsb&#250;&#240;ir</i>. The Ingstads demonstrated that Norsemen had reached America about 500 years before <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s207_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-s207-33">[32]</a></sup> Later archaeological evidence suggests that <i>Vinland</i> may have been the areas around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_of_St._Lawrence" title="Gulf of St. Lawrence">Gulf of St. Lawrence</a> and that the L\'Anse aux Meadows site was a ship repair station and waypoint for voyages there. That does not necessarily contradict the identification of L\'Anse aux Meadows with <i>Leifsb&#250;&#240;ir</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s207_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-s207-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> since the two sagas appear to describe <i>Vinland</i> as a wider region which included several settlements. The <i>Saga of Erik the Red</i> mentions two other settlements in <i>Vinland</i>: a settlement called <i><a href="/wiki/Straumfj%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Straumfj&#246;r&#240;">Straumfj&#491;r&#240;r</a></i>, which lay beyond <i>Kjalarnes</i> promontory and the <a href="/wiki/Wonderstrands" title="Wonderstrands">Wonderstrands</a>, and one called <i>H&#243;p</i>, which was located even farther south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="675" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg/220px-Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg/330px-Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg/440px-Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seattle%27s_Leif_Erikson_statue.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leif Erikson memorial statue at Shilshole Bay Marina (<a href="/wiki/Port_of_Seattle" title="Port of Seattle">Port of Seattle</a>)</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>\n<p>Leif was described as a wise, considerate, and strong man of striking appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canmys_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-canmys-36">[35]</a></sup> During his stay in the Hebrides, he fell in love with a noblewoman, Thorgunna, who gave birth to their son Thorgils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ingstad_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ingstad-17">[16]</a></sup> Thorgils was later sent to Leif in Greenland, but he did not become popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canmys_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-canmys-36">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Leif\'s first trip to <i>Vinland</i>, he returned to the family estate of <i><a href="/wiki/Brattahl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Brattahl&#237;&#240;">Brattahl&#237;&#240;</a></i> in Greenland, and started preaching Christianity to the Greenlanders. His father Erik reacted coldly to the suggestion that he should abandon his religion, while his mother Thj&#243;&#240;hildr quickly became a Christian and built a church called Thj&#243;&#240;hild\'s Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> <span id="Thorkell"></span>Leif is last mentioned alive in 1019, and by 1025 he had passed on his chieftaincy of <i><a href="/wiki/Tunulliarfik_Fjord" title="Tunulliarfik Fjord">Eir&#237;ksfj&#491;r&#240;r</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snl_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-snl-16">[15]</a></sup> to another son, Thorkell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canmys_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-canmys-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> Nothing is mentioned about his death in the sagas&#8212;he probably died in Greenland some time between these dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canmys_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-canmys-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Nothing further is known about his family beyond the succession of Thorkell as chieftain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canmys_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-canmys-36">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse_and_Medieval_Europe">Norse and Medieval Europe</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="395" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg/220px-Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg/330px-Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg/440px-Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Faroe_stamps_225-226_Discovery_of_America.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Discovery of America</i> Faroe Islands postage stamp</div></div></div>\n<p>Leif\'s successful expedition in Vinland encouraged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Norse colonization of the Americas">other Norsemen to also make the journey</a>. The first apparent contact between the Norse and the indigenous people, who the Norse later referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Skr%C3%A6ling" title="Skr&#230;ling">skr&#230;lingjar</a></i>, was made by his brother Thorvald, and resulted in hostilities and killing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2036_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-s2036-40">[39]</a></sup> In the end there were no permanent Norse settlements in Vinland, although sporadic voyages at least to <i>Markland</i> for forages, timber and trade possibly lasted for centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> The casual tone of references to these areas may suggest that their discovery was not seen as particularly significant by contemporaries, or that it was assumed to be public knowledge, or both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vinhis_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-vinhis-24">[23]</a></sup> Knowledge of the Vinland journeys might have spread around medieval Europe, as writers such as <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a> made mention of remote lands to the west. It has been suggested that the knowledge of Vinland might have been maintained in European seaports in the 15th century, and that Christopher Columbus, who claimed in a letter to have visited Iceland in 1477, could have heard stories of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s2036_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-s2036-40">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Travels_and_commemoration">Travels and commemoration</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1124" data-file-width="736" decoding="async" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG/170px-Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG/255px-Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG/340px-Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leif_Erikson_6c_1968_issue.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_stamp" title="Commemorative stamp">commemorative stamp</a>, issued 9 October 1968 (<a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson_Day" title="Leif Erikson Day">Leif Erikson Day</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Stories of Leif\'s journey to North America had a profound effect on the identity and self-perception of later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_American" title="Scandinavian American">Nordic Americans</a> and Nordic immigrants to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup> The first statue of Leif (by <a href="/wiki/Anne_Whitney" title="Anne Whitney">Anne Whitney</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> was erected in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> in 1887 at the instigation of <a href="/wiki/Eben_Norton_Horsford" title="Eben Norton Horsford">Eben Norton Horsford</a>, who was among those who believed that <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a> could have been located on the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup> not long after, <a href="/wiki/Leif,_the_Discoverer_(Whitney)" title="Leif, the Discoverer (Whitney)">another casting of Whitney\'s statue</a> was erected in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> A statue was also erected in Chicago in 1901, having been originally commissioned for the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World\'s Columbian Exposition">World\'s Columbian Exposition</a> to coincide with the arrival of the reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Viking_(ship)" title="Viking (ship)"><i>Viking</i> ship</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>, Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup> Another work of art made for the 1893 World\'s Columbian Exposition, the painting <i>Leiv Eiriksson oppdager Amerika</i> by <a href="/wiki/Christian_Krohg" title="Christian Krohg">Christian Krohg</a>, was in the possession of a Leif Erikson Memorial Association in Chicago before being given back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Norway" title="National Gallery of Norway">National Gallery of Norway</a> in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snl2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-snl2-45">[44]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the centenary of the first official immigration of Norwegians to America, President <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a> stated at the 1925 <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Fair" title="Minnesota State Fair">Minnesota State Fair</a>, to a crowd of 100,000 people, that Leif had indeed been the first European to discover America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup> Further statues of him were erected at the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_State_Capitol" title="Minnesota State Capitol">Minnesota State Capitol</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul,_Minnesota" title="Saint Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a> in 1949, near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> in <a href="/wiki/Duluth,_Minnesota" title="Duluth, Minnesota">Duluth</a> in 1956, and in downtown <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dregni_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-dregni-18">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Sagas do not give the exact date of Leif Erikson\'s landfall in America, they only state that it was in the fall of the year. At the suggestion of Christian A.
