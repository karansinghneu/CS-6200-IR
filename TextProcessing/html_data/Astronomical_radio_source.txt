class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1932, American <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karl_Jansky" title="Karl Jansky">Karl Jansky</a> detected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a> coming from an unknown source in the center of our <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>. Jansky was studying the origins of radio frequency interference for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Laboratories" title="Bell Laboratories">Bell Laboratories</a>.  He found "...a steady hiss type static of unknown origin", which eventually he concluded had an extraterrestrial origin. This was the first time that radio waves were detected from outer space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The first radio sky survey was conducted by <a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">Grote Reber</a> and was completed in 1941. In the 1970s, some stars in our galaxy were found to be radio emitters, one of the strongest being the unique <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary</a> <a href="/wiki/MWC_349" title="MWC 349">MWC 349</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources:_solar_system">Sources: solar system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources: solar system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sun">The Sun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Sun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As the nearest star, the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is the brightest radiation source in most frequencies, down to the radio spectrum at 300&#160;MHz (1 m wavelength).  When the Sun is quiet, the galactic background noise dominates at longer wavelengths. During <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storms</a>, the Sun will dominate even at these low frequencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jupiter">Jupiter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jupiter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Oscillation of electrons trapped in the  <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Jupiter" title="Magnetosphere of Jupiter">magnetosphere of Jupiter</a> produce strong radio signals, particularly bright in the decimeter band.\n</p><p>The magnetosphere of Jupiter is responsible for intense episodes of radio emission from the planet\'s polar regions. Volcanic activity on Jupiter\'s moon <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a> injects gas into Jupiter\'s magnetosphere, producing a torus of particles about the planet. As Io moves through this torus, the interaction generates <a href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_wave" title="Alfv&#233;n wave">Alfv&#233;n waves</a> that carry ionized matter into the polar regions of Jupiter. As a result, radio waves are generated through a <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron" title="Cyclotron">cyclotron</a> <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_maser" title="Astrophysical maser">maser mechanism</a>, and the energy is transmitted out along a cone-shaped surface. When Earth intersects this cone, the radio emissions from Jupiter can exceed the solar radio output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources:_galactic">Sources: galactic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sources: galactic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_galactic_center">The galactic center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The galactic center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">galactic center</a> of the Milky Way was the first radio source to be detected. It contains a number of radio sources, including <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_A*" title="Sagittarius A*">Sagittarius A*</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_A*#Central_black_hole" title="Sagittarius A*">supermassive black hole at its center</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supernova_remnants">Supernova remnants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Supernova remnants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Supernova_remnant" title="Supernova remnant">Supernova remnants</a> often show diffuse radio emission. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Cassiopeia_A" title="Cassiopeia A">Cassiopeia A</a>, the brightest extrasolar radio source in the sky, and the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutron_Stars">Neutron Stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neutron Stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulsars">Pulsars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pulsars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pulsar_schematic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pulsar_schematic.svg/220px-Pulsar_schematic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pulsar_schematic.svg/330px-Pulsar_schematic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pulsar_schematic.svg/440px-Pulsar_schematic.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pulsar_schematic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic view of a pulsar. The sphere in the middle represents the neutron star, the curves indicate the magnetic field lines, the protruding cones represent the emission beams and the green line represents the axis on which the star rotates.</div></div></div>\n<p>Supernovas sometimes leave behind dense spinning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_stars" title="Neutron stars">neutron stars</a> called <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>. They emit jets of charged particles which emit <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron radiation</a> in the radio spectrum. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Pulsar" title="Crab Pulsar">Crab Pulsar</a>, the first pulsar to be discovered. Pulsars and <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> (dense central cores of extremely distant galaxies) were both discovered by radio astronomers. In 2003 astronomers using the <a href="/wiki/Parkes_Observatory" title="Parkes Observatory">Parkes</a> <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> discovered two pulsars orbiting each other, the first such system known.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Rotating_Radio_Transient_.28RRAT.29_Sources"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotating_Radio_Transient_(RRAT)_Sources">Rotating Radio Transient (RRAT) Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Rotating Radio Transient (RRAT) Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Rotating_radio_transient" title="Rotating radio transient">Rotating radio transients</a> (RRATs) are a type of neutron stars discovered in 2006 by a team led by <a href="/wiki/Maura_McLaughlin" title="Maura McLaughlin">Maura McLaughlin</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory" title="Jodrell Bank Observatory">Jodrell Bank Observatory</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> in the UK. RRATs are believed to produce radio emissions which are very difficult to locate, because of their transient nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Early efforts have been able to detect radio emissions (sometimes called <b>RRAT flashes</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> for less than one second a day, and, like with other single-burst signals, one must take great care to distinguish them from terrestrial radio interference. Distributing computing and the Astropulse algorithm may thus lend itself to further detection of RRATs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_forming_regions">Star forming regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Star forming regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Short <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a> are emitted from complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> in dense clouds of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> where <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> are giving birth.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">Spiral galaxies</a> contain clouds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutral_hydrogen" title="Neutral hydrogen">neutral hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> which emit radio waves. The radio frequencies of these two molecules were used to map a large portion of the Milky Way galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources:_extra-galactic">Sources: extra-galactic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sources: extra-galactic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_galaxies">Radio galaxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Radio galaxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many galaxies are strong radio emitters, called <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a>. Some of the more notable are <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messier_87" title="Messier 87">Messier 87</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">Quasars</a> (short for "quasi-stellar radio source") were one of the first point-like radio sources to be discovered. Quasars\' extreme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift" title="Red shift">red shift</a> led us to conclude that they are distant active galactic nuclei, believed to be powered by <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>.    <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_Galactic_Nucleus" title="Active Galactic Nucleus">Active galactic nuclei</a> have jets of charged particles which emit <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron radiation</a>.  One example is <a href="/wiki/3C_273" title="3C 273">3C 273</a>, the optically brightest quasar in the sky.\n</p><p>Merging <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters" title="Galaxy groups and clusters">galaxy clusters</a> often show diffuse radio emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmic_microwave_background">Cosmic microwave background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cosmic microwave background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">Cosmic microwave background</a></div>\n<p>The cosmic microwave background is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a> <a href="/wiki/Background_radiation" title="Background radiation">background radiation</a> left over from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> (the rapid expansion, roughly 13.8 billion years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> that was the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extragalactic_pulses">Extragalactic pulses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Extragalactic pulses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fast_radio_burst" title="Fast radio burst">Fast radio burst</a></div>\n<p>D. R. Lorimer and others analyzed archival survey data and found a 30-<a href="/wiki/Jansky" title="Jansky">jansky</a> dispersed burst, less than 5 milliseconds in duration, located 3&#176; from the <a href="/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Small Magellanic Cloud">Small Magellanic Cloud</a>. They reported that the burst properties argue against a physical association with our Galaxy or the Small Magellanic Cloud. In a recent paper, they argue that current models for the free electron content in the universe imply that the burst is less than 1 giga<a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a> distant. The fact that no further bursts were seen in 90 hours of additional observations implies that it was a singular event such as a supernova or coalescence (fusion) of relativistic objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It is suggested that hundreds of similar events could occur every day and, if detected, could serve as cosmological probes. Radio pulsar surveys such as Astropulse-SETI@home offer one of the few opportunities to monitor the radio sky for impulsive burst-like events with millisecond durations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Because of the isolated nature of the observed phenomenon, the nature of the source remains speculative.  Possibilities include a black hole-<a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a> collision, a neutron star-neutron star collision, a black hole-black hole collision, or some phenomenon not yet considered.\n</p><p>In 2010 there was a new report of 16 similar pulses from the Parkes Telescope which were clearly of terrestrial origin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but in 2013 four pulse sources were identified that supported the likelihood of a genuine extragalactic pulsing population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These pulses are known as <i>fast radio bursts</i> (FRBs). The first observed burst has become known as the <i>Lorimer burst</i>. <a href="/wiki/Blitzar" title="Blitzar">Blitzars</a> are one proposed explanation for them.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources:_not_yet_observed">Sources: not yet observed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sources: not yet observed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primordial_black_holes">Primordial black holes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Primordial black holes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to the Big Bang Model, during the first few moments after the Big Bang, pressure and temperature were extremely great. Under these conditions, simple fluctuations in the density of matter may have resulted in local regions dense enough to create black holes. Although most regions of high density would be quickly dispersed by the expansion of the universe, a primordial black hole would be stable, persisting to the present.\n</p><p>One goal of <a href="/wiki/Astropulse" title="Astropulse">Astropulse</a> is to detect postulated mini black holes that might be evaporating due to "<a href="/wiki/Hawking_radiation" title="Hawking radiation">Hawking radiation</a>". Such mini black holes are postulated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> to have been created during the Big Bang, unlike currently known black holes. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Rees" title="Martin Rees">Martin Rees</a> has theorized that a black hole, exploding via Hawking radiation, might produce a signal that\'s detectable in the radio.  The Astropulse project hopes that this evaporation would produce radio waves that Astropulse can detect. The evaporation wouldn\'t create radio waves directly. Instead, it would create an expanding fireball of high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a> and particles. This fireball would interact with the surrounding magnetic field, pushing it out and generating radio waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ET">ET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: ET">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Search_for_extraterrestrial_intelligence" title="Search for extraterrestrial intelligence">Search for extraterrestrial intelligence</a></div>\n<p>Previous searches by various "search for extraterrestrial intelligence" (SETI) projects, starting with <a href="/wiki/Project_Ozma" title="Project Ozma">Project Ozma</a>, have looked for extraterrestrial communications in the form of narrow-band signals, analogous to our own radio stations. The <a href="/wiki/Astropulse" title="Astropulse">Astropulse</a> project argues that since we know nothing about how ET might communicate, this might be a bit closed-minded. Thus, the Astropulse Survey can be viewed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> as complementary to the narrow-band SETI@home survey as a by-product of the search for physical phenomena.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_undiscovered_phenomena">Other undiscovered phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other undiscovered phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Explaining their recent discovery of a powerful bursting radio source, NRL astronomer Dr. Joseph Lazio stated:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> "Amazingly, even though the sky is known to be full of transient objects emitting at X- and gamma-ray wavelengths, very little has been done to look for radio bursts, which are often easier for astronomical objects to produce."  The use of coherent dedispersion algorithms and the computing power provided by the SETI network may lead to discovery of previously undiscovered phenomena.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">Active galactic nucleus</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">Astrometry</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">Electromagnetic radiation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">Radio astronomy</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_X-ray_source" title="Astrophysical X-ray source">Astrophysical X-ray source</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_radio_source&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Koupelis, Theo; Karl F.
