Spores form part of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_life_cycle" title="Biological life cycle">life cycles</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_spore" title="Bacterial spore">Bacterial spores</a> are not part of a sexual cycle but are resistant structures used for survival under unfavourable conditions. <a href="/wiki/Myxozoa" title="Myxozoa">Myxozoan</a> spores release amoebulae into their hosts for parasitic infection, but also reproduce within the hosts through the pairing of two nuclei within the plasmodium, which develops from the amoebula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Spores are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unicellular" title="Unicellular">unicellular</a> and are produced by <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sporangium" title="Sporangium">sporangium</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diploid" title="Diploid">diploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Sporophyte" title="Sporophyte">sporophyte</a>. Under favourable conditions the spore can develop into a new <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> using <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitotic</a> division, producing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicellular" title="Multicellular">multicellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Gametophyte" title="Gametophyte">gametophyte</a>, which eventually goes on to produce gametes. Two gametes fuse to form a <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a> which develops into a new sporophyte. This cycle is known as <a href="/wiki/Alternation_of_generations" title="Alternation of generations">alternation of generations</a>.\n</p><p>The spores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_plant" title="Seed plant">seed plants</a>, however, are produced internally and the megaspores, formed within the ovules and the microspores are involved in the formation of more complex structures that form the dispersal units, the <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> grains.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term <i>spore</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word &#963;&#960;&#959;&#961;&#940; <i>spora</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seed</a>, sowing", related to &#963;&#960;&#972;&#961;&#959;&#962; <i>sporos</i>, "sowing," and &#963;&#960;&#949;&#943;&#961;&#949;&#953;&#957; <i>speirein</i>, "to sow."\n</p><p>In common parlance, the difference between a "spore" and a "<a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gamete</a>" (both together called gonites) is that a spore will germinate and develop into a <a href="/wiki/Sporeling" title="Sporeling">sporeling</a>, while a gamete needs to combine with another gamete to form a zygote before developing further.\n</p><p>The main difference between spores and seeds as <a href="/wiki/Biological_dispersal" title="Biological dispersal">dispersal units</a> is that spores are unicellular, while seeds contain within them a multicellular gametophyte that produces a developing embryo, the multicellular sporophyte of the next generation. Spores germinate to give rise to haploid gametophytes, while seeds germinate to give rise to diploid sporophytes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_spore-producing_organisms">Classification of spore-producing organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification of spore-producing organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">Vascular plant</a> spores are always <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a>. Vascular plants are either <b>homosporous (or isosporous)</b> or <b>heterosporous</b>. Plants that are homosporous produce spores of the same size and type. \n</p><p>Heterosporous plants, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seed_plant" title="Seed plant">seed plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spikemoss" title="Spikemoss">spikemosses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iso%C3%ABtes" title="Iso&#235;tes">quillworts</a>, and ferns of the order <a href="/wiki/Salviniales" title="Salviniales">Salviniales</a> produce spores of two different sizes: the larger spore (megaspore) in effect functioning as a "female" spore and the smaller (microspore) functioning as a "male". Such plants typically give rise to the two kind of spores from within separate sporangia, either a <b>megasporangium</b> that produces megaspores or a <b>microsporangium</b> that produces microspores. In flowering plants, these sporangia occur within the carpel and anthers, respectively.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fungi">Fungi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fungi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">Fungi</a> commonly produce spores, as a result of sexual, or asexual, reproduction. Spores are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haploid" title="Haploid">haploid</a> and grow into mature haploid individuals through <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitotic</a> division of cells (<a href="/wiki/Urediniospore" title="Urediniospore">Urediniospores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teliospore" title="Teliospore">Teliospores</a> among rusts are dikaryotic). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dikaryotic" title="Dikaryotic">Dikaryotic</a> cells result from the fusion of two haploid gamete cells. Among sporogenic dikaryotic cells, karyogamy (the fusion of the two haploid nuclei) occurs to produce a diploid cell. Diploid cells undergo meiosis to produce haploid spores.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_spores">Classification of spores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Many fungi thereby possess specialized mechanical and physiological mechanisms as well as spore-surface structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobin" title="Hydrophobin">hydrophobins</a>, for spore ejection. These mechanisms include, for example, forcible discharge of ascospores enabled by the structure of the ascus and accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Osmolyte" title="Osmolyte">osmolytes</a> in the fluids of the ascus that lead to explosive discharge of the ascospores into the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trail_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trail-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The forcible discharge of single spores termed <i>ballistospores</i> involves formation of a small drop of water (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buller%27s_drop" title="Buller\'s drop">Buller\'s drop</a>), which upon contact with the spore leads to its projectile release with an initial acceleration of more than 10,000 <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force">g</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pringle_et_al._8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pringle_et_al.-8">[8]</a></sup> Other fungi rely on alternative mechanisms for spore release, such as external mechanical forces, exemplified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puffballs" title="Puffballs">puffballs</a>. Attracting insects, such as flies, to fruiting structures, by virtue of their having lively colours and a putrid odour, for dispersal of fungal spores is yet another strategy, most prominently used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stinkhorns" title="Stinkhorns">stinkhorns</a>.\n</p><p>In Common Smoothcap moss (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Atrichum_undulatum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Atrichum undulatum (page does not exist)">Atrichum undulatum</a></i>), the vibration of sporophyte has been shown to be an important mechanism for spore release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the case of spore-shedding <a href="/wiki/Vascular_plant" title="Vascular plant">vascular plants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, wind distribution of very light spores provides great capacity for dispersal. Also, spores are less subject to animal predation than seeds because they contain almost no food reserve; however they are more subject to fungal and bacterial predation. Their chief advantage is that, of all forms of progeny, spores require the least energy and materials to produce.\n</p><p>In the spikemoss <i><a href="/wiki/Selaginella_lepidophylla" title="Selaginella lepidophylla">Selaginella lepidophylla</a></i>, dispersal is achieved in part by an unusual type of <a href="/wiki/Diaspore_(botany)" title="Diaspore (botany)">diaspore</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tumbleweed" title="Tumbleweed">tumbleweed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Gallery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 155px"><div style="width: 155px">\n<div class="thumb" style="width: 150px;"><div style="margin:30px auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg"><img alt="" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg/120px-Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg/180px-Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg/240px-Bartramia_ithyphylla_sporen.jpeg 2x" width="120"/></a></div></div>\n<div class="gallerytext">\n<p>Spores of the <a href="/wiki/Moss" title="Moss">moss</a> <i>Bartramia ithyphylla</i>.
