This sparless, ram-air inflated kite, has a complex bridle formed of many strings attached to the face of the wing.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>kite</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tether" title="Tether">tethered</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavier_than_air_flight" title="Heavier than air flight">heavier-than-air</a> craft with <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> surfaces that react against the air to create lift and drag. A kite consists of wings, tethers and anchors. Kites often have a bridle and tail to guide the face of the kite so the wind can lift it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Some kite designs don&#8217;t need a bridle; <a href="/wiki/Box_kite" title="Box kite">box kites</a> can have a single attachment point. A kite may have fixed or moving anchors that can balance the kite. One technical definition is that a kite is &#8220;a collection of tether-coupled wing sets&#8220;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> that sustains the kite in flight is generated when air moves around the kite\'s surface, producing low pressure above and high pressure below the wings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The interaction with the wind also generates horizontal <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> along the direction of the wind. The resultant force vector from the lift and drag force components is opposed by the tension of one or more of the <a href="/wiki/Rope" title="Rope">lines</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tether" title="Tether">tethers</a> to which the kite is attached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The anchor point of the kite line may be static or moving (e.g., the towing of a kite by a running person, boat, free-falling anchors as in paragliders and fugitive parakites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or vehicle).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The same principles of fluid flow apply in liquids, so kites can be used in underwater currents, but there are no everyday uses as yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Man-lifting_kite" title="Man-lifting kite">Man-lifting kites</a> were made for reconnaissance, entertainment and during development of the first practical <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a>.\n</p><p>Kites have a long and varied history and many different types are flown individually and at <a href="/wiki/List_of_kite_festivals" title="List of kite festivals">festivals</a> worldwide. Kites may be flown for <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> or other <a href="#Practical_uses">practical</a> uses. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_kites" title="Sport kites">Sport kites</a> can be flown in aerial <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">ballet</a>, sometimes as part of a competition. <a href="/wiki/Power_kite" title="Power kite">Power kites</a> are multi-line steerable kites designed to generate large <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> which can be used to power activities such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_surfing" title="Kite surfing">kite surfing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_landboarding" title="Kite landboarding">kite landboarding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_fishing" title="Kite fishing">kite fishing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_buggying" title="Kite buggying">kite buggying</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_kiting" title="Snow kiting">snow kiting</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" Drakes</i></div></div></div>\n<p>Kites were invented in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, though their exact origin can only be speculated. Many early sources point to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In China, materials ideal for kite building were readily available including <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> fabric for sail material; fine, high-tensile-strength silk for flying line; and resilient <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> for a strong, lightweight framework.\n</p><p>The kite has been claimed as the invention of the 5th-century BC Chinese philosophers <a href="/wiki/Mozi" title="Mozi">Mozi</a> (also Mo Di, or Mo Ti) and <a href="/wiki/Lu_Ban" title="Lu Ban">Lu Ban</a> (also Gongshu Ban, or Kungshu Phan). By 549 AD <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> kites were certainly being flown, as it was recorded that in that year a paper kite was used as a message for a rescue mission. Ancient and medieval Chinese sources describe kites being used for measuring distances, testing the wind, lifting men, signaling, and communication for military operations. The earliest known Chinese kites were flat (not bowed) and often rectangular. Later, tailless kites incorporated a stabilizing bowline. Kites were decorated with mythological motifs and legendary figures; some were fitted with strings and whistles to make musical sounds while flying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a Anthropomorphic kites made from cloth and wood were used in religious ceremonies to send prayers to the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Polynesian kite traditions are used by anthropologists to get an idea of early "primitive" Asian traditions that are believed to have at one time existed in Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="307" data-file-width="650" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg/310px-Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg/465px-Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg/620px-Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kinderspiele_1828_Drachensteigen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boys flying a kite. Bay, California</a> Kite Festival 2014</div></div></div>\n<p>Kites were late to arrive in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, although windsock-like banners were known and used by the Romans. Stories of kites were first brought to Europe by Marco Polo towards the end of the 13th century, and kites were brought back by sailors from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> in the 16th and 17th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G-Kites_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-G-Kites-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Kyeser" title="Konrad Kyeser">Konrad Kyeser</a> described <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_kite" title="Dragon kite">dragon kites</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bellifortis" title="Bellifortis">Bellifortis</a></i> about 1400 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196112_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196112-21">[21]</a></sup> Although kites were initially regarded as mere curiosities, by the 18th and 19th centuries they were being used as vehicles for scientific research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G-Kites_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-G-Kites-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1752 <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> published an account of a <a href="/wiki/Kite_experiment" title="Kite experiment">kite experiment</a> to prove that <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> was caused by <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>.\n</p><p>Kites were also instrumental in the research of the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a>, and others, as they developed the first airplane in the late 1800s. Several different designs of <a href="/wiki/Man-lifting_kite" title="Man-lifting kite">man-lifting kites</a> were developed. The period from 1860 to about 1910 became the "golden age of kiting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 20th century many new kite designs were developed. These included <a href="/wiki/William_Abner_Eddy#Kites" title="William Abner Eddy">Eddy</a>\'s tailless diamond, the <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_kite" title="Tetrahedral kite">tetrahedral kite</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rogallo_wing" title="Rogallo wing">Rogallo wing</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sled_kite" title="Sled kite">sled kite</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parafoil" title="Parafoil">parafoil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Power_kite" title="Power kite">power kites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_Kites_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Kites-23">[23]</a></sup>  Kites were used for scientific purposes, especially in meteorology, aeronautics, wireless communications and <a href="/wiki/Kite_aerial_photography" title="Kite aerial photography">photography</a>. The Rogallo wing was adapted for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stunt_kite" title="Stunt kite">stunt kites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hang_gliding" title="Hang gliding">hang gliding</a> and the parafoil was adapted for <a href="/wiki/Parachuting" title="Parachuting">parachuting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paragliding" title="Paragliding">paragliding</a>. \n</p><p>The rapid development of mechanically powered aircraft diminished interest in kites. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> saw a limited use of kites for military purposes (<a href="/wiki/Survival_radio" title="Survival radio">survival radio</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Focke_Achgelis_Fa_330" title="Focke Achgelis Fa 330">Focke Achgelis Fa 330</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_radio_antenna_kites" title="Military radio antenna kites">military radio antenna kites</a>). \n</p><p>Kites are now mostly used for recreation. Lightweight synthetic materials (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ripstop_nylon" title="Ripstop nylon">ripstop nylon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastic_film" title="Plastic film">plastic film</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> tube and rod) are used for kite making. Synthetic rope and cord (<a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyneema" title="Dyneema">dyneema</a>) are used as bridle and kite line.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg/220px-Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg/330px-Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg/440px-Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sparless_Styrofoam_kite_in_flight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sparless <a href="/wiki/Styrofoam" title="Styrofoam">styrofoam</a> kites</div></div></div>\n<p>Designs often emulate flying insects, birds, and other beasts, both real and mythical. The finest Chinese kites are made from split bamboo (usually golden bamboo), covered with silk, and hand painted. On larger kites, clever hinges and latches allow the kite to be disassembled and compactly folded for storage or transport. Cheaper mass-produced kites are often made from printed <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> rather than silk.\n</p><p>Tails are used for some <a href="/wiki/Inflatable_single-line_kite" title="Inflatable single-line kite">single-line kite</a> designs to keep the kite\'s nose pointing into the wind. Spinners and spinsocks can be attached to the flying line for visual effect. There are rotating wind socks which spin like a <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>. On large display kites these tails, spinners and spinsocks can be 50 feet (15&#160;m) long or more.\n</p><p>Modern aerobatic kites use two or four lines to allow fine control of the kite\'s angle to the wind. Traction kites may have an additional line to de-power the kite and quick-release mechanisms to disengage flyer and kite in an emergency.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_uses">Practical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kite_applications" title="Kite applications">Kite applications</a></div>\n<p>Kites have been used for human flight, military applications, science and meteorology, photography, lifting radio antennas, generating power, aerodynamics experiments, and much more.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_applications">Military applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kites have been used for <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> purposes in the past, such as  signaling, delivery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munition" title="Munition">munitions</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a>, both by lifting an observer above the field of battle and by using <a href="/wiki/Kite_aerial_photography" title="Kite aerial photography">kite aerial photography</a>.\n</p><p>Kites were first used in warfare by the Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-24">[24]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> the <i>Fire Crow</i>, a kite carrying incendiary powder, a fuse, and a burning stick of incense was developed as a weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i>, in 647 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Yu-sin" title="Kim Yu-sin">Kim Yu-sin</a>, a Korean general of <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> rallied his troops to defeat rebels by using flaming kites which also scared the enemy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Russian chronicles mention Prince <a href="/wiki/Oleg_of_Novgorod" title="Oleg of Novgorod">Oleg of Novgorod</a> use of kites during the siege of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in 906: "and he crafted horses and men of paper, armed and gilded, and lifted them into the air over the city; the Greeks saw them and feared them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Milemete" title="Walter de Milemete">Walter de Milemete</a>\'s 1326 <i>De nobilitatibus, sapientiis, et prudentiis regum</i> treatise depicts a group of knights flying kite laden with a black-powder filled firebomb over the wall of city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kites were also used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Admiral_Yi" title="Admiral Yi">Admiral Yi</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> (1392&#8211;1910) of Korea. During the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%931598)" title="Japanese invasions of Korea (1592&#8211;1598)">Japanese invasions of Korea (1592&#8211;1598)</a>, Admiral Yi commanded his navy using kites. His kites had specific markings directing his fleet to perform various orders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cody_manlifter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="292" data-file-width="250" decoding="async" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Cody_manlifter.jpg/170px-Cody_manlifter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/Cody_manlifter.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cody_manlifter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of Cody\'s "manlifter" kites in 1908</div></div></div>\n<p>In the modern era the British Army used kites to haul human lookouts into the air for observation purposes, using the kites developed by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Franklin_Cody" title="Samuel Franklin Cody">Samuel Franklin Cody</a>.  Barrage kites were used to protect shipping during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Kites were also used for anti-aircraft target practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\nKites and kytoons were used for lofting communications antenna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submarines" title="Submarines">Submarines</a> lofted observers in rotary kites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Palestinians from the <a href="/wiki/Gaza_Strip" title="Gaza Strip">Gaza Strip</a> have flown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firebomb_kite" title="Firebomb kite">firebomb kites</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Israel%E2%80%93Gaza_barrier" title="Israel&#8211;Gaza barrier">Israel&#8211;Gaza barrier</a>, setting fires on the Israeli side of the border,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> hundreds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunams" title="Dunams">dunams</a> of Israeli crop fields were burned by firebomb kites launched from Gaza, with an estimated economic loss of several millions of <a href="/wiki/Israeli_new_shekel" title="Israeli new shekel">shekels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_meteorology">Science and meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Science and meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kites have been used for scientific purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>\'s famous experiment proving that <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> is <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. Kites were the precursors to the traditional <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, and were instrumental in the development of early flying craft. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a> experimented with very large <a href="/wiki/Man-lifting_kite" title="Man-lifting kite">man-lifting kites</a>, as did the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Hargrave" title="Lawrence Hargrave">Lawrence Hargrave</a>. Kites had a historical role in lifting scientific instruments to measure atmospheric conditions for <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds" title="Francis Ronalds">Francis Ronalds</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Radcliffe_Birt" title="William Radcliffe Birt">William Radcliffe Birt</a> described a very stable kite at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Observatory" title="King\'s Observatory">Kew Observatory</a> as early as 1847 that was trialled for the purpose of supporting self-registering meteorological instruments at height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_aerials_and_light_beacons">Radio aerials and light beacons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radio aerials and light beacons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kites can be used for radio purposes, by kites carrying antennas for <a href="/wiki/Medium_frequency" title="Medium frequency">MF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low_frequency" title="Low frequency">LF</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLF" title="VLF">VLF</a>-transmitters. This method was used for the reception station of the first transatlantic transmission by <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Marconi</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moored_balloon" title="Moored balloon">Captive balloons</a> may be more convenient for such experiments, because kite-carried antennas require a lot of wind, which may be not always possible with heavy equipment and a ground conductor. It must be taken into account during experiments, that a conductor carried by a kite can lead to high voltage toward ground, which can endanger people and equipment, if suitable precautions (grounding through resistors or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonant_circuit" title="Resonant circuit">parallel resonant circuit</a> tuned to transmission frequency) are not taken.\n</p><p>Kites can be used to carry light effects such as lightsticks or battery powered lights.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kite_traction">Kite traction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kite traction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kite333.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Kite333.JPG/220px-Kite333.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Kite333.JPG/330px-Kite333.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Kite333.JPG/440px-Kite333.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kite333.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A quad-line traction kite, commonly used as a power source for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_surfing" title="Kite surfing">kite surfing</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Kites can be used to pull people and vehicles downwind. Efficient <a href="/wiki/Foil_kite" title="Foil kite">foil-type kites</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Power_kite" title="Power kite">power kites</a> can also be used to sail upwind under the same principles as used by other sailing craft, provided that lateral forces on the ground or in the water are redirected as with the keels, center boards, wheels and ice blades of traditional sailing craft. In the last two decades several kite sailing sports have become popular, such as kite buggying, kite landboarding, <a href="/wiki/Kiteboating" title="Kiteboating">kite boating</a> and kite surfing. Snow kiting has also become popular in recent years.\n</p><p>Kite sailing opens several possibilities not available in traditional sailing:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Wind speeds are greater at higher altitudes</li>\n<li>Kites may be manoeuvered dynamically which increases the force available dramatically</li>\n<li>There is no need for mechanical structures to withstand bending forces; vehicles or hulls can be very light or dispensed with all together</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Underwater_kites">Underwater kites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Underwater kites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tidal_stream_generator" title="Tidal stream generator">Underwater kites</a> are being developed to harvest renewable power from the flow of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_uses">Cultural uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cultural uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_kite_festivals" title="List of kite festivals">Kite festivals</a> are a popular form of entertainment throughout the world. They include large local events, traditional festivals which have been held for hundreds<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of years and major international festivals which bring in kite flyers from other countries  to display their unique art kites and demonstrate the latest technical kites.\n</p><p>Many countries have kite museums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> These museums may have a focus on historical kites, preserving the country&#8217;s kite traditions. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kite flying is popular in many Asian countries, where it often takes the form of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_fighting" title="Kite fighting">kite fighting</a>", in which participants try to snag each other\'s kites or cut other kites down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fighter_kite" title="Fighter kite">Fighter kites</a> are usually small, flat, flattened diamond-shaped kites made of paper and bamboo. Tails are not used on fighter kites so that agility and maneuverability are not compromised.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, kite flying is a popular game, and is known in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dari_(Eastern_Persian)" title="Dari (Eastern Persian)">Dari</a> as <i>Gudiparan Bazi</i>. Some kite fighters pass their strings through a mixture of ground glass powder and glue, which is legal. The resulting strings are very abrasive and can sever the competitor\'s strings more easily. The abrasive strings can also injure people. During the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> rule in Afghanistan, kite flying was banned, among various other recreations.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, kite flying is often known as Gudi-Bazi or Patang-bazi. Although kite flying is a popular ritual for the celebration of spring festival known as  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jashn-e-Baharaan" title="Jashn-e-Baharaan">Jashn-e-Baharaan</a> (lit. Spring Festival) or <a href="/wiki/Basant_(season)" title="Basant (season)">Basant</a>, kites are flown throughout the year. <a href="/wiki/Fighter_kite" title="Fighter kite">Kite fighting</a> is a very popular pastime all around Pakistan, but mostly in urban centers across the country (especially <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a>).  The kite fights are at their highest during the spring celebrations and the fighters enjoy competing with rivals to cut-loose the string of the others kite, this is popularly known as "Paecha". During the spring festival, kite flying competitions are held across the country and the skies are colored with kites. As people cut-loose an opponents kite, shouts of \'wo kata\' ring through the air. They reclaim the kites, after they have been cut-loose, by running after them. This is a popular ritual especially among the youth (similar to scenes depicted in the Kite Runner which is based in neighboring Afghanistan).  Kites and strings are a big business in the country and many types of strings are used: glass-coated strings, metal strings and tandi. However, kite flying was recently banned in <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab</a> due to recent motorcyclist deaths caused by glass-coated or metal kite-strings.  Kup, Patang, Guda, and Nakhlaoo are some of the kites used. They vary in balance, weight and speed.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> kites is flown as both sport and recreation. One of the most popular kites variants is from <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>. Balinese kites are unique and they has different design and forms; birds, butterflies, dragons, ships, etc. In <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, kites are flown without tails. Instead small flutes are attached allowing the wind to "hum" a musical tune. There are other forms of sound-making kites. In Bali, large bows are attached to the front of the kites to make a deep throbbing vibration, and in Malaysia row of gourds with sound-slots are used to create a whistle as the kite flies. Malaysia has also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_Museum_(Malacca)" title="Kite Museum (Malacca)">Kite Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kites are very popular in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, with the states of Gujarat , Bihar, Jharkhand, West Bengal, Rajasthan and Punjab notable for their kite fighting festivals. Highly maneuverable single-string paper and bamboo kites are flown from the rooftops while using line friction in an attempt to cut each other\'s kite lines, either by letting the cutting line loose at high speed or by pulling the line in a fast and repeated manner. During the Indian spring festival of <a href="/wiki/Makar_Sankranti" title="Makar Sankranti">Makar Sankranti</a>, near the middle of January, millions of people fly kites all over northern India.  Kite flying in Hyderabad starts a month before this, but kite flying/fighting is an important part of other celebrations, including <a href="/wiki/Republic_Day" title="Republic Day">Republic Day</a>, Independence Day, <a href="/wiki/Raksha_Bandhan" title="Raksha Bandhan">Raksha Bandhan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishvakarman#Vishwakarma_Puja" title="Vishvakarman">Viswakarma Puja day</a> in late September and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Janmashtami" title="Janmashtami">Janmashtami</a>. An international kite festival is held every year before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uttarayan" title="Uttarayan">Uttarayan</a> for three days in <a href="/wiki/Vadodara" title="Vadodara">Vadodara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surat" title="Surat">Surat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahmedabad" title="Ahmedabad">Ahmedabad</a>.\n</p><p>Kites have been flown in China since ancient times. <a href="/wiki/Weifang" title="Weifang">Weifang</a> is home to the largest kite museum in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="claim of largest needs verifying (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It also hosts an annual international kite festival on the large salt flats south of the city. There are several kite museums in Japan, UK, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, Indonesia, Taiwan, Thailand and the USA.\n</p><p>In the olden days, Malays in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, kites were used for fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Japan, kite flying is traditionally a children\'s play in New Year holidays and in the Boys\' Festival in May. South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, flying a kite is a very popular leisure activity for children, teenagers and even young adults. Mostly these are boys, and it is overwhelmingly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kite_fighting" title="Kite fighting">kite fighting</a> a game whose goal is to maneuver their own kites to cut the other persons\' kites\' strings during flight, and followed by <a href="/wiki/Kite_running" title="Kite running">kite running</a> where participants race through the streets to take the free-drifting kites. As in other countries with similar traditions, injuries are common and motorcyclists in particular need to take precautions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, kites are very popular, especially during Independence Day festivities (September 18).\nIn Peru, kites are also very popular. There are kite festival in parks and beache mostly on August. \n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, kites can be seen flown in parks and recreation areas during August which is known to be windy. It is during this month that most people, especially the young ones would fly kites.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, kites are flown at Easter, an activity in which all ethnic and religious groups participate. Kites are generally not flown at any other time of year. Kites start appearing in the sky in the weeks leading up to Easter and school children are taken to parks for the activity. It all culminates in a massive airborne celebration on Easter Monday especially in Georgetown, the capital, and other coastal areas. The history of the practice is not entirely clear but given that Easter is a Christian festival, it is said that kite flying is symbolic of the Risen Lord. Moore<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> describes the phenomenon in the 19th century as follows:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A very popular Creole pastime was the flying of kites. Easter Monday, a public holiday, was the great kite-flying day on the sea wall in Georgetown and on open lands in villages. Young and old alike, male and female, appeared to be seized by kite-flying mania. Easter 1885 serves as a good example. "The appearance of the sky all over Georgetown, but especially towards the Sea Wall, was very striking, the air being thick with kites of all shapes and sizes, covered with gaily coloured paper, all riding bravely on the strong wind.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>(His quotation is from a letter to The Creole newspaper of December 29, 1858.)</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The exact origins of the practice of kite flying (exclusively) at Easter are unclear. Brereton and Yelvington<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> speculate that kite flying was introduced by Chinese indentured immigrants to the then colony of British Guiana in the mid 19th century. The world record for the largest kite flown for at least 20 minutes is "The Flag of Kuwait".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buckland_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buckland-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The single-kite altitude record is held by a triangular-box delta kite. On 23 September 2014 a team led by Robert Moore, flew a 129 square feet (12&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) kite to 16,009 feet (4,880&#160;m) above ground level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The record altitude was reached after 8 series of attempts over a ten-year period from a remote location in western NSW, Australia. The 9.2 feet (3&#160;m) tall and 19.6 feet (6&#160;m) wide Dunton-Taylor delta kite\'s flight was controlled by a winch system using 40,682 feet (12,400&#160;m) of ultra high strength Dyneema line. The flight took about eight hours from ground and return. The height was measured with on-board GPS telemetry transmitting positional data in real time to a ground-based computer and also back-up GPS data loggers for later analysis.<sup class="reference" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: General safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Man_flying_kite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/81/Man_flying_kite.jpg/220px-Man_flying_kite.jpg" kites to fly erratically</div></div></div>\n<p>There are safety issues involved in kite-flying. Kite lines can strike and tangle on electrical power lines, causing power blackouts and running the risk of electrocuting the kite flier. Wet kite lines or wire can act as a conductor for static electricity and lightning when the weather is stormy. Kites with large surface area or powerful lift can lift kite fliers off the ground or drag them into other objects. In urban areas there is usually a ceiling on how high a kite can be flown, to prevent the kite and line infringing on the airspace of helicopters and light aircraft.  It is also possible for fighter kites to kill people, as happened in India when three spectators were killed in separate incidents during Independence Day, August, 2016&#8212;precipitating a ban on
