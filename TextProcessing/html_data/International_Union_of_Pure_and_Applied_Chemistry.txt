style="padding-right:0.6em;">Website</th><td><a class="external text" href="http://iupac.org" rel="nofollow">IUPAC.org</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</b> (<b>IUPAC</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/a&#618;/: \'i\' in \'tide\'">a&#618;</span><span title="/ju&#720;/: \'u\' in \'cute\'">ju&#720;</span><span title="\'p\' in \'pie\'">p</span><span title="/&#230;/: \'a\' in \'bad\'">&#230;</span><span title="\'k\' in \'kind\'">k</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/ju&#720;/: \'u\' in \'cute\'">ju&#720;</span></span>-/</a></span></span>) is an international federation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Adhering_Organizations" title="National Adhering Organizations">National Adhering Organizations</a> that represents chemists in individual countries. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_Science" title="International Council for Science">International Council for Science</a> (ICSU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nao_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nao-2">[2]</a></sup> IUPAC is registered in <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Z&#252;rich">Z&#252;rich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and the administrative office, known as the "IUPAC Secretariat", is in <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle_Park" title="Research Triangle Park">Research Triangle Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. This administrative office is headed by IUPAC\'s executive director,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> currently Lynn Soby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>IUPAC was established in 1919 as the successor of the International Congress of Applied Chemistry for the advancement of <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. Its members, the National Adhering Organizations, can be national <a href="/wiki/List_of_chemistry_societies" title="List of chemistry societies">chemistry societies</a>, national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences" title="Academy of Sciences">academies of sciences</a>, or other bodies representing chemists. There are fifty-four National Adhering Organizations and three Associate National Adhering Organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nao_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nao-2">[2]</a></sup> IUPAC\'s Inter-divisional Committee on Nomenclature and Symbols (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry_nomenclature" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry nomenclature">IUPAC nomenclature</a>) is the recognized world authority in developing standards for the naming of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_elements" title="Chemical elements">chemical elements</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">compounds</a>. Since its creation, IUPAC has been run by many different committees with different responsibilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-governing_committees_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-governing_committees-5">[5]</a></sup> These committees run different projects which include standardizing <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature" title="Nomenclature">nomenclature</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interdivisional_Committee_on_Terminology_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Interdivisional_Committee_on_Terminology-6">[6]</a></sup> finding ways to bring chemistry to the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHEMRAWN_activities_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHEMRAWN_activities-7">[7]</a></sup> and publishing works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry_Editorial_Advisory_Board_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry_Editorial_Advisory_Board-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Project_Committee_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Project_Committee-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evaluation_Committee_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evaluation_Committee-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>IUPAC is best known for its works standardizing nomenclature in chemistry and other fields of science, but IUPAC has publications in many fields including chemistry, biology and physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_IUPAC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_IUPAC-11">[11]</a></sup> Some important work IUPAC has done in these fields includes standardizing <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a> base sequence code names; publishing books for environmental scientists, chemists, and physicists; and improving education in science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_IUPAC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_IUPAC-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IYC_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IYC-12">[12]</a></sup> IUPAC is also known for standardizing the atomic weights of the elements through one of its oldest standing committees, the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Isotopic_Abundances_and_Atomic_Weights" title="Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights">Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights</a> (CIAAW).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" history</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg"><img alt="A black and white image of a bald man in a dark outfit, with a bushy white beard and moustache" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1234" data-file-width="1122" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg/170px-Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg/255px-Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg/340px-Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frkekul%C3%A9.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Friedrich August Kekul&#233; von Stradonitz</div></div></div>\n<p>The need for an international standard for chemistry was first addressed in 1860 by a committee headed by German scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_Kekul%C3%A9_von_Stradonitz" title="Friedrich August Kekul&#233; von Stradonitz">Friedrich August Kekul&#233; von Stradonitz</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Working_group" title="Working group">committee</a> was the first international conference to create an international naming system for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_IUPAC_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_IUPAC-11">[11]</a></sup> The ideas that were formulated in that conference evolved into the official <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_IUPAC_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_IUPAC-11">[11]</a></sup> IUPAC stands as a legacy of this meeting, making it one of the most important historical international collaborations of <a href="/wiki/List_of_chemistry_societies" title="List of chemistry societies">chemistry societies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_IUPAC_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_IUPAC-11">[11]</a></sup> Since this time, IUPAC has been the official organization held with the responsibility of updating and maintaining official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_nomenclature" title="Organic nomenclature">organic nomenclature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_The_Central_Science_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_The_Central_Science-13">[13]</a></sup> IUPAC as such was established in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> One notable country excluded from this early IUPAC is Germany. Germany\'s exclusion was a result of prejudice towards Germans by the Allied powers after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wissenschaften_und_Wissenschaftspolitik:_Bestandsaufnahmen_zu_Formationen,_Br&#252;chen_und_Kontinuit&#228;ten_im_Deutschland_des_20._Jahrhunderts_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wissenschaften_und_Wissenschaftspolitik:_Bestandsaufnahmen_zu_Formationen,_Br&#252;chen_und_Kontinuit&#228;ten_im_Deutschland_des_20._Jahrhunderts-15">[15]</a></sup> Germany was finally admitted into IUPAC during 1929. However, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> was removed from IUPAC during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.\n</p><p>During World War II, IUPAC was affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied powers</a>, but had little involvement during the war effort itself. After the war, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> were eventually readmitted to IUPAC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wissenschaften_und_Wissenschaftspolitik:_Bestandsaufnahmen_zu_Formationen,_Br&#252;chen_und_Kontinuit&#228;ten_im_Deutschland_des_20._Jahrhunderts_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wissenschaften_und_Wissenschaftspolitik:_Bestandsaufnahmen_zu_Formationen,_Br&#252;chen_und_Kontinuit&#228;ten_im_Deutschland_des_20._Jahrhunderts-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osullivan_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-osullivan-16">[16]</a></sup> Since World War II, IUPAC has been focused on standardizing nomenclature and methods in science without interruption.\n</p><p>In 2016, IUPAC denounced the use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical weapon</a>. The organization pointed out their concerns in a letter to Ahmet &#220;z&#252;mc&#252;, the director of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_the_Prohibition_of_Chemical_Weapons" title="Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons">Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons</a> (OPCW), in regards to the practice of utilizing chlorine for <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Civil_War" title="Syrian Civil War">weapon usage in Syria</a> among other locations. The letter stated, "Our organizations deplore the use of chlorine in this manner. The indiscriminate attacks, possibly carried out by a member state of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Weapons_Convention" title="Chemical Weapons Convention">Chemical Weapons Convention</a> (CWC), is of concern to chemical scientists and engineers around the globe and we stand ready to support your mission of implementing the CWC." According to the CWC, "the use, stockpiling, distribution, development or storage of any chemical weapons is forbidden by any of the 192 state party signatories."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Committees_and_governance">Committees and governance</span></h2>\n<p>IUPAC is governed by several committees that all have different responsibilities. This journal debuted in 1960. The goal statement for <i>Pure and Applied Chemistry</i> is to "publish highly topical and credible works at the forefront of all aspects of pure and applied chemistry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_journal_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-official_journal-52">[52]</a></sup> The journal itself is available by subscription, but older issues are available in the archive on IUPAC\'s website.\n</p><p><i>Pure and Applied Chemistry</i> was created as a central way to publish IUPAC endorsed articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_journal_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-First_journal-53">[53]</a></sup> Before its creation, IUPAC did not have a quick, official way to distribute new chemistry information.\n</p><p>Its creation was first suggested at the Paris IUPAC Meeting of 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_journal_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-First_journal-53">[53]</a></sup> During this meeting the commercial publisher of the journal was discussed and decided on. In 1959, IUPAC Pure and Applied Chemistry Editorial Advisory Board was created and put in charge of the journal. The idea of one journal being a definitive place for a vast amount of chemistry was difficult for the committee to grasp at first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First_journal_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-First_journal-53">[53]</a></sup> However, it was decided that the journal would reprint old journal editions to keep all chemistry knowledge available.\n</p>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><i><b>Compendium of Chemical Terminology</b></i>\n</td>\n<td>\n<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compendium_of_Chemical_Terminology" title="Compendium of Chemical Terminology">Compendium of Chemical Terminology</a></i>, also known as the "Gold Book", was originally worked on by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gold_(chemist)" title="Victor Gold (chemist)">Victor Gold</a>.
