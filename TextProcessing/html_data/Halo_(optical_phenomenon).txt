halo</a></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Halo</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#7941;&#955;&#969;&#962;</span>, <i>hal&#333;s</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the name for a family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_phenomenon" title="Optical phenomenon">optical phenomena</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> interacting with <a href="/wiki/Ice_crystals" title="Ice crystals">ice crystals</a> suspended in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">the atmosphere</a>. Halos can have many forms, ranging from colored or white rings to arcs and spots in the sky. Many of these appear near the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> or <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, but others occur elsewhere or even in the opposite part of the sky. Among the best known halo types are the <b>circular halo</b> (properly called the <a href="/wiki/22%C2%B0_halo" title="22&#176; halo">22&#176; halo</a>), <a href="/wiki/Light_pillar" title="Light pillar">light pillars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sun_dog" title="Sun dog">sun dogs</a>, but many others occur; some are fairly common while others are (extremely) rare.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ice_crystals" title="Ice crystals">ice crystals</a> responsible for halos are typically suspended in <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus clouds</a> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> (5&#8211;10&#160;km (3.1&#8211;6.2&#160;mi)), but in cold weather they can also float near the ground, in which case they are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">diamond dust</a>. The particular shape and orientation of the crystals are responsible for the type of halo observed. <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">Light</a> is <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Ice_crystals" title="Ice crystals">ice crystals</a> and may split into <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">colors</a> because of <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersion</a>. The crystals behave like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, refracting and reflecting light between their faces, sending shafts of light in particular directions.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_optics" title="Atmospheric optics">Atmospheric optical phenomena</a> like halos were used as part of weather lore, which was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a> means of <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a> before <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> was developed. They often do indicate that rain will fall within the next 24 hours, since the cirrostratus clouds that cause them can signify an approaching frontal system.\n</p><p>Other common types of optical phenomena involving water droplets rather than ice crystals include the <a href="/wiki/Glory_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Glory (optical phenomenon)">glory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> had mentioned halos and parhelia, in antiquity, the first European descriptions these for centuries, the first reference being a section of the "Official History of the Chin Dynasty" (<i>Chin Shu</i>) in 637, on the "Ten Haloes", giving technical terms for 26 solar halo phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="V.C3.A4dersolstavlan"></span><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>; "The Sundog Painting", literally "The Weather Sun Painting") is arguably also one of the oldest known depictions of a halo display, including a pair of <a href="/wiki/Sun_dog" title="Sun dog">sun dogs</a>. For two hours in the morning of 20 April 1535, the skies over the city were filled with white circles and arcs crossing the sky, while additional suns (i.e., sun dogs) appeared around the sun.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_pillar">Light pillar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Light pillar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Light_pillar" title="Light pillar">Light pillar</a></div>\n<p>A light pillar, or sun pillar, appears as a vertical pillar or column of light rising from the sun near sunset or sunrise, though it can appear below the sun, particularly if the observer is at a high elevation or altitude.  Hexagonal plate- and column-shaped ice crystals cause the phenomenon.  Plate crystals generally cause pillars only when the sun is within 6 degrees of the horizon; column crystals can cause a pillar when the sun is as high as 20 degrees above the horizon.  The crystals tend to orient themselves near-horizontally as they fall or float through the air, and the width and visibility of a sun pillar depend on crystal alignment.\n</p><p>Light pillars can also form around the moon, and around street lights or other bright lights.  Pillars forming from ground-based light sources may appear much taller than those associated with the sun or moon.  Since the observer is closer to the light source, crystal orientation matters less in the formation of these pillars.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circular_halo">Circular halo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Circular halo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/22%C2%B0_halo" title="22&#176; halo">22&#176; halo</a> and <a href="/wiki/46%C2%B0_halo" title="46&#176; halo">46&#176; halo</a></div>\n<p>Among the best-known halos is the <a href="/wiki/22%C2%B0_halo" title="22&#176; halo">22&#176; halo</a>, often just called "halo", which appears as a large ring around the Sun or Moon with a radius of about 22&#176; (roughly the width of an outstretched hand at arm\'s length). The ice crystals that cause the 22&#176; halo are oriented semi-randomly in the atmosphere, in contrast to the horizontal orientation required for some other halos such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_dogs" title="Sun dogs">sun dogs</a> and light pillars. As a result of the optical properties of the ice crystals involved, no light is reflected towards the inside of the ring, leaving the sky noticeably darker than the sky around it, and giving it the impression of a "hole in the sky".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atoptics_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atoptics-3">[3]</a></sup> The 22&#176; halo is not to be confused with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corona_(meteorology)" title="Corona (meteorology)">corona</a>, which is a different optical phenomenon caused by water droplets rather than ice crystals, and which has the appearance of a multicolored disk rather than a ring.\n</p><p>Other haloes can form at <a href="/wiki/46%C2%B0_halo" title="46&#176; halo">46&#176; to the sun</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Parhelic_circle" title="Parhelic circle">at the horizon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Circumzenithal_arc" title="Circumzenithal arc">around the zenith</a>, and can appear as full haloes or incomplete arcs.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Bottlinger.27s_ring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bottlinger\'s_ring">Bottlinger\'s ring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bottlinger\'s ring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <i>Bottlinger\'s ring</i> is a rare type of halo that is elliptical instead of circular. It has a small diameter, which makes it very difficult to see in the Sun\'s glare and more likely to be spotted around the dimmer <a href="/wiki/Subsun" title="Subsun">Subsun</a>, often seen from mountain tops or airplanes. Bottlinger\'s rings are not well understood yet. It is suggested that they are formed by very flat <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramidal</a> ice crystals with faces at uncommonly low angles, suspended horizontally in the atmosphere. These precise and physically problematic requirements would explain why the halo is very rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atoptics-bottlinger\'s-ring_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-atoptics-bottlinger\'s-ring-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_names">Other names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Cornish" title="Anglo-Cornish">Anglo-Cornish</a> dialect of English, a halo round the sun or the moon is called a <i>cock\'s eye</i> and is a <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">token</a> of bad weather. The term is related to the <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> word <i>kog-heol</i> (sun cock) which has the same meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nance_&#8211;_Cornish_Sea-Words_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nance_&#8211;_Cornish_Sea-Words-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, the halo round the sun is called <i>Indrasabha</i> with a connotation of the assembly court of  <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Lord Indra</a> &#8211; the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_deities" title="Hindu deities">Hindu god</a> of lightning, thunder and rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-THT-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_halos">Artificial halos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Artificial halos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The natural phenomena may be reproduced artificially by several means. Firstly, by computer simulations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> or secondly by experimental means. Regarding the latter, one may either take a single crystal and rotate it around the appropriate axis/axes, or take a chemical approach. A still further and more indirect experimental approach is to find analogous refraction geometries.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogous_refraction_approach">Analogous refraction approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Analogous refraction approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Artificial_CZA.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="840" data-file-width="1194" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Artificial_CZA.png/220px-Artificial_CZA.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Artificial_CZA.png/330px-Artificial_CZA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Artificial_CZA.png/440px-Artificial_CZA.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Artificial_CZA.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Analogous refraction demonstration experiment for the Circumzenithal Arc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtCZA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArtCZA-9">[9]</a></sup> Here, it is mistakenly labelled as an artificial rainbow in Gilberts book<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert1920_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert1920-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div><p> This approach employs the fact that in some cases the average geometry of refraction through an ice crystal may be imitated / mimicked via the refraction through another geometrical object. In this way, the <a href="/wiki/Circumzenithal_arc" title="Circumzenithal arc">Circumzenithal arc</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Circumhorizontal_arc" title="Circumhorizontal arc">Circumhorizontal arc</a> and the suncave Parry arcs may be recreated by refraction through rotationally symmetric (i.e. non-prismatic) static bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtCZA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArtCZA-9">[9]</a></sup> A particularly simple table-top experiment reproduces artificially the colorful circumzenithal and circumhorizontal arcs using a water glass only. The refraction through the cylinder of water turns out to be (almost) identical to the rotationally averaged refraction through an upright hexagonal ice crystal / plate-oriented crystals, thereby creating vividly colored circumzenithal and the circumhorizontal arcs. In fact, the water glass experiment is often confused as representing a rainbow and has been around at least since 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert1920_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert1920-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following Huygens\' idea of the (false) mechanism of the 22&#176; parhelia, one may also illuminate (from the side) a water-filled cylindrical glass with an inner central obstruction of half the glasses\' diameter to achieve upon projection on a screen an appearance which closely resembles parhelia (cf. footnote [39] in Ref.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtCZA_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ArtCZA-9">[9]</a></sup> or see here<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DIYExperiments_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-DIYExperiments-11">[11]</a></sup>), i.e. an inner red edge transitioning into a white band at larger angles on both sides of the direct transmission direction. However, while the visual match is close, this particular experiment does not involve a fake caustic mechanism and is thus no real analogue.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_approaches">Chemical approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chemical approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest chemical recipes to generate artificial halos has been put forward by Brewster and studied further by A. Cornu in 1889.\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The idea was to generate crystals by precipitation of a salt solution. The innumerable small crystals hereby generated will then, upon illumination with light, cause halos corresponding to the particular crystal geometry and the orientation / alignment. Several recipes exist and continue to be discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tammer1998_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tammer1998-13">[13]</a></sup> Rings are a common outcome of such experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> But also Parry arcs have been artificially produced in this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_approaches">Mechanical approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mechanical approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_axis">Single axis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Single axis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The earliest experimental studies on halo phenomena have been attributed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> to Auguste Bravais in 1847.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bravais_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bravais-17">[17]</a></sup> Bravais used an equilateral glass prism which he spun around its vertical axis. When illuminated by parallel white light, this produced an artificial <a href="/wiki/Parhelic_circle" title="Parhelic circle">Parhelic circle</a> and many of the embedded parhelia. Similarly, A. Wegener used hexagonal rotating crystals to produce artificial subparhelia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In a more recent version of this experiment, many more embedded parhelia have been found using commercially available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> hexagonal BK7 glass crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Simple experiments like these can be used for educational purposes and demonstration experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tammer1998_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tammer1998-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selmke2015_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Selmke2015-21">[21]</a></sup> Unfortunately, using glass crystals one cannot reproduce the circumzenithal arc or the circumhorizontal arc due to total internal reflections preventing the required ray-paths when <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle n&lt;{\\sqrt {2}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>n</mi>\n<mo>&lt;</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<msqrt>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</msqrt>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle n&lt;{\\sqrt {2}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\displaystyle n&lt;{\\sqrt {2}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9673b3a7124b4a0175e8726125642ce9f449985" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:7.592ex; height:3.009ex;"/></span>.\n</p><p>Even earlier than Bravais, the Italian scientist F. Other crystals such as NaF also have a refractive index close to ice and have been used in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_axes">Two axes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Two axes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In order to produce artificial halos such as the tangent arcs or the circumscribed halo one should rotate a single columnar hexagonal crystal about 2 axes. Similarly, the Lowitz arcs can be created by rotating a single plate crystal about two axes. This can be done by engineered halo machines. The first such machine was constructed in 2003;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> several more followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SelmkeSelmke2016_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-SelmkeSelmke2016-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gro&#223;mann2015_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gro&#223;mann2015-29">[29]</a></sup> Putting such machines inside spherical projection screens, and by the principle of the so-called sky transform,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the analogy is nearly perfect. A realization using micro-versions of the aforementioned machines produces authentic distortion-free projections of such complex artificial halos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtCZA_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-ArtCZA-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoredPanda_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoredPanda-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SelmkeSelmke2016_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-SelmkeSelmke2016-25">[25]</a></sup> Finally, superposition of several images and projections produced by such halo machines may be combined to create a single image. The resulting superposition image is then a representation of complex natural halo displays containing many different orientation sets of ice prisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoredPanda_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoredPanda-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SelmkeSelmke2016_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-SelmkeSelmke2016-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_axes">Three axes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halo_(optical_phenomenon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Three axes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The experimental reproduction of circular halos is the most difficult using a single crystal only, while it is the simplest and typically achieved one using chemical recipes.
