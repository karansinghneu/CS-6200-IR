object passes in closer proximity to another, usually a spacecraft maneuver in space exploration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Spacecraft which are specifically designed for this purpose are known as flyby spacecraft, although the term has also been used in regard to <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> flybys of Earth for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Two common goals are for exploration or for a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a>.\n</p><p>One important subconcept of a flyby in spaceflight, is the time and distance of closest approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This distance is often reported, along with a time in regards to flybys in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a title="Edit section: Spacecraft flyby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Flyby maneuvers can be conducted with a planet or non-planet object such as a comet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">Planetary flybys</a> have occurred with <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> for example:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_Earth_flybys" title="List of Earth flybys">List of Earth flybys</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mars_flyby" title="Mars flyby">Mars flyby</a></li></ul>\n<p>An example of a comet flyby is when <a href="/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer" title="International Cometary Explorer">International Cometary Explorer</a> (formerly ISEE-3) passed about 4800 miles (7,800 kilometers) from the <a href="/wiki/Comet_nucleus" title="Comet nucleus">nucleus</a> of <a href="/wiki/21P/Giacobini%E2%80%93Zinner" title="21P/Giacobini&#8211;Zinner">Comet Giacobini-Zinner</a> in September 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another application of the flyby is of Earth\'s Moon, usually called a <i>lunar flyby</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission-2" title="Exploration Mission-2">Orion EM-2 mission</a> has a mission design that would include a lunar flyby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div rendezvous, where a spacecraft does not enter orbit but rendezvous before or after a flyby of the planet with another spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree2001flyby_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-portree2001flyby-12">[12]</a></sup> Mars flyby rendezvous was evaluated at NASA\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_Spacecraft_Center" title="Manned Spacecraft Center">Manned Spacecraft Center</a> in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree2001flyby_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-portree2001flyby-12">[12]</a></sup> At that time NASA developed designs for a combination of a Mars lander, short-stay surface habitat, and ascent vehicle called a <a href="/wiki/Mars_Excursion_Module" title="Mars Excursion Module">Mars Excursion Module</a> (MEM); the ascent stage performed the rendezvous with another spacecraft that did a flyby of Mars without entering orbit or landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree2001flyby_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-portree2001flyby-12">[12]</a></sup> Compared to MOR, a flyby rendezvous means one spacecraft does not have to orbit Mars, so the resources needed on a return journey to Earth are not taken in and out of Mars orbit for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree2001flyby_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-portree2001flyby-12">[12]</a></sup>  (See also <a href="/wiki/Mars_cycler" title="Mars cycler">Mars cycler</a>)\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mariner_4" title="Mariner 4">Mariner IV</a> flyby of Mars in July 1965 returned more accurate atmospheric data about Mars and much closer views of its surface then previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired12_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired12-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mariner_6_and_7" title="Mariner 6 and 7">Mariner 6 and Mariner 7</a> flyby of Mars in 1969 caused another breakthrough in knowledge about the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Mariner 6 &amp; 7 infrared radiometer results from he flyby showed that the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a> was composed mostly of carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), and they were also able to detect trace amounts <a href="/wiki/Water_on_Mars" title="Water on Mars">water on the surface of Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, the twin <a href="/wiki/Mars_Cube_One" title="Mars Cube One">Mars Cube One</a> performed a flyby to relay communication for <a href="/wiki/InSight" title="InSight">InSight</a> lander EDL (they were launched towards Mars with the cruise stage carrying the InSight lander).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Both MarCOs reached Mars and successfully relayed data during the Entry, Descent, and Landing phase of Insight on November 26, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Kuiper belt</dt></dl>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New
