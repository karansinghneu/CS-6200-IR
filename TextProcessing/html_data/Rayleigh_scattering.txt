reddening of the Sun at <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Rayleigh scattering</b> (pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/e&#618;/: \'a\' in \'face\'">e&#618;</span><span title="\'l\' in \'lie\'">l</span><span title="/i/: \'y\' in \'happy\'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">RAY</span>-lee</i></a>), named after the British physicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh" title="Lord Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> (John William Strutt),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the predominantly <a href="/wiki/Elastic_scattering" title="Elastic scattering">elastic scattering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> by particles much smaller than the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the radiation. Rayleigh scattering does not change the state of material and is, hence, a <a href="/wiki/Parametric_process_(optics)" title="Parametric process (optics)">parametric process</a>. The particles may be individual atoms or molecules. It can occur when light travels through transparent solids and liquids, and is most prominently seen in <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>. Rayleigh scattering results from the electric <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> of the particles. The oscillating electric field of a light wave acts on the charges within a particle, causing them to move at the same frequency. The particle therefore becomes a small radiating dipole whose radiation we see as scattered light. This radiation is integral part of the photon and no excitation or deexcitation occurs.\n</p><p>Rayleigh scattering of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> causes <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">diffuse sky radiation</a>, which is the reason for the blue color of the <a href="/wiki/Daytime" title="Daytime">daytime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Golden_hour_(photography)" title="Golden hour (photography)">yellowish</a> to reddish hue of the low <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.\n</p><p>For wave frequencies well below the <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a> frequency of the scattering particle (normal <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_relation" title="Dispersion relation">dispersion</a> regime), the amount of scattering is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inversely_proportional" title="Inversely proportional">inversely proportional</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_power" title="Fourth power">fourth power</a> of the wavelength.\n</p><p>Rayleigh scattering of molecular nitrogen and oxygen in the atmosphere includes elastic scattering as well as the inelastic contribution from rotational <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering" title="Raman scattering">Raman scattering</a> in air, since the changes in wavenumber of the scattered photon are typically smaller than 50&#160;cm<sup>&#8722;1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This can lead to changes in the rotational state of the molecules. Furthermore, the inelastic contribution has the same wavelengths dependency as the elastic part.\n</p><p>Scattering by particles similar to, or larger than, the wavelength of light is typically treated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mie_theory" title="Mie theory">Mie theory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Discrete_dipole_approximation" title="Discrete dipole approximation">discrete dipole approximation</a> and other computational techniques. Rayleigh scattering applies to particles that are small with respect to wavelengths of light, and that are optically "soft" (i.e., with a refractive index close to 1). On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Anomalous_diffraction_theory" title="Anomalous diffraction theory">anomalous diffraction theory</a> applies to optically soft but larger particles.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1859, while attempting to determine whether any contaminants remained in the purified air he used for infrared experiments, <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> discovered that bright light scattering off nanoscopic particulates was faintly blue-tinted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He conjectured that a similar scattering of sunlight gave the sky its <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">blue hue</a>, but he could not explain the preference for blue light, nor could atmospheric dust explain the intensity of the sky\'s color.\n</p><p>In 1871, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh" title="Lord Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> published two papers on the color and polarization of skylight to quantify <a href="/wiki/Tyndall_effect" title="Tyndall effect">Tyndall\'s effect</a> in water droplets in terms of the tiny particulates\' volumes and <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive indices</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1881 with the benefit of <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>\'s 1865 <a href="/wiki/History_of_Maxwell%27s_equations" title="History of Maxwell\'s equations">proof of the electromagnetic nature of light</a>, he showed that his equations followed from electromagnetism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1899, he showed that they applied to individual molecules, with terms containing particulate volumes and refractive indices replaced with terms for molecular <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span The picture on the right is shot through a <a href="/wiki/Polarizing_filter_(photography)" title="Polarizing filter (photography)">polarizing filter</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Polarizer" title="Polarizer">polarizer</a> transmits light that is <a href="/wiki/Linear_polarization" title="Linear polarization">linearly polarized</a> in a specific direction.</div></div></div>\n<p>The strong wavelength dependence of the scattering (~<i>&#955;</i><sup>&#8722;4</sup>) means that shorter (<a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>) wavelengths are scattered more strongly than longer (<a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>) wavelengths. This results in the indirect blue light coming from all regions of the sky.  Rayleigh scattering is a good approximation of the manner in which light scattering occurs within various media for which scattering particles have a small size (<a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameter</a>).\n</p><p>A portion of the beam of light coming from the sun scatters off molecules of gas and other small particles in the atmosphere. Here, Rayleigh scattering primarily occurs through <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>\'s interaction with randomly located air molecules. It is this scattered light that gives the surrounding sky its brightness and its color. As previously stated, Rayleigh scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of wavelength, so that shorter wavelength violet and blue light will scatter more than the longer wavelengths (yellow and especially red light). However, the Sun, like any star, has its own spectrum and so <i>I</i><sub>0</sub> in the scattering formula above is not constant but falls away in the violet. In addition the oxygen in the Earth\'s atmosphere absorbs wavelengths at the edge of the ultra-violet region of the spectrum.  The resulting color, which appears like a pale blue, actually is a mixture of all the scattered colors, mainly blue and green.  Conversely, glancing toward the sun, the colors that were not scattered away &#8212; the longer wavelengths such as red and yellow light &#8212; are directly visible, giving the sun itself a slightly yellowish hue. Viewed from space, however, the sky is black and the sun is white.\n</p><p>The reddening of the sun is intensified when it is near the horizon because the light being received directly from it must pass through more of the atmosphere. The effect is further increased because the sunlight must pass through a greater proportion of the atmosphere nearer the earth\'s surface, where it is denser. This removes a significant proportion of the shorter wavelength (blue) and medium wavelength (green) light from the direct path to the observer. The remaining unscattered light is therefore mostly of longer wavelengths and appears more red.\n</p><p>Some of the scattering can also be from sulfate particles. For years after large <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">Plinian eruptions</a>, the blue cast of the sky is notably brightened by the persistent sulfate load of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratospheric" title="Stratospheric">stratospheric</a> gases. Some works of the artist <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a> may owe their vivid red colours to the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">Mount Tambora</a> in his lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zerefos_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-zerefos-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In locations with little <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>, the moonlit night sky is also blue, because moonlight is reflected sunlight, with a slightly lower <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature" title="Color temperature">color temperature</a> due to the brownish color of the moon. The moonlit sky is not perceived as blue, however, because at low light levels human vision comes mainly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rod_cells" title="Rod cells">rod cells</a> that do not produce any color perception (<a href="/wiki/Purkinje_effect" title="Purkinje effect">Purkinje effect</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
