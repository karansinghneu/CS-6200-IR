href="/wiki/File:Soviet_moonrover.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soviet lunar rover</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Lunokhod</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1051;&#1091;&#1085;&#1086;&#1093;&#1086;&#1076;</span>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Russian" title="Help:IPA/Russian">[l&#650;n&#592;&#712;xot]</a></span>, "Moonwalker") was a series of <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lunar_rover" title="Lunar rover">lunar rovers</a> designed to <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">land on the Moon</a> between 1969 and 1977. Lunokhod 1 was the first roving remote-controlled robot to land on another world.\n</p><p>The 1969 Lunokhod 1A (Lunokhod 0, Lunokhod No.&#160;201) was destroyed during launch, the 1970 <i><a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">Lunokhod 1</a></i> and the 1973 <i><a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">Lunokhod 2</a></i> landed on the moon, and <i>Lunokhod 3</i> (Lunokhod No.&#160;205, planned for 1977) was never launched. The successful missions were in operation concurrently with the <a href="/wiki/Zond_program" title="Zond program">Zond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna</a> series of Moon flyby, <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiter</a> and landing missions.\n</p><p>The Lunokhods were primarily designed to support the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_manned_lunar_programs" title="Soviet manned lunar programs">Soviet manned moon missions</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_race" title="Moon race">Moon race</a>. Instead, they were used as remote-controlled <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robots</a> for exploration of the lunar surface and return its pictures after the successful <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> manned lunar landings and cancellation of the Soviet manned moon program.\n</p><p>The Lunokhods were transported to the lunar surface by <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna</a> spacecraft, which were launched by <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a> rockets. The moon lander part of the Luna spacecraft for Lunokhods was similar to the one for <a href="/wiki/Sample-return_mission" title="Sample-return mission">sample-return missions</a>. The Lunokhods were designed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Kemurdzhian" title="Alexander Kemurdzhian">Alexander Kemurdzhian</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin" title="Lavochkin">Lavochkin</a>.\n</p><p>Not until the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a> was another remote-controlled vehicle put on an extraterrestrial body.  In 2010, nearly forty years after the 1971 loss of signal from <i>Lunokhod 1</i>, the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter</a> photographed its tracks and final location, and researchers, using a telescopic pulsed-laser rangefinder, detected the robot\'s <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">retroreflector</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bleicher_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bleicher-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TNA-400.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/TNA-400.jpg/200px-TNA-400.jpg" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TNA-400.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parabolic dish TNA-400 and abandoned NIP-10</div></div></div>\n<p>Lunokhod\'s original primary mission was the back-up for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_manned_lunar_programs" title="Soviet manned lunar programs">L3 manned moon expeditions</a> and for the later <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(moonbase)" title="Zvezda (moonbase)">Zvezda</a> <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">lunar base</a>.\n</p><p>For mission safety, weeks before the manned mission on <a href="/wiki/LK_(spacecraft)" title="LK (spacecraft)">LK lander</a>, an LK-R lander in unmanned L3 lunar expedition complex and two Lunokhod automated rovers would be sent to the Moon for preliminary study of surface around LK-R and LK sites, for work as radio beacons for precision landings of LK-R and LK, and for visual estimate of status of them. LK-R used as a reserve escape craft in case of disability to start from Moon of LK and Lunokhods used by cosmonaut for transfer to LK-R in necessity and for regular research. This manned version of Lunokhod were additionally equipped with oxygen stock with hose socket, standing pads and manual control for the cosmonaut in front part.\n</p><p>In mid-1968, at the facility KIP-10 or NIP-10 (&#1050;&#1048;&#1055;-10 or &#1053;&#1048;&#1055;-10)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in the secret <a href="/wiki/Urban-type_settlement" title="Urban-type settlement">village</a> Shkolnoye (<a class="extiw" href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A8%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%B5_(%D0%9A%D1%80%D1%8B%D0%BC)" title="ru:&#1064;&#1082;&#1086;&#1083;&#1100;&#1085;&#1086;&#1077; (&#1050;&#1088;&#1099;&#1084;)">ru:&#1064;&#1082;&#1086;&#1083;&#1100;&#1085;&#1086;&#1077; (&#1050;&#1088;&#1099;&#1084;)</a> - <a href="/wiki/Closed_city" title="Closed city">closed town</a> Simferopol-28), near <a href="/wiki/Simferopol" title="Simferopol">Simferopol</a>, a lunodrom (&#1083;&#1091;&#1085;&#1086;&#1076;&#1088;&#1086;&#1084; - moondrome) was built. It covered an area of one hectare (120 meters by 70 meters) and was very similar to some parts of the lunar surface. It was constructed using more than 3,000 cubic meters of soil, and included 54 craters up to 16 m in diameter and around about 160 rocks of various sizes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The whole area was surrounded with bricks, painted in gray and black. It was used to analyze problems with the Lunokhod chassis and cosmonaut\'s skill to control one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Closed town Simferopol-28 contained the most significant tracking facility in the Soviet Union, having the largest number of antennas, the largest area, and the most personnel of any of the Soviet tracking facilities. The facility was one of a network of ten facilities which contain earth satellite vehicle tracking equipment and provide command/control for Soviet near-space civil and military events. Additionally, this facility supported <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">all lunar programs</a> of Soviet Union, in association with the <a href="/wiki/Pluton_(complex)" title="Pluton (complex)">Evpatoria Deep Space Tracking Facility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At least four complete vehicles were constructed, with the serial numbers 201, 203, 204 and 205.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunokhod_201">Lunokhod 201</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lunokhod 201">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After years of secret engineering development and training, the first Lunokhod (vehicle 8&#1045;&#1051;&#8470;201) was launched on February 19, 1969. Within a few seconds the rocket disintegrated and the first Lunokhod was lost.  The rest of the world did not learn of the rocket\'s valuable payload until years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> The failure resulted in the radioactive heat source, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polonium_210" title="Polonium 210">polonium 210</a>, being spread over a large region of Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eneryspace_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-eneryspace-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunokhod_1"><i>Lunokhod 1</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunokhod 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">Lunokhod 1</a></div>\n<p>After the destruction of the original Lunokhod, Soviet engineers began work immediately on another lunar vehicle.  <i><a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">Lunokhod 1</a></i> (vehicle 8&#1045;&#1051;&#8470;203) was the first of two unmanned lunar rovers successfully landed on the Moon by the Soviet Union as part of its Lunokhod program. The spacecraft which carried <i>Lunokhod 1</i> was named <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_17" title="Luna 17">Luna 17</a></i>. <i>Lunokhod 1</i> was the first roving remote-controlled robot to land on another world.\n</p><p><i>Luna 17</i> was launched on November 10, 1970 at 14:44:01 UTC. After reaching Earth <a href="/wiki/Parking_orbit" title="Parking orbit">parking orbit</a>, the final stage of <i>Luna 17</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s launching rocket fired to place it into a trajectory towards the Moon (November 10, 1970 at 14:54 UTC). After two course correction manoeuvres (on November 12 and 14) it entered lunar orbit on November 15, 1970 at 22:00 UTC.\n</p><p>The spacecraft soft-landed on the Moon in the <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">Sea of Rains</a> on November 17, 1970 at 03:47 UTC. The lander had dual ramps from which the payload, <i>Lunokhod 1</i>, could descend to the lunar surface. At 06:28 UT the rover moved down the ramps and onto the Moon.\n</p><p>To be able to work in a vacuum a special fluoride based lubricant was used for the rover\'s mechanical parts, and the electric motors, one in each wheel hub, were enclosed in pressurised containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The rover ran during the lunar day, stopping occasionally to recharge its batteries via the solar panels. At night the rover hibernated until the next sunrise, heated by the <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_heater_unit" title="Radioisotope heater unit">radioisotope heater unit</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rover_description">Rover description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a compartment with a large convex lid on eight independently powered wheels. Its length was 2.3 metres. <i>Lunokhod 1</i> was equipped with a cone-shaped antenna, a highly directional <a href="/wiki/Helical_antenna" title="Helical antenna">helical antenna</a>, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_cameras" title="Television cameras">television cameras</a>, and special extendable devices to impact the lunar soil for density measurements and mechanical property tests.\n</p><p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_spectrometer" title="X-ray spectrometer">X-ray spectrometer</a>, an <a href="/wiki/X-ray_telescope" title="X-ray telescope">X-ray telescope</a>, <b>Cosmic Ray Detector</b>, and a <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">Laser</a> device were also included. The vehicle was powered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> which were recharged during the lunar day by a <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cell</a> array mounted on the underside of the lid. During the lunar nights, the lid was closed and a <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>-210 heat source kept the internal components at <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>.\n</p><p>The rover stood 135&#160;cm (4&#160;ft 5 in) high and had a mass of 840&#160;kg (1,850&#160;lb). It was about 170&#160;cm (5&#160;ft 7 in) long and 160&#160;cm (4&#160;ft 11 in) wide and had eight wheels each with an independent suspension, motor and brake. The rover had two speeds, approximately 1 and 2&#160;km/h (0.6 and 1.2&#160;mph).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Payload">Payload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Payload">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The rover\'s payload included cameras (two TV and four panoramic telephotometers), a RIFMA X-ray fluorescence spectrometer, an RT-1 X-ray telescope, a PrOP odometer/penetrometer, a RV-2N radiation detector, and a TL laser retroreflector.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lunokhod_2"><i>Lunokhod 2</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lunokhod 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">Lunokhod 2</a></div>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">Lunokhod 2</a></i> (vehicle 8&#1045;&#1051;&#8470;204) was the second and more advanced of the two unmanned lunar rovers landed on the Moon by the Soviet Union as part of the Lunokhod program. The launcher put the spacecraft into Earth parking orbit on January 8, 1973, followed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translunar_injection" title="Translunar injection">translunar injection</a>. On January 12, 1973, <i>Luna 21</i> was braked into a 90 by 100&#160;km (56 by 62 miles) <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit</a>.\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_21" title="Luna 21">Luna 21</a></i> spacecraft landed on the Moon to deploy the second Soviet lunar rover, <i>Lunokhod 2</i>. The primary objectives of the mission were to collect images of the lunar surface, examine ambient light levels to determine the feasibility of astronomical observations from the Moon, perform laser ranging experiments from Earth, observe solar X-rays, measure local magnetic fields, and study mechanical properties of the lunar surface material.\n</p><p>The landing occurred on January 15, 1973 at 23:35 UT in <a href="/wiki/Le_Monnier_(crater)" title="Le Monnier (crater)">Le Monnier crater</a> (25.85 degrees N, 30.45 degrees E).\n</p><p>After landing the <i>Lunokhod 2</i> took TV images of the surrounding area, then rolled down a ramp to the surface at 01:14 UT on 1973-01-16. It then took pictures of the <i>Luna 21</i> lander and landing site.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rover_description_2">Rover description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rover description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Lunokhod 2</i> was equipped with three <a href="/wiki/Slow-scan_television" title="Slow-scan television">slow-scan television</a> cameras, one mounted high on the rover for navigation, which could return high resolution images at different rates&#8212;3.2, 5.7, 10.9 or 21.1 seconds per frame (not frames per second). These images were used by the five-man team of controllers on Earth who sent driving commands to the rover in real time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> There were four panoramic cameras mounted on the rover.\n</p><p>Power was supplied by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panel</a> on the inside of a round hinged lid which covered the instrument bay, which would charge the batteries when opened. A <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>-210 radioactive heat source was used to keep the rover warm during the long lunar nights.\n</p><p>Scientific instruments included a <a href="/wiki/Soil_mechanics" title="Soil mechanics">soil mechanics</a> tester, <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Solar</a> <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> experiment, an <a href="/wiki/Photometer" title="Photometer">astrophotometer</a> to measure visible and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light levels, a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> deployed in front of the rover on the end of a 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a> (8&#160;ft 2 in) boom, a <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometer</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Photodetector" title="Photodetector">photodetector</a> (Rubin-1) for laser detection experiments, and a French-supplied laser <a href="/wiki/Corner_reflector" title="Corner reflector">corner reflector</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Payload_2">Payload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Many mechanical tests of the moon\'s surface, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_ranging" title="Laser ranging">laser ranging</a> measurements, and other experiments were completed during this time.\n</p><p>For comparison, the similarly sized NASA <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rovers</a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_rover" title="Spirit rover">Spirit</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover">Opportunity</a></i> had, by their fifth anniversary in January 2009, traveled a total of 21&#160;km (13 miles) and transmitted over 125,000 images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chernobyl_legacy">Chernobyl legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunokhod_programme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chernobyl legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to a French documentary TV film <i><a href="/wiki/Tank_on_the_Moon" title="Tank on the Moon">Tank on the Moon</a></i> by Jean Afanassieff, the Lunokhod design returned to limelight 15 years later due to the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl nuclear power plant disaster</a> on April 26, 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rightpundits_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rightpundits-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_German" title="East German">East German</a>-built remote controlled <a href="/wiki/Bulldozer" title="Bulldozer">bulldozers</a> available to Soviet <a href="/wiki/Civil_defense" title="Civil defense">civil defense</a> troops weighed dozens of tons&#160;&#8211;  too heavy to operate on the remaining parts of the partially collapsed reactor building roof. Human laborers could not be employed effectively to <a href="/wiki/Shovel" title="Shovel">shovel</a> debris, since work shifts were limited to 90 second intervals due to intense <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lunokhod designers were called back from retirement, and in two weeks rovers were made which used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_decay" title="Nuclear decay">nuclear decay</a> heat sources for internal rack climate control, their electronic systems were already hardened to partly resist radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rightpundits_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-rightpundits-19">[19]</a></sup> This benefit allowed the 1986 designers to quickly devise a derived vehicle type for nuclear disaster recovery work. On July 15, two rovers, called STR-1,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup> were delivered to the Chernobyl accident zone and proved useful for clearing debris, earning awards for the designers. Due to extremely high radiation levels, both STR-1 rovers eventually failed, and human workers (later named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquidator_(Chernobyl)" title="Liquidator (Chernobyl)">liquidators</a>) were once again called in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rightpundits_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-rightpundits-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations_and_ownership">Locations and ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
