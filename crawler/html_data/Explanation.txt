For the Netflix series, see <a href="/wiki/Explained_(TV_series)" title="Explained (TV series)">Explained (TV series)</a>.</div>\n<p>An <b>explanation</b> is a set of <a href="/wiki/Statement_(logic)" title="Statement (logic)">statements</a> usually constructed to <a href="/wiki/Description" title="Description">describe</a> a set of facts which clarifies the <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">causes</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/context" title="wiktionary:context">context</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logical_consequence" title="Logical consequence">consequences</a> of those facts. This description of the facts et cetera may establish <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_inference" title="Rule of inference">rules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">laws</a>, and may clarify the existing rules or laws in relation to any objects, or phenomena examined.  The components of an explanation can be <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/implicit" title="wikt:implicit">implicit</a>, and interwoven with one another.\n</p><p>An explanation is often underpinned by an <a href="/wiki/Understanding" title="Understanding">understanding</a> or norm that can be represented by different media such as music, text, and graphics. Thus, an explanation is subjected to <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_(logic)" title="Interpretation (logic)">interpretation</a>, and discussion.\n</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_research" title="Scientific research">scientific research</a>, explanation is one of several purposes for empirical research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Explanation is a way to uncover new knowledge, and to report relationships among different aspects of studied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenomena" title="Phenomena">phenomena</a>. Explanation attempts to answer the "why"  and "how" questions. Explanations have varied <a href="/wiki/Explanatory_power" title="Explanatory power">explanatory power</a>. The formal <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> is the theoretical tool used to verify explanation in empirical research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments">Arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Argument" title="Argument">Argument</a></div>\n<p>While arguments attempt to show that something is, will be, or should be the case, explanations try to show <i>why</i> or <i>how</i> something is or will be. If Fred and Joe address the issue of <i>whether</i> or not Fred\'s cat has fleas, Joe may state: "Fred, your cat has fleas. Observe the cat is scratching right now." Joe has made an <i>argument that</i> the cat has fleas. However, if Fred and Joe agree on the fact that the cat has fleas, they may further question <i>why</i> this is so and put forth an <i>explanation</i>: "The reason the cat has fleas is that the weather has been damp." The difference is that the attempt is not to settle whether or not some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claim_(logic)" title="Claim (logic)">claim</a> is true, but to show <i>why</i> it is true.\n</p><p>In this sense, arguments aim to contribute <i>knowledge</i>, whereas explanations aim to contribute <i>understanding</i>.\n</p><p>Arguments and explanations largely resemble each other in <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorical</a> use. This is the cause of much difficulty in <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">thinking critically</a> about <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">claims</a>. There are several reasons for this difficulty.\n</p>\n<ul><li>People often are not themselves clear on whether they are arguing for or explaining something.</li>\n<li>The same types of words and phrases are used in presenting explanations and arguments.</li>\n<li>The terms \'explain\' or \'explanation,\' et cetera are frequently used in arguments.</li>\n<li>Explanations are often used within arguments and presented so as to serve <i>as arguments</i>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Justification">Justification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Justification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_justification" title="Theory of justification">Theory of justification</a></div>\n<p>Justification is the reason why someone properly holds a <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">belief</a>, the explanation as to why the belief is a true one, or an account of how one knows what one knows. In much the same way arguments and explanations may be confused with each other, so too may explanations and justifications. Statements which are justifications of some action take the form of arguments. For example, attempts to justify a theft usually explain the motives (e.g., to feed a starving family).\n</p><p>It is important to be aware when an explanation is <i>not</i> a justification. A criminal profiler may offer an explanation of a suspect\'s behavior (e.g.; the person lost their job, the person got evicted, etc.). Such statements may help us understand why the person committed the crime, however an uncritical listener may believe the speaker is trying to gain sympathy for the person and his or her actions. It does not follow that a person proposing an explanation has any sympathy for the views or actions being explained. This is an important distinction because we need to be able to understand and explain terrible events and behavior in attempting to discourage or prevent them.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are many and varied events, objects, and facts which require explanation. So too, there are many different types of explanation. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> recognized at least <a href="/wiki/Four_causes" title="Four causes">four types</a> of explanation. Other types of explanation are <a href="/wiki/Deductive-nomological_model" title="Deductive-nomological model">Deductive-nomological</a>, Functional, Historical, Psychological, Reductive, Teleological, Methodological explanations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meta-explanation">Meta-explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meta-explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The notion of meta-explanation is important in behavioral scenarios that involve conflicting agents. In these scenarios, implicit or explicit conflict can be caused by contradictory agents\' interests, as communicated in their explanations for why they behaved in a particular way, by a lack of knowledge of the situation, or by a mixture of explanations of multiple factors. In many cases to assess the plausibility of explanations, one must analyze two following components and their interrelations: (1) explanation at the actual object level (explanation itself) and (2) explanation at the higher level (meta-explanation). Comparative analysis of the roles of both is conducted to assess the plausibility of how agents explain the scenarios of their interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Object-level explanation assesses the plausibility of individual claims by using a traditional approach to handle argumentative structure of a dialog. Meta-explanation links the structure of a current scenario with that of previously learned scenarios of multi-agent interaction. The scenario structure includes agents\' communicative actions and argumentation defeat relations between the subjects of these actions. The data for both object-level and meta-explanation can be visually specified, and a plausibility of how agent behavior in a scenario can be visually explained. Meta-explanation in the form of machine learning of scenario structure can be augmented by conventional explanation by finding arguments in the form of defeasibility analysis of individual claims, to increase the accuracy of plausibility assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A ratio between object-level and meta-explanation can be defined as the relative accuracy of plausibility assessment based on the former and latter sources. The groups of scenarios can then be clustered based on this ratio; hence, such a ratio is an important parameter of human behavior associated with explaining something to other humans.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
