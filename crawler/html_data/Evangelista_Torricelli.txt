class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelista_Torricelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelista_Torricelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Evangelista Torricelli was born on 15 October 1608 in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>; he invented the barometer in Florence, Italy. The firstborn child of Gaspare Torricelli and Caterina Angetti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magill2013_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magill2013-2">[2]</a></sup> His family was from <a href="/wiki/Faenza" title="Faenza">Faenza</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Ravenna" title="Province of Ravenna">Province of Ravenna</a>, then part of the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a>. His father was a textile worker and the family was very poor. Seeing his talents, his parents sent him to be educated in Faenza, under the care of his uncle, Giacomo (Jacob), a <a href="/wiki/Camaldolese" title="Camaldolese">Camaldolese</a> <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a>, who first ensured that his nephew was given a sound basic education. He then entered young Torricelli into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> College in 1624, possibly the one in Faenza itself, to study mathematics and philosophy until 1626, by which time his father, Gaspare, had died. The uncle then sent Torricelli to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> to study science under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monk <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Castelli" title="Benedetto Castelli">Benedetto Castelli</a>, professor of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at the Collegio della Sapienza (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome" title="Sapienza University of Rome">Sapienza University of Rome</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4">[4]</a></sup>\nCastelli was a student of <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_Robinson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_Robinson-5">[5]</a></sup>\n"Benedetto Castelli made experiments on running water (1628), and he was entrusted by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_VIII" title="Pope Urban VIII">Pope Urban VIII</a> with hydraulic undertakings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith-6">[6]</a></sup>\nThere is no actual evidence that Torricelli was enrolled at the university. It is almost certain that Torricelli was taught by Castelli. In exchange he worked for him as his secretary from 1626 to 1632 as a private arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\nBecause of this, Torricelli was exposed to experiments funded by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_VIII" title="Pope Urban VIII">Pope Urban VIII</a>. While living in Rome, Torricelli became also the student of the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Bonaventura_Cavalieri" title="Bonaventura Cavalieri">Bonaventura Cavalieri</a>, with whom he became great friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_Robinson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_Robinson-5">[5]</a></sup> It was in Rome that Torricelli also became friends with two other  students of Castelli, <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_Magiotti" title="Raffaello Magiotti">Raffaello Magiotti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Nardi" title="Antonio Nardi">Antonio Nardi</a>. Galileo referred to Torricelli, Magiotti, and Nardi affectionately as his "triumvirate" in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span Firenze</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1632, shortly after the publication of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>\'s <i>Dialogues of the New Science</i>, Torricelli wrote to Galileo of reading it "with the delight [...] of one who, having already practiced all of geometry most diligently [...] and having studied <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> and seen almost everything of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Kepler</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Copernicus</a>, and was a Galileian in profession and sect". (The Vatican condemned Galileo in June 1633, and this was the only known occasion on which Torricelli openly declared himself to hold the Copernican view.)  \n</p><p>Aside from several letters, little is known of Torricelli\'s activities in the years between 1632 and 1641, when Castelli sent Torricelli\'s <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">monograph</a> of the path of projectiles to Galileo, then a prisoner in his villa at <a href="/wiki/Arcetri" title="Arcetri">Arcetri</a>. Although Galileo promptly invited Torricelli to visit, he did not accept until just three months before Galileo\'s death. The reason for this was that Torricelli\'s mother, Caterina Angetti died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_Robinson_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_Robinson-5">[5]</a></sup> "(T)his short intercourse with the great mathematician enabled Torricelli to finish the fifth dialogue under the personal direction of its author; it was published by Viviani, another pupil of Galileo, in 1674."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith-6">[6]</a></sup> After Galileo\'s death on 8 January 1642, Grand Duke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinando_II_de%27_Medici" title="Ferdinando II de\' Medici">Ferdinando II de\' Medici</a> asked him to succeed Galileo as the grand-ducal mathematician and chair of mathematics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pisa" title="University of Pisa">University of Pisa</a>.  Right before the appointment, Torricelli was considering returning to Rome because of there being nothing left for him in Florence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_Robinson_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_Robinson-5">[5]</a></sup> In this role he solved some of the great mathematical problems of the day, such as finding a <a href="/wiki/Cycloid" title="Cycloid">cycloid</a>\'s area and center of gravity.  As a result of this study, he wrote the book the <i>Opera Geometrica</i> in which he described his observations.  The book was published in 1644.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_Robinson_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_Robinson-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Little was known about Torricelli in regard to his works in geometry when he accepted the honorable position, but after he published <i>Opera Geometrica</i> two years later, he became highly esteemed in that discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mancosu_and_Ezio_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mancosu_and_Ezio-9">[9]</a></sup>  "He was interested in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">Optics</a>, and invented a method whereby microscopic lenses might be made of glass which could be easily melted in a lamp."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frederick_John_Jervis-Smith-6">[6]</a></sup>  As a result, he designed and built a number of telescopes and simple microscopes; several large lenses, engraved with his name, are still preserved in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.   On 11 June 1644, he famously wrote in a letter to <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo_Ricci" title="Michelangelo Ricci">Michelangelo Ricci</a>: \n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Noi viviamo sommersi nel fondo d\'un pelago d\'aria. (We live submerged at the bottom of an ocean of air.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>However his work on the cycloid involved him in a controversy with <a href="/wiki/Gilles_de_Roberval" title="Gilles de Roberval">Gilles de Roberval</a>, who accused him of plagiarizing his earlier solution of the problem of its <a href="/wiki/Quadrature_(mathematics)" title="Quadrature (mathematics)">quadrature</a>. Although there seems no room for doubt that Torricelli\'s was arrived at independently, the matter was still in dispute up to his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_11-0"><a "Belonging to that first period are his pamphlets on Solidi spherali, Contatti and the major part of the\npropositions and sundry problems which were gathered together by <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Viviani" title="Vincenzo Viviani">Viviani</a> after Torricelli\'s death. Its communication by Castelli to Galileo in 1641, with a proposal that Torricelli should reside with him, led to Torricelli traveling to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, where he met Galileo, and acted as his amanuensis during the three remaining months of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barometer">Barometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelista_Torricelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Barometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Torricelli\'s chief invention was the mercury barometer. "This instrument is named from two Greek words, signifying two measures of weight, since by it a column of air is weighed against a column of mercury."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Timbs_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Timbs-14">[14]</a></sup>  The barometer arose from the need to solve a practical problem.   Pump makers of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a> attempted to raise  water to a height of 12 meters or more, but found that 10 meters was the limit with a suction pump (as recounted in Galileo\'s Dialogue). Torricelli employed <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, thirteen times more dense than water. In 1643 he created a tube approximately one meter long, sealed at the top, filled it with mercury, and set it vertically into a basin of mercury. The column of mercury fell to about 76&#160;cm, leaving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torricellian_vacuum" title="Torricellian vacuum">Torricellian vacuum</a> above. As we now know, the column\'s height fluctuated with changing <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>; this was the first barometer. The discovery of the principle of the <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a> has perpetuated his fame ("Torricellian tube", "Torricellian vacuum"). The <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a>, a unit of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> measurements, is named after him.  "12 years before Torricelli\'s observations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descartes" title="Descartes">Descartes</a>, the French philosopher, had made the same observation, although he does not appear to have turned it to any account."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Timbs_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-John_Timbs-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Torricelli.27s_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Torricelli\'s_law">Torricelli\'s law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evangelista_Torricelli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Torricelli\'s law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Torricelli also discovered a law, now known as <a href="/wiki/Torricelli%27s_law" title="Torricelli\'s law">Torricelli\'s law</a>, regarding the speed of a fluid flowing out of an opening, which was later shown to be a particular case of <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_principle" title="Bernoulli\'s principle">Bernoulli\'s principle</a>. "Perhaps his most notable achievement in the field of projectiles was to establish for the first time the idea of an <a href="/wiki/Envelope_(mathematics)" title="Envelope (mathematics)">envelope</a>: projectiles sent out at [...] the same speed in all directions trace out parabolas which are all tangent to a common paraboloid.
