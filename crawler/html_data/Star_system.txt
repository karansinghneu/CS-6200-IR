A large number of stars bound by gravitation is generally called a <i><a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star cluster</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a></i>, although, broadly speaking, they are also star systems. Star systems are not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a>, which include planets and similar bodies [such as comets.]\n</p><p>A star system of two stars is known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a></i>, <i>binary star system</i> or <i>physical <a href="/wiki/Double_star" title="Double star">double star</a></i>. If there are no <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal</a> effects, no perturbation from other forces, and no transfer of <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> from one star to the other, such a system is stable, and both stars will trace out an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical</a> <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">barycenter</a> of the system indefinitely. <i>(See <a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">Two-body problem</a>)</i>. Examples of binary systems are <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procyon" title="Procyon">Procyon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_X-1" title="Cygnus X-1">Cygnus X-1</a>, the last of which probably consists of a star and a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>.\n</p><p>A <b>multiple star system</b> consists of three or more <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> that appear from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to be close to one another in the sky. This may result from the stars actually being physically close and <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitationally</a> bound to each other, in which case it is a <i>physical</i> multiple star, or this closeness may be merely apparent, in which case it is an <i>optical</i> multiple star (meaning that the stars may appear to be close to each other when viewed from planet Earth, as they both seem to occupy the same point in the sky, but in reality, one star may be much further away from Earth than the other, which is not readily apparent unless one can view them from a different angle). Physical multiple stars are also commonly called <i>multiple stars</i> or <i>multiple star systems</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tokc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tokc-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toko_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-toko-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most multiple star systems are <i>triple stars</i>. Systems with four or more components are less likely to occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toko_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-toko-5">[5]</a></sup> Multiple-star systems are called <i>triple</i>, <i>trinary</i> or <i>ternary</i> if they contain three stars; <i>quadruple</i> or <i>quaternary</i> if they contain four stars; <i>quintuple</i> or <i>quintenary</i> with five stars; <i>sextuple</i> or <i>sextenary</i> with six stars; <i>septuple</i> or <i>septenary</i> with seven stars. These systems are smaller than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_star_cluster" title="Open star cluster">open star clusters</a>, which have more complex dynamics and typically have from 100 to 1,000 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galactic_Dynamics_1987_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galactic_Dynamics_1987-7">[7]</a></sup> Most multiple star systems known are triple; for higher multiplicities, the number of known systems with a given multiplicity decreases exponentially with multiplicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tok1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-tok1-8">[8]</a></sup> For example, in the 1999 revision of Tokovinin\'s catalog<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tokc_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tokc-3">[3]</a></sup> of physical multiple stars, 551 out of the 728 systems described are triple. However, because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_effect" title="Selection effect">selection effects</a>, knowledge of these statistics is very incomplete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Multiple-star systems can be divided into two main dynamical classes: <a href="#Hierarchical_systems">hierarchical</a> systems which are stable and consist of nested orbits that don\'t interact much and so each level of the hierarchy can be treated as a <i><a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">Two-body problem</a></i>, or the <a href="#Trapezia">trapezia</a> which have unstable strongly interacting orbits and are modelled as an <i><a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem">n-body problem</a></i>, exhibiting <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div While only two stars are apparent, it is actually a quadruple system containing two sets of binary stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Most multiple-star systems are organized in what is called a <i>hierarchical system</i>: the stars in the system can be divided into two smaller groups, each of which traverses a larger orbit around the system\'s <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>. Each of these smaller groups must also be hierarchical, which means that they must be divided into smaller subgroups which themselves are hierarchical, and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Each level of the hierarchy can be treated as a <i><a href="/wiki/Two-body_problem" title="Two-body problem">two-body problem</a></i> by considering close pairs as if they were a single star. In these systems there is little interaction between the orbits and the stars\' motion will continue to approximate stable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-toko_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-toko-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heintz1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-heintz1-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion">Keplerian</a> orbits around the system\'s center of mass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> unlike the unstable <a href="#Trapezia">trapezia</a> systems or the even more complex <a href="/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics)" title="Dynamics (mechanics)">dynamics</a> of the large number of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple_star_systems">Triple star systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Triple star systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><span id="triple_star"></span><span id="Triple_star"></span>\n</p><p>In a <i>physical</i> triple star system, each star <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbits</a> the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> of the system. Usually, two of the stars form a close <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary system</a>, and the third orbits this pair at a distance much larger than that of the binary orbit. This arrangement is called <i>hierarchical</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evans_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-evans-16">[16]</a></sup> The reason for this is that if the inner and outer orbits are comparable in size, the system may become dynamically unstable, leading to a star being ejected from the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Triple stars that are <i>not</i> all gravitationally bound might comprise a physical binary and an <i>optical</i> companion, such as <a href="/wiki/Beta_Cephei" title="Beta Cephei">Beta Cephei</a>, or rarely, a purely <i>optical</i> triple star, such as <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Serpentis" title="Gamma Serpentis">Gamma Serpentis</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_multiplicities">Higher multiplicities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Higher multiplicities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mobile-diagrams.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Mobile-diagrams.png/220px-Mobile-diagrams.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/Mobile-diagrams.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mobile-diagrams.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mobile diagrams: <div><ol style="list-style-type:lower-alpha"><li style="margin-left:-12px;">multiplex</li><li style="margin-left:-12px;">simplex, binary system</li><li style="margin-left:-12px;">simplex, triple system</li><li style="margin-left:-12px;">simplex, quadruple system, hierarchy 2</li><li style="margin-left:-12px;">simplex, quadruple system, hierarchy 3;</li><li style="margin-left:-12px;">simplex, quintuple system, hierarchy 4.</li></ol></div></div></div></div>\n<p>Hierarchical multiple star systems with more than three stars can produce a number of more complicated arrangements, which can be illustrated by what Evans (1968) has called a <i>mobile diagram</i>. These are similar to ornamental mobiles hung from the ceiling. Some examples can be seen in the figure to the right. Each level of the diagram illustrates the decomposition of the system into two or more systems with smaller size. Evans calls a diagram <i>multiplex</i> if there is a node with more than two children, i.e. if the decomposition of some subsystem involves two or more orbits with comparable size. Because, as we have already seen for triple stars, this may be unstable, multiple stars are expected to be <i>simplex</i>, meaning that at each level there are exactly two children. Evans calls the number of levels in the diagram its <i>hierarchy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>A simplex diagram of hierarchy 1, as in (b), describes a binary system.</li>\n<li>A simplex diagram of hierarchy 2 may describe a triple system, as in (c), or a quadruple system, as in (d).</li>\n<li>A simplex diagram of hierarchy 3 may describe a system with anywhere from four to eight components. The mobile diagram in (e) shows an example of a quadruple system with hierarchy 3, consisting of a single distant component orbiting a close binary system, with one of the components of the close binary being an even closer binary.</li>\n<li>A real example of a system with hierarchy 3 is <a href="/wiki/Castor_(star)" title="Castor (star)">Castor</a>, also known as Alpha Geminorum or &#945; Gem. It consists of what appears to be a <a href="/wiki/Visual_binary" title="Visual binary">visual binary</a> <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> which, upon closer inspection, can be seen to consist of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic binary</a> stars. By itself, this would be a quadruple hierarchy 2 system as in (d), but it is orbited by a fainter more distant component, which is also a close red dwarf binary. This forms a sextuple system of hierarchy 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The maximum hierarchy occurring in A. A. Tokovinin\'s Multiple Star Catalogue, as of 1999, is 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For example, the stars Gliese 644A and Gliese 644B form what appears to be a close visual <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a>; because Gliese 644B is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary" title="Spectroscopic binary">spectroscopic binary</a>, this is actually a triple system. The triple system has the more distant visual companion Gliese 643 and the still more distant visual companion Gliese 644C, which, because of their common motion with Gliese 644AB, are thought to be gravitationally bound to the triple system. This forms a quintuple system whose mobile diagram would be the diagram of level 4 appearing in (f).;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Higher hierarchies are also possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evans_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-evans-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Most of these higher hierarchies either are stable or suffer from internal <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Others consider complex multiple stars will in time theoretically disintegrate into less complex multiple stars, like more common observed triples or quadruples are possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trapezia">Trapezia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trapezia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Trapezia are usually very young, unstable systems. These are thought to form in stellar nurseries, and quickly fragment into stable multiple stars, which in the process may eject components as galactic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runaway_stars" title="Runaway stars">high-velocity stars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heintztrapezia_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-heintztrapezia-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> They are named after the multiple star system known as the <a href="/wiki/Trapezium_Cluster" title="Trapezium Cluster">Trapezium Cluster</a> in the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heintztrapezia_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-heintztrapezia-28">[28]</a></sup> Such systems are not rare, and commonly appear close to or within bright <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>. These stars have no standard hierarchical arrangements, but compete for stable orbits. This relationship is called <i>interplay</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heintz_1978_68_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heintz_1978_68-30">[30]</a></sup> Such stars eventually settle down to a close binary with a distant companion, with the other star(s) previously in the system ejected into interstellar space at high velocities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heintz_1978_68_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heintz_1978_68-30">[30]</a></sup> Example of such events may explain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Runaway_star" title="Runaway star">runaway stars</a> that might have been ejected during a collision of two binary star groups or a multiple system. This event is credited with ejecting <a href="/wiki/AE_Aurigae" title="AE Aurigae">AE Aurigae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mu_Columbae" title="Mu Columbae">Mu Columbae</a> and <a href="/wiki/53_Arietis" title="53 Arietis">53 Arietis</a> at above 200&#160;km&#183;s<sup>&#8722;1</sup> and has been traced to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trapezium_cluster" title="Trapezium cluster">Trapezium cluster</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a> some two million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blaauw_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blaauw-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoogerwerf_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoogerwerf-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designations_and_nomenclature">Designations and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Designations and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_star_designations">Multiple star designations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Multiple star designations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The components of multiple stars can be specified by appending the suffixes <i>A</i>, <i>B</i>, <i>C</i>, etc., to the system\'s designation. Suffixes such as <i>AB</i> may be used to denote the pair consisting of <i>A</i> and <i>B</i>. The sequence of letters <i>B</i>, <i>C</i>, etc. may be assigned in order of separation from the component <i>A</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heintz19_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heintz19-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fmt_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-fmt-34">[34]</a></sup> Components discovered close to an already known component may be assigned suffixes such as <i>Aa</i>, <i>Ba</i>, and so forth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fmt_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-fmt-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_in_the_Multiple_Star_Catalogue">Nomenclature in the Multiple Star Catalogue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nomenclature in the Multiple Star Catalogue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="177" decoding="async" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png/170px-Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Subsystem notation in Tokovinin\'s Multiple Star Catalogue</div></div></div>\n<p>A. A. Tokovinin\'s Multiple Star Catalogue uses a system in which each subsystem in a mobile diagram is encoded by a sequence of digits. In the mobile diagram (d) above, for example, the widest system would be given the number 1, while the subsystem containing its primary component would be numbered 11 and the subsystem containing its secondary component would be numbered 12. Subsystems which would appear below this in the mobile diagram will be given numbers with three, four, or more digits. When describing a non-hierarchical system by this method, the same subsystem number will be used more than once; for example, a system with three visual components, A, B, and C, no two of which can be grouped into a subsystem, would have two subsystems numbered 1 denoting the two binaries AB and AC. In this case, if B and C were subsequently resolved into binaries, they would be given the subsystem numbers 12 and 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_multiple_star_system_nomenclature">Future multiple star system nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Star_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future multiple star system nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The current nomenclature for double and multiple stars can cause confusion as binary stars discovered in different ways are given different designations (for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_star_designation" title="Double star designation">discoverer designations</a> for visual binary stars and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_star_designations" title="Variable star designations">variable star designations</a> for eclipsing binary stars), and, worse, component letters may be assigned differently by different authors, so that, for example, one person\'s <i>A</i> can be another\'s <i>C</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post191_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-post191-36">[36]</a></sup> Discussion starting in 1999 resulted in four proposed schemes to address this problem:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post191_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-post191-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>KoMa, a hierarchical scheme using upper- and lower-case letters and Arabic and Roman numerals;</li>\n<li>The Urban/Corbin Designation Method, a hierarchical numeric scheme similar to the <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification" title="Dewey Decimal Classification">Dewey Decimal Classification</a> system;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The Sequential Designation Method, a non-hierarchical scheme in which components and subsystems are assigned numbers in order of discovery;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and</li>\n<li>WMC, the Washington Multiplicity Catalog, a hierarchical scheme in which the suffixes used in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Double_Star_Catalog" title="Washington Double Star Catalog">Washington Double Star Catalog</a> are extended with additional suffixed letters and numbers.</li></ul>\n<p>For a designation system, identifying the hierarchy within the system has the advantage that it makes identifying subsystems and computing their properties easier. However, it causes problems when new components are discovered at a level above or intermediate to the existing hierarchy. In this case, part of the hierarchy will shift inwards. Components which are found to be nonexistent, or are later reassigned to a different subsystem, also cause problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 24th General Assembly of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> in 2000, the WMC scheme was endorsed and it was resolved by Commissions 5, 8, 26, 42, and 45 that it should be expanded into a usable uniform designation scheme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post191_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-post191-36">[36]</a></sup> A sample of a catalog using the WMC scheme, covering half an hour of <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a>, was later prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The issue was discussed again at the 25th General Assembly in 2003, and it was again resolved by commissions 5, 8, 26, 42, and 45, as well as the Working Group on Interferometry, that the WMC scheme should be expanded and further developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sample WMC is hierarchically organized; the hierarchy used is based on observed orbital periods or separations. Since it contains many visual <a href="/wiki/Double_star" title="Double star">double stars</a>, which may be optical rather than physical, this hierarchy may be only apparent. It uses upper-case letters (A, B, ...) for the first level of the hierarchy, lower-case letters (a, b, ...) for the second level, and numbers (1, 2, ...) for the third. Subsequent levels would use alternating lower-case letters and numbers, but no examples of this were found in the sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-post191_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-post191-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
