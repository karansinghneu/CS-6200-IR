id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Property_rights&amp;redirect=no" title="Property rights">Property rights</a>)</span></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>right to property</b> or <b>right to own property</b> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a>) is often classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_right" title="Human right">human right</a> for <a href="/wiki/Natural_person" title="Natural person">natural persons</a> regarding their possessions. A general recognition of a right to <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private property</a> is found more rarely and is typically heavily constrained insofar as property is owned by <a href="/wiki/Legal_person" title="Legal person">legal persons</a> (i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporations" title="Corporations">corporations</a>) and where it is used for production rather than consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A right to property is recognised in Article 17 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>, but it is not recognised in the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> or the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protocol_1,_article_1" title="Protocol 1, article 1">Protocol 1, article 1</a> acknowledges a right for natural and legal persons to "peaceful enjoyment of his possessions", subject to the "general interest or to secure the payment of taxes".\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The right to property is one of the most controversial human rights, both in terms of its existence and interpretation. The controversy about the definition of the right meant that it was not included in the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> or the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doebbler-p141-142_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doebbler-p141-142-3">[3]</a></sup> Controversy centres upon who is deemed to have property rights protected (e.g. human beings or also corporations), the type of property which is protected (property used for the purpose of consumption or production) and the reasons for which property can be restricted (for instance, for regulations, taxation or nationalisation in the public interest). In all human rights instruments, either implicit or express restrictions exist on the extent to which property is protected. Article 17 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a> (UDHR) enshrines the right to property as follows: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">(1) Everyone has the right to own property alone as well as in association with others.\n<p>(2) No one shall be arbitrarily deprived of his property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The object of the right to property as it is usually understood nowadays consists of property already owned or possessed, or of property acquired or to be acquired by a person through lawful means. Not in opposition but in contrast to this, some proposals also defend a universal right to private property, in the sense of a right of every person to effectively receive a certain amount of property, grounded in a claim to Earth\'s natural resources or other theories of justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/African_Charter_on_Human_and_Peoples%27_Rights" title="African Charter on Human and Peoples\' Rights">African Charter on Human and Peoples\' Rights</a></div>\n<p>The African Charter on Human and Peoples\' Rights (ACHPR) protects the right to property most explicitly in Article 14,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p372_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p372-6">[6]</a></sup> stating: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">The right to property shall be guaranteed. It may only be encroached upon in the interest of public need or in the general interest of the community and in accordance with the provisions of appropriate laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>Property rights are furthermore recognised in Article 13 of the ACHPR, which states that every citizen has the right to participate freely in the government of his country, the right to equal access to public services and "the right of access to public property and services in strict equality of all persons before the law". Article 21 of the ACHPR recognises the right of all peoples to freely dispose of their wealth and natural resources and that this right shall be exercised in the exclusive interest of the people, who may not be deprived of this right. Article 21 also provides that "in case of spoliation the dispossessed people shall have the right to the lawful recovery of its property as well as to adequate compensation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/American_Declaration_of_the_Rights_and_Duties_of_Man" title="American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man">American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>When the text of the UDHR was negotiated, other states in the Americas argued that the right to property should be limited to the protection of private property necessary for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsistence" title="Subsistence">subsistence</a>. Their suggestion was opposed, but was enshrined in the <a href="/wiki/American_Declaration_of_the_Rights_and_Duties_of_Man" title="American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man">American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man</a>, which was negotiated at the same time and adopted one year before the UDHR in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p370_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p370-9">[9]</a></sup> Article 23 of the declaration states: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Every Person has the right to own such private property as meets the essential needs of decent living and helps to maintain the dignity of the individual and of the home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The definition of the right to property is heavily influenced by Western concepts of property rights, but because property rights vary considerably in different legal systems it has not been possible to establish international standards on property rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The regional human rights instruments of Europe, Africa and the Americas recognise the right to protection of property to varying degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p365_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p365-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="American Convention on Human Rights">American Convention on Human Rights</a> (ACHR) recognises the right to protection of property, including the right to "just compensation". The ACHR also prohibits usury and other exploitation, which is unique amongst human rights instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p370_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p370-9">[9]</a></sup> Article 21 of the ACHR states: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">(1) Everyone has the right to the use and enjoyment of his property. The law may subordinate such use and enjoyment to the interest of society.\n<p>(2) No one shall be deprived of his property except upon payment of just compensation, for reasons of public utility or social interest, and in the cases and according to the forms established by law.\n(3) Usury and any other form of exploitation of man by man shall be prohibited by law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a></div>\n<p>After failed attempts to include the right to protection of property in the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> (ECHR), European states enshrined the right to protection of property in Article 1 of Protocol I to the ECHR as the "right to peaceful enjoyment of possessions",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> where the right to protection of property is defined as such: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">(1) Every natural or legal person is entitled to the peaceful enjoyment of his possessions. No one shall be deprived of his possessions except in the public interest and subject to the conditions provided for by law and by the general principles of international law.\n<p>(2) The preceding provisions shall not, however, in any way impair the right of a State to enforce such laws as it deems necessary to control the use of property in accordance with the general interest or to secure the payment of taxes or other contributions or penalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>Therefore European human rights law recognises the right to peaceful enjoyment of property, makes deprivation of possessions subject to certain conditions and recognises that states can balance the right to peaceful possession of property against the public interest. The <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> has interpreted "possessions" to include not only tangible property, but also economic interests, contractual agreements with economic value, compensation claims against the state and public law related claims such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensions" title="Pensions">pensions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  The European Court of Human Rights has held that the right to property is not absolute and states have a wide degree of discretion to limit the rights. As such, the right to property is regarded as a more flexible right than other human rights. States\' degree of discretion is defined in <i>Handyside v. United Kingdom</i>, heard by the European Court of Human Rights in 1976. Notable cases where the European Court of Human Rights has found the right to property having been violated include <i>Sporrong and Lonnroth v. Sweden</i>, heard in 1982, where Swedish law kept property under the threat of expropriation for an extended period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doebbler-p141-142_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doebbler-p141-142-3">[3]</a></sup> The highest economic compensation following a judgment of the Strasbourg Court on this matter was given (1,3 million euro) in case <i>Beyeler v. Italy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_conventions">International conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Property rights are also recognised in the <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Racial_Discrimination" title="International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination">International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination</a> which states in Article 5 that everyone has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_equality_before_the_law" title="Right to equality before the law">right to equality before the law</a> without distinction as to race, colour and national or ethnic origin, including the "right to own property alone as well as in association with others" and "the right to inherit". The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Elimination_of_All_Forms_of_Discrimination_against_Women" title="Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women">Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women</a> recognises the property rights in Article 16, which establishes the same right for both spouses to ownership, acquisition, management, administration, enjoyment and disposition of property and Article 15, which establishes women&#8217;s\' right to conclude contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p372_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p372-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Property rights are also enshrined in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention relating to the Status of Refugees">Convention relating to the Status of Refugees</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Protection_of_the_Rights_of_All_Migrant_Workers_and_Members_of_Their_Families" title="Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families">Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of Their Families</a>. These international human rights instruments for minorities do not establish a separate right to property, but prohibit discrimination in relation to property rights where such rights are guaranteed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_other_rights">Relationship to other rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to other rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The right to private property was a crucial demand in early quests for political freedom and equality and against feudal control of property. Property can serve as the basis for the entitlements that ensure the realisation of the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_an_adequate_standard_of_living" title="Right to an adequate standard of living">right to an adequate standard of living</a> and it was only property owners which were initially granted <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_vote" title="Right to vote">right to vote</a>. Because not everybody is a property owner, the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_work" title="Right to work">right to work</a> was enshrined to allow everybody to attain an adequate standard of living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Today, discrimination on the basis of property ownership is commonly seen as a serious threat to the equal enjoyment of human rights by all and non-discrimination clauses in <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a> frequently include property as a ground on the basis of which discrimination is prohibited (see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_equality_before_the_law" title="Right to equality before the law">right to equality before the law</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alfredsson-p372_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alfredsson-p372-6">[6]</a></sup> The protection of private property may come into conflict with <a href="/wiki/Economic,_social_and_cultural_rights" title="Economic, social and cultural rights">economic, social and cultural rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a>, such as the right to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_of_expression" title="Freedom of expression">freedom of expression</a>. To mitigate this the right to property is commonly limited to protect the public interest. Many states also maintain systems of communal and collective ownership. Property rights have frequently been regarded as preventing the realisation of human rights for all, through for example slavery and the exploitation of others. Unequal distribution of wealth often follows line of sex, race and minorities, therefore property rights may appear to be part of the problem, rather than as an interest that merits protection. Property rights have been at the centre of recent human rights debates on land reform, the return of cultural artifacts by collectors and museums to indigenous peoples and the popular sovereignty of peoples over natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Europe, the notion of private property and property rights emerged in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchants" title="Merchants">merchants</a> gave rise to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercantilist" title="Mercantilist">mercantilist</a> ideas. In 16th-century Europe, <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> advanced property rights using biblical terminology. The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_work_ethic" title="Protestant work ethic">Protestant work ethic</a> and views on man\'s destiny came to underline social views in emerging capitalist economies in <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_Europe" title="Early modern Europe">early modern Europe</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The right to private property emerged as a radical demand for <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> <i>vis-a-vis</i> the state in 17th-century revolutionary Europe, but in the 18th and 19th centuries the right to property as a human right became subject of intense controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Civil_War">English Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English Civil War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a></div><p>\nThe arguments advanced by the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a> during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> on <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_vote" title="Right to vote">right to vote</a>, informed subsequent debates in other countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Levellers emerged as a political movement in mid-17th century England in the aftermath of the Protestant Reformation. They believed that property which had been earned as the fruit of one\'s labour was sacred under the Bible\'s <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">commandment</a> "thou shall not steal". As such, they believed that the right to acquire property from one\'s work was sacred. Levellers\' views on the right to property and the right not to be deprived of property as a civil and political right were developed by the <a href="/wiki/Pamphleteer" title="Pamphleteer">pamphleteer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Overton_(pamphleteer)" title="Richard Overton (pamphleteer)">Richard Overton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ishay-p91-94_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ishay-p91-94-22">[22]</a></sup> In "An Arrow against all Tyrants" (1646), Overton argued: </p><blockquote><p>To every individual in nature is given an individual property by nature not to be invaded or usurped by any. For everyone, as he is himself, so he has a self propertiety, else he could not be himself; and of this no second may presume to deprive of without manifest violation and affront to the very principles of nature of the rules of equity and justice between man and man. Mine and thine cannot be, except this. No man has power over my rights and liberties, and I over no man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The views of the Levellers, who enjoyed support amongst small-scale property-owners and craftsmen, were not shared by all revolutionary parties of the English Civil War. At the 1647 General Council, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ireton" title="Henry Ireton">Henry Ireton</a> argued against equating the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">right to life</a> with the right to property. They argued that doing so would establish the right to take anything that one may want, irrespective of the rights of others. The Leveller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Rainborough" title="Thomas Rainborough">Thomas Rainborough</a> responded, relying on Overton\'s arguments, that the Levellers required respect for others\' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_rights" title="Natural rights">natural rights</a>. The definition of property and whether it was acquired as the fruit of one\'s labour and as such a natural right was subject to intense debate because the right to vote depended on property ownership. Political freedom was at the time associated with property ownership and individual independence. Cromwell and Ireton maintained that only property in <a href="/wiki/Freehold_(law)" title="Freehold (law)">freehold land</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chartered_company" title="Chartered company">chartered trading rights</a> gave a man the right to vote. They argued that this type of property ownership constituted a "take in society", which entitles men to political power. In contrast, Levellers argued that all men who are not servants, alms-recipients or beggars should be considered as property owners and be given voting rights. They believed that political freedom could only be secured by individuals, such as craftsmen, engaging in independent economic activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ishay-p91-94_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ishay-p91-94-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossides-p54_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rossides-p54-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Levellers were primarily concerned with the civil and political rights of small-scale property owners and workers, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a>, a smaller revolutionary group led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerard_Winstanley" title="Gerard Winstanley">Gerard Winstanley</a>, focused on the rights of the rural poor who worked on <a href="/wiki/Landed_property" title="Landed property">landed property</a>. The Diggers argued that private property was not consistent with justice and that the land that had been confiscated from the Crown and Church should be turned into communal land to be cultivated by the poor. According to the Diggers, the right to vote should be extended to all and everybody had the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_an_adequate_standard_of_living" title="Right to an adequate standard of living">right to an adequate standard of living</a>. With the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration of the English monarchy</a> in 1660, all confiscated land returned to the Crown and Church. Some property rights were recognised and limited voting rights were established. The ideas of the Levellers on property and civil and political rights remained influential and were advanced in the subsequent 1688 <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ishay-p91-94_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ishay-p91-94-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rossides-p54_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rossides-p54-24">[24]</a></sup> but restrictions on the right to vote based on property meant that only a fraction of the British population had the suffrage. In 1780 only 214,000 property-owning men were entitled to vote in England and Wales, less than 3 percent of the population of 8 million. The <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a> restricted the right to vote to men who owned property with an annual value of &#163;10, giving approximately 4 percent of the adult male population the right to vote. The reforms of 1867 extended the right to vote to approximately 8 percent. The working class (which increased dramatically with the Industrial Revolution) and industrialists remained effectively excluded from the political system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="John_Locke_and_the_American_and_French_Revolutions">John Locke and the American and French Revolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: John Locke and the American and French Revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="981" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg/200px-Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg/300px-Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg/400px-Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Locke_treatises_of_government_page.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>\'s 1689 <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Treatises_of_Government" title="Two Treatises of Government">Two Treatises of Government</a></i> in which Locke calls "lives, liberties and estates" the "property" of individuals</div></div></div>\n<p>The English philosopher <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> (1632&#8211;1704) developed the ideas of property, civil and political rights further. In his <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Treatises_of_Government" title="Two Treatises of Government">Second Treatise on Civil Government</a></i> (1689), Locke proclaimed that "everyman has a property in his person; this nobody has a right to but himself. The labor of his body and the work of his hand, we may say, are properly his".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He argued that property ownership derives from one\'s labor, though those who do not own property and only have their labor to sell should not be given the same political power as those who owned property. Labourers, small-scale property owners and large-scale property owners should have civil and political rights in proportion to the property they owned. According to Locke, the right to property and the right to life were inalienable rights and that it was the duty of the state to secure these rights for individuals. Locke argued that the safeguarding of natural rights, such as the right to property, along with the separation of powers and other checks and balances, would help to curtail political abuses by the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ishay-p91-94_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ishay-p91-94-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Locke\'s <a href="/wiki/Labor_theory_of_property" title="Labor theory of property">labor theory of property</a> and the separation of powers greatly influenced the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. The entitlement to civil and political rights, such as the right to vote, was tied to the question of property in both revolutions. American revolutionaries, such as <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, opposed universal suffrage, advocating votes only for those who owned a "stake" in society. <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> argued that extending the right to vote to all could lead in the right to property and justice being "overruled by a majority without property". While it was initially suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to establish the right to vote for all men, eventually the right to vote in the nascent United States was extended to white men who owned a specified amount of real estate and personal property. French revolutionaries recognised property rights in Article 17 of the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen" title="Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen">Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen</a> (1791), which stated that no one "may be deprived of property rights unless a legally established public necessity required it and upon condition of a just and previous indemnity". Articles 3 and 6 declared that "all citizens have the right to contribute personally or through their representatives" in the political system and that "all citizens being equal before [the law], are equally admissible to all public offices, positions and employment according to their capacity, and without other distinction than that of virtues and talents". However, in practice the French revolutionaries did not extend civil and political rights to all, although the property qualification required for such rights was lower than that established by the American revolutionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the French revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Joseph_Siey%C3%A8s" title="Emmanuel Joseph Siey&#232;s">Abb&#233; Siey&#232;s</a>, "all the inhabitants of a country should enjoy the right of a passive citizen... but those alone who contribute to the public establishment are like the true shareholders in the great social enterprise. They alone are the true active citizens, the true members of the association". Three months after the Declaration had been adopted, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">domestic servants</a>, women and those who did not pay taxes equal to three days of labor were declared "passive citizens". Sieyes wanted to see the rapid expansion of commercial activities and favoured the unrestricted accumulation of property. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre" title="Maximilien Robespierre">Maximilien Robespierre</a> warned that the free accumulation of wealth ought to be limited and that the right to property should not be permitted to violate the rights of others, particularly poorer citizens, including the working poor and peasants. Robespierre\'s views were eventually excluded from the <a href="/wiki/French_Constitution_of_1793" title="French Constitution of 1793">French Constitution of 1793</a> and a property qualification for civil and political rights was maintained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Property_law" title="Property law">Property law</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/English_land_law" title="English land law">English land law</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Property_rights_(economics)" title="Property rights (economics)">Property rights (economics)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Married_Women%27s_Property_Act_1882" title="Married Women\'s Property Act 1882">Married Women\'s Property Act 1882</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Right_to_property&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
