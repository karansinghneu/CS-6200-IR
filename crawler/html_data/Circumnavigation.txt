This article focuses on the circumnavigation of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. The first circumnavigation of Earth was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Magellan%27s_circumnavigation" title="Timeline of Magellan\'s circumnavigation">Magellan-Elcano expedition</a>, which sailed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seville,_Spain" title="Seville, Spain">Seville, Spain</a> in 1519 and returned in 1522, after crossing the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian</a> oceans.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i>circumnavigation</i> is a noun formed from the verb <i>circumnavigate</i>, from the past participle of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> verb <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumnavigare#Latin" title="wikt:circumnavigare">circumnavigare</a></i>, from <i>circum</i> "around" + <i>navigare</i> "to sail" (see further <a href="/wiki/Navigation#Etymology" title="Navigation">Navigation &#167; Etymology</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>If a person walks completely around either Pole, he crosses all <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridians</a>, but this is not generally considered a "circumnavigation." The trajectory of a true (global) circumnavigation forms a <a href="/wiki/Loop_(topology)" title="Loop (topology)">continuous loop</a> on the surface of Earth separating two halves of comparable area. A basic definition of a global circumnavigation would be a route which covers roughly a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>, and in particular one which passes through at least one pair of points <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">antipodal</a> to each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In practice, people use different definitions of world circumnavigation to accommodate practical constraints, depending on the method of travel. Since the planet is <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">quasispheroidal</a>, a trip from one Pole to the other, and back again on the other side, would technically be a circumnavigation, but <a href="/wiki/Arctic_ice_pack" title="Arctic ice pack">practical difficulties</a> generally preclude such a voyage although it was successfully undertaken in the early 1980s by <a href="/wiki/Ranulph_Fiennes" title="Ranulph Fiennes">Ranulph Fiennes</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first single voyage of global circumnavigation was that of the ship <i>Victoria</i>, between 1519 and 1522, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Magellan%27s_circumnavigation" title="Timeline of Magellan\'s circumnavigation">Magellan&#8211;Elcano expedition</a>. It was a Castilian (Spanish) voyage of discovery, led initially by the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a> between 1519 and 1521, and then by the spanish <a href="/wiki/Juan_Sebasti%C3%A1n_Elcano" title="Juan Sebasti&#225;n Elcano">Juan Sebasti&#225;n Elcano</a> from 1521 to 1522. The voyage started in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, crossed the Atlantic Ocean, and after several stopovers rounded the southern tip of South America where the expedition discovered the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Magellan" title="Strait of Magellan">Strait of Magellan</a>, named after the fleet\'s captain. It then continued across the Pacific discovering a number of islands on its way, including <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> before arriving in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. After Magellan\'s death in the Philippines in 1521, Elcano took command of the expedition and continued the journey across the Indian Ocean, round the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, north along the Atlantic Ocean, and back to Spain in 1522. Elcano and a small group of 18 men were actually the only members of the expedition to make the full circumnavigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apart from some scholars, it is not generally accepted that Magellan and some crew members (possibly some other Portuguese and the Malay-Sumatrese <a href="/wiki/Enrique_of_Malacca" title="Enrique of Malacca">Enrique of Malacca</a>, who survived to the Philippines and <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>) previously completed a full circumnavigation on several voyages, since <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> (where Magellan had been twice before, in 1509 and in 1511-1512) lie southwest of <a href="/wiki/Cebu" title="Cebu">Cebu</a> (Philippines). If he had also been in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moluccas" title="Moluccas">Moluccas</a> islands (located southeast of Cebu) in early 1512 (dubious and controversial), he completed and clearly exceeded an entire circumnavigation of Earth in longitude&#8212;though one circumnavigation in the strict sense implies a return to the same exact point. However, traveling west from Europe, in 1521, Magellan reached a region of Southeast Asia (in the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>), which he had reached on previous voyages traveling east. Magellan thereby achieved a nearly complete personal circumnavigation of the globe for the first time in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1577, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> sent <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> to start an expedition against the Spanish along the Pacific coast of the Americas. Drake set out from Plymouth, England in November 1577, aboard <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Hind" title="Golden Hind">Pelican</a></i>, which Drake renamed <i>Golden Hind</i> mid-voyage. In September 1578, he passed through the southern tip of South America, named <a href="/wiki/Drake_Passage" title="Drake Passage">Drake Passage</a>, which connects the southwestern part of the Atlantic Ocean with the southeastern part of the Pacific Ocean. In June 1579, Drake landed somewhere north of Spain\'s northern-most claim in Alta California, which is known as <a href="/wiki/Drakes_Bay" title="Drakes Bay">Drakes Bay</a>, California. Drake completed the second circumnavigation of the world in September 1580, becoming the first commander to lead an entire circumnavigation.\n</p><p>For the wealthy, long voyages around the world, such as was done by <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a>, became possible in the 19th century, and the two World Wars moved vast numbers of troops around the planet. However, it was later improvements in technology and rising incomes that made such trips relatively common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical">Nautical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nautical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The nautical global and fastest circumnavigation record is currently held by a wind-powered vessel, the trimaran <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IDEC_3" title="IDEC 3">IDEC 3</a>. The record was established by six sailors: <a href="/wiki/Francis_Joyon" title="Francis Joyon">Francis Joyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Pella" title="Alex Pella">Alex Pella</a>, Cl&#233;ment Surtel, Gw&#233;nol&#233; Gahinet, S&#233;bastien Audigane and Bernard Stamm; who wrote themselves into history books on 26 January 2017, by circumnavigating the globe in 40 days, 23 hours, 30 minutes and 30 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The absolute speed sailing record around the world followed the North Atlantic Ocean, Equator, South Atlantic Ocean, Southern Ocean, South Atlantic Ocean, Equator, North Atlantic Ocean route in an easterly direction.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_powered">Wind powered</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wind powered">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CircumnavigationByTrades.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/CircumnavigationByTrades.png/300px-CircumnavigationByTrades.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/CircumnavigationByTrades.png/450px-CircumnavigationByTrades.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/CircumnavigationByTrades.png/600px-CircumnavigationByTrades.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CircumnavigationByTrades.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The route of a typical modern sailing circumnavigation, via the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> is shown in red; its antipodes are shown in yellow.</div></div></div>\n<p>The map on the right shows, in red, a typical, non-competitive, route for a <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a> circumnavigation of the world by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama</a> canals; overlaid in yellow are the points antipodal to all points on the route. It can be seen that the route roughly approximates a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>, and passes through two pairs of antipodal points. This is a route followed by many <a href="/wiki/Cruising_(maritime)" title="Cruising (maritime)">cruising</a> sailors, going in the western direction; the use of the trade winds makes it a relatively easy sail, although it passes through a number of zones of calms or light winds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CircumnavigationByCapes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/CircumnavigationByCapes.png/300px-CircumnavigationByCapes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/CircumnavigationByCapes.png/450px-CircumnavigationByCapes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/CircumnavigationByCapes.png/600px-CircumnavigationByCapes.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CircumnavigationByCapes.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The route of a typical yacht racing circumnavigation is shown in red; its antipodes are shown in yellow.</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Yacht_racing" title="Yacht racing">yacht racing</a>, a round-the-world route approximating a great circle would be quite impractical, particularly in a non-stop race where use of the Panama and Suez Canals would be impossible. Yacht racing therefore defines a world circumnavigation to be a passage of at least 21,600&#160;nautical miles (40,000&#160;km) in length which crosses the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, crosses every <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> and finishes in the same port as it starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The second map on the right shows the route of the <a href="/wiki/Vend%C3%A9e_Globe" title="Vend&#233;e Globe">Vend&#233;e Globe</a> round-the-world race in red; overlaid in yellow are the points antipodal to all points on the route. It can be seen that the route does not pass through any pairs of antipodal points. Since the winds in the higher southern latitudes predominantly blow west-to-east it can be seen that there are an easier route (west-to-east) and a harder route (east-to-west) when circumnavigating by sail; this difficulty is magnified for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square-rig" title="Square-rig">square-rig</a> vessels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Around_the_world_sailing_record" title="Around the world sailing record">around the world sailing records</a>, there is a rule saying that the length must be at least 21,600&#160;nautical miles calculated along the shortest possible track from the starting port and back that does not cross land and does not go below 63&#176;S. It is allowed to have one single waypoint to lengthen the calculated track. The equator must be crossed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The solo wind powered circumnavigation record of 42 days, 16 hours, 40 minutes and 35 seconds was established by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Gabart" title="Fran&#231;ois Gabart">Fran&#231;ois Gabart</a> on the maxi-multihull sailing yacht <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MACIF&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MACIF (page does not exist)">MACIF</a> and completed on 7 December 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The voyage followed the North Atlantic Ocean, Equator, South Atlantic Ocean, Southern Ocean, South Atlantic Ocean, Equator, North Atlantic Ocean route in an easterly direction.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanically_powered">Mechanically powered</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mechanically powered">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since the advent of world cruises in 1922, by <a href="/wiki/Cunard_Line" title="Cunard Line">Cunard\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/RMS_Laconia_(1921)" title="RMS Laconia (1921)"><i>Laconia</i></a>, thousands of people have completed circumnavigations of the globe at a more leisurely pace. Typically, these voyages begin in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> or <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, and proceed westward. Routes vary, either travelling through the Caribbean and then into the Pacific Ocean via the Panama Canal, or around <a href="/wiki/Cape_Horn" title="Cape Horn">Cape Horn</a>. From there ships usually make their way to Hawaii, the islands of the South Pacific, Australia, New Zealand, then northward to Hong Kong, South East Asia, and India. At that point, again, routes may vary: one way is through the Suez Canal and into the Mediterranean; the other is around <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> and then up the west coast of Africa. These cruises end in the port where they began.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The current mechanically powered circumnavigation record of 60 days 23 hours and 49 minutes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> was established by a voyage of the wave-piercing trimaran <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthrace" title="Earthrace">Earthrace</a> which was completed on 27 June 2008. The voyage followed the North Atlantic Ocean, Panama Canal, Pacific Ocean, Indian Ocean, Suez Canal, Mediterranean Sea route in a westerly direction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">Aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the development of commercial aviation at least tens of thousands of people have flown around the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some regular routes, such as <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American</a> Flight One (and later <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> Flight One), circled the globe, and today planning such a trip through connections is simple.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/First_aerial_circumnavigation" title="First aerial circumnavigation">first successful attempt</a> to circumnavigate the planet by air occurred in 1924, flown by aviators of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Air_Service" title="U.S. Army Air Service">U.S. Army Air Service</a> in a quartet of <a href="/wiki/Douglas_World_Cruiser" title="Douglas World Cruiser">Douglas World Cruiser</a> biplanes.\n</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">lighter-than-air</a> aircraft of any type to circumnavigate under its own power was the <a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">rigid airship</a> <a href="/wiki/LZ_127_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin">LZ 127 <i>Graf Zeppelin</i></a>, which did so in 1929.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Aviation records take account of the wind circulation patterns of the world; in particular the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet streams</a>, which circulate in the northern and southern hemispheres without crossing the equator. There is therefore no requirement to cross the equator, or to pass through two antipodal points, in the course of setting a round-the-world aviation record. Thus, for example, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Fossett" title="Steve Fossett">Steve Fossett</a>\'s global circumnavigation by balloon was entirely contained within the southern hemisphere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>For powered aviation, the course of a round-the-world record must start and finish at the same point and cross all meridians; the course must be at least 36,770 kilometres (19,850&#160;nmi) long (which is approximately the length of the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a>). The course must include set control points at latitudes outside the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic</a> circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In ballooning, which is totally at the mercy of the winds, the requirements are even more relaxed. The course must cross all meridians, and must include a set of checkpoints which are all outside of two circles, chosen by the pilot, having radii of 3,335.85 kilometres (2,072.80&#160;mi) and enclosing the poles (though not necessarily centred on them).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronautics">Astronautics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Circumnavigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Astronautics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first person to fly in space, <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>, also became the first person to complete an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital spaceflight</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a> spaceship within 2 hours in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human-powered">Human-powered</span><span
