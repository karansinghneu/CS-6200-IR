href="/wiki/Annual_plant" title="Annual plant">annually-grown</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> plant. It is found only in <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">cultivation</a>, where it is the most commonly grown of all plants in the genus <i>Nicotiana</i>, and its leaves are commercially grown in many countries to be processed into tobacco. It grows to heights between 1 and 2 meters. Research is ongoing into its ancestry among wild <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana" title="Nicotiana">Nicotiana</a></i> species, but it is believed to be a hybrid of <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_sylvestris" title="Nicotiana sylvestris">Nicotiana sylvestris</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_tomentosiformis" title="Nicotiana tomentosiformis">Nicotiana tomentosiformis</a></i>, and possibly <i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_otophora" title="Nicotiana otophora">Nicotiana otophora</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicotiana_tabacum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The plant is indigenous to the island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Arawak" title="Arawak">Arawak</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta&#237;no">Taino</a> people were the first to use it and cultivate it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> \nIn 1560, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Nicot_de_Villemain" title="Jean Nicot de Villemain">Jean Nicot de Villemain</a>, then French ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, brought tobacco seeds and leaves as a "wonder drug" to the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> court. In 1586 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botanist" title="Botanist">botanist</a> Jaques Dalechamps gave the plant the name of <i>Herba nicotiana</i>, which was also adopted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linn%C3%A9" title="Linn&#233;">Linn&#233;</a>. It was considered a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decorative_plant" title="Decorative plant">decorative plant</a> at first, then a <a href="/wiki/Panacea" title="Panacea">panacea</a>, before it became a common snuff and tobacco plant. Tobacco arrived in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> at the beginning of the 17th century. The leaf extract was a popular pest control method up to the beginning of the 20th century. In 1851, the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_Stas" title="Jean Stas">Jean Stas</a> documented the use of tobacco extract as a murder <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poison</a>. The Belgian count <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Visart_de_Bocarm%C3%A9" title="Hippolyte Visart de Bocarm&#233;">Hippolyte Visart de Bocarm&#233;</a> had poisoned his brother-in-law with tobacco leaf extract in order to acquire some urgently needed money. This was the first exact proof of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaloids" title="Alkaloids">alkaloids</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic_medicine" title="Forensic medicine">forensic medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_ecology">Habitat and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicotiana_tabacum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>N. tabacum</i> is a native of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a> but it is now commercially <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">cultivated</a> worldwide. Other varieties are cultivated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ornamental_plants" title="Ornamental plants">ornamental plants</a> or grow as a <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weed</a>.\n</p><p><i>N. tabacum</i> is sensitive to temperature, air, ground humidity and the type of land. Temperatures of 20&#8211;30&#160;&#176;C (68&#8211;86&#160;&#176;F) are best for adequate growth; an atmospheric humidity of 80 to 85% and soil without a high level of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> are also optimal.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasites">Parasites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicotiana_tabacum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parasites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Potato Tuber moth (<i><a href="/wiki/Phthorimaea_operculella" title="Phthorimaea operculella">Phthorimaea operculella</a></i>) is an oligophagous insect that prefers to feed on plants of the family Solanaceae such as tobacco plants. Female <i>P. operculella</i> use the leaves to lay their eggs and the hatched larvae will eat away at the mesophyll of the leaf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_description">Botanical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicotiana_tabacum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Botanical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Nicotiana tabacum Linn&#233;</i> is a robust annual little branched herb up to 2.5&#160;m (8.2&#160;ft) high with large green leaves and long trumpet-shaped white-pinkish flowers. All parts are sticky, covered with short viscid-glandular hairs, which exude a yellow secretion containing nicotine.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaves">Leaves</span><span The dried leaves can also be used; they remain effective for 6 months after drying. The juice of the leaves can be rubbed on the body as an insect repellent. The leaves can be dried and chewed as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intoxicant" title="Intoxicant">intoxicant</a>. The dried leaves are also used as snuff or are smoked. This is the main species that is used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cigarettes" title="Cigarettes">cigarettes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cigar" title="Cigar">cigars</a>, and other products for smokers. A <a href="/wiki/Drying_oil" title="Drying oil">drying oil</a> is obtained from the seed.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curing_and_aging">Curing and aging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicotiana_tabacum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Curing and aging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After tobacco is harvested, it is cured (dried), and then aged to improve its flavour. There are four common methods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curing_tobacco" title="Curing tobacco">curing tobacco</a>: air curing, fire curing, flue curing, and sun curing. The curing method used depends on the type of tobacco and its intended use.\nAir-cured tobacco is sheltered from wind and sun in a well-ventilated barn, where it air dries for six to eight weeks. Air-cured tobacco is low in sugar, which gives the tobacco smoke a light, sweet flavour, and high in nicotine. Cigar and burley tobaccos are air cured.\n</p><p>In fire curing, smoke from a low-burning fire on the barn floor permeates the leaves. This gives the leaves a distinctive smoky aroma and flavor. Fire curing takes three to ten weeks and produces a tobacco low in sugar and high in nicotine. Pipe tobacco, chewing tobacco, and snuff are fire cured.\n</p><p>Flue-cured tobacco is kept in an enclosed barn heated by flues (pipes) of hot air, but the tobacco is not directly exposed to smoke. This method produces cigarette tobacco that is high in sugar and has medium to high levels of nicotine. It is the fastest method of curing, requiring about a week. Virginia tobacco that has been flue cured is also called bright tobacco, because flue curing turns its leaves gold, orange, or yellow.\n</p><p>Sun-cured tobacco dries uncovered in the sun. This method is used in Greece, Turkey, and other Mediterranean countries to produce oriental tobacco. Sun-cured tobacco is low in sugar and nicotine and is used in cigarettes.\n</p><p>Once the tobacco is cured, workers tie it into small bundles of about 20 leaves, called hands, or use a machine to make large blocks, called bales. The hands or bales are aged for one to three years to improve flavor and reduce bitterness.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
