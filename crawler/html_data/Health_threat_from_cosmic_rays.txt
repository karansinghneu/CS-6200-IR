rays</b> is the danger posed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_cosmic_rays" title="Galactic cosmic rays">galactic cosmic rays</a> (GCR) and <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2015, the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Office_of_Inspector_General" title="NASA Office of Inspector General">NASA Office of Inspector class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The deep-space radiation environment in ACE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="546" data-file-width="824" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/250px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/375px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg/500px-PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA16938-RadiationSources-InterplanetarySpace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sources of ionizing radiation in interplanetary space.</div></div></div>\n<p>The radiation environment of deep space is different from that on the Earth\'s surface or in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, due to the much larger flux of high-energy galactic cosmic rays (GCRs), along with radiation from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">solar proton events</a> (SPEs) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_belt" title="Radiation belt">radiation belts</a>. \n</p><p>Galactic cosmic rays (GCRs) consist of high energy <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> (85%), helium (14%) and other high energy <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a> (<a href="/wiki/HZE_ions" title="HZE ions">HZE ions</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THREE_-_Space_Radiation_Introduction_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-THREE_-_Space_Radiation_Introduction-1">[1]</a></sup>  Solar energetic particles consist primarily of protons accelerated by the Sun to high energies via proximity to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">solar flares</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">coronal mass ejections</a>. \nHeavy ions and low energy protons and helium particles are highly ionizing forms of radiation, which produce distinct biological damage compared to X-rays and gamma-rays. Microscopic energy deposition from highly ionizing particles consists of a core radiation track due to direct ionizations by the particle and low energy electrons produced in ionization, and a penumbra of higher energy electrons that may extend hundreds of microns from the particles path in tissue. The core track produces extremely large clusters of ionizations within a few <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a>, which is qualitatively distinct from energy deposition by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-rays" title="X-rays">X-rays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a>; hence human epidemiology data which only exists for these latter forms of radiation is limited in predicting the health risks from space radiation to astronauts.\n</p><p>But of course the radiation belts are within Earth&#8217;s magnetosphere and do not occur in deep space, while organ dose equivalents on the International Space Station are dominated by GCR not trapped radiation. Microscopic energy deposition in cells and tissues is distinct for GCR compared to X-rays on Earth, leading to both qualitative and quantitative differences in biological effects, while there is no human epidemiology data for GCR for cancer and other fatal risks.\n</p><p>The solar cycle is an approximately 11-year period of varying solar activity including solar maximum where the solar wind is strongest and solar minimum where the solar wind is weakest. Galactic cosmic rays create a continuous radiation dose throughout the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> that increases during solar minimum and decreases during solar maximum (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">solar activity</a>). The inner and outer radiation belts are two regions of trapped particles from the solar wind that are later accelerated by dynamic interaction with the Earth\'s magnetic field. While always high, the radiation dose in these belts can increase dramatically during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storms" title="Geomagnetic storms">geomagnetic storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Substorm" title="Substorm">substorms</a>. Solar proton events (SPEs) are bursts of energetic protons accelerated by the Sun. They occur relatively rarely and can produce extremely high radiation levels. Without thick shielding, SPEs are sufficiently strong to cause acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">radiation poisoning</a> and death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa2-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Life on the Earth\'s surface is protected from galactic cosmic rays by a number of factors:\n</p>\n<ol><li>The Earth\'s atmosphere is opaque to primary cosmic rays with energies below about 1 gigaelectron volt (GeV), so only secondary radiation can reach the surface. The secondary radiation is also attenuated by absorption in the atmosphere, as well as by radioactive decay in flight of some particles, such as muons. Particles entering from a direction far from the zenith are especially attenuated. The world\'s population receives an average of 0.4 milli<a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">sieverts</a> (mSv) of cosmic radiation annually (separate from other sources of radiation exposure like inhaled radon) due to atmospheric shielding. At 12&#160;km altitude, above most of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere\'s</a> protection, radiation as an annual rate rises to 20&#160;mSv at the equator to 50&#8211;120&#160;mSv at the poles, varying between solar maximum and minimum conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Missions beyond low Earth orbit transit the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belts</a>. Thus they may need to be shielded against exposure to cosmic rays, Van Allen radiation, or solar flares. The region between two and four Earth radii lies between the two radiation belts and is sometimes referred to as the "safe zone".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> See the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#Implications_for_space_travel" title="Van Allen radiation belt">implications of the Van Allen belts for space travel</a> for more information.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a>, embedded in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, also deflects cosmic rays. As a result, cosmic ray fluxes within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a> are inversely correlated with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">Electromagnetic radiation</a> created by lightning in clouds only a few miles high can create a safe zone in the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belts</a> that surround the earth. This zone, known as the "Van Allen Belt slot", may be a safe haven for <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">medium Earth orbits</a> (MEOs), protecting them from the Sun\'s intense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">radiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashes_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-flashes-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-space1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demirkol_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-demirkol-18">[18]</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>As a result, the energy input of GCRs to the atmosphere is negligible &#8211; about 10<sup>&#8722;9</sup> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> &#8211; roughly the same as starlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRC_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Of the above factors, all but the first one apply to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> craft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. Exposures on the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a> average 150&#160;mSv per year, although frequent crew rotations minimize individual risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historicalexposure_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-historicalexposure-20">[20]</a></sup> Astronauts on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a> missions received on average 1.2&#160;mSv/day and 1.4&#160;mSv/day respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historicalexposure_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-historicalexposure-20">[20]</a></sup>  Since the durations of the Apollo and Skylab missions were days and months, respectively, rather than years, the doses involved were smaller than would be expected on future long-term missions such as to a near-Earth asteroid or to Mars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-3">[3]</a></sup> (unless far more shielding could be provided).\n</p><p>On 31 May 2013, NASA scientists reported that a possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">manned mission to Mars</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-3">[3]</a></sup> may involve a great <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation risk</a> based on the amount of <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">energetic particle radiation</a> detected by the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">radiation assessment detector</a> (RAD) on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> while traveling from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in 2011&#8211;2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531a_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531b_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCI-20130531b-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20130530_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20130530-23">[23]</a></sup>  However, the absorbed dose and dose equivalent for a Mars mission were predicted in the early 1990s by Badhwar, Cucinotta, and others (see for example Badhwar, Cucinotta et al., Radiation Research vol. 138, 201-208, 1994) and the result of the MSL experiment are to a large extent consistent with these earlier predictions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_health_effects">Human health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a of radiation doses, includes the amount detected on the trip from Earth to Mars by the RAD on the MSL (2011&#8211;2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCI-20130531a_21-1"><a For example, the exposure from 6 months aboard the ISS is roughly a factor of 10 greater than that from an abdominal CT scan.</div></div></div>\n<p>The potential acute and chronic health effects of space radiation, as with other ionizing radiation exposures, involve both direct damage to DNA, indirect effects due to generation of reactive oxygen species, and changes to the biochemistry of cells and tissues, which can alter gene transcription and the tissue microenvironment along with producing DNA mutations. Acute (or early radiation) effects result from high radiation doses, and these are most likely to occur after solar particle events (SPEs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THREE_-_Acute_Effects_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-THREE_-_Acute_Effects-24">[24]</a></sup> Likely chronic effects of space radiation exposure include both stochastic events such as radiation <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenesis" title="Carcinogenesis">carcinogenesis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and deterministic degenerative tissue effects. To date, however, the only pathology associated with space radiation exposure is a higher risk for <a href="/wiki/Cataract#Radiation" title="Cataract">radiation cataract</a> among the astronaut corps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The health threat depends on the flux, energy spectrum, and nuclear composition of the radiation. The flux and energy spectrum depend on a variety of factors: short-term solar weather, long-term trends (such as an apparent increase since the 1950s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icrc2005.tifr.res.in_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-icrc2005.tifr.res.in-28">[28]</a></sup>), and position in the Sun\'s magnetic field. These factors are incompletely understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Mars_Radiation_Environment_Experiment" title="Mars Radiation Environment Experiment">Mars Radiation Environment Experiment</a> (MARIE) was launched in 2001 in order to collect more data.\nEstimates are that humans unshielded in interplanetary space would receive annually roughly 400 to 900&#160;mSv (compared to 2.4&#160;mSv on Earth) and that a Mars mission (12 months in flight and 18 months on Mars) might expose shielded astronauts to roughly 500 to 1000&#160;mSv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icrc2005.tifr.res.in_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-icrc2005.tifr.res.in-28">[28]</a></sup> These doses approach the 1 to 4&#160;Sv career limits advised by the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_on_Radiation_Protection_and_Measurements" title="National Council on Radiation Protection and Measurements">National Council on Radiation Protection and Measurements</a> (NCRP) for <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> activities in 1989, and the more recent NCRP recommendations of 0.5 to 2 Sv in 2000 based on updated information on dose to risk conversion factors. Dose limits depend on age at exposure and sex due to difference in <a href="/wiki/Susceptible_individual" title="Susceptible individual">susceptibility</a> with age, the added risks of <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian cancers</a> to women, and the variability of cancer risks such as <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> between men and women.\n</p><p>The quantitative biological effects of cosmic rays are poorly known, and are the subject of ongoing research. Several experiments, both in space and on Earth, are being carried out to evaluate the exact degree of danger. Additionally, the impact of the space microgravity environment on DNA repair has in part confounded the interpretation of some results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  Experiments over the last 10 years have shown results both higher and lower than predicted by current quality factors used in radiation protection, indicating large uncertainties exist. Experiments in 2007 at <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">Brookhaven National Laboratory</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/NASA_Space_Radiation_Laboratory" title="NASA Space Radiation Laboratory">NASA Space Radiation Laboratory</a> (NSRL) suggest that  biological damage due to a given exposure is actually about half what was previously estimated: specifically, it turns out that low energy protons cause more damage than high energy ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> This is explained by the fact that slower particles have more time to interact with molecules in the body. This may be interpreted as an acceptable result for space travel as the cells affected end up with greater energy deposition and are more likely to die without proliferating into tumors. This is in contrast to the current dogma on radiation exposure to human cells which considers lower energy radiation of higher weighting factor for tumor formation. Relative biological effectiveness (RBE) depends on radiation type described by particle charge number, Z, and kinetic energy per amu, E, and varies with tumor type with limited experimental data suggesting <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>\'s having the lowest RBE, liver tumors the highest RBE, and limited or no experimental data on RBE available for cancers that dominate human cancer risks including lung, stomach, breast, and <a href="/wiki/Bladder_cancer" title="Bladder cancer">bladder cancers</a>. Studies of Harderian gland tumors in a single strain of female mice with several heavy ions have been made, however it is not clear how well the RBE for this tumor type represents the RBE for human cancers such as lung, stomach, breast and bladder cancers nor how RBE changes with sex and genetic background.\n</p><p>Part of the <a href="/wiki/ISS_year_long_mission" title="ISS year long mission">ISS year long mission</a> is to determine the health impacts of cosmic ray exposure over the course of one year spent aboard the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.\n</p><p>However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_size" title="Sample size">sample sizes</a> for accurately estimating health risks directly from crew observations for the risks of concern (cancer, cataracts, cognitive and memory changes, late CNS risks, circulatory diseases, etc.) are large (typically &gt;&gt;10 persons) and necessarily involve long post-mission observation times (&gt;10 years). It will be difficult for a sufficient number of astronauts to occupy the ISS and for the missions to continue long enough to make an impact on risk predictions for late effects due to statistical limitations. Hence the need for ground-based research to predict cosmic ray health risks. In addition, radiation safety requirements mandate that risks should be adequately understood prior to astronauts incurring significant risks, and methods developed to mitigate the risks if necessary.\n</p><p>In September 2017, NASA reported <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation levels</a> on the surface of the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> were temporarily <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(radiation)" title="Orders of magnitude (radiation)">doubled</a>, and were associated with an <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">aurora</a> 25-times brighter than any observed earlier, due to a massive, and unexpected, <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">solar storm</a> in the middle of the month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHYS-20170930_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHYS-20170930-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_nervous_system">Central nervous system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Central nervous system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system_effects_from_radiation_exposure_during_spaceflight" title="Central nervous system effects from radiation exposure during spaceflight">Central nervous system effects from radiation exposure during spaceflight</a></div>\n<p>Hypothetical early and late effects on the central nervous system are of great concern to NASA and an area of active current research interest. It is postulated short- and long-term effects of CNS exposure to galactic cosmic radiation are likely to pose significant neurological health risks to human long-term space travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Estimates suggest considerable exposure to high energy heavy (HZE) ions as well as protons and secondary radiation during Mars or prolonged Lunar missions with estimates of whole body effective doses ranging from 0.17 to greater than 1.0&#160;Sv.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Given the high <a href="/wiki/Linear_energy_transfer" title="Linear energy transfer">linear energy transfer</a> potential of such particles, a considerable proportion of those cells exposed to HZE radiation are likely to die. Based on calculations of heavy ion fluences during space flight as well as various experimental cell models, as many as 5% of an astronaut&#8217;s cells might be killed during such missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> With respect to cells in critical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_regions" title="Brain regions">brain regions</a>, as many as 13% of such cells may be traversed at least once by an iron ion during a three-year Mars mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIRED-20140212_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-WIRED-20140212-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Several <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> astronauts reported seeing light <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_visual_phenomena" title="Cosmic ray visual phenomena">flashes</a>, although the precise biological mechanisms responsible are unclear. Likely pathways include heavy ion interactions with <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retinal photoreceptors</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation" title="Cherenkov radiation">Cherenkov radiation</a> resulting from particle interactions within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitreous_humor" title="Vitreous humor">vitreous humor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This phenomenon has been replicated on Earth by scientists at various institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> As the duration of the longest Apollo flights was less than two weeks, the astronauts had limited cumulative exposures and a corresponding low risk for radiation <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenesis" title="Carcinogenesis">carcinogenesis</a>. In addition, there were only 24 such astronauts, making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_analysis" title="Statistical analysis">statistical analysis</a> of any potential health effects problematic.\n</p><p>In the above discussion dose equivalents is units of Sievert (Sv) are noted, however the Sv is a unit for comparing cancer risks for different types of ionizing radiation. For CNS effects absorbed doses in Gy are more useful, while the RBE for CNS effects is poorly understood. Furthermore, stating "hypothetical" risk is problematic, while space radiation CNS risk estimates have largely focused on early and late detriments to memory and cognition (e.g. Cucinotta, Alp, Sulzman, and Wang, Life Sciences in Space Research, 2014).\n</p><p>On 31 December 2012, a <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>-supported study reported that manned spaceflight may harm the brains of astronauts and accelerate the onset of <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer\'s disease">Alzheimer\'s disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PLOS-20121231_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-PLOS-20121231-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceref_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-spaceref-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NasaWatch-20130103_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-NasaWatch-20130103-46">[46]</a></sup> This research is problematic due to many factors, inclusive of the intensity of which mice were exposed to radiation which far exceeds normal mission rates.\n</p><p>A review of CNS space radiobiology by Cucinotta, Alp, Sulzman, and Wang (Life Sciences in Space Research, 2014) summarizes research studies in small animals of changes to cognition and memory, neuro-inflammation, neuron morphology, and impaired neurogenesis in the hippocampus. Studies using simulated space radiation in small animals suggest temporary or long-term cognitive detriments could occur during a long-term space mission. Changes to neuron morphology in mouse hippocampus and pre-frontal cortex occur for heavy ions at low doses (&lt;0.3 Gy). Studies in mice and rats of chronic neuro-inflammation and behavioral changes  show variable results at low doses (~0.1 Gy or lower). Further research is needed to understand if such cognitive detriments induced by space radiation would occur in astronauts and whether they would negatively impact a Mars mission.\n</p><p>The cumulative heavy ion doses in space are low such that critical cells and cell components will receive only 0 or 1 particle traversal. The cumulative heavy ion dose for a Mars mission near solar minimum would be ~0.05 Gy and lower for missions at other times in the solar cycle. This suggests dose-rate effects will not occur for heavy ions as long as the total doses used in experimental studies in reasonably small (&lt;~0.1 Gy). At larger doses (&gt;~0.1 Gy) critical cells and cell components could receive more than one particle traversal, which is not reflective of the deep space environment for extended duration missions such as a mission to Mars. An alternative assumption would be if a tissues micro-environment is modified by a long-range signaling effect or change to biochemistry, whereby a particle traversal to some cells modifies the response of other cells not traversed by particles. There is limited experimental evidence, especially for central nervous system effects, available to evaluate this alternative assumption.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shielding">Shielding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Shielding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cosmic_ray_shielding_with_secondary_radiation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="320" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Cosmic_ray_shielding_with_secondary_radiation.jpg/220px-Cosmic_ray_shielding_with_secondary_radiation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/Cosmic_ray_shielding_with_secondary_radiation.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosmic_ray_shielding_with_secondary_radiation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Standard spacecraft shielding, integrated into hull design, is strong protection from most solar radiation, but defeats this purpose with high-energy cosmic rays, as it simply splits this into showers of secondary particles. This shower of secondary and fragmented particles may be reduced by the use of hydrogen or light elements for shielding.</div></div></div>\n<p>Material shielding can be effective against galactic cosmic rays, but thin shielding may actually make the problem worse for some of the higher energy rays, because more shielding causes an increased amount of <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">secondary radiation</a>, although thick shielding could counter such too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mass_shielding_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-mass_shielding-47">[47]</a></sup> The aluminium walls of the ISS, for example, are believed to produce a net reduction in radiation exposure. In interplanetary space, however, it is believed that thin aluminium shielding would give a net increase in radiation exposure but would gradually decrease as more shielding is added to capture generated secondary radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandone.org_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-islandone.org-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Studies of space radiation shielding should include tissue or water equivalent shielding along with the shielding material under study. This observation is readily understood by noting that the average tissue self-shielding of sensitive organs is about 10&#160;cm, and that secondary radiation produced in tissue such as low energy protons, helium and heavy ions are of high <a href="/wiki/Linear_energy_transfer" title="Linear energy transfer">linear energy transfer</a> (LET) and make significant contributions (&gt;25%) to the overall biological damage from GCR. Studies of aluminum, polyethylene, liquid hydrogen, or other shielding materials, will involve secondary radiation not reflective of secondary radiation produced in tissue, hence the need to include tissue equivalent shielding in studies of space radiation shielding effectiveness.\n</p><p>Several strategies are being studied for ameliorating the effects of this radiation hazard for planned human interplanetary spaceflight:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Spacecraft can be constructed out of hydrogen-rich plastics, rather than aluminium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa3_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa3-50">[50]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Material shielding has been considered:\n<ul><li>Liquid hydrogen, which would be brought along as fuel in any case, tends to give relatively good shielding, while producing relatively low levels of secondary radiation. Therefore, the fuel could be placed so as to act as a form of shielding around the crew. However, as fuel is consumed by the craft, the crew\'s shielding decreases.</li>\n<li>Water, which is necessary to sustain life, could also contribute to shielding. But it too is consumed during the journey unless waste products are utilized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist-51">[51]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Asteroids could serve to provide shielding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li></ul></li>\n<li>Magnetic deflection of charged radiation particles and/or electrostatic repulsion is a hypothetical alternative to pure conventional mass shielding under investigation. In theory, power requirements for a 5-meter torus drop from an excessive 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">GW</a> for a simple pure electrostatic shield (too discharged by space electrons) to a moderate 10&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatts</a> (kW) by using a hybrid design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandone.org_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-islandone.org-48">[48]</a></sup>  However, such complex active shielding is untried, with workability and practicalities more uncertain than material shielding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandone.org_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-islandone.org-48">[48]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Special provisions would also be necessary to protect against a solar proton event, which could increase fluxes to levels that would kill a crew in hours or days rather than months or years. Potential mitigation strategies include providing a small habitable space behind a spacecraft\'s water supply or with particularly thick walls or providing an option to abort to the protective environment provided by the Earth\'s magnetosphere. The Apollo mission used a combination of both strategies. Upon receiving confirmation of an SPE, astronauts would move to the Command Module, which had thicker aluminium walls than the Lunar Module, then return to Earth. It was later determined from measurements taken by instruments flown on Apollo that the Command Module would have provided sufficient shielding to prevent significant crew harm.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>None of these strategies currently provide a method of protection that would be known to be sufficient<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-parker-54">[54]</a></sup> while conforming to likely limitations on the mass of the payload at present (around $10,000/kg) launch prices. Scientists such as University of Chicago professor emeritus Eugene Parker are not optimistic it can be solved anytime soon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-parker-54">[54]</a></sup>  For passive mass shielding, the required amount could be too heavy to be affordably lifted into space without changes in economics (like hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Non-rocket_spacelaunch" title="Non-rocket spacelaunch">non-rocket spacelaunch</a> or usage of extraterrestrial resources) &#8212; many hundreds of metric tons for a reasonably-sized crew compartment. For instance, a NASA design study for an ambitious large spacestation envisioned 4 metric tons per square meter of shielding to drop radiation exposure to 2.5&#160;mSv annually (&#177; a factor of 2 uncertainty), less than the tens of millisieverts or more in some populated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Background_Radiation#Natural_background_radiation" title="Background Radiation">high natural background radiation areas</a> on Earth, but the sheer mass for that level of mitigation was considered practical only because it involved first building a lunar <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">mass driver</a> to launch material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mass_shielding_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-mass_shielding-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several active shielding methods have been considered that might be less massive than passive shielding, but they remain speculative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandone.org_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-islandone.org-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Since the type of radiation penetrating farthest through thick material shielding, deep in interplanetary space, is GeV positively charged nuclei, a repulsive electrostatic field has been proposed, but this has problems including plasma instabilities and the power needed for an accelerator constantly keeping the charge from being neutralized by deep-space electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>  A more common proposal is magnetic shielding generated by superconductors (or plasma currents). Among the difficulties with this proposal is that, for a compact system, magnetic fields up to 10&#8211;20 teslas could be required around a manned spacecraft, higher than the several teslas in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRI" title="MRI">MRI</a> machines. Such high fields can produce headaches and migraines in MRI patients, and long-duration exposure to such fields has not been studied. Opposing-electromagnet designs might cancel the field in the crew sections of the spacecraft, but would require more mass. It is also possible to use a combination of a magnetic field with an electrostatic field, with the spacecraft having zero total charge. The hybrid design would theoretically ameliorate the problems, but would be complex and possibly infeasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandone.org_48-4"><a href="#cite_note-islandone.org-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Part of the uncertainty is that the effect of human exposure to galactic cosmic rays is poorly known in quantitative terms. The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Space_Radiation_Laboratory" title="NASA Space Radiation Laboratory">NASA Space Radiation Laboratory</a> is currently studying the effects of radiation in living organisms as well as protective shielding.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drugs">Drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another line of research is the development of drugs that enhance the body\'s natural capacity to repair damage caused by radiation. Some of the drugs that are being considered are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retinoids" title="Retinoids">retinoids</a>, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamins" title="Vitamins">vitamins</a> with <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> properties, and molecules that retard cell division, giving the body time to fix damage before harmful mutations can be duplicated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timing_of_missions">Timing of missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Timing of missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to the potential negative effects of astronaut exposure to cosmic rays, solar activity may play a role in future space travel. Because galactic cosmic ray fluxes within the Solar System are lower during periods of strong solar activity, interplanetary travel during solar maximum should minimize the average dose to astronauts.\n</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Forbush_decrease" title="Forbush decrease">Forbush decrease</a> effect during coronal mass ejections can temporarily lower the flux of galactic cosmic rays, the short duration of the effect (1&#8211;3 days) and the approximately 1% chance that a CME generates a dangerous solar proton event limits the utility of timing missions to coincide with CMEs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_selection">Orbital selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Orbital selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Radiation dosage from the Earth\'s radiation belts is typically mitigated by selecting orbits that avoid the belts or pass through them relatively quickly. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, with low inclination, will generally be below the inner belt.\n</p><p>The orbits of the Earth-Moon system <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_Points" title="Lagrange Points">Lagrange points</a> <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>2</sub></a> - <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>5</sub></a> take them out of the protection of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> for approximately two-thirds of the time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The orbits of Earth-Sun system Lagrange Points <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L3" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>3</sub></a> - <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>5</sub></a> are always outside the protection of the Earth\'s magnetosphere.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
