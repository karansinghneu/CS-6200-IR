href="/wiki/File:Gaspra_rotation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Successive images of a rotating Gaspra</div></div></div>\n<p>Apart from a multitude of small craters, Gaspra has half a dozen large flat areas and concavities. One of these flat areas, (Dunne Regio), is a 5&#215;7-kilometer area that is flat to within 200&#160;m. It is uncertain whether these are the result of impacts or whether they are instead facets formed when Gaspra broke off its parent asteroid. In the weak, lopsided <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> of Gaspra, <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact craters</a> would naturally take on such flat, lopsided shapes, making this determination difficult. The flat facets and concavities give Gaspra a very angular appearance.\n</p><p>Gaspra appears to be fairly <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>-rich among the <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">S-type asteroids</a> (the surface appears to contain olivine and <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a> in the proportions 4:1 to 7:1<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granahan94_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Granahan94-7">[7]</a></sup>). There are no prominent albedo or color patterns, although a subtle color variation is seen across the surface (see image above).\n</p><p>Gaspra\'s surface lacks unambiguous craters of a size comparable to its radius, like those seen for example on <a href="/wiki/253_Mathilde" title="253 Mathilde">253 Mathilde</a>. A probable reason is that the collision that produced the <a href="/wiki/Flora_family" title="Flora family">Flora family</a> and Gaspra was relatively recent on an astronomical timescale, so that Gaspra has not yet had the opportunity to acquire many large craters since. Analysis of cratering rates suggests the age of the surface is between about 20 to 300 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veverka94_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Veverka94-8">[8]</a></sup> It was suggested in 2007 that the fresh, steep craters on Gaspra were formed by the <a href="/wiki/Baptistina_family" title="Baptistina family">Baptistina family</a>-forming event that happened near it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Grooves about 100&#8211;300&#160;m wide, up to 2.5&#160;km long, and tens of meters deep are seen on Gaspra\'s surface, which may be related to Gaspra\'s formation along with the rest of the Flora family in an asteroid collision. Their presence also suggest that it is a single coherent body, rather than a rubble pile. The grooves were likely created by impacts that shattered the underlying rock. A system of much more prominent grooves is seen on the Martian moon <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a>. The pitted appearance of some grooves may suggest that the surface is covered by a <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veverka94_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Veverka94-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The extensiveness of regolith on Gaspra and its presence overall is a matter of debate, and not fully understood. Visually, the somewhat subdued and mantled appearance suggests a substantial regolith. Also, correlations are seen between the subtle color variations and local topography, and it has been suggested that this is caused by the slow migration of regolith to lower areas. It is, however, difficult to explain the origin of a putative regolith. Firstly, Gaspra\'s <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a> is very small, so small that it is difficult to understand how it could keep a significant portion of fragments ejected by impacts from escaping. This may be alleviated if Gaspra is a porous body or started with a large regolith, but one has to explain how the original regolith appeared. A possible resolution of the issue may be that Gaspra obtained a regolith during the Flora-family forming impact that also created Gaspra itself.  Secondly, it has been estimated that the matter ejected by all the craters would be only enough to cover Gaspra with 10 m of regolith. However, some craters are much deeper than this without showing any structural difference on their walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belton92_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belton92-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gaspra\'s pole has been determined to point in the direction of <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">RA</a> 0h40m&#177;10m, <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> 27&#177;2&#176;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas94_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas94-5">[5]</a></sup> This is equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic coordinates</a> (&#946;, &#955;) = (21&#176;, 20&#176;) and gives an <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> of 72&#176;.\n</p><p>The Galileo flyby was too distant for a body of Gaspra\'s small size to noticeably affect Galileo\'s trajectory, so no information on Gaspra\'s mass was obtained. (Galileo also visited <a href="/wiki/243_Ida" title="243 Ida">243 Ida</a> where it discovered a moon, allowing a mass estimate there.)\n</p><p>Gaspra\'s surface area has been calculated at about 525&#160;km<sup>2</sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas94_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas94-5">[5]</a></sup> which, for comparison, is about the size of <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, or half the land area of Hong Kong.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo</a> flew by Gaspra on 29 October 1991, passing within 1,600 kilometers at a relative speed of about 8 kilometers per second (18,000&#160;mph). 57 images were returned to Earth, the closest taken from a distance of 5300&#160;km. The best images have a resolution of about 54 meters/pixel. The area around the southern pole was not seen during the flyby, but the remaining 80% of the asteroid was imaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veverka94_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Veverka94-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because Gaspra\'s position was only known to within about 200&#160;km before the encounter, and the camera\'s field of view was only about 5&#176; across, Galileo would not know where to point to capture images of the asteroid once it was closer than 70,000&#160;km. This would render the encounter not very interesting scientifically. To overcome this problem, a pioneering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_navigation" title="Optical navigation">optical navigation</a> campaign was implemented by the Galileo spacecraft team to reduce the uncertainty of Gaspra\'s position using images captured during the approach to Gaspra. This was spectacularly successful and allowed the spacecraft to obtain images from as close as 5300&#160;km. At this closest range, the pointing was still not known quite accurately enough, but the camera actually took a 51 image mosaic so as to capture Gaspra on at least one image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veverka94_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Veverka94-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=951_Gaspra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-jpldata-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-7"><sup><i><b>h</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-jpldata_1-8"><sup><i><b>i</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://ssd.jpl.nasa.gov/sbdb.cgi?sstr=2000951" rel="nofollow">"JPL Small-Body Database Browser: 951 Gaspra (1916 S45)"</a> (2017-07-05 last obs.).
