the types of bodies in the Solar System</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>minor planet</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> in direct <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> (or more broadly, any <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary system</a>) that is neither a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> nor exclusively classified as a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dual_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dual-1">[a]</a></sup> Before 2006 the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) officially used the term <i>minor planet</i>, but during that year\'s meeting it <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet" title="IAU definition of planet">reclassified</a> minor planets and comets into <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body" title="Small Solar System body">small Solar System bodies</a> (SSSBs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-res-2">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Minor planets can be <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy)" title="Trojan (astronomy)">trojans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">centaurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> objects, and other <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian objects</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> As of 2018, the orbits of 757,626 minor planets were archived at the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a>,  516,386 of which had received permanent numbers <span class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">(for the <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_planets" title="List of minor planets">complete list</a>, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_planets#Main_index" title="List of minor planets">index</a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-ArchiveStatistics_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-ArchiveStatistics-4">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first minor planet to be discovered was <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a> in 1801. The term <i>minor planet</i> has been used since the 19th century to describe these objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> The term <b>planetoid</b> has also been used, especially for larger (planetary) objects such as those the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) has called <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a> since 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> Historically, the terms <i>asteroid</i>, <i>minor planet</i>, and <i>planetoid</i> have been more or less synonymous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-a-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encarta_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-encarta-8">[7]</a></sup> This terminology has become more complicated by the discovery of numerous minor planets beyond the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, especially trans-Neptunian objects that are generally not considered asteroids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encarta_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-encarta-8">[7]</a></sup> A minor planet seen releasing gas may be dually classified as a comet.\n</p><p>Objects are called dwarf planets if their own gravity is sufficient to achieve <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">hydrostatic equilibrium</a> and form an <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoidal</a> shape. All other minor planets and comets are called <i>small Solar System bodies</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-res-2">[1]</a></sup> The IAU stated that the term <i>minor planet</i> may still be used, but the term <i>small Solar System body</i> will be preferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> However, for purposes of numbering and naming, the traditional distinction between minor planet and comet is still used.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Populations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor-planet_groups" title="List of minor-planet groups">List of minor-planet groups</a></div>\n<p>Hundreds of thousands of minor planets have been discovered within the Solar System and thousands more are discovered each month. The Minor Planet Center has documented over 188 million observations and 757,626 minor planets, of which 516,386 have orbits known well enough to be assigned <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions" title="Astronomical naming conventions">permanent official numbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-ArchiveStatistics_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-ArchiveStatistics-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Of these, 21,787 have official names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-ArchiveStatistics_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-ArchiveStatistics-4">[3]</a></sup> As of  30&#160;December&#160;2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the lowest-numbered unnamed minor planet is <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(3708)_1974_FV1" title="(3708) 1974 FV1">(3708) 1974 FV<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">1</span></span></a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> and the highest-numbered named minor planet is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/516560_Annapolisroyal" title="516560 Annapolisroyal">516560 Annapolisroyal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are various broad minor-planet populations:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">Asteroids</a>; traditionally, most have been bodies in the inner Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encarta_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-encarta-8">[7]</a></sup>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">Near-Earth asteroids</a>, those whose orbits take them inside the orbit of Mars. Further subclassification of these, based on orbital distance, is used:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neog_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-neog-13">[12]</a></sup>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apohele_asteroid" title="Apohele asteroid">Apohele asteroids</a> orbit inside of Earth\'s perihelion distance and thus are contained entirely within the orbit of Earth.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Aten_asteroid" title="Aten asteroid">Aten asteroids</a>, those that have semi-major axes of less than Earth\'s and aphelion (furthest distance from the Sun) greater than 0.983 AU.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_asteroids" title="Apollo asteroids">Apollo asteroids</a> are those asteroids with a semimajor axis greater than Earth\'s, while having a perihelion distance of 1.017 AU or less. Like Aten asteroids, Apollo asteroids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth-crosser_asteroid" title="Earth-crosser asteroid">Earth-crossers</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Amor_asteroid" title="Amor asteroid">Amor asteroids</a> are those near-<a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> asteroids that approach the orbit of Earth from beyond, but do not cross it. Amor asteroids are further subdivided into four subgroups, depending on where their semimajor axis falls between Earth\'s orbit and the asteroid belt;</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_trojan" title="Earth trojan">Earth trojans</a>, asteroids sharing Earth\'s orbit and gravitationally locked to it. As of 2011, the only one known is <a href="/wiki/2010_TK7" title="2010 TK7">2010 TK<sub>7</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature475_7357_481_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature475_7357_481-14">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mars_trojan" title="Mars trojan">Mars trojans</a>, asteroids sharing Mars\'s orbit and gravitationally locked to it. As of 2007, eight such asteroids are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sp465_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-sp465-15">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">Asteroid belt</a>, whose members follow roughly circular orbits between Mars and Jupiter. These are the original and best-known group of asteroids.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jupiter_trojan" title="Jupiter trojan">Jupiter trojans</a>, asteroids sharing Jupiter\'s orbit and gravitationally locked to it. Numerically they are estimated to equal the main-belt asteroids.</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Distant_minor_planet" title="Distant minor planet">Distant minor planets</a>; an umbrella term for minor planets in the outer Solar System.\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centaur_(planetoid)" title="Centaur (planetoid)">Centaurs</a>, bodies in the outer Solar System between Jupiter and Neptune. They have unstable orbits due to the gravitational influence of the giant planets, and therefore must have come from elsewhere, probably outside Neptune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Neptune_trojan" title="Neptune trojan">Neptune trojans</a>, bodies sharing Neptune\'s orbit and gravitationally locked to it. Although only a handful are known, there is evidence that Neptune trojans are more numerous than either the asteroids in the asteroid belt or the Jupiter trojans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_objects" title="Trans-Neptunian objects">Trans-Neptunian objects</a>, bodies at or beyond the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>, the outermost planet.\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a>, objects inside an apparent population drop-off approximately 55 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> from the Sun.\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">Classical Kuiper belt objects</a> like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Makemake_(dwarf_planet)" title="Makemake (dwarf planet)">Makemake</a>, also known as cubewanos, are in primordial, relatively circular orbits that are not in resonance with Neptune.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Resonant_trans-Neptunian_object" title="Resonant trans-Neptunian object">Resonant Kuiper belt objects</a>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutinos" title="Plutinos">Plutinos</a>, bodies like <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> that are in a 2:3 resonance with Neptune.</li></ul></li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Scattered_disc" title="Scattered disc">Scattered disc</a> objects like <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelia</a> outside the Kuiper belt. These are thought to have been scattered by Neptune.\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Resonant_trans-Neptunian_object" title="Resonant trans-Neptunian object">Resonant scattered disc objects</a>.</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Detached_object" title="Detached object">Detached objects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">Sedna</a>, with both aphelia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelia</a> outside the Kuiper belt.\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sednoid" title="Sednoid">Sednoids</a>, detached objects with perihelia greater than 75 AU (Sedna and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/2012_VP113" title="2012 VP113">2012 VP<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">113</span></span></a></span>).</li></ul></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Oort_cloud" title="Oort cloud">Oort cloud</a>, a hypothetical population thought to be the source of <a href="/wiki/Comet#Orbital_characteristics" title="Comet">long-period comets</a> that may extend out to 50,000 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> from the Sun.</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Minor_planets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">Astronomical naming conventions &#167;&#160;Minor planets</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Minor_planet_count.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="790" decoding="async" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Minor_planet_count.svg/250px-Minor_planet_count.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Minor_planet_count.svg/375px-Minor_planet_count.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Minor_planet_count.svg/500px-Minor_planet_count.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Minor_planet_count.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Out of a total of more than 700,000 discovered minor planets, 66% have been numbered (green) and 34% remain unnumbered (red). Only a small fraction of 20,071 minor planets (3%) have been named (purple).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-ArchiveStatistics_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-ArchiveStatistics-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>All <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_bodies" title="Astronomical bodies">astronomical bodies</a> in the Solar System need a distinct designation. The <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Minor_planets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">naming of minor planets</a> runs through a three-step process. First, a provisional designation is given upon discovery&#8212;because the object still may turn out to be a false positive or <a href="/wiki/Lost_minor_planet" title="Lost minor planet">become lost later on</a>&#8212;called a <i>provisionally designated minor planet</i>. After the <a href="/wiki/Observation_arc" title="Observation arc">observation arc</a> is accurate enough to predict its future location, a minor planet is formally designated and receives a number. It is then a <i>numbered minor planet</i>. Finally, in the third step, it may be named by its discoverers. However, only a small fraction of all minor planets have been named. The vast majority is either numbered or has still only a provisional designation. Example of the naming process:\n</p>\n<ul><li><span class="nowrap">1932 HA</span> &#8211; provisional designation upon discovery on 24 April 1932</li>\n<li><span class="nowrap">(1862) 1932 HA</span> &#8211; formal designation, receives an official number</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/1862_Apollo" title="1862 Apollo">1862 Apollo</a> &#8211; named Minor planet, receives a name, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy#Minor_planets" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">alphanumeric code</a> is dropped</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisional_designation">Provisional designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Provisional designation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">Provisional designation in astronomy</a></div>\n<p>A newly discovered minor planet is given a <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">provisional designation</a>. For example, the provisional designation <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/2002_AT4" title="2002 AT4">2002 AT<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">4</span></span></a></span> consists of the year of discovery (2002) and an alphanumeric code indicating the half-month of discovery and the sequence within that half-month. Once an asteroid\'s orbit has been confirmed, it is given a number, and later may also be given a name (e.g. <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">433 Eros</a>). The formal naming convention uses parentheses around the number, but dropping the parentheses is quite common. Informally, it is common to drop the number altogether, or to drop it after the first mention when a name is repeated in running text.\n</p><p>Minor planets that have been given a number but not a name keep their provisional designation, e.g. <a href="/wiki/(29075)_1950_DA" title="(29075) 1950 DA">(29075) 1950 DA</a>. Because modern discovery techniques are finding vast numbers of new asteroids, they are increasingly being left unnamed. The earliest discovered to be left unnamed was for a long time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(3360)_1981_VA" title="(3360) 1981 VA">(3360) 1981 VA</a>, now <a href="/wiki/3360_Syrinx" title="3360 Syrinx">3360 Syrinx</a>; as of September 2008, this distinction is held by (3708)<span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1974_FV1" title="1974 FV1"> 1974 FV<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">1</span></span></a></span>. On rare occasions, a small object\'s provisional designation may become used as a name in itself: the then unnamed <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/(15760)_1992_QB1" title="(15760) 1992 QB1">(15760) 1992 QB<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">1</span></span></a></span> gave its "name" to a group of objects that became known as <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical Kuiper belt objects</a> ("cubewanos") before it was finally named <a href="/wiki/15760_Albion" title="15760 Albion">15760 Albion</a> in January 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A few objects are cross-listed as both comets and asteroids, such as <a href="/wiki/4015_Wilson%E2%80%93Harrington" title="4015 Wilson&#8211;Harrington">4015 Wilson&#8211;Harrington</a>, which is also listed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/107P/Wilson%E2%80%93Harrington" title="107P/Wilson&#8211;Harrington">107P/Wilson&#8211;Harrington</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering">Numbering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Numbering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet_designation" title="Minor planet designation">Minor planet designation</a></div>\n<p>Minor planets are awarded an official number once their orbits are confirmed. With the increasing rapidity of discovery, these are now six-figure numbers. The switch from five figures to six figures arrived with the publication of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Circular" title="Minor Planet Circular">Minor Planet Circular</a> (MPC) of October 19, 2005, which saw the highest numbered minor planet jump from 99947 to 118161.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-ArchiveStatistics_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-ArchiveStatistics-4">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_conflicts_with_minor_planets" title="Name conflicts with minor planets">Name conflicts with minor planets</a></div>\n<p>The first few asteroids were named after figures from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a> but as such names started to dwindle the names of famous people, literary characters, discoverer\'s wives, children, and even television characters were used.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd>The first asteroid to be given a non-mythological name was <a href="/wiki/20_Massalia" title="20 Massalia">20 Massalia</a>, named after the Greek name for the city of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The first to be given an entirely non-Classical name was <a href="/wiki/45_Eugenia" title="45 Eugenia">45 Eugenia</a>, named after Empress <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A9nie_de_Montijo" title="Eug&#233;nie de Montijo">Eug&#233;nie de Montijo</a>, the wife of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>. For some time only female (or feminized) names were used; <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> was the first man to have an asteroid named after him, but his name was feminized to <a href="/wiki/54_Alexandra" title="54 Alexandra">54 Alexandra</a>. This unspoken tradition lasted until <a href="/wiki/334_Chicago" title="334 Chicago">334 Chicago</a> was named; even then, female names show up in the list for years after.</dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eccentric">Eccentric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eccentric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd>As the number of asteroids began to run into the hundreds, and eventually, in the thousands, discoverers began to give them increasingly frivolous names. The first hints of this were <a href="/wiki/482_Petrina" title="482 Petrina">482 Petrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/483_Seppina" title="483 Seppina">483 Seppina</a>, named after the discoverer\'s pet dogs. However, there was little controversy about this until 1971, upon the naming of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2309_Mr._Spock" title="2309 Mr. Spock">2309 Mr. Spock</a> (the name of the discoverer\'s cat). Although the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">IAU</a> subsequently banned pet names as sources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> eccentric asteroid names are still being proposed and accepted, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4321_Zero" title="4321 Zero">4321 Zero</a>, <a href="/wiki/6042_Cheshirecat" title="6042 Cheshirecat">6042 Cheshirecat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9007_James_Bond" title="9007 James Bond">9007 James Bond</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/13579_Allodd" title="13579 Allodd">13579 Allodd</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/24680_Alleven" title="24680 Alleven">24680 Alleven</a>, and <a href="/wiki/26858_Misterrogers" title="26858 Misterrogers">26858 Misterrogers</a>.</dd></dl>\n<h4><span id="Discoverer.27s_name"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Discoverer\'s_name">Discoverer\'s name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discoverer\'s name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd>A well-established rule is that, unlike comets, minor planets may not be named after their discoverer(s). One way to circumvent this rule has been for astronomers to exchange the courtesy of naming their discoveries after each other. An exception to this rule is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/96747_Crespodasilva" title="96747 Crespodasilva">96747 Crespodasilva</a>, which was named after its discoverer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucy_d%27Escoffier_Crespo_da_Silva" title="Lucy d\'Escoffier Crespo da Silva">Lucy d\'Escoffier Crespo da Silva</a>, because she died shortly after the discovery, at age 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<dl><dd>Names were adapted to various languages from the beginning. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Ceres" title="1 Ceres">1 Ceres</a>, <i>Ceres</i> being its Anglo-Latin name, was actually named <i>Cerere</i>, the Italian form of the name. German, French, Arabic, and Hindi use forms similar to the English, whereas Russian uses a form, <i>Tserera</i>, similar to the Italian. In Greek, the name was translated to &#916;&#942;&#956;&#951;&#964;&#961;&#945; (<a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>), the Greek equivalent of the Roman goddess Ceres. In the early years, before it started causing conflicts, asteroids named after Roman figures were generally translated in Greek; other examples are &#7981;&#961;&#945; (<a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>) for <a href="/wiki/3_Juno" title="3 Juno">3 Juno</a>, &#7961;&#963;&#964;&#943;&#945; (<a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">Hestia</a>) for <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">4 Vesta</a>, &#935;&#955;&#969;&#961;&#943;&#962; (<a href="/wiki/Chloris" title="Chloris">Chloris</a>) for <a href="/wiki/8_Flora" title="8 Flora">8 Flora</a>, and &#928;&#943;&#963;&#964;&#951; (<a href="/wiki/Pistis" title="Pistis">Pistis</a>) for <a href="/wiki/37_Fides" title="37 Fides">37 Fides</a>. In Chinese, the names are not given the Chinese forms of the deities they are named after, but rather typically have a syllable or two for the character of the deity or person, followed by &#31070; \'god(dess)\' or &#22899; \'woman\' if just one syllable, plus &#26143; \'star/planet\', so that most asteroid names are written with three Chinese characters. Thus Ceres is &#31296;&#31070;&#26143; \'grain goddess planet\',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Pallas is &#26234;&#31070;&#26143; \'wisdom goddess planet\', etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties_of_comets_and_minor_planets">Physical properties of comets and minor planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physical properties of comets and minor planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Commission 15<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commission15_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Commission15-25">[24]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> is dedicated to the Physical Study of Comets &amp; Minor Planets.\n</p><p>Archival data on the physical properties of comets and minor planets are found in the PDS Asteroid/Dust Archive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDS_Asteroid/Dust_Archive_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDS_Asteroid/Dust_Archive-26">[25]</a></sup> This includes <a href="/wiki/Standard_asteroid_physical_characteristics" title="Standard asteroid physical characteristics">standard asteroid physical characteristics</a> such as the properties of binary systems, occultation timings and diameters, masses, densities, rotation periods, surface temperatures, albedoes, spin vectors, taxonomy, and absolute magnitudes and slopes. In addition, European Asteroid Research Node (E.A.R.N.), an association of asteroid research groups, maintains a Data Base of Physical and Dynamical Properties of Near Earth Asteroids.<sup
