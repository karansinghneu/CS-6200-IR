He was worshipped from at least the late <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Egypt" title="Prehistoric Egypt">prehistoric Egypt</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">Ptolemaic Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">Roman Egypt</a>. Different forms of Horus are recorded in history and these are treated as distinct gods by <a href="/wiki/Egyptology" title="Egyptology">Egyptologists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> These various forms may possibly be different manifestations of the same multi-layered deity in which certain attributes or <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretic</a> relationships are emphasized, not necessarily in opposition but complementary to one another, consistent with how the Ancient Egyptians viewed the multiple facets of reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was most often depicted as a falcon, most likely a <a href="/wiki/Lanner_falcon" title="Lanner falcon">lanner falcon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Peregrine_falcon" title="Peregrine falcon">peregrine falcon</a>, or as a man with a falcon head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The earliest recorded form of Horus is the <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity" title="Tutelary deity">tutelary deity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nekhen" title="Nekhen">Nekhen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a>, who is the first known national god, specifically related to the  ruling <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> who in time came to be regarded as a manifestation of Horus in life and <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> in death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> The most commonly encountered family relationship describes Horus as the son of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> and Osiris, and he plays a key role in the <a href="/wiki/Osiris_myth" title="Osiris myth">Osiris myth</a> as Osiris\'s heir and the rival to <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, the murderer of Osiris. In another tradition <a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a> is regarded as his mother and sometimes as his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-1">[1]</a></sup> Horus served many functions, most notably being a god of kingship and the sky.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a>\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Horus is recorded in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">Egyptian hieroglyphs</a> as <i>&#7717;r.w</i> "Falcon"; the pronunciation has been reconstructed as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet Additional meanings are thought to have been "the distant one" or "one who is above, over".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As the language changed over time, it appeared in <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic varieties</a> variously as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ho&#720;&#638;</span> or <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#295;o&#720;&#638;</span> and was adopted into <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> as <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#8047;&#961;&#959;&#962;</span> <i>H&#333;ros</i> (pronounced at the time as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ho&#720;&#638;os</span>). It also survives in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Egyptian" title="Late Egyptian">Late Egyptian</a> and Coptic <a href="/wiki/Theophoric_name" title="Theophoric name">theophoric name</a> forms such as <a href="/wiki/Siese" title="Siese">Siese</a> \n"son of Isis" and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Harsiese_(disambiguation)" title="Harsiese (disambiguation)">Harsiese</a> "Horus, Son of Isis".\n</p><p>Nekheny may have been another falcon god worshipped at <a href="/wiki/Nekhen" title="Nekhen">Nekhen</a>, city of the falcon, with whom Horus was identified from early on.\n</p><p>Horus may be shown as a falcon on the <a href="/wiki/Narmer_Palette" title="Narmer Palette">Narmer Palette</a>, dating from about the <a href="/wiki/31st_century_BC" title="31st century BC">31st century BC</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Note_of_changes_over_time">Note of changes over time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Note of changes over time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In early Egypt, Horus was the brother of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nephthys" title="Nephthys">Nephthys</a>. As different cults formed, he became the son of Isis and Osiris. Isis remained the sister of Osiris, Set, and Nephthys.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horus_and_the_pharaoh">Horus and the pharaoh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Horus and the pharaoh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Texts" title="Pyramid Texts">Pyramid Texts</a></i> (c. 2400&#8211;2300 BC) describe the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> in different characters as both Horus and Osiris. The pharaoh as Horus in life became the pharaoh as Osiris in death, where he was united with the other gods. New incarnations of Horus succeeded the deceased pharaoh on earth in the form of new pharaohs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The lineage of Horus, the eventual product of unions between the children of <a href="/wiki/Atum" title="Atum">Atum</a>, may have been a means to explain and justify pharaonic power. The gods produced by Atum were all representative of cosmic and terrestrial forces in Egyptian life. By identifying Horus as the offspring of these forces, then identifying him with Atum himself, and finally identifying the Pharaoh with Horus, the Pharaoh theologically had dominion over all the world.\n</p><p>The notion of Horus as the pharaoh seems to have been superseded by the concept of the pharaoh as the son of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Fifth Dynasty of Egypt">Fifth Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" </td></tr></tbody></table>\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th style="background: navajowhite; border-bottom: 1px solid peru; padding: 0.5em">r&#705;-&#7717;r-3&#7723;ty "Ra-Horakhty"<br/>in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyphs</a>\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sky_god">Sky god</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sky god">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since Horus was said to be the sky, he was considered to also contain the sun and moon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It became said<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the sun was his right eye and the moon his left, and that they traversed the sky when he, a falcon, flew across it. Later, the reason that the moon was not as bright as the sun was explained by a tale, known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Contendings_of_Horus_and_Seth" title="The Contendings of Horus and Seth">The Contendings of Horus and Seth</a></i>. In this tale, it was said that Set, the patron of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">Upper Egypt</a>, and Horus, the patron of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>, had battled for Egypt brutally, with neither side victorious, until eventually, the gods sided with Horus.\n</p><p>As Horus was the ultimate victor he became known as <i>&#7717;r.w wr</i> "Horus the Great", but more usually translated "Horus the Elder". In the struggle, Set had lost a <a href="/wiki/Testicle" title="Testicle">testicle</a>, and Horus\' eye was gouged out.\n</p><p>Horus was occasionally shown in art as a naked boy with a finger in his mouth sitting on a <a href="/wiki/Nymphaea_lotus#The_white_lotus_in_Ancient_Egypt" title="Nymphaea lotus">lotus</a> with his mother. In the form of a youth, Horus was referred to as <i>nfr &#7717;r.w</i> "Good Horus", transliterated Neferhor, Nephoros or Nopheros (reconstructed as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">na&#720;firu &#295;a&#720;ruw</span>).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eye_of_Horus.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="706" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Eye_of_Horus.svg/160px-Eye_of_Horus.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Eye_of_Horus.svg/240px-Eye_of_Horus.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Eye_of_Horus.svg/320px-Eye_of_Horus.svg.png 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eye_of_Horus.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" title="Eye of Horus">Eye of Horus</a> or <i>Wedjat</i></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" title="Eye of Horus">Eye of Horus</a> is an ancient Egyptian symbol of protection and royal power from deities, in this case from Horus or <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>. The symbol is seen on images of Horus\' mother, Isis, and on other deities associated with her. In the Egyptian language, the word for this symbol was "wedjat" (<i>w&#607;t</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was the eye of one of the earliest of Egyptian deities, <a href="/wiki/Wadjet" title="Wadjet">Wadjet</a>, who later became associated with <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mut" title="Mut">Mut</a>, and Hathor as well. Wadjet was a <a href="/wiki/Solar_deity" title="Solar deity">solar deity</a> and this symbol began as her all-seeing eye. In early artwork, Hathor is also depicted with this eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Funerary amulets were often made in the shape of the Eye of Horus. The Wedjat or Eye of Horus is "the central element" of seven "<a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faience" title="Faience">faience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnelian" title="Carnelian">carnelian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a>" bracelets found on the mummy of <a href="/wiki/Shoshenq_II" title="Shoshenq II">Shoshenq II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman-14">[14]</a></sup> The Wedjat "was intended to protect the king [here] in the afterlife"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman-14">[14]</a></sup> and to ward off evil. Egyptian and Near Eastern sailors would frequently paint the symbol on the bow of their vessel to ensure safe sea travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_between_Horus_and_Set">Conflict between Horus and Set</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conflict between Horus and Set">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="984" data-file-width="1732" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg/220px-Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg/330px-Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg/440px-Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egypte_louvre_091_faucon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Horus, <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shen_ring" title="Shen ring">Shen rings</a> in his grasp</div></div></div>\n<p>Horus was told by his mother, Isis, to protect the people of Egypt from <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, the god of the desert, who had killed Horus\' father, Osiris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Horus had many battles with Set, not only to avenge his father, but to choose the rightful ruler of Egypt. In these battles, Horus came to be associated with Lower Egypt, and became its patron.\n</p><p>According to <i>The Contendings of Horus and Seth</i>, Set is depicted as trying to prove his dominance by seducing Horus and then having <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> with him. However, Horus places his hand between his thighs and catches Set\'s <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>, then subsequently throws it in the river so that he may not be said to have been inseminated by Set. Horus then deliberately spreads his own semen on some <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a>, which was Set\'s favorite food. After Set had eaten the lettuce, they went to the gods to try to settle the argument over the rule of Egypt. The gods first listened to Set\'s claim of dominance over Horus, and call his semen forth, but it answered from the river, invalidating his claim. Then, the gods listened to Horus\' claim of having dominated Set, and call his semen forth, and it answered from inside Set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_-_Figure_of_a_Horus_Falcon_-_Walters_571484_-_Right.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="819" decoding="async" Horus and Set challenged each other to a boat race, where they each raced in a boat made of stone. Horus and Set agreed, and the race started. But Horus had an edge: his boat was made of wood painted to resemble stone, rather than true stone. Set\'s boat, being made of heavy stone, sank, but Horus\' did not. Horus then won the race, and Set stepped down and officially gave Horus the throne of Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup> After the New Kingdom, Set was still considered lord of the desert and its oases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In many versions of the story, Horus and Set divide the realm between them. This division can be equated with any of several fundamental dualities that the Egyptians saw in their world. Horus may receive the fertile lands around the Nile, the core of Egyptian civilization, in which case Set takes the barren desert or the foreign lands that are associated with it; Horus may rule the earth while Set dwells in the sky; and each god may take one of the two traditional halves of the country, Upper and Lower Egypt, in which case either god may be connected with either region. Yet in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memphite_Theology" title="Memphite Theology">Memphite Theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a>, as judge, first apportions the realm between the claimants and then reverses himself, awarding sole control to Horus. In this peaceable union, Horus and Set are reconciled, and the dualities that they represent have been resolved into a united whole. Through this resolution, order is restored after the tumultuous conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEte_Velde196759&#8211;63_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEte_Velde196759&#8211;63-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Egyptologists have often tried to connect the conflict between the two gods with political events early in Egypt\'s history or prehistory. The cases in which the combatants divide the kingdom, and the frequent association of the paired Horus and Set with the union of Upper and Lower Egypt, suggest that the two deities represent some kind of division within the country. Egyptian tradition and archaeological evidence indicate that Egypt was united at the beginning of its history when an Upper Egyptian kingdom, in the south, conquered Lower Egypt in the north. The Upper Egyptian rulers called themselves "followers of Horus", and Horus became the tutelary deity of the unified nation and its kings. Yet Horus and Set cannot be easily equated with the two halves of the country. Both deities had several cult centers in each region, and Horus is often associated with Lower Egypt and Set with Upper Egypt. Other events may have also affected the myth. Before even Upper Egypt had a single ruler, two of its major cities were <a href="/wiki/Nekhen" title="Nekhen">Nekhen</a>, in the far south, and <a href="/wiki/Nagada" title="Nagada">Nagada</a>, many miles to the north. The rulers of Nekhen, where Horus was the patron deity, are generally believed to have unified Upper Egypt, including Nagada, under their sway. Set was associated with Nagada, so it is possible that the divine conflict dimly reflects an enmity between the cities in the distant past. Much later, at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Second Dynasty of Egypt">Second Dynasty</a> (c. 2890&#8211;2686 BCE), Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Seth-Peribsen" title="Seth-Peribsen">Seth-Peribsen</a> used the <a href="/wiki/Set_animal" title="Set animal">Set animal</a> to writing his <a href="/wiki/Serekh" title="Serekh">serekh</a> name in place of the falcon hieroglyph representing Horus. His successor <a href="/wiki/Khasekhemwy" title="Khasekhemwy">Khasekhemwy</a> used both Horus and Set in the writing of his serekh. This evidence has prompted conjecture that the Second Dynasty saw a clash between the followers of the Horus king and the worshippers of Set led by Seth-Peribsen. Khasekhemwy\'s use of the two animal symbols would then represent the reconciliation of the two factions, as does the resolution of the myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Heru-pa-khered_.28Horus_the_Younger.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Heru-pa-khered_(Horus_the_Younger)">Heru-pa-khered (Horus the Younger)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heru-pa-khered (Horus the Younger)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Horus the Younger, <a href="/wiki/Harpocrates" title="Harpocrates">Harpocrates</a> to the Ptolemaic Greeks, is represented in the form of a youth wearing a lock of hair (a sign of youth) on the right of his head while sucking his finger. In addition, he usually wears the united crowns of Egypt, the crown of Upper Egypt and the crown of Lower Egypt. He is a form of the rising sun, representing its earliest light.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Her-ur_.28Horus_the_Elder.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Her-ur_(Horus_the_Elder)">Her-ur (Horus the Elder)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Her-ur (Horus the Elder)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In this form, he was represented as the god of light and the husband of <a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a>. He was one of the oldest gods of ancient Egypt. He became the patron of <a href="/wiki/Nekhen" title="Nekhen">Nekhen (Hierakonpolis)</a> and the first national god (\'God of the Kingdom\'). Later, he also became the patron of the pharaohs, and was called the son of truth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> &#8211; signifying his role as an important upholder of <a href="/wiki/Maat" title="Maat">Maat</a>. He was seen as a great falcon with outstretched wings whose right eye was the sun and the left one was the moon. In this form, he was sometimes given the title <b>Kemwer</b>, meaning \'(the) great black (one)\'.\n</p><p>The Greek form of <i>Her-ur</i> (or <i>Har wer</i>) is <b>Haroeris</b>. Other variants include <i>Hor Merti</i> \'Horus of the two eyes\' and <i>Horkhenti Irti</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_Horus_Osiris">Golden Horus Osiris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Golden Horus Osiris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Horus gradually took on the nature as both the son of Osiris and Osiris himself. He was referred to as Golden Horus Osiris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In the temple of Denderah he is given the full royal titulary of both that of Horus and Osiris. Some accounts have Horus Osiris being brought back to life by Isis, but there is no proven connection with the story of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, as some have suggested, and many serious scholars reject such a connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebrations_of_Horus">Celebrations of Horus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Celebrations of Horus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Macrobius" title="Macrobius">Macrobius</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicon" title="Chronicon">Chronicon</a></i> noted the annual <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> celebration of Horus, specifying the time as the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a>. An analysis of the works of <a href="/wiki/Epiphanius_of_Salamis" title="Epiphanius of Salamis">Epiphanius of Salamis</a> noted the Egyptian winter solstice celebration of Horus in <a href="/wiki/Panarion" title="Panarion">Panarion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In
