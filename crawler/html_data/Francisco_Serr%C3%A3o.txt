Serr&#227;o</b>  (died 1521) was a <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">explorer</a> and a cousin of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan" title="Ferdinand Magellan">Ferdinand Magellan</a>. His 1512 voyage was the first known <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> sailing east past <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> through modern <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>. He became a confidante of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sultan_Bayan_Sirrullah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sultan Bayan Sirrullah (page does not exist)">Sultan Bayan Sirrullah</a>, the ruler of Ternate, becoming his personal advisor. He remained in Ternate where he died around the same time Magellan died.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="tocnumber">4.2</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Voyage_to_the_Indies">Voyage to the Indies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Serr%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Voyage to the Indies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Serr&#227;o served as captain of one of three vessels (and second in overall command under <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Abreu" title="Ant&#243;nio de Abreu">Ant&#243;nio de Abreu</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp7_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp7-1">[1]</a></sup>) sent from Malacca by <a href="/wiki/Afonso_de_Albuquerque" title="Afonso de Albuquerque">Afonso de Albuquerque</a> to find the Spice Islands of <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda</a> in Maluku in 1511.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp24_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp24-2">[2]</a></sup> Banda was the world\'s only source of <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mace_(spice)" title="Mace (spice)">mace</a>, spices used as flavourings, medicines, preserving agents, that were at the time highly valued in European markets. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a> sought to dominate the source, rather than relying on Arab traders who sold it to the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetians</a> for exorbitant prices.\n</p><p>Malay pilots guided the expedition east via <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and along the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Sunda_Islands" title="Lesser Sunda Islands">Lesser Sundas</a> before steering them north to Banda via <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">Ambon</a>. When Serr&#227;o\'s ship had berthed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gresik" title="Gresik">Gresik</a> on Java, he married a <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> woman as his wife, who then accompanied him on the expedition\'s further journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1512 his ship was shipwrecked but managed to reach Luco-Pino island (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitu" title="Hitu">Hitu</a>), north of Ambon. The expedition remained in Banda for about one month, purchasing and filling their ships with nutmeg and mace, as well as <a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">cloves</a> in which Banda had a thriving <a href="/wiki/Entrep%C3%B4t" title="Entrep&#244;t">entrep&#244;t</a> trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp7_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp7-1">[1]</a></sup> Serr&#227;o left Banda in a Chinese <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">junk</a> purchased from a regional trader to replace his lost ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp7_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp7-1">[1]</a></sup> D\'Abreu sailed through Ambon while Serr&#227;o went ahead towards Maluku islands.\n</p><p>With nine Portuguese crew and nine Indonesians, the ship foundered in a squall and broke up on a reef off a small island. When the island\'s inhabitants, notorious shipwreck scavengers, surveyed the wreck from a boat, Serr&#227;o\'s crew posed as unarmed and helpless but wealthy castaways. As the scavengers drew near, the Portuguese attacked and commandeered both their craft and crew. Their inadvertent rescuers were then forced to take them to Ambon, where they disembarked in Hitu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp7_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp7-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Serr&#227;o\'s armour, <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">muskets</a> and marksmanship impressed the powerful chiefs of Hitu who were warring against <a href="/wiki/Luhu,_Indonesia" title="Luhu, Indonesia">Luhu</a>, the principal settlement on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seram" title="Seram">Seram</a>\'s Hoamal Peninsula near Hitu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Portuguese were also welcomed in the area as buyers of food and spices during a lull in the spice trade due to a temporary disruption to Javanese and <a href="/wiki/Malays_(ethnic_group)" title="Malays (ethnic group)">Malay</a> sailings to the area following the 1511 conflicts in Malacca. The visitors were recruited as military allies and their subsequent exploits were heard in the rival neighbours of <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidore" title="Tidore">Tidore</a> who both rushed emissaries to induce the visitors to assist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFSp24_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFSp24-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Supporting the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Ternate" title="Sultanate of Ternate">Sultanate of Ternate</a>, the Portuguese strongest power, Serr&#227;o served as the head of a <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenary</a> band of Portuguese warriors under the service of the island\'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sultan_Bayan_Sirrullah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sultan Bayan Sirrullah (page does not exist)">Sultan Bayan Sirrullah</a>, one of two feuding powerful sultans who controlled the <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spice</a> trade. They became close friends and the Sultan appointed Serr&#227;o as his personal adviser for all matters, including military (Portuguese document purport) and family issues. Having been well received by the Sultan, Francisco Serr&#227;o decided to remain there, not making any efforts to return to Malacca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barbosa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-barbosa-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">Final years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Serr%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Final years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Francisco Serr&#227;o\'s letters to Ferdinand Magellan, carried to Portugal via <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Malacca" title="Portuguese Malacca">Portuguese Malacca</a> and describing the \'Spice Islands\', helped Magellan persuade the King of Spain to finance his circumnavigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp8_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp8-6">[6]</a></sup> Before they met each other, Serr&#227;o mysteriously died in Ternate at almost the same time Magellan was killed in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> (in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mactan_Island" title="Mactan Island">Mactan Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cebu" title="Cebu">Cebu</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barbosa_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-barbosa-5">[5]</a></sup> One theory suggests Serr&#227;o died of poison administered by the Sultan of Ternate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HANNARDp8_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HANNARDp8-6">[6]</a></sup> His family ties with <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Serr%C3%A3o" title="Jo&#227;o Serr&#227;o">Jo&#227;o Serr&#227;o</a> remain unclear in the <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a> of Portuguese expeditions to <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. The only written document is a list of captains\' names in the fleet of Magellan\'s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barbosa_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-barbosa-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
