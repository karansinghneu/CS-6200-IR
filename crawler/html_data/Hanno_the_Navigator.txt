Navigator</b> (<a href="/wiki/Punic_language" title="Punic language">Punic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="xpu">&#67847;&#8236;&#67853;&#8236;&#67840;&#8236;&#8236;</span>, <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">&#7716;N&#702;</span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHuss1985[httpsbooksgooglecokrbooksidNvEK7kc3qnQCpgPA565_565]_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuss1985[httpsbooksgooglecokrbooksidNvEK7kc3qnQCpgPA565_565]-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Carthage" title="Ancient Carthage">Carthaginian</a> explorer of the sixth or fifth century BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> best known for his naval exploration of the western coast of Africa.\nThe only source of his voyage is a Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">periplus</a></i>.  According to some modern analyses of his route, Hanno\'s expedition could have reached as far south as <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, however, others have taken him no further than class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Carthage dispatched Hanno at the head of a fleet of 60 ships to explore and <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonize</a> the northwestern coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarmington196474&#8211;76_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarmington196474&#8211;76-3">[3]</a></sup> He sailed through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Gibraltar" title="Straits of Gibraltar">straits of Gibraltar</a>, founded or repopulated seven colonies along the African coast of what is now Morocco, and explored significantly farther along the Atlantic coast of the continent.  Hanno encountered various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">indigenous peoples</a> on his journey and met with a variety of welcomes.\n</p><p><span id="Gorillas"></span>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gorillai">Gorillai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gorillai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the terminus of Hanno\'s voyage, the explorer found an island heavily populated with what were described as hirsute and savage people.  Attempts to capture the males failed, but three of the females were taken. These were so ferocious that they were killed, and their skins preserved for transport home to Carthage. The skins were kept in the Temple of Juno (<a href="/wiki/Tanit" title="Tanit">Tanit</a> or <a href="/wiki/Astarte" title="Astarte">Astarte</a>) on Hanno\'s return and, according to <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, survived until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destruction_of_Carthage" title="Destruction of Carthage">Roman destruction of Carthage</a> in 146 BC, some 350 years after Hanno\'s expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jona_Lendering_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Lendering-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The interpreters travelling with Hanno called the people <i>Gorillai</i> (in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> text <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#915;&#972;&#961;&#953;&#955;&#955;&#945;&#953;</span>). When the American physician and missionary <a href="/wiki/Thomas_S._Savage" title="Thomas S. Savage">Thomas Staughton Savage</a> and naturalist <a href="/wiki/Jeffries_Wyman" title="Jeffries Wyman">Jeffries Wyman</a> first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_description" title="Scientific description">described</a> the <a href="/wiki/Gorilla" title="Gorilla">gorillas</a> in the 19th century, the apes were named <i>Troglodytes gorilla</i> after the description in Hanno.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In its inmost recess was an island similar to that formerly described, which contained in like manner a lake with another island, inhabited by a rude description of people. The females were much more numerous than the males, and had rough skins: our interpreters called them Gorillae. We pursued but could take none of the males; they all escaped to the top of precipices, which they mounted with ease, and threw down stones; we took three of the females, but they made such violent struggles, biting and tearing their captors, that we killed them, and stripped off the skins, which we carried to Carthage: being out of provisions we could go no further.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>The periplus Hanno, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Periplus_account">Periplus account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Periplus account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HannoRouteMap.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1492" data-file-width="1396" decoding="async" height="235" href="/wiki/File:HannoRouteMap.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "Mount Cameroon" personnel interpretation of the route.</div></div></div>\n<p>The primary source for Hanno\'s expedition is a Greek <i><a href="/wiki/Periplus" title="Periplus">periplus</a></i>, supposedly a translation of a tablet Hanno is reported to have hung up on his return to Carthage in the temple of <a href="/wiki/Baal_Hammon" title="Baal Hammon">Ba\'al Hammon</a>, whom Greek writers identified with <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Kronos</a>. The full title translated from Greek is <i>The Voyage of Hanno, commander of the Carthaginians, round the parts of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">Libya</a> beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Heracles" title="Pillars of Heracles">Pillars of Heracles</a>, which he deposited in the Temple of Kronos</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In the fifth century, the text was translated into a rather mediocre Greek. It was not a complete rendering; several abridgments were made. The abridged translation was copied several times by Greek and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> clerks. Currently, there are only two copies, dating back to the ninth and the fourteenth centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jona_Lendering_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Lendering-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first of these manuscripts is known as the Palatinus Graecus 398 and can be studied in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">University Library of Heidelberg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The other text is in the Codex Vatopedinus 655, found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatopedi_monastery" title="Vatopedi monastery">Vatopedi monastery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Mount Athos</a>, Greece, and dated to the beginning of the 14th century; the codex is divided between the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bull_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bull-10">[10]</a></sup> and the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_Nationale" title="Biblioth&#232;que Nationale">Biblioth&#232;que Nationale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jona_Lendering_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Lendering-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Ancient_authors.27_accounts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_authors\'_accounts">Ancient authors\' accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient authors\' accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The text was known to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrian_of_Nicomedia" title="Arrian of Nicomedia">Arrian of Nicomedia</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Herodotus.27_account"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Herodotus\'_account">Herodotus\' account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Herodotus\' account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Greek historian Herodotus (ca.480&#8211;425 BC) gives a story based probably upon Hanno\'s original report.\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Carthaginians tell us that they trade with a race of men who live in a part of Libya beyond the Pillars of Herakles. On reaching this country, they unload their goods, arrange them tidily along the beach, and then, returning to their boats, raise a smoke. Seeing the smoke, the natives come down to the beach, place on the ground a certain quantity of gold in exchange for the goods, and go off again to a distance. The Carthaginians then come ashore and take a look at the gold; and if they think it presents a fair price for their wares, they collect it and go away; if, on the other hand, it seems too little, they go back aboard and wait, and the natives come and add to the gold until they are satisfied. There is perfect honesty on both sides; the Carthaginians never touch the gold until it equals in value what they have offered for sale, and the natives never touch the goods until the gold has been taken away.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Herodotus  of Halicarnassus, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span id="Pliny_the_Elder.27s_account"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pliny_the_Elder\'s_account">Pliny the Elder\'s account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pliny the Elder\'s account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, Hanno started his journey at the same time that <a href="/wiki/Himilco" title="Himilco">Himilco</a> started to explore the European Atlantic coast. Pliny reports that Hanno actually managed to circumnavigate the African continent, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">Gades</a> to <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Arrian.27s_account"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrian\'s_account">Arrian\'s account</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arrian\'s account">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Arrian mentions Hanno\'s voyage at the end of his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anabasis_of_Alexander" title="Anabasis of Alexander">Anabasis of Alexander</a></i> VIII (Indica):\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Moreover, Hanno the Libyan started out from <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> and passed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Heracles" title="Pillars of Heracles">Pillars of Heracles</a> and sailed into the outer Ocean, with Libya on his port side, and he sailed on towards the east, five-and-thirty days all told. But when at last he turned southward, he fell in with every sort of difficulty, want of water, blazing heat, and fiery streams running into the sea.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Arrian of Nicomedia, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArrianBrunt1978_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArrianBrunt1978-14">[14]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_analysis_of_the_route">Modern analysis of the route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanno_the_Navigator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern analysis of the route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A number of modern scholars have commented upon Hanno\'s voyage.  In many cases, the analysis has been to refine information and interpretation of the original account.  William Smith points out that the complement of personnel totalled 30,000, and that the core mission included the intent to found Carthaginian (or in the older parlance \'Libyophoenician\') towns.\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Some scholars have questioned whether this many people accompanied Hanno on his expedition, and suggest 5,000 is a more accurate number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jona_Lendering_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Lendering-4">[4]</a></sup> Robin Law notes that "It is a measure of the obscurity of the problem that while some commentators have argued that Hanno reached the Gabon area, others have taken him no further than southern Morocco."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Harden reports a general consensus that the expedition reached at least as far as <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarden1971168_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarden1971168-17">[17]</a></sup> Some agree he could have reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">Gambia</a>. However, Harden mentions disagreement as to the farthest limit of Hanno\'s explorations: <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>. He notes the description of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a>, a 4,040-metre (13,250&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>, more closely matches Hanno\'s description than Guinea\'s 890-metre (2,920&#160;ft) Mount Kakulima. Warmington prefers Mount Kakulima, considering Mount Cameroon too distant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarmington196479_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarmington196479-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Warmington suggests<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWarmington196476_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarmington196476-19">[19]</a></sup> that difficulties in reconciling the account\'s specific details with present geographical understanding are consistent with classical reports of Carthaginian determination to maintain sole control of trade into the Atlantic.\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This report was the object of criticism by some ancient writers, including the <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, and in modern times a whole literature of scholarship has grown up around it. The account is incoherent and at times certainly incorrect, and attempts to identify the various places mentioned on the basis of the sailing directions and distances almost all fail. Some scholars resort to textual emendations, justified in some cases; but it is probable that what we have before us is a report deliberately edited so that the places could not be identified by the competitors of Carthage. From everything we know about Carthaginian practice, the resolute determination to keep all knowledge of and access to the western markets from the Greeks, it is incredible that they would have allowed the publication of an accurate description of the voyage for all to read. What we have is an official version of the real report made by Hanno which conceals or falsifies vital information while at the same time gratifying the pride of the Carthaginians in their achievements. The very purpose of the voyage, the consolidation of the route to the gold market, is not even mentioned.\n</p></blockquote><p>The historian Raymond Mauny, in his 1955 article <i>La navigation sur les c&#244;tes du Sahara pendant l\'antiquit&#233;</i>, argued that the ancient navigators (Hannon, Euthym&#232;ne, Scylax, etc ..) could not have sailed south in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> farther than <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bojador" title="Cape Bojador">Cape Bojador</a>. He pointed out that antique geographers knew of the Canary Islands but nothing further south. Ships with square sails, without stern rudder, might navigate south, but the winds and currents throughout the year would prevent the return trip from Senegal to Morocco. Oared ships might be able to achieve the return northward, but only with very great difficulties. Mauny assumed that Hanno did not get farther than the <a href="/wiki/Draa_River" title="Draa River">Dr&#226;a</a>. He attributed artifacts found on <a href="/wiki/Mogador_Island" title="Mogador Island">Mogador Island</a> to the expedition described in the <a href="/wiki/Periplus_of_Pseudo-Scylax" title="Periplus of Pseudo-Scylax">Periplus of Pseudo-Scylax</a> and notes that no evidence of Mediterranean trade further south had yet been found.  The author ends by suggesting archaeological investigation of the islands along the coast, such as <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, or the &#238;le de Hern&#233; (Dragon Island near <a href="/wiki/Dakhla,_Western_Sahara" title="Dakhla, Western Sahara">Dakhla, Western Sahara</a>) where ancient adventurers may have been stranded and settled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
