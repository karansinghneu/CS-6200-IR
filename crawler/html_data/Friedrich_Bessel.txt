colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel</b> (<small>German: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[&#712;b&#603;s&#601;l]</a></span>; 22 July 1784 &#8211; 17 March 1846) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesist</a>. He was the first astronomer who determined reliable values for the distance from the sun to another star by the method of <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a>. A special type of mathematical functions were named <a href="/wiki/Bessel_function" title="Bessel function">Bessel functions</a> after Bessel\'s death, though they had originally been discovered by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli" title="Daniel Bernoulli">Daniel Bernoulli</a> and then generalised by Bessel.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Friedrich_Bessel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bessel was born in <a href="/wiki/Minden" title="Minden">Minden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a>, administrative center of <a href="/wiki/Minden-Ravensberg" title="Minden-Ravensberg">Minden-Ravensberg</a>, as second son of a civil servant. He was born into a large family in Germany. At the age of 14 Bessel was apprenticed to the import-export concern Kulenkamp at <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a>. The business\'s reliance on cargo ships led him to turn his <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> skills to problems in <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>. This in turn led to an interest in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> as a way of determining <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>.\n</p><p>Bessel came to the attention of a major figure of German astronomy at the time, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_Olbers" title="Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers">Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers</a>, by producing a refinement on the orbital calculations for <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">Halley\'s Comet</a> in 1804, using old observation data taken from <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harriot" title="Thomas Harriot">Thomas Harriot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Torporley" title="Nathaniel Torporley">Nathaniel Torporley</a> in 1607.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Two years later Bessel left Kulenkamp and became <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter" title="Johann Hieronymus Schr&#246;ter">Johann Hieronymus Schr&#246;ter</a>\'s assistant at <a href="/wiki/Lilienthal,_Lower_Saxony" title="Lilienthal, Lower Saxony">Lilienthal</a> Observatory near <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a>. There he worked on <a href="/wiki/James_Bradley" title="James Bradley">James Bradley</a>\'s stellar observations to produce precise positions for some 3,222 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 1810, at the age of 25, Bessel was appointed director of the newly founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Observatory" title="K&#246;nigsberg Observatory">K&#246;nigsberg Observatory</a> by King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William III of Prussia">Frederick William III of Prussia</a>. On the recommendation of fellow mathematician and physicist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">Carl Friedrich Gauss</a> (with whom he regularly corresponded)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> he was awarded an honorary doctor degree from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of G&#246;ttingen">University of G&#246;ttingen</a> in March 1811.\n</p><p>Around that time, the two men engaged in an epistolary correspondence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, when they met in person in 1825, they quarrelled; the details are not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1842 Bessel took part in the annual meeting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association for the Advancement of Science</a> in Manchester, accompanied by the geophysicist <a href="/wiki/Georg_Adolf_Erman" title="Georg Adolf Erman">Georg Adolf Erman</a> and the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi" title="Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi">Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi</a>.\n</p><p>Bessel married Johanna, the daughter of the chemist and pharmacist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Gottfried_Hagen" title="Karl Gottfried Hagen">Karl Gottfried Hagen</a> who was the uncle of the physician and biologist <a href="/wiki/Hermann_August_Hagen" title="Hermann August Hagen">Hermann August Hagen</a> and the hydraulic engineer <a href="/wiki/Gotthilf_Hagen" title="Gotthilf Hagen">Gotthilf Hagen</a>, the latter also Bessel\'s student and assistant from 1816 to 1818. The physicist <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ernst_Neumann" title="Franz Ernst Neumann">Franz Ernst Neumann</a>, Bessel\'s close companion and colleague, was married to Johanna Hagen\'s sister Florentine. Neumann introduced Bessel\'s exacting methods of measurement and data reduction into his mathematico-physical seminar, which he co-directed with <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi" title="Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi">Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi</a> at K&#246;nigsberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These exacting methods had a lasting impact upon the work of Neumann\'s students and upon the Prussian conception of precision in measurement.\n</p><p>Bessel had two sons and three daughters. His eldest daughter, Marie, married Georg Adolf Erman, member of the scholar family <a href="/wiki/Erman" title="Erman">Erman</a>. One of their sons was the renowned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptologist" title="Egyptologist">Egyptologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Erman" title="Adolf Erman">Adolf Erman</a>.\n</p><p>After several months of illness Bessel died in March 1846 at his observatory from <a href="/wiki/Retroperitoneal_fibrosis" title="Retroperitoneal fibrosis">retroperitoneal fibrosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Friedrich_Bessel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="445" data-file-width="785" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg/220px-Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg/330px-Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg/440px-Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Koenigsberg_observatory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Observatory" title="K&#246;nigsberg Observatory">K&#246;nigsberg Observatory</a> in  1830. It was destroyed by bombing in the Second World War.</div></div></div>\n<p>While the observatory was still in construction Bessel elaborated the <i>Fundamenta Astronomiae</i> based on Bradley\'s observations. As a preliminary result he produced tables of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a> that won him the <a href="/wiki/Lalande_Prize" title="Lalande Prize">Lalande Prize</a> from the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">French Academy of Sciences</a> in 1811. The K&#246;nigsberg Observatory began operation in 1813.\n</p><p>Starting in 1819, Bessel determined the position of over 50,000 stars using a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_circle" title="Meridian circle">meridian circle</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georg_von_Reichenbach" title="Georg von Reichenbach">Reichenbach</a>, assisted by some of his qualified students. The most prominent of them was <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Argelander" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander">Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander</a>.\n</p><p>With this work done, Bessel was able to achieve the feat for which he is best remembered today: he is credited with being the first to use <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">calculating the distance to a star</a>. Astronomers had believed for some time that parallax would provide the first accurate measurement of interstellar distances&#8212;in fact, in the 1830s there was a fierce competition between astronomers to be the first to measure a <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">stellar parallax</a> accurately. In 1838 Bessel won the race, announcing that <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">61 Cygni</a> had a parallax of 0.314 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a>; which, given the diameter of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s orbit, indicated that the star is 10.3 ly away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  Given the current measurement of 11.4 ly, Bessel\'s figure had an error of 9.6%. Nearly at the same time <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_Struve" title="Friedrich Georg Wilhelm Struve">Friedrich Georg Wilhelm Struve</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_James_Henderson" title="Thomas James Henderson">Thomas Henderson</a> measured the parallaxes of <a href="/wiki/Vega" title="Vega">Vega</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a>.\n</p><p>As well as helping determine the parallax of 61 Cygni, Bessel\'s precise measurements using a new meridian circle from <i>Adolf Repsold</i> allowed him to notice deviations in the motions of <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Procyon" title="Procyon">Procyon</a>, which he deduced must be caused by the gravitational attraction of unseen companions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\nHis announcement of Sirius\'s "dark companion" in 1844 was the first correct claim of a previously unobserved companion by positional measurement, and eventually led to the discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sirius_B" title="Sirius B">Sirius B</a>.\n</p><p>Bessel was the first scientist who realized the effect later called <i><a href="/wiki/Personal_equation" title="Personal equation">personal equation</a></i>, that several simultaneously observing persons determine slightly different values, especially recording the transition time of stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1824, Bessel developed a new method for calculation the circumstances of eclipses using the so-called <a href="/wiki/Besselian_elements" title="Besselian elements">Besselian elements</a>.  His method simplified the calculation to such an extent, without sacrificing accuracy, that it is still in use today.\n</p><p>Bessel\'s work in 1840 contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_of_Neptune" title="Discovery of Neptune">discovery of Neptune</a> in 1846 at <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Observatory" title="Berlin Observatory">Berlin Observatory</a>, several months after Bessel\'s death. On Bessel\'s proposal (1825) the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Prussian Academy of Sciences</a> started the edition of the <i>Berliner Akademische Sternkarten</i> (<i>Berlin Academic Star Charts</i>) as an international project. One unpublished new chart enabled <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Galle" title="Johann Gottfried Galle">Johann Gottfried Galle</a> to find Neptune near the position calculated by LeVerrier in 1846.\n</p><p>In the second decade of the 19th century while studying the dynamics of \'many-body\' gravitational systems, Bessel developed what are now known as <a href="/wiki/Bessel_function" title="Bessel function">Bessel functions</a>. Critical for the solution of certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">differential equations</a>, these functions are used throughout both classical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>.\n</p><p>Bessel is responsible for the correction to the formula for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_variance" title="Sample variance">sample variance</a> estimator named in his honour. This is the use of the factor <i>n</i>&#160;&#8722;&#160;1 in the denominator of the formula, rather than just <i>n</i>. This occurs when the <i>sample mean</i> rather than the <i>population mean</i> is used to centre the data and since the sample mean is a linear combination of the data the residual to the sample mean overcounts the number of degrees of freedom by the number of constraint equations &#8212; in this case one. <i>(Also see <a href="/wiki/Bessel%27s_correction" title="Bessel\'s correction">Bessel\'s correction</a>)</i>.\n</p><p>An additional field of work was <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\nBessel published a method for solving the main \n<a href="/wiki/Geodesics_on_an_ellipsoid" title="Geodesics on an ellipsoid">geodesic problem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\nHe was responsible for the survey of East Prussia which joined the Prussian and Russian triangulation networks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\nand he obtained an estimate of increased accuracy for the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">figure of the Earth</a>,\nnowadays referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Bessel_ellipsoid" title="Bessel ellipsoid">Bessel ellipsoid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite lacking a university education, Bessel was a major figure in astronomy during his lifetime. He was elected as member of the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Prussian Academy of Sciences</a> in 1812, the <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">French Academy of Sciences</a> in 1816,  foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a> in 1823, and fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> in 1825. In 1832, he was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAAS_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1827 Bessel became member of the Royal Institute of the Netherlands, predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bessel won the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Medal_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society">Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society</a> twice in 1829 and 1841.\n</p><p>The largest <a href="/wiki/Bessel_(crater)" title="Bessel (crater)">crater</a> in the Moon\'s <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">Mare Serenitatis</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-belt" title="Main-belt">main-belt</a> asteroid <a href="/wiki/1552_Bessel" title="1552 Bessel">1552&#160;Bessel</a> were named in his honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1552-Bessel_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-1552-Bessel-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
