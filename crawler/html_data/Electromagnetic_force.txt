<b>electromagnetic force</b>, a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_interaction" title="Physical interaction">physical interaction</a> that occurs between <a The other three fundamental interactions are the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At high energy the weak force and electromagnetic force are unified as a single <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak force</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lightning.0257.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Lightning.0257.jpg/220px-Lightning.0257.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Lightning.0257.jpg/330px-Lightning.0257.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Lightning.0257.jpg/440px-Lightning.0257.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lightning.0257.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_discharge" title="Electrostatic discharge">electrostatic discharge</a> that travels between two charged regions.</div></div></div>\n<p>Electromagnetic phenomena are defined in terms of the electromagnetic force, sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a>, which includes both <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a> as different manifestations of the same phenomenon. The electromagnetic force plays a major role in determining the internal properties of most objects encountered in daily life.  Ordinary matter takes its form as a result of <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> between individual atoms and <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in matter, and is a manifestation of the electromagnetic force. <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">Electrons</a> are bound by the electromagnetic force to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" title="Atomic nuclei">atomic nuclei</a>, and their orbital shapes and their influence on nearby atoms with their electrons is described by quantum mechanics. The electromagnetic force governs all chemical processes, which arise from interactions between the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_orbital" title="Molecular orbital">electrons</a> of neighboring atoms.\n</p><p>There are numerous <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_descriptions_of_the_electromagnetic_field" title="Mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field">mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field</a>.  In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_electrodynamics" title="Classical electrodynamics">classical electrodynamics</a>, electric fields are described as <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electric potential</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faraday_law_of_induction" title="Faraday law of induction">Faraday\'s law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">electromagnetic induction</a> and magnetism, and <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell\'s equations">Maxwell\'s equations</a> describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated and altered by each other and by charges and currents.\n</p><p>The theoretical implications of electromagnetism, particularly the establishment of the speed of light based on properties of the "medium" of propagation (<a href="/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism)" title="Permeability (electromagnetism)">permeability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">permittivity</a>), led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> in 1905.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_theory">History of the theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C3%98rsted.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2918" data-file-width="2309" decoding="async" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%C3%98rsted.jpg/140px-%C3%98rsted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%C3%98rsted.jpg/210px-%C3%98rsted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%C3%98rsted.jpg/280px-%C3%98rsted.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C3%98rsted.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_%C3%98rsted" title="Hans Christian &#216;rsted">Hans Christian &#216;rsted</a>.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_electromagnetic_theory" title="History of electromagnetic theory">History of electromagnetic theory</a></div>\n<p>Originally, electricity and magnetism were considered to be two separate forces. This view changed, however, with the publication of <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>\'s 1873 <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism" title="A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism">A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism</a></i> in which the interactions of positive and negative charges were shown to be mediated by one force. There are four main effects resulting from these interactions, all of which have been clearly demonstrated by experiments:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Electric charges <i><span id="attract">attract</span></i> or <i><span id="repel">repel</span></i> one another with a force <a href="/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)#Inverse_proportionality" title="Proportionality (mathematics)">inversely proportional</a> to the square of the distance between them: unlike charges attract, like ones repel.</li>\n<li>Magnetic poles (or states of polarization at individual points) attract or repel one another in a manner similar to positive and negative charges and always exist as pairs: every north pole is yoked to a south pole.</li>\n<li>An electric current inside a wire creates a corresponding circumferential magnetic field outside the wire.  Its direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise) depends on the direction of the current in the wire.</li>\n<li>A current is induced in a loop of wire when it is moved toward or away from a magnetic field, or a magnet is moved towards or away from it; the direction of current depends on that of the movement.</li></ol>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1140" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg/140px-Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg/210px-Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg/280px-Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andre-marie-ampere2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="Andr&#233;-Marie Amp&#232;re">Andr&#233;-Marie Amp&#232;re</a></div></div></div>\n<p>While preparing for an evening lecture on 21 April 1820, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_%C3%98rsted" title="Hans Christian &#216;rsted">Hans Christian &#216;rsted</a> made a surprising observation. As he was setting up his materials, he noticed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_needle" title="Compass needle">compass needle</a> deflected away from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_north" title="Magnetic north">magnetic north</a> when the electric current from the battery he was using was switched on and off. This deflection convinced him that magnetic fields radiate from all sides of a wire carrying an electric current, just as light and heat do, and that it confirmed a direct relationship between electricity and magnetism.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS-faraday.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1035" data-file-width="693" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS-faraday.jpg/140px-SS-faraday.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS-faraday.jpg/210px-SS-faraday.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/SS-faraday.jpg/280px-SS-faraday.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS-faraday.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a></div></div></div>\n<p>At the time of discovery, &#216;rsted did not suggest any satisfactory explanation of the phenomenon, nor did he try to represent the phenomenon in a mathematical framework. However, three months later he began more intensive investigations. Soon thereafter he published his findings, proving that an electric current produces a magnetic field as it flows through a wire. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGS" title="CGS">CGS</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">magnetic induction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oersted" title="Oersted">oersted</a>) is named in honor of his contributions to the field of electromagnetism.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1192" data-file-width="898" decoding="async" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg/140px-James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg/210px-James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg/280px-James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James-clerk-maxwell3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a></div></div></div>\n<p>His findings resulted in intensive research throughout the scientific community in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamics" title="Electrodynamics">electrodynamics</a>. They influenced French physicist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re" title="Andr&#233;-Marie Amp&#232;re">Andr&#233;-Marie Amp&#232;re</a>\'s developments of a single mathematical form to represent the magnetic forces between current-carrying conductors. &#216;rsted\'s discovery also represented a major step toward a unified concept of energy.\n</p><p>This unification, which was observed by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>, extended by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>, and partially reformulated by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">Oliver Heaviside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz" title="Heinrich Hertz">Heinrich Hertz</a>, is one of the key accomplishments of 19th century <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_physics" title="Mathematical physics">mathematical physics</a>. It has had far-reaching consequences, one of which was the understanding of the nature of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. Unlike what was proposed by the electromagnetic theory of that time, light and other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic waves</a> are at present seen as taking the form of <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantized</a>, self-propagating <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillatory</a> electromagnetic field disturbances called <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>. Different <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a> of oscillation give rise to the different forms of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a> at the lowest frequencies, to visible light at intermediate frequencies, to <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> at the highest frequencies.\n</p><p>&#216;rsted was not the only person to examine the relationship between electricity and magnetism. In 1802, <a href="/wiki/Gian_Domenico_Romagnosi" title="Gian Domenico Romagnosi">Gian Domenico Romagnosi</a>, an Italian legal scholar, deflected a magnetic needle using a Voltaic pile. The factual setup of the experiment is not completely clear, so if current flew across the needle or not. An account of the discovery was published in 1802 in an Italian newspaper, but it was largely overlooked by the contemporary scientific community, because Romagnosi seemingly did not belong to this community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<p>An earlier (1735), and often neglected, connection between electricity and magnetism was reported by a Dr. Cookson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The account stated, "A tradesman at Wakefield in Yorkshire, having put up a great number of knives and forks in a large box ...  and having placed the box in the corner of a large room, there happened a sudden storm of thunder, lightning, &amp;c.  ... The owner emptying the box on a counter where some nails lay, the persons who took up the knives, that lay on the nails, observed that the knives took up the nails. On this the whole number was tried, and found\nto do the same, and that, to such a degree as to take up large nails, packing\nneedles, and other iron things of considerable weight ..."   <a href="/wiki/E._T._Whittaker" title="E. T. Whittaker">E. T. Whittaker</a> suggested in 1910 that\nthis particular event was responsible for lightning to be "credited with the power of magnetizing steel; and it was doubtless this which led Franklin in 1751 to attempt to magnetize a sewing-needle by means of the discharge of Leyden jars." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_forces">Fundamental forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fundamental forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="305" decoding="async" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif/220px-Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Circular.Polarization.Circularly.Polarized.Light_Right.Handed.Animation.305x190.255Colors.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Representation of the electric field vector of a wave of circularly polarized electromagnetic radiation.</div></div></div>\n<p>The electromagnetic force is one of the four known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_force" title="Fundamental force">fundamental forces</a>. The other fundamental forces are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak nuclear force</a>, which binds to all known particles in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a>, and causes certain forms of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a>. (In <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a> though, the <a href="/wiki/Electroweak_interaction" title="Electroweak interaction">electroweak interaction</a> is the unified description of two of the four known fundamental interactions of nature: electromagnetism and the weak interaction);</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong nuclear force</a>, which binds <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Nucleon" title="Nucleon">nucleons</a>, and binds nucleons to form <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational force</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>All other forces (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, contact forces) are derived from these four fundamental forces (including momentum which is carried by the movement of particles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The electromagnetic force is responsible for practically all phenomena one encounters in daily life above the nuclear scale, with the exception of gravity. Roughly speaking, all the forces involved in interactions between <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> can be explained by the electromagnetic force acting between the electrically charged <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nuclei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> of the atoms. Electromagnetic forces also explain how these particles carry momentum by their movement. This includes the forces we experience in "pushing" or "pulling" ordinary material objects, which result from the <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> that act between the individual <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> in our bodies and those in the objects. The electromagnetic force is also involved in all forms of <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical phenomena</a>.\n</p><p>A necessary part of understanding the intra-atomic and intermolecular forces is the effective force generated by the momentum of the electrons\' movement, such that as electrons move between interacting atoms they carry momentum with them. As a collection of electrons becomes more confined, their minimum momentum necessarily increases due to the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>. The behaviour of matter at the molecular scale including its density is determined by the balance between the electromagnetic force and the force generated by the exchange of momentum carried by the electrons themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_electrodynamics">Classical electrodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classical electrodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_electrodynamics" title="Classical electrodynamics">Classical electrodynamics</a></div>\n<p>In 1600, <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">William Gilbert</a> proposed, in his <i><a href="/wiki/De_Magnete" title="De Magnete">De Magnete</a></i>, that electricity and magnetism, while both capable of causing attraction and repulsion of objects, were distinct effects. Mariners had noticed that lightning strikes had the ability to disturb a compass needle. The link between lightning and electricity was not confirmed until <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>\'s proposed experiments in 1752.  One of the first to discover and publish a link between man-made electric current and magnetism was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romagnosi" title="Romagnosi">Romagnosi</a>, who in 1802 noticed that connecting a wire across a <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a> deflected a nearby <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> needle. However, the effect did not become widely known until 1820, when &#216;rsted performed a similar experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oersted_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oersted-7">[7]</a></sup> &#216;rsted\'s work influenced Amp&#232;re to produce a theory of electromagnetism that set the subject on a mathematical foundation.\n</p><p>A theory of electromagnetism, known as <a href="/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism" title="Classical electromagnetism">classical electromagnetism</a>, was developed by various physicists during the period between 1820 and 1873 when it culminated in the publication of a <a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_on_Electricity_and_Magnetism" title="A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism">treatise</a> by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>, which unified the preceding developments into a single theory and discovered the electromagnetic nature of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In classical electromagnetism, the behavior of the electromagnetic field is described by a set of equations known as <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell\'s equations">Maxwell\'s equations</a>, and the electromagnetic force is given by the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the peculiarities of classical electromagnetism is that it is difficult to reconcile with <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, but it is compatible with special relativity. According to Maxwell\'s equations, the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in a vacuum is a universal constant that is dependent only on the <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">electrical permittivity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_permeability" title="Magnetic permeability">magnetic permeability</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">free space</a>. This violates <a href="/wiki/Galilean_invariance" title="Galilean invariance">Galilean invariance</a>, a long-standing cornerstone of classical mechanics. One way to reconcile the two theories (electromagnetism and classical mechanics) is to assume the existence of a <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a> through which the light propagates. However, subsequent experimental efforts failed to detect the presence of the aether. After important contributions of <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">Hendrik Lorentz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincar&#233;">Henri Poincar&#233;</a>, in 1905, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> solved the problem with the introduction of special relativity, which replaced classical kinematics with a new theory of kinematics compatible with classical electromagnetism. (For more information, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_special_relativity" title="History of special relativity">History of special relativity</a>.)\n</p><p>In addition, relativity theory implies that in moving frames of reference, a magnetic field transforms to a field with a nonzero electric component and conversely, a moving electric field transforms to a nonzero magnetic component, thus firmly showing that the phenomena are two sides of the same coin. Hence the term "electromagnetism". (For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism_and_special_relativity" title="Classical electromagnetism and special relativity">Classical electromagnetism and special relativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Covariant_formulation_of_classical_electromagnetism" title="Covariant formulation of classical electromagnetism">Covariant formulation of classical
