The smallest, reddest galaxies, are some of the most distant galaxies to have been imaged by an optical telescope.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Deep space exploration</b> (or <b>deep-space exploration</b>) is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_technology" title="Space technology">space technology</a> that is involved with exploring the distant regions of <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Physical <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">exploration of space</a> is conducted both by <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflights</a> (deep-space astronautics) and by <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a>.\n</p><p>At present the furthest space probe mankind has constructed and launched from Earth is <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a>, which was announced on December 5, 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to have reached the outer edge of the Solar system,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and entered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">interstellar space</a> on August 25, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Deep space exploration further than this vessel\'s capacity is not yet possible due to limitations in the space-engine technology currently available.\n</p><p>Some of the best candidates for future deep space engine technologies include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-matter" title="Anti-matter">anti-matter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beam-powered_propulsion" title="Beam-powered propulsion">beamed propulsion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The latter, beamed propulsion, appears to be the best candidate for deep space exploration presently available, since it uses known physics and known technology that is being developed for other purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forward1996_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forward1996-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="Current_research">Current research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_space_exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2012, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> announced the award of $500,000 to former astronaut <a href="/wiki/Mae_Jemison" title="Mae Jemison">Mae Jemison</a> to fund a project with the goal of sending future astronauts out of the Solar System. Jemison aims to increase public interest in future deep space exploration projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Upon awarding the money to Jemison, a "<a href="/wiki/100_Year_Starship" title="100 Year Starship">100 Year Starship</a>" symposium was held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a> to discuss interstellar travel. Topics discussed include "time-distance solutions; life sciences in space exploration; destinations and habitats; becoming an interstellar civilization; space technologies enhancing life on earth; and commercial opportunities from interstellar efforts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Research in deep space is ongoing and rapidly developing. In 2011, after the retirement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a>, NASA announced its intentions to invest money into developing three technologies vital to deep space exploration. The "must-have technologies" include a deep space atomic clock, a large solar sail and a more advanced laser communications system to improve communication, navigation, and propulsion in future missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In June 2013, NASA announced the selection of eight American astronauts that will begin to train for future deep space missions beyond low Earth orbit. NASA intends that these eight astronauts to train for future Mars or asteroid travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Single Aperture Far-Infrared Observatory (<a href="/wiki/SAFIR" title="SAFIR">SAFIR</a>), a proposed cryogenic space telescope, is tentatively set to launch in 2015 with the hopes of exploring "the formation of the first stars and galaxies" in deep space. The telescope will be more than 1000 times more sensitive than two current telescope spacecrafts, the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">Herschel Space Observatory</a>. NASA hopes to use SAFIR to learn about black holes, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution" title="Galaxy formation and evolution">galaxy formation and evolution</a> and the formation of <a href="/wiki/Star_system" title="Star system">star systems</a> in the far reaches of space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
