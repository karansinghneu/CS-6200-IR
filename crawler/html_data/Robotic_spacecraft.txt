Mercury</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>robotic spacecraft</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_spacecraft" title="Unmanned spacecraft">unmanned spacecraft</a>, usually under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telerobotic" title="Telerobotic">telerobotic</a> control. A robotic spacecraft designed to make scientific research measurements is often called a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a>. Many space missions are more suited to telerobotic rather than <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">manned</a> operation, due to lower cost and lower risk factors.  In addition, some planetary destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> or the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> are too hostile for human survival, given current technology. Outer planets such as <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> are too distant to reach with current manned spaceflight technology, so telerobotic probes are the only way to explore them.\n</p><p>Many artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> are robotic spacecraft, as are many <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">landers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">rovers</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs" title="Soviet space dogs">two dogs</a> Dezik and Tsygan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Four other such flights were made through the fall of 1951.\n</p><p>The first artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, was put into a 215-by-939-kilometer (116 by 507&#160;nmi) Earth orbit by the USSR) on 4 October 1957. On 3 November 1957, the USSR orbited <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_2" title="Sputnik 2">Sputnik 2</a>. Weighing 113 kilograms (249&#160;lb), Sputnik 2 carried the first living animal into orbit, the dog <a href="/wiki/Laika" title="Laika">Laika</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since the satellite was not designed to detach from its <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a>\'s upper stage, the total mass in orbit was 508.3 kilograms (1,121&#160;lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In a <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">close race with the Soviets</a>, the United States launched its first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Explorer_1" title="Explorer 1">Explorer 1</a>, into a 193-by-1,373-nautical-mile (357 by 2,543&#160;km) orbit on 31 January 1958. Explorer I was a 80.75-inch (205.1&#160;cm) long by 6.00-inch (15.2&#160;cm) diameter cylinder weighing 30.8 pounds (14.0&#160;kg), compared to  Sputnik 1, a 58-centimeter (23&#160;in) sphere which weighed 83.6 kilograms (184&#160;lb). Explorer 1 carried sensors which confirmed the existence of the Van Allen belts, a major scientific discovery at the time, while Sputnik 1 carried no scientific sensors. On 17 March 1958, the US orbited its second satellite, <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_1" title="Vanguard 1">Vanguard 1</a>, which was about the size of a grapefruit, and remains in a 360-by-2,080-nautical-mile (670 by 3,850&#160;km) orbit as of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.\n</p><p>Nine other countries have successfully launched satellites using their own launch vehicles: France (1965), Japan and China (1970), the United Kingdom (1971), India (1980), Israel (1988), <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> (2009), <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> (2012), and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> (2018).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span The main benefit for having this technology is because that these kinds of liquids have relatively high density, which allows the volume of the propellent tank to be small, therefore increasing space efficacy. The positively charged ions are guided   to pass through positively charged grids that contains thousands of precise aligned holes are running at high voltages. Then, the aligned positively charged ions accelerates through a negative charged accelerator grid that further increases the speed of the ions up to 90,000&#160;mph. The momentum of these positively charged ions provides the thrust to propel the spacecraft forward. The advantage of having this kind of propulsion is that it is incredibly efficient in maintaining constant velocity, which is needed for deep-space travel. However, the amount of thrust produced is extremely low and that it needs a lot of electrical power to operate.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_devices">Mechanical devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mechanical devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mechanical components often need to be moved for deployment after launch or prior to landing. In addition to the use of motors, many one-time movements are controlled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic" title="Pyrotechnic">pyrotechnic</a> devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robotic_vs._unmanned_spacecraft">Robotic vs. unmanned spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Robotic vs. unmanned spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Robotic spacecraft are specifically designed system for a specific hostile environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Due to their specification for a particular environment, it varies greatly in complexity and capabilities. While an <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_spacecraft" title="Unmanned spacecraft">unmanned spacecraft</a> is a spacecraft without personnel or crew and is operated by automatic (proceeds with an action without human intervention) or remote control (with human intervention). The term \'unmanned spacecraft\' does not imply that the spacecraft is robotic.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Robotic spacecraft use <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> to radio back to Earth acquired data and vehicle status information. Although generally referred to as "remotely controlled" or "telerobotic", the earliest orbital spacecraft &#8211; such as Sputnik 1 and Explorer 1 &#8211; did not receive control signals from Earth. Soon after these first spacecraft, command systems were developed to allow remote control from the ground. Increased <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_robot" title="Autonomous robot">autonomy</a> is important for distant probes where the light travel time prevents rapid decision and control from Earth. Newer probes such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini&#8211;Huygens">Cassini&#8211;Huygens</a></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rovers" title="Mars Exploration Rovers">Mars Exploration Rovers</a> are highly autonomous and use on-board computers to operate independently for extended periods of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_probes">Space probes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Space probes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">Space probe</a></div>\n<p>A space probe is a robotic spacecraft that does not orbit Earth, but instead, explores further into outer space.[1] A space probe may approach the Moon; travel through interplanetary space; flyby, orbit, or land on other planetary bodies; or enter interstellar space.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="SpaceX.E2.80.99s_Dragon"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="SpaceX&#8217;s_Dragon">SpaceX&#8217;s Dragon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robotic_spacecraft&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: SpaceX&#8217;s Dragon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_Dragon" title="SpaceX Dragon">SpaceX Dragon</a></div>\n<p>An example of a fully robotic spacecraft in the modern world would be SpaceX&#8217;s Dragon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The SpaceX Dragon is a robotic spacecraft designed to send not only cargo to Earth&#8217;s orbit, but also humans as well. The SpaceX Dragon&#8217;s total height is 7.2&#160;m (23.6&#160;ft) with a diameter of 3.7&#160;m (12&#160;ft). The total launch payload mass is 6,000&#160;kg (13,228&#160;lbs) and a total return mass of 3,000&#160;kg (6,614&#160;lbs), along with a total launch payload volume of 25m^3 (883&#160;ft^3) and a total return payload volume of 11m^3 (388&#160;ft^3). The total duration of the Dragon in Earth&#8217;s orbit is two years.\n</p><p>\nIn 2012 the SpaceX Dragon made history by becoming the first commercial robotic spacecraft to deliver cargo to the International Space Station and to safely return cargo to Earth in the same trip. This feat that the Dragon made was only achieved previously by governments.
