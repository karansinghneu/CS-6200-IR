href="#cite_note-IAUresB2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnras_style_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mnras_style-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAS_style_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAS_style-3">[3]</a></sup> ua,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or AU) is a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">unit of length</a>, roughly the distance from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. However, that distance varies as Earth orbits the Sun, from a maximum (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a>) to a minimum (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>) and back again once a year. Originally conceived as the average of Earth\'s aphelion and perihelion, since 2012 it has been defined as exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011149597870700000&#9824;</span>149<span style="margin-left:.25em;">597</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">870</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">700</span></span> metres or about 150&#160;million kilometres (93&#160;million miles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The astronomical unit is used primarily for measuring distances within the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> or around other stars. It is also a fundamental component in the definition of another unit of astronomical length, the <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of symbol usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A variety of unit symbols and abbreviations have been in use for the astronomical unit. In a 1976 resolution, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) used the symbol <i>A</i> for the astronomical unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU76_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU76-6">[6]</a></sup> In the astronomical literature, the symbol AU was (and remains) common. In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (BIPM) recommended ua as the symbol for the unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bureau_International_des_Poids_et_Mesures_2006_126_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bureau_International_des_Poids_et_Mesures_2006_126-7">[7]</a></sup> In the non-normative Annex C to <a href="/wiki/ISO_80000-3" title="ISO 80000-3">ISO 80000-3</a> (2006), the symbol of the astronomical unit is "ua". In 2012, the IAU, noting "that various symbols are presently in use for the astronomical unit", recommended the use of the symbol "au".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUresB2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAUresB2-1">[1]</a></sup> In the 2014 revision of the SI Brochure, the BIPM used the unit symbol "au".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAS_style_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAS_style-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_Brochure2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI_Brochure2012-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_unit_definition">Development of unit definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of unit definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth\'s orbit">Earth\'s orbit</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth\'s orbit">Earth\'s orbit</a> around the Sun is an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a> is defined to be half of the straight <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">line segment</a> that joins the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Perihelion and aphelion">perihelion and aphelion</a>. The centre of the Sun lies on this straight line segment, but not at its midpoint. Because ellipses are well-understood shapes, measuring the points of its extremes defined the exact shape mathematically, and made possible calculations for the entire orbit as well as predictions based on observation. In addition, it mapped out exactly the largest straight-line distance that Earth traverses over the course of a year, defining times and places for observing the largest <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> (apparent shifts of position) in nearby stars. Knowing Earth\'s shift and a star\'s shift enabled the star\'s distance to be calculated. But all measurements are subject to some degree of error or uncertainty, and the uncertainties in the length of the astronomical unit only increased uncertainties in the stellar distances. Improvements in precision have always been a key to improving astronomical understanding. Throughout the twentieth century, measurements became increasingly precise and sophisticated, and ever more dependent on accurate observation of the effects described by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a> and upon the mathematical tools it used.\n</p><p>Improving measurements were continually checked and cross-checked by means of improved understanding of the laws of <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a>, which govern the motions of objects in space. The expected positions and distances of objects at an established time are calculated (in AU) from these laws, and assembled into a collection of data called an <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">ephemeris</a>. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> HORIZONS System provides one of several ephemeris computation services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Horizons-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1976, in order to establish a yet more precise measure for the astronomical unit, the IAU formally <a href="/wiki/IAU_(1976)_System_of_Astronomical_Constants" title="IAU (1976) System of Astronomical Constants">adopted a new definition</a>. Although directly based on the then-best available observational measurements, the definition was recast in terms of the then-best mathematical derivations from celestial mechanics and planetary ephemerides. It stated that "the astronomical unit of length is that length (<i>A</i>) for which the <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_gravitational_constant" title="Gaussian gravitational constant">Gaussian gravitational constant</a> (<i>k</i>) takes the value <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998172020989500000&#9824;</span>0.017<span style="margin-left:.25em;">202</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">098</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">95</span></span> when the units of measurement are the astronomical units of length, mass and time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU76_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAU76-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tr&#252;mper_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tr&#252;mper-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairbridge_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairbridge-11">[11]</a></sup> Equivalently, by this definition, one AU is "the radius of an unperturbed circular Newtonian orbit about the sun of a particle having infinitesimal mass, moving with an <a href="/wiki/Angular_frequency" title="Angular frequency">angular frequency</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998172020989500000&#9824;</span>0.017<span style="margin-left:.25em;">202</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">098</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">95</span>&#160;radians per day</span>";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIbrochure_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIbrochure-12">[12]</a></sup> or alternatively that length for which the <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravitational_parameter" title="Standard gravitational parameter">heliocentric gravitational constant</a> (the product <i>G</i><var>M</var><sub>&#9737;</sub>) is equal to (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998172020989500000&#9824;</span>0.017<span style="margin-left:.25em;">202</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">098</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">95</span></span>)<sup>2</sup>&#160;AU<sup>3</sup>/d<sup>2</sup>, when the length is used to describe the positions of objects in the Solar System.\n</p><p>Subsequent explorations of the Solar System by <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probes</a> made it possible to obtain precise measurements of the relative positions of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System#Inner_planets" title="Solar System">inner planets</a> and other objects by means of <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a>. As with all radar measurements, these rely on measuring the time taken for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a> to be reflected from an object. Because all photons move at the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in vacuum, a fundamental constant of the universe, the distance of an object from the probe is calculated as the product of the speed of light and the measured time. However, for precision the calculations require adjustment for things such as the motions of the probe and object while the photons are transiting. In addition, the measurement of the time itself must be translated to a standard scale that accounts for relativistic time dilation. Comparison of the ephemeris positions with time measurements expressed in the <a href="/wiki/Barycentric_Dynamical_Time" title="Barycentric Dynamical Time">TDB</a> scale leads to a value for the speed of light in astronomical units per day (of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004864000000000000&#9824;</span>86<span style="margin-left:.25em;">400</span>&#160;s</span>). By 2009, the IAU had updated its standard measures to reflect improvements, and calculated the speed of light at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7008299792458018110&#9824;</span>173.144<span style="margin-left:.25em;">632</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">6847</span>(69)&#160;AU/d</span> (TDB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1983, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> (CIPM) modified the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI, or "modern" metric system) to make the metre defined as the distance travelled in a vacuum by light in 1/299792458 second. This replaced the previous definition, valid between 1960 and 1983, which was that the metre equalled a certain number of wavelengths of a certain emission line of krypton-86. (The reason for the change was an improved method of measuring the speed of light.) The speed of light could then be expressed exactly as <i>c</i><sub>0</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7008299792458000000&#9824;</span>299<span style="margin-left:.25em;">792</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">458</span>&#160;m/s</span>, a standard also adopted by the <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">IERS</a> numerical standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IERS_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-IERS-14">[14]</a></sup> From this definition and the 2009 IAU standard, the time for light to traverse an AU is found to be &#964;<sub>A</sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002499004783806100&#9824;</span>499.004<span style="margin-left:.25em;">783</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">8061</span><span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>0.000<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">01</span>&#160;s</span>, more than 8 minutes. <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">JPL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">IAA&#8211;RAS</a> did not yet standardize all relativistic effects, and thus were not constant for all observers. id="Usage_and_significance">Usage and significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage and significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the definitions used before 2012, the astronomical unit was dependent on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric_gravitational_constant" title="Heliocentric gravitational constant">heliocentric gravitational constant</a>, that is the product of the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a> <i>G</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass"><var>M</var><sub>&#9737;</sub></a>. Neither <i>G</i> nor <var>M</var><sub>&#9737;</sub> can be measured to high accuracy separately, but the value of their product is known very precisely from observing the relative positions of planets (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_Third_Law" title="Kepler\'s Third Law">Kepler\'s Third Law</a> expressed in terms of Newtonian gravitation). Only the product is required to calculate planetary positions for an <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">ephemeris</a>, so ephemerides are calculated in astronomical units and not in SI units.\n</p><p>The calculation of ephemerides also requires a consideration of the effects of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>. In particular, time intervals measured on Earth\'s surface (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_time" title="Terrestrial time">terrestrial time</a>, TT) are not constant when compared to the motions of the planets: the terrestrial second (TT) appears to be longer during the Northern Hemisphere winter and shorter during the Northern Hemisphere summer when compared to the "planetary second" (conventionally measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_dynamical_time" title="Barycentric dynamical time">barycentric dynamical time</a>, TDB). This is because the distance between Earth and the Sun is not fixed (it varies between <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999983289891200000&#9824;</span>0.983<span style="margin-left:.25em;">289</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">8912</span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011152097701010286&#9824;</span>1.016<span style="margin-left:.25em;">710</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">3335</span>&#160;AU</span>) and, when Earth is closer to the Sun (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>), the Sun\'s gravitational field is stronger and Earth is moving faster along <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth\'s orbit">its orbital path</a>. As the metre is defined in terms of the second and the speed of light is constant for all observers, the terrestrial metre appears to change in length compared to the "planetary metre" on a periodic basis.\n</p><p>The metre is defined to be a unit of <a href="/wiki/Proper_length" title="Proper length">proper length</a>, but the SI definition does not specify the <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity)" title="Metric tensor (general relativity)">metric tensor</a> to be used in determining it. Indeed, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> (CIPM) notes that "its definition applies only within a spatial extent sufficiently small that the effects of the non-uniformity of the gravitational field can be ignored".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As such, the metre is undefined for the purposes of measuring distances within the Solar System. The 1976 definition of the astronomical unit was incomplete because it did not specify the <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> in which time is to be measured, but proved practical for the calculation of ephemerides: a fuller definition that is consistent with general relativity was proposed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huang_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huang-21">[21]</a></sup> and "vigorous debate" ensued<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodd_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodd-22">[22]</a></sup> until August 2012 when the IAU adopted the current definition of 1 astronomical unit = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011149597870700000&#9824;</span>149<span style="margin-left:.25em;">597</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">870</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">700</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a>.\n</p><p>The astronomical unit is typically used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_system" title="Stellar system">stellar system</a> scale distances, such as the size of a protostellar disk or the heliocentric distance of an asteroid, whereas other units are used for <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">other distances in astronomy</a>. The astronomical unit is too small to be convenient for interstellar distances, where the <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-year</a> are widely used. The parsec (parallax arcsecond) is defined in terms of the astronomical unit, being the distance of an object with a <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcsecond</a>. The light-year is often used in popular works, but is not an approved non-SI unit and is rarely used by professional astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodd1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodd1-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When simulating a <a href="/wiki/Numerical_model_of_the_Solar_System" title="Numerical model of the Solar System">numerical model of the Solar System</a>, the astronomical unit provides an appropriate scale that minimizes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_overflow" title="Arithmetic overflow">overflow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_underflow" title="Arithmetic underflow">underflow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Truncation" title="Truncation">truncation</a>) errors in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a> calculations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On_the_Sizes_and_Distances_of_the_Sun_and_Moon" title="On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon">On the Sizes and Distances of the Sun and Moon</a></i>, which has long been ascribed to <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">Aristarchus</a>, says that he calculated the distance to the Sun to be between 18 and 20 times the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">distance to the Moon</a>, whereas the true ratio is about 389.174. He then derived a maximum lunar distance of <span class="sfrac nowrap">64<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><span style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">6</span></span></span> Earth radii. Because of cancelling errors in his parallax figure, his theory of the Moon\'s orbit, and other factors, this figure was approximately correct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vh1619_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-vh1619-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He then measured the apparent sizes of the Sun and the Moon and concluded that the apparent diameter of the Sun was equal to the apparent diameter of the Moon at the Moon\'s greatest distance, and from records of lunar eclipses, he estimated this apparent diameter, as well as the apparent diameter of the shadow cone of Earth traversed by the Moon during a lunar eclipse. Given these data, the distance of the Sun from Earth can be trigonometrically computed to be <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003121000000000000&#9824;</span>1210</span> Earth radii. This gives a ratio of solar to lunar distance of approximately 19, matching Aristarchus\'s figure. Although Ptolemy\'s procedure is theoretically workable, it is very sensitive to small changes in the data, so much so that changing a measurement by a few percent can make the solar distance infinite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vh1619_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-vh1619-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Greek astronomy was transmitted to the medieval Islamic world, astronomers made some changes to Ptolemy\'s cosmological model, but did not greatly change his estimate of the Earth&#8211;Sun distance. For example, in his introduction to Ptolemaic astronomy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Fargh%C4%81n%C4%AB" title="Al-Fargh&#257;n&#299;">al-Fargh&#257;n&#299;</a> gave a mean solar distance of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003117000000000000&#9824;</span>1170</span> Earth radii, whereas in his <i><a href="/wiki/Zij" title="Zij">zij</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Batt%C4%81n%C4%AB" title="Al-Batt&#257;n&#299;">al-Batt&#257;n&#299;</a> used a mean solar distance of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003110800000000000&#9824;</span>1108</span> Earth radii. Subsequent astronomers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-B%C4%ABr%C5%ABn%C4%AB" title="Al-B&#299;r&#363;n&#299;">al-B&#299;r&#363;n&#299;</a>, used similar values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Later in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Copernicus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> also used comparable figures (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003114200000000000&#9824;</span>1142</span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003115000000000000&#9824;</span>1150</span> Earth radii), and so Ptolemy\'s approximate Earth&#8211;Sun distance survived through the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> was the first to realize that Ptolemy\'s estimate must be significantly too low (according to Kepler, at least by a factor of three) in his <i><a href="/wiki/Rudolphine_Tables" title="Rudolphine Tables">Rudolphine Tables</a></i> (1627). <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion">Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion</a> allowed astronomers to calculate the relative distances of the planets from the Sun, and rekindled interest in measuring the absolute value for Earth (which could then be applied to the other planets). The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> allowed far more accurate measurements of angles than is possible with the naked eye. Flemish astronomer <a href="/wiki/Godefroy_Wendelin" title="Godefroy Wendelin">Godefroy Wendelin</a> repeated Aristarchus\' measurements in 1635, and found that Ptolemy\'s value was too low by a factor of at least eleven.\n</p><p>A somewhat more accurate estimate can be obtained by observing the <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">transit of Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bell-36">[36]</a></sup> By measuring the transit in two different locations, one can accurately calculate the <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of Venus and from the relative distance of Earth and Venus from the Sun, the <a href="/wiki/Parallax#Solar_parallax" title="Parallax">solar parallax</a> <i>&#945;</i> (which cannot be measured directly due to the brightness of the Sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver-37">[37]</a></sup>). <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Horrocks" title="Jeremiah Horrocks">Jeremiah Horrocks</a> had attempted to produce an estimate based on <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus,_1639" title="Transit of Venus, 1639">his observation of the 1639 transit</a> (published in 1662), giving a solar parallax of 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a>, similar to Wendelin\'s figure. They were also the first astronomers to have access to an accurate and reliable value for the radius of Earth, which had been measured by their colleague <a href="/wiki/Jean_Picard" title="Jean Picard">Jean Picard</a> in 1669 as <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003326900000000000&#9824;</span>3269</span> thousand <i><a href="/wiki/Toise" title="Toise">toises</a></i>. the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a>, <i>k</i> is the numerical value of <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_gravitational_constant" title="Gaussian gravitational constant">Gaussian gravitational constant</a> and <i>D</i> is the time period of one day.\nThe Sun is constantly losing mass by radiating away energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> so the orbits of the planets are steadily expanding outward from the Sun. This has led to calls to abandon the astronomical unit as a unit of measurement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> has an exact defined value in SI units and the Gaussian gravitational constant <i>k</i> is fixed in the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_system_of_units" title="Astronomical system of units">astronomical system of units</a>, measuring the light time per unit distance is exactly equivalent to measuring the product <i>G</i><var>M</var><sub>&#9737;</sub> in SI units. Hence, it is possible to construct ephemerides entirely in SI units, which is increasingly becoming the norm.\n</p><p>A 2004 analysis of radiometric measurements in the inner Solar System suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Secular_variation" title="Secular variation">secular increase</a> in the unit distance was much larger than can be accounted for by solar radiation, +<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001150000000000000&#9824;</span>15<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#177;</span>4</span> metres per century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The measurements of the secular variations of the astronomical unit are not confirmed by other authors and are quite controversial.\nFurthermore, since 2010, the astronomical unit has not been estimated by the planetary ephemerides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astronomical_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following table contains some distances given in astronomical units.
