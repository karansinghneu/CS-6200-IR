The feeling that one is about to vomit is called <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, which often precedes, but does not always lead to, vomiting. <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">Antiemetics</a> are sometimes necessary to suppress nausea and vomiting. In severe cases, where <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a> develops, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous" title="Intravenous">intravenous</a> fluid may be required. Self-induced vomiting can be a component of an eating disorder, such as <a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">bulimia nervosa</a>, and is itself now an eating disorder on its own, <a href="/wiki/Purging_disorder" title="Purging disorder">purging disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Vomiting is different from <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(digestion)" title="Regurgitation (digestion)">regurgitation</a>, although the two terms are often used interchangeably. Regurgitation is the return of undigested food back up the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a>, without the force and displeasure associated with vomiting. be dangerous if the gastric content enters the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory tract</a>. Under normal circumstances the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gag_reflex" title="Gag reflex">gag reflex</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coughing" title="Coughing">coughing</a> prevent this from occurring; however, these protective reflexes are compromised in persons under the influences of certain substances such as <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a>. The individual may choke and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiate</a> or suffer an <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_pneumonia" title="Aspiration pneumonia">aspiration pneumonia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dehydration_and_electrolyte_imbalance">Dehydration and electrolyte imbalance</span></h3>\n<p>Prolonged and excessive vomiting depletes the body of water (dehydration), and may alter the electrolyte status. Gastric vomiting leads to the loss of acid (protons) and chloride directly. Combined with the resulting <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_tide" title="Alkaline tide">alkaline tide</a>, this leads to <a href="/wiki/Hypochloremia" title="Hypochloremia">hypochloremic</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_alkalosis" title="Metabolic alkalosis">metabolic alkalosis</a> (low <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> levels together with high <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></a></span> and <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and increased blood <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>) and often <a href="/wiki/Hypokalemia" title="Hypokalemia">hypokalemia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> depletion). The hypokalemia is an indirect result of the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> compensating for the loss of acid. With the loss of intake of food the individual may eventually become <a href="/wiki/Cachexia" title="Cachexia">cachectic</a>. A less frequent occurrence results from a vomiting of intestinal contents, including bile acids and <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which can cause <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis" title="Metabolic acidosis">metabolic acidosis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. The area postrema is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumventricular_organ" title="Circumventricular organ">circumventricular organ</a> and as such lies outside the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood&#8211;brain barrier">blood&#8211;brain barrier</a>; it can therefore be stimulated by blood-borne drugs that can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emetic" title="Emetic">stimulate vomiting</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiemetics" title="Antiemetics">inhibit it</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are various sources of input to the vomiting center:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The chemoreceptor trigger zone at the base of the fourth ventricle has numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dopamine_receptors" title="Dopamine receptors">dopamine D<sub>2</sub> receptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/5-HT_receptor" title="5-HT receptor">serotonin 5-HT<sub>3</sub> receptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opioid_receptor" title="Opioid receptor">opioid receptors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine_receptor" title="Acetylcholine receptor">acetylcholine receptors</a>, and receptors for <a href="/wiki/Substance_P" title="Substance P">substance P</a>. Irritation of the GI mucosa by chemotherapy, radiation, distention, or acute infectious <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a> activates the 5-HT<sub>3</sub> receptors of these inputs.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">CNS</a> mediates vomiting that arises from psychiatric disorders and stress from higher brain centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The vomiting act encompasses three types of outputs initiated by the chemoreceptor trigger zone: Motor, <a href="/wiki/Parasympathetic_nervous_system" title="Parasympathetic nervous system">parasympathetic nervous system</a> (PNS), and <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">sympathetic nervous system</a> (SNS). They are as follows:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Increased <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">salivation</a> to protect <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">tooth enamel</a> from stomach acids. (Excessive vomiting leads to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erosion_(dental)" title="Erosion (dental)">dental erosion</a>). This is part of the PNS output.</li>\n<li>The body takes a deep breath to avoid <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration" title="Pulmonary aspiration">aspirating</a> vomit.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Retroperistalsis" title="Retroperistalsis">Retroperistalsis</a> starts from the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> and sweeps up digestive tract contents into the stomach, through the relaxed <a href="/wiki/Pylorus" title="Pylorus">pyloric sphincter</a>.</li>\n<li>Intrathoracic pressure lowers (by <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inspiration</a> against a closed <a href="/wiki/Glottis" title="Glottis">glottis</a>), coupled with an increase in abdominal pressure as the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdominal muscles</a> contract, propels stomach contents into the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardia" title="Cardia">lower esophageal sphincter</a> relaxes. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phases">Phases</span></h3>\n<p>The vomiting act has two phases.  In the retching phase, the abdominal muscles undergo a few rounds of coordinated contractions together with the diaphragm and the muscles used in respiratory inspiration. For this reason, an individual may confuse this phase with an episode of violent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiccups" title="Hiccups">hiccups</a>. In this retching phase, nothing has yet been expelled. In the next phase, also termed the expulsive phase, intense pressure is formed in the stomach brought about by enormous shifts in both the diaphragm and the abdomen.  These shifts are, in essence, vigorous contractions of these muscles that last for extended periods of time - much longer than a normal period of muscular contraction.  The pressure is then suddenly released when the upper esophageal sphincter relaxes resulting in the expulsion of gastric contents. Individuals who do not regularly exercise their abdominal muscles may experience pain in those muscles for a few days. The relief of pressure and the release of endorphins into the bloodstream after the expulsion causes the vomiter to feel better.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">Contents</span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">Gastric secretions</a> and likewise vomit are highly <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a>. ancient times as emetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Care must be taken with salt, as excessive intake can potentially be harmful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate" title="Copper(II) sulfate">Copper sulfate</a> was also used in the past as an emetic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid4385403_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid4385403-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It is now considered too toxic for this use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8385-8172-2_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8385-8172-2-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">Hydrogen peroxide</a> is used as an emetic in veterinary practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_cues">Social cues</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg/220px-Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg/330px-Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg/440px-Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nationalmuseet_-_Cophenaghen_-_brygos_vomiting1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A drunk man vomiting, while a young slave is holding his forehead. <a href="/wiki/Brygos_Painter" title="Brygos Painter">Brygos Painter</a>, 500&#8211;470 BC</div></div></div>\n<p>It is quite common that, when one person vomits, others nearby become nauseated, particularly when smelling the vomit of others, often to the point of vomiting themselves. It is believed that this is an <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> trait among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primates" title="Primates">primates</a>. Many primates in the wild tend to browse for food in small groups. Should one member of the party react adversely to some ingested food, it may be advantageous (in a survival sense) for other members of the party to also vomit. This tendency in human populations has been observed at drinking parties, where excessive consumption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> may cause a number of party members to vomit nearly simultaneously, this being triggered by the initial vomiting of a single member of the party. This phenomenon has been touched on in popular culture: notorious instances appear in the films <i><a href="/wiki/Mr_Creosote" title="Mr Creosote">Monty Python\'s The Meaning of Life</a></i> (1983) and <i><a href="/wiki/Stand_by_Me_(film)" title="Stand by Me (film)">Stand By Me</a></i> (1986).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Intense vomiting in <a href="/wiki/Ayahuasca" title="Ayahuasca">ayahuasca</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceremony" title="Ceremony">ceremonies</a> is a common phenomenon. However, people who experience "la purga" after drinking ayahuasca, in general, regard the practice as both a physical and spiritual cleanse and often come to welcome it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It has been suggested that the consistent emetic effects of ayahuasca&#8212;in addition to its many other therapeutic properties&#8212;was of medicinal benefit to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">indigenous peoples</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon</a>, in helping to clear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasites</a> from the gastrointestinal system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There have also been documented cases of a single ill and vomiting individual inadvertently causing others to vomit, when they are especially fearful of also becoming ill, through a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_hysteria" title="Mass hysteria">mass hysteria</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea-sickness_bag,_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png/220px-Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png/330px-Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png/440px-Sea-sickness_bag%2C_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea-sickness_bag,_Merseyside_Maritime_Museum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Special bags are often supplied on boats for sick passengers to vomit into.</div></div></div>\n<p>Most people try to contain their vomit by vomiting into a sink, toilet, or trash can, as vomit is difficult and unpleasant to clean. On airplanes and boats, <a href="/wiki/Sickness_bag" title="Sickness bag">special bags</a> are supplied for sick passengers to vomit into. A special disposable bag (leakproof, puncture-resistant, odorless) containing absorbent material that solidifies the vomit quickly is also available, making it convenient and safe to store until there is an opportunity to dispose of it conveniently.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>People who vomit chronically (e.g., as part of an <a href="/wiki/Eating_disorder" title="Eating disorder">eating disorder</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">bulimia nervosa</a>) may devise various ways to hide this disorder.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>An online study of people\'s responses to "horrible sounds" found vomiting "the most disgusting". Professor Trevor Cox of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salford" title="University of Salford">University of Salford</a>\'s Acoustic Research Centre said that "We are pre-programmed to be repulsed by horrible things such as vomiting, as it is fundamental to staying alive to avoid nasty stuff." It is thought that <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a> is triggered by the sound of vomiting to protect those nearby from possibly diseased food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellanea">Miscellanea</span></h3>\n<ul><li>Self-induced\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eating_disorders" title="Eating disorders">Eating disorders</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">bulimia nervosa</a>)</li>\n<li>To eliminate an ingested poison (some poisons should not be vomited as they may be more toxic when inhaled or aspirated; it is better to ask for help before inducing vomiting)</li>\n<li>Some people who engage in <a href="/wiki/Binge_drinking" title="Binge drinking">binge drinking</a> induce vomiting to make room in their stomachs for more alcohol consumption.</li>\n<li>Participants of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milk_challenge" title="Milk challenge">Milk challenge</a> typically end up vomiting most of the milk they consume back up, as proteins in the ingested milk (such as <a href="/wiki/Casein" title="Casein">casein</a> rapidly denature and unravel on contact with gastric acid and <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">protease</a> enzymes, rapidly filling the stomach. Once the stomach becomes full, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stretch_receptors" title="Stretch receptors">stretch receptors</a> in the stomach wall trigger signals to vomit to expel any further liquid the participant ingests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>\n<li>People suffering from nausea may induce vomiting in hopes of feeling better.</li></ul></li>\n<li>After <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a> (<a href="/wiki/Postoperative_nausea_and_vomiting" title="Postoperative nausea and vomiting">postoperative nausea and vomiting</a>)</li>\n<li>Disagreeable sights or <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a>, smells or thoughts (such as decayed matter, others\' vomit, thinking of vomiting), etc.</li>\n<li>Extreme pain, such as intense <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headache</a> or <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> (heart attack)</li>\n<li>Violent emotions</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cyclic_vomiting_syndrome" title="Cyclic vomiting syndrome">Cyclic vomiting syndrome</a> (a poorly understood condition with attacks of vomiting)</li>\n<li>High doses of <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> sometimes trigger a vomit reflex.</li>\n<li>Violent fits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coughing" title="Coughing">coughing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiccups" title="Hiccups">hiccups</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">Anxiety</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">Depression</a></li>\n<li>Overexertion (doing too much strenuous exercise can lead to vomiting shortly afterward).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rumination_syndrome" title="Rumination syndrome">Rumination syndrome</a>, an underdiagnosed and poorly understood disorder that causes sufferers to regurgitate food shortly after ingestion.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types">Other types</span></h3>\n<ul><li>Projectile vomiting refers to vomiting that ejects the gastric contents with great force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-med_dict_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-med_dict-24">[24]</a></sup> It is a classic symptom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infantile_hypertrophic_pyloric_stenosis" title="Infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis">infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis</a>, in which it typically follows feeding and can be so forceful that some material exits through the nose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sleisenger783_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sleisenger783-25">[25]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span></h2>\n<p>An <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetic</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">drug</a> that is effective against vomiting and <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>. Antiemetics are typically used to treat <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverse_effect_(medicine)" title="Adverse effect (medicine)">side effects</a> of medications such as <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Antiemetics act by inhibiting the receptor sites associated with emesis. Hence, anticholinergics, antihistamines, dopamine antagonists, serotonin antagonists, and cannabinoids are used as antiemetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Evidence to support the use of antiemetics for nausea and vomiting among adults in the emergency department is poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fur2015_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fur2015-27">[27]</a></sup> It is unclear if any medication is better than another or better than no active treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fur2015_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fur2015-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Nausea"
