href="/wiki/Toxicology" title="Toxicology">toxicology</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Hypoxia</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a condition in which the body or a region of the body is deprived of adequate <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> supply at the <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> level. Hypoxia may be classified as either <i>generalized</i>, affecting the whole body, or <i>local</i>, affecting a region of the body. Although hypoxia is often a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathological" title="Pathological">pathological</a> condition, variations in arterial oxygen concentrations can be part of the normal physiology, for example, during <a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation_training" title="Hypoventilation training">hypoventilation training</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or strenuous physical exercise.\n</p><p>Hypoxia differs from <a href="/wiki/Hypoxemia" title="Hypoxemia">hypoxemia</a> and anoxemia in that hypoxia refers to a state in which oxygen supply is insufficient, whereas hypoxemia and anoxemia refer specifically to states that have low or zero arterial oxygen supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Hypoxia in which there is complete deprivation of oxygen supply is referred to as <b>anoxia</b>.\n</p><p>Generalized hypoxia occurs in healthy people when they ascend to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude" title="High altitude">high altitude</a>, where it causes <a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">altitude sickness</a> leading to potentially fatal complications: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_pulmonary_edema" title="High altitude pulmonary edema">high altitude pulmonary edema</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HAPE" title="HAPE">HAPE</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_cerebral_edema" title="High altitude cerebral edema">high altitude cerebral edema</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HACE" title="HACE">HACE</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicalProblems_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MedicalProblems-4">[4]</a></sup>  Hypoxia also occurs in healthy individuals when breathing mixtures of gases with a low oxygen content, e.g. while diving underwater especially when using closed-circuit <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> systems that control the amount of oxygen in the supplied air. Mild, non-damaging intermittent hypoxia is used intentionally during <a href="/wiki/Altitude_training" title="Altitude training">altitude training</a> to develop an athletic performance adaptation at both the systemic and cellular level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hypoxia is a common complication of <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">preterm birth</a> in newborn infants. Because the lungs develop late in pregnancy, premature infants frequently possess underdeveloped lungs. To improve lung function, doctors frequently place infants at risk of hypoxia inside incubators (also known as humidicribs) that provide <a href="/wiki/Continuous_positive_airway_pressure" title="Continuous positive airway pressure">continuous positive airway pressure</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalized_hypoxia">Generalized hypoxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Generalized hypoxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The symptoms of generalized hypoxia depend on its severity and acceleration of onset.\n</p><p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">altitude sickness</a>, where hypoxia develops gradually, the symptoms include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" title="Fatigue (medical)">fatigue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypoesthesia" title="Hypoesthesia">numbness</a> / tingling of <a href="/wiki/Limb_(anatomy)" title="Limb (anatomy)">extremities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hypoxia" title="Cerebral hypoxia">cerebral anoxia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_University_Press_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_University_Press-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pia_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pia-7">[7]</a></sup> These symptoms are often difficult to identify, but early detection of symptoms can be critical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pia_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pia-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In severe hypoxia, or hypoxia of very rapid onset, <a href="/wiki/Ataxia" title="Ataxia">ataxia</a>, confusion / disorientation / <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">hallucinations</a> / behavioral change, severe <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a> / reduced level of consciousness, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papilloedema" title="Papilloedema">papilloedema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tachypnea" title="Tachypnea">breathlessness</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_University_Press_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_University_Press-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pallor" title="Pallor">pallor</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tachycardia" title="Tachycardia">tachycardia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_hypertension" title="Pulmonary hypertension">pulmonary hypertension</a> eventually leading to the late signs <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">cyanosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradycardia" title="Bradycardia">slow heart rate</a> / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cor_pulmonale" title="Cor pulmonale">cor pulmonale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">low blood pressure</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a> eventually leading to <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> is a darker red when it is not bound to oxygen (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deoxyhemoglobin" title="Deoxyhemoglobin">deoxyhemoglobin</a>), as opposed to the rich red color that it has when bound to oxygen (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhemoglobin" title="Oxyhemoglobin">oxyhemoglobin</a>), when seen through the skin it has an increased tendency to reflect blue light back to the eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In cases where the oxygen is displaced by another molecule, such as carbon monoxide, the skin may appear \'cherry red\' instead of cyanotic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Hypoxia can cause <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">premature birth</a>, and injure the liver, among other deleterious effects.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_hypoxia">Local hypoxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local hypoxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>If tissue is not being perfused properly, it may feel cold and appear pale; if severe, hypoxia can result in <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">cyanosis</a>, a blue discoloration of the skin. If hypoxia is very severe, a tissue may eventually become gangrenous.\nExtreme pain may also be felt at or around the site.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Tissue hypoxia from low oxygen delivery may be due to low haemoglobin concentration (anaemic hypoxia), low cardiac output (stagnant hypoxia) or low haemoglobin saturation (hypoxic hypoxia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The consequence of oxygen deprivation in tissues is a switch to anaerobic metabolism at the cellular level. As such, reduced systemic blood flow may result in increased serum lactate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup> Serum lactate levels have been correlated with illness severity and mortality in critically ill adults and in ventilated neonates with respiratory distress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Oxygen passively diffuses in the lung <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveoli" title="Alveoli">alveoli</a> according to a pressure gradient. Oxygen diffuses from the breathed air, mixed with water vapour, to <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arterial</a> blood, where its partial pressure is around 100&#160;mmHg (13.3&#160;kPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-02_calc_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-02_calc-15">[15]</a></sup> In the blood, oxygen is bound to hemoglobin, a protein in <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a>. The binding capacity of hemoglobin is influenced by the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/partial_pressure" title="wikt:partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of oxygen in the environment, as described in the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve" title="Oxygen&#8211;hemoglobin dissociation curve">oxygen&#8211;hemoglobin dissociation curve</a>. A smaller amount of oxygen is transported in solution in the blood.\n</p><p>In peripheral tissues, oxygen again diffuses down a pressure gradient into cells and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, where it is <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">used to produce energy</a> in conjunction with the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hypoxia can result from a failure at any stage in the delivery of oxygen to cells. This can include decreased partial pressures of oxygen, problems with diffusion of oxygen in the lungs, insufficient available hemoglobin, problems with blood flow to the end tissue, and problems with breathing rhythm.\n</p><p>Experimentally, oxygen diffusion becomes rate limiting (and lethal) when arterial oxygen partial pressure falls to 60&#160;mmHg (5.3&#160;kPa) or below.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Almost all the oxygen in the blood is bound to hemoglobin, so interfering with this carrier molecule limits oxygen delivery to the periphery. Hemoglobin increases the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood by about 40-fold,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MARTIN1999_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-MARTIN1999-17">[17]</a></sup> with the ability of hemoglobin to carry oxygen influenced by the partial pressure of oxygen in the environment, a relationship described in the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve" title="Oxygen&#8211;hemoglobin dissociation curve">oxygen&#8211;hemoglobin dissociation curve</a>. When the ability of hemoglobin to carry oxygen is interfered with, a hypoxic state can result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>997&#8211;999</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ischemia">Ischemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ischemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">Ischemia</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">Ischemia</a>, meaning insufficient blood flow to a tissue, can also result in hypoxia. This is called \'ischemic hypoxia\'. This can include an <a href="/wiki/Embolus" title="Embolus">embolic event</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> that decreases overall blood flow, or trauma to a tissue that results in damage. An example of insufficient blood flow causing local hypoxia is <a href="/wiki/Gangrene" title="Gangrene">gangrene</a> that occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Diseases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripheral_vascular_disease" title="Peripheral vascular disease">peripheral vascular disease</a> can also result in local hypoxia. For this reason, symptoms are worse when a limb is used. Pain may also be felt as a result of increased hydrogen ions leading to a decrease in blood pH (acidity) created as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_metabolism" title="Anaerobic metabolism">anaerobic metabolism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon monoxide poisoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">Carbon monoxide poisoning</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">Carbon monoxide</a> competes with oxygen for binding sites on hemoglobin molecules. As carbon monoxide binds with hemoglobin hundreds of times tighter than oxygen, it can prevent the carriage of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">Carbon monoxide</a> poisoning can occur acutely, as with smoke intoxication, or over a period of time, as with cigarette smoking. Due to physiological processes, carbon monoxide is maintained at a resting level of 4&#8211;6 ppm. This is increased in urban areas (7&#8211;13 ppm) and in smokers (20&#8211;40 ppm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_ppm_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-CO_ppm-21">[21]</a></sup>  A carbon monoxide level of 40 ppm is equivalent to a reduction in hemoglobin levels of 10 g/L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO_ppm_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-CO_ppm-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>CO has a second toxic effect, namely removing the allosteric shift of the oxygen dissociation curve and shifting the foot of the curve to the left.  In so doing, the hemoglobin is less likely to release its oxygens at the peripheral tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AllYouReally_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-AllYouReally-23">[23]</a></sup>  Certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemoglobinopathies" title="Hemoglobinopathies">abnormal hemoglobin variants</a> also have higher than normal affinity for oxygen, and so are also poor at delivering oxygen to the periphery.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Altitude">Altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a the amount of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The reduction in the href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>i<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypoxic_breathing_gases">Hypoxic breathing gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hypoxic href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a> may contain an insufficient partial pressure of oxygen, particularly in malfunction of rebreathers. Such situations may lead to unconsciousness without symptoms since carbon dioxide levels are normal and the human body senses pure hypoxia poorly.\n</p><p>A similar problem exists when inhaling certain odorless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gases" title="Asphyxiant gases">asphyxiant gases</a>. Asphyxiant gases reduce/displace the normal oxygen concentration in breathing air, where prolonged exposure to this hypoxic breathing gas leads to unconsciousness, followed by death by <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas_asphyxiation" title="Inert gas asphyxiation">inert gas asphyxiation</a> (suffocation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-29">[29]</a></sup> When oxygen level dips below 19.5% v/v, the air is considered oxygen-deficient, where oxygen concentrations below 16% volume are considered highly dangerous for humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-29">[29]</a></sup> As asphyxiant gases are relatively inert and odorless, their presence may not be noticed until the effects of elevated blood carbon dioxide (hypercapnia) are recognized by the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-29">[29]</a></sup> Inert gas asphyxiation may be deliberate with use of a <a href="/wiki/Suicide_bag" title="Suicide bag">suicide bag</a>. Accidental death has occurred in cases where concentrations of nitrogen in controlled atmospheres, or methane in mines, has not been detected or appreciated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Hemoglobin\'s function can also be lost by chemically oxidizing its iron atom to its ferric form.  This form of inactive hemoglobin is called <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobin" title="Methemoglobin">methemoglobin</a> and can be made by ingesting sodium nitrite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> as well as certain drugs and other chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anemia">Anemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">Anemia</a></div>\n<p>Hemoglobin plays a substantial role in carrying oxygen throughout the body,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MARTIN1999_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-MARTIN1999-17">[17]</a></sup> and when it is deficient, <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a> can result, causing \'anaemic hypoxia\' if tissue <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a> is decreased. <a href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency" title="Iron deficiency">Iron deficiency</a> is the most common cause of anemia. As iron is used in the synthesis of hemoglobin, less hemoglobin will be synthesised when there is less iron, due to insufficient intake, or poor absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>997&#8211;999</span></sup>\n</p><p>Anemia is typically a chronic process that is compensated over time by increased levels of <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red blood cells</a> via upregulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erythropoetin" title="Erythropoetin">erythropoetin</a>. A chronic hypoxic state can result from a poorly compensated anaemia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DAVIDSONS2010_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-DAVIDSONS2010-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>997&#8211;999</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Histotoxic_hypoxia">Histotoxic hypoxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Histotoxic hypoxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyanide_poisoning">Cyanide poisoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cyanide poisoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Histotoxic hypoxia results when the quantity of oxygen reaching the cells is normal, but the cells are unable to use the oxygen effectively as a result of disabled oxidative phosphorylation enzymes. This may occur in <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_poisoning" title="Cyanide poisoning">cyanide poisoning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_compensation">Physiological compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Physiological compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acute">Acute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Acute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>If oxygen delivery to cells is insufficient for the demand (hypoxia), electrons will be shifted to <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvic acid</a> in the process of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>.  This temporary measure (anaerobic metabolism) allows small amounts of energy to be released.  Lactic acid build up (in tissues and blood) is a sign of inadequate mitochondrial oxygenation, which may be due to hypoxemia, poor blood flow (e.g., shock) or a combination of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  If severe or prolonged it could lead to cell death.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In humans, hypoxia is detected by the peripheral chemoreceptors in the <a href="/wiki/Carotid_body" title="Carotid body">carotid body</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aortic_body" title="Aortic body">aortic body</a>, with the carotid body chemoreceptors being the major mediators of reflex responses to hypoxia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This response does not control ventilation rate at normal p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, but below normal the activity of neurons innervating these receptors increases dramatically, so much so to override the signals from central chemoreceptors in the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, increasing p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> despite a falling pCO<sub>2</sub>\n</p><p>It is seen in a few humans (encountered with hypoxia), there is word loss in their speech due to their state of confusion and cell damage in the brain.\n</p><p>In most tissues of the body, the response to hypoxia is <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a>. By widening the blood vessels, the tissue allows greater perfusion.\n</p><p>By contrast, in the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, the response to hypoxia is <a href="/wiki/Vasoconstriction" title="Vasoconstriction">vasoconstriction</a>. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Hypoxic_pulmonary_vasoconstriction" title="Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction">hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction</a>, or "HPV".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic">Chronic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Chronic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When the pulmonary capillary pressure remains elevated chronically (for at least 2 weeks), the lungs become even more resistant to pulmonary edema because the lymph vessels expand greatly, increasing their capability of carrying fluid away from the interstitial spaces perhaps as much as 10-fold. Therefore, in patients with chronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitral_stenosis" title="Mitral stenosis">mitral stenosis</a>, pulmonary capillary pressures of 40 to 45&#160;mm Hg have been measured without the development of lethal pulmonary edema.[Guytun and Hall physiology]\n</p><p>Hypoxia exists when there is a reduced amount of oxygen in the tissues of the body. Hypoxemia refers to a reduction in PO2 below the normal range, regardless of whether gas exchange is impaired in the lung, CaO2 is adequate, or tissue hypoxia exists. There are several potential physiologic mechanisms for hypoxemia, but in patients with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COPD" title="COPD">COPD</a> the predominant one is V/Q mismatching, with or without alveolar hypoventilation, as indicated by PaCO2. Hypoxemia caused by V/Q mismatching as seen in COPD is relatively easy to correct, so that only comparatively small amounts of supplemental oxygen (less than 3 L/min for the majority of patients) are required for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LTOT" title="LTOT">LTOT</a>. Although hypoxemia normally stimulates ventilation and produces dyspnea, these phenomena and the other symptoms and signs of hypoxia are sufficiently variable in patients with COPD as to be of limited value in patient assessment. Chronic alveolar hypoxia is the main factor leading to development of cor pulmonale&#8212;right ventricular hypertrophy with or without overt right ventricular failure&#8212;in patients with COPD. Pulmonary hypertension adversely affects survival in COPD, to an extent that parallels the degree to which resting mean pulmonary artery pressure is elevated. Although the severity of airflow obstruction as measured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FEV1" title="FEV1">FEV1</a> is the best correlate with overall prognosis in patients with COPD, chronic hypoxemia increases mortality and morbidity for any severity of disease. Large-scale studies of LTOT in patients with COPD have demonstrated a dose-response relationship between daily hours of oxygen use and survival. There is reason to believe that continuous, 24-hours-per-day oxygen use in appropriately selected patients would produce a survival benefit even greater than that shown in the NOTT and MRC studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypoxia_(medical)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>To counter the effects of high-altitude diseases, the body must return arterial p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> toward normal. <a href="/wiki/Acclimatization" title="Acclimatization">Acclimatization</a>, the means by which the body adapts to higher altitudes, only partially restores p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> to standard levels. <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">Hyperventilation</a>, the body&#8217;s most common response to high-altitude conditions, increases alveolar p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> by raising the depth and rate of breathing. However, while p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> does improve with hyperventilation, it does not return to normal. Studies of miners and astronomers working at 3000 meters and above show improved alveolar p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> with full acclimatization, yet the p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> level remains equal to or even below the threshold for continuous oxygen therapy for patients with <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a> (COPD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_2004_789&#8211;800_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-West_2004_789&#8211;800-38">[38]</a></sup> In addition, there are complications involved with acclimatization. <a href="/wiki/Polycythemia" title="Polycythemia">Polycythemia</a>, in which the body increases the number of red blood cells in circulation, thickens the blood, raising the danger that the heart can&#8217;t pump it.\n</p><p>In high-altitude conditions, only oxygen enrichment can counteract the effects of hypoxia. By increasing the concentration of oxygen in the air, the effects of lower barometric pressure are countered and the level of arterial p<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is restored toward normal capacity. A small amount of supplemental oxygen reduces the equivalent altitude in climate-controlled rooms. At 4000&#160;m, raising the oxygen concentration level by 5 percent via an oxygen concentrator and an existing ventilation system provides an altitude equivalent of 3000&#160;m, which is much more tolerable for the increasing number of low-landers who work in high altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In a study of astronomers working in Chile at 5050&#160;m, oxygen concentrators increased the level of oxygen concentration by almost 30 percent (that is, from 21 percent to 27 percent). This resulted in increased worker productivity, less fatigue, and improved sleep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_2004_789&#8211;800_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-West_2004_789&#8211;800-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oxygen_concentrator" title="Oxygen concentrator">Oxygen concentrators</a> are uniquely suited for this purpose. They require little maintenance and electricity, provide a constant source of oxygen, and eliminate the expensive, and often dangerous, task of transporting oxygen cylinders to remote areas.
