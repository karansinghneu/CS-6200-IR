title="Light-year">ly</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>parsec</b> (symbol: <b>pc</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">unit of length</a> used to measure large distances to <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a> outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. A parsec is defined as the distance at which one <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a> <a href="/wiki/Subtended_angle" title="Subtended angle">subtends</a> an angle of one <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc#Symbols_and_abbreviations" title="Minute and second of arc">arcsecond</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which corresponds to <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005648000000000000&#9824;</span>648<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="texhtml">&#960;</span></span></span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_units" title="Astronomical units">astronomical units</a>. One parsec is equal to about 3.26&#160;<a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> (30&#160;trillion&#160;km or 19&#160;trillion&#160;mi [n.b. a \'trillion\' here means a \'million million\' - using the current "two-legged tripod" number system]). The nearest star, <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>, is about 1.3 parsecs (4.2 light-years) from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most of the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> visible to the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">unaided eye</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a> are within 500 parsecs of the Sun.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The parsec unit was probably first suggested in 1913 by the British <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hall_Turner" title="Herbert Hall Turner">Herbert Hall Turner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dyson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-dyson-3">[3]</a></sup> Named as a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax"><i>par</i>allax</a> of one arc<i>sec</i>ond, it was defined to make calculations of astronomical distances from only their raw observational data quick and easy for astronomers. Partly for this reason, it is the unit preferred in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, though the light-year remains prominent in <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> texts and common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usage" title="Usage">usage</a>. Although parsecs are used for the shorter distances within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, multiples of parsecs are required for the larger scales in the universe, including <a href="/wiki/Kilo-" title="Kilo-">kilo</a>parsecs (kpc) for the more distant objects within and around the Milky Way, <a href="/wiki/Mega-" title="Mega-">mega</a>parsecs (Mpc) for mid-distance galaxies, and <a href="/wiki/Giga-" title="Giga-">giga</a>parsecs (Gpc) for many <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> and the most distant galaxies.\n</p><p>In August 2015, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAU" title="IAU">IAU</a> passed Resolution B2, which, as part of the definition of a standardized absolute and apparent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolometric_magnitude" title="Bolometric magnitude">bolometric magnitude</a> scale, mentioned an existing explicit definition of the parsec as exactly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005648000000000000&#9824;</span>648<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="texhtml">&#960;</span></span></span>&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_units" title="Astronomical units">astronomical units</a>, or approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016308567758149137&#9824;</span>3.085<span style="margin-left:.25em;">677</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">581</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">491</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">37</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>16</sup></span>&#160;metres (based on the IAU 2012 exact SI definition of the astronomical unit). This corresponds to the small-angle definition of the parsec found in many contemporary astronomical references.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="History_and_derivation">History and derivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parsec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and derivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">Stellar parallax</a></div>\n<p>The parsec is defined as being equal to the length of the longer leg of an extremely elongated imaginary <a href="/wiki/Right_triangle" title="Right triangle">right triangle</a> in space. The two dimensions on which this triangle is based are its shorter leg, of length one astronomical unit (the average Earth-Sun distance), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtended" title="Subtended">subtended angle</a> of the vertex opposite that leg, measuring one arc second. Applying the rules of trigonometry to these two values, the unit length of the other leg of the triangle (the parsec) can be derived.\n</p><p>One of the oldest methods used by astronomers to calculate the distance to a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a> is to record the difference in angle between two measurements of the position of the star in the sky. The first measurement is taken from the Earth on one side of the Sun, and the second is taken approximately half a year later, when the Earth is on the opposite side of the Sun. The distance between the two positions of the Earth when the two measurements were taken is twice the distance between the Earth and the Sun. The difference in angle between the two measurements is twice the parallax angle, which is formed by lines from the Sun and Earth to the star at the distant <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)#Of_an_angle" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertex</a>. Then the distance to the star could be calculated using trigonometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAparallax_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAparallax-5">[5]</a></sup> The first successful published direct measurements of an object at interstellar distances were undertaken by German astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Bessel" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel">Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel</a> in 1838, who used this approach to calculate the 3.5-parsec distance of <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">61 Cygni</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ParallaxeV2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="550" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ParallaxeV2.png/300px-ParallaxeV2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ParallaxeV2.png/450px-ParallaxeV2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/ParallaxeV2.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ParallaxeV2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>stellar parallax motion from annual parallax</div></div></div>\n<p>The parallax of a star is defined as half of the <a href="/wiki/Angular_distance" title="Angular distance">angular distance</a> that a star appears to move relative to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a> as Earth orbits the Sun. Equivalently, it is the subtended angle, from that star\'s perspective, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">semimajor axis</a> of the Earth\'s orbit. The star, the Sun and the Earth form the corners of an imaginary right triangle in space: the right angle is the corner at the Sun, and the corner at the star is the parallax angle. The length of the opposite side to the parallax angle is the distance from the Earth to the Sun (defined as one astronomical unit (au), and the length of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjacent_side_(right_triangle)#Trigonometric_ratios_in_right_triangles" title="Adjacent side (right triangle)">adjacent</a> side gives the distance from the sun to the star. Therefore, given a measurement of the parallax angle, along with the rules of <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometry</a>, the distance from the Sun to the star can be found. A parsec is defined as the length of the side adjacent to the vertex occupied by a star whose parallax angle is one arcsecond.\n</p><p>The use of the parsec as a unit of distance follows naturally from Bessel\'s method, because the distance in parsecs can be computed simply as the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">reciprocal</a> of the parallax angle in arcseconds (i.e. if the parallax angle is 1&#160;arcsecond, the object is 1&#160;pc from the Sun; if the parallax angle is 0.5&#160;arcseconds, the object is 2&#160;pc away; etc.). No <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" title="Trigonometric function">trigonometric functions</a> are required in this relationship because the very small angles involved mean that the approximate solution of the <a href="/wiki/Skinny_triangle" title="Skinny triangle">skinny triangle</a> can be applied.\n</p><p>Though it may have been used before, the term <i>parsec</i> was first mentioned in an astronomical publication in 1913. <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Watson_Dyson" title="Frank Watson Dyson">Frank Watson Dyson</a> expressed his concern for the need of a name for that unit of distance. He proposed the name <i>astron</i>, but mentioned that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Charlier" title="Carl Charlier">Carl Charlier</a> had suggested <i><a href="/wiki/Siriometer" title="Siriometer">siriometer</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hall_Turner" title="Herbert Hall Turner">Herbert Hall Turner</a> had proposed <i>parsec</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dyson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-dyson-3">[3]</a></sup> It was Turner\'s proposal that stuck.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_value_of_a_parsec">Calculating the value of a parsec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parsec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Calculating the value of a parsec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parsec_(1).svg" title="Diagram of parsec."><img alt="Diagram of parsec." data-file-height="94" data-file-width="375" decoding="async" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Parsec_%281%29.svg/400px-Parsec_%281%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Parsec_%281%29.svg/600px-Parsec_%281%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Parsec_%281%29.svg/800px-Parsec_%281%29.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a></dd></dl>\n<p>In the diagram above (not to scale), <b>S</b> represents the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, and <b>E</b> the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> at one point in its orbit. title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Messier_object" title="Messier object">M31</a>) is about 780&#160;kpc (2.5&#160;million light-years) away from the Earth.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs">Megaparsecs and gigaparsecs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parsec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Megaparsecs and gigaparsecs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A distance of one&#160;million parsecs is commonly denoted by the megaparsec (Mpc). Astronomers typically express the distances between neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a> in megaparsecs.\n</p><p>Galactic distances are sometimes given in units of Mpc/<i>h</i> (as in "50/<i>h</i>&#160;Mpc", also written "<span class="nowrap">50 Mpc <i>h</i><sup>&#8722;1</sup></span>"). <i>h</i> is a parameter in the range <span class="nowrap">0.5 &lt; <i>h</i> &lt; 0.75</span> reflecting the uncertainty in the value of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a> <i>H</i> for the rate of expansion of the universe: <span class="nowrap"><i>h</i> = <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>H</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">100&#160;km/s/Mpc</span></span></span>. The Hubble constant becomes relevant when converting an observed <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> <i>z</i> into a distance <i>d</i> using the formula <span class="nowrap"><i>d</i> &#8776; <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">c</a></i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>H</i></span></span> &#215; <i>z</i></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>One gigaparsec (Gpc) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">one billion</a> parsecs &#8212; one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(length)" title="Orders of magnitude (length)">units of length</a> commonly used. One gigaparsec is about 3.26&#160;billion light-years, or roughly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">14</span></span> of the distance to the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_horizon#Practical_horizons" title="Cosmological horizon">horizon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a> (dictated by the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation" title="Cosmic background radiation">cosmic background radiation</a>). Astronomers typically use gigaparsecs to express the sizes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos" title="Large-scale structure of the cosmos">large-scale structures</a> such as the size of, and distance to, the <a href="/wiki/CfA2_Great_Wall" title="CfA2 Great Wall">CfA2 Great Wall</a>; the distances between galaxy clusters; and the distance to <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>.\n</p><p>For example:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a> is about 0.78&#160;Mpc (2.5&#160;million light-years) from the Earth.</li>\n<li>The nearest large <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy cluster</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Cluster" title="Virgo Cluster">Virgo Cluster</a>, is about 16.5&#160;Mpc (54&#160;million light-years) from the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The galaxy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RXJ1242-11" title="RXJ1242-11">RXJ1242-11</a>, observed to have a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> core similar to the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>\'s, is about 200&#160;Mpc (650&#160;million light-years) from the Earth.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_filament" title="Galaxy filament">galaxy filament</a> <a href="/wiki/Hercules%E2%80%93Corona_Borealis_Great_Wall" title="Hercules&#8211;Corona Borealis Great Wall">Hercules&#8211;Corona Borealis Great Wall</a>, currently the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_largest_known_cosmic_structures" title="List of largest known cosmic structures">largest known structure</a> in the universe, is about 3&#160;Gpc (10&#160;billion light-years) across.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Particle_horizon" title="Particle horizon">particle horizon</a> (the boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>) has a radius of about 14.0&#160;Gpc (46&#160;billion light-years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_units">Volume units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parsec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Volume units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>To determine the number of stars in the Milky Way, volumes in cubic kiloparsecs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vol_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-vol-16">[a]</a></sup> (kpc<sup>3</sup>) are selected in various directions. All the stars in these volumes are counted and the total number of stars statistically determined. The number of globular clusters, dust clouds, and interstellar gas is determined in a similar fashion. To determine the number of galaxies in <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a>, volumes in cubic megaparsecs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vol_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-vol-16">[a]</a></sup> (Mpc<sup>3</sup>) are selected. All the galaxies in these volumes are classified and tallied. The total number of galaxies can then be determined statistically. The huge <a href="/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes_void" title="Bo&#246;tes void">Bo&#246;tes void</a> is measured in cubic megaparsecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KirshnerOemler1981_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-KirshnerOemler1981-17">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>, volumes of cubic gigaparsecs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vol_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-vol-16">[a]</a></sup> (Gpc<sup>3</sup>) are selected to determine the distribution of matter in the visible universe and to determine the number of galaxies and quasars. The Sun is the only star in its cubic parsec,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vol_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-vol-16">[a]</a></sup> (pc<sup>3</sup>) but in globular clusters the stellar density could be from 100 to 1000 per cubic parsec.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parsec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The parsec features in Han Solo\'s claim that he "made the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kessel_Run" title="Kessel Run">Kessel Run</a> in less than 12 parsecs," in both <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_New_Hope" title="A New Hope">A New Hope</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Solo:_A_Star_Wars_Story" title="Solo: A Star Wars Story">Solo: A Star Wars Story</a></i>. Many say that parsec was used incorrectly in <i>A New Hope</i>, as a measurement of time, not distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> The term is also occasionally used in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
