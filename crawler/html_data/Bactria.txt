More broadly Bactria was the area north of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a>, west of the Pamirs and south of the Tian Shan with the Amu Darya flowing west through the BC.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bactria%E2%80%93Margiana_Archaeological_Complex" title="Bactria&#8211;Margiana Archaeological Complex">Bactria&#8211;Margiana Archaeological Complex</a> (BMAC, also known as the "Oxus civilization") is the modern archaeological designation for a <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, dated to c. 2200&#8211;1700 BC, located in present-day eastern <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, northern Afghanistan, southern <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, centred on the upper <a href="/wiki/Amu_Darya" title="Amu Darya">Amu Darya</a> (Oxus River), an area covering ancient Bactria. Its sites were discovered and named by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Sarianidi" title="Viktor Sarianidi">Viktor Sarianidi</a> (1976). Bactria was the Greek name for Old Persian <i>B&#257;xtri&#353;</i> (from native *<i>B&#257;x&#231;i&#353;</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (named for its capital Bactra, modern <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>), in what is now northern Afghanistan, and <i>Margiana</i> was the Greek name for the Persian <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrapy</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margu" title="Margu">Margu</a>, the capital of which was <a href="/wiki/Merv" title="Merv">Merv</a>, in today\'s Turkmenistan.\n</p><p>The early Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Ctesias" title="Ctesias">Ctesias</a>, c. 400 BC (followed by <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>), alleged that the legendary Assyrian king <a href="/wiki/Ninus" title="Ninus">Ninus</a> had defeated a Bactrian king named <a href="/wiki/Oxyartes" title="Oxyartes">Oxyartes</a> in c. 2140 BC, or some 1000 years before the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>. Since the decipherment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform_script" title="Cuneiform script">cuneiform script</a> in the 19th century, however, which enabled actual Assyrian records to be read, historians have ascribed little value to the Greek account.\n</p><p>According to some writers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>, Bactria was the homeland of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranians" title="Indo-Iranians">Indo-Iranians</a> who moved southwest into Iran and the northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> around 2500&#8211;2000 BC. Later, it became the northern province of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It was in these regions, where the fertile soil of the mountainous country is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Turan_Depression" title="Turan Depression">Turan Depression</a>, that the prophet <a href="/wiki/Zoroaster" title="Zoroaster">Zoroaster</a> was said to have been born and gained his first adherents. <a href="/wiki/Avestan" title="Avestan">Avestan</a>, the language of the oldest portions of the <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrian</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Avesta" title="Avesta">Avesta</a></i>, was one of the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_languages#Proto-Iranian_and_Old_Iranian_languages" title="Iranian languages">old Iranian languages</a>, and is the oldest attested member of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">Eastern Iranian languages</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" national resistance but was captured by other warlords and delivered to Alexander. He was then tortured and killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alexander">Alexander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bactria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alexander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdiana</a>. In the south, beyond the Oxus, he met strong resistance. After two years of war and a strong insurgency campaign, Alexander managed to establish little control over Bactria. After Alexander\'s death, <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> tells us that Philip received dominion over Bactria, but <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a> names <a href="/wiki/Amyntas_(son_of_Nicolaus)" title="Amyntas (son of Nicolaus)">Amyntas</a> to that role. At the Treaty of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triparadisus" title="Triparadisus">Triparadisus</a>, both Diodorus Siculus and <a href="/wiki/Arrian" title="Arrian">Arrian</a> agree that the satrap <a href="/wiki/Stasanor" title="Stasanor">Stasanor</a> gained control over Bactria. Eventually, Alexander\'s empire was divided up among the generals in Alexander\'s army. Bactria became a part of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>, named after its founder, <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seleucid_Empire">Seleucid Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bactria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Seleucid Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(ancient_kingdom)" title="Macedonia (ancient kingdom)">Macedonians</a>, especially Seleucus I and his son <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_I_Soter" title="Antiochus I Soter">Antiochus I</a>, established the Seleucid Empire and founded a great many Greek <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">towns</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek language</a> became dominant for some time there.\n</p><p>The paradox that Greek presence was more prominent in Bactria than in areas far closer to Greece can possibly be explained<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (June 2013)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> by past deportations of Greeks to Bactria. For instance, during the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_I" title="Darius I">Darius I</a>, the inhabitants of the Greek city of <a href="/wiki/Barca_(ancient_city)" title="Barca (ancient city)">Barca</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a>, were deported to Bactria for refusing to surrender assassins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In addition, Xerxes also settled the "Branchidae" in Bactria; they were the descendants of Greek priests who had once lived near <a href="/wiki/Didyma" title="Didyma">Didyma</a> (western Asia Minor) and betrayed the temple to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Herodotus also records a Persian commander threatening to enslave daughters of the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Revolt" title="Ionian Revolt">revolting Ionians</a> and send them to Bactria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, these few examples are not indicative of massive deportations of Greeks to central Asia.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" difficulties faced by the Seleucid kings and the attacks of Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_II_Philadelphus" title="Ptolemy II Philadelphus">Ptolemy II Philadelphus</a> gave the satrap of Bactria, <a He was the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>. Diodotus and his successors were able to maintain themselves against the attacks of the Seleucids&#8212;particularly from <a href="/wiki/Antiochus_III_the_Great" title="Antiochus III the Great">Antiochus III the Great</a>, who was ultimately defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Romans</a> (190 BC).\n</p><p>The Greco-Bactrians were so powerful that they were able to expand their territory as far as India:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>As for Bactria, a part of it lies alongside Aria towards the north, though most of it lies above Aria and to the east of it. And much of it produces everything except oil. For a short time, they wielded great power: a great Greek empire seemed to have arisen far in the East. But this empire was torn by internal dissension and continual usurpations. When Demetrius advanced far east of the Indus River, one of his generals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucratides" title="Eucratides">Eucratides</a>, made himself king of Bactria, and soon in every province there arose new usurpers, who proclaimed themselves kings and fought against each other.\n</p><p>Most of them we know only by their coins, a great many of which are found in Afghanistan. By these wars, the dominant position of the Greeks was undermined even more quickly than would otherwise have been the case. After Demetrius and Eucratides, the kings abandoned the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attic_standard" title="Attic standard">Attic standard</a> of coinage and introduced a native standard, no doubt to gain support from outside the Greek minority.\n</p><p>In the Indus valley, this went even further. The Indo-Greek king <a href="/wiki/Menander_I" title="Menander I">Menander I</a> (known as Milinda in India), recognized as a great conqueror, <a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhism" title="Greco-Buddhism">converted to Buddhism</a>. His successors managed to cling to power until the last known Indo-Greek ruler, a king named <a href="/wiki/Strato_II" title="Strato II">Strato II</a>, who ruled in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punjab_region" title="Punjab region">Punjab region</a> until around 55 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Other sources, however, place the end of Strato II\'s reign as late as 10 AD.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Daxia.2C_Tukhara_and_Tokharistan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Daxia,_Tukhara_and_Tokharistan">Daxia, Tukhara and Tokharistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bactria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Daxia, Tukhara and Tokharistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a></i>, <i>Ta-Hsia</i>, or <i>Ta-Hia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#22823;&#22799;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">D&#224;xi&#224;</span></i>) was the name given in antiquity by the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han Chinese</a> to <b>Tukhara</b> or <b>Tokhara</b>: the central part of Bactria. The name "Daxia" appears in Chinese from the 3rd century BC to designate a little-known kingdom located somewhere west of China. This was possibly a consequence of the first contacts between China and the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>.\n</p><p>During the 2nd century BC, the Greco-Bactrians were conquered by nomadic <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European</a> tribes from the north, beginning with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakas" title="Sakas">Sakas</a> (160 BC). The Sakas were overthrown in turn by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Da_Yuezhi" title="Da Yuezhi">Da Yuezhi</a> ("Greater Yuezhi") during subsequent decades. The Yuezhi had conquered Bactria by the time of the visit of the Chinese envoy <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> (circa 127 BC), who had been sent by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han</a> emperor to investigate lands to the west of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rene_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rene-15">[15]</a></sup>\nThe first mention of these events in European literature appeared in the 1st century BC, when <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> described how "the Asii, Pasiani, Tokhari, and Sakarauli" had taken part in the "destruction of the Greco-Bactrian kingdom". <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> subsequently mentioned the central role of the Tokhari among other tribes in Bactria. As <i>Tukhara</i> or <i>Tokhara</i> it included areas that were later part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surxondaryo_Province" title="Surxondaryo Province">Surxondaryo Province</a> in Uzbekistan, southern <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> and northern Afghanistan. (The Tokhari and their language should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Tocharians" title="Tocharians">Tocharian people</a> who lived in the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a> between the 3rd and 9th centuries AD, or the <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian languages</a> that form another branch of <a <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>. Based on the reports of Zhang Qian, the <i>Shiji</i> describe Daxia as an important urban civilization of about one million people, living in walled cities under small city kings or magistrates. Daxia was an affluent country with rich markets, trading in an incredible variety of objects, coming from as far as Southern China. By the time Zhang Qian visited, there was no longer a major king, and the Bactrians were under the suzerainty of the Yuezhi. Zhang Qian depicted a rather sophisticated but demoralised people who were afraid of war. Following these reports, the Chinese emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han_China" title="Emperor Wu of Han China">Wu Di</a> was informed of the level of sophistication of the urban civilizations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>, Bactria and <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>, and became interested in developing commercial relationship with them: <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Son of Heaven on hearing all this reasoned thus: <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> and the possessions of Daxia and Anxi <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>  are large countries, full of rare things, with a population living in fixed abodes and given to occupations somewhat identical with those of the people of <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a>, but with weak armies, and placing great value on the rich produce of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote><p> These contacts immediately led to the dispatch of multiple embassies from the Chinese, which helped to develop trade along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silk_Roads" title="Silk Roads">Silk Roads</a>.\n</p><div class="thumb tright"><div href="/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises" title="Kujula Kadphises">Kujula Kadphises</a>, the <i>xihou</i> (prince) of the Yuezhi, united the region in the early 1st century and laid the foundations for the powerful, but short-lived, <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a>.\n</p><p>In the 3rd century AD, Tukhara was under the rule of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Sasanians" title="Indo-Sasanians">Kushanshas</a></i> (Indo-Sasanians).\n</p><p>The form <b>Tokharistan</b> &#8211; the suffix <i>-stan</i> means "place of" in Sanskrit &#8211; appeared for the first time in the 4th century, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> texts, such as the <i>Vibhasa-sastra</i>. <b>Tokhara</b> was known in Chinese sources as <i>Tuhuluo</i> (&#21520;&#21628;&#32645;)  which is first mentioned during the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> era. In the Tang dynasty, the name is transcribed as Tuhuoluo (&#22303;&#35905;&#32645;). Other Chinese names are Doushaluo &#20828;&#27801;&#32645;, Douquluo &#20828;&#20297;&#32645; or Duhuoluo &#35241;&#36008;&#32645;.\n</p><p>During the 5th century, Bactria was controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Xionites" title="Xionites">Xionites</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hephthalite" title="Hephthalite">Hephthalites</a>, but was subsequently reconquered by the Sassanid Empire.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_of_Islam">Introduction of Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bactria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Introduction of Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_conquest_of_Afghanistan" title="Islamic conquest of Afghanistan">Islamic conquest of Afghanistan</a></div>\n<p>By the mid-7th century, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Rashidun_Caliphate" title="Rashidun Caliphate">Rashidun Caliphate</a>, had come to rule much of the Middle East and western areas of Central Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 663, the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a> attacked the Buddhist <a href="/wiki/Kabul_Shahi" title="Kabul Shahi">Shahi</a> dynasty ruling in Tokharistan. The Umayyad forces captured the area around <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>, including the Buddhist monastery at <a href="/wiki/Nava_Vihara" title="Nava Vihara">Nava Vihara</a>, causing the Shahis to retreat to the Kabul Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 8th century, a Persian from Balkh known as Saman Khuda left Zoroastrianism for Islam while living under the Umayyads. His children founded the Samanid Empire (875&#8211;999). Farsi became the official language and had a higher status than Bactrian, because it was the language of Muslim rulers. It eventually replaced the latter as the common language due to the preferential treatment as well as colonization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bactrian_people">Bactrian people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bactria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bactrian people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BactrianZoroastrian.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="643" data-file-width="475" decoding="async" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/BactrianZoroastrian.jpg/220px-BactrianZoroastrian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/BactrianZoroastrian.jpg/330px-BactrianZoroastrian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/BactrianZoroastrian.jpg/440px-BactrianZoroastrian.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BactrianZoroastrian.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painted clay and <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> head of a <a href="/wiki/Mobad" title="Mobad">mobad</a> wearing a distinctive Bactrian-style headdress, <a href="/wiki/Takhti-Sangin" title="Takhti-Sangin">Takhti-Sangin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>, third&#8211;second century BC</div></div></div>\n<p>Bactrians were the inhabitants of Bactria. Several important trade routes from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>) passed through Bactria and, as early as the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, this had allowed the accumulation of vast amounts of wealth by the mostly nomadic population. The first proto-urban civilization in the area arose during the <a href="/wiki/2nd_millennium_BC" title="2nd millennium BC">2nd millennium BC</a>.\n</p><p>Control of these lucrative trade routes, however, attracted foreign interest, and in the 6th century BC the Bactrians were conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persians</a>, and in the 4th century BC by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>. These conquests marked the end of Bactrian independence. From around 304 BC the area formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>, and from around 250 BC it was the centre of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian kingdom">Greco-Bactrian kingdom</a>, ruled by the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> who had settled there following the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>.\n</p><p>The Greco-Bactrians, also known in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yavanas" title="Yavanas">Yavanas</a>, worked in cooperation with the native Bactrian aristocracy. By the early 2nd century BC the Greco-Bactrians had created an impressive empire that stretched southwards to include northwest India. By about 135 BC, however, this kingdom had been overrun by invading <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> tribes, an invasion that later brought about the rise of the powerful <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a>.\n</p><p>Bactrians were recorded in Strabo\'s Geography\'\n</p><p>"Now in early times the Sogdians and Bactrians did not differ much from the nomads in their modes of life and customs, although the Bactrians were a little more civilised; however, of these, as of the others, Onesicritus does not report their best traits, saying, for instance, that those who have become helpless because of old age or sickness are thrown out alive as prey to dogs kept expressly for this purpose, which in their native tongue are called "undertakers," and that while the land outside the walls of the metropolis of the Bactrians looks clean, yet most of the land inside the walls is full of human bones; but that Alexander broke up the custom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nThe Bactrians spoke <a href="/wiki/Bactrian_language" title="Bactrian language">Bactrian</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Iranian" title="East Iranian">north-eastern Iranian</a> language. Bactrian became extinct, replaced by north-eastern<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Iranian languages such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yidgha_language" title="Yidgha language">Yidgha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munji_language" title="Munji language">Munji</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ishkashimi_language" title="Ishkashimi language">Ishkashmi</a>. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_Iranica" title="Encyclopaedia Iranica">Encyclopaedia Iranica</a></i> states:<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Bactrian thus occupies an intermediary position between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yidgha_language" title="Yidgha language">Yidgha</a>-<a href="/wiki/Munji_language" title="Munji language">Munji</a> on the one hand, <a href="/wiki/Sogdian_language" title="Sogdian language">Sogdian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khwarezmian_language" title="Khwarezmian language">Choresmian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parthian_language" title="Parthian language">Parthian</a> on the other: it is thus in its natural and rightful place in Bactria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote><p> The principal religions of the area before <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> were <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Nelson_Frye" title="Richard Nelson Frye">Richard Nelson Frye</a>, a leading historian of Iranian and Central Asian history, the Persian migration to Central Asia may be considered the beginning of the modern <a href="/wiki/Tajiks" title="Tajiks">Tajik</a> nation, and ethnic Persians, along with some elements of East-Iranian Bactrians and <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdians</a>, as the main ancestors of modern Tajiks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclop&#230;dia Britannica">Encyclop&#230;dia Britannica</a></i> states:<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Tajiks are the direct descendants of the Iranian peoples whose continuous presence in Central Asia and northern Afghanistan is attested from the middle of the 1st millennium <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872446130">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps">bc</span>. The ancestors of the Tajiks constituted the core of the ancient population of Khw&#257;rezm (Khorezm) and Bactria, which formed part of Transoxania (Sogdiana). They were included in the empires of Persia and Alexander the Great, and they intermingled with such later invaders as the Kush&#257;ns and Hepthalites in the 1st&#8211;6th centuries <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps">ad</span>. Over the course of time, the eastern Iranian dialect that was used by the ancient Tajiks eventually gave way to Farsi, a western dialect spoken in Iran and Afghanistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
