98&#8211;117)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Roman mythology</b> is the body of traditional stories pertaining to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Founding_of_Rome" title="Founding of Rome">legendary origins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">religious system</a>, as represented in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_literature" title="Latin literature">literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_art" title="Roman art">visual arts of the Romans</a>. "Roman mythology" may also refer to the modern study of these representations, and to the subject matter as represented in the literature and art of other cultures in any period.\n</p><p>The Romans usually treated their traditional narratives as historical, even when these have miraculous or supernatural elements. The stories are often concerned with politics and morality, and how an individual\'s personal integrity relates to his or her responsibility to the community or Roman state. Heroism was an important theme. When the stories illuminate Roman religious practices, they are more concerned with ritual, <a href="/wiki/Augur" title="Augur">augury</a>, and institutions than with <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cosmogony" title="Cosmogony">cosmogony</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The study of Roman religion and <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myth</a> is complicated by the early influence of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion" title="Ancient Greek religion">Greek religion</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_peninsula" title="Italian peninsula">Italian peninsula</a> during Rome\'s <a href="/wiki/Protohistory" title="Protohistory">protohistory</a>, and by the later artistic imitation of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_literature" title="Ancient Greek literature">Greek literary models</a> by Roman authors. In matters of theology, the Romans were curiously eager to identify <a href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_deities" title="List of Roman deities">their own gods</a> with those of the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Interpretatio_graeca" title="Interpretatio graeca">interpretatio graeca</a></i>), and to reinterpret stories about <a href="/wiki/List_of_Greek_mythological_figures" title="List of Greek mythological figures">Greek deities</a> under the names of their Roman counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Rome\'s early myths and legends also have a dynamic relationship with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscan_mythology" title="Etruscan mythology">Etruscan religion</a>, less documented than that of the Greeks.\n</p><p>While Roman mythology may lack a body of divine narratives as extensive as that found in Greek literature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">Romulus and Remus</a> suckling the she-wolf is as famous as any image from <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> except for the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_Horse" title="Trojan Horse">Trojan Horse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Because Latin literature was more widely known in Europe throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, the interpretations of Greek myths by the Romans often had a greater influence on narrative and pictorial representations of "<a href="/wiki/Classical_mythology" title="Classical mythology">classical mythology</a>" than Greek sources. In particular, the versions of Greek myths in <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i>, written during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, came to be regarded as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_(literary)" title="Canon (literary)">canonical</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rimini219.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In this wall painting from <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> looks on while the physician <a href="/wiki/Iapyx" title="Iapyx">Iapyx</a> tends to the wound of her son, <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>; the tearful boy is her grandson <a href="/wiki/Ascanius" title="Ascanius">Ascanius, also known as Iulus</a>, legendary ancestor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Julio-Claudian_dynasty" title="Julio-Claudian dynasty">Julio-Claudian dynasty</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Because <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> played the central role in Roman religion that myth did for the Greeks, it is sometimes doubted that the Romans had much of a native mythology.  This perception is a product of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classical scholarship</a> of the 19th century, which valued Greek civilization as more "authentically creative."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> From the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> to the 18th century, however, Roman myths were an inspiration particularly for <a href="/wiki/Western_painting" title="Western painting">European painting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">[6]</a></sup> The Roman tradition is rich in historical myths, or <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a>, concerning the foundation and rise of the city. These narratives focus on human actors, with only occasional intervention from deities but a pervasive sense of divinely ordered destiny. In Rome\'s earliest period, history and myth have a mutual and complementary relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/T._P._Wiseman" title="T. P. Wiseman">T. P. Wiseman</a> notes:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The Roman stories still <i>matter</i>, as they mattered to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">Dante</a> in 1300 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> in 1600 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding fathers of the United States">founding fathers of the United States</a> in 1776. What does it take to be a <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">free citizen</a>? Can a <a href="/wiki/Superpower" title="Superpower">superpower</a> still be a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a>? How does well-meaning <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> turn into murderous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyranny" title="Tyranny">tyranny</a>?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Major sources for Roman myth include the <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vergil" title="Vergil">Vergil</a>  and the first few books of <a href="/wiki/Livy" title="Livy">Livy</a>\'s history as well as Dionysius\' s <i>Roman Antiquities</i>. Other important sources are the <i><a href="/wiki/Fasti_(poem)" title="Fasti (poem)">Fasti</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, a six-book poem structured by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_calendar" title="Roman calendar">Roman religious calendar</a>, and the fourth book of elegies by <a href="/wiki/Propertius" title="Propertius">Propertius</a>. Scenes from Roman myth also appear in Roman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wall_painting" title="Wall painting">wall painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">coins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_sculpture" title="Roman sculpture">sculpture</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">reliefs</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_myths">Founding myths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding myths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Founding_of_Rome" title="Founding of Rome">Founding of Rome</a></div>\n<p>The <i>Aeneid</i> and Livy\'s early history are the best extant sources for <a href="/wiki/Founding_of_Rome" title="Founding of Rome">Rome\'s founding myths</a>. Material from Greek heroic legend was grafted onto this native stock at an early date.  The Trojan prince <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a> was cast as husband of <a href="/wiki/Lavinia" title="Lavinia">Lavinia</a>, daughter of King <a href="/wiki/Latinus" title="Latinus">Latinus</a>, patronymical ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/Latins_(Italic_tribe)" title="Latins (Italic tribe)">Latini</a>,  and therefore through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_kings_of_Alba_Longa" title="Latin kings of Alba Longa">convoluted revisionist genealogy</a> as forebear of <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">Romulus and Remus</a>.  By extension, the Trojans were adopted as the mythical ancestors of the Roman people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-headline" id="Religion_and_myth">Religion and myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion and myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome" title="Religion in ancient Rome">Religion in ancient Rome</a></div>\n<p>Narratives of divine activity played  a more important role in the system of Greek religious belief than among the Romans, for whom ritual and cult were primary. Although Roman religion did not have a basis in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scriptures" title="Scriptures">scriptures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exegesis" title="Exegesis">exegesis</a>, priestly literature was one of the earliest written forms of <a href="/wiki/Latin_literature" title="Latin literature">Latin prose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The books <i>(libri)</i> and commentaries <i>(commentarii)</i> of the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Pontiffs" title="College of Pontiffs">College of Pontiffs</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Augur" title="Augur">augurs</a> contained religious procedures, prayers, and rulings and opinions on points of religious law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Although at least some of this archived material was available for consultation by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Roman senate</a>, it was often <i>occultum genus litterarum</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> an arcane form of literature to which by definition only priests had access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Prophecies pertaining to world history and to Rome\'s destiny turn up fortuitously at critical junctures in history, discovered suddenly in the nebulous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibylline_books" title="Sibylline books">Sibylline books</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Tarquinius_Superbus" title="Lucius Tarquinius Superbus">Tarquin the Proud</a> (according to legend) purchased in the late 6th century BC from the <a href="/wiki/Cumaean_Sibyl" title="Cumaean Sibyl">Cumaean Sibyl</a>. Some aspects of archaic Roman religion survived in the lost theological works of the 1st-century BC scholar <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro</a>, known through other classical and Christian authors.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arte_romana,_triade_capitolina,_160-180_dc_(guidonia_montecelio,_museo_civico_archeologico)_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2940" data-file-width="4014" 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" Triad">Capitoline Triad</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest pantheon included Janus, <a href="/wiki/Vesta_(mythology)" title="Vesta (mythology)">Vesta</a>, and a leading so-called <a href="/wiki/Archaic_Triad" title="Archaic Triad">Archaic Triad</a> of Jupiter, Mars, and Quirinus, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flamines_maiores" title="Flamines maiores">flamens were of the highest order</a>. According to tradition, <a href="/wiki/Numa_Pompilius" title="Numa Pompilius">Numa Pompilius</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabine" title="Sabine">Sabine</a> second <a href="/wiki/King_of_Rome" title="King of Rome">king of Rome</a>, founded Roman religion; Numa was believed to have had as his consort and adviser a Roman goddess or <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymph</a> of fountains and of prophecy, <a href="/wiki/Egeria_(mythology)" title="Egeria (mythology)">Egeria</a>. The Etruscan-influenced <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Triad" title="Capitoline Triad">Capitoline Triad</a> of Jupiter, Juno and Minerva later became central to official religion, replacing the Archaic Triad &#8212; an unusual example within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_religion" title="Indo-European religion">Indo-European religion</a> of a supreme triad formed of two female deities and only one male. The cult of <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">Diana</a> became established on the <a href="/wiki/Aventine_Hill" title="Aventine Hill">Aventine Hill</a>, but the most famous Roman manifestation of this goddess may be <a href="/wiki/Diana_Nemorensis" title="Diana Nemorensis">Diana Nemorensis</a>, owing to the attention paid to her cult by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.G._Frazer" title="J.G. Frazer">J.G. Frazer</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythography" title="Mythography">mythographical</a> classic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough" title="The Golden Bough">The Golden Bough</a></i>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2680" data-file-width="2458" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg/220px-Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg/330px-Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg/440px-Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pompeii_-_Casa_dei_Vettii_-_Ixion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Punishment of <a href="/wiki/Ixion" title="Ixion">Ixion</a>: in the center stands <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> holding the <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">caduceus</a>, and on the right <a href="/wiki/Juno_(mythology)" title="Juno (mythology)">Juno</a> sits on her throne. Behind her <a href="/wiki/Iris_(mythology)" title="Iris (mythology)">Iris</a> stands and gestures. On the left <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Blond" title="Blond">blond</a> figure) stands behind the wheel, manning it, with Ixion already tied to it. <a href="/wiki/Nephele" title="Nephele">Nephele</a> sits at Mercury\'s feet. - Roman fresco  from the eastern wall of the <a href="/wiki/Triclinium" title="Triclinium">triclinium</a> in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_the_Vettii" title="House of the Vettii">House of the Vettii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pompeian_Styles" title="Pompeian Styles">Fourth Style</a> (60-79 AD).</div></div></div>\n<p>The gods represented distinctly the practical needs of daily life, and Ancient Romans scrupulously accorded them the appropriate rites and offerings. Early Roman divinities included a host of "specialist gods" whose names were invoked in the carrying out of various specific activities. Fragments of old ritual accompanying such acts as plowing or sowing reveal that at every stage of the operation a separate deity was invoked, the name of each deity being regularly derived from the verb for the operation. <a href="/wiki/Tutelary_deity#Ancient_Rome" title="Tutelary deity">Tutelary deities</a> were particularly important in ancient Rome.\n</p><p>Thus, <a href="/wiki/Janus" title="Janus">Janus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vesta_(mythology)" title="Vesta (mythology)">Vesta</a> guarded the door and hearth, the <a href="/wiki/Lares" title="Lares">Lares</a> protected the field and house, <a href="/wiki/Pales" title="Pales">Pales</a> the pasture, <a href="/wiki/Saturn_(mythology)" title="Saturn (mythology)">Saturn</a> the sowing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceres_(Roman_mythology)" title="Ceres (Roman mythology)">Ceres</a> the growth of the grain, <a href="/wiki/Pomona_(mythology)" title="Pomona (mythology)">Pomona</a> the fruit, and <a href="/wiki/Consus" title="Consus">Consus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ops" title="Ops">Ops</a> the harvest. Even the majestic <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>, the ruler of the gods, was honored for the aid his rains might give to the farms and vineyards. In his more encompassing character he was considered, through his weapon of lightning, the director of human activity. Due to his widespread domain, the Romans regarded him as their protector in their military activities beyond the borders of their own community. Prominent in early times were the gods <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quirinus" title="Quirinus">Quirinus</a>, who were often identified with each other. Mars was a god of war; he was honored in March and October. Modern scholars see Quirinus as the patron of the armed community in time of peace.\n</p><p>The 19th-century scholar <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wissowa" title="Georg Wissowa">Georg Wissowa</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> thought that the Romans distinguished two classes of gods, the <i><a href="/wiki/Di_indigetes" title="Di indigetes">di indigetes</a></i> and the <i>di novensides</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Novensiles" title="Novensiles">novensiles</a></i>:  the <i>indigetes</i> were the original gods of the Roman state, their names and nature indicated by the titles of the earliest priests and by the fixed festivals of the calendar, with 30 such gods honored by special festivals; the <i>novensides</i> were later divinities whose cults were introduced to the city in the historical period, usually at a known date and in response to a specific crisis or felt need. <a href="/wiki/Arnaldo_Momigliano" title="Arnaldo Momigliano">Arnaldo Momigliano</a> and others, however, have argued that this distinction cannot be maintained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hannibalic_War" title="Hannibalic War">war with Hannibal</a>, any distinction between "indigenous" and "immigrant" gods begins to fade, and the Romans embraced diverse gods from various cultures as a sign of strength and universal divine favor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p><br/>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_gods">Foreign gods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Foreign gods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2160" data-file-width="3224" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg/220px-Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg/330px-Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg/440px-Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fresque_Mithraeum_Marino.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithras" title="Mithras">Mithras</a> in a Roman wall painting</div></div></div>\n<p>The absorption of neighboring local gods took place as the Roman state conquered neighboring territories. The Romans commonly granted the local gods of a conquered territory the same honors as the earlier gods of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_cult_(ancient_Rome)" title="Imperial cult (ancient Rome)">Roman state religion</a>. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Castor_and_Pollux" title="Castor and Pollux">Castor and Pollux</a>, the conquered settlements in Italy seem to have contributed to the Roman pantheon <a href="/wiki/Diana_(mythology)" title="Diana (mythology)">Diana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hercules" title="Hercules">Hercules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a>, and deities of lesser rank, some of whom were Italic divinities, others originally derived from the Greek culture of <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a>. In 203 BC, Rome imported the cult object embodying <a href="/wiki/Cybele" title="Cybele">Cybele</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pessinus" title="Pessinus">Pessinus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a> and welcomed its arrival with due <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#caerimoniae" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">ceremony</a>. Both <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a>, poets contemporary in the mid-1st century BC, offer disapproving glimpses of Cybele\'s wildly ecstatic cult.\n</p><p>In some instances, deities of an enemy power were formally invited through the ritual of <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#evocatio" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">evocatio</a></i> to take up their abode in new sanctuaries at Rome.\n</p><p>Communities of foreigners <i>(<a href="/wiki/Peregrinus_(Roman)" title="Peregrinus (Roman)"> peregrini</a>)</i> and former slaves <i>(libertini)</i> continued their own religious practices within the city. In this way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithras" title="Mithras">Mithras</a> came to Rome and his popularity within the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a> spread his cult as far afield as <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>. The important Roman deities were eventually identified with the more <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphic</a> Greek gods and goddesses, and assumed many of their attributes and myths.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
