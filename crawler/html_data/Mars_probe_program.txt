For programs called Mars, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mars_(disambiguation)" title="Mars (disambiguation)">Mars (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>The <b>Mars program</b> was a series of <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_spacecraft" title="Unmanned spacecraft">unmanned spacecraft</a> launched by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> between 1960 and 1973. The spacecraft were intended to <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars" title="Exploration of Mars">explore</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, and included <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">flyby</a> probes, <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">landers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiters</a>.\n</p><p>Early Mars spacecraft were small, and launched by <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya</a> rockets. Starting with two failures in 1969, the heavier <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a> rocket was used to launch larger 5 tonne spacecraft, consisting of an orbiter and a lander to Mars. The orbiter bus design was likely somewhat rushed into service and immature,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> considering that it performed very unreliably in the <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a> variant after 1975. This reliability problem was common to much Soviet space hardware from the late 1960s and early 1970s and was largely corrected with a deliberate policy, implemented in the mid-1970s, of consolidating (or "debugging") existing designs rather than introducing new ones.  The names of the "Mars" missions do not need to be translated, as the word "Mars" is spelled and pronounced approximately the same way in English and Russian.\n</p><p>In addition to the Mars program, the Soviet Union also sent a probe to Mars as part of the <a href="/wiki/Zond_program" title="Zond program">Zond program</a>; <a href="/wiki/Zond_2" title="Zond 2">Zond 2</a>, however it failed en route. Two more spacecraft were sent during the <a href="/wiki/Phobos_program" title="Phobos program">Phobos program</a>. In 1996, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> launched <a href="/wiki/Mars_96" title="Mars 96">Mars 96</a>, its first interplanetary mission since the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a>, however it failed to depart Earth orbit.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" Mars were the two <a href="/wiki/Mars_1M" title="Mars 1M">Mars 1M</a> spacecraft, which each had a mass of about 650&#160;kg. Both were launched in 1960 and failed to achieve orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsnik_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsnik-1">[1]</a></sup>  The spacecraft were dubbed <i>Marsnik</i> by the Western media.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_2MV">Mars 2MV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mars 2MV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_1" title="Mars 1">Mars 1</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mars_1" title="Mars 1">Mars 1</a> was launched in 1962 but failed en route to Mars. Two other Soviet launches at around the same time, <a href="/wiki/Mars_2MV-4_No.1" title="Mars 2MV-4 No.1">Mars 2MV-4 No.1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_2MV-3_No.1" title="Mars 2MV-3 No.1">Mars 2MV-3 No.1</a> were 900-kilogram (2,000&#160;lb) spacecraft, however both failed to leave Earth orbit due to problems with the upper stages of their carrier rockets.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_2M">Mars 2M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mars 2M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mars_2M_No.521" title="Mars 2M No.521">Mars 2M No.521</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_2M_No.522" title="Mars 2M No.522">Mars 2M No.522</a>, known in the West as Mars 1969A and B, were heavier spacecraft with masses of 5 tonnes (4.9 long tons; 5.5 short tons). They were launched by <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a> rockets, and consisted of orbiters. Both were destroyed during launch.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_4M">Mars 4M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mars 4M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Mars 4M spacecraft; <a href="/wiki/Mars_2" title="Mars 2">Mars 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a> missions consisted of identical spacecraft, each with an orbiter and an attached lander, which became the first spacecraft to reach the surface of Mars.\n</p><p>The orbiters\' primary scientific objectives were to image the Martian surface and clouds, determine the temperature on Mars, study the topography, composition and physical properties of the surface, measure properties of the atmosphere, monitor the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> and the interplanetary and Martian <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, and act as communications relays to send signals from the landers to Earth.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" The descent module entered the Martian atmosphere at roughly 6.0&#160;km/s at a steeper angle than planned. The descent system malfunctioned and the lander crashed at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=45_S_30_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">30&#176;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45&#176;S 30&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-45; -30</span></span></span></a></span>, delivering the Soviet Union coat of arms to the surface. Meanwhile, the orbiter engine performed a burn to put the spacecraft into a 1380 x 24,940&#160;km, 18&#8209;hour orbit about Mars with an inclination of 48.9 degrees. Scientific instruments were generally turned on for about 30 minutes near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_3">Mars 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mars 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1972_CPA_4114.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="892" data-file-width="1248" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1972_CPA_4114.jpg/220px-1972_CPA_4114.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1972_CPA_4114.jpg/330px-1972_CPA_4114.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/1972_CPA_4114.jpg/440px-1972_CPA_4114.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1972_CPA_4114.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a> lander stamp</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>Launch date/time:\n<ul><li>Mars 3: May 28, 1971 at 15:26:30 UTC</li></ul></li>\n<li>Launch mass (including fuel):\n<ul><li>Combined: 4650&#160;kg</li>\n<li>Orbiter: 3440&#160;kg</li>\n<li>Lander: 1210&#160;kg</li></ul></li>\n<li>On-orbit dry mass: 2265&#160;kg</li>\n<li>Dimensions: 4.1 meters tall, 2 meters across (5.9 meters across with solar panels deployed)</li></ul>\n<p>Mars 3\'s descent module was released at 09:14 UT on December 2, 1971, 4 hours 35 minutes before reaching Mars. The descent module entered the Martian atmosphere at roughly 5.7&#160;km/s. Through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_braking" title="Aerodynamic braking">aerodynamic braking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachutes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retrorocket" title="Retrorocket">retrorockets</a>, the lander achieved a soft landing at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=45_S_158_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">45&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">158&#176;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">45&#176;S 158&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-45; -158</span></span></span></a></span> and began operations. However, after 20 seconds the instruments stopped working for unknown reasons, perhaps as a result of the massive surface dust storms raging at the time of landing. Mars 3 lander still managed to transmit a portion of the first picture of Martian surface. Meanwhile, the orbiter had suffered from a partial loss of fuel and did not have enough to put itself into a planned 25-hour orbit. The engine instead performed a truncated burn to put the spacecraft into a long 12-day-19-hour period orbit about Mars with an inclination thought to be similar to that of Mars 2 (48.9 degrees).\n</p><p>Both landers had a small Mars \'<a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">rover</a>\' on board, which would move across the surface on skis while connected to the lander with a 15-meter umbilical. Two small metal rods were used for autonomous obstacle avoidance, as radio signals from Earth would take too long to drive the rovers using remote control. Each rover had both a densitometer and a dynamic penetrometer, to test the density and the bearing strength of the soil. Because of the demise of the landers, neither rover saw action.\n</p><p>The Mars 2 and 3 orbiters sent back a large volume of data covering the period from December 1971 to March 1972, although transmissions continued through August. It was announced that Mars 2 and 3 had completed their missions by August 22, 1972, after 362 orbits completed by Mars 2 and 20 orbits by Mars 3. The probes sent back a total of 60 pictures. The images and data enabled creation of surface relief maps, and gave information on the Martian <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_3MS">Mars 3MS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mars 3MS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_419" title="Kosmos 419">Kosmos 419</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Kosmos_419" title="Kosmos 419">Kosmos 419</a> was launched on May 5, 1971. It consisted of only an orbiter, and was intended to become the first spacecraft to orbit Mars (<a href="/wiki/Areocentric_orbit" title="Areocentric orbit">areocentric orbit</a>), thereby beating the American <a href="/wiki/Mariner_8" title="Mariner 8">Mariner 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a> spacecraft.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mars_4" title="Mars 4">Mars 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_5" title="Mars 5">Mars 5</a> orbiters, launched in 1973, were designed to orbit Mars and return information on the composition, structure, and properties of the Martian atmosphere and surface. The spacecraft were also designed to act as communications links to the Mars 6 and 7 landers. Like earlier heavy spacecraft, they were launched by Proton-K rockets.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_4">Mars 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mars 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_4" title="Mars 4">Mars 4</a></div>\n<ul><li>Launch date/time:\n<ul><li>Mars 4: July 21, 1973 at 19:30:59 UTC</li></ul></li>\n<li>On-orbit mass:\n<ul><li>Dry: 2270&#160;kg</li>\n<li>Fully fuelled: 3440&#160;kg</li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>The Mars 4 orbiter reached Mars on February 10, 1974. Due to a flaw in the transistor 2&#1058;-312 which resulted in its degradation during the voyage to Mars, the retro-rockets designed to slow the craft into Mars orbit did not fire, and Mars 4 flew by the planet at a range of 2200&#160;km. It returned one swath of pictures and some radio occultation data which constituted the first detection of the nightside <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> on Mars. It continued to return interplanetary data from <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a> after the flyby.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_5">Mars 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mars 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_5" title="Mars 5">Mars 5</a></div>\n<ul><li>Launch date/time:\n<ul><li>Mars 5: July 25, 1973 at 18:55:48 UTC</li></ul></li>\n<li>On-orbit mass:\n<ul><li>Dry: 2270&#160;kg</li>\n<li>Fully fuelled: 3440&#160;kg</li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>Mars 5 reached Mars on February 12, 1974 at 15:45 UT and was inserted into an elliptical 1755 by 32,555&#160;km, 24 h 53 min orbit with an inclination of 35.3 degrees. Nearly synchronized with the rotation of the planet, its two phototelevision cameras could be commanded to take 12 pictures during each close approach. The Vega camera used a wide area 52mm lens with color filters, the Zulfar camera used a telescopic 350mm lens and long-pass orange filter. Images were transmitted in a rapid 220-line mode, and then selected pictures were retransmitted at 880 or 1760 line resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Images_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Images-2">[2]</a></sup> Mars 5 collected data for 22 orbits until a loss of pressurization in the transmitter housing ended the mission. About 60 images were returned over a nine-day period showing swaths of the area south of <a href="/wiki/Valles_Marineris" title="Valles Marineris">Valles Marineris</a>, from <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=5_N_330_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">5&#176;N</span> <span class="longitude">330&#176;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">5&#176;N 330&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">5; -330</span></span></span></a></span> to <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=20_S_130_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">20&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">130&#176;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">20&#176;S 130&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-20; -130</span></span></span></a></span>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_3MP">Mars 3MP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mars 3MP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1973 the speed required to place a spacecraft in an interplanetary trajectory had to be increased.  Thus the Proton could not deliver spacecraft with an orbiter and an attached lander to the necessary trajectory to reach Mars, as had been possible in 1971. To resolve this problem, four spacecrafts were launched. The Mars 4 and 5 orbiters, which had been launched separately, were used to relay communications, and to complete mission objectives which would have been completed by landers. Two landers were launched with orbiter type buses (Mars 6 and 7), but without fuel to enter orbit of the Mars satellite.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_6">Mars 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mars 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_6" title="Mars 6">Mars 6</a></div>\n<p>Mars 6 successfully lifted off on August 5, 1973, into an intermediate Earth orbit on a <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a>/<a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">D</a> booster and then launched into a Mars transfer trajectory. Total fueled launch mass of the lander and bus was 3260&#160;kg. It reached Mars on March 12, 1974. The descent module separated from the bus at a distance of 48,000&#160;km from Mars. The bus continued on into a heliocentric orbit after passing within 1600&#160;km of Mars. The descent module entered the atmosphere at 09:05:53 UT at a speed of 5.6&#160;km/s. The parachute opened at 09:08:32 UT after the module had slowed its speed to 600&#160;m/s by <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">aerobraking</a>. During this time the craft was collecting data and transmitting it directly to the bus for immediate relay to Earth. Contact with the descent module was lost at 09:11:05 UT in "direct proximity to the surface", probably either when the retrorockets fired or when it hit the surface at an estimated 61&#160;m/s. Mars 6 landed at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=23.90_S_19.42_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">23&#176;54&#8242;S</span> <span class="longitude">19&#176;25&#8242;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">23.90&#176;S 19.42&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-23.90; -19.42</span></span></span></a></span> in the <a href="/wiki/Margaritifer_Terra" title="Margaritifer Terra">Margaritifer Terra</a> region of Mars. The landed mass was 635&#160;kg. The descent module transmitted 224 seconds of data before transmissions ceased, the first data returned from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a>. Much of the data was unreadable due to a flaw in a transistor which led to degradation of the system during its journey to Mars.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_7">Mars 7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mars 7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_7" title="Mars 7">Mars 7</a></div>\n<p>Mars 7 successfully lifted off on August 9, 1973, into an intermediate Earth orbit on a Proton-K/D booster and then launched into a Mars transfer trajectory. Total fueled launch mass of the lander and bus was 3260&#160;kg. It reached Mars on March 9, 1974. Due to a problem in the operation of one of the on-board systems (attitude control or retro-rockets) the landing probe separated prematurely (4 hours before encounter) and missed the planet by 1300&#160;km. The early separation was probably due to a computer error which resulted from degradation of the systems during the trip to Mars. The intended landing site was <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mars_program&amp;params=50_S_28_W_globe:Mars"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">50&#176;S</span> <span class="longitude">28&#176;W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">50&#176;S 28&#176;W</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-50; -28</span></span></span></a></span>. The lander and bus continued on into heliocentric orbits.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_4NM_and_5NM">Mars 4NM and 5NM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mars 4NM and 5NM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Mars 4NM and Mars 5NM projects would have seen heavier spacecraft launched by <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1</a> rockets. They would have deployed heavy <a href="/wiki/Marsokhod" title="Marsokhod">Marsokhod</a> rovers onto the surface, and conducted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">sample return missions</a>. The N1 failed on all four of its test flights, and was never used to launch any Mars spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_5M">Mars 5M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mars 5M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mars_5M" title="Mars 5M">Mars 5M</a> (Mars 79) was a sample return mission developed in 1977 to be double launched in 1979 by Proton launchers and then docked in Earth orbit for a joint flight of orbital and return modules to Mars. The project was canceled due to the low reliability of the <a href="/wiki/Igla_(spacecraft_docking_system)" title="Igla (spacecraft docking system)">Igla automatic docking system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
