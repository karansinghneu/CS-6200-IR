broadcasting">public broadcaster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_syndication" title="Broadcast syndication">television program distributor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">non-profit</a> organization and is the most prominent provider of <a href="/wiki/Educational_television" title="Educational television">educational television</a> programming to public television stations in the United States, distributing series such as <i><a href="/wiki/American_Experience" title="American Experience">American Experience</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/America%27s_Test_Kitchen" title="America\'s Test Kitchen">America\'s Test Kitchen</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiques_Roadshow_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Antiques Roadshow (U.S. TV series)">Antiques Roadshow</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_(TV_series)" title="Arthur (TV series)">Arthur</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Finding_Your_Roots" title="Finding Your Roots">Finding Your Roots</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontline_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Frontline (U.S. TV series)">Frontline</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magic_School_Bus_(TV_series)" title="The Magic School Bus (TV series)">The Magic School Bus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Masterpiece_(TV_series)" title="Masterpiece (TV series)">Masterpiece</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Rogers%27_Neighborhood" title="Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood">Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(TV_series)" title="Nature (TV series)">Nature</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">Nova</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/PBS_NewsHour" title="PBS NewsHour">PBS NewsHour</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/This_Old_House" title="This Old House">This Old House</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_shows_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_shows-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>PBS is funded by <a href="/wiki/List_of_PBS_member_stations" title="List of PBS member stations">member station</a> dues, the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_Public_Broadcasting" title="Corporation for Public Broadcasting">Corporation for Public Broadcasting</a>, corporate contributions, <a href="/wiki/National_Datacast" title="National Datacast">National Datacast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pledge_drive" title="Pledge drive">pledge drives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Private_foundation_(United_States)" title="Private foundation (United States)">private foundations</a> and individual citizens. All proposed funding is subjected to a set of standards to ensure the program is free of influence from the funding source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the mid-2000s, <a href="/wiki/GfK" title="GfK">Roper Opinion Research</a> polls commissioned by PBS have consistently placed the service as the most-trusted national institution in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_infographic_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_infographic-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A 2016&#8211;2017 study by <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Media_Research" title="Nielsen Media Research">Nielsen</a> found 80% of all US television households view the network\'s programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_PBS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-About_PBS-1">[1]</a></sup> However, PBS is not responsible for all programming carried on public television stations, a large proportion of which come from <a href="/wiki/Network_affiliate" title="Network affiliate">affiliates</a>, including such member stations as <a href="/wiki/WGBH-TV" title="WGBH-TV">WGBH</a>, <a href="/wiki/WETA-TV" title="WETA-TV">WETA</a>, <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>, <a href="/wiki/WTTW" title="WTTW">WTTW</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Public_Television" title="American Public Television">American Public Television</a> and independent producers. This distinction is a frequent source of viewer confusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The service has more than 350 <a href="/wiki/Network_affiliate#Member_stations" title="Network affiliate">member</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_station" title="Television station">television stations</a>, many owned by educational institutions, by non-profit groups affiliated with a local public school district or collegiate educational institution, or by <a href="/wiki/State_governments_of_the_United_States" title="State governments of the United States">state government</a>-owned or state government-related entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StationIndex_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-StationIndex-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div history</a></i></div></div></div>\n<p>Founded by Hartford N. Gunn Jr., PBS began operations on October 5, 1970, taking over many of the functions of its predecessor, <a href="/wiki/National_Educational_Television" title="National Educational Television">National Educational Television</a> (NET), which later merged with <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a> station WNDT to form <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1973, it merged with <a href="/wiki/Educational_Television_Stations" title="Educational Television Stations">Educational Television Stations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JARVIK_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-JARVIK-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unlike the five major <a href="/wiki/Commercial_broadcasting" title="Commercial broadcasting">commercial broadcast</a> <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">television networks</a> in the United States, <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>, <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Network" title="Fox Network">Fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_CW" title="The CW">The CW</a> &#8211; which compensate their affiliate stations to carry their programs &#8211; PBS is not a network but a program distributor that provides television content and related services to its member stations. Each station is charged with the responsibility of programming local content such as news, interviews, cultural and <a href="/wiki/Public_affairs_(broadcasting)" title="Public affairs (broadcasting)">public affairs</a> programs for their individual market or state that supplements content provided by PBS and other public television distributors.\n</p><p>In a television network structure, affiliates give up portions of their local advertising airtime in exchange for carrying network programming, and the network pays its affiliates a share of the revenue it earns from advertising. By contrast, PBS member stations pay fees for the shows acquired and distributed by the national organization. Under this relationship, PBS member stations have greater latitude in local scheduling than their commercial broadcasting counterparts. Scheduling of PBS-distributed series may vary greatly depending on the market. This can be a source of tension as stations seek to preserve their localism, and PBS strives to market a consistent national lineup. However, PBS has a policy of "common carriage," which requires most stations to clear the national prime time programs on a common <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_programming" title="Broadcast programming">programming</a> schedule to market them nationally more effectively. Management at former <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> member <a href="/wiki/KCET" title="KCET">KCET</a> cited unresolvable financial and programming disputes among its major reasons for leaving PBS after over 40 years in January 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KCET_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-KCET-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although PBS has a set schedule of programming, particularly in regard to its <a href="/wiki/Prime_time" title="Prime time">prime time</a> schedule, member stations reserve the right to schedule PBS-distributed programming in other time slots or not clear it at all if they choose to do so; few of the service\'s members carry all its programming. Most PBS stations <a href="/wiki/Timeshift_channel" title="Timeshift channel">timeshift</a> some distributed programs. Once PBS accepts a program offered for distribution, PBS, rather than the originating member station, retains exclusive rebroadcasting rights during an agreed period. Suppliers retain the right to sell the program in non-broadcast media such as <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a>, books, and sometimes PBS <a href="/wiki/Merchandising" title="Merchandising">licensed merchandise</a>.\n</p><p>In 1991, the <a href="/wiki/Corporation_for_Public_Broadcasting" title="Corporation for Public Broadcasting">Corporation for Public Broadcasting</a> resumed production for most PBS shows that debuted prior to 1977, with the exceptions of <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Week" title="Washington Week">Washington Week in Review</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Week" title="Wall Street Week">Wall Street Week</a></i> (CPB resumed production of <i>Washington Week</i> in 1997).\n</p><p>In 1994, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicle_of_Philanthropy" title="The Chronicle of Philanthropy">The Chronicle of Philanthropy</a></i> released the results of the largest study on the popularity and credibility of charitable and non-profit organizations. PBS ranked as the 11th "most popular charity/non-profit in America" from over 100 charities researched in the study conducted by the industry publication, with 38.2% of Americans over the age of 12 choosing "love" and "like a lot" for PBS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2009, PBS signed up for the <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">Nielsen ratings</a> <a href="/wiki/Audience_measurement" title="Audience measurement">audience measurement</a> reports, and began to be included in its primetime and daily "Television Index" reports, alongside the major commercial broadcast networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In May 2011, PBS announced that it would incorporate breaks containing underwriter spots for corporate and foundation sponsors, <a href="/wiki/Promo_(media)" title="Promo (media)">program promotions</a> and identification spots within four breaks placed within episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(TV_series)" title="Nature (TV series)">Nature</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">NOVA</a></i>, airing episodes broken up into segments of up to 15 minutes, rather than airing them as straight 50- to 55-minute episodes. The strategy began that fall, with the intent to expand the in-program breaks to the remainder of the schedule if successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2011, PBS released <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">apps</a> for <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Android_(OS)" title="Android (OS)">Android</a> to allow viewing of full-length videos on mobile devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> An update in 2015 added <a href="/wiki/Chromecast" title="Chromecast">Chromecast</a> support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On February 28, 2012, PBS partnered with AOL to launch <i><a href="/wiki/Makers:_Women_Who_Make_America" title="Makers: Women Who Make America">Makers: Women Who Make America</a></i>, a digital documentary series focusing on high-achieving women in male-dominated industries such as war, comedy, space, business, Hollywood and politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>PBS initially struggled to compete with online media such as <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> for market share. In a 2012 speech to 850 top executives from PBS stations, Senior Vice President of Digital <a href="/wiki/Jason_Seiken" title="Jason Seiken">Jason Seiken</a> warned that PBS was in danger of being disrupted by YouTube studios such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maker_Studios" title="Maker Studios">Maker Studios</a>. In the speech, later described as a "seminal moment" for public television,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> he laid out his vision for a new style of PBS digital video production. Station leadership rallied around his vision and Seiken formed PBS Digital Studios, which began producing educational but edgy videos, something Seiken called "PBS-quality with a YouTube sensibility."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The studio\'s first hit, an auto-tuned version of the theme from one of their most famous television programs, <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Rogers%27_Neighborhood" title="Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood">Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood</a></i>, was one of YouTube\'s 10 most viral videos of 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> By 2013, monthly video views on PBS.org had risen from 2 million to a quarter-billion, PBS.org traffic had surpassed that of the CBS, NBC, and ABC web sites, PBSKids.org had become the dominant US children\'s site for video, and PBS had won more 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webby_Awards" title="Webby Awards">Webby Awards</a> than any other media company in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 8, 2013, full-length episodes of PBS\' prime time, news and children\'s programs were made available through the <a href="/wiki/Roku" title="Roku">Roku</a> streaming player; programming is available on Roku as separate streaming channels for PBS and PBS Kids content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming">Programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_programs_broadcast_by_PBS" title="List of programs broadcast by PBS">List of programs broadcast by PBS</a></div>\n<p>The evening and primetime schedule on PBS features a diverse array of programming including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_arts" title="Fine arts">fine arts</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Great_Performances" title="Great Performances">Great Performances</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">drama</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Masterpiece_(TV_series)" title="Masterpiece (TV series)">Masterpiece</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Family_(2002_TV_series)" title="American Family (2002 TV series)">American Family: Journey of Dreams</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">Nova</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(TV_series)" title="Nature (TV series)">Nature</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/American_Experience" title="American Experience">American Experience</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/American_Masters" title="American Masters">American Masters</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/History_Detectives" title="History Detectives">History Detectives</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiques_Roadshow_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Antiques Roadshow (U.S. TV series)">Antiques Roadshow</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Austin_City_Limits" title="Austin City Limits">Austin City Limits</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Soundstage_(TV_series)" title="Soundstage (TV series)">Soundstage</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/Public_affairs_(broadcasting)" title="Public affairs (broadcasting)">public affairs</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontline_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Frontline (U.S. TV series)">Frontline</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/PBS_NewsHour" title="PBS NewsHour">PBS NewsHour</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Week" title="Washington Week">Washington Week</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Nightly_Business_Report" title="Nightly Business Report">Nightly Business Report</a>); <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">independent films</a> and <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentaries</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/POV_(TV_series)" title="POV (TV series)">P.O.V.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Independent_Lens" title="Independent Lens">Independent Lens</a></i>); <a href="/wiki/Home_improvement" title="Home improvement">home improvement</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/This_Old_House" title="This Old House">This Old House</a></i>); and <a href="/wiki/Talk_show" title="Talk show">interviews</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Amanpour_%26_Company" title="Amanpour &amp; Company">Amanpour &amp; Company</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tavis_Smiley_(TV_series)" title="Tavis Smiley (TV series)">Tavis Smiley</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dick_Cavett_Show" title="The Dick Cavett Show">The Dick Cavett Show</a></i>). In 2012, PBS began organizing much of its prime time programming around a genre-based schedule (for example, drama series encompass the Sunday schedule, while science-related programs are featured on Wednesdays).\n</p><p>PBS broadcasts children\'s programming as part of the service\'s (and including content supplied by other distributors not programmed by the service, its member stations\') morning and afternoon schedule. As the children\'s programs it distributes are intended to educate as well as entertain its target audience, PBS and its stations have long been in compliance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E/I" title="E/I">educational programming</a> guidelines set by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> in response to the enactment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_Television_Act" title="Children\'s Television Act">Children\'s Television Act of 1990</a>. Many member stations have historically also broadcast <a href="/wiki/Distance_education" title="Distance education">distance education</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Instructional_television" title="Instructional television">instructional television</a> programs, typically during daytime slots; though with the advent of digital television, which has allowed stations to carry these programs on <a href="/wiki/Digital_subchannel" title="Digital subchannel">digital subchannels</a> in lieu of the main PBS feed or exclusively over the Internet, many member stations/networks have replaced distance education content with children\'s and other programming,.\n</p><p>Unlike its radio counterpart, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a>, PBS does not have a central program production arm or news division. All of the programming carried by PBS, whether news, documentary or entertainment, is created by (or in most cases produced under contract with) other parties, such as individual member stations. <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> member <a href="/wiki/WGBH-TV" title="WGBH-TV">WGBH-TV</a> is one of the largest producers of <a href="/wiki/Educational_television" title="Educational television">educational television</a> programming, including shows like <i><a href="/wiki/American_Experience" title="American Experience">American Experience</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arthur_(TV_series)" title="Arthur (TV series)">Arthur</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masterpiece_Theatre" title="Masterpiece Theatre">Masterpiece Theatre</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">Nova</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Antiques_Roadshow" title="Antiques Roadshow">Antiques Roadshow</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontline_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Frontline (U.S. TV series)">Frontline</a></i>, as well as many other children\'s and lifestyle programs. News programs are produced by <a href="/wiki/WETA-TV" title="WETA-TV">WETA-TV</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PBS_Newshour" title="PBS Newshour">PBS Newshour</a></i>) in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/WPBT" title="WPBT">WPBT</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami</a>. Newark, New Jersey/<a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> member <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a> produces or distributes programs such as <i><a href="/wiki/Secrets_of_the_Dead" title="Secrets of the Dead">Secrets of the Dead</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(TV_series)" title="Nature (TV series)">Nature</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberchase" title="Cyberchase">Cyberchase</a></i>. PBS also works with other networks for programming such as <a href="/wiki/CNN_International" title="CNN International">CNN International</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Amanpour_%26_Company" title="Amanpour &amp; Company">Amanpour &amp; Company</a></i> which is a co-production of CNN International and WNET.\n</p><p>PBS member stations are known for rebroadcasting British television <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Costume_drama" title="Costume drama">costume dramas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britcoms" title="Britcoms">comedies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> programs (acquired from the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and other sources) such as <i><a href="/wiki/Downton_Abbey" title="Downton Abbey">Downton Abbey</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/%27Allo_%27Allo!" title="\'Allo \'Allo!">\'Allo \'Allo!</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Are_You_Being_Served%3F" title="Are You Being Served?">Are You Being Served?</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Benny_Hill_Show" title="The Benny Hill Show">The Benny Hill Show</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Dwarf" title="Red Dwarf">Red Dwarf</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_and_Rise_of_Reginald_Perrin" title="The Fall and Rise of Reginald Perrin">The Fall and Rise of Reginald Perrin</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Father_Ted" title="Father Ted">Father Ted</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Fawlty_Towers" title="Fawlty Towers">Fawlty Towers</a></i>; <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Enfield_and_Chums" title="Harry Enfield and Chums">Harry Enfield and Chums</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Keeping_Up_Appearances" title="Keeping Up Appearances">Keeping Up Appearances</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Monty_Python%27s_Flying_Circus" title="Monty Python\'s Flying Circus">Monty Python\'s Flying Circus</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Bean" title="Mr. Bean">Mr. Bean</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vicar_of_Dibley" title="The Vicar of Dibley">The Vicar of Dibley</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_(TV_series)" title="Sherlock (TV series)">Sherlock</a></i>; consequently, this has led to jocular references that the service\'s name stands for "Primarily British Series". However, a significant amount of sharing takes place. The BBC and British broadcasters such as <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> often cooperate with PBS stations, producing material that is shown on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>. Less frequently, Canadian, Australian and other international programming appears on PBS stations (such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_Green_Show" title="The Red Green Show">The Red Green Show</a></i>, currently distributed by syndicator Executive Program Services); public broadcasting syndicators are more likely to offer this programming to U.S.-based public television stations.\n</p><p>PBS is not the only distributor of public television programming to the member stations. Other distributors have emerged from the roots of companies that maintained loosely held regional public television stations in the 1960s. <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>-based <a href="/wiki/American_Public_Television" title="American Public Television">American Public Television</a> (which, among other names, was formerly known as Eastern Educational Network and the American Program Service) is second only to PBS for distributing programs to U.S. non-commercial stations. Another distributor is NETA (formerly SECA), whose properties have included <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shapies" title="The Shapies">The Shapies</a></i> and <i>Jerry Yarnell School of Fine Art</i>. In addition, the member stations themselves also produce a variety of local shows, some of which subsequently receive national distribution through PBS or other distributors.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rerun" title="Rerun">Rerun programming</a> is generally uncommon on PBS or its member stations, with some exceptions. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lawrence_Welk_Show" title="The Lawrence Welk Show">The Lawrence Welk Show</a></i> has aired continuously in reruns on PBS (through the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Educational_Television_Authority" title="Oklahoma Educational Television Authority">Oklahoma Educational Television Authority</a>) almost every weekend since 1986. Other programs that have been aired in reruns are generally past PBS series whose hosts have retired or are now deceased (for example, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joy_of_Painting" title="The Joy of Painting">The Joy of Painting</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Rogers%27_Neighborhood" title="Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood">Mister Rogers\' Neighborhood</a></i>) or have simply ended production (such as <i><a href="/wiki/DragonflyTV" title="DragonflyTV">DragonflyTV</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Clifford_the_Big_Red_Dog_(TV_series)" title="Clifford the Big Red Dog (TV series)">Clifford the Big Red Dog</a></i>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PBS_Kids">PBS Kids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: PBS Kids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PBS_Kids" title="PBS Kids">PBS Kids</a></div>\n<p>Launched in 1994 as PTV, PBS Kids is the brand for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_programming" title="Children\'s programming">children\'s programs</a> aired by PBS. The PBS Kids network, which was launched in 1999 and operated until 2005, was largely funded by satellite provider <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a>. The channel ceased operations on September 26, 2005, in favor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sprout_(TV_network)" title="Sprout (TV network)">PBS Kids Sprout</a>, a commercial <a href="/wiki/Digital_cable" title="Digital cable">digital cable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite television</a> channel originally operated as a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sesame_Workshop" title="Sesame Workshop">Sesame Workshop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apax_Partners" title="Apax Partners">Apax Partners</a> (<a href="/wiki/NBCUniversal" title="NBCUniversal">NBCUniversal</a>, which Comcast acquired in 2011, later acquired the other partners\' interests in the channel in 2012). However, the original programming block still exists on PBS, filling daytime and in some cases, weekend morning schedules on its member stations; many members also carry 24-hour locally programmed children\'s networks featuring PBS Kids content on one of their digital subchannels. As of 2018, PBS Kids and <a href="/wiki/KidsClick" title="KidsClick">KidsClick</a> are the only two children\'s programming blocks on U.S. broadcast television.\n</p><p>As PBS is often known for doing, PBS Kids has broadcast imported series from other countries; these include British series originally broadcast by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> and <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a>. PBS also carried <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> events,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> football. Notable football commentators included <a href="/wiki/Upton_Bell" title="Upton Bell">Upton Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marty_Glickman" title="Marty Glickman">Marty Glickman</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bob_Casciola" title="Bob Casciola">Bob Casciola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Dowling_(American_football)" title="Brian Dowling (American football)">Brian Dowling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sean_McDonough" title="Sean McDonough">Sean McDonough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Corrigan_(sportscaster)" title="Jack Corrigan (sportscaster)">Jack Corrigan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Other sports programs included interview series such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Way_It_Was_(TV_series)" title="The Way It Was (TV series)">The Way It Was</a></i> and <i>The Sporting Life</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The board of directors is responsible for governing and setting policy for PBS, consisting of 27 members: 14 professional directors (station managers), 12 general directors (outside directors), and the PBS president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about-board_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-board-34">[34]</a></sup> All PBS Board members serve three-year terms, without pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about-board_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-board-34">[34]</a></sup> PBS member stations elect the 14 professional directors; the board elects the 12 general directors and appoints the PBS president and CEO; and the entire board elects its officers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Member_stations">Member stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Member stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_PBS_member_stations" title="List of PBS member stations">List of PBS member stations</a></div>\n<p>As of  March 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, PBS maintains current memberships with 354 television stations encompassing 50 states, the District of Columbia and four U.S. possessions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StationIndex_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-StationIndex-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-re_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-re-36">[36]</a></sup> as such, it is the only television broadcaster in the United States &#8211; commercial or non-commercial &#8211; which has station partners licensed in every U.S. state (by comparison, none of the five major commercial broadcast networks has affiliates in certain states where PBS has members, most notably <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>). The service has an estimated national reach of 93.74% of all households in the United States (or 292,926,047 Americans with at least one television set).\n</p><p>PBS stations are commonly operated by <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">nonprofit organizations</a>, state agencies, local authorities (such as municipal boards of education), or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> in their <a href="/wiki/City_of_license" title="City of license">city of license</a>; this is similar (albeit more centralized in states where a licensee owns multiple stations rebroadcasting the main PBS member) to the early model of commercial broadcasting in the U.S., in which network-affiliated stations were initially owned by companies that owned few to no other television stations elsewhere in the country. In some U.S. states, a group of PBS stations throughout the entire state may be organized into a single <a href="/wiki/State_network" title="State network">regional "subnetwork"</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Public_Television" title="Alabama Public Television">Alabama Public Television</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Educational_Television_Network" title="Arkansas Educational Television Network">Arkansas Educational Television Network</a>); in this model, PBS programming and other content is distributed by the originating station in the subnetwork to other <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_relay_station" title="Broadcast relay station">full-power stations that serve as satellites as well as any low-power translators</a> in other areas of the state. Some states may be served by such a regional network and simultaneously have PBS member stations in a certain city (such as the case with secondary member <a href="/wiki/KBDI-TV" title="KBDI-TV">KBDI-TV</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, which is not related to <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> member network <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_PBS" title="Rocky Mountain PBS">Rocky Mountain PBS</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Flagship_(broadcasting)" title="Flagship (broadcasting)">flagship station</a> and primary Denver PBS member, KRMA-TV) that operate autonomously from the regional member network.\n</p><p>As opposed to the present commercial broadcasting model in which network programs are often carried exclusively on one television station in a given market, PBS may maintain more than one member station in certain markets, which may be owned by the licensee of the market\'s primary PBS member station or owned by a separate licensee (as a prime example, <a href="/wiki/KOCE-TV" title="KOCE-TV">KOCE-TV</a>, <a href="/wiki/KLCS" title="KLCS">KLCS</a> and <a href="/wiki/KVCR-DT" title="KVCR-DT">KVCR-DT</a> &#8211; which are all individually owned &#8211; serve as PBS stations for the Los Angeles <a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">market</a>; KCET served as the market\'s primary PBS member until it left the service in January 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> at which time it was replaced by KOCE). KCET expects to rejoin PBS later in 2018, thus giving the Los Angeles area four different member stations.\n</p><p>For these cases, PBS utilizes the Program Differentiation Plan, which divides by percentage the amount of programs distributed by the service that each member can carry on their schedule; often, this assigns a larger proportion of PBS-distributed programming to the primary member station, with the secondary members being allowed to carry a lesser amount of program offerings from the service\'s schedule. Unlike public broadcasters in most other countries, PBS cannot own any of the stations that broadcasts its programming; therefore it is one of the few television programming bodies that does not have any <a href="/wiki/Owned-and-operated_station" title="Owned-and-operated station">owned-and-operated stations</a>. This is partly due to the origins of the PBS stations themselves, and partly due to historical <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_license" title="Broadcast license">broadcast license</a> issues.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participating_stations">Participating stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Participating stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most PBS member stations have produced at least some nationally distributed programs. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On-air_fundraising">On-air fundraising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: On-air fundraising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since 53% to 60% of public television\'s revenues come from private membership donations and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grants" title="Grants">grants</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-revenue_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-revenue-38">[38]</a></sup> most stations solicit individual donations by methods including <a href="/wiki/Fundraising" title="Fundraising">fundraising</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pledge_drive" title="Pledge drive">pledge drives</a> or <a href="/wiki/Telethon" title="Telethon">telethons</a>, which disrupt regularly scheduled programming. This has been perceived as potentially annoying since regularly scheduled programming is often replaced with specials aimed at a wider audience (such as music specials aimed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Boom" title="Baby Boom">Baby Boomer</a> generation and financial, health and motivational programs) to solicit new members and donations; during fundraising events, these programs are often interrupted within the broadcast by long-form segments (of six to eight minutes in length) encouraging viewers to donate to their PBS member.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Underwriting_spot" title="Underwriting spot">Underwriting spots</a> are aired at the end of each program, which differ from traditional commercials in several ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Each spot must be approved to meet several guidelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The main guidelines state that underwriting spots cannot be qualitative in any way, nor can they have any call to action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Accusations_of_political.2Fideological_bias"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_of_political/ideological_bias">Accusations of political/ideological bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Accusations of political/ideological bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A 1982 broadcast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Information_Agency" title="United States Information Agency">United States Information Agency</a> program <i>Let Poland be Poland</i> about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_of_Communism_in_Poland_(1989)" title="End of Communism in Poland (1989)">martial law declared in Poland in 1981</a> was widely viewed in the U.S., but met with skepticism on the part of eastern European broadcasters (communist countries at the time) due to concerns that the program\'s "provocative and anticommunist" tone was intended as propaganda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999 at least three public television stations were caught selling or trading their mailing lists with the Democratic National Committee. Under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRS" title="IRS">IRS</a> regulations nonprofit organizations are prohibited from participating in political actions. Officials from the Corporation for Public Broadcasting condemned the practice and conducted an investigation into the matter. The stations involved were in New York, Boston, and Washington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Individual programs aired by PBS have been the targets of organized campaigns by individuals and groups with opposing views, including former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Education" title="United States Secretary of Education">United States Secretary of Education</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Spellings" title="Margaret Spellings">Margaret Spellings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc-012605_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-012605-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bill_Moyers" title="Bill Moyers">Bill Moyers</a> resigned in 2005 after more than three decades as a PBS regular, citing political pressure to alter the content of his program and saying Chairman of the Board of the Corporation for Public Broadcasting <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Tomlinson" title="Kenneth Tomlinson">Kenneth Tomlinson</a> had mounted a "vendetta" against him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-farhi_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-farhi-47">[47]</a></sup> Moyers eventually returned to host <i><a href="/wiki/Bill_Moyers_Journal" title="Bill Moyers Journal">Bill Moyers Journal</a></i>, after Tomlinson\'s resignation from CPB. Subsequently, PBS made room temporarily for rightwing commentator <a href="/wiki/Tucker_Carlson" title="Tucker Carlson">Tucker Carlson</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a> and co-host of <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN\'s</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Crossfire_(U.S._TV_program)" title="Crossfire (U.S. TV program)">Crossfire</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Journal_Editorial_Report" title="The Journal Editorial Report">The Journal Editorial Report</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gigot" title="Paul Gigot">Paul Gigot</a>, an editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> editorial page (this program has since moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News Channel</a>) to partially balance out the perceived left-leaning PBS shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBSBias_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBSBias-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit_with_Pacific_Arts">Lawsuit with Pacific Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lawsuit with Pacific Arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 1990s, PBS became involved in a dispute over home video licensing rights with <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Arts_Corporation" title="Pacific Arts Corporation">Pacific Arts Corporation</a>, a multimedia company owned and operated by former <a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">Monkees</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nesmith" title="Michael Nesmith">Michael Nesmith</a>.\n</p><p>In 1990, Pacific Arts secured a contract with PBS to distribute their back catalog of programming on VHS under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PBS_Home_Video" title="PBS Home Video">PBS Home Video</a> banner. However, in the early 1990s, Pacific Arts and PBS went through a series of serious disagreements. Lawsuits were filed: by Nesmith and Pacific Arts against PBS for breach of contract, intentional misrepresentation, intentional concealment, negligent misrepresentation, and interference with contract; and by PBS against Nesmith and Pacific Arts for lost royalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-current_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-current-49">[49]</a></sup> The lawsuits escalated in 1994 and 1995 into major litigation between the parties over these rights and payments. PBS and Nesmith and Pacific Arts vigorously prosecuted these multimillion-dollar counter-suits.\n</p><p>The six plaintiffs included PBS, <a href="/wiki/WGBH-TV" title="WGBH-TV">WGBH</a>, <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ken_Burns" title="Ken Burns">Ken Burns</a>-owned American Documentaries and Radio Pioneers Film Project and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_Television_Workshop" title="Children\'s Television Workshop">Children\'s Television Workshop</a>. They sought approximately $5 million in disputed royalties, advances, guarantees and license fees for programs and the use of the PBS logo from the defendants Pacific Arts and Nesmith.\n</p><p>Due to the cost of the litigation, Pacific Arts was forced to cease distribution operations and suspended the use of the PBS logo on the Pacific Arts videos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-current_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-current-49">[49]</a></sup> Though Pacific Arts distribution system had ceased operating, the various plaintiffs were counting on capturing a personal financial guarantee Nesmith had made to PBS in the original PBS deal in 1990.\n</p><p>The cases went to jury trial in Federal Court in Los Angeles in February 1999. After three days of deliberation, the jury unanimously sided with Nesmith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pacarts_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-pacarts-50">[50]</a></sup> The court awarded Pacific Arts $14,625,000 for loss of its rights library, plus $29,250,000 in punitive damages. The jury awarded $3 million to Nesmith personally, including $2 million in punitive damages for a total award to Nesmith and Pacific Arts of $48,875,000. The jury resolved the outstanding license fee issues by ordering Pacific Arts and Nesmith to pay approximately $1.2 million to American Documentaries for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Civil_War_(TV_series)" title="The Civil War (TV series)">The Civil War</a></i>, about $230,000 to <a href="/wiki/WGBH-TV" title="WGBH-TV">WGBH</a>, and $150,000 to <a href="/wiki/WNET" title="WNET">WNET</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-current_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-current-49">[49]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the ruling, Nesmith expressed his personal disappointment with PBS and was quoted by <a href="/wiki/BBC_News" title="BBC News">BBC News</a> as stating "It\'s like finding your grandmother stealing your stereo. You\'re happy to get your stereo back, but it\'s sad to find out your grandmother is a thief."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The decision never went to an appeals court and the final amount paid to Pacific Arts and Nesmith was an undisclosed sum agreed to in an out-of-court settlement.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Warning.2C_Alert_and_Response_Network_.28WARN.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Warning,_Alert_and_Response_Network_(WARN)">Warning, Alert and Response Network (WARN)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PBS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Warning, Alert and Response Network (WARN)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>PBS provides an alternate path for <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Emergency_Alerts" title="Wireless Emergency Alerts">WEA</a> alerts to wireless carriers. The alerts are transmitted through the PBS satellite network on the <a href="/wiki/AMC-21" title="AMC-21">AMC-21</a> satellite to PBS stations who broadcast the messages over their transmitters for reception by wireless carriers at their cell sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WARN/PBS_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-WARN/PBS-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS//UTUBE_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS//UTUBE-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The network is funded by a grant through <a href="/wiki/National_Telecommunications_and_Information_Administration" title="National Telecommunications and Information Administration">National Telecommunications and Information Administration</a> (NTIA).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
