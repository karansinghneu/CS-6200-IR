<small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[&#712;ka&#946;u &#240;&#592; &#712;&#946;o&#592; &#643;p&#616;&#712;&#638;&#592;&#771;s&#592;]</a></span>) is a rocky <a href="/wiki/Headlands_and_bays" title="Headlands and bays">headland</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.\n</p><p>A common misconception is that the Cape of Good Hope is the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. This misconception was based on the misbelief that the Cape was the dividing point between the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Oceans</a>. Contemporary geographic knowledge instead states the southernmost point of Africa is <a href="/wiki/Cape_Agulhas" title="Cape Agulhas">Cape Agulhas</a> about 150 kilometres (90&#160;mi) to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East-southeast" title="East-southeast">east-southeast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The currents of the two oceans meet at the point where the warm-water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agulhas_current" title="Agulhas current">Agulhas current</a> meets the cold-water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benguela_current" title="Benguela current">Benguela current</a> and turns back on itself. That oceanic meeting point fluctuates between Cape Agulhas and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Point" title="Cape Point">Cape Point</a> (about 1.2 kilometres (0.75&#160;mi) east of the Cape of Good Hope).\n</p>\n<div class="mw-kartographer-container thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width: 360px;"><a class="mw-kartographer-map" data-height="360" data-lat="-34.3579413" data-lon="18.4723393" data-overlays=\'["_aa770d1d64952d251dffb0edd8e2eed54990f5dd"]\' data-style="osm-intl" data-width="360" data-zoom="7" href="/wiki/Special:Map/7/-34.3579413/18.4723393/en" mw-data="interface" style="background-image: url(https://maps.wikimedia.org/img/osm-intl,7,-34.3579413,18.4723393,360x360.png?lang=en&amp;domain=en.wikipedia.org&amp;title=Cape+of+Good+Hope&amp;groups=_aa770d1d64952d251dffb0edd8e2eed54990f5dd); height: 360px;"></a><div class="thumbcaption">The Cape of Good Hope is at the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a> approximately 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>When following the western side of the African coastline from the equator, however, the Cape of Good Hope marks the point where a ship begins to travel more eastward than southward. Thus, the first modern rounding of the cape in 1488 by <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">Portuguese</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a> was a milestone in the attempts by the Portuguese to establish direct trade relations with the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> (although <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> mentioned a claim that the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicians</a> had done so <a href="/wiki/Necho_II#Phoenician_expedition" title="Necho II">far earlier</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Dias called the cape <i>Cabo das Tormentas</i> ("Cape of Storms"; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Stormkaap</i>), which was the original name of the "Cape of Good Hope".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As one of the <a href="/wiki/Great_capes" title="Great capes">great capes</a> of the South Atlantic Ocean, the Cape of Good Hope has long been of special significance to sailors, many of whom refer to it simply as "<b>the Cape</b>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Waypoint" title="Waypoint">waypoint</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Route" title="Cape Route">Cape Route</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Clipper_route" title="Clipper route">clipper route</a> followed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clipper_ship" title="Clipper ship">clipper ships</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and still followed by several offshore <a href="/wiki/Yacht_racing" title="Yacht racing">yacht races</a>.\n</p><p>The term <i>Cape of Good Hope</i> is also used in three other ways:\n</p>\n<ul><li>It is a section of the <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain_National_Park" title="Table Mountain National Park">Table Mountain National Park</a>, within which the cape of the same name, as well as Cape Point, falls. Prior to its incorporation into the national park, this section constituted the Cape Point Nature Reserve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n<li>It was the name of the early <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> established by the Dutch in 1652, on the Cape Peninsula.</li>\n<li>Just before the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa" title="Union of South Africa">Union of South Africa</a> was formed, the term referred to the entire region that in 1910 was to become the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope_Province" title="Cape of Good Hope Province">Cape of Good Hope Province</a> (usually shortened to <i>the Cape Province</i>).</li></ul>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cyzicus" title="Eudoxus of Cyzicus">Eudoxus of Cyzicus</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="\'d\' in \'dye\'">d</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="\'k\' in \'kind\'">k</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">&#917;&#8020;&#948;&#959;&#958;&#959;&#962;</span>, <i>E&#250;doxos</i>; fl. c. 130 BC) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Greek" title="Ethnic Greek">Greek</a> <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigator</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_VIII_Physcon" title="Ptolemy VIII Physcon">Ptolemy VIII</a>, king of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic dynasty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, who found the wreck of a ship in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> that appeared to have come from <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">Gades</a> (today\'s <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a> in Spain), rounding the Cape.\n</p><p>When Eudoxus was returning from his second voyage to India, the wind forced him south of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Aden" title="Gulf of Aden">Gulf of Aden</a> and down the coast of Africa for some distance. Somewhere along the coast of East Africa, he found the remains of the ship. Due to its appearance and the story told by the natives, Eudoxus concluded that the ship was from Gades and had sailed anti-clockwise around Africa, passing the Cape and entering the Indian Ocean. This inspired him to repeat the voyage and attempt a <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigation</a> of the continent. Organising the expedition on his own account he set sail from Gades and began to work down the African coast. The difficulties were too great, however, and he was obliged to return to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After this failure he again set out to circumnavigate Africa. His eventual fate is unknown. Although some, such as <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a>, claimed that Eudoxus did achieve his goal, the most probable conclusion is that he perished on the journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1450 <a href="/wiki/Fra_Mauro_map" title="Fra Mauro map">Fra Mauro map</a>, the Indian Ocean is depicted as connected to the Atlantic. Fra Mauro puts the following inscription by the southern tip of Africa, which he names the "Cape of Diab", describing the exploration by a ship from the East around 1420:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marco_Polo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marco_Polo-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FraMauroChineseShip.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="789" data-file-width="1419" decoding="async" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/FraMauroChineseShip.jpg/220px-FraMauroChineseShip.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/FraMauroChineseShip.jpg/330px-FraMauroChineseShip.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/FraMauroChineseShip.jpg/440px-FraMauroChineseShip.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FraMauroChineseShip.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of the Fra Mauro Map describing the construction of the junks that navigate in the Indian Ocean.</div></div></div>\n<blockquote><p>"Around 1420 a ship, or junk, from India crossed the Sea of India  towards the Island of Men and the Island of Women, off Cape Diab, between the Green Islands and the shadows. It sailed for 40 days in a south-westerly direction  without ever finding anything other than wind and water. According to these people themselves, the ship  went some 2,000 miles ahead until - once favourable conditions came to an end - it turned round and sailed back to Cape Diab in 70 days".</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"The ships called junks (lit. "Zonchi") that navigate these seas carry four masts or more, some of which can be raised or lowered, and have 40 to 60 cabins for the merchants and only one tiller. They can navigate without a <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a>, because they have an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrologer" title="Astrologer">astrologer</a>, who stands on the side and, with an <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">astrolabe</a> in hand, gives orders to the navigator". (Text from the Fra Mauro map, 09-P25.)</p></blockquote>\n<p>Fra Mauro explained that he obtained the information from "a trustworthy source", who traveled with the expedition, possibly the Venetian explorer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_da_Conti" title="Niccol&#242; da Conti">Niccol&#242; da Conti</a> who happened to be in <a href="/wiki/Kozhikode" title="Kozhikode">Calicut</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> at the time the expedition left:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"What is more, I have spoken  with a person worthy of trust, who says that  he sailed in an Indian ship caught in the fury  of a tempest  for 40 days out in the Sea of India, beyond the <a href="/wiki/Sofala" title="Sofala">Cape of Soffala</a> and the Green Islands towards west-southwest; and according to the astrologers who act as their guides, they had advanced almost 2,000 miles. Thus one can believe and confirm  what is said by both these and those, and that they had therefore sailed 4,000 miles".</p></blockquote>\n<p>Fra Mauro also comments that the account of the expedition, together with the relation by <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> of the travels of <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cyzicus" title="Eudoxus of Cyzicus">Eudoxus of Cyzicus</a> from <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> through the southern Ocean in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Antiquity</a>, led him to believe that the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> was not a closed sea and that <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> could be circumnavigated by her southern end (Text from Fra Mauro map, 11, G2). This knowledge, together with the map depiction of the African continent, probably encouraged the Portuguese to intensify their effort to round the tip of Africa.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cape_Peninsula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4618" data-file-width="6500" decoding="async" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cape_Peninsula.jpg/300px-Cape_Peninsula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cape_Peninsula.jpg/450px-Cape_Peninsula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cape_Peninsula.jpg/600px-Cape_Peninsula.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cape_Peninsula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>, illustrating the position of the Cape of Good Hope. Africa.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div Hope.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Era" title="Early Modern Era">Early Modern Era</a>, the first European to reach the cape was the Portuguese explorer <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a> on 12 March 1488, who named it the "Cape of Storms" (<i>Cabo das Tormentas</i>). It was later renamed by <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">John II of Portugal</a> as "Cape of Good Hope" (<i>Cabo da Boa Esperan&#231;a</i>) because of the great optimism engendered by the opening of a sea route to <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and the East.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Khoikhoi" title="Khoikhoi">Khoikhoi</a> people lived in the cape area when the Dutch first settled there in 1652. The Khoikhoi had arrived in this area about fifteen hundred years before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Dutch called them <a href="/wiki/Khoikhoi" title="Khoikhoi">Hottentots</a>, a term that has now come to be regarded as pejorative.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> colonial administrator <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Riebeeck" title="Jan van Riebeeck">Jan van Riebeeck</a> established a resupply camp for the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> some 50&#160;km north of the cape in <a href="/wiki/Table_Bay" title="Table Bay">Table Bay</a> on 6 April 1652 and this eventually developed into <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>. Supplies of fresh food were vital on the long journey around Africa and Cape Town became known as "The Tavern of the Seas".\n</p><p>On 31 December 1687 a community of <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> (French Protestants) arrived at the Cape of Good Hope from the Netherlands. They had escaped from France and fled to the Netherlands to flee religious persecution in France. One example was Pierre Joubert who came from <a href="/wiki/La_Motte-d%27Aigues" title="La Motte-d\'Aigues">La Motte-d\'Aigues</a>. The Dutch East India Company needed skilled farmers at the Cape of Good Hope and the Dutch Government saw opportunities to settle Huguenots at the Cape and sent them there. The Cape colony gradually grew over the next 150 years or so until it stretched for hundreds of kilometres to the north and north-east.\n</p><p>During the Napoleonic Wars, the Dutch Republic was occupied by the French in 1795. Thus the Cape Colony became a French vassal and enemy of the British. Therefore, the United Kingdom invaded and occupied the Cape Colony that same year. The British relinquished control of the territory in 1803 but returned and reoccupied the Colony on 19 January 1806 following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Blaauwberg" title="Battle of Blaauwberg">Battle of Blaauwberg</a>. The Dutch ceded the territory to the British in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Treaty_of_1814" title="Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1814">Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1814</a> The Cape was then administered as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a> and it remained a British colony until it was incorporated into the independent <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa" title="Union of South Africa">Union of South Africa</a> in 1910 (now known as the Republic of South Africa).\n</p><p>The Portuguese government erected two navigational beacons, <b>Dias Cross</b> and <b>da Gama Cross</b>, to commemorate <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Bartolomeu Dias</a> as explorers who as mentioned were the first explorers to reach the cape. When lined up, the crosses point to Whittle Rock (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Cape_of_Good_Hope&amp;params=34_21_24.63_S_18_28_26.36_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">34&#176;21&#8242;24.63&#8243;S</span> <span class="longitude">18&#176;28&#8242;26.36&#8243;E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">34.3568417&#176;S 18.4739889&#176;E</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">-34.3568417; 18.4739889</span></span></span></a></span>), a large, permanently submerged shipping hazard in <a href="/wiki/False_Bay" title="False Bay">False Bay</a>. Two other beacons in <a href="/wiki/Simon%27s_Town" title="Simon\'s Town">Simon\'s Town</a> provide the intersection.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_of_Good_Hope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2942" data-file-width="3923" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg/220px-Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg/330px-Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg/440px-Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kapstadt_-_Kap_der_guten_Hoffnung.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sign at the Cape of Good Hope, 2018</div></div></div>\n<p>The Cape of Good Hope is at the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Peninsula</a>, about 2.3&#160;kilometres (1.4&#160;mi) west and a little south of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Point" title="Cape Point">Cape Point</a> on the south-east corner. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a> is about 50 kilometres to the north of the Cape, in <a href="/wiki/Table_Bay" title="Table Bay">Table Bay</a> at the north end of the peninsula.  The peninsula forms the western boundary of <a href="/wiki/False_Bay" title="False Bay">False Bay</a>.  Geologically, the rocks found at the two capes, and indeed over much of the peninsula, are part of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Peninsula#Geology" title="Cape Peninsula">Cape Supergroup</a>, and are formed of the same type of sandstones as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_Mountain_(South_Africa)" title="Table Mountain (South Africa)">Table Mountain</a> itself. Both the Cape of Good Hope and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Point" title="Cape Point">Cape Point</a> offer spectacular scenery; the whole of the southernmost portion of the Cape Peninsula is a wild, rugged, scenic and generally unspoiled national park.\n</p><p>The term <i>the Cape </i> has also been used in a wider sense, to indicate the area of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">the European colony</a> centred on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>, and the later <a href="/wiki/Cape_Province" title="Cape Province">South African province</a>. Since 1994, it has been broken up into three smaller provinces: the <a href="/wiki/Western_Cape" title="Western Cape">Western Cape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Cape" title="Eastern Cape">Eastern Cape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Cape" title="Northern Cape">Northern Cape</a>; parts of the province were also absorbed into the <a href="/wiki/North_West_(South_African_province)" title="North West (South African province)">North West</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Ostrich">ostrich</a> at the Cape of Good Hope</div></div></div>\n<p>With its diverse habitat, ranging from rocky mountain tops to beaches and open sea, the Cape of Good Hope is home to at least 250 species of birds including one of the two mainland colonies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spheniscus_demersus" title="Spheniscus demersus">African penguins</a>.\n</p><p>"Bush birds" tend to be rather scarce because of the coarse, scrubby nature of <a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">fynbos</a> vegetation. When flowering, however, <a href="/wiki/Protea" title="Protea">proteas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erica" title="Erica">ericas</a> attract <a href="/wiki/Sunbird" title="Sunbird">sunbirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugarbird" title="Sugarbird">sugarbirds</a>, and other species in search of nectar. For most of the year, there are more small birds in coastal thicket than in fynbos.\n</p><p>The Cape of Good Hope section of Table Mountain National Park is home to several species of antelope. Bontebok and eland are easily seen, and <a href="/wiki/Red_hartebeest" title="Red hartebeest">red hartebeest</a> can be seen in the grazing lawns in Smitswinkel Flats. Grey rhebok are less commonly seen and are scarce, but may be observed along the beach hills at Olifantsbos. Most visitors are unlikely to ever see either Cape grysbok or klipspringer.\n</p><p>The Cape of Good Hope section is home to four <a href="/wiki/Cape_mountain_zebra" title="Cape mountain zebra">Cape mountain zebra</a>. They might be seen by the attentive or lucky visitor, usually in Smitswinkel Flats.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg/220px-Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg/330px-Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg/440px-Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rock_hyrax_at_Cape_of_Good_Hope.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">Dassie</a> at the Cape of Good Hope</div></div></div>\n<p>There are a wealth of small animals such as <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. Small mammals include <a href="/wiki/Rock_hyrax" title="Rock hyrax">rock hyrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Four-striped_grass_mouse" title="Four-striped grass mouse">four-striped grass mouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsh_mongoose" title="Marsh mongoose">water mongoose</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_clawless_otter" title="Cape clawless otter">Cape clawless otter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fallow_deer" title="Fallow deer">fallow deer</a>.\n</p><p>The area offers excellent vantage points for whale watching. The <a href="/wiki/Southern_right_whale" title="Southern right whale">southern right whale</a> is the species most likely to be seen in <a href="/wiki/False_Bay" title="False Bay">False Bay</a> between June and November. Other species are the <a href="/wiki/Humpback_whale" title="Humpback whale">humpback whale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bryde%27s_whale" title="Bryde\'s whale">Bryde\'s whale</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">Seals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dusky_dolphin" title="Dusky dolphin">dusky dolphins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Killer_whale" title="Killer whale">killer whales</a> have also been seen.\n</p><p>The strategic position of the Cape of Good Hope between two major ocean currents, ensures a rich diversity of marine life. There is a difference between the sea life west of Cape Point and that to the east due to the markedly differing sea temperatures.\n</p><p>The South African Marine Living Resources Act is strictly enforced throughout the <a href="/wiki/Table_Mountain_National_Park" title="Table Mountain National Park">Table Mountain National Park</a>, and especially in marine protected areas. Disturbance or removal of any marine organisms is strictly prohibited between Schusters Bay and Hoek van Bobbejaan, but is allowed in other areas during season and with relevant permits.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chacma_baboons">Chacma baboons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_of_Good_Hope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chacma baboons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Chacma baboons (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papio_ursinus" title="Papio ursinus">Papio ursinus</a></i>) are the mammals most intimately associated with the Cape of Good Hope. Baboons inside the Cape of Good Hope section of the park are a major tourist attraction. There are 11 troops consisting of about 375 individuals throughout the entire Cape Peninsula. Six of these 11 troops either live entirely within the Cape of Good Hope section of the park, or use the section as part of their range. The Cape Point, Kanonkop, Klein Olifantsbos, and Buffels Bay troops live entirely inside the Cape of Good Hope section of the Park. The Groot Olifantsbos and Plateau Road troops range into the park.\n</p><p>Chacma baboons are widely distributed across southern Africa and are classified as &#8243;least concern" in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.iucnredlist.org/apps/redlist/details/full/16022/0" rel="nofollow"><i>IUCN Red List of Threatened Species</i></a>. However, the South African Parks Department states in its publication <i>Mountains in the Sea</i> that the baboon population on the Cape is "critically endangered." This is due to habitat loss, genetic isolation, and conflicts with humans. Cape baboons have been eliminated from the majority of their range across the Cape Peninsula, and the Cape of Good Hope section of Table Mountain National Park provides a sanctuary for the troops that live within its boundaries. It provides relative safety from nearby towns, where people have killed many baboons after the baboons raid their houses looking for food. Baboons are also frequently injured or killed outside of the park by cars and by electrocution on power lines. Inside the park, some management policies such as allowing barbecues and picnics in the baboon home ranges cause detriment to the troops, as they become embroiled in conflicts with guests to the park.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_of_Good_Hope&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG/220px-Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG/330px-Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG/440px-Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flora_at_Cape_Peninsula.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">Fynbos</a> at Cape Peninsula</div></div></div>\n<p>The Cape of Good Hope is an integral part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Floristic_Kingdom" title="Cape Floristic Kingdom">Cape Floristic Kingdom</a>, the smallest but richest of the world\'s six floral kingdoms. This comprises a treasure trove of 1100 species of indigenous plants, of which a number are <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> (occur naturally nowhere else on earth). The main type of <a href="/wiki/Fynbos" title="Fynbos">fynbos</a> ("fine bush") vegetation at the Cape of Good Hope is <a href="/wiki/Peninsula_Sandstone_Fynbos" title="Peninsula Sandstone Fynbos">Peninsula Sandstone Fynbos</a>, an endangered vegetation type that is endemic to the Cape Peninsula. Coastal <a href="/wiki/Hangklip_Sand_Fynbos" title="Hangklip Sand Fynbos">Hangklip Sand Fynbos</a> grows on low-lying alkaline sands and, right by the sea, small patches of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Flats_Dune_Strandveld" title="Cape Flats Dune Strandveld">Cape Flats Dune Strandveld</a> can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Characteristic fynbos plants include <a href="/wiki/Protea" title="Protea">proteas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erica" title="Erica">ericas</a> (heath), and <a href="/wiki/Restio" title="Restio">restios</a> (reeds). Some of the most striking and well-known members belong to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteacae" title="Proteacae">Proteacae</a> family, of which up to 24 species occur. These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_protea" title="King protea">king protea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protea" title="Protea">sugarbush</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_pincushion" title="Tree pincushion">tree pincushion</a> and golden <a href="/wiki/Cone_bush" title="Cone bush">cone bush</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Leucadendron" title="Leucadendron">Leucadendron</a> laureolum</i>).\n</p><p>Many popular horticultural plants like <a href="/wiki/Pelargonium" title="Pelargonium">pelargoniums</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freesia" title="Freesia">freesias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">daisies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lily" title="Lily">lilies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iris_(plant)" title="Iris (plant)">irises</a> also have their origins in fynbos.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legends">Legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
