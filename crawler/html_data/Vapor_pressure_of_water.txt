class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_vapor" title="Saturated vapor">in thermodynamic equilibrium with its condensed state</a>. As for other substances, water vapour pressure is a function of <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and can be determined with the <a detailed discussion of accuracy and considerations of the inaccuracy in temperature measurements is presented in Alduchov and Eskridge (1996). The analysis here shows the simple unattributed formula and the Antoine equation are reasonably accurate at 100&#160;&#176;C, but quite poor for lower temperatures above freezing. <a href="/wiki/Tetens_equation" title="Tetens equation">Tetens</a> is much more accurate over the range from 0 to 50&#160;&#176;C and very competitive at 75&#160;&#176;C, but Antoine\'s is superior at 75&#160;&#176;C and above. The unattributed formula must have zero error at around 26&#160;&#176;C, but is of very poor accuracy outside a very narrow range. Tetens\' equations are generally much more accurate and arguably simpler for use at everyday temperatures (e.g., in meteorology). As expected, <a href="/wiki/Arden_Buck_equation" title="Arden Buck equation">Buck\'s equation</a> for <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T</span> &gt; 0&#160;&#176;C is significantly more accurate than Tetens, and its superiority increases markedly above 50&#160;&#176;C, though it is more complicated to use. The Buck equation is even superior to the more complex <a href="/wiki/Goff%E2%80%93Gratch_equation" title="Goff&#8211;Gratch equation">Goff-Gratch equation</a> over the range needed for practical meteorology.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_approximations">Numerical approximations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapour_pressure_of_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Numerical approximations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For serious computation, Lowe (1977)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowe1977_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lowe1977-4">[4]</a></sup> developed two pairs of equations for temperatures above and below freezing, with different levels of accuracy. They are all very accurate (compared to <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius&#8211;Clapeyron relation">Clausius-Clapeyron</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Goff%E2%80%93Gratch_equation" title="Goff&#8211;Gratch equation">Goff-Gratch</a>) but use nested polynomials for very efficient computation.
