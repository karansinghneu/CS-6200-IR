(NASA)</div></div></div>\n<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">humans went into space</a>, several other <a href="/wiki/Animals_in_space" title="Animals in space">animals were launched into space</a>, including numerous other <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>, so that scientists could investigate the <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> effects of <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space travel</a>. The United States launched flights containing primate passengers primarily between 1948-1961 with one flight in 1969 and one in 1985. <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> launched two monkey-carrying flights in 1967. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> launched monkeys between 1983 and 1996. Most primates were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anesthetized" title="Anesthetized">anesthetized</a> before lift-off. Overall thirty-two monkeys flew in the space program; none flew more than once. Numerous backup monkeys also went through the programs but never flew. Monkeys and apes from several species were used, including <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_macaque" title="Rhesus macaque">rhesus macaque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crab-eating_macaque" title="Crab-eating macaque">crab-eating macaque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_squirrel_monkey" title="Common squirrel monkey">squirrel monkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_pig-tailed_macaque" title="Southern pig-tailed macaque">pig-tailed macaques</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" flight</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chimpanzee_Enos_before_the_flight_of_Mercury-Atlas_5.jpg"><img alt="" launch</div></div></div>\n<p>The first primate astronaut was Albert, a <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_macaque" title="Rhesus macaque">rhesus macaque</a>, who on June 11, 1948, rode to over 63&#160;km (39&#160;mi) on a <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a>. Albert died of suffocation during the flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Force_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Air_Force-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Albert was followed by Albert II who survived the V-2 flight but died on impact on June 14, 1949, after a parachute failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Force_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Air_Force-2">[2]</a></sup> Albert II became the first monkey and the first primate in space as his flight reached 134&#160;km (83&#160;mi) - past the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a> of 100&#160;km taken to designate the beginning of space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-4">[4]</a></sup> Albert III died at 35,000 feet (10.7&#160;km) in an explosion of his V2 on September 16, 1949. Albert IV, on the last monkey V-2 flight, died on impact on December 8 that year after another parachute failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Force_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Air_Force-2">[2]</a></sup> His flight reached 130.6&#160; km. Alberts, I, II, and IV were rhesus macaque while Albert III was a <a href="/wiki/Crab-eating_macaque" title="Crab-eating macaque">Crab-eating macaque</a>.\n</p><p>Monkeys later flew on <a href="/wiki/Aerobee" title="Aerobee">Aerobee</a> rockets. On April 18, 1951, a monkey, possibly called Albert V, died due to parachute failure. Yorick, also called Albert VI, along with 11 mouse crewmates, reached 236,000&#160;ft (72&#160;km, 44.7&#160;mi) and survived the landing, on September 20, 1951, the first monkey to do so (the dogs <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_dogs#Dezik,_Tsygan_and_Lisa" title="Soviet space dogs">Dezik, and Tsygan</a> had survived a trip to space in July of that year), although he died 2 hours later. Two of the mice also died after recovery; all of the deaths were thought to be related to stress from overheating in the sealed capsule in the New Mexico sun while awaiting the recovery team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Air_Force_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Air_Force-2">[2]</a></sup> Albert VI\'s flight surpassed the 50-mile boundary the U.S. used for spaceflight but was below the international definition of space. Patricia and Mike, two cynomolgus monkeys, flew on May 21, 1952, and survived, but their flight was only to 26 kilometers.\n</p><p>On December 13, 1958, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordo_(space_monkey)" title="Gordo (space monkey)">Gordo</a>, also called Old Reliable, a squirrel monkey, survived being launched aboard <a href="/wiki/PGM-19_Jupiter#Biological_flights" title="PGM-19 Jupiter">Jupiter AM-13</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">US Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-4">[4]</a></sup> He was killed due to mechanical failure of the parachute recovery system in the rocket <a href="/wiki/Nose_cone" title="Nose cone">nose cone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Able_air_and_space.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2626" data-file-width="1752" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Able_air_and_space.jpg/220px-Able_air_and_space.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Able_air_and_space.jpg/330px-Able_air_and_space.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Able_air_and_space.jpg/440px-Able_air_and_space.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Able_air_and_space.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Able on display at the National Air and Space Museum</div></div></div><p>On May 28, 1959, aboard the <a href="/wiki/PGM-19_Jupiter#Biological_flights" title="PGM-19 Jupiter">JUPITER AM-18</a>, Able, a rhesus macaque, and <a href="/wiki/Miss_Baker" title="Miss Baker">Miss Baker</a>, a squirrel monkey flew a successful mission. Able was born at the Ralph Mitchell Zoo in Independence, Kansas. They travelled in excess of 16,000&#160;km/h, and withstood 38 <a href="/wiki/G-force" title="G-force"><i>g</i></a> (373&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup>). Able died June 1, 1959, while undergoing surgery to remove an infected medical electrode, from a reaction to the anesthesia. Baker became the first monkey to survive the stresses of spaceflight and the related medical procedures. Baker died November 29, 1984, at the age of 27 and is buried on the grounds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Space_%26_Rocket_Center" title="United States Space &amp; Rocket Center">United States Space &amp; Rocket Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>. Able was preserved, and is now on display at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>. Their names were taken from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Army/Navy_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet">1943-1955 US military phonetic alphabet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On December 4, 1959, Sam, a rhesus macaque, flew on the <a href="/wiki/Little_Joe_2" title="Little Joe 2">Little Joe 2</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury program</a> to 53 miles high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beischer1962_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beischer1962-4">[4]</a></sup> Miss Sam, also a rhesus macaque, followed in 1960, on <a href="/wiki/Little_Joe_1B" title="Little Joe 1B">Little Joe 1B</a> although her flight was only to 8&#160;mi (13&#160;km) in a test of emergency procedures. <a href="/wiki/Ham_(chimpanzee)" title="Ham (chimpanzee)">Ham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enos_(chimpanzee)" title="Enos (chimpanzee)">Enos</a> also flew in the Mercury program but they were <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a>. The names \'Sam\' and \'Ham\' were acronyms. Sam was named in homage to the School of Aerospace Medicine at <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Air_Force_Base" title="Brooks Air Force Base">Brooks Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. The name \'Ham\' was taken from Holloman Aerospace Medicine at <a href="/wiki/Holloman_Air_Force_Base" title="Holloman Air Force Base">Holloman Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Goliath, a squirrel monkey, died in the explosion of his <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket_family)" title="Atlas (rocket family)">Atlas rocket</a> on November 10, 1961. A rhesus macaque called Scatback flew a sub-orbital flight on December 20, 1961, but was lost at sea after landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BurgessDubbs2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BurgessDubbs2007-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bonny, a pig-tailed macaque, flew on <a href="/wiki/Biosatellite_program" title="Biosatellite program">Biosatellite 3</a>, a mission which lasted from June 29 to July 8, 1969. This was the first multi-day monkey flight but came after longer human spaceflights were common. He died within a day of landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/STS-51-B" title="STS-51-B">Spacelab 3</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> flight <a href="/wiki/STS-51-B" title="STS-51-B">STS-51-B</a> featured two squirrel monkeys named No. 3165 and No. 384-80. The flight was from April 29 to May 6, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monkeys_and_apes_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> launched a <a href="/wiki/Pig-tailed_macaque" title="Pig-tailed macaque">pig-tailed macaque</a> named Martine on a Vesta rocket on March 7, 1967, and another named Pierette on March 13. These suborbital flights reached 243&#160;km (151&#160;mi) and 234&#160;km (145&#160;mi), respectively. Martine became the first monkey to survive more than a couple of hours after flying above the international definition of the edge of space. (Ham and Enos, launched earlier by the United States, had been chimpanzees).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union_and_Russia">Soviet Union and Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monkeys_and_apes_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soviet Union and Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Socialist_Republics" title="Union of Soviet Socialist Republics">Soviet</a> /<a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> space program used only <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_macaque" title="Rhesus macaque">rhesus macaques</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Bion_(satellite)" title="Bion (satellite)">Bion satellite program</a> in 1980s and 1990s. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The names of the monkeys began with sequential letters of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">Russian alphabet</a> (&#1040;, &#1041;, &#1042;, &#1043;, &#1044;, &#1045;, &#1025;, &#1046;, &#1047;...). The animals all survived their missions but for a single fatality in post-flight surgery, after which the program was cancelled.\n</p>\n<ol><li>The first monkeys launched by Soviet space program, Abrek and Bion, flew on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_6" title="Bion 6">Bion 6</a>. They remained aloft from December 14, 1983 &#8211; December 20, 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Next came <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_7" title="Bion 7">Bion 7</a> with monkeys Verny and Gordy from July 10, 1985 &#8211; July 17, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Then Dryoma and Yerosha on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_8" title="Bion 8">Bion 8</a> from September 29, 1987 &#8211; October 12, 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> After returning from space Dryoma was presented to <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_9" title="Bion 9">Bion 9</a> with monkeys Zhakonya and Zabiyaka followed from September 15, 1989 to September 28, 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The two took the space endurance record for monkeys at 13 days, 17 hours in space.</li>\n<li>Monkeys Krosh and Ivasha flew on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_10" title="Bion 10">Bion 10</a> from December 29, 1992 to January 7, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Krosh produced offspring, after rehabilitation upon returning to Earth.</li>\n<li>Lapik and Multik were the last monkeys in space until <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> launched one of its own in 2013. The pair flew aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bion_11" title="Bion 11">Bion 11</a> from December 24, 1996, to January 7, 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lsda.jsc.nasa.gov_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lsda.jsc.nasa.gov-17">[17]</a></sup> Upon return, Multik died while under anesthesia for US biopsy sampling on January 8. Lapik nearly  died while undergoing the identical procedure. No follow-up research has been conducted to determine whether these two incidents, together with the 1959 loss of the US monkey Able in post-flight surgery, contraindicate the administration of anesthesia during or shortly after spaceflights. Further US support of the Bion program was cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lsda.jsc.nasa.gov_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-lsda.jsc.nasa.gov-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monkeys_and_apes_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On December 23, 1969, as part of the \'Operaci&#243;n Navidad\' (Operation Christmas), <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> launched Juan (a <a href="/wiki/Tufted_capuchin" title="Tufted capuchin">tufted capuchin</a>, native to Argentina\'s <a href="/wiki/Misiones_Province" title="Misiones Province">Misiones Province</a>) using a two-stage <a href="/wiki/Rigel_(rocket)" title="Rigel (rocket)">Rigel 04</a> rocket. It ascended perhaps up to 82 kilometers and then was recovered successfully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other sources give 30, 60 or 72 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> All of these are below the international definition of space (100&#160;km). Later, on the February 1, 1970 the experience was repeated with a female monkey of the same species using a X-1 Panther <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>. Although it reached a higher altitude than its predecessor, it was lost after the capsule\'s parachute failed.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monkeys_and_apes_in_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People\'s Republic of China">PRC</a> spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_2" title="Shenzhou 2">Shenzhou 2</a> launched on January 9, 2001. Inside the reentry module (precise information is lacking due to the secrecy surrounding China\'s space program) a monkey, dog, and rabbit rode aloft in a test of the spacecraft\'s life support systems. The SZ2 reentry module landed in <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> on January 16. No images of the recovered capsule appeared in the press, leading to the widespread inference that the flight ended in failure. <a href="/wiki/Agence_France-Presse" title="Agence France-Presse">AFP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sky_News" title="Sky News">Sky News</a> reported that <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> had sent a
