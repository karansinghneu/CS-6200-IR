In models for the gravitational formation of structure with <a href="/wiki/Cold_dark_matter" title="Cold dark matter">cold dark matter</a>, the smallest structures collapse first and eventually build the largest structures, clusters of galaxies. Clusters are then formed relatively recently between 10 billion years ago and now. Groups and clusters may contain ten to thousands of individual galaxies. The clusters themselves are often associated with larger, non-gravitationally bound, groups called <a href="/wiki/Supercluster" title="Supercluster">superclusters</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_group" title="Galaxy group">Galaxy group</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1306" data-file-width="2172" decoding="async" height="132" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg/330px-Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg/440px-Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_positions_of_thousands_of_galaxies_in_the_VIPERS_survey.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the positions of thousands of galaxies in the VIPERS survey<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Groups of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> are the smallest aggregates of galaxies. They typically contain no more than 50 galaxies in a diameter of 1 to 2 <a href="/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs" title="Parsec">megaparsecs</a> (Mpc)(see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E22_m" title="1 E22 m">10<sup>22</sup> m</a> for distance comparisons). Their mass is approximately 10<sup>13</sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar masses</a>. The spread of velocities for the individual galaxies is about 150&#160;km/s. However, this definition should be used as a guide only, as larger and more massive galaxy systems are sometimes classified as galaxy groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Groups are the most common structures of galaxies in the universe, comprising at least 50% of the galaxies in the local universe. Groups have a mass range between those of the very large <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a> and clusters of galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013A&amp;A...552A..80M_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2013A&amp;A...552A..80M-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Our own Galaxy, the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, is contained in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a> of more than 40 galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2017 S. Paul, R. S. John et al. define clear  distinguishing parameters for classifying &#8216;galaxy groups&#8217; and &#8216;clusters&#8217; on the basis of scaling laws that they followed <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>. According to this paper, those large scale structures in the universe with mass less than 8 &#215; 10<sup>13</sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a> is classified as Galaxy group.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clusters_of_galaxies">Clusters of galaxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_groups_and_clusters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clusters of galaxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">Galaxy cluster</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:COMBO-17.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/COMBO-17.jpg/220px-COMBO-17.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/COMBO-17.jpg/330px-COMBO-17.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/COMBO-17.jpg/440px-COMBO-17.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:COMBO-17.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rich scattering of galaxies was captured by the <a href="/wiki/MPG/ESO_telescope" title="MPG/ESO telescope">MPG/ESO telescope</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Clusters are larger than groups, although there is no sharp dividing line between the two. When observed visually, clusters appear to be collections of galaxies held together by mutual gravitational attraction. However, their velocities are too large for them to remain gravitationally bound by their mutual attractions, implying the presence of either an additional invisible mass component, or an additional attractive force besides gravity. X-ray studies have revealed the presence of large amounts of intergalactic gas known as the <a href="/wiki/Intracluster_medium" title="Intracluster medium">intracluster medium</a>. This gas is very hot, between 10<sup>7</sup>K and 10<sup>8</sup>K, and hence emits X-rays in the form of <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">bremsstrahlung</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_spectral_line" title="Atomic spectral line">atomic line emission</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ACO_3341.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2086" data-file-width="2480" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/ACO_3341.jpg/180px-ACO_3341.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/ACO_3341.jpg/270px-ACO_3341.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/ACO_3341.jpg/360px-ACO_3341.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ACO_3341.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Galaxy cluster ACO 3341 seen by <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">VLT</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Visible_Multi_Object_Spectrograph" title="Visible Multi Object Spectrograph">VIMOS</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The total mass of the gas is greater than that of the galaxies by roughly a factor of two. However, this is still not enough mass to keep the galaxies in the cluster. Since this gas is in approximate <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">hydrostatic equilibrium</a> with the overall cluster gravitational field,  the total mass distribution can be determined. It turns out the total mass deduced from this measurement is approximately six times larger than the mass of the galaxies or the hot gas. The missing component is known as <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> and its nature is unknown. In a typical cluster perhaps only 5% of the total mass is in the form of galaxies, maybe 10% in the form of hot X-ray emitting gas and the remainder is dark matter. Brownstein and Moffat<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> use a theory of modified gravity to explain X-ray cluster masses without dark matter. Observations of the <a href="/wiki/Bullet_Cluster" title="Bullet Cluster">Bullet Cluster</a> are the strongest evidence for the existence of dark matter;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> however, Brownstein and Moffat<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> have shown that their modified gravity theory can also account for the properties of the cluster.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_methods">Observational methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_groups_and_clusters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Observational methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3316" data-file-width="3316" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg/220px-Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg/330px-Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg/440px-Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galaxy_Cluster_LCDCS-0829.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Galaxy Cluster LCDCS-0829 acting like a giant magnifying glass. This strange effect is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_lensing" title="Gravitational lensing">gravitational lensing</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Clusters of galaxies have been found in <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_catalog" title="Astronomical catalog">surveys</a> by a number of observational techniques and have been studied in detail using many methods:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Visible-light_astronomy" title="Visible-light astronomy">Optical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">infrared</a>:  The individual galaxies of clusters can be studied through optical or infrared imaging and spectroscopy. Galaxy clusters are found by optical or infrared telescopes by searching for overdensities, and then confirmed by finding several galaxies at a similar <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>. Infrared searches are more useful for finding more distant (higher <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>) clusters.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">X-ray</a>:  The hot plasma emits X-rays that can be detected by <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a>. The cluster gas can be studied using both X-ray imaging and X-ray spectroscopy. Clusters are quite prominent in X-ray surveys and along with <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">AGN</a> are the brightest X-ray emitting extragalactic objects.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">Radio</a>:  A number of <a href="/wiki/Radio_halo" title="Radio halo">diffuse structures</a> emitting at radio frequencies have been found in clusters. Groups of radio sources (that may include <a href="/wiki/Radio_halo" title="Radio halo">diffuse structures</a> or AGN) have been used as tracers of cluster location. At high <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> imaging around individual radio sources (in this case AGN) has been used to detect proto-clusters (clusters in the process of forming).</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunyaev-Zel%27dovich_effect" title="Sunyaev-Zel\'dovich effect">Sunyaev-Zel\'dovich effect</a>:  The hot electrons in the intracluster medium scatter radiation from the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> through inverse <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">Compton scattering</a>. This produces a "shadow" in the observed <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> at some radio frequencies.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">Gravitational lensing</a>:  Clusters of galaxies contain enough matter to distort the observed orientations of galaxies behind them. The observed distortions can be used to model the distribution of dark matter in the cluster.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_and_density">Temperature and density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_groups_and_clusters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature and density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1100" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif/lossy-page1-220px-Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif/lossy-page1-330px-Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif/lossy-page1-440px-Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Most_remote_mature_cluster.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Most Distant Mature Galaxy Cluster<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> taken with ESO\'s <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a> in Chile and with NAOJ\'s <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Telescope" title="Subaru Telescope">Subaru Telescope</a> in Hawaii</div></div></div>\n<p>Clusters of galaxies are the most recent and most massive objects to have arisen in the hierarchical structure formation of the Universe and the study of clusters tells one about the way galaxies form and evolve. Clusters have two important properties: their masses are large enough to retain any energetic gas ejected from member galaxies and the thermal energy of the gas within the cluster is observable within the X-Ray bandpass. The observed state of gas within a cluster is determined by a combination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_heating" title="Shock heating">shock heating</a> during accretion, radiative cooling, and thermal feedback triggered by that cooling. The <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and substructure of the intracluster X-Ray gas therefore represents the entire thermal history of cluster formation. To better understand this thermal history one needs to study the entropy of the gas because entropy is the quantity most directly changed by increasing or decreasing the thermal energy of intracluster gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_groups_and_clusters">List of groups and clusters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_groups_and_clusters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: List of groups and clusters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_galaxy_groups_and_clusters" title="List of galaxy groups and clusters">List of galaxy groups and clusters</a></div>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Name / Designation\n</th>\n<th>Notes\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>\n</td>\n<td>The group where the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is located\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Virgo_Cluster" title="Virgo Cluster">Virgo Cluster</a>\n</td>\n<td>This cluster of galaxies is the nearest one to us\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_groups_and_clusters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 24em;
