href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term arises from the Latin <i>superanus</i>, meaning "chief" (which finds it root in the Latin <i>super</i> (meaning "over")) and the Latin <i>regnum</i>, meaning "kingship".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_approaches">Different approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Different approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The concepts of sovereignty have been discussed throughout history, and are still actively debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> Its definition, concept, and application has changed throughout, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</a>. The current notion of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state sovereignty</a> contains four aspects consisting of territory, population, authority and recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Stephen_D._Krasner" title="Stephen D. Krasner">Stephen D. Krasner</a>, the term could also be understood in four different ways: \n</p>\n<ul><li>domestic sovereignty &#8211; actual control over a state exercised by an authority organized within this state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li>interdependence sovereignty &#8211; actual control of movement across state\'s borders, assuming the borders exist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li>international legal sovereignty &#8211; formal recognition by other sovereign states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Westphalian_sovereignty" title="Westphalian sovereignty">Westphalian sovereignty</a> &#8211; lack of other authority over state other than the domestic authority (examples of such other authorities could be a non-domestic church, a non-domestic political organization, or any other external agent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Often, these four aspects all appear together, but this is not necessarily the case &#8211; they are not affected by one another, and there are historical examples of states that were non-sovereign in one aspect while at the same time being sovereign in another of these aspects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krasner_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Krasner-7">[7]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Wallerstein" title="Immanuel Wallerstein">Immanuel Wallerstein</a>, another fundamental feature of sovereignty is that it is a claim that must be recognised by others if it is to have any meaning: \n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8220;\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Sovereignty is more than anything else a matter of legitimacy [...that] requires reciprocal recognition. Sovereignty is a hypothetical trade, in which two potentially conflicting sides, respecting de facto realities of power, exchange such recognitions as their least costly strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: \'Times New Roman\', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">&#8221;\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical">Classical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> jurist <a href="/wiki/Ulpian" title="Ulpian">Ulpian</a> observed that:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>The people transferred all their <i><a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a></i> and power to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_emperor" title="Roman emperor">Emperor</a>. Cum lege regia, quae de imperio eius lata est, populus ei et in eum omne suum imperium et potestatem conferat (Digest I.4.1)</li>\n<li>The emperor is not bound by the laws. Princeps legibus solutus est (Digest I.3.31)</li>\n<li>A decision by the emperor has the force of a statute. Quod principi placuit legis habet vigorem. (Digest I.4.1)</li></ul>\n<p>Ulpian was expressing the idea that the Emperor exercised a rather absolute form of sovereignty, that originated in the people, although he did not use the term expressly.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Ulpian\'s statements were known in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">medieval Europe</a>, but sovereignty was an important concept in medieval times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> Medieval monarchs were <i>not</i> sovereign, at least not strongly so, because they were constrained by, and shared power with, their <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> Furthermore, both were strongly constrained by custom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sovereignty existed during the Medieval period as the <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> rights of nobility and royalty, and in the <i>de facto</i> capability of individuals to make their own choices in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Around c. 1380&#8211;1400, the issue of feminine sovereignty was addressed in Geoffrey Chaucer\'s <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> collection of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales" title="Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i>, specifically in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wife_of_Bath%27s_Tale" title="The Wife of Bath\'s Tale">The Wife of Bath\'s Tale</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A later English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthurian" title="Arthurian">Arthurian</a> romance, <i>The Wedding of Sir Gawain and Dame Ragnell</i> (c. 1450),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir-Gawaine_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sir-Gawaine-12">[12]</a></sup> uses many of the same elements of the Wife of Bath\'s tale, yet changes the setting to the court of King Arthur and the Knights of the Round Table. The story revolves around the knight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Gawain" title="Sir Gawain">Sir Gawain</a> granting to Dame Ragnell, his new bride, what is purported to be wanted most by women: sovereignty.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We desire most from men,<br/>\n</p><p>From men both lund and poor,<br/>\nTo have sovereignty without lies.<br/>\nFor where we have sovereignty, all is ours,<br/>\nThough a knight be ever so fierce,<br/>\nAnd ever win mastery.<br/>\nIt is our desire to have master<br/>\nOver such a sir.<br/>\n</p><p>\nSuch is our purpose.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>The Wedding of Sir Gawain and Dame Ragnell (c. 1450), <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sir-Gawaine_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sir-Gawaine-12">[12]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reformation">Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Sovereignty reemerged as a concept in the late 16th century, a time when civil wars had created a craving for stronger central authority, when monarchs had begun to gather power onto their own hands at the expense of the nobility, and the modern <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a> was emerging. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Bodin" title="Jean Bodin">Jean Bodin</a>, partly in reaction to the chaos of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_wars_of_religion" title="French wars of religion">French wars of religion</a>, presented theories of sovereignty calling for strong central authority in the form of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>. In his 1576 treatise <i><a href="/wiki/Jean_Bodin#Les_Six_livres_de_la_R.C3.A9publique" title="Jean Bodin">Les Six Livres de la R&#233;publique</a></i> ("Six Books of the Republic") Bodin argued that it is inherent in the nature of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_(politics)" title="State (politics)">state</a> that sovereignty must be:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Absolute: On this point he said that the sovereign must be hedged in with obligations and conditions, must be able to legislate without his (or its) subjects\' consent, must not be bound by the laws of his predecessors, and could not, because it is illogical, be bound by his own laws.</li>\n<li>Perpetual: Not temporarily delegated as to a strong leader in an emergency or to a state employee such as a <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrate</a>. He held that sovereignty must be perpetual because anyone with the power to enforce a time limit on the governing power must be above the governing power, which would be impossible if the governing power is absolute.</li></ul>\n<p>Bodin rejected the notion of transference of sovereignty from people to the ruler (also known as <i>the sovereign</i>); natural law and divine law confer upon the sovereign the right to rule. And the sovereign is not above divine law or natural law. He is above (<i>ie.</i> not bound by) only <a href="/wiki/Positive_law" title="Positive law">positive law</a>, that is, laws made by humans. He emphasized that a sovereign is bound to observe certain basic rules derived from the divine law, the law of nature or reason, and the law that is common to all nations (jus gentium), as well as the fundamental laws of the state that determine who is the sovereign, who succeeds to sovereignty, and what limits the sovereign power. Thus, Bodin&#8217;s sovereign was restricted by the constitutional law of the state and by the higher law that was considered as binding upon every human being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> The fact that the sovereign must obey divine and natural law imposes ethical constraints on him. Bodin also held that the <i>lois royales</i>, the fundamental laws of the French monarchy which regulated matters such as succession, are natural laws and are binding on the French sovereign.\n</p><p>Despite his commitment to absolutism, Bodin held some moderate opinions on how government should in practice be carried out. He held that although the sovereign is not obliged to, it is advisable for him, as a practical expedient, to convene a <a href="/wiki/Senate" title="Senate">senate</a> from whom he can obtain advice, to delegate some power to magistrates for the practical administration of the law, and to use the <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">Estates</a> as a means of communicating with the people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bodin believed that &#8220;the most divine, most excellent, and the state form most proper to royalty is governed partly aristocratically and partly democratically&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With his doctrine that sovereignty is conferred by divine law, Bodin predefined the scope of the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_of_Enlightenment">Age of Enlightenment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Age of Enlightenment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the Age of Enlightenment, the idea of sovereignty gained both legal and moral force as the main Western description of the meaning and power of a State. In particular, the "<a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">Social contract</a>" as a mechanism for establishing sovereignty was suggested and, by 1800, widely accepted, especially in the new <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, though also in Great Britain to a lesser extent.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes_book)" title="Leviathan (Hobbes book)">Leviathan</a></i> (1651) arrived a conception of sovereignty similar to Bodin\'s, which had just achieved legal status in the "<a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>", but for different reasons. He created the first modern version of the social contract (or contractarian) theory, arguing that to overcome the "nasty, brutish and short" quality of life without the cooperation of other human beings, people must join in a "commonwealth" and submit to a "Soveraigne [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Power" that is able to compel them to act in the common good. This expediency argument attracted many of the early proponents of sovereignty. Hobbes strengthened the definition of sovereignty beyond either Westphalian or Bodin\'s, by saying that it must be:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Absolute: because conditions could only be imposed on a sovereign if there were some outside arbitrator to determine when he had violated them, in which case the sovereign would not be the final authority.</li>\n<li>Indivisible: The sovereign is the only final authority in his territory; he does not share final authority with any other entity. Hobbes held this to be true because otherwise there would be no way of resolving a disagreement between the multiple authorities.</li></ul>\n<p>Hobbes\' hypothesis&#8212;that the ruler\'s sovereignty is contracted to him by the people in return for his maintaining their physical safety&#8212;led him to conclude that if and when the ruler fails, the people recover their ability to protect themselves by forming a new contract.\n</p><p>Hobbes\'s theories decisively shape the concept of sovereignty through the medium of <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> theories. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a>\'s (1712&#8211;1778) definition of <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a> (with early antecedents in <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Su%C3%A1rez" title="Francisco Su&#225;rez">Francisco Su&#225;rez</a>\'s theory of the origin of power), provides that the people are the legitimate sovereign. Rousseau considered sovereignty to be inalienable; he condemned the distinction between the origin and the exercise of sovereignty, a distinction upon which <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">representative democracy</a> is founded. <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montesquieu" title="Montesquieu">Montesquieu</a> are also key figures in the unfolding of the concept of sovereignty; their views differ with Rousseau and with Hobbes on this issue of alienability.\n</p><p>The second book of Jean-Jacques Rousseau\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Du_Contrat_Social,_ou_Principes_du_droit_politique" title="Du Contrat Social, ou Principes du droit politique">Du Contrat Social, ou Principes du droit politique</a></i> (1762) deals with sovereignty and its rights. Sovereignty, or the <a href="/wiki/General_will" title="General will">general will</a>, is inalienable, for the will cannot be transmitted; it is indivisible, since it is essentially general; it is infallible and always right, determined and limited in its power by the common interest; it acts through laws. Law is the decision of the general will in regard to some object of common interest, but though the general will is always right and desires only good, its judgment is not always enlightened, and consequently does not always see wherein the common good lies; hence the necessity of the legislator. But the legislator has, of himself, no authority; he is only a guide who drafts and proposes laws, but the people alone (that is, the sovereign or general will) has authority to make and impose them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Rousseau, in the Social Contract<i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></i>\nargued, "the growth of the State giving the trustees of public authority more and means to abuse their power, the more the Government has to have force to contain the people, the more force the Sovereign should have in turn in order to contain the Government," with the understanding that the Sovereign is "a collective being of wonder" (Book II, Chapter I) resulting from "the general will" of the people, and that "what any man, whoever he may be, orders on his own, is not a law" (Book II, Chapter VI) &#8211; and furthermore predicated on the assumption that the people have an unbiased means by which to ascertain the general will.  Thus the legal maxim, "there is no law without a sovereign."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-max-seydel_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-max-seydel-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_types">Definition and types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Definition and types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" &#8220; ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" &#8221; ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">There exists perhaps no conception the meaning of which is more controversial than that of sovereignty. It is an indisputable fact that this conception, from the moment when it was introduced into political science until the present day, has never had a meaning which was universally agreed upon.</div>\n<p><cite class="left-aligned" style=""><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lassa_Oppenheim" title="Lassa Oppenheim">Lassa Oppenheim</a> (30-03-1858 &#8211; 07-10-1919), an authority on international law<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absoluteness">Absoluteness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Absoluteness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An important factor of sovereignty is its degree of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absoluteness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-link.springer.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-link.springer.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">[5]</a></sup> A sovereign power has absolute sovereignty when it is not restricted by a constitution, by the laws of its predecessors, or by <a href="/wiki/Custom_(law)" title="Custom (law)">custom</a>, and no areas of law or policy are reserved as being outside its control. <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">International law</a>; policies and actions of neighboring states; cooperation and respect of the populace; means of enforcement; and resources to enact policy are factors that might limit sovereignty. For example, parents are not guaranteed the right to decide some matters in the upbringing of their children independent of societal regulation, and municipalities do not have unlimited jurisdiction in local matters, thus neither parents nor municipalities have absolute sovereignty. Theorists have diverged over the desirability of increased absoluteness.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusivity">Exclusivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Exclusivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A key element of sovereignty in a legalistic sense is that of exclusivity of <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. Specifically, the degree to which decisions made by a sovereign entity might be contradicted by another authority.  Along these lines, the German sociologist <a href="/wiki/Max_Weber" title="Max Weber">Max Weber</a> proposed that sovereignty is a community\'s monopoly on the legitimate use of force; and thus any group claiming the same right must either be brought under the yoke of the sovereign, proven illegitimate, or otherwise contested and defeated for sovereignty to be genuine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> International law, competing branches of government, and authorities reserved for subordinate entities (such as <a href="/wiki/Federated_state" title="Federated state">federated states</a> or republics) represent legal infringements on exclusivity. Social institutions such as religious bodies, corporations, and competing political parties might represent <i>de facto</i> infringements on exclusivity.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="De_jure_and_de_facto"><i>De jure</i> and <i>de facto</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: De jure and de facto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">De jure</a></i>, or legal, sovereignty concerns the expressed and institutionally recognised right to exercise control over a territory. <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">De facto</a></i>, or actual, sovereignty is concerned with whether control in fact exists. Cooperation and respect of the populace; control of resources in, or moved into, an area; means of enforcement and security; and ability to carry out various functions of state all represent measures of <i>de facto</i> sovereignty. When control is practiced predominantly by military or police force it is considered <i>coercive sovereignty</i>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereignty_and_independence">Sovereignty and independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sovereignty and independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<table class="box-Refimprove_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. sovereignty is sometimes viewed synonymously with <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a>, however, sovereignty can be transferred as a legal right whereas independence cannot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talmon_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-talmon-19">[19]</a></sup> A state can achieve <i>de facto</i> independence long after acquiring sovereignty, such as in the case of Cambodia, Laos and Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talmon_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-talmon-19">[19]</a></sup> Additionally, independence can also be suspended when an entire region becomes subject to an occupation such as when <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> had been overrun by the forces to take part in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iraq_War_of_2003" title="Iraq War of 2003">Iraq War of 2003</a>, Iraq had not been annexed by any country, so its sovereignty during this period was not contested by any state including those present on the territory. Alternatively, independence can be lost completely when sovereignty itself becomes the subject of dispute. The pre-World War II administrations of <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> maintained an exile existence (and considerable international recognition) whilst their territories were annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and governed locally by their pro-Soviet functionaries. When in 1991 Latvia, Lithuania and Estonia re-enacted independence, it was done so on the basis of continuity directly from the pre-Soviet republics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talmon_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-talmon-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Another complicated sovereignty scenario can arise when regime itself is the subject of dispute. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_(1945%E2%80%9389)" title="History of Poland (1945&#8211;89)">People\'s Republic of Poland</a> which governed Poland from 1945 to 1989 is now seen to have been an illegal entity by the modern Polish administration. The post-1989 Polish state claims direct continuity from the <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Second Polish Republic</a> which ended in 1939. For other reasons however, Poland maintains its communist-era outline as opposed to its pre-World War II shape which included areas now in <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> but did not include some of its western regions that were then in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.\n</p><p>At the opposite end of the scale, there is no dispute regarding the self-governance of certain self-proclaimed states such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Abkhazia" title="Republic of Abkhazia">Republic of Abkhazia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_South_Ossetia" title="Republic of South Ossetia">Republic of South Ossetia</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Kosovo" title="Republic of Kosovo">Republic of Kosovo</a> (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_with_limited_recognition" title="List of states with limited recognition">List of states with limited recognition</a>) since their governments neither answer to a bigger state, nor is their governance subjected to supervision. The sovereignty (i.e. legal right to govern) however, is disputed in all three cases as the first two entities are claimed by <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a> and the third by <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal">Internal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Internal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Internal sovereignty is the relationship between a sovereign power and the political community. A central concern is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political_science)" title="Legitimacy (political science)">legitimacy</a>: by what right does a government exercise authority? Claims of legitimacy might refer to the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a> or to a <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>With Sovereignty meaning holding supreme, independent authority over a region or state, Internal Sovereignty refers to the internal affairs of the state and the location of supreme power within it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  A state that has internal sovereignty is one with a government that has been elected by the people and has the popular legitimacy.  Internal sovereignty examines the internal affairs of a state and how it operates.  It is important to have strong internal sovereignty in relation to keeping order and peace.  When you have weak internal sovereignty, organisations such as rebel groups will undermine the authority and disrupt the peace.  The presence of a strong authority allows you to keep agreement and enforce sanctions for the violation of laws.  The ability for leadership to prevent these violations is a key variable in determining internal sovereignty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  The lack of internal sovereignty can cause war in one of two ways: first, undermining the value of agreement by allowing costly violations; and second, requiring such large subsidies for implementation that they render war cheaper than peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Leadership needs to be able to promise members, especially those like armies, police forces, or paramilitaries will abide by agreements.  The presence of strong internal sovereignty allows a state to deter opposition groups in exchange for bargaining.  It has been said that a more decentralized authority would be more efficient in keeping peace because the deal must please not only the leadership but also the opposition group.  While the operations and affairs within a state are relative to the level of sovereignty within that state, there is still an argument between who should hold the authority in a sovereign state.\n</p><p>This argument between who should hold the authority within a sovereign state is called the traditional doctrine of public sovereignty.  This discussion is between an internal sovereign or an authority of public sovereignty.  An internal sovereign is a political body that possesses ultimate, final and independent authority; one whose decisions are binding upon all citizens, groups and institutions in society. Early thinkers believe sovereignty should be vested in the hands of a single person, a monarch.  They believed the overriding merit of vesting sovereignty in a single individual was that sovereignty would therefore be indivisible; it would be expressed in a single voice that could claim final authority.  An example of an internal sovereign or monarch is Louis XIV of France during the seventeenth century;  Louis XIV claimed that he was the state.  Jean-Jacques Rousseau rejected monarchical rule in favor of the other type of authority within a sovereign state, public sovereignty.  Public Sovereignty is the belief that ultimate authority is vested in the people themselves, expressed in the idea of the general will.  This means that the power is elected and supported by its members, the authority has a central goal of the good of the people in mind.  The idea of public sovereignty has often been the basis for modern democratic theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_internal_sovereignty">Modern internal sovereignty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern internal sovereignty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Within the modern governmental system, internal sovereignty is usually found in states that have public sovereignty and rarely found within a state controlled by an internal sovereign.  A form of government that is a little different from both is the UK parliament system.  From 1790 to 1859 it was argued that sovereignty in the UK was vested neither in the Crown nor in the people but in the "Monarch in Parliament".  This is the origin of the doctrine of parliamentary sovereignty and is usually seen as the fundamental principle of the British constitution.  With these principles of parliamentary sovereignty majority control can gain access to unlimited constitutional authority, creating what has been called "elective dictatorship" or "modern autocracy".  Public sovereignty in modern governments is a lot more common with examples like the USA, Canada, Australia and India where government is divided into different levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External">External</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: External">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state#Recognition" title="Sovereign state">Sovereign state &#167;&#160;Recognition</a></div>\n<p>External sovereignty concerns the relationship between a sovereign power and other states. For example, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> uses the following criterion when deciding under what conditions other states recognise a political entity as having sovereignty over some territory;\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%; ;"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"Sovereignty." A government which exercises de facto administrative control over a country and is not subordinate to any other government in that country or a foreign sovereign state.</div>\n<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">(<i>The Arantzazu Mendi</i>, [1939] A.C. 256), Stroud\'s Judicial Dictionary</cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>External sovereignty is connected with questions of international law &#8211; such as: when, if ever, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_intervention" title="Foreign intervention">intervention</a> by one country into another\'s territory permissible?\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years\' War">Thirty Years\' War</a>, a European religious conflict that embroiled much of the continent, the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> in 1648 established the notion of territorial sovereignty as a norm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-intervention" title="Non-intervention">noninterference in the affairs of other nations</a>, so-called <a href="/wiki/Westphalian_sovereignty" title="Westphalian sovereignty">Westphalian sovereignty</a>, even though the actual treaty itself reaffirmed the multiple levels of sovereignty of the Holy Roman Empire. This resulted as a natural extension of the older principle of <i><a href="/wiki/Cuius_regio,_eius_religio" title="Cuius regio, eius religio">cuius regio, eius religio</a></i> (Whose realm, his religion), leaving the Roman Catholic Church with little ability to interfere with the internal affairs of many European states.  It is a myth, however, that the Treaties of Westphalia created a new European order of equal sovereign states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>, sovereignty means that a government possesses full control over affairs within a territorial or geographical area or limit. Determining whether a specific entity is sovereign is not an exact science, but often a matter of diplomatic dispute. There is usually an expectation that both <i>de jure</i> and <i>de facto</i> sovereignty rest in the same organisation at the place and time of concern. Foreign governments use varied criteria and political considerations when deciding whether or not to recognise the sovereignty of a state over a territory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Membership in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> requires that "[t]he admission of any such state to membership in the United Nations will be effected by a decision of the General Assembly upon the recommendation of the Security Council."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sovereignty may be recognized even when the sovereign body possesses no territory or its territory is under partial or total occupation by another power. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> was in this position between the annexation in 1870 of the <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papal States</a> by Italy and the signing of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateran_Treaties" title="Lateran Treaties">Lateran Treaties</a> in 1929, a 59-year period during which it was recognised as sovereign by many (mostly Roman Catholic) states despite possessing no territory &#8211; a situation resolved when the Lateran Treaties granted the Holy See sovereignty over the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>. Another case, <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a></i>, <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">though often contested,</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta" title="Sovereign Military Order of Malta">Sovereign Military Order of Malta</a>, the third sovereign entity inside Italian territory (after <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">San Marino</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_City_State" title="Vatican City State">Vatican City State</a>) and the second inside the Italian capital (since in 1869 the Palazzo di Malta and the Villa Malta receive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterritorial" title="Extraterritorial">extraterritorial</a> rights, in this way becoming the only "sovereign" territorial possessions of the modern Order), which is the last existing heir to one of several once militarily significant, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crusader_state" title="Crusader state">crusader states</a> of sovereign <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_order_(society)" title="Military order (society)">military orders</a>. In 1607 its Grand masters were also made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reichsf%C3%BCrst" title="Reichsf&#252;rst">Reichsf&#252;rst</a> (princes of the Holy Roman Empire) by the Holy Roman Emperor, granting them seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">Reichstag</a>, at the time the closest permanent equivalent to a UN-type general assembly; confirmed 1620). These sovereign rights were never deposed, only the territories were lost. 100 modern states still maintain full diplomatic relations with the order<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (now <i>de facto</i> "the most prestigious service club"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), and the UN awarded it observer status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_ARES48265_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN_ARES48265-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government-in-exile" title="Government-in-exile">governments-in-exile</a> of many European states (for instance, Norway, Netherlands or <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> were regarded as sovereign despite their territories being under foreign occupation; their governance resumed as soon as the occupation had ended. The government of <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> was in a similar situation <i>vis-&#224;-vis</i> the <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraqi</a> occupation of its country during 1990&#8211;1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nolan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nolan-30">[30]</a></sup>  The government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> was recognized as sovereign over <a href="/wiki/Greater_China" title="Greater China">China</a> from 1911 to 1971 despite that its <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> territory became occupied by Communist Chinese forces since 1949. In 1971 it lost <a href="/wiki/China_and_the_United_Nations" title="China and the United Nations">UN recognition</a> to <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Chinese Communist</a>-led People\'s Republic of China and its <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Taiwan" title="Political status of Taiwan">sovereign and political status as a state became disputed</a> and it lost its ability to use "China" as its name and therefore became commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_of_the_Red_Cross#Legal_status" title="International Committee of the Red Cross">International Committee of the Red Cross</a> is commonly mistaken to be sovereign. It has been granted various degrees of special privileges and legal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunity_from_prosecution" title="Immunity from prosecution">immunities</a> in many countries,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> that in cases like Switzerland are considerable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Committee is a private organisation governed by Swiss law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_and_pooled">Shared and pooled</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Shared and pooled">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Just as the office of <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> can be vested jointly in several persons within a state, the sovereign jurisdiction over a single political territory can be shared jointly by two or more consenting powers, notably in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Condominium_(international_law)" title="Condominium (international law)">condominium</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Likewise the member states of international organizations may voluntarily bind themselves by treaty to a supranational organization, such as a <a href="/wiki/Continental_union" title="Continental union">continental union</a>. In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union#Sovereignty" title="Member state of the European Union">European Union members states this is called "pooled sovereignty"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another example of shared and pooled sovereignty is the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union 1707</a> which created the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a> now known as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It was a full economic union, meaning the Scottish and English systems of currency, taxation and laws regulating trade were aligned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchison2002p314_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchison2002p314-38">[38]</a></sup> Nonetheless, Scotland and England never fully surrendered or pooled all of their governance sovereignty; they retained many of their previous national institutional features and characteristics, particularly relating to their legal, religious and educational systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>, created in 1998 through <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution in the United Kingdom</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014#Legality_of_a_referendum" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">negotiated terms</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a> which resulted in the people of Scotland deciding to continue the pooling of its sovereignty with the rest of the United Kingdom.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nation-states">Nation-states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nation-states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A community of people who claim the right of <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a> based on a common ethnicity, history and culture might seek to establish sovereignty over a region, thus creating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nation-state" title="Nation-state">nation-state</a>. Such nations are sometimes recognised as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_area" title="Autonomous area">autonomous areas</a> rather than as fully sovereign, independent states.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federations">Federations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Federations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Federal_republic" title="Federal republic">federal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_of_government" title="System of government">system of government</a>, <i>sovereignty</i> also refers to powers which a constituent state or republic possesses independently of the national government. In a <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederation</a> constituent entities retain the right to withdraw from the national body, but in a federation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. A fundamental polarity is between theories that assert that sovereignty is vested directly in the sovereigns by divine or natural right and theories that assert it originates from the people. In the latter case there is a further division into those that assert that the people transfer their sovereignty to the sovereign (Hobbes), and those that assert that the people retain their sovereignty (Rousseau).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>During the brief period of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchies</a> in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a> was an important competing justification for the exercise of sovereignty. The <a href="/wiki/Mandate_of_Heaven" title="Mandate of Heaven">Mandate of Heaven</a> had some similar implications in China.\n</p><p>A republic is a form of government in which the people, or some significant portion of them, retain sovereignty over the government and where offices of state are not granted through heritage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A common modern definition of a republic is a government having a <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> who is not a monarch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WordNet_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-WordNet-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M-W_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-M-W-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Democracy is based on the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a></i>. In a <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a> the public plays an active role in shaping and deciding policy. <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">Representative democracy</a> permits a transfer of the exercise of sovereignty from the people to a <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative body</a> or an executive (or to some combination of legislature, executive and <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">Judiciary</a>). Many representative democracies provide limited direct democracy through <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Initiative" title="Initiative">initiative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recall</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">Parliamentary sovereignty</a> refers to a representative democracy where the parliament is ultimately sovereign and not the executive power nor the judiciary.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">Classical liberals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill#Liberty" title="John Stuart Mill">Stuart Mill</a> consider every individual as sovereign.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_Realism" title="Political Realism">Realists</a> view sovereignty as being untouchable and as guaranteed to legitimate nation-states.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rationalism_(politics)" title="Rationalism (politics)">Rationalists</a> see sovereignty similarly to realists. However, rationalism states that the sovereignty of a nation-state may be violated in extreme circumstances, such as human rights abuses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)" title="Internationalism (politics)">Internationalists</a> believe that sovereignty is outdated and an unnecessary obstacle to achieving peace, in line with their belief of a \'global community\'. In the light of the abuse of power by sovereign states such as Hitler\'s Germany or Stalin\'s Soviet Union, they argue that human beings are not necessarily protected by the state whose citizens they are, and that the respect for state sovereignty on which the UN Charter is founded is an obstacle to humanitarian intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Anarchists and some <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarians</a> deny the sovereignty of states and governments. Anarchists often argue for a specific individual kind of sovereignty, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarch_(sovereign_individual)" title="Anarch (sovereign individual)">Anarch as a sovereign individual</a>. <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dal&#237;">Salvador Dal&#237;</a>, for instance, talked of "anarcho-monarchist" (as usual for him, tongue in cheek); <a href="/wiki/Antonin_Artaud" title="Antonin Artaud">Antonin Artaud</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Elagabalus" title="Elagabalus">Heliogabalus</a>: Or, The Crowned Anarchist</i>; <a href="/wiki/Max_Stirner" title="Max Stirner">Max Stirner</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ego_and_Its_Own" title="The Ego and Its Own">The Ego and Its Own</a></i>; <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bataille" title="Georges Bataille">Georges Bataille</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a> of a kind of "antisovereignty". Therefore, anarchists join a classical conception of the individual as sovereign of himself, which forms the basis of <a href="/wiki/Political_consciousness" title="Political consciousness">political consciousness</a>. The unified consciousness is sovereignty over one\'s own body, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nietzsche" title="Nietzsche">Nietzsche</a> demonstrated (see also <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Klossowski" title="Pierre Klossowski">Pierre Klossowski</a>\'s book on <i>Nietzsche and the Vicious Circle</i>). <i>See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovereignty_of_the_individual" title="Sovereignty of the individual">sovereignty of the individual</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-ownership" title="Self-ownership">self-ownership</a></i>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">Imperialists</a> hold a view of sovereignty where power rightfully exists with those states that hold the greatest ability to impose the will of said state, by force or threat of force, over the populace of other states with weaker military or political will. They effectively deny the sovereignty of the individual in deference to either the \'good\' of the whole, or to <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>\n<p>According to Matteo Laruffa "sovereignty resides in every public action and policy as the exercise of executive powers by institutions open to the participation of citizens to the decision-making processes"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_rule_of_law">Relation to rule of law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sovereignty&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Relation to rule of law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Another topic is whether <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">the law is held to be sovereign</a>, that is, whether it is above political or other interference. Sovereign law constitutes a true state of law, meaning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letter_of_the_law" title="Letter of the law">letter of the law</a> (if constitutionally correct) is applicable and enforceable, even when against the political will of the nation, as long as not formally changed following the constitutional procedure. Strictly speaking, any deviation from this principle constitutes a revolution or a coup d\'&#233;tat, regardless of the intentions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
