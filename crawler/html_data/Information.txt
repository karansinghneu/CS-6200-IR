it is that which answers the question of "what an <a href="/wiki/Entity" title="Entity">entity</a> is" and is thus that which specifies the nature of that entity, as well as the essentiality of its properties. Information is associated with <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>, as data is meaningful information and represents the values attributed to parameters, and knowledge signifies understanding of an abstract or concrete concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The existence of information is uncoupled with an observer, which refers to that which accesses information to discern that which it specifies; information exists beyond an <a href="/wiki/Event_horizon" title="Event horizon">event horizon</a> for example, and in the case of knowledge, the information itself requires a cognitive observer to be accessed. \n</p><p>In terms of communication, information is expressed either as the content of a <a href="/wiki/Message" title="Message">message</a> or through direct or indirect <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a>. That which is <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceived</a> can be construed as a message in its own right, and in that sense, information is always conveyed as the content of a message.\n</p><p>Information can be <a href="/wiki/Code" title="Code">encoded</a> into various forms for <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(telecommunications)" title="Transmission (telecommunications)">transmission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Language_interpretation" title="Language interpretation">interpretation</a> (for example, information may be encoded into a <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sign_(semiotics)" title="Sign (semiotics)">signs</a>, or transmitted via a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_(electronics)" title="Signal (electronics)">signal</a>). It can also be <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encrypted</a> for safe storage and communication.\n</p><p>Information reduces <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty" title="Uncertainty">uncertainty</a>. The uncertainty of an event is measured by its probability of occurrence and is inversely proportional to that. The more uncertain an event, the more information is required to resolve uncertainty of that event. The <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> is a typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_information" title="Unit of information">unit of information</a>, but other units such as the <a href="/wiki/Nat_(unit)" title="Nat (unit)">nat</a> may be used. For example, the information encoded in one "fair" coin flip is log<sub>2</sub>(2/1) = 1 bit, and in two fair coin flips is\nlog<sub>2</sub>(4/1) = 2 bits.\n</p><p>The concept of <i>information</i> has different meanings in different contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floridi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Floridi-2">[2]</a></sup> Thus the concept becomes related to notions of <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href=\'/wiki/Information_history#History_of_the_word_and_concept_"information"\' title="Information history">History of the word and concept "information"</a></div>\n<p>The English word apparently derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> stem (<i>information-</i>) of the nominative (<i>informatio</i>): this noun derives from the verb <i>informare</i> (to inform) in the sense of "to give form to the mind", "to discipline", "instruct", "teach". <i>Inform</i> itself comes (via French <i>informer</i>) from the Latin verb <i>informare</i>, which means to give form, or to form an idea of. Furthermore, Latin itself already contained the word <i>informatio</i> meaning concept or idea, but the extent to which this may have influenced the development of the word <i>information</i> in English is not clear.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word for <i>form</i> was <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%BF%CF%81%CF%86%CE%AE" title="wiktionary:&#956;&#959;&#961;&#966;&#942;">&#956;&#959;&#961;&#966;&#942;</a> (<i>morphe</i>; cf. morph) and also <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BC%B6%CE%B4%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:&#949;&#7990;&#948;&#959;&#962;">&#949;&#7990;&#948;&#959;&#962;</a> (<i>eidos</i>) "kind, idea, shape, set", the latter word was famously used in a technical philosophical sense by <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> (and later <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>) to denote the ideal identity or essence of something (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Forms" title="Theory of Forms">Theory of Forms</a>). \'Eidos\' can also be associated with thought, <a href="/wiki/Proposition" title="Proposition">proposition</a>, or even concept.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> word for <i>information</i> is <a class="extiw" href="https://el.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A0%CE%BB%CE%B7%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%86%CE%BF%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B1" title="el:&#928;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;">&#960;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;</a>, which transliterates (<i>pl&#275;rophoria</i>) from \n<a class="external text" href="http://www.wordreference.com/gren/%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AE%CF%81%CE%B7%CF%82" rel="nofollow">&#960;&#955;&#942;&#961;&#951;&#962;</a> (<i>pl&#275;r&#275;s</i>) "fully" and <a class="external text" href="http://www.wordreference.com/gren/%CF%86%CE%AD%CF%81%CF%89" rel="nofollow">&#966;&#941;&#961;&#969;</a> (<i>phorein</i>) frequentative of (<i>pherein</i>) <i>to carry through</i>. It literally means "bears fully" or "conveys fully". In modern Greek the word <a class="extiw" href="https://el.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A0%CE%BB%CE%B7%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%86%CE%BF%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B1" title="el:&#928;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;">&#928;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;</a> is still in daily use and has the same meaning as the word <i>information</i> in English. In addition to its primary meaning, the word <a class="extiw" href="https://el.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A0%CE%BB%CE%B7%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%86%CE%BF%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B1" title="el:&#928;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;">&#928;&#955;&#951;&#961;&#959;&#966;&#959;&#961;&#943;&#945;</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> has deep roots in Aristotle\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiotic_triangle" title="Semiotic triangle">semiotic triangle</a>. In this regard it can be interpreted to communicate information to the one decoding that specific type of <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">sign</a>. This is something that occurs frequently with the etymology of many words in ancient and modern Greek where there is a very strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denotative" title="Denotative">denotative</a> relationship between the signifier, e.g. the word symbol that conveys a specific encoded interpretation, and the signified, e.g. a concept whose meaning the interpreter attempts to decode.\n</p><p>In English, &#8220;information&#8221; is an uncountable <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">mass noun</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_theory_approach">Information theory approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Information theory approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">Information theory</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a>, <i>information</i> is taken as an ordered <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequence</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbols" title="Symbols">symbols</a> from an alphabet, say an input alphabet &#967;, and an output alphabet &#978;. Information processing consists of an input-output function that maps any input sequence from &#967; into an output sequence from &#978;. The mapping may be probabilistic or deterministic. It may have memory or be memoryless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wicker_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wicker-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_sensory_input">As sensory input</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As sensory input">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Often information can be viewed as a type of input to an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> or <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>. Inputs are of two kinds; some inputs are important to the function of the organism (for example, food) or system (<a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>) by themselves. In his book <i>Sensory Ecology</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Dusenbery called these causal inputs. Other inputs (information) are important only because they are associated with causal inputs and can be used to <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">predict</a> the occurrence of a causal input at a later time (and perhaps another place). Some information is important because of association with other information but eventually there must be a connection to a causal input. In practice, information is usually carried by weak stimuli that must be detected by specialized sensory systems and amplified by energy inputs before they can be functional to the organism or system. For example, light is mainly (but not only, e.g. plants can grow in the direction of the lightsource) a causal input to plants but for animals it only provides information. The colored light reflected from a flower is too weak to do much photosynthetic work but the visual system of the bee detects it and the bee\'s nervous system uses the information to guide the bee to the flower, where the bee often finds nectar or pollen, which are causal inputs, serving a nutritional function.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_representation_and_complexity">As representation and complexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As representation and complexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive scientist</a> and applied mathematician Ronaldo Vigo argues that information is a concept that requires at least two related entities to make quantitative sense. These are, any dimensionally defined category of objects S, and any of its subsets R. R, in essence, is a representation of S, or, in other words, conveys representational (and hence, conceptual) information about S. Vigo then defines the amount of information that R conveys about S as the rate of change in the <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a> of S whenever the objects in R are removed from S. Under "Vigo information", pattern, invariance, complexity, representation, and information&#8212;five fundamental constructs of universal science&#8212;are unified under a novel mathematical framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Among other things, the framework aims to overcome the limitations of <a href="/wiki/Shannon%E2%80%93Hartley_theorem" title="Shannon&#8211;Hartley theorem">Shannon-Weaver information</a> when attempting to characterize and measure subjective information.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_influence_that_leads_to_transformation">As an influence that leads to transformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: As an influence that leads to transformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Information is any type of pattern that influences the formation or transformation of other patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In this sense, there is no need for a conscious mind to perceive, much less appreciate, the pattern.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Consider, for example, <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. The sequence of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a> is a pattern that influences the formation and development of an organism without any need for a conscious mind. One might argue though that for a human to consciously define a pattern, for example a nucleotide, naturally involves conscious information processing.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">Systems theory</a> at times seems to refer to information in this sense, assuming information does not necessarily involve any conscious mind, and patterns circulating (due to <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a>) in the system can be called information. In other words, it can be said that information in this sense is something potentially perceived as representation, though not created or presented for that purpose. For example, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Bateson" title="Gregory Bateson">Gregory Bateson</a> defines "information" as a "difference that makes a difference".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>If, however, the premise of "influence" implies that information has been perceived by a conscious mind and also interpreted by it, the specific context associated with this interpretation may cause the transformation of the information into <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>. Complex definitions of both "information" and "knowledge" make such semantic and logical analysis difficult, but the condition of "transformation" is an important point in the study of information as it relates to knowledge, especially in the business discipline of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge management</a>. In this practice, tools and processes are used to assist a <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_worker" title="Knowledge worker">knowledge worker</a> in performing research and making decisions, including steps such as:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Review information to effectively derive value and meaning</li>\n<li>Reference <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> if available</li>\n<li>Establish <a href="/wiki/Relevance" title="Relevance">relevant</a> <a href="/wiki/Context_(language_use)" title="Context (language use)">context</a>, often from many possible contexts</li>\n<li>Derive new knowledge from the information</li>\n<li>Make decisions or recommendations from the resulting knowledge</li></ul>\n<p>Stewart (2001) argues that transformation of information into knowledge is critical, lying at the core of value creation and <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a> for the modern enterprise.\n</p><p>The Danish Dictionary of Information Terms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> argues that information only provides an answer to a posed question. Whether the answer provides knowledge depends on the informed person. So a generalized definition of the concept should be: "Information" = An answer to a specific question".\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Marshall_McLuhan" title="Marshall McLuhan">Marshall McLuhan</a> speaks of <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">media</a> and their effects on human cultures, he refers to the structure of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_artifact" title="Cultural artifact">artifacts</a> that in turn shape our behaviors and mindsets. Also, <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a> are often said to be "information" in this sense.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_property_in_physics">As a property in physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As a property in physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_information" title="Physical information">Physical information</a></div>\n<p>Information has a well-defined meaning in physics. In 2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._D._Bekenstein" title="J. D. Bekenstein">J. D. Bekenstein</a> claimed that a growing trend in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> was to define the physical world as being made up of information itself (and thus information is defined in this way) (see <a href="/wiki/Digital_physics" title="Digital physics">Digital physics</a>). Examples of this include the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_entanglement" title="Quantum entanglement">quantum entanglement</a>, where particles can interact without reference to their separation or the speed of light. Material information itself cannot travel faster than light even if that information is transmitted indirectly. This could lead to all attempts at physically observing a particle with an "entangled" relationship to another being slowed down, even though the particles are not connected in any other way other than by the information they carry.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_universe_hypothesis" title="Mathematical universe hypothesis">mathematical universe hypothesis</a> suggests a new paradigm, in which virtually everything, from particles and fields, through biological entities and consciousness, to the <a href="/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">multiverse</a> itself, could be described by mathematical patterns of information. By the same token, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_void" title="Cosmic void">cosmic void</a> can be conceived of as the absence of material information in space (setting aside the virtual particles that pop in and out of existence due to quantum fluctuations, as well as the gravitational field and the dark energy). Nothingness can be understood then as that within which no matter, energy, space, time, or any other type of information could exist, which would be possible if symmetry and structure break within the manifold of the multiverse (i.e. the manifold would have tears or holes).\n</p><p>Another link is demonstrated by the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_demon" title="Maxwell\'s demon">Maxwell\'s demon</a> thought experiment. In this experiment, a direct relationship between information and another physical property, <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>, is demonstrated. A consequence is that it is impossible to destroy information without increasing the entropy of a system; in practical terms this often means generating heat. Another more philosophical outcome is that information could be thought of as interchangeable with <a href="/wiki/Energy#Transformations_of_energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. Toyabe et al. experimentally showed in nature that information can be converted into work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Thus, in the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logic_gates" title="Logic gates">logic gates</a>, the theoretical lower bound of thermal energy released by an <i>AND gate</i> is higher than for the <i>NOT gate</i> (because information is destroyed in an <i>AND gate</i> and simply converted in a <i>NOT gate</i>). Physical information is of particular importance in the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computers" title="Quantum computers">quantum computers</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, information is any kind of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/event" title="wikt:event">event</a> that affects the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_state" title="Thermodynamic state">state</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamic system</a> that can interpret the information.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_application_of_information_study">The application of information study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The application of information study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The information cycle (addressed as a whole or in its distinct components) is of great concern to <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_systems" title="Information systems">information systems</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>. (such as magnetic or optical, including <a href="/wiki/Holographic_data_storage" title="Holographic data storage">holographic methods</a>), etc. Information does not cease to exist, it may only get communication</a> represents the convergence of informatics, telecommunication and audio-visual media &amp; content.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technologically_mediated_information">Technologically mediated information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Technologically mediated information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is estimated that the world\'s technological capacity to store information grew from 2.6 (optimally compressed) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exabytes" title="Exabytes">exabytes</a> in 1986 &#8211; which is the informational equivalent to less than one 730-MB <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> per person (539 MB per person) &#8211; to 295 (optimally compressed) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exabytes" title="Exabytes">exabytes</a> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-13">[13]</a></sup> This is the informational equivalent of almost 61 <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> per person in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilbertvideo2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hilbertvideo2011-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The world&#8217;s combined technological capacity to receive information through one-way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast" title="Broadcast">broadcast</a> networks was the informational equivalent of 174 <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspapers</a> per person per day in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HilbertLopez2011_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The world\'s combined effective capacity to exchange information through two-way <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a> networks was the informational equivalent of 6 newspapers per person per day in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hilbertvideo2011_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hilbertvideo2011-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of 2007, an estimated 90% of all new information is digital, mostly stored on hard drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_records">As records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: As records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Records are specialized forms of information. Essentially, records are information produced consciously or as by-products of business activities or transactions and retained because of their value. Primarily, their value is as evidence of the activities of the organization but they may also be retained for their informational value. Sound <a href="/wiki/Records_management" title="Records management">records management</a> ensures that the integrity of records is preserved for as long as they are required.\n</p><p>The international standard on records management, ISO 15489, defines records as "information created, received, and maintained as evidence and information by an organization or person, in pursuance of legal obligations or in the transaction of business".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The International Committee on Archives (ICA) Committee on electronic records defined a record as, "recorded information produced or received in the initiation, conduct or completion of an institutional or individual activity and that comprises content, context and structure sufficient to provide evidence of the activity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ica_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ica-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Records may be maintained to retain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_memory" title="Corporate memory">corporate memory</a> of the organization or to meet legal, fiscal or accountability requirements imposed on the organization. Willis expressed the view that sound management of business records and information delivered "...six key requirements for good <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">corporate governance</a>...transparency; accountability; due process; compliance; meeting statutory and common law requirements; and security of personal and corporate information."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWillis2005_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWillis2005-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Template:Harvard_citation_documentation#Wikilink_to_citation_does_not_work" title="Template:Harvard citation documentation"><span title="Template:Harvard citation documentation#Wikilink to citation does not work">citation not found</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiotics">Semiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Semiotics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Buckland" title="Michael Buckland">Michael Buckland</a> has classified "information" in terms of its uses: "information as process", "information as knowledge", and "information as thing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Beynon-Davies" title="Paul Beynon-Davies">Beynon-Davies</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> explains the multi-faceted concept of information in terms of signs and signal-sign systems. Signs themselves can be considered in terms of four inter-dependent levels, layers or branches of <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a>: pragmatics, semantics, syntax, and empirics. These four layers serve to connect the social world on the one hand with the physical or technical world on the other.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">Pragmatics</a> is concerned with the purpose of communication. Pragmatics links the issue of signs with the context within which signs are used. The focus of pragmatics is on the intentions of living agents underlying communicative behaviour. In other words, pragmatics link language to action.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">Semantics</a> is concerned with the meaning of a message conveyed in a communicative act. Semantics considers the content of communication. Semantics is the study of the meaning of signs - the association between signs and behaviour. Semantics can be considered as the study of the link between symbols and their referents or concepts &#8211; particularly the way that signs relate to human behavior.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">Syntax</a> is concerned with the formalism used to represent a message. Syntax as an area studies the form of communication in terms of the logic and grammar of sign systems. Syntax is devoted to the study of the form rather than the content of signs and sign-systems.\n</p><p>Nielsen (2008) discusses the relationship between semiotics and information in relation to dictionaries. He introduces the concept of <a href="/wiki/Lexicographic_information_cost" title="Lexicographic information cost">lexicographic information costs</a> and refers to the effort a user of a dictionary must make to first find, and then understand data so that they can generate information.\n</p><p>Communication normally exists within the context of some social situation. The social situation sets the context for the intentions conveyed (pragmatics) and the form of communication. In a communicative situation intentions are expressed through messages that comprise collections of inter-related signs taken from a language mutually understood by the agents involved in the communication. Mutual understanding implies that agents involved understand the chosen language in terms of its agreed syntax (syntactics) and semantics. The sender codes the message in the language and sends the message as signals along some communication channel (empirics). The chosen communication channel has inherent properties that determine outcomes such as the speed at which communication can take place, and over what distance.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
