id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dayuan</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Da Yuan" and "Tayuan" redirect here. For the dynasty in China, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a>. For place in Taiwan, see <a href="/wiki/Dayuan_District" title="Dayuan District">Dayuan District</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ta-YuanMap.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="778" data-file-width="1301" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ta-YuanMap.jpg/320px-Ta-YuanMap.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ta-YuanMap.jpg/480px-Ta-YuanMap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Ta-YuanMap.jpg/640px-Ta-YuanMap.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ta-YuanMap.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <b>Dayuan</b> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>) was one of the three advanced civilizations of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> around 130 BCE, together with <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactria" title="Greco-Bactria">Greco-Bactria</a>, according to the Chinese historical work <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Dayuan</b> (<b>Ta-yuan</b>; <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a> reconstructed pronunciation: /dha&#720;ts &#660;wan/; <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a> reconstructed pronunciation according to <a href="/wiki/Edwin_G._Pulleyblank" title="Edwin G. Pulleyblank">Edwin G. Pulleyblank</a>: /daj &#660;uan/; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7%E5%AE%9B" title="wikt:&#22823;&#23451;">&#22823;&#23451;</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">D&#224;yu&#257;n</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Ta<sup>4</sup>-yuan<sup>1</sup></span></i>; literally: "Great <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionians</a>") was a country in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana_valley" title="Ferghana valley">Ferghana valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, described in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_literature" title="Chinese literature">Chinese</a> historical works of <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Grand_Historian" title="Records of the Grand Historian">Records of the Grand Historian</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i>. It is mentioned in the accounts of the famous Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> in 130 BCE and the numerous embassies that followed him into Central Asia. The country of Dayuan is generally accepted as relating to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana_Valley" title="Ferghana Valley">Ferghana Valley</a>, and its Greek city <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Khujand" title="Khujand">Khujand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>).\n</p><p>These Chinese accounts describe the Dayuan as urbanized dwellers with <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_race" title="Caucasian race">Caucasian</a> features, living in walled cities and having "customs identical to those of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrians" title="Greco-Bactrians">Greco-Bactrians</a>", a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic</a> kingdom that was ruling <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a> at that time in today\'s northern Afghanistan. The Dayuan are also described as manufacturers and great lovers of wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Dayuan were the descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Colonies_in_antiquity" title="Colonies in antiquity">Greek colonists</a> that were settled by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a> in 329 BCE (see <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a>), and prospered within the Hellenistic realm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucids" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrians" title="Greco-Bactrians">Greco-Bactrians</a>, until they were isolated by the migrations of the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> around 160 BCE. It appears that the name "Yuan" was simply a transliteration of <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yavana" title="Yavana">Yavana</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Yona" title="Yona">Yona</a></i>, used throughout antiquity in Asia to designate <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionians</a>"), so that Dayuan would mean "Great Ionians".\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tayuan.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="707" data-file-width="416" decoding="async" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Tayuan.png/140px-Tayuan.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Tayuan.png/210px-Tayuan.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Tayuan.png/280px-Tayuan.png 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tayuan.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Characters for <b>Dayuan</b>, associating the character for "Great", and a character originally depicting two persons seated under one roof, but used for its sound value here.  It is pronounced "yuan" instead of the more usual "wan".</div></div></div>\n<p>The interaction between the Dayuan and the Chinese is historically crucial, since it  represents one of the first major contacts between an urbanized Western civilization and the Chinese civilization, opening the way to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> that was to link the East and the West in material and cultural exchange from the 1st century BCE to the 15th century.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hellenistic rule (329&#8211;160 BCE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UrumqiWarrior.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="445" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/UrumqiWarrior.jpg/340px-UrumqiWarrior.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UrumqiWarrior.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Probable Greek soldier, woollen wall hanging, <a href="/wiki/Sampul_tapestry" title="Sampul tapestry">Sampul tapestry</a>, 3rd-2nd century BCE, Sampul, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urumqi" title="Urumqi">Urumqi</a> <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> Museum.</div></div></div>\n<p>The region of Ferghana was conquered by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> in 329 BCE and became his most advanced base in Central Asia. He founded the fortified city of <a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a> (Lit. "Alexandria the Furthest") in the southwestern part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a> valley, on the southern bank of the river <a href="/wiki/Syr_Darya" title="Syr Darya">Syr Darya</a> (ancient Jaxartes), at the location of the modern city of <a href="/wiki/Khujand" title="Khujand">Khujand</a> (also called Khozdent, formerly Leninabad), in the state of <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>. Alexander built a 6 kilometer long brick wall around the city and, as similarly in the cases of the other cities he founded, had a garrison of his retired veterans and wounded settle there.\n</p><p>The whole of <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a> and the area of Ferghana remained under the control of the Hellenistic <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a> until 250 BCE.  The region then wrested independence under the leadership of its Greek governors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diodotus_of_Bactria" title="Diodotus of Bactria">Diodotus of Bactria</a>, to become the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Greco-Bactrian_kingdom_.28250.E2.80.93160_BCE.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Greco-Bactrian_kingdom_(250&#8211;160_BCE)">Greco-Bactrian kingdom (250&#8211;160 BCE)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Greco-Bactrian kingdom (250&#8211;160 BCE)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EuthydemusI.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="118" data-file-width="237" decoding="async" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/EuthydemusI.jpg/200px-EuthydemusI.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/EuthydemusI.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EuthydemusI.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_I" title="Euthydemus I">Euthydemus I</a> (230&#8211;200 BC)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrians</a> held their territory, and according to the Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> even went beyond Alexandria Eschate and <i>"extended their empire as far as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">Seres</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Phryni" title="Phryni">Phryni</a>"</i> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/cgi-bin/ptext?lookup=Strab.+11.11.1" rel="nofollow">Strabo XI.XI.I</a>). There are indications that they may have led expeditions as far as <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, leading to the first known contacts between China and the West around 200 BC. Various statuettes and representations of Greek soldiers have been found north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tien_Shan" title="Tien Shan">Tien Shan</a>, and are today on display in the museum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urumqi" title="Urumqi">Urumqi</a> (Boardman).\n</p><p>Around 160 BC, the area of Ferghana seems to have been invaded by <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Saka</a> tribes (called the Sai-Wang by the Chinese). The Sai-Wang, originally settled in the <a href="/wiki/Ili_River" title="Ili River">Ili</a> valley in the general area of Lake <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Issyk_Kul" title="Issyk Kul">Issyk Kul</a>, were retreating southward after having been dislodged by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yueh-Chi" title="Yueh-Chi">Yuezhi</a> (who themselves were fleeing from the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" Tarn, \n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The remaining of the Sai-Wang tribes apparently seized the Greek province of Ferghana&#8230; It was easy at this time to occupy Ferghana: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucratides" title="Eucratides">Eucratides</a> had just overthrown the <a href="/wiki/Euthydemus_I" title="Euthydemus I">Euthydemid</a> dynasty, he himself was with his army in India, and in 159 he met his death&#8230; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocles" title="Heliocles">Heliocles</a>, preoccupied first with the recovery of Bactria and then with the invasion of India, must have let this outlying province go</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>W. W. Dayuan had probably by then become a kingdom of nomadic people exacting tribute from the local urbanized Hellenistic population.\n</p><p>Also in 106&#8211;101 BCE, during their conflict with China, the country of Dayuan is said to have been an ally with the neighbouring tribes of <a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a> (probably Sodgia). The Chinese also record the name of the king of Dayuan as "Mu-Kua", a Saka name rendered in Greek as Mauakes or Maues. (Another Scythian ruler by the name of <a href="/wiki/Maues" title="Maues">Maues</a> was later a ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Scythian" title="Indo-Scythian">Indo-Scythian</a> kingdom in northern India, during the 1st century BCE).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Yuezhi_migration">Yuezhi migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yuezhi migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han</a> Chronicles In 132BCE the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> were driven out of the <a href="/wiki/Ili_River" title="Ili River">Ili River</a> valley by the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a>. They fled south from the Ili river area, by-passed the urban civilization of the Dayuan in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>, and re-settled north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, definitively cutting Dayuan from contact with the Greco-Bactrian kingdom. The Yuezhi would further expand southward into Bactria proper around 125 BCE, and then going on to form the <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a> in the 1st century CE.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Interaction_with_China_.28130_BCE_onward.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_China_(130_BCE_onward)">Interaction with China (130 BCE onward)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interaction with China (130 BCE onward)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Dayuan remained a healthy and powerful civilization which had numerous contacts and exchanges with China from 130 BCE.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Zhang_Qian.27s_Report"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Zhang_Qian\'s_Report">Zhang Qian\'s Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zhang Qian\'s Report">edit</a><span BCE, at the time of <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a>\'s embassy to Central Asia, the Dayuan were described as inhabitants of a region corresponding to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>, far to the west of the Chinese empire. \n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" The seat of the Governor General lies to the east at a distance of 4,031 li.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Han Shu</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>To their south-west were the territories of the Yuezhi, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrians" title="Greco-Bactrians">Greco-Bactrians</a> further south still, beyond the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>.\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The great Yueh-chih is situated about 2000 or 3000 li west of Dayuan; they dwell north of the river Kuei (Oxus). class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123.5b</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The Shiji then explains that the Yuezhi originally inhabited the <a href="/wiki/Hexi_Corridor" title="Hexi Corridor">Hexi Corridor</a>, before they were defeated by the Xiongnu under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mao-tun" and Bactria to the south.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Urbanized_city-dwellers">Urbanized city-dwellers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Dayuan are said by <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> to be identical to those of the Bactrians in the south, who actually formed the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" The men all have deep-set eyes and profuse beards  and whiskers. They are skillful at commerce and will haggle over a fraction of a cent. Women are held in great respect, and the men make decisions on the advice of their women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>They were great manufacturers and lovers of <a href="/wiki/History_of_wine" title="History of wine">wine</a>:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Round about Dayuan they make wine from grapes. Wealthy people store up as much as 10,000 stones and over in their cellars, and keep it for several tens of years without spoiling. The people are fond of wine.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>According to the Shiji, grapes and <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> were introduced to China from Dayuan following Zhang Qian\'s embassy:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The regions around Dayuan make wine out of grapes, the wealthier inhabitants keeping as much as 10,000 or more <a href="/wiki/Picul" title="Picul">piculs</a> stored away. It can be kept for as long as twenty or thirty years without spoiling. The people love their wine and the horses love their alfalfa. The Han envoys brought back grape and alfalfa seeds to China and the emperor for the first time tried growing these plants in areas of rich soil. Later, when the Han acquired large numbers of the "heavenly horses" and the envoys from foreign states began to arrive with their retinues, the lands on all sides of the emperor\'s summer palaces and pleasure towers were planted with grapes and alfalfa for as far as the eye could see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245-1">[1]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The Shiji also claims that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_casting" title="Metal casting">metal casting</a> was introduced to the Dayuan region by Han <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserter" title="Deserter">deserters</a>:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>... the casting of coins and vessels was formerly unknown. Later, however, when some of the Chinese soldiers attached to the Han embassies ran away and surrendered to the people of the area, they taught them how to cast metal and manufacture weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations_with_China">Relations with China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relations with China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Heavenly_Horses" title="War of the Heavenly Horses">War of the Heavenly Horses</a></div>\n<p>Following the reports of Zhang Qian (who was originally sent to obtain an alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>, in vain), the Chinese emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Han_China" title="Emperor Wu of Han China">Wudi</a> became interested in developing commercial relationships with the sophisticated urban civilizations of Ferghana, Bactria and Parthia:\n"The Son of Heaven on hearing all this reasoned thus: Ferghana (Dayuan) and the possessions of Bactria and Parthia are large countries, full of rare things, with a population living in fixed abodes and given to occupations somewhat identical with those of the Chinese people, but with weak armies, and placing great value on the rich produce of China" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123)\n</p><p>The Chinese subsequently sent numerous embassies, around ten every year, to these countries and as far as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucid" title="Seleucid">Seleucid</a> Syria.\n"Thus more embassies were dispatched to An-si (<a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>), An-ts\'ai (the Aorsi, or <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>), Li-kan (Syria under the Seleucids), T\'iau-chi (<a href="/wiki/Chaldea" title="Chaldea">Chaldea</a>), and Shon-tu (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>)... As a rule, rather more than ten such missions went forward in the course of a year, and at the least five or six." (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a>, 123)\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HanHorse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="453" decoding="async" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/HanHorse.jpg/250px-HanHorse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/HanHorse.jpg/375px-HanHorse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/HanHorse.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HanHorse.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fragment of a terracotta horse sculpture of the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty#Eastern_Han" title="Han dynasty">Later Han Dynasty</a> (1st-2nd century AD/CE)</div></div></div>\n<p>The Chinese were also strongly attracted by the tall and powerful horses ("heavenly horses") in the possession of the Dayuan, which were of capital importance to fight the nomad Xiongnu. The refusal of the Dayuan to offer them enough horses along with a series of conflicts and mutual disrespect resulted in the death of the Chinese ambassador and the confiscation of the gold sent as payment for the horses.\n</p><p>Enraged, and thinking Dayuan weak, the Chinese Emperor in 104 BCE sent out <a href="/wiki/Li_Guangli" title="Li Guangli">Li Guangli</a>, the brother of his favorite concubine. He was given 6,000 horsemen and \'30,000 young men of bad reputation rounded up from the provinces\'. General Li lost many men along the way in petty fights with local rulers. After a severe defeat at a place called Yucheng Li concluded that he was not strong enough to take the enemy capital and therefore returned to <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> (about 102 BC).\n</p><p>Emperor Wudi responded by giving Li Guangli a much larger army along with a huge number of oxen, donkeys and camels to carry supplies. With this force he had no difficulty reaching Ershi, the Dayuan capital. After a 40-day siege the Chinese had broken through the outer wall and cut off the water supply. The nobles of Ershi killed their king and sent his head to Li Guangli, offering the Chinese all the horses they wanted. Li accepted the offer, appointed one of the nobles to be the new king and withdrew with the horses. On his return journey all the petty states accepted Chinese sovereignty. He reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jade_Gate" title="Jade Gate">Jade Gate</a> about 100 BC with 10,000 men and 1,000 horses.\n</p><p>Contacts with the West were re-established following the peace treaty with the Yuan. Ambassadors were once again sent to the West, caravans were sent to Bactria.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="An_era_of_East-West_trade_and_cultural_exchange">An era of East-West trade and cultural exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: An era of East-West trade and cultural exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> essentially came into being from the 1st century BCE, following the efforts of China to consolidate a road to the Western world, both through direct settlements in the area of the Tarim Basin and diplomatic relations with the countries of the Dayuan, Parthians and Bactrians further west.\n</p><p>Intense trade followed soon, confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> craze for Chinese <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> (supplied by the Parthians) from the 1st century BC, to the point that the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Senate" title="Roman Senate">Senate</a> issued, in vain, several edicts to prohibit the wearing of silk, on economic and moral grounds. This is attested by at least three significant authors:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> (64/ 63 BCE&#8211;c. 24 CE).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a> (c. 3 BCE&#8211;65 CE).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a> (23&#8211;79 CE).</li></ul>\n<p>This is also the time when the <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> faith and the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhism" title="Greco-Buddhism">Greco-Buddhist</a> culture started to travel along the Silk Road, penetrating China from around the 1st century BCE.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Buddhism" title="History of Buddhism">History of Buddhism</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhism" title="Greco-Buddhism">Greco-Buddhism</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footnotes">Footnotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dayuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Footnotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Watson,_Burton_1993_pp._244-245_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Watson, Burton(1993). <i>Records of the Grand Historian by Sima Qian</i>.
