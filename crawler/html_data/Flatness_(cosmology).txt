class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Flatness_problem" title="Flatness problem">Flatness problem</a></div>\n<p>The concept of "curvature of space" is fundamental to <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmology</a>. A space without curvature is called a "flat space" or <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. \nWhether the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is &#8220;flat&#8243; could determine its ultimate fate; whether it will expand forever, or ultimately collapse back into itself. The <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> of spacetime has been measured by the <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe</a> (WMAP) to be nearly flat. According to the WMAP 5-year results and analysis, &#8220;WMAP determined that the universe is flat, from which it follows that the mean <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> in the universe is equal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedman_equations" title="Friedman equations">critical density</a> (within a 1% margin of error). This is equivalent to a mass density of 9.9 &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;30</sup> g/cm<sup>3</sup>, which is equivalent to only 5.9 protons per cubic meter.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMAP-5_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMAP-5-1">[1]</a></sup> The WMAP data are consistent with a flat geometry, with &#937; = 1.02 +/- 0.02.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
