originating with the US Space Agency</div>\n<p><b>NASA spinoff technologies</b> are commercial products and services which have been developed with the help of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, through research and development contracts, such as <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Innovation_Research" title="Small Business Innovation Research">Small Business Innovation Research</a> (SBIR) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_Business_Technology_Transfer_Program" title="Small Business Technology Transfer Program">STTR</a> awards, licensing of NASA patents, use of NASA facilities, technical assistance from NASA personnel, or data from NASA research. Information on new NASA technology that may be useful to industry is available in periodical and website form in "NASA Tech Briefs", while successful examples of commercialization are reported annually in the NASA publication "Spinoffs".\n</p><p>In 1979, notable <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> author <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a> helped bring awareness to the spinoffs when he was asked to appear before <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> after recovering from one of the earliest known <a href="/wiki/Vascular_bypass" title="Vascular bypass">vascular bypass</a> operations to correct a blocked artery; in his testimony, reprinted in the book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expanded_Universe_(Heinlein)" title="Expanded Universe (Heinlein)">Expanded Universe</a></i>, he claimed that four NASA spinoff technologies made the surgery possible, and it was a few from a long list of NASA spinoff technologies from space development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For more than 50 years, the NASA Technology Transfer Program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  has connected <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> resources to private industry, referring to the commercial products as spinoffs. Well-known products that NASA claims as spinoffs include <a href="/wiki/Memory_foam" title="Memory foam">memory foam</a> (originally named temper foam), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze-dried_food" title="Freeze-dried food">freeze-dried food</a>, firefighting equipment, emergency "<a href="/wiki/Space_blanket" title="Space blanket">space blankets</a>", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DustBuster" title="DustBuster">DustBusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cochlear_implant" title="Cochlear implant">cochlear implants</a>, <a href="/wiki/LZR_Racer" title="LZR Racer">LZR Racer</a> swimsuits, and <a href="/wiki/Active_pixel_sensor" title="Active pixel sensor">CMOS image sensors</a>. As of 2016, NASA claims that there are nearly 2,000 other spinoffs in the fields of computer technology, environment and agriculture, health and medicine, public safety, transportation, recreation, and industrial productivity.  Contrary to common belief, NASA did not invent <a href="/wiki/Tang_(drink)" title="Tang (drink)">Tang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">Velcro</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teflon" title="Teflon">Teflon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spinfaq_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-spinfaq-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2008, NASA announced an interactive Web feature, <i>NASA @ Home and City</i> which uses <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a> animation to show some examples of everyday products claimed to be spinoffs.<sup class="reference" id="History_of_the_Spinoff_publication">History of the <i>Spinoff</i> publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the Spinoff publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Spinoff</i> is a NASA publication featuring technology made available to the public. Since 1976, NASA has featured an average of 50 technologies each year in the annual publication, and <i>Spinoff</i> maintains a searchable database of these technologies. When products first spun off from space research, NASA presented a black and white report in 1973, titled the "Technology Utilization Program Report". Because of interest in the reports, NASA decided to create the annual publications in color. <i>Spinoff</i> was first published in 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1976_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1976-5">[5]</a></sup> and since then, NASA has distributed free copies to universities, the media, inventors and the general public. <i>Spinoff</i> describes how NASA works with various industries and small businesses to bring new technology to the public. As of 2016, there were over 1,920 <i>Spinoff</i> products in the database dating back to 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_medicine">Health and medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health and medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_ear_thermometers">Infrared ear thermometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Infrared ear thermometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Diatek Corporation and NASA developed an <a href="/wiki/Infrared_thermometer" title="Infrared thermometer">aural thermometer</a> that measures the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> emitted by the <a href="/wiki/Eardrum" title="Eardrum">eardrum</a>, similar to the way the temperature of stars and planets are measured. This method avoids contact with mucous membranes and permits rapid temperature measurement of newborn or incapacitated patients. NASA supported the Diatek Corporation through the Technology Affiliates Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1991_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1991-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventricular_assist_device">Ventricular assist device</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ventricular assist device">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Collaboration between NASA, Dr. Michael DeBakey, Dr. George Noon, and MicroMed Technology Inc. resulted in a heart pump for patients awaiting heart transplants. The MicroMed DeBakey ventricular assist device (VAD) functions as a "bridge to heart transplant" by pumping blood until a donor heart is available. This technology is used by Eye surgeons to track eye movements at a rate of 4,000 times per second while reshaping the cornea, the clear front surface of the eye, using a laser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2003_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2003-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_limbs">Artificial limbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Artificial limbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>NASA&#8217;s continued funding, coupled with its collective innovations in robotics and shock-absorption/comfort materials are inspiring and enabling the private sector to create new and better solutions for animal and human prostheses. href="#cite_note-Spinoff2004-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Additionally, other private-sector adaptations of NASA&#8217;s temper foam technology have brought about custom-moldable materials offering the Thermacool, an electric air conditioning system, uses a new compressor whose rotary pump design runs off an energy-efficient, brushless DC motor and allows pilots to use the air conditioner before the engine starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2007_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2007-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highway_safety">Highway safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Highway safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Safety grooving, the cutting of grooves in concrete to increase traction and prevent injury, was first developed to reduce aircraft accidents on wet runways. Represented by the International Grooving and Grinding Association, the industry expanded into highway and pedestrian applications. Safety grooving originated at Langley Research Center, which assisted in testing the grooving at airports and on highways. Skidding was reduced, stopping distance decreased, and a vehicle&#8217;s cornering ability on curves was increased. The process has been extended to animal holding pens, parking lots, and other potentially slippery surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1985_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1985-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improved_radial_tires">Improved radial tires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Improved radial tires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company developed a fibrous material, five times stronger than steel, for NASA to use in parachute shrouds to soft-land the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking_Lander" title="Viking Lander">Viking Lander</a> spacecraft on the Martian surface. Recognizing the durability of the material, Goodyear expanded the technology and went on to produce a new radial tire with a tread life expected to be 10,000 miles (16,000&#160;km) greater than conventional radials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1976_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1976-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_detection">Chemical detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Chemical detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>NASA contracted with Intelligent Optical Systems (IOS) to develop moisture- and pH-sensitive sensors to warn of corrosive conditions in aircraft before damage occurs. After completing the work with NASA, IOS was tasked by the U.S. Department of Defense to further develop the sensors for detecting chemical warfare agents and potential threats, such as toxic industrial compounds and nerve agents. IOS has sold the chemically sensitive fiber optic cables to major automotive and aerospace companies, who are finding a variety of uses for the devices such as aiding experimentation with nontraditional power sources, and as an economical "alarm system" for detecting chemical release in large facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2007_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2007-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_safety">Public safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Public safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_enhancing_and_analysis_systems">Video enhancing and analysis systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Video enhancing and analysis systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Intergraph Government Solutions developed its Video Analyst System (VAS) by building on Video Image Stabilization and Registration (VISAR) technology created by NASA to help FBI agents analyze video footage. Originally used for enhancing video images from nighttime videotapes made with hand-held camcorders, VAS is a tool for video enhancement and analysis offering support of full-resolution digital video, stabilization, frame-by-frame analysis, conversion of analog video to digital storage formats, and increased visibility of filmed subjects without altering underlying footage. Aside from law enforcement and security applications, VAS has also been adapted to serve the military for reconnaissance, weapons deployment, damage assessment, training, and mission debriefing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2001_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2001-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmine_Removal">Landmine Removal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Landmine Removal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Thiokol" title="Thiokol">Thiokol</a> has used surplus rocket fuel through an agreement with NASA\'s Marshall Space Flight Center used the fuel to produce a flare that can safely destroy landmines. The fuel that is left unused from a launch will become a solid, which cannot be reused but can be used as an ingredient needed to create the Demining Device flare. The Demining Device flare uses a battery-triggered electric match to ignite and neutralize land mines in the field without detonation. The flare uses the solid rocket fuel to burn a hole in a mine\'s case and burns away the explosive contents so the mine can be disarmed without hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2000_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2000-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire-resistant_reinforcement">Fire-resistant reinforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fire-resistant reinforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Built and designed by Avco Corporation, the Apollo heat shield was coated with a material whose purpose was to burn and thus dissipate energy during reentry while charring, to form a protective coating to block heat penetration. NASA subsequently funded Avco&#8217;s development of other applications of the heat shield, such as fire-retardant paints and foams for aircraft, which led to an intumescent epoxy material, which expands in volume when exposed to heat or flames, acting as an insulating barrier and dissipating heat through burn-off. Further innovations include steel coatings devised to make high-rise buildings and public structures safer by swelling to provide a tough and stable insulating layer over the steel for up to 4 hours of fire protection, ultimately to slow building collapse and provide more time for escape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2006_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2006-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firefighting_equipment">Firefighting equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Firefighting equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Firefighting equipment in the United States is based on lightweight materials developed for the U.S. Space Program. The broadest fire-related technology transfer is the breathing apparatus for protection from smoke inhalation injury. \n</p><p>Additionally, NASA&#8217;s inductorless electronic circuit technology led to lower-cost, more rugged, short-range two-way radio now used by firefighters. Martek Biosciences Corporation\'s founders and principal scientists acquired their expertise in this area while working on the NASA program. The microalgae food supplement was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Space_Foundation" title="Space Foundation">Space Foundation</a> Space Technology Hall of Fame in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1996_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1996-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portable_cordless_vacuums">Portable cordless vacuums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Portable cordless vacuums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For the Apollo space mission, NASA required a portable, self-contained drill capable of extracting core samples from below the lunar surface. <a href="/wiki/Black_%26_Decker" title="Black &amp; Decker">Black &amp; Decker</a> was tasked with the job, and developed a computer program to optimize the design of the drill\'s motor and ensure minimal power consumption. That computer program led to the development of a cordless miniature vacuum cleaner called the DustBuster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1981_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1981-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freeze_drying">Freeze drying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Freeze drying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In planning for the long-duration Apollo missions, NASA conducted extensive research into space food. One of the techniques developed in 1938 by Nestl&#233; was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze_drying" title="Freeze drying">freeze drying</a>. In the United States, Action Products later commercialized this technique for other foods, concentrating on snack food resulting in products like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_ice_cream" title="Space ice cream">Space ice cream</a>. The foods are cooked, quickly frozen, and then slowly heated in a vacuum chamber to remove the ice crystals formed by the freezing process. The final product retains 98%<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of its nutrition and weighs much less than before drying.  The ratio of weight before and after drying depends strongly on the particular food item but a typical freeze-dried weight is 20% of the original weight.\n</p><p>Today, one of the benefits of this advancement in food preservation includes simple, nutritious meals available to disabled and otherwise homebound senior adults unable to take advantage of existing meal programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1978_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1978-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1980_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1980-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1994_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1994-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_age_swimsuit">Space age swimsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Space age swimsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Langley Research Center\'s wind tunnel testing facilities and fluid flow analysis software supported <a href="/wiki/Speedo" title="Speedo">Speedo</a>\'s design of a space age-enriched swimsuit. In March 2008, athletes wearing the LZR Racer broke 13 swimming world records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_image_sensor">Digital image sensor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Digital image sensor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The invention of digital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_sensors" title="Image sensors">image sensors</a> used in products like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_phones" title="Mobile phones">mobile phones</a> and <a href="/wiki/GoPro" title="GoPro">GoPro</a> cameras traces back to NASA JPL scientist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Fossum" title="Eric Fossum">Eric Fossum</a> who wanted to miniaturize cameras for interplanetary missions. Fossum invented <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> image sensors that have become NASA\'s most ubiquitous spinoff technology, enabling the use of cameras inside mobile phones. Fossum found a way to reduce the signal noise that had plagued earlier attempts at CMOS imagers, applying a technique called intra-pixel charge transfer with correlated double sampling that results in a clearer image, this led to the creation of CMOS <a href="/wiki/Active_pixel_sensor" title="Active pixel sensor">active pixel sensors</a>, which are used today in all <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> cameras and many other applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air-scrubbers">Air-scrubbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Air-scrubbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Based on a discovery made in the 1990s at the Wisconsin Center for Space Automation and Robotics where Researchers, with the help of the Space Product Development Program at Marshall Space Flight Center, were trying to find a way to eliminate ethylene that accumulates around plants growing in spacecraft and then found a solution: light-induced oxidation. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV" title="UV">UV</a> light hits titanium dioxide, it frees electrons that turn oxygen and moisture into charged particles that oxidize air contaminants such as volatile organic compounds, turning them into carbon dioxide and water. This air scrubber also eliminates other airborne organic compounds and neutralized bacteria, viruses, and molds. An air scrubber with light-induced oxidation can cleans air, surfaces and clothes and nearly 30 <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> teams now have this scrubber technology in their facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" engineers are collaborating with qualified companies to develop systems intended to sustain the astronauts living on the International Space Station and future Moon and space missions. This system turns wastewater from respiration, sweat, and urine into drinkable water. By combining the benefits of chemical <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>, and ultra-filtration processes, this technology can yield safe, drinkable water from the most challenging sources, such as in underdeveloped regions where well water may be heavily contaminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2004_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2004-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1995_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1995-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_Cells">Solar Cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Solar Cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Monocrystalline_silicon" title="Monocrystalline silicon">Single-crystal</a> silicon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a> are now widely available at low cost. The technology behind these solar devices&#8212;which provide up to 50% more power than conventional solar cells&#8212;originated with the efforts of a NASA-sponsored 28-member coalition forming the Environmental Research Aircraft and Sensor Technology (ERAST) Alliance. ERAST&#8217;s goal was to develop remotely piloted aircraft, intended to fly unmanned at high altitudes for days at a time and requiring advanced solar power sources that did not add weight. As a result, SunPower Corporation created advanced silicon-based cells for terrestrial or airborne applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2005_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2005-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>66&#8211;67</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_remediation">Pollution remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Pollution remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>NASA&#8217;s microencapsulating technology enabled the creation of a "Petroleum Remediation Product," which safely cleans petroleum-based pollutants from water. The PRP uses thousands of microcapsules&#8212;tiny balls of beeswax with hollow centers. Water cannot penetrate the microcapsule&#8217;s cell, but oil is absorbed into the beeswax spheres as they float on the water&#8217;s surface. Contaminating chemical compounds that originally come from crude oil (such as fuels, motor oils, or petroleum hydrocarbons) are caught before they settle, limiting damage to ocean beds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2006_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2006-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1994_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1994-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Correcting_for_GPS_signal_errors">Correcting for GPS signal errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Correcting for GPS signal errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 1990s, NASA scientists at JPL developed software capable of correcting for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a> signal errors, enabling accuracy within inches/centimetres; it is called Real-Time GIPSY (RTG). <a href="/wiki/John_Deere" title="John Deere">John Deere</a> licensed the software and used it to develop self-driving farm equipment. As of 2016, as nearly 70% of North American farmland is cultivated by self-driving tractors, which rely on RTG that was developed at NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_location">Water location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Water location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Dr. Alain Gachet founded Radar Technologies International (RTI) in 1999 to use satellite generated data to identify probable locations of precious metals and during its use found it could also detect water. The system developed with this data, WATEX, uses about 80 percent of its data inputs from publicly available NASA information. This free information allowed RTI to develop the WATEX system to successfully locate water sources, such as in 2004 at refugee camps during the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Darfur" title="War in Darfur">War in Darfur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_technology">Computer technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Computer technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_analysis_software">Structural analysis software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Structural analysis software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>NASA <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> engineers have created thousands of computer programs over the decades equipped to design, test, and analyze stress, vibration, and acoustical properties of a broad assortment of aerospace parts and structures. COSMIC maintains a library of computer programs from NASA and other government agencies and sells them at a fraction of the cost of developing a new program. NASA Structural Analysis Computer Software was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Space_Foundation" title="Space Foundation">Space Foundation</a> Space Technology Hall of Fame in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1991_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1991-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1988_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1988-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1980_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1980-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1986_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1986-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1990_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1990-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1998_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1998-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remotely_controlled_ovens">Remotely controlled ovens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Remotely controlled ovens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Embedded Web Technology (EWT) software&#8212;originally developed by NASA for use by astronauts operating experiments on the International Space Station&#8212;lets a user monitor and/or control a device remotely over the Internet. The application delivers real-time satellite data, including movies and stills, of Earth, that enable users to learn about subjects such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, Earth\'s dynamic systems and plant life on land and in the oceans. The content is accompanied by short descriptions about the Data and why it is important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenStack">OpenStack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: OpenStack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>NASA developed a cloud compute platform to give additional compute and storage resources for its engineers, called <a href="/wiki/Nebula_(computing_platform)" title="Nebula (computing platform)">Nebula</a>. Today, the same scientist and Luna Innovations are using a digital adaptation of this same device for non-destructive evaluation (NDE) of railroad ties, groundwater analysis, radiation, and as a medical testing device to assess levels of internal swelling and pressure for patients suffering from intracranial pressure and compartment syndrome, a painful condition that results when pressure within muscles builds to dangerous levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff2005_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff2005-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1978_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1978-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_safety">Food safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Food safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Faced with the problem of how and what to feed an astronaut in a sealed capsule under weightless conditions while planning for human spaceflight, NASA enlisted the aid of The Pillsbury Company to address two principal concerns: eliminating crumbs of food that might contaminate the spacecraft&#8217;s atmosphere and sensitive instruments, and assuring absolute absence of disease-producing bacteria and toxins. Pillsbury developed the Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point (HACCP) concept to address NASA&#8217;s second concern. HACCP is designed to prevent food safety problems rather than to catch them after they have occurred. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has applied HACCP guidelines for the handling of seafood, juice, and dairy products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spinoff1991_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spinoff1991-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For space missions, gold is particularly useful because it is good at reflecting light, which will help to detect celestial objects from far away and gold does not oxidize so it will not tarnish, unlike most other metals. Due to both benefits, the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a> uses a lot of gold for its mirrors. NASA partnered with Epner Technology, a Brooklyn-based business that has been gold-plating for generations to develop the technology to gold plate the telescope\'s parts.  This NASA technology transfer to Epner gave the company a reputation for durable gold coatings and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences</a> was having to replate <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscar</a> statues that had faded over time.  Epner has contracted with the Academy to gold plate all future Oscars while offering a lifetime guarantee to replate, for free, any faded Oscar; its gold plating has lasted for decades in space without fading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mistakenly_attributed_NASA_spinoffs">Mistakenly attributed NASA spinoffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_spinoff_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Mistakenly attributed NASA spinoffs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The following is a list of technologies sometimes mistakenly attributed directly to NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spinfaq_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-spinfaq-3">[3]</a></sup> In many cases, NASA popularized technology or aided its development, due to its usefulness in space, which ultimately resulted in the technology\'s creation.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barcodes" title="Barcodes">Barcodes</a> - The barcode was invented in 1948. These were used by NASA and a number of spinoff products came out of those projects such as portable cordless vacuums.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_Resonance_Imaging" title="Magnetic Resonance Imaging">Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)</a>, best known as a device for body scanning. However, in the late 1960s, NASA partnered with a company to make a highly accurate quartz clock.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoke_detectors" title="Smoke detectors">Smoke detectors</a> - NASA\'s connection to the modern smoke detector is that it developed one with adjustable sensitivity as part of the <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a> project; this development helps with nuisance tripping.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Pen" title="Space Pen">Space Pen</a> - An <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a> states that NASA spent a large amount of money to develop a pen that would write in space (the result purportedly being the Fisher Space Pen), while the Soviets used pencils. While NASA did spend funds to create a pen to work in space, the project was cancelled due to public opposition, and U.S. astronauts used pencils until the Fisher space pen was invented by a third party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physics1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-physics1-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snopes1_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-snopes1-47">[47]</a></sup>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tang_(drink)" title="Tang (drink)">Tang juice powder</a> - Tang was developed by <a href="/wiki/General_Foods" title="General Foods">General Foods</a> in 1957.
