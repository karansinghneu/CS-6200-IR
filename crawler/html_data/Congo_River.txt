language">Kongo</a>: <i lang="kg">Nz&#226;di K&#244;ngo</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Fleuve Congo</i>; <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">rio Congo</i>), formerly known as the <b>Zaire River</b> under the <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Mobutu regime</a>, is the second longest river in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, shorter  only than the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>, as well as the second largest river in the world <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_by_discharge" title="List of rivers by discharge">by discharge</a> volume, following only the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a>. It is also the world\'s deepest recorded river, with measured depths in excess of 220&#160;m (720&#160;ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2008-2">[2]</a></sup>\nThe Congo-<a href="/wiki/Lualaba_River" title="Lualaba River">Lualaba</a>-<a href="/wiki/Chambeshi_River" title="Chambeshi River">Chambeshi River</a> system has an overall length of 4,700&#160;km (2,920&#160;mi), which makes it the world\'s ninth-<a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_by_length" title="List of rivers by length">longest river</a>. The Chambeshi is a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Lualaba_River" title="Lualaba River">Lualaba River</a>, and <i>Lualaba</i> is the name of the Congo River upstream of <a href="/wiki/Boyoma_Falls" title="Boyoma Falls">Boyoma Falls</a>, extending for 1,800&#160;km (1,120&#160;mi).\n</p><p>Measured along with the Lualaba, the main <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributary</a>, the Congo River has a total length of 4,370&#160;km (2,715&#160;mi). It is the only river to cross the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> twice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo Basin</a> has a total area of about 4,000,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1,500,000&#160;sq&#160;mi), or 13% of the entire African landmass.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congo_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name Congo/Kongo river originates from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kongo" title="Kingdom of Kongo">Kingdom of Kongo</a> once located on the southern bank of the river. The kingdom in turn was named for the indigenous Bantu <a href="/wiki/Kongo_people" title="Kongo people">Kongo people</a>, known in the 17th century as "Esikongo".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> South of the Kingdom of Kongo proper lay the similarly named <a href="/wiki/Kakongo" title="Kakongo">Kakongo</a> kingdom, mentioned in 1535. <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ortelius" title="Abraham Ortelius">Abraham Ortelius</a> in his world map of 1564 labeled as "Manicongo" the city at the mouth of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tribal names in <i>Kongo</i> possibly derive from a word for a public gathering or tribal assembly. The modern name of the <a href="/wiki/Kongo_people" title="Kongo people">Kongo people</a> or <i>Bakongo</i> was introduced in the early 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The name <i>Zaire</i> is from a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> adaptation of a <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">Kikongo</a> word, <i lang="kg" title="Kongo language text">nzere</i> ("river"), a truncation of <i lang="kg" title="Kongo language text">nzadi o nzere</i> ("river swallowing rivers").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The river was known as <i>Zaire</i> during the 16th and 17th centuries; <i>Congo</i> seems to have replaced <i>Zaire</i> gradually in English usage during the 18th century, and <i>Congo</i> is the preferred English name in 19th-century literature, although references to <i>Zahir</i> or <i>Zaire</i> as the name used by the inhabitants remained common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> are named after it, as was the previous <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo_(L%C3%A9opoldville)" title="Republic of the Congo (L&#233;opoldville)">Republic of the Congo</a> which had gained independence in 1960 from the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a>.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Zaire" title="Republic of Zaire">Republic of Zaire</a> during 1971&#8211;1997 was also named after the river, after its name in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basin_and_course">Basin and course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congo_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basin and course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo Basin</a></div>\n<p>The Congo\'s <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> covers 4,014,500 square kilometres (1,550,000&#160;sq&#160;mi),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bossche_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bossche-1">[1]</a></sup> an area larger than <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The Congo\'s <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> at its mouth ranges from 23,000 to 75,000 cubic metres per second (810,000 to 2,650,000&#160;cu&#160;ft/s), with an average of 41,000 cubic metres per second (1,400,000&#160;cu&#160;ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bossche_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-bossche-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The river and its <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributaries</a> flow through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congolian_forests" title="Congolian forests">Congo Rainforest</a>, the second largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forest" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a> area in the world, second only to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> in South America. The river also has the second-largest flow in the world, behind the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a>; the third-largest drainage basin of any river, behind the Amazon and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="R&#237;o de la Plata">Plate</a> rivers; and is one of the deepest rivers in the world, at depths greater than 220&#160;m (720&#160;ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs2008_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs2008-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Because its drainage basin includes areas both north and south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, its flow is stable, as there is always at least one part of the river experiencing a rainy season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The sources of the Congo are in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_(geographic_feature)" title="Highland (geographic feature)">highlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> of the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mweru" title="Lake Mweru">Lake Mweru</a>, which feed the <a href="/wiki/Lualaba_River" title="Lualaba River">Lualaba River</a>, which then becomes the Congo below <a href="/wiki/Boyoma_Falls" title="Boyoma Falls">Boyoma Falls</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chambeshi_River" title="Chambeshi River">Chambeshi River</a> in Zambia is generally taken as the source of the Congo in line with the accepted practice worldwide of using the longest tributary, as with the Nile River.\n</p><p>The Congo flows generally toward the northwest from <a href="/wiki/Kisangani" title="Kisangani">Kisangani</a> just below the Boyoma falls, then gradually bends southwestwards, passing by <a href="/wiki/Mbandaka" title="Mbandaka">Mbandaka</a>, joining with the <a href="/wiki/Ubangi_River" title="Ubangi River">Ubangi River</a>, and running into the <a href="/wiki/Pool_Malebo" title="Pool Malebo">Pool Malebo</a> (Stanley Pool). <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a> (formerly L&#233;opoldville) and <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville" title="Brazzaville">Brazzaville</a> are on opposite sides of the river at the Pool, where the river narrows and falls through a number of <a href="/wiki/Waterfall#Types_of_waterfalls" title="Waterfall">cataracts</a> in deep canyons (collectively known as the <a href="/wiki/Livingstone_Falls" title="Livingstone Falls">Livingstone Falls</a>), running by <a href="/wiki/Matadi" title="Matadi">Matadi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boma,_Kongo_Central" title="Boma, Kongo Central">Boma</a>, and into the sea at the small town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muanda" title="Muanda">Muanda</a>.\n</p><p>The Congo River Basin is one of the distinct physiographic sections of the larger Mid-African province, which in turn is part of the larger African massive physiographic division.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributaries_to_the_great_Congo_river"><a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">Tributaries</a> to the great Congo During the rainy season over 50,000 cubic metres (1,800,000&#160;cu&#160;ft) of water per second flow into the Atlantic Ocean. Opportunities for the Congo River and its tributaries to generate <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> are therefore enormous. Scientists have calculated that the entire <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo Basin</a> accounts for 13 percent of global <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> potential. This would provide sufficient power for all of <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-Saharan Africa</a>\'s electricity needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weetlogs.scilogs.be_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-weetlogs.scilogs.be-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Currently there are about forty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydropower_plant" title="Hydropower plant">hydropower plants</a> in the Congo Basin. The largest are the <a href="/wiki/Inga_dams" title="Inga dams">Inga dams</a>, about 200 kilometres (120&#160;mi) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>. The project was launched in the early 1970s, when the first dam was completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The plan as originally conceived called for the construction of five dams that would have had a total generating capacity of 34,500 megawatts. To date only the Inga I and Inga II dams have been built, generating 1,776 MW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weetlogs.scilogs.be_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-weetlogs.scilogs.be-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2005, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" title="Government-owned corporation">state-owned</a> power company, <a href="/wiki/Eskom" title="Eskom">Eskom</a>, announced a proposal to expand generation through improvements and the construction of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Inga_Dam" title="Grand Inga Dam">a new hydroelectric dam</a>. The project would bring the maximum output of the facility to 40 gigawatts (54,000,000&#160;hp), twice that of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is feared that these new hydroelectric dams could lead to the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of many of the fish species that are native to the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoughWaters_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-RoughWaters-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_history">Natural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="176" <a href="/wiki/Bonobo" title="Bonobo">bonobo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" title="Common chimpanzee">common chimpanzee</a> from their <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" href="/wiki/Allen%27s_swamp_monkey" title="Allen\'s swamp monkey">Allen\'s swamp monkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dryas_monkey" title="Dryas monkey">dryas monkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_genet" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In terms of aquatic life, the Congo River Basin has a very high <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">species richness</a>, and among the highest known concentrations of endemics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian2009_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian2009-19">[19]</a></sup> Until now, almost 700 fish species have been recorded from the Congo River Basin, and large sections remain virtually unstudied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SudanicCongoOubangi_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SudanicCongoOubangi-20">[20]</a></sup> This is by far the highest diversity of any African river system (in comparison, the next richest are the <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volta_River" title="Volta River">Volta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> with about 210, 140 and 130 fish species, respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winemiller2008_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winemiller2008-21">[21]</a></sup> Due to this and the great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological" title="Ecological">ecological</a> differences between the regions in the Congo basin, it is often divided into multiple <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregions</a> (instead of treating it as a single ecoregion). Among these ecoregions, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_Congo_Rapids" title="Lower Congo Rapids">Lower Congo Rapids</a> alone has more than 300 fish species, including approximately 80 endemics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoughWaters_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-RoughWaters-14">[14]</a></sup> while the southwestern part (<a href="/wiki/Kasai_River" title="Kasai River">Kasai Basin</a>) alone has more than 200 fish species, of which about a quarter are endemic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasai_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasai-22">[22]</a></sup> The dominant fish families &#8211; at least in parts of the river &#8211; are <a href="/wiki/Cyprinidae" title="Cyprinidae">Cyprinidae</a> (carp/cyprinids, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Labeo_simpsoni" title="Labeo simpsoni">Labeo simpsoni</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Mormyridae" title="Mormyridae">Mormyridae</a> (elephantfishes), <a href="/wiki/Alestidae" title="Alestidae">Alestidae</a> (African tetras), <a href="/wiki/Mochokidae" title="Mochokidae">Mochokidae</a> (squeaker catfishes), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cichlidae" title="Cichlidae">Cichlidae</a> (cichlids).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UpperLualaba_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-UpperLualaba-23">[23]</a></sup> Among the natives in the river is the huge, highly carnivorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_tigerfish" title="Giant tigerfish">giant tigerfish</a>. Three of the more unusual endemics are the whitish (non-<a href="/wiki/Biological_pigment" title="Biological pigment">pigmented</a>) and blind <i><a href="/wiki/Lamprologus_lethops" title="Lamprologus lethops">Lamprologus lethops</a></i>, which is believed to live as deep as 160 metres (520&#160;ft) below the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoughWaters_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-RoughWaters-14">[14]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterochromis_multidens" title="Heterochromis multidens">Heterochromis multidens</a></i>, which appears to be more closely related to cichlids of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> than other African cichlids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Caecobarbus_geertsii" title="Caecobarbus geertsii">Caecobarbus geertsii</a></i>, the only known <a href="/wiki/Cavefish" title="Cavefish">cavefish</a> in Central Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> There are also numerous endemic frogs and snails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UpperLualaba_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-UpperLualaba-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowerCongoRapids_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LowerCongoRapids-26">[26]</a></sup> Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_dam" title="Hydroelectric dam">hydroelectric dams</a> are planned on the river, and these may lead to the <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> of many of the endemics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoughWaters_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-RoughWaters-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several species of <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slender-snouted_crocodile" title="Slender-snouted crocodile">slender-snouted</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nile_crocodile" title="Nile crocodile">Nile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_crocodile" title="Dwarf crocodile">dwarf crocodile</a> are native to the Congo River Basin. <a href="/wiki/African_manatee" title="African manatee">African manatees</a> inhabit the lower parts of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congo_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congo_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="image" href="/wiki/File:Routes_of_European_explorers_in_Africa,_to_1853.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2041" data-file-width="1466" decoding="async" height="306" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Routes_of_European_explorers_in_Africa,_to_1853.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In this 1853 map of Africa, the remaining <i>Unexplored Region</i> essentially corresponds to the Congo basin</div></div></div>\n<p>The entire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_basin" title="Congo basin">Congo basin</a> is populated by <a href="/wiki/Bantu_peoples" title="Bantu peoples">Bantu peoples</a>, divided into several hundred ethnic or tribal groups (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_of_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Ethnic groups of the Democratic Republic of the Congo">ethnic groups of the Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>). <a href="/wiki/Bantu_expansion" title="Bantu expansion">Bantu expansion</a> is estimated to have reached the Middle Congo by about 500 BC, and the Upper Congo by the first century AD. Remnants of the aboriginal population displaced by the Bantu migration, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pygmies" title="Pygmies">Pygmies</a>/<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abatwa" title="Abatwa">Abatwa</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Ubangian_languages" title="Ubangian languages">Ubangian</a> phylum, remain in the remote forest areas of the Congo basin.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kongo" title="Kingdom of Kongo">Kingdom of Kongo</a> was formed around 1400 on the left banks of the lower Congo River. Its territorial control along the river remained limited to what corresponds to the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bas-Congo" title="Bas-Congo">Bas-Congo</a> province.  European exploration of the Congo begins in  1482, when Portuguese explorer <a href="/wiki/Diogo_C%C3%A3o" title="Diogo C&#227;o">Diogo C&#227;o</a> discovered the river estuary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-28">[28]</a></sup> (likely in August 1482), which he marked by a <a href="/wiki/Padr%C3%A3o" title="Padr&#227;o">Padr&#227;o</a>, or stone pillar (still existing, but only in fragments) erected on Shark Point. C&#227;o also sailed up the river for a short distance, establishing contact with the Kingdom of Congo. The full course of the river remained unknown throughout the early modern period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The upper Congo basin runs west of the <a href="/wiki/Albertine_Rift" title="Albertine Rift">Albertine Rift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-28">[28]</a></sup> Its connection to the Congo was unknown until 1877.\nThe extreme northeast of the Congo basin was reached by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nilotic_expansion" title="Nilotic expansion">Nilotic expansion</a> at some point between the 15th and 18th centuries, by the ancestors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Luo" title="Southern Luo">Southern Luo</a> speaking  <a href="/wiki/Alur_people" title="Alur people">Alur people</a>.\n<a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Lacerda" title="Francisco de Lacerda">Francisco de Lacerda</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Zambezi" title="Zambezi">Zambezi</a> reached the uppermost part of the Congo basin (the <a href="/wiki/Kazembe" title="Kazembe">Kazembe</a> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Luapula_River" title="Luapula River">Luapula</a> basin) in 1796.\n</p><p>The upper Congo River, known as the <a href="/wiki/Lualaba_River" title="Lualaba River">Lualaba</a> was first reached by the <a href="/wiki/Arab_slave_trade" title="Arab slave trade">Arab slave trade</a> by the 19th century. <a href="/wiki/Nyangwe" title="Nyangwe">Nyangwe</a> was founded as a slavers\' outpost around 1860.\n<a href="/wiki/David_Livingstone" title="David Livingstone">David Livingstone</a> was the first European to reach Nyangwe in March 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-28">[28]</a></sup> Livingstone proposed to prove that the Lualaba connected to the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a>, but on 15 July, he witnessed a massacre of about 400 Africans by Arab slavers in Nyangwe, which experience left him too horrified and shattered to continue his mission to find the sources of the Nile, so he turned back to  <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livingstone_1871_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livingstone_1871-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeal_1973_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeal_1973-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1308" data-file-width="1776" decoding="async" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg/220px-Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg/330px-Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg/440px-Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Advance_Column_of_the_Emin_Pasha_Relief_Expedition_1890.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Henry M Stanley with the officers of the Advance Column, Cairo, 1890. From the left: Dr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Heazle_Parke" title="Thomas Heazle Parke">Thomas Heazle Parke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Nelson" title="Robert H. Nelson">Robert H. Jephson</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The middle reaches of the Congo remained unexplored from either the east or west, until <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Henry Morton Stanley</a>\'s expedition of 1876&#8211;77.\nAt the time one of the last open questions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Africa" title="Exploration of Africa">exploration of Africa</a> (or indeed of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Age_of_exploration" title="Age of exploration">world</a>)\nwhether the Lualaba River fed the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> (Livingstone\'s theory), the Congo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> or even the <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger</a>.\nFinanced in 1874, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley%27s_first_trans-Africa_exploration" title="Henry Morton Stanley\'s first trans-Africa exploration">Stanley\'s first trans-Africa exploration</a>\nstarted in <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">Zanzibar</a>, and reached the Lualaba on October 17, 1876.\nOverland he reached <a href="/wiki/Nyangwe" title="Nyangwe">Nyangwe</a>, the centre of a lawless area containing cannibal tribes at which <a href="/wiki/Tippu_Tip" title="Tippu Tip">Tippu Tip</a> based his trade in slaves. Stanley managed to hire a force from Tippu Tip, to guard him for the next 150 kilometres (90&#160;mi) or so, for 90 days. The party left Nyangwe overland through the dense Matimba forest. On November 19 they reached the Lualaba again. Since the going through the forest was so heavy, Tippu Tip turned around with his party on December 28, leaving Stanley on his own, with 143 people, including 8 children and 16 women. They had 23 canoes. His first encounter with a local tribe was with the cannibal Wenya. In total Stanley would report 32 unfriendly meetings on the river, some violent, even though he attempted to negotiate a peaceful thoroughfare. But the tribes were wary as their only experience of outsiders was of slave traders.\n</p><p>On January 6, 1877, after 640 kilometres (400&#160;mi), they reached <a href="/wiki/Boyoma_Falls" title="Boyoma Falls">Boyoma Falls</a> (called Stanley Falls for some time after), consisting of seven cataracts spanning 100 kilometres (60&#160;mi) which they had to bypass overland. It took them to February 7 to reach the end of the falls. Here Stanley learned that the river was called <i>Ikuta Yacongo</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> proving to him that he had reached the Congo, and that the Lualaba did not feed the Nile.\n</p><p>From this point, the tribes were no longer cannibals, but possessed firearms, apparently as a result of Portuguese influence. Some four weeks and 1,900 kilometres (1,200&#160;mi) later he reached Stanley Pool (now <a href="/wiki/Pool_Malebo" title="Pool Malebo">Pool Malebo</a>), the site of the present day cities <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazzaville" title="Brazzaville">Brazzaville</a>. Further downstream were the <a href="/wiki/Livingstone_Falls" title="Livingstone Falls">Livingstone Falls</a>, misnamed as Livingstone had never been on the Congo: a series of 32 falls and rapids with a fall of 270 metres (900&#160;ft) over 350 kilometres (220&#160;mi).\nOn 15 March they started the descent of the falls, which took five months and cost numerous lives. From the Isangile Falls, five falls from the foot, they beached the canoes and <i>Lady Alice</i> and left the river, aiming for the Portuguese outpost of <a href="/wiki/Boma,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Boma, Democratic Republic of the Congo">Boma</a> via land. On August 3 they reached the hamlet Nsada. From there Stanley sent four men with letters forward to Boma, asking for food for his starving people. On August 7 relief came, being sent by representatives from the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> trading firm Hatton &amp; Cookson. On August 9 they reached Boma, 1,001 days since leaving Zanzibar on November 12, 1874. The party then consisted of 108 people, including three children born during the trip. Most probably (Stanley\'s own publications give inconsistent figures), he lost 132 people through disease, hunger, drowning, killing and desertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a> was founded as a trading post by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morton_Stanley" title="Henry Morton Stanley">Stanley</a> in 1881 and named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opoldville" title="L&#233;opoldville">L&#233;opoldville</a> in honour of <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II_of_Belgium" title="Leopold II of Belgium">Leopold II of Belgium</a>. The Congo basin was claimed by Leopold II as <a href="/wiki/Congo_Free_State" title="Congo Free State">Congo Free State</a> in 1885.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg/220px-Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg/330px-Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg/440px-Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monument_aux_pionniers_belges_au_Congo_001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Congo River Allegory by Thomas Vin&#231;otte.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges_in_the_Congo_Basin">Bridges in the Congo Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Congo_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bridges in the Congo Basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo_basin" title="Congo basin">Congo river basin</a> is notable for the lack of <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> crossing the main rivers, although there are a number of ferries available for crossing the great <i>Congo river</i> and the major <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributaries</a>. The main reasoning is the mere width of the Congo river and main rivers, and the second is the lack of funds to set up permanent river crossings, this is however slowly changing to the better.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges_on_the_Congo_proper_and_Lualaba">Bridges on the Congo proper and Lualaba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
