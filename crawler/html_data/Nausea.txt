title="Gastroenterology">Gastroenterology</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Nausea</b> is an unpleasant, diffuse sensation of unease and discomfort, often perceived as an urge to <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> While not painful, it can be a debilitating symptom if prolonged, and has been described as placing discomfort on the chest, upper abdomen, or back of the throat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>, the purpose of nausea is to discourage the person or animal from repeating whatever caused the unpleasantness. The memory of pain elicits safer or evasive actions; the memory of nausea elicits revulsion towards whatever was eaten before vomiting it up &#8212; even if it was not the cause of the nausea.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Nausea is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-specific_symptom" title="Non-specific symptom">non-specific symptom</a>, which means that it has many possible causes. Some common causes of nausea are <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dizziness" title="Dizziness">dizziness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Migraine" title="Migraine">migraine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fainting" title="Fainting">fainting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_blood_sugar" title="Low blood sugar">low blood sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a> (stomach infection) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">food poisoning</a>. Nausea is a side effect of many medications including chemotherapy, or <a href="/wiki/Morning_sickness" title="Morning sickness">morning sickness</a> in early pregnancy. Nausea may also be caused by <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disgust" title="Disgust">disgust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-S1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-S2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-S3-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Medications taken to prevent and treat nausea are called <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetics</a>. The most commonly prescribed antiemetics in the US are <a href="/wiki/Promethazine" title="Promethazine">promethazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metoclopramide" title="Metoclopramide">metoclopramide</a> and the newer, extremely effective <a href="/wiki/Ondansetron" title="Ondansetron">ondansetron</a>. <i>nautia</i>, <a href="/wiki/Motion_sickness" title="Motion sickness">motion sickness</a>, "feeling sick or queasy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">Gastrointestinal infections</a> (37%) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">food poisoning</a> are the two most common causes of acute nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> Side effects from medications (3%) and pregnancy are also relatively frequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> There are many causes of chronic nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> Nausea and vomiting remain undiagnosed in 10% of the cases. Aside from morning sickness, there are no gender differences in complaints of nausea. After childhood, doctor consultations decrease steadily with age. Only a fraction of one percent of doctor visits by those over 65 are due to nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFPHelena07_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFPHelena07-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastrointestinal">Gastrointestinal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gastrointestinal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gastrointestinal infection is one of the most common causes of acute nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> Chronic nausea may be the presentation of many gastrointestinal disorders, occasionally as the major symptom, such as <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigestion" title="Indigestion">functional dyspepsia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastroparesis" title="Gastroparesis">gastroparesis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-celiac_gluten_sensitivity" title="Non-celiac gluten sensitivity">non-celiac gluten sensitivity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn\'s disease">Crohn\'s disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis" title="Hepatitis">hepatitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract#Upper_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">upper gastrointestinal</a> malignancy, and <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_cancer" title="Pancreatic cancer">pancreatic cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VoltaCaio2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-VoltaCaio2017-9">[9]</a></sup> Uncomplicated <i><a href="/wiki/Helicobacter_pylori" title="Helicobacter pylori">Helicobacter pylori</a></i> infection does not cause chronic nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_poisoning">Food poisoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Food poisoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">Food poisoning</a> usually causes an abrupt onset of nausea and vomiting one to six hours after ingestion of contaminated food and lasts for one to two days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA07_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-USA07-10">[10]</a></sup>  It is due to toxins produced by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> in food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA07_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-USA07-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medications">Medications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many medications can potentially cause nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA07_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-USA07-10">[10]</a></sup>  Some of the most frequently associated include cytotoxic <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> regimens for cancer and other diseases, and <a href="/wiki/General_anaesthesia" title="General anaesthesia">general anaesthetic agents</a>. An old cure for migraine, <a href="/wiki/Ergotamine" title="Ergotamine">ergotamine</a>, is well known to cause devastating nausea in some patients; a person using it for the first time will be prescribed an <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetic</a> for relief if needed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pregnancy">Pregnancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pregnancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Nausea or "<a href="/wiki/Morning_sickness" title="Morning sickness">morning sickness</a>" is common during early <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> but may occasionally continue into the second and third trimesters. In the first trimester nearly 80% of women have some degree of nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Pregnancy should therefore be considered as a possible cause of nausea in any women of child bearing age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USA07_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-USA07-10">[10]</a></sup>  While usually it is mild and self-limiting, severe cases known as <a href="/wiki/Hyperemesis_gravidarum" title="Hyperemesis gravidarum">hyperemesis gravidarum</a> may require treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preg07_12-0"><a abuse">Alcohol</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_approach">Diagnostic approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diagnostic approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patient_history">Patient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Patient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Taking a thorough patient history may reveal important clues to the cause of nausea and vomiting. If the patient\'s symptoms have an acute onset, then drugs, toxins, and infections are likely. In contrast, a long-standing history of nausea will point towards a chronic illness as the culprit. The timing of nausea and vomiting after eating food is an important factor to pay attention to. Symptoms that occur within an hour of eating may indicate an obstruction proximal to the small intestine, such as <a href="/wiki/Gastroparesis" title="Gastroparesis">gastroparesis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pyloric_stenosis" title="Pyloric stenosis">pyloric stenosis</a>. An obstruction further down in the intestine or colon will cause delayed vomiting. An infectious cause of nausea and vomiting such as gastroenteritis may present several hours to days after the food was ingested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H2015_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-H2015-15">[15]</a></sup> The contents of the emesis is a valuable clue towards determining the cause. Bits of fecal matter in the emesis indicate obstruction in the distal intestine or the colon. Emesis that is of a bilious nature (greenish in color) localizes the obstruction to a point past the stomach. Emesis of undigested food points to an obstruction prior to the gastric outlet, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achalasia" title="Achalasia">achalasia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zenker%27s_diverticulum" title="Zenker\'s diverticulum">Zenker\'s diverticulum</a>. If patient experiences reduced abdominal pain after vomiting, then obstruction is a likely etiology. However, vomiting does not relieve the pain brought on by <a href="/wiki/Pancreatitis" title="Pancreatitis">pancreatitis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cholecystitis" title="Cholecystitis">cholecystitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H2015_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-H2015-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_exam">Physical exam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Physical exam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It is important to watch out for signs of dehydration, such as <a href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypotension" title="Orthostatic hypotension">orthostatic hypotension</a> and loss of skin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turgor" title="Turgor">turgor</a>. Auscultation of the abdomen can produce several clues to the cause of nausea and vomiting. A high-pitched tinkling sound indicates possible <a href="/wiki/Bowel_obstruction" title="Bowel obstruction">bowel obstruction</a>, while a splashing "<a href="/wiki/Succussion_splash" title="Succussion splash">succussion</a>" sound is more indicative of gastric outlet obstruction. Eliciting pain on the abdominal exam when pressing on the patient may indicate an inflammatory process. Signs such as papilledema, visual field losses, or focal neurological deficits are red flag signs for elevated intracranial pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H2015_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-H2015-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostic_testing">Diagnostic testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Diagnostic testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When a history and physical exam are not enough to determine the cause of nausea and vomiting, certain diagnostic tests may prove useful. A chemistry panel would be useful for electrolyte and metabolic abnormalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> Liver function tests and lipase would identify pancreaticobiliary diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16">[16]</a></sup> Abdominal X-rays showing air-fluid levels indicate bowel obstruction, while an X-ray showing air-filled bowel loops are more indicative of <a href="/wiki/Ileus" title="Ileus">ileus</a>. More advanced imaging and procedures may be necessary, such as a CT scan, upper endoscopy, colonoscopy, barium enema, or MRI. Abnormal GI motility can be assessed using specific tests like gastric scintigraphy, wireless motility capsules, and small-intestinal manometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-H2015_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-H2015-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Research on nausea and vomiting has relied on using animal models to mimic the anatomy and neuropharmacologic features of the human body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The physiologic mechanism of nausea is a complex process that has yet to be fully elucidated. There are four general pathways that are activated by specific triggers in the human body that go on to create the sensation of nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">Central nervous system</a> (CNS): Stimuli can affect areas of the CNS including the cerebral cortex and the limbic system. These areas are activated by elevated intracranial pressure, irritation of the meninges (i.e. blood or infection), and extreme emotional triggers such as anxiety.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor_trigger_zone" title="Chemoreceptor trigger zone">Chemoreceptor trigger zone</a> (CTZ): The CTZ is located in the <a href="/wiki/Area_postrema" title="Area postrema">area postrema</a> in the floor of the fourth ventricle within the brain. This area is outside the blood brain barrier, and is therefore readily exposed to substances circulating through the blood and cerebral spinal fluid. Common triggers of the CTZ include metabolic abnormalities, toxins, and medications. Activation of the CTZ is mediated by <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> (D2) receptors, <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a> (5HT3) receptors, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurokinin" title="Neurokinin">neurokinin</a> receptors (NK1).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">Vestibular system</a>: This system is activated by disturbances to the vestibular apparatus in the inner ear. These include movements that cause motion sickness and dizziness. This pathway is triggered via <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a> (H1) receptors and <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a> (ACh) receptors.</li>\n<li>Peripheral Pathways: These pathways are triggered via chemoreceptors and mechanoreceptors in the gastrointestinal tract, as well as other organs such as the heart and kidneys. Common activators of these pathways include toxins present in the gastrointestinal lumen and distension of the gastrointestinal lumen from blockage or dysmotility of the bowels. Signals from these pathways travel via multiple neural tracts including the <a href="/wiki/Vagus_nerve" title="Vagus nerve">vagus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glossopharyngeal_nerve" title="Glossopharyngeal nerve">glossopharyngeal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Splanchnic" title="Splanchnic">splanchnic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system" title="Sympathetic nervous system">sympathetic</a> nerves.</li></ul>\n<p>Signals from any of these pathways then travel to the brainstem, activating several structures including the <a href="/wiki/Solitary_nucleus" title="Solitary nucleus">nucleus of the solitary tract</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_nucleus_of_vagus_nerve" title="Dorsal nucleus of vagus nerve">dorsal motor nucleus</a> of the vagus, and <a href="/wiki/Central_pattern_generator" title="Central pattern generator">central pattern generator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These structures go on to signal various downstream effects of nausea and vomiting. The body\'s motor muscle responses involve halting the muscles of the gastrointestinal tract, and in fact causing reversed propulsion of gastric contents towards the mouth while increasing abdominal muscle contraction. Autonomic effects involve increased salivation and the sensation of feeling faint that often occurs with nausea and vomiting.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>If dehydration is present due to loss of fluids from severe vomiting, <a href="/wiki/Fluid_replacement" title="Fluid replacement">rehydration</a> with oral electrolyte solutions is preferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup>  If this is not effective or possible, intravenous rehydration may be required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-1">[1]</a></sup> Medical care is recommended if: a person cannot keep any liquids down, has symptoms more than 2 days, is weak, has a fever, has stomach pain, vomits more than two times in a day or does not urinate for more than 8 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medications_2">Medications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Medications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many pharmacologic medications are available for the treatment of nausea. There is no medication that is clearly superior to other medications for all cases of nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The choice of <a href="/wiki/Antiemetic" title="Antiemetic">antiemetic</a> medication may be based on the situation during which the person experiences nausea. For people with motion sickness and vertigo, antihistamines and anticholinergics such as <a href="/wiki/Meclizine" title="Meclizine">meclizine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyoscine_hydrobromide" title="Hyoscine hydrobromide">scopolamine</a> are particularly effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> Nausea and vomiting associated with migraine headaches respond best to dopamine antagonists such as <a href="/wiki/Metoclopramide" title="Metoclopramide">metoclopramide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prochlorperazine" title="Prochlorperazine">prochlorperazine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorpromazine" title="Chlorpromazine">chlorpromazine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> In cases of gastroenteritis, serotonin antagonists such as <a href="/wiki/Ondansetron" title="Ondansetron">ondansetron</a> were found to suppress nausea and vomiting, as well as reduce the need for IV fluid resuscitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> The combination of <a href="/wiki/Pyridoxine" title="Pyridoxine">pyridoxine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doxylamine" title="Doxylamine">doxylamine</a> is the first line treatment for pregnancy-related nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dimenhydrinate" title="Dimenhydrinate">Dimenhydrinate</a> is an inexpensive and effective over the counter medication for preventing postoperative nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Other factors to consider when choosing an antiemetic medication include the person\'s preference, side-effect profile, and cost.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_medicine">Alternative medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Alternative medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In certain people, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannabinoids" title="Cannabinoids">cannabinoids</a> may be effective in reducing chemotherapy associated nausea and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Several studies have demonstrated the therapeutic effects of cannabinoids for nausea and vomiting in the advanced stages of illnesses such as cancer and AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In hospital settings topical anti-nausea gels are not indicated because of lack of research backing their efficacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAHPMfive_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAHPMfive-28">[28]</a></sup> Topical gels containing <a href="/wiki/Lorazepam" title="Lorazepam">lorazepam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diphenhydramine" title="Diphenhydramine">diphenhydramine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haloperidol" title="Haloperidol">haloperidol</a> are sometimes used for nausea but are not equivalent to more established therapies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAHPMfive_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-AAHPMfive-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">Ginger</a> has also been shown to be potentially effective in treating several types of nausea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medind_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-medind-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ernst-meta_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ernst-meta-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tentative evidence supports <a href="/wiki/Acupuncture" title="Acupuncture">acupuncture</a> at point PC6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeEtAl2015_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeeEtAl2015-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prognosis">Prognosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Prognosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The outlook depends on the cause. Most people recover within few hours or a day. While short-term nausea and vomiting are generally harmless, they may sometimes indicate a more serious condition. When associated with prolonged vomiting, it may lead to <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a> or dangerous <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> imbalances or both. Repeated intentional vomiting, characteristic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulimia" title="Bulimia">bulimia</a>, can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_acid" title="Stomach acid">stomach acid</a> to wear away at the <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">enamel</a> in teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nausea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Nausea and or <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> is the main complaint in 1.6% of visits to family physicians in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFPHelena07_8-1"><a
