href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1057;&#1086;&#1102;&#769;&#1079;</span>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Russian" title="Help:IPA/Russian">[s&#592;&#712;jus]</a></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a> by the Korolev Design Bureau (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. The Soyuz succeeded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voskhod_spacecraft" title="Voskhod spacecraft">Voskhod spacecraft</a> and was originally built as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_manned_lunar_programs" title="Soviet manned lunar programs">Soviet manned lunar programs</a>. The Soyuz spacecraft is launched on a <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz rocket</a>, the most reliable <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> in the world to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Soyuz rocket design is based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok_(rocket)" title="Vostok (rocket)">Vostok</a> launcher, which in turn was based on the 8K74 or <a href="/wiki/R-7A_Semyorka" title="R-7A Semyorka">R-7A Semyorka</a>, a Soviet <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>. All Soyuz spacecraft are launched from the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>. class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first Soyuz flight was unmanned and started on November 28, 1966. The first Soyuz mission with a crew, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">Soyuz 1</a>, launched on 23 April 1967 but ended with a crash due to a parachute failure, killing <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">cosmonaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Komarov" title="Vladimir Komarov">Vladimir Komarov</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_2" title="Soyuz 2">following flight</a> was unmanned. <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_3" title="Soyuz 3">Soyuz 3</a>, launched on October 26, 1968, became the program\'s first successful manned mission. The only other flight to suffer a fatal accident, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a>, killed its crew of three when the cabin depressurized prematurely just before reentry. These were the only humans to date to have died above the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite these early incidents, Soyuz is widely considered the world\'s safest, most cost-effective human spaceflight vehicle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> established by its unparalleled length of operational history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Soyuz spacecraft were used to carry <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">cosmonauts</a> to and from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut" title="Salyut">Salyut</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> Soviet space stations, and are now used for transport to and from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS). At least one Soyuz spacecraft is docked to ISS at all times for use as an escape craft in the event of an emergency. The spacecraft is intended to be replaced by the six-person <a href="/wiki/Federation_(spacecraft)" title="Federation (spacecraft)">Federation spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg/220px-Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg/330px-Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg/440px-Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_parts.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing the three elements of the Soyuz TMA spacecraft.</div></div></div>\n<p>A Soyuz spacecraft consists of three parts (from front to back):\n</p>\n<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">spheroid</a> <a href="/wiki/Orbital_module" title="Orbital module">orbital module</a>, which provides accommodation for the crew during their mission;</li>\n<li>A small aerodynamic <a href="/wiki/Reentry_capsule" title="Reentry capsule">reentry module</a>, which returns the crew to Earth;</li>\n<li>A cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Service_module" title="Service module">service module</a> with solar panels attached, which contains the instruments and engines.</li></ul>\n<p>The orbital and service modules are single-use and are destroyed upon reentry in the atmosphere. Though this might seem wasteful, it reduces the amount of heat shielding required for reentry, saving mass compared to designs containing all of the living space and life support in a single capsule. This allows smaller rockets to launch the spacecraft or can be used to increase the habitable space available to the crew (6.2&#160;m<sup>3</sup> in Apollo CM vs 7.5&#160;m<sup>3</sup>  in Soyuz) in the mass budget. The orbital and reentry portions are habitable living space, with the service module containing the fuel, main engines and instrumentation.\n</p><p>Soyuz can carry up to three crew members and provide life support for about 30 person days. The life support system provides a nitrogen/oxygen atmosphere at sea level partial pressures. The atmosphere is regenerated through <a href="/wiki/Potassium_superoxide" title="Potassium superoxide">potassium superoxide</a> (KO<sub>2</sub>) cylinders, which absorb most of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> produced by the crew and regenerates the oxygen, and <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">lithium hydroxide</a> (LiOH) cylinders which absorb leftover CO<sub>2</sub>.\n</p><p>The vehicle is protected during launch by a <a href="/wiki/Payload_fairing" title="Payload fairing">payload fairing</a>, which is jettisoned along with the SAS at &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> minutes into launch. It has an automatic docking system. The ship can be operated automatically, or by a pilot independently of ground control.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_escape_system">Launch escape system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch escape system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">Vostok</a> spacecraft utilized an ejector seat to bail out the cosmonaut in the event of a low-altitude launch failure, as well as during reentry, however it would probably have been ineffective in the first 20 seconds after liftoff when the altitude would be too low for the parachute to deploy. Inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a> LES, Soviet designers began work on a similar system in 1962. This included developing a complex sensing system to monitor various launch vehicle parameters and trigger an abort if a booster malfunction occurred. Based on data from R-7 launches over the years, engineers developed a list of the most likely failure modes for the vehicle and could narrow down abort conditions to premature separation of a strap-on booster, low engine thrust, loss of combustion chamber pressure, or loss of booster guidance. The Spacecraft Abort System (SAS; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1057;&#1080;&#1089;&#1090;&#1077;&#1084;&#1072; &#1040;&#1074;&#1072;&#1088;&#1080;&#1081;&#1085;&#1086;&#1075;&#1086; &#1057;&#1087;&#1072;&#1089;&#1077;&#1085;&#1080;&#1103;</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">translit.</a>&#160;</small><i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">Sistema Avarijnogo Spaseniya</i>) could also be manually activated from the ground, but unlike American spacecraft, there was no way for the cosmonauts to trigger it themselves.\n</p><p>Since it turned out to be almost impossible to separate the entire payload shroud from the Soyuz service module cleanly, the decision was made to have the shroud split between the service module and descent module during an abort. Four folding stabilizers were added to improve aerodynamic stability during ascent. Two test runs of the SAS were carried out in 1966-67.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shayler2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shayler2009-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The basic design of the SAS has remained almost unchanged in 50 years of use and all Soyuz launches carry it. The only modification was in 1972 when the aerodynamic fairing over the SAS motor nozzles was removed for weight-saving reasons as the redesigned Soyuz 7K-T spacecraft carried extra life support equipment. The unmanned Progress resupply ferry has a dummy escape tower and removes the stabilizer fins from the payload shroud. There have been three failed launches of a manned Soyuz vehicle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_18-1" title="Soyuz 18-1">Soyuz 18-1</a> in 1975, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_T-10-1" title="Soyuz T-10-1">Soyuz T-10-1</a>  in 1983 and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_MS-10" title="Soyuz MS-10">Soyuz MS-10</a> in October 2018. The 1975 failure was aborted after escape tower jettison. In 1983, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_T-10-1" title="Soyuz T-10-1">Soyuz T-10-1</a>\'s SAS successfully rescued the cosmonauts from an on-pad fire and explosion of the launch vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Most recently in 2018, the SAS sub-system in the payload shroud of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_MS-10" title="Soyuz MS-10">Soyuz MS-10</a> successfully rescued the cosmonauts from a rocket failure 2 minutes and 45 second after liftoff after the escape tower had already been jettisoned.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_module">Orbital module</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbital module">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_module" title="Orbital module">Orbital module</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg/220px-Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg/330px-Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg/440px-Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_orbital_module.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soyuz spacecraft\'s Orbital Module</div></div></div>\n<p>The forepart of the spacecraft is the Orbital Module (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1073;&#1099;&#1090;&#1086;&#1074;&#1086;&#1081; &#1086;&#1090;&#1089;&#1077;&#1082;</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">translit.</a>&#160;</small><i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">bytovoi otsek</i>), also known as habitation section. It houses all the equipment that will not be needed for reentry, such as experiments, cameras or cargo. The module also contains a toilet, docking avionics and communications gear. Internal volume is 6&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (212&#160;cu&#160;ft), living space 5&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (177&#160;cu&#160;ft). On the latest Soyuz versions (since Soyuz TM), a small window was introduced, providing the crew with a forward view.\n</p><p>A hatch between it and the Descent Module can be closed so as to isolate it to act as an airlock if needed, crew members exiting through its side port (near the descent module). On the launch pad, the crew enter the spacecraft through this port.\n</p><p>This separation also lets the Orbital Module be customized to the mission with less risk to the life-critical descent module. The convention of orientation in a <a href="/wiki/Micro-g_environment" title="Micro-g environment">micro-g environment</a> differs from that of the Descent Module, as crew members stand or sit with their heads to the docking port. Also the rescue of the crew whilst on the launch pad or with the SAS system is complicated because of the orbital module.\n</p><p>Separation of the Orbital Module is critical for a safe landing; without separation of the Orbital Module, it is not possible for the crew to survive landing in the Descent Module. This is because the Orbital Module would interfere with proper deployment of the Descent Module\'s parachutes, and the extra mass exceeds the capability of the main parachute and braking engines to provide a safe soft landing speed. In view of this, the Orbital Module was separated before the ignition of the return engine until the late 1980s. This guaranteed that the Descent Module and Orbital Module would be separated before the Descent Module was placed in a reentry trajectory. However, after the problematic landing of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-5" title="Soyuz TM-5">Soyuz TM-5</a> in September 1988 this procedure was changed and the Orbital Module is now separated after the return maneuver. This change was made as the TM-5 crew could not deorbit for 24 hours after they jettisoned their Orbital Module, which contained their sanitation facilities and the docking collar needed to attach to MIR. The risk of not being able to separate the Orbital Module is effectively judged to be less than the risk of needing the facilities in it, following a failed deorbit.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent_module">Descent module</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Descent module">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descent_module" title="Descent module">Descent module</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg/220px-Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg/330px-Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg/440px-Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_descent_module.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soyuz spacecraft\'s Descent Module</div></div></div>\n<p>The Descent Module (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1057;&#1087;&#1091;&#1089;&#1082;&#1072;&#769;&#1077;&#1084;&#1099;&#1081; &#1040;&#1087;&#1087;&#1072;&#1088;&#1072;&#769;&#1090;</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">tr.</a></small> <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian language text">Spusk&#225;yemy Appar&#225;t</i>), also known as a reentry capsule, is used for launch and the journey back to Earth. Half of the Descent Module is covered by a heat-resistant covering to protect it during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reentry" title="Reentry">reentry</a>; this half faces the Earth during reentry. It is slowed initially by the atmosphere, then by a braking parachute, followed by the main parachute which slows the craft for landing. At one meter above the ground, solid-fuel braking engines mounted behind the <a href="/wiki/Heat_shield" title="Heat shield">heat shield</a> are fired to give a soft landing. One of the design requirements for the Descent Module was for it to have the highest possible volumetric efficiency (internal volume divided by hull area). The best shape for this is a sphere &#8212; as the pioneering <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)#Design" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">Vostok</a> spacecraft\'s Descent Module used &#8212; but such a shape can provide no lift, which results in a purely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_reentry" title="Ballistic reentry">ballistic reentry</a>. Ballistic reentries are hard on the occupants due to high deceleration and cannot be steered beyond their initial deorbit burn. That is why it was decided to go with the "headlight" shape that the Soyuz uses &#8211; a hemispherical forward area joined by a barely angled (seven degrees) conical section to a classic spherical section heat shield. This shape allows a small amount of lift to be generated due to the unequal weight distribution. The nickname was thought up at a time when nearly every headlight was circular. The small dimensions of the Descent Module led to it having only two-man crews after the death of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> crew. The later Soyuz T spacecraft solved this issue. Internal volume of Soyuz SA is 4&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (141&#160;cu&#160;ft); 2.5&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (88&#160;cu&#160;ft) is usable for crew (living space).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_module">Service module</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Service module">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Service_module" title="Service module">Service module</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg/220px-Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg/330px-Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg/440px-Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz-TMA_propulsion_module.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soyuz spacecraft\'s Instrumentation/Propulsion Module</div></div></div>\n<p>At the back of the vehicle is the Service Module (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1087;&#1088;&#1080;&#1073;&#1086;&#769;&#1088;&#1085;&#1086;-&#1072;&#1075;&#1088;&#1077;&#1075;&#1072;&#769;&#1090;&#1085;&#1099;&#1081; &#1086;&#1090;&#1089;&#1077;&#769;&#1082;</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Russian" title="Romanization of Russian">tr.</a></small> <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian language text">prib&#243;rno-agreg&#225;tny ots&#233;k</i>). It has a pressurized container shaped like a bulging can (instrumentation compartment, <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">priborniy otsek</i>) that contains systems for temperature control, electric power supply, long-range <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> communications, radio telemetry, and instruments for orientation and control. A non-pressurized part of the Service Module (propulsion compartment, <i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">agregatniy otsek</i>) contains the main engine and a liquid-fuelled <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">propulsion system</a> for maneuvering in orbit and initiating the descent back to Earth. The ship also has a system of low-thrust engines for orientation, attached to the intermediate compartment (<i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">perekhodnoi otsek</i>). Outside the Service Module are the sensors for the orientation system and the solar array, which is oriented towards the Sun by rotating the ship. An incomplete separation between the Service and Reentry Modules led to emergency situations during <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_5" title="Soyuz 5">Soyuz 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-10" title="Soyuz TMA-10">Soyuz TMA-10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-11" title="Soyuz TMA-11">Soyuz TMA-11</a>, which led to an incorrect reentry orientation (crew ingress hatch first). The failure of several explosive bolts did not cut the connection between the Service/Reentry Modules on the latter two flights.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reentry_procedure">Reentry procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reentry procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Soyuz uses a method similar to the US <a href="/wiki/Apollo_command_and_service_module" title="Apollo command and service module">Apollo command and  service module</a> to deorbit itself. The spacecraft is turned engine-forward and the main engine is fired for deorbiting on the far side of Earth ahead of its planned landing site. This requires the least propellant for <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_entry" title="Atmospheric entry">reentry</a>; the spacecraft travels on an elliptical <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">Hohmann transfer orbit</a> to the entry interface point where atmospheric drag slows it enough to fall out of orbit.\n</p><p>Early Soyuz spacecraft would then have the Service and Orbital Modules detach simultaneously from the Descent Module. As they are connected by tubing and electrical cables to the Descent Module, this would aid in their separation and avoid having the Descent Module alter its orientation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Later Soyuz spacecraft detached the Orbital Module before firing the main engine, which saved propellant. Since the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-5" title="Soyuz TM-5">Soyuz TM-5</a> landing issue, the Orbital Module is once again detached only after the reentry firing, which led to (but did not cause) emergency situations of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-10" title="Soyuz TMA-10">Soyuz TMA-10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-11" title="Soyuz TMA-11">TMA-11</a>. The Orbital Module cannot remain in orbit as an addition to a space station, as the airlock hatch between the Orbital and Reentry Modules is a part of the Reentry Module, and the Orbital Module therefore depressurizes after separation.\n</p><p>Reentry firing is usually done on the "dawn" side of the Earth, so that the spacecraft can be seen by recovery helicopters as it descends in the evening twilight, illuminated by the Sun when it is above the shadow of the Earth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Soyuz craft is designed to come down on land, usually somewhere in the deserts of Kazakhstan in central Asia. This is in contrast to early US manned spacecraft, which splashed down in the ocean.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_systems">Spacecraft systems</span><span <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_A-B-V" title="Soyuz A-B-V">Soyuz A-B-V</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a> initially promoted the Soyuz A-B-V circumlunar complex (<i>7K-9K-11K</i>) concept (also known as L1) in which a two-man craft Soyuz 7K would rendezvous with other components (9K and 11K) in Earth orbit to assemble a lunar excursion vehicle, the components being delivered by the proven <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">R-7 rocket</a>.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_generation">First generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: First generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_7K-OK(A)_drawing.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="400" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_7K-OK(A)_drawing.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soyuz 7K-OK(A) spacecraft with an active docking unit.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="216" data-file-width="387" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg/330px-RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg/440px-RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RP1357_p22_Salyut_1-type_Soyuz.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soyuz 7K-OKS for Salyut space stations.</div></div></div>\n<p>The manned Soyuz spacecraft can be classified into design generations. <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">Soyuz 1</a> through <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> (1967&#8211;1971) were first-generation vehicles, carrying a crew of up to three without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">spacesuits</a> and distinguished from those following by their bent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a> and their use of the <a href="/wiki/Igla_(spacecraft_docking_system)" title="Igla (spacecraft docking system)">Igla</a> automatic docking navigation system, which required special radar antennas. This first generation encompassed the original <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-OK" title="Soyuz 7K-OK">Soyuz 7K-OK</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-OKS" title="Soyuz 7K-OKS">Soyuz 7K-OKS</a> for docking with the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a> space station. The probe and drogue docking system permitted internal transfer of cosmonauts from the Soyuz to the station.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L1" title="Soyuz 7K-L1">Soyuz 7K-L1</a> was designed to launch a crew from the Earth to circle the moon, and was the primary hope for a Soviet circumlunar flight. It had several test flights in the <a href="/wiki/Zond_program" title="Zond program">Zond program</a> from 1967&#8211;1970 (<a href="/wiki/Zond_4" title="Zond 4">Zond 4</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zond_8" title="Zond 8">Zond 8</a>), which produced multiple failures in the 7K-L1\'s reentry systems. The remaining 7K-L1s were scrapped. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-L3" title="Soyuz 7K-L3">Soyuz 7K-L3</a> was designed and developed in parallel to the Soyuz 7K-L1, but was also scrapped. <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">Soyuz 1</a> was plagued with technical issues, and cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Komarov" title="Vladimir Komarov">Vladimir Komarov</a> was killed when the spacecraft crashed during its return to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. This was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_accidents_and_incidents" title="Space accidents and incidents">in-flight fatality</a> in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_spaceflight" title="History of spaceflight">history of spaceflight</a>.\n</p><p>The next manned version of the Soyuz was the Soyuz 7K-OKS. It was designed for <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> flights and had a docking port that allowed internal transfer between spacecraft. The Soyuz 7K-OKS had two manned flights, both in 1971. <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a>, the second flight, depressurized upon reentry, killing its three-man crew.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_generation">Second generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Second generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="195" data-file-width="359" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg/220px-RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg/330px-RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg/440px-RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RP1357_p24_Soyuz_Ferry.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Upgraded Soyuz 7K-T version.</div></div></div>\n<p>The second generation, called <i>Soyuz Ferry</i> or <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-T" title="Soyuz 7K-T">Soyuz 7K-T</a>, comprised <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_12" title="Soyuz 12">Soyuz 12</a> through <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_40" title="Soyuz 40">Soyuz 40</a> (1973&#8211;1981).\n</p><p>It was developed out of the <a href="/wiki/Military_Soyuz" title="Military Soyuz">military Soyuz</a> concepts studied in previous years and was capable of carrying 2 cosmonauts with <a href="/wiki/Sokol_space_suit" title="Sokol space suit">Sokol space suits</a> (after the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> accident). Several models were planned, but none actually flew in space. These versions were named <i><a href="/wiki/Soyuz_P" title="Soyuz P">Soyuz P</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_PPK&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz PPK (page does not exist)">Soyuz PPK</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_R&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz R (page does not exist)">Soyuz R</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-VI" title="Soyuz 7K-VI">Soyuz 7K-VI</a></i>, and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_OIS&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soyuz OIS (page does not exist)">Soyuz OIS</a></i> (Orbital Research Station).\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-T/A9" title="Soyuz 7K-T/A9">Soyuz 7K-T/A9</a> version was used for the flights to the military <a href="/wiki/Almaz" title="Almaz">Almaz</a> space station.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Soyuz_7K-TM" title="Soyuz 7K-TM">Soyuz 7K-TM</a> was the spacecraft used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo-Soyuz Test Project">Apollo-Soyuz Test Project</a> in 1975, which saw the first and only docking of a Soyuz spacecraft with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Apollo Command/Service Module</a>. It was also flown in 1976 for the Earth-science mission, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_22" title="Soyuz 22">Soyuz 22</a>. Soyuz 7K-TM served as a technological bridge to the third generation.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_generation">Third generation</span><span
