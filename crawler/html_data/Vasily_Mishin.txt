search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox biography vcard" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold"><div class="fn" style="display:inline">Vasily Mishin</div></th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a class="image" Buvalyne, <a href="/wiki/Pavlovo-Posadsky_District" title="Pavlovo-Posadsky District">Pavlovo-Posadsky District</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moscow_region" title="Moscow region">Moscow region</a>, Russia, and studied mathematics at the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Aviation_Institute" title="Moscow Aviation Institute">Moscow Aviation Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mishin was a Soviet rocket scientist and one of the first Soviet specialists to see <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> Germany\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-2" title="V-2">V-2</a> facilities at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, along with others such as <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergey Korolev</a>, who preceded him as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">OKB-1</a> design bureau head, and <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Glushko" title="Valentin Glushko">Valentin Glushko</a>, who succeeded him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mishin worked with Korolev as his deputy in the Experimental Design Bureau<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> working on projects such as the development of the first Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBM</a> as well in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sputnik_program" title="Sputnik program">Sputnik</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Vostok_programme" title="Vostok programme">Vostok</a> programs. He became head of Korolev\'s OKB-1 design bureau and was the Chief Designer after Korolev\'s death in 1966, during surgery to remove a tumor from Korolev\'s colon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>  He inherited the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N1_rocket" title="N1 rocket">N1 rocket</a> program, intended to land a man on the Moon, but which turned out to be fatally flawed (largely due to lack of adequate funding).\n</p><p>N1 development began on September 14, 1956, a decade before Mishin took control. It was selected for a lunar landing mission,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which required a design capable of putting ninety-five tons of cargo into orbit, up from fifty and later seventy-five ton requirements earlier in development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Under Korolev, a precedent of forgoing much of the usual <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine_test_facility" title="Rocket engine test facility">ground testing</a> had been begun. According to Korolev, this was because proper facilities would not be funded, and it would also allow for earlier test flights. Some of the failures Mishin faced during his leadership could have been avoided if further testing had been conducted at this stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> To handle engine failures, the <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)#Control_system" title="N1 (rocket)">KORD</a> system was created under Mishin. To prevent the rocket from having uneven flight that would result from the unbalanced thrust caused by a malfunctioning motor, the faulty motor and the motor opposite it in the rocket base would be turned off. KORD would also make the calculations necessary to compensate for the missing motors, which would allow the same flight path to be maintained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The N1, despite its necessity for planned missions, was never successfully flown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The first test flight, on February 3, 1969, had internal plumbing issues which led to a fire one minute in. It did, however, demonstrate the KORD system working successfully as well as proper deployment of the ejection safety system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The second launch, on July 3, experienced failure seconds after ignition, causing the rocket to fall back on the Launchpad and create significant damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The third N1 launch occurred on June 22, 1971, after improvements were made to KORD, the cabling, and fuel pumps, and the addition of an extinguishment system and filters. Before the launch, the individual engines were further tested and the Launchpad was repaired. For the first time all thirty engines of the first stage fired successfully, which was the cause of the failure of the flight. When all engines fired together, it created unexpectedly high <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">roll</a> (rotation along the axis of thrust), which was beyond the strength of the compensating <a href="/wiki/Vernier_thruster" title="Vernier thruster">vernier engines</a>, designed to keep stable flight. This was another failure that might have been prevented with proper ground testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the fourth and what would become the final flight of the N1, further refinements were made, including four additional vernier engines, additional heat shielding for internal components, a new digital control system, and additional sensors paired with a high speed relay system. The Soviet Space program was now eyeing creating a base on the moon, but first needed to finally succeed with this design. The launch was on November 23, 1972, with a Mishin approved flight plan to orbit the moon forty-two times, with flight activities such as taking pictures of future landing sites, before returning to earth on December 4. The rocket preceded farther than its predecessors, but shortly before the first stage was to separate one engine caught fire, causing the entire structure to explode, but not before the escape structure again properly deployed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite his skills in rocketry, Mishin was not known as an able administrator. He is often blamed for the failure of the program to put a man on the moon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup> and faced criticisms for his alcohol consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">[15]</a></sup> He was described by Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev as "not [having] the slightest idea how to cope with the many thousands of people, the management of whom had been loaded onto his shoulders, nor make the huge irreversible government machine work for him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 1967, <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexey_Leonov" title="Alexey Leonov">Alexey Leonov</a> criticised Mishin\'s "poor knowledge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz spacecraft</a> and the details of its operation, his lack of cooperation in working with the cosmonauts in flight and training activities" and asked <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Kamanin" title="Nikolai Kamanin">Nikolai Kamanin</a> for him to be cited in the official report into the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">Soyuz 1</a> crash, which killed <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Komarov" title="Vladimir Komarov">Vladimir Komarov</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Leonov described Mishin as "hesitant, uninspiring, poor at making decisions, over-reluctant to take risks and bad at managing the cosmonaut corps"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-17">[17]</a></sup> Other failures during his term of leadership were the deaths of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a> crew, the loss of three space stations, and computer failures in four probes sent to Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On May 15, 1974, while he was in the hospital, Mishin was replaced by a rival, Chief Engine Designer <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Glushko" title="Valentin Glushko">Valentin Glushko</a>, after all four N1 test launches failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He continued his educational and research works as the head of rocket department of Moscow Aviation Institute.\n</p><p>Vasily Mishin was awarded the title <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hero_of_Socialist_Labor" title="Hero of Socialist Labor">Hero of Socialist Labor</a> for his work with the Soviet space program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He died in Moscow on October 10, 2001 at the age of 84.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>His diaries, containing information on the program from 1960 to 1974, were purchased by the Perot Foundation in 1993. In 1997 a small part of the collection was donated to the National Air and Space Museum to be put on display, and in 2004 copies were donated to NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vasily_Mishin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-:0-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-:0_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-:0_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-:0_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Harvey, Brian (2007). <i>Soviet and Russian Lunar Exploration</i>.
