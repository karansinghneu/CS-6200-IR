It describes the amount of light that passes through, is emitted or reflected from a particular area, and falls within a given <a href="/wiki/Solid_angle" title="Solid angle">solid angle</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> unit for luminance is <a href="/wiki/Candela_per_square_metre" title="Candela per square metre">candela per square metre</a> (cd/m<sup>2</sup>). A non-SI term for the same unit is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nit_(unit)" title="Nit (unit)">nit</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGS" title="CGS">CGS</a> unit of luminance is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stilb_(luminance)" title="Stilb (luminance)">stilb</a>, which is equal to links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Luminance is often used to characterize emission or reflection from flat, <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">diffuse</a> surfaces. The luminance indicates how much <a href="/wiki/Luminous_flux" title="Luminous flux">luminous power</a> will be detected by an <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eye</a> looking at the surface from a particular angle of view. Luminance is thus an indicator of how <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">bright</a> the surface will appear. In this case, the solid angle of interest is the solid angle subtended by the eye\'s <a href="/wiki/Pupil" title="Pupil">pupil</a>. Luminance is used in the video industry to characterize the brightness of displays. A typical computer display emits between 50 and <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002300000000000000&#9824;</span>300&#160;cd/m<sup>2</sup></span>. The sun has a luminance of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009160000000000000&#9824;</span>1.6<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>9</sup>&#160;cd/m<sup>2</sup></span> at noon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Luminance is <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(physics)" title="Invariant (physics)">invariant</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric_optics" title="Geometric optics">geometric optics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This means that for an ideal optical system, the luminance at the output is the same as the input luminance. For real, passive, optical systems, the output luminance is <i>at most</i> equal to the input. As an example, if one uses a lens to form an image that is smaller than the source object, the luminous power is concentrated into a smaller area, meaning that the <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> is higher at the image. The light at the image plane, however, fills a larger solid angle so the luminance comes out to be the same assuming there is no loss at the lens. The image can never be "brighter" than the source.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
