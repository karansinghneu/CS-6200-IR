id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>astrophysical jet</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> phenomenon where outflows of <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionised</a> matter are emitted as an extended beam along the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">axis of rotation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When this greatly accelerated matter in the beam approaches the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, astrophysical jets become <b>relativistic jets</b> as they show effects from <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morabito_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morabito-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The formation and powering of astrophysical jets are highly complex phenomena that are associated with many types of high-energy astronomical sources. They likely arise from dynamic interactions within <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">accretion disks</a>, whose active processes are commonly connected with compact central objects such as <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>. One explanation is that tangled <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morabito_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morabito-2">[2]</a></sup> are organised to aim two diametrically opposing beams away from the central source by angles only several degrees wide <span class="nowrap">(c. &gt; 1%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kundt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kundt-3">[3]</a></sup></span> Jets may also be influenced by a <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> effect known as <a href="/wiki/Frame-dragging" title="Frame-dragging">frame-dragging</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Most of the largest and most active jets are created by <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black holes</a> (SMBH) in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galaxies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a> or within galaxy clusters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Such jets can exceed millions of <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kundt_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kundt-3">[3]</a></sup> Other astronomical objects that contain jets include <a href="/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable_star" title="Cataclysmic variable star">cataclysmic variable stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/X-ray_binary" title="X-ray binary">X-ray binaries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst" title="Gamma ray burst">Gamma ray bursts</a> (GRB). Others are associated with star forming regions including <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">T Tauri stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbig%E2%80%93Haro_object" title="Herbig&#8211;Haro object">Herbig&#8211;Haro objects</a>, which are caused by the interaction of jets with the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_outflow" title="Bipolar outflow">Bipolar outflows</a> or jets may also be associated with <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> or with evolved <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_nebula" title="Protoplanetary nebula">post-AGB</a> stars, <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebulae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_nebula" title="Bipolar nebula">bipolar nebulae</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrophysical_jet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relativistic jets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><span id="Relativistic_jet"></span>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="343" data-file-width="393" decoding="async" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/270px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/405px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg/540px-Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg.png 2x" width="270"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galaxies-AGN-Inner-Structure.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Relativistic jet. The environment around the <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">AGN</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">relativistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> is collimated into jets which escape along the pole(s) of the <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Relativistic jets are beams of ionised matter accelerated close to the speed of light. Most have been observationally associated with central black holes of some <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">active galaxies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>, and also by galactic <a href="/wiki/Stellar_black_hole" title="Stellar black hole">stellar black holes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsars</a>. Beam lengths may extend between several thousand,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> hundreds of thousands<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or millions of parsecs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kundt_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kundt-3">[3]</a></sup> Jet velocities when approaching the speed of light show significant effects of the <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special theory of relativity</a>; for example, <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_beaming" title="Relativistic beaming">relativistic beaming</a> that changes the apparent beam brightness (see the \'one-sided\' jets below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:M87_jet.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1276" data-file-width="1222" decoding="async" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/220px-M87_jet.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/330px-M87_jet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/440px-M87_jet.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:M87_jet.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy_M87" title="Elliptical galaxy M87">Elliptical galaxy M87</a> emitting a relativistic jet, as seen by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Massive central black holes in galaxies have the most powerful jets, but their structure and behaviours are similar to those of smaller galactic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_stars" title="Neutron stars">neutron stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>. These SMBH systems are often called <a href="/wiki/Microquasar" title="Microquasar">microquasars</a> and show a large range of velocities. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS433" title="SS433">SS433</a> jet, for example, has a velocity of 0.23<a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">c</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Relativistic jet formation may also explain observed <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst" title="Gamma-ray burst">gamma-ray bursts</a>. Notably, even weaker and less relativistic jets may be associated with many binary systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Mechanisms behind the composition of jets remain uncertain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> though some studies favour models where jets are composed of an electrically neutral mixture of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>, while others are consistent with jets composed of positron&#8211;electron plasma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brandeis.edu_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-brandeis.edu-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-12">[12]</a></sup> Trace nuclei swept up in a relativistic positron&#8211;electron jet would be expected to have extremely high energy, as these heavier nuclei should attain velocity equal to the positron and electron velocity.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation_as_possible_energy_source">Rotation as possible energy source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrophysical_jet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rotation as possible energy source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Because of the enormous amount of energy needed to launch a relativistic jet, some jets are possibly  powered by spinning <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>. However, the frequency of high-energy astrophysical sources with jets suggest combination of different mechanisms indirectly identified with the energy within the associated accretion disk and X-ray emissions from the generating source. Two early theories have been used to explain how energy can be transferred from a black hole into an astrophysical jet:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Blandford%E2%80%93Znajek_process" title="Blandford&#8211;Znajek process">Blandford&#8211;Znajek process</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This theory explains the extraction of energy from magnetic fields around an accretion disk, which are dragged and twisted by the spin of the black hole. Relativistic material is then feasibly launched by the tightening of the field lines.</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Penrose_process" title="Penrose process">Penrose mechanism</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Here energy is extracted from a rotating black hole by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_dragging" title="Frame dragging">frame dragging</a>, which was later theoretically proven to be able to extract relativistic particle energy and momentum,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and subsequently shown to be a possible mechanism for jet formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_jets_from_neutron_stars">Relativistic jets from neutron stars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Astrophysical_jet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relativistic jets from neutron stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lighthouse_nebula.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="741" data-file-width="864" decoding="async" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Lighthouse_nebula.jpg/250px-Lighthouse_nebula.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Lighthouse_nebula.jpg/375px-Lighthouse_nebula.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Lighthouse_nebula.jpg/500px-Lighthouse_nebula.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lighthouse_nebula.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The pulsar IGR J11014-6103 with supernova remnant origin, nebula and jet.</div></div></div>\n<p>Jets may also be observed from spinning neutron stars. An example is pulsar <a href="/wiki/IGR_J11014-6103" title="IGR J11014-6103">IGR J11014-6103</a>, which has the largest jet so far observed in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> Galaxy whose velocity is estimated at 80% the speed of light. (0.8c.) X-ray observations have been obtained but there is no detected radio signature nor accretion disk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Initially, this pulsar was presumed to be rapidly spinning but later measurements indicate the spin rate is only 15.9&#160;Hz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Such a slow spin rate and lack of accretion material suggest the jet is neither rotation nor accretion powered, though it appears aligned with the pulsar rotation axis and perpendicular to the pulsar\'s true motion.\n</p>\n<h2><span
