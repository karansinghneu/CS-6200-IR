href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>), also spelled <b>&#230;ther</b> or <b>ether</b> and also called <b>quintessence</b>, is the material that fills the region of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> above the <a href="/wiki/Sublunary_sphere" title="Sublunary sphere">terrestrial sphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smoot_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-smoot-2">[2]</a></sup> The concept of aether was used in several theories to explain several natural phenomena, such as the traveling of light and gravity. In the late 19th century, physicists postulated that aether permeated all throughout space, providing a medium through which light could travel in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, but evidence for the presence of such a medium was not found in the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment">Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment</a>, and this result has been interpreted as meaning that no such <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a> exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythological_origins">Mythological origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mythological origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aether_(mythology)" title="Aether (mythology)">Aether (mythology)</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Empyrean" title="Empyrean">Empyrean</a></div>\n<p>The word  <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#945;&#7984;&#952;&#942;&#961;</span> (<i>aith&#275;r</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Greek" title="Homeric Greek">Homeric Greek</a> means "pure, fresh air" or "clear sky". In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, it was thought to be the pure essence that the gods breathed, filling the space where they lived, analogous to the <i><a href="/wiki/Air_(classical_element)" title="Air (classical element)">air</a></i> breathed by mortals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gm.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gm.com-4">[4]</a></sup> It is also personified as a deity, <a href="/wiki/Aether_(mythology)" title="Aether (mythology)">Aether</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Erebus" title="Erebus">Erebus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nyx_(mythology)" title="Nyx (mythology)">Nyx</a> in traditional Greek mythology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gm.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gm.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Aether is related to <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">&#945;&#7988;&#952;&#969;</span> "to incinerate",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and intransitive "to burn, to shine" (related is the name <i>Aithiopes</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopians</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Aethiopia" title="Aethiopia">Aethiopia</a>), meaning "people with a burnt (black) visage").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fage2526_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fage2526-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fifth_element">Fifth element</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fifth element">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemaicsystem-small.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="638" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Ptolemaicsystem-small.png/220px-Ptolemaicsystem-small.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Ptolemaicsystem-small.png/330px-Ptolemaicsystem-small.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Ptolemaicsystem-small.png/440px-Ptolemaicsystem-small.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemaicsystem-small.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medieval concept of the cosmos. The innermost spheres are the terrestrial spheres, while the outer are made of aether and contain the celestial bodies</div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">Timaeus</a></i> (58d) speaking about air, Plato mentions that "there is the most translucent kind which is called by the name of aether (&#945;&#943;&#952;&#951;&#961;)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but otherwise he adopted the classical system of four elements. <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, who had been Plato\'s student at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akademia" title="Akademia">Akademia</a>, agreed on this point with his former mentor, emphasizing additionally that fire sometimes has been mistaken for aether. However, in his Book <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Heavens" title="On the Heavens">On the Heavens</a></i> he introduced a new "first" element to the system of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">classical elements</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ionian_School_(philosophy)" title="Ionian School (philosophy)">Ionian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" title="Greek philosophy">philosophy</a>. He noted that the four terrestrial classical elements were subject to change and naturally moved linearly. The first element however, located in the celestial regions and heavenly bodies, moved circularly and had none of the qualities the terrestrial classical elements had. It was neither hot nor cold, neither wet nor dry. With this addition the system of elements was extended to five and later commentators started referring to the new first one as the fifth and also called it <i>aether</i>, a word that Aristotle had not used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Aether did not follow <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_physics" title="Aristotelian physics">Aristotelian physics</a> either. Aether was also incapable of motion of quality or motion of quantity. Aether was only capable of local motion. Aether naturally moved in circles, and had no contrary, or unnatural, motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Aristotle also noted that crystalline spheres made of aether held the celestial bodies. The idea of crystalline spheres and natural circular motion of aether led to Aristotle\'s explanation of the observed orbits of stars and planets in perfectly circular motion in crystalline aether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smoot_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-smoot-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Medieval scholastic philosophers granted <i>aether</i> changes of density, in which the bodies of the planets were considered to be more dense than the medium which filled the rest of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fludd" title="Robert Fludd">Robert Fludd</a> stated that the aether was of the character that it was "subtler than light". Fludd cites the 3rd-century view of <a href="/wiki/Plotinus" title="Plotinus">Plotinus</a>, concerning the aether as penetrative and non-material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Arche" title="Arche">Arche</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quintessence">Quintessence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quintessence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">Quintessence (physics)</a></div>\n<p>Quintessence is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latinate" title="Latinate">Latinate</a> name of the fifth element used by medieval alchemists for a medium similar or identical to that thought to make up the heavenly bodies. It was noted that there was very little presence of quintessence within the terrestrial sphere. Due to the low presence of quintessence, earth could be affected by what takes place within the heavenly bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheAlchemists_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheAlchemists-14">[14]</a></sup> This theory was developed in the 14th century text <i>The testament of Lullius</i>, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Llull" title="Ramon Llull">Ramon Llull</a>. The use of quintessence became popular within medieval alchemy. Quintessence stemmed from the medieval elemental system, which consisted of the four classical elements, and aether, or quintessence, in addition to two chemical elements representing metals: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a>, "the stone which burns", which characterized the principle of combustibility, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, which contained the idealized principle of metallic properties.\n</p><p>This elemental system spread rapidly throughout all of Europe and became popular with alchemists, especially in medicinal alchemy. Medicinal alchemy then sought to isolate quintessence and incorporate it within medicine and elixirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheAlchemists_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheAlchemists-14">[14]</a></sup> Due to quintessence\'s pure and heavenly quality, it was thought that through consumption one may rid oneself of any impurities or illnesses. In <i>The book of Quintessence</i>, a 15th-century English translation of a continental text, quintessence was used as a medicine for many of man\'s illnesses. A process given for the creation of quintessence is <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of alcohol seven times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Over the years, the term quintessence has become synonymous with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elixirs" title="Elixirs">elixirs</a>, medicinal <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher\'s stone">philosopher\'s stone</a> itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aether_theories" title="Aether theories">Aether theories</a></div>\n<p>With the <a href="/wiki/History_of_physics#18th-century_developments" title="History of physics">18th century physics developments</a>, physical models known as "aether theories" made use of a similar concept for the explanation of the propagation of electromagnetic and gravitational forces. As early as the 1670s, Newton used the idea of aether to help match observations to strict mechanical rules of his physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  However, the early modern aether had little in common with the aether of classical elements from which the name was borrowed. These aether theories are considered to be scientifically obsolete, as the development of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> showed that Maxwell\'s equations do not require the aether for the transmission of these forces. However, Einstein himself noted that his own model which replaced these theories could itself be thought of as an aether, as it implied that the empty space between objects had its own physical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the early modern aether models being superseded by general relativity, occasionally some physicists have attempted to reintroduce the concept of aether in an attempt to address perceived deficiencies in current physical models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> One proposed model of <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> has been named "<a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">quintessence</a>" by its proponents, in honor of the classical element.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This idea relates to the hypothecial form of dark energy postulated as an explanation of observations of an accelerating universe. It has also been called a <a href="/wiki/Fifth_force" title="Fifth force">fifth fundamental force</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_and_light">Aether and light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aether and light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">Luminiferous aether</a></div>\n<p>The motion of light was a long-standing investigation in physics for hundreds of years before the 20th century. The use of aether to describe this motion was popular during the 17th and 18th centuries, including a theory proposed by <a href="/wiki/Johann_II_Bernoulli" title="Johann II Bernoulli">Johann II Bernoulli</a>, who was recognized in 1736 with the prize of the French Academy. In his theory, all space is permeated by aether containing "excessively small whirlpools". These whirlpools allow for aether to have a certain elasticity, transmitting vibrations from the corpuscular packets of light as they travel through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This theory of <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a> would influence the <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave theory</a> of light proposed by <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a>, in which light traveled in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longitudinal_waves" title="Longitudinal waves">longitudinal waves</a>  via an "omnipresent, perfectly elastic medium having zero density, called aether". At the time, it was thought that in order for light to travel through a vacuum, there must have been a medium filling the void through which it could propagate, as sound through air or ripples in a pool. Later, when it was proved that the nature of light wave is <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse</a> instead of longitudinal, Huygens\' theory was replaced by subsequent theories proposed by <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">Maxwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis-Victor_de_Broglie" title="Louis-Victor de Broglie">de Broglie</a>, which rejected the existence and necessity of aether to explain the various optical phenomena. These theories were supported by the results of the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment">Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment</a> in which evidence for the motion of aether was conclusively absent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The results of the experiment influenced many physicists of the time and contributed to the eventual development of Einstein\'s <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">theory of special relativity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_and_gravitation">Aether and gravitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aether_(classical_element)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aether and gravitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="407" decoding="async" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/220px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/330px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sir Isaac Newton</div></div></div>\n<p>Aether has been used in various gravitational theories as a medium to help explain gravitation and what causes it. It was used in one of Sir <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>\'s first published theories of gravitation, <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophi&#230; Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophi&#230; Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i> (the <i>Principia</i>). He based the whole description of planetary motions on a theoretical law of dynamic interactions. He renounced standing attempts at accounting for this particular form of interaction between distant bodies by introducing a mechanism of propagation through an intervening medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld1969_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld1969-24">[24]</a></sup> He calls this intervening medium aether. In his aether model, Newton describes aether as a medium that "flows" continually downward toward the Earth\'s surface and is partially absorbed and partially diffused. This "circulation" of aether is what he associated the force of gravity with to help explain the action of gravity in a non-mechanical fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld1969_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld1969-24">[24]</a></sup> This theory described different aether densities, creating an aether density gradient. His theory also explains that aether was dense within objects and rare without them. As particles of denser aether interacted with the rare aether they were attracted back to the dense aether much like cooling vapors of water are attracted back to each other to form water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton1679_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-newton1679-25">[25]</a></sup> In the <i>Principia</i> he attempts to explain the elasticity and movement of aether by relating aether to his static model of fluids. This elastic interaction is what caused the pull of gravity to take place, according to this early theory, and allowed an explanation for action at a distance instead of action through direct contact. Newton also explained this changing rarity and density of aether in his letter to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a> in 1679.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton1679_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-newton1679-25">[25]</a></sup> He illustrated aether and its field around objects in this letter as well and used this as a way to inform Robert Boyle about his theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letter_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-letter-26">[26]</a></sup> Although Newton eventually changed his theory of gravitation to one involving force and the laws of motion, his starting point for the modern understanding and explanation of gravity came from his original aether model on gravitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robishaw_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-robishaw-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
