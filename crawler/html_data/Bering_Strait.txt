title="Category:Islands by body of water">Islands</a></th><td><a href="/wiki/Diomede_Islands" title="Diomede Islands">Diomede Islands</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Bering Strait</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">strait</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>, which separates Russia and Alaska slightly south of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> at about 65&#176; 40\' N <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. The present Russia-US east-west boundary is at 168&#176; 58\' 37" W. The Strait is named after <a href="/wiki/Vitus_Bering" title="Vitus Bering">Vitus Bering</a>, a Danish explorer in the service of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>.\n</p><p>The Strait has been the subject of the scientific hypothesis <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_strait_theory" title="Bering strait theory">that humans migrated from Asia to North America across a land bridge</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a> when lower ocean levels&#160;&#8211; perhaps a result of glaciers locking up vast amounts of water&#160;&#8211; exposed a wide stretch of the sea floor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> both at the present strait and in the shallow sea north and south of it. This view of how <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a> entered America has been the dominant one for several decades and continues to be the most accepted one. Numerous successful crossings without the use of a boat have also been recorded since at least the early 20th century.\n</p><p>Since 2012, the Russian coast of the Bering Strait has been a <a href="/wiki/Border_Security_Zone_of_Russia" title="Border Security Zone of Russia">closed military zone</a>. Through organized trips and the use of special permits, it is possible for foreigners to visit. All arrivals must be through an airport or a cruise port, near the Bering Strait only at <a href="/wiki/Anadyr_(town)" title="Anadyr (town)">Anadyr</a> or <a href="/wiki/Provideniya" title="Provideniya">Provideniya</a>. Unauthorized travelers who arrive on shore after crossing the strait, even those with visas, may be arrested, imprisoned briefly, fined, deported and banned from future visas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT71212_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT71212-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" id="Geography_and_science">Geography and science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Bering Strait is about 82 kilometres (51&#160;mi) wide at its narrowest point, between <a href="/wiki/Cape_Dezhnev" title="Cape Dezhnev">Cape Dezhnev</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chungu_Peninsula&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chungu Peninsula (page does not exist)">Chungu Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, the easternmost point (167&#176; 42\' W) of the <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> continent and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Prince_of_Wale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cape Prince of Wale (page does not exist)">Cape Prince of Wale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, United States, the westernmost point (164&#176; 15\' W) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_continent" title="North American continent">North American continent</a>. Its depth varies between 30 metres (98&#160;ft) and 50 metres (160&#160;ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It borders with the <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_Sea" title="Chukchi Sea">Chukchi Sea</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>) to north and with the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a> to south.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> runs equidistant between the Strait\'s <a href="/wiki/Diomede_Islands" title="Diomede Islands">Diomede Islands</a> at a distance of 1.5&#160;km (1&#160;mi), leaving the Russian and American sides usually on different calendar days, with Cape Dezhnev 21 hours ahead of the American side (20 hours during <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">daylight saving time</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The area is sparsely populated.\n</p><p>The eastern coast belongs to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. Notable towns on the American coast of the Strait include <a href="/wiki/Nome,_Alaska" title="Nome, Alaska">Nome</a> (3,788 people) and the small settlement of <a href="/wiki/Teller,_Alaska" title="Teller, Alaska">Teller</a> (228 people).\n</p><p>The western coast belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Chukotka Autonomous Okrug</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Federal_subjects_of_Russia" title="Federal subjects of Russia">federal subject</a> of Russia. Major towns that lie along the Strait include <a href="/wiki/Lorino,_Chukotka_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Lorino, Chukotka Autonomous Okrug">Lorino</a> (1,267 people) and <a href="/wiki/Lavrentiya" title="Lavrentiya">Lavrentiya</a> (1,459 people).\n</p><p>The Diomede Islands lie midway in the Strait. The village in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Diomede" title="Little Diomede">Little Diomede</a> has a school which belongs to Alaska\'s <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait_School_District" title="Bering Strait School District">Bering Strait School District</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expeditions">Expeditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expeditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Operational_Navigation_Chart_C-8%2C_2nd_edition.jpg/600px-Operational_Navigation_Chart_C-8%2C_2nd_edition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Operational_Navigation_Chart_C-8%2C_2nd_edition.jpg/800px-Operational_Navigation_Chart_C-8%2C_2nd_edition.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div href="/wiki/Defense_Mapping_Agency" title="Defense Mapping Agency">Defense Mapping Agency</a> topographical map of the Bering Strait, 1973</div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest reference of the strait were from maps from the Polo family, based on the adventures of <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-History3-4">[4]</a></sup> From at least 1562, European geographers thought that there was a <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Ani%C3%A1n" title="Strait of Ani&#225;n">Strait of Ani&#225;n</a> between Asia and North America. In 1648, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semyon_Dezhnyov" title="Semyon Dezhnyov">Semyon Dezhnyov</a> probably passed through the strait, but his report did not reach Europe. Danish-born Russian navigator <a href="/wiki/Vitus_Bering" title="Vitus Bering">Vitus Bering</a> entered it in 1728. In 1732, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gvozdev" title="Mikhail Gvozdev">Mikhail Gvozdev</a> crossed it for the first time, from Asia to America. <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Erik_Nordenski%C3%B6ld" title="Adolf Erik Nordenski&#246;ld">Adolf Erik Nordenski&#246;ld</a> in 1878&#8211;79 sailed along the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, thereby proving that there was no northern land bridge from Asia to North America.\n</p><p>In March 1913, Captain Max Gottschalk (German) crossed from the east cape of Siberia to <a href="/wiki/Shishmaref,_Alaska" title="Shishmaref, Alaska">Shishmaref, Alaska</a>, on dogsled via Little and Big Diomede islands. He was the first documented modern voyager to cross from Russia to North America without the use of a boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Captain_Max_Gottschalk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Captain_Max_Gottschalk-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1987, swimmer <a href="/wiki/Lynne_Cox" title="Lynne Cox">Lynne Cox</a> swam a 4.3-kilometre (2.7&#160;mi) course between the Diomede Islands from Alaska to the Soviet Union in 3.3&#160;&#176;C (37.9&#160;&#176;F) water during the last years of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June and July 1989, three teams of sea kayakers combined to attempt the first modern sea kayak crossing of the Bering Strait. The groups were seven Alaskans referring to their effort as \'Paddling Into Tomorrow\' (crossing the international dateline), a four-man British expedition, <i>Kayaks Across the Bering Strait</i> and an unnamed group of three Californians.\n</p><p>In 1998, Russian adventurer <a href="/wiki/Dmitry_Shparo" title="Dmitry Shparo">Dmitry Shparo</a> and his son Matvey crossed the frozen Bering Strait on skis.\n</p><p>In March 2006, Briton <a href="/wiki/Karl_Bushby" title="Karl Bushby">Karl Bushby</a> and French-American adventurer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dimitri_Kieffer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dimitri Kieffer (page does not exist)">Dimitri Kieffer</a> crossed the strait on foot, walking across a frozen 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) section in 15 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They were soon arrested for not entering Russia through a border control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>August 2008 marked the first crossing of the Bering Strait using an amphibious road-going vehicle. The specially modified <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover_Defender" title="Land Rover Defender">Land Rover Defender 110</a> was driven by Steve Burgess and Dan Evans across the straits on its second attempt following the interruption of the first by bad weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2012, a Korean team led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Sung-Taek" title="Hong Sung-Taek">Hong Sung-Taek</a> crossed the straits on foot in six days. They started from Chukotka Peninsula, the east coast of Russia on February 23 and arrived in Wales, the western coastal town in Alaska on February 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2012, six adventurers associated with "Dangerous Waters", a reality adventure show under production, made the crossing on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_water_craft" title="Personal water craft">Sea-Doos</a> but were arrested and permitted to return to Alaska on their Sea-Doos after being briefly detained in <a href="/wiki/Lavrentiya" title="Lavrentiya">Lavrentiya</a>, administrative center of the <a href="/wiki/Chukotsky_District" title="Chukotsky District">Chukotsky District</a>. They were treated well and given a tour of the village\'s museum, but not permitted to continue south along the Pacific coast. The men had visas but the western coast of the Bering Strait is a <a href="/wiki/Border_Security_Zone_of_Russia" title="Border Security Zone of Russia">closed military zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT71212_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT71212-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Between August 4 and 10 (US time), 2013, a team of 65 swimmers from 17 countries performed a relay swim across the Bering Strait, the first such swim in history. They swam from Cape Dezhnev, Russia, to Cape Prince of Wales, United States (roughly 110&#160;km, due to the current).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> They had direct support from the Russian Navy, using one of its ships, and assistance with permission.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_crossing">Proposed crossing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proposed crossing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait_crossing" title="Bering Strait crossing">Bering Strait crossing</a></div>\n<p>A physical link between Asia and North America via the Bering Strait nearly became a reality in 1864 when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian-American_telegraph" title="Russian-American telegraph">Russian-American telegraph</a> company began preparations for an overland telegraph line connecting Europe and America via the east. It was abandoned when the undersea <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Cable" title="Atlantic Cable">Atlantic Cable</a> proved successful.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>A further proposal for a bridge-and-tunnel link from Siberia to Alaska was made by French engineer <a href="/wiki/Baron_Loicq_de_Lobel" title="Baron Loicq de Lobel">Baron Loicq de Lobel</a> in 1906. Czar Nicholas II of Russia issued an order authorising a Franco-American syndicate represented by de Lobel to begin work on the Trans-Siberian Alaska railroad project, but no physical work ever commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Suggestions have been made to construct a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bering_Strait_bridge" title="Bering Strait bridge">Bering Strait bridge</a> between Alaska and Siberia. However, despite the unprecedented engineering, political, and financial challenges, Russia green-lighted the US $65-billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKM-World_Link" title="TKM-World Link">TKM-World Link</a> tunnel project in August 2011. If completed, the 103&#160;km (64 mile) project would have been the world\'s longest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> China considered construction of a "China-Russia-Canada-America" railroad line that would include construction of a 200&#160;km (120&#160;mi) long underwater tunnel that would cross the Bering Strait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_dam">Proposed dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1956, the Soviet Union proposed to the US a joint bi-national project to warm the Arctic Ocean and melt some of the ice cap. As designed by Petr Borisov, the Soviet project called for a 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) wide dam across the Bering Strait. It would block the cold Pacific current from entering the Arctic. By pumping low-salinity cold surface water across the dam to the Pacific, warmer and higher salinity sea water from the Atlantic Ocean would be introduced into the Arctic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196106_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196106-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> However, citing national security concerns, the CIA and FBI experts opposed the Soviet plan by arguing that while the plan was feasible, it would compromise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NORAD" title="NORAD">NORAD</a> and thus the dam could be built at only an immense cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Soviet scientist D. A. Drogaytsev, also opposed the idea, stating that the sea north of the dam and north-flowing rivers in Siberia would become unnavigable year round, and extend the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi</a> and other deserts to the northern Siberia coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196106_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-ley196106-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_P._Steinmetz" title="Charles P. Steinmetz">Charles P. Steinmetz</a> earlier proposed to widen the Bering Strait by removing <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_Island" title="St. Lawrence Island">St. Lawrence Island</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Seward_Peninsula" title="Seward Peninsula">Seward</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chukotski_Peninsula" title="Chukotski Peninsula">Chukotski Peninsulas</a>. A strait 200 miles wide would let the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Current" title="Japan Current">Japan Current</a> melt the Arctic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196106_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-ley196106-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 21st century another dam has also been proposed, however the aim of the proposal is to preserve the Arctic ice cap against global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id=".22Ice_Curtain.22_border"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'"Ice_Curtain"_border\'>"Ice Curtain" border</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bering_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title=\'Edit section: "Ice Curtain" border\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg/400px-Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg/600px-Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diomede_Islands_Bering_Sea_Jul_2006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Little Diomede Island (US, left) and Big Diomede Island (Russia, right)</div></div></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, the Bering Strait marked the border between the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and the United States. The <a href="/wiki/Diomede_Islands" title="Diomede Islands">Diomede Islands</a>&#8212;Big Diomede (Russia) and Little Diomede (US)&#8212;are only 3.8&#160;km (2.4&#160;mi) apart. Traditionally, the indigenous peoples in the area had frequently crossed the border back and forth for "routine visits, seasonal festivals and subsistence trade", but were prevented from doing so during the Cold War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The border became known as the "Ice Curtain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It was completely closed, and there was no regular passenger air or boat traffic. In 1987, American swimmer <a href="/wiki/Lynne_Cox" title="Lynne Cox">Lynne Cox</a> symbolically helped ease tensions between the two countries by swimming across the border,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and was congratulated jointly by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
