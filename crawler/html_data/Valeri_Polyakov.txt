colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Valeri Vladimirovich Polyakov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1042;&#1072;&#1083;&#1077;&#1088;&#1080;&#1081; &#1042;&#1083;&#1072;&#1076;&#1080;&#1084;&#1080;&#1088;&#1086;&#1074;&#1080;&#1095; &#1055;&#1086;&#1083;&#1103;&#1082;&#1086;&#1074;</span>, born <b>Valeri Ivanovich Korshunov</b> on April 27, 1942) is a former Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a>. He is the holder of the record for the longest single <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">stay in space</a> in human history, staying aboard the <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> for more than 14 months (437 days 18 hours) during one trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-1">[1]</a></sup> His combined space experience is more than 22 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Selected as a cosmonaut in 1972, Polyakov made his first flight into space aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-6" title="Soyuz TM-6">Soyuz TM-6</a> in 1988. He returned to Earth 240 days later aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-7" title="Soyuz TM-7">TM-7</a>. Polyakov completed his second flight into space in 1994&#8211;1995, spending 437 days in space between launching on <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-18" title="Soyuz TM-18">Soyuz TM-18</a> and landing on <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-20" title="Soyuz TM-20">TM-20</a>, setting the record for the longest time continuously spent in space by an individual in human history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valeri_Polyakov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Polyakov was born in <a href="/wiki/Tula,_Russia" title="Tula, Russia">Tula</a> in the USSR on April 27, 1942. Born Valeri Ivanovich Korshunov, Polyakov legally changed his name after being adopted by his stepfather in 1957. He was educated at the Tula Secondary School No. 4, from which he graduated in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He enrolled in the I. M. Sechenov 1st Moscow Medical Institute, where he graduated with a doctoral degree. After, he enrolled in the Institute of Medical and Biological Problems, Ministry of Public Health, Moscow, where he specialized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_medicine" title="Aerospace medicine">astronautics medicine</a>. Polyakov dedicated himself to the field of space medicine in 1964 after the flight of the first physician in space, <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yegorov" title="Boris Yegorov">Boris Yegorov</a>, aboard <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_1" title="Voskhod 1">Voskhod 1</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmonaut_career">Cosmonaut career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valeri_Polyakov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cosmonaut career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cosmonaut_Polyakov_Watches_Discovery%27s_Rendezvous_With_Mir_-_GPN-2002-000078.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4176" data-file-width="4116" decoding="async" 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosmonaut_Polyakov_Watches_Discovery%27s_Rendezvous_With_Mir_-_GPN-2002-000078.jpg" 1995.</div></div></div>\n<p>Polyakov was selected as a cosmonaut in <a href="/wiki/List_of_astronauts_by_year_of_selection#1972" title="List of astronauts by year of selection">Medical Group 3</a> on March 22, 1972. His first flight into space occurred on <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-6" title="Soyuz TM-6">Soyuz TM-6</a> in 1988. After staying aboard the Mir space station and conducting research for 240 days, Polyakov returned to Earth aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-7" title="Soyuz TM-7">Soyuz TM-7</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Polyakov\'s second spaceflight, the longest human spaceflight in history, began on January 8, 1994 with the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-18" title="Soyuz TM-18">Soyuz TM-18</a> mission. He spent approximately 437 days aboard Mir conducting experiments and performing scientific research. During this flight, he completed just over 7,000 orbits of the Earth. On January 9, 1995, after 366 days in space, Polyakov formally broke the spaceflight duration record previously set by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Titov" title="Vladimir Titov">Vladimir Titov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musa_Manarov" title="Musa Manarov">Musa Manarov</a> six years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leavingearth_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-leavingearth-3">[3]</a></sup>  He returned to Earth aboard <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-20" title="Soyuz TM-20">Soyuz TM-20</a> on March 22, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> Upon landing, Polyakov opted not to be carried the few feet between the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-TM" title="Soyuz-TM">Soyuz capsule</a> and a nearby lawn chair, instead walking the short distance. In doing so, he wished to prove that humans could be physically capable of working on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> after a long-duration transit phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wired-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Polyakov volunteered for his 437-day flight to learn how the human body would respond to the <a href="/wiki/Micro-g_environment" title="Micro-g environment">micro-gravity</a> environment on long-duration missions to Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-wired-4">[4]</a></sup> Upon returning from his second spaceflight, Polyakov held the record for the most total time in space. This record was later broken by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Avdeyev" title="Sergei Avdeyev">Sergei Avdeyev</a> and is currently held by <a href="/wiki/Gennady_Padalka" title="Gennady Padalka">Gennady Padalka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wired_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-wired-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mirnasa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mirnasa-5">[5]</a></sup> Data from Polyakov\'s flight has been used by researchers to determine that humans are able to maintain a healthy mental state during long-duration spaceflight just as they would on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journal_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-journal-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Polyakov underwent medical assessments before, during, and after the flight. He also underwent two follow-up examinations six months after returning to Earth. When researchers compared the results of these medical exams, it was revealed that although there were no impairments of <a href="/wiki/Jungian_cognitive_functions" title="Jungian cognitive functions">cognitive functions</a>, Polyakov experienced a clear decline in mood as well as a feeling of increased workload during the first few weeks of spaceflight and return to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journal_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-journal-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sy-7">[7]</a></sup> Polyakov\'s mood stabilized to pre-flight levels between the second and fourteenth month of his mission. It was also revealed that Polyakov did not suffer from any prolonged performance impairments after returning to Earth. In light of these findings, researchers concluded that a stable mood and overall function could be maintained during extended duration spaceflights, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">manned missions to Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-journal_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-journal-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceflights">Spaceflights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valeri_Polyakov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spaceflights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-6" title="Soyuz TM-6">Soyuz TM-6</a> / <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-7" title="Soyuz TM-7">Soyuz TM-7</a> &#8211; August 28, 1988 to April 27, 1989 &#8211; 240 days, 22 hours, 34 minutes</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-18" title="Soyuz TM-18">Soyuz TM-18</a> / <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-20" title="Soyuz TM-20">Soyuz TM-20</a> &#8211; January 8, 1994 to March 22, 1995 &#8211; 437 days, 17 hours, 58 minutes</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valeri_Polyakov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Polyakov retired from his position as a cosmonaut in June 1995, with a total of just over 678 days in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-astronautix-8">[8]</a></sup> He participated in experiment SFINCSS-99 (Simulation of Flight of International Crew on Space Station) in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Polyakov is currently the Deputy Director of the Ministry of Public Health in Moscow, where he oversees the medical aspects of long-duration space missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup> He is a member of the Russian Chief Medical Commission, participating in the qualification and selection of cosmonauts. He also holds membership in the International Space Researcher\'s Association and the <a href="/wiki/International_Academy_of_Astronautics" title="International Academy of Astronautics">International Academy of Astronautics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iaa_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-iaa-10">[10]</a></sup> Polyakov is married and has one child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronautix_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-astronautix-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since returning from space, Polyakov remained active in the discipline of international spaceflight, becoming a "cosmonaut-investigator" for the United States, Austria, Germany, and France during their respective space science missions to the Mir space station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valeri_Polyakov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Polyakov has won several awards for his spaceflight and academic achievements, including the <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Hero of the Soviet Union">Hero of the Soviet Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_the_Russian_Federation" title="Hero of the Russian Federation">Hero of the Russian Federation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lenin" title="Order of Lenin">Order of Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Order of the Legion of Honour</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Parasat" title="Order of Parasat">Order of Parasat</a>. He is a member of organizations related to <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>, including the Russian Chief Medical Commission on cosmonauts\' certification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Polyakov holds the title of "Pilot-Cosmonaut of the USSR" and has published several works pertaining to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a>, medical aspects of space missions, and the results of research conducted on long-duration spaceflights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmmuseum_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-nmmuseum-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Polyakov\'s record for longest cumulative time in space of 678 days over two missions stood until surpassed in 1999 by cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Avdeyev" title="Sergei Avdeyev">Sergei Avdeyev</a> with a total of 747 days in space during three different missions.<sup
