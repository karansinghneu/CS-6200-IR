The serpent is represented by the constellation <a href="/wiki/Serpens" title="Serpens">Serpens</a>. Ophiuchus was one of the 48 constellations listed by the 2nd-century astronomer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, and it remains one of the 88 modern constellations. href="/wiki/Aquila_(constellation)" title="Aquila (constellation)">Aquila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serpens" title="Serpens">Serpens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hercules_(constellation)" title="Hercules (constellation)">Hercules</a>, northwest of the center of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. The southern part lies between <a href="/wiki/Scorpius" title="Scorpius">Scorpius</a> to the west and <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">Sagittarius</a> to the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>, it is best visible in summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is opposite <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>. Ophiuchus is depicted as a man grasping a <a href="/wiki/Serpens" title="Serpens">serpent</a>; the interposition of his body divides the snake constellation Serpens into two parts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpens_Caput" title="Serpens Caput">Serpens Caput</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpens_Cauda" title="Serpens Cauda">Serpens Cauda</a>, which are in modern times taken with Ophiuchus as one constellation. Ophiuchus straddles the equator but lies predominantly to its south. To wit, Rasalhague, a fairly conspicuous star in its north, never dips below the plane of potential visibility (ignoring the sun\'s presence in this constellation in November) so is Circumpolar north of 17<sup>h</sup>34<sup>m</sup>56<sup>s</sup> declination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The constellation extends southward to &#8722;30&#176; declination. Segments of the ecliptic that lie within Ophiuchus lie south of &#8722;20&#176; declination. A determination of exactly where these stars are visible on Earth would depend on <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya_effect" title="Novaya Zemlya effect">Novaya Zemlya effect</a>, mountains and clouds.\n</p><p>In contrast to Orion, it is in the period November&#8211;January (summer in the Southern Hemisphere, winter in the Northern Hemisphere) when Ophiuchus is in the daytime sky and thus not visible at most latitudes. However, for much of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> in the Northern Hemisphere\'s winter months, the Sun is below the horizon even at midday. Stars (and thus parts of Ophiuchus, especially Rasalhague) are then visible at twilight for a few hours around local noon, low in the South. In the Northern Hemisphere\'s spring and summer months, when Ophiuchus is normally visible in the night sky, the constellation is actually not visible, at those times and places in the Arctic when <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">midnight sun</a> obscures the stars. In countries close to the equator Ophiuchus appears overhead in June around midnight and in the October evening sky.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span href="/w/index.php?title=Ophiuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_stars_in_Ophiuchus" title="List of stars in Ophiuchus">List of stars in Ophiuchus</a></div>\n<p>The brightest stars in Ophiuchus include <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Ophiuchi" title="Alpha Ophiuchi">&#945; Ophiuchi</a>, called Rasalhague ("head of the serpent charmer"), at magnitude 2.07, and <a href="/wiki/Eta_Ophiuchi" title="Eta href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>&lt;/ref&gt; and <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Ophiuchi" title="Lambda Ophiuchi">&#955; Ophiuchi</a>, or Marfik ("the elbow").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/RS_Ophiuchi" title="RS Ophiuchi">RS Ophiuchi</a> is part of a class called recurrent <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">novae</a>, whose brightness increase at irregular intervals by hundreds of times in a period of just a few days. It is thought to be at the brink of becoming a type-1a <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Barnard%27s_Star" title="Barnard\'s Star">Barnard\'s Star</a>, one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars" title="List of nearest stars">nearest stars</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> (the only stars closer are the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a> <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">binary star</a> system and <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>), lies in Ophiuchus. It is located to the left of &#946; and just north of the V-shaped group of stars in an area that was once occupied by the now-obsolete constellation of <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Poniatovii" title="Taurus Poniatovii">Taurus Poniatovii</a> (Poniatowski\'s Bull). In 2005, astronomers using data from the <a href="/wiki/Green_Bank_Telescope" title="Green Bank Telescope">Green Bank Telescope</a> discovered a <a href="/wiki/Superbubble" title="Superbubble">superbubble</a> so large that it extends beyond the plane of the galaxy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huge_superbubble_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-huge_superbubble-11">[11]</a></sup> It is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophiuchus_Superbubble" title="Ophiuchus Superbubble">Ophiuchus Superbubble</a>.\n</p><p>In April 2007, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomers</a> announced that the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a>-built <a href="/wiki/Odin_(satellite)" title="Odin (satellite)">Odin satellite</a> had made the first detection of clouds of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecular</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in space, following observations in the constellation Ophiuchus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SN_1604" title="SN 1604">supernova of 1604</a> was first observed on 9 October 1604, near &#952; Ophiuchi. <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> saw it first on 16 October and studied it so extensively that the <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> was subsequently called Kepler\'s Supernova. He published his findings in a book titled <i>De stella nova in pede Serpentarii</i> (On the New Star in Ophiuchus\' Foot). <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a> used its brief appearance to counter the <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotelian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dogma" title="Dogma">dogma</a> that the heavens are changeless. In 2009 it was announced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GJ_1214" title="GJ 1214">GJ 1214</a>, a star in Ophiuchus, undergoes repeated, cyclical dimming with a period of about 1.5 days consistent with the transit of a small orbiting planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The planet\'s low density (about 40% that of Earth) suggests that the planet may have a substantial component of low-density gas&#8212;possibly <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The proximity of this star to Earth (42 light years) makes it a tempting target for further observations. In April 2010, the naked-eye star <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Ophiuchi" title="Zeta Ophiuchi">&#950; Ophiuchi</a> was occulted by the asteroid <a href="/wiki/824_Anastasia" title="824 Anastasia">824 Anastasia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep-sky_objects">Deep-sky objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ophiuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deep-sky objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Ophiuchus contains several <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Cluster_IC_4665" title="Open Cluster IC 4665">IC 4665</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGC_6633" title="NGC 6633">NGC 6633</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M9" title="Globular Cluster M9">M9</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M10" title="Globular Cluster M10">M10</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M12" title="Globular Cluster M12">M12</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M14" title="Globular Cluster M14">M14</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M19" title="Globular Cluster M19">M19</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M62" title="Globular Cluster M62">M62</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_Cluster_M107" title="Globular Cluster M107">M107</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebula</a> IC 4603-4604.\n<a href="/wiki/Messier_10" title="Messier 10">M10</a> is a fairly close <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a>, only 20,000 light-years from Earth. Now the Serpent is wreathed about his two hands &#8211; a little above his right hand, but in many folds high above his left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>To the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_greece" title="Ancient greece">ancient Greeks</a>, the constellation represented the god <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> struggling with a huge snake that guarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oracle_of_Delphi" title="Oracle of Delphi">Oracle of Delphi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Later myths identified Ophiuchus with <a href="/wiki/Laoco%C3%B6n" title="Laoco&#246;n">Laoco&#246;n</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Trojan</a> priest of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a>, who warned his fellow Trojans about the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_Horse" title="Trojan Horse">Trojan Horse</a> and was later slain by a pair of sea serpents sent by the gods to punish him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-22">[22]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_era" title="Roman era">Roman era</a> mythography,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the figure represents the healer <a href="/wiki/Asclepius" title="Asclepius">Asclepius</a>, who learned the secrets of keeping death at bay after observing one serpent bringing another healing herbs. To prevent the entire human race from becoming <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortal</a> under Asclepius\' care, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> killed him with a bolt of <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, but later placed his image in the heavens to honor his good works. In medieval <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_astronomy" title="Islamic astronomy">Islamic astronomy</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azophi" title="Azophi">Azophi</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Fixed_Stars" title="Book of Fixed Stars">Uranometry</a></i>, 10th century), the constellation was known as <i>Al-&#7716;awwa&#702;</i>, "the snake-charmer".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a> describes Ophiuchus as trampling on <a href="/wiki/Scorpius" title="Scorpius">Scorpius</a> with his feet. This is depicted in Renaissance to Early Modern <a href="/wiki/Star_chart" title="Star chart">star charts</a>, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht D&#252;rer">Albrecht D&#252;rer</a> in 1515; in some depictions (such as that of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/De_Stella_Nova" title="De Stella Nova">De Stella Nova</a></i>, 1606), Scorpius also seems to threaten to sting Serpentarius in the foot.\nThis is consistent with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azophi" title="Azophi">Azophi</a>, who already included <a href="/wiki/Psi_Ophiuchi" title="Psi Ophiuchi">&#968; Oph</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omega_Ophiuchi" title="Omega Ophiuchi">&#969; Oph</a> as the snake-charmer\'s "left foot", and <a href="/wiki/Theta_Ophiuchi" title="Theta Ophiuchi">&#952; Oph</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omicron_Ophiuchi" title="Omicron Ophiuchi">&#959; Oph</a> as his "right foot", making Ophiuchus a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zodiacal_constellation" title="Zodiacal constellation">zodiacal constellation</a> at least as regards his feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This arrangement has been taken as symbolic in later literature, and placed in relation to the words spoken by God to the serpent in the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a> (Genesis 3:15).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" However, this confuses <a href="/wiki/Astrological_sign" title="Astrological sign">sign</a> with <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a>. The signs of the zodiac are a twelve-fold division of the ecliptic, so that each sign spans 30&#176; of celestial longitude, approximately the distance the Sun travels in a month, and (in the Western tradition) are aligned with the seasons so that the <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">March equinox</a> always falls on the boundary between Pisces and Aries.\n</p><p>Constellations, on the other hand, are unequal in size and are based on the positions of the stars. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zodiacal_constellation#Zodiacal_constellations" title="Zodiacal constellation">constellations of the zodiac</a> have only a loose association with the signs of the zodiac, and do not in general coincide with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In Western astrology the constellation of Aquarius, for example, largely corresponds to the sign of Pisces. Similarly, the constellation of Ophiuchus occupies most (November 29 &#8211; December 18<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>) of the sign of Sagittarius (November 23 &#8211; December 21). The differences are due to the fact that the time of year that the sun passes through a particular zodiac constellation\'s position has slowly changed (because of the <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">precession of the equinoxes</a>) over the centuries from when the Greeks, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">Babylonians</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dacians" title="Dacians">Dacians</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zamolxis" title="Zamolxis">Zamolxis</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> originally developed the Zodiac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Citations">Citations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ophiuchus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Citations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Ford, Dominic. <a class="external text" href="https://in-the-sky.org/data/constellation.php?id=60" rel="nofollow">"The Constellation Ophiuchus - In-The-Sky.org"</a>. <i>in-the-sky.org</i><span class="reference-accessdate">.
