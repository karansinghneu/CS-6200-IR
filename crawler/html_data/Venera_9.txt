Its mission was to act as a communications relay for the lander and to explore cloud layers and atmospheric parameters with several instruments and experiments. It performed 17 survey missions from October 26, 1975 to December 25, 1975.\n</p><p>The orbiter consisted of a cylinder with two <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panel</a> wings and a high gain parabolic antenna attached to the curved surface. A bell-shaped unit holding propulsion systems was attached to the bottom of the cylinder, and mounted on top was a 2.4-metre (7.9&#160;ft) sphere which held the lander. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbiter_design">Orbiter design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbiter design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The instruments comprising the orbiter included:\n</p>\n<ul><li>1.6-2.8 <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">&#956;m</a> IR <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">Spectrometer</a></li>\n<li>8-28 &#956;m IR Radiometer</li>\n<li>352&#160;nm UV Photometer</li>\n<li>2 Photo-<a href="/wiki/Polarimeter" title="Polarimeter">polarimeters</a> (335-800&#160;nm)</li>\n<li>300-800&#160;nm Spectrometer</li>\n<li>Lyman-&#945; (alpha) H/D Spectrometer</li>\n<li>Bistatic Radar Mapping</li>\n<li>CM, DM Radio Occultations</li>\n<li>Triaxial <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">Magnetometer</a></li>\n<li>345-380&#160;nm <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a> Camera</li>\n<li>355-445&#160;nm Camera</li>\n<li>6 Electrostatic Analyzers</li>\n<li>2 Modulation <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">Ion</a> Traps</li>\n<li>Low-Energy <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">Proton</a> / <a href="/wiki/Alpha_wave" title="Alpha wave">Alpha</a> detector</li>\n<li>Low-Energy <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">Electron</a> detector</li>\n<li>3 <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">Semiconductor</a> Counters</li>\n<li>2 Gas-Discharge Counters</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_detector" title="Cherenkov detector">Cherenkov detector</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_Venus_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_Venus-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lander">Lander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The lander was encased in a spherical shell before landing to help protect it from the heat of entry as it slowed from 10.7 km/s to 150 m/s.  This sphere was then separated with explosive bolts and a three-domed parachute was deployed which slowed the lander further to 50 m/s at an altitude of 63 km above the planet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keldysh_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-keldysh-6">[6]</a></sup>.\n</p><p>The descent through the cloud layer took about 20 minutes, during which time the lander took measurements of the atmosphere and radioed the information to the orbiter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keldysh_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-keldysh-6">[6]</a></sup>.  To minimize lander damage in the hot atmosphere, the parachute was released at an altitude of 50 km, and the ring-shaped aerodynamic shield provided braking.  The Venusian atmosphere is so dense near the surface that this shield provided a descent rate of 7 m/s as the lander touched down<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keldysh_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-keldysh-6">[6]</a></sup>.  The landing device, a hollow ring surrounding the lower part of the lander, was partly crushed upon touchdown to take up most of the landing impact<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keldysh_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-keldysh-6">[6]</a></sup>.\n</p><p>On October 20, 1975, the lander spacecraft separated from the orbiter, and landing was made with the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> near zenith at 05:13&#160;UTC on October 22. <i>Venera 9</i> landed within a 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) radius of <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Venera_9&amp;params=31.01_N_291.64_E_globe:Venus"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">31&#176;01&#8242;N</span> <span class="longitude">291&#176;38&#8242;E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#65279; / &#65279;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">31.01&#176;N 291.64&#176;E</span><span style="display:none">&#65279; / <span class="geo">31.01; 291.64</span></span></span></a></span>, near <a href="/wiki/Beta_Regio" title="Beta Regio">Beta Regio</a>, on a steep (20&#176;) slope covered with boulders (suspected to be the slope of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> valley, <a href="/wiki/Aikhulu_Chasma" title="Aikhulu Chasma">Aikhylu Chasma</a>). The entry sphere weighed 1,560&#160;kg (3,440&#160;lb) and the surface payload was 660&#160;kg (1,455&#160;lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was the first spacecraft to return an image from the surface of another planet.  Many of the instruments began working immediately after touchdown and the cameras were operational 2 minutes later.  These instruments revealed a smooth surface with numerous stones.  The lander measured a light level of 14,000 lux, similar to that of Earth in full daylight but no direct sunshine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keldysh_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-keldysh-6">[6]</a></sup>.\n</p><p>A system of circulating fluid was used to distribute the heat load. This system, plus pre-cooling prior to entry, permitted operation of the lander for 53 minutes after landing, at which time radio contact with the orbiter was lost as the orbiter moved out of radio range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_Venus_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_Venus-5">[5]</a></sup> During descent, heat dissipation and deceleration were accomplished sequentially by protective hemispheric shells, three parachutes, a disc-shaped drag brake, and a compressible, metal, doughnut-shaped landing cushion. The landing was about 2,200&#160;km (1,400&#160;mi) from the <i><a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">Venera 10</a></i> landing site.\n</p><p><i>Venera 9</i> measured clouds that were 30&#8211;40&#160;km (19&#8211;25&#160;mi) thick with bases at 30&#8211;35&#160;km (19&#8211;22&#160;mi) altitude. It also measured atmospheric chemicals including <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. Other measurements included surface pressure of about 9,100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kilopascals</a> (90&#160;<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>), temperature of 485&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">&#176;C</a> (905&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">&#176;F</a>), and surface light levels comparable to those at <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> mid-latitudes on a cloudy summer day. <i>Venera 9</i> was the first probe to send back black and white television pictures from the Venusian surface showing no shadows, no apparent dust in the air, and a variety of 30 to 40&#160;cm (12 to 16&#160;in) rocks which were not eroded. Planned 360-degree panoramic pictures could not be taken because one of two camera lens covers failed to come off, limiting pictures to 180 degrees. This failure recurred with <i><a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">Venera 10</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lander_payload">Lander payload</span><span
