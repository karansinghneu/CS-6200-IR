He was also a <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosopher of science</a> and a populariser of science. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddington_limit" title="Eddington limit">Eddington limit</a>, the natural limit to the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> of stars, or the radiation generated by accretion onto a compact object, is named in his honour.\n</p><p>Around 1920, he anticipated the discovery and mechanism of nuclear fusion processes in stars, in his paper "The Internal Constitution of the Stars".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-eddington-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-eddington2-4">[4]</a></sup> At that time, the source of stellar energy was a complete mystery; Eddington was the first to correctly speculate that the source was fusion of hydrogen into helium.\n</p><p>He is famous for his work concerning the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>. Eddington wrote a number of articles that announced and explained <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein\'s</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> to the English-speaking world. <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> severed many lines of scientific communication and new developments in German science were not well known in England.  He also conducted an expedition to observe the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919" title="Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919">solar eclipse of 29 May 1919</a> that provided one of the earliest confirmations of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, and he became known for his popular expositions and interpretations of the theory.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Eddington was born 28 December 1882 in <a href="/wiki/Kendal" title="Kendal">Kendal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>), England, the son of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> parents, Arthur Henry Eddington, headmaster of the Quaker School, and Sarah Ann Shout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>His father taught at a Quaker training college in Lancashire before moving to Kendal to become headmaster of Stramongate School. He died in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a> epidemic which swept England in 1884. His mother was left to bring up her two children with relatively little income. The family moved to <a href="/wiki/Weston-super-Mare" title="Weston-super-Mare">Weston-super-Mare</a> where at first Stanley (as his mother and sister always called Eddington) was educated at home before spending three years at a preparatory school.  The family lived at a house called Varzin, 42 Walliscote Road, Weston-super-Mare.  There is a commemorative plaque on the building explaining Sir Arthur\'s contribution to science.\n</p><p>In 1893 Eddington entered Brynmelyn School. He proved to be a most capable scholar, particularly in mathematics and English literature. His performance earned him a scholarship to Owens College, Manchester (what was later to become the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Manchester" title="Victoria University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a>) in 1898, which he was able to attend, having turned 16 that year. He spent the first year in a general course, but turned to <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> for the next three years. Eddington was greatly influenced by his physics and mathematics teachers, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schuster" title="Arthur Schuster">Arthur Schuster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horace_Lamb" title="Horace Lamb">Horace Lamb</a>. At Manchester, Eddington lived at Dalton Hall, where he came under the lasting influence of the Quaker mathematician J. W. Graham. His progress was rapid, winning him several scholarships and he graduated with a B.Sc. in physics with First Class Honours in 1902.\n</p><p>Based on his performance at Owens College, he was awarded a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> in 1902. His tutor at Cambridge was <a href="/wiki/Robert_Alfred_Herman" title="Robert Alfred Herman">Robert Alfred Herman</a> and in 1904 Eddington became the first ever second-year student to be placed as <a href="/wiki/Senior_Wrangler_(University_of_Cambridge)" title="Senior Wrangler (University of Cambridge)">Senior Wrangler</a>.  After receiving his M.A. in 1905, he began research on <a href="/wiki/Thermionic_emission" title="Thermionic emission">thermionic emission</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a>. This did not go well, and meanwhile he spent time teaching mathematics to first year engineering students. This hiatus was brief. Through a recommendation by <a href="/wiki/E._T._Whittaker" title="E. T. Whittaker">E. T. Whittaker</a>, his senior colleague at Trinity College, he secured a position at the Royal Observatory in Greenwich where he was to embark on his career in astronomy, a career whose seeds had been sown even as a young child when he would often "try to count the stars".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Eddington.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2752" data-file-width="3991" decoding="async" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Arthur_Eddington.jpg/260px-Arthur_Eddington.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Arthur_Eddington.jpg/390px-Arthur_Eddington.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Arthur_Eddington.jpg/520px-Arthur_Eddington.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Eddington.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plaque at 42 Walliscote Road, Weston-super-Mare</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In January 1906, Eddington was nominated to the post of chief assistant to the <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">Royal Greenwich Observatory</a>. He left Cambridge for Greenwich the following month. He was put to work on a detailed analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">433 Eros</a> on <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plates</a> that had started in 1900. He developed a new statistical method based on the apparent drift of two background stars, winning him the <a href="/wiki/Smith%27s_Prize" title="Smith\'s Prize">Smith\'s Prize</a> in 1907. The prize won him a Fellowship of Trinity College, Cambridge. In December 1912 <a href="/wiki/George_Darwin" title="George Darwin">George Darwin</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, died suddenly and Eddington was promoted to his chair as the <a href="/wiki/Plumian_Professor_of_Astronomy_and_Experimental_Philosophy" title="Plumian Professor of Astronomy and Experimental Philosophy">Plumian Professor of Astronomy and Experimental Philosophy</a> in early 1913. Later that year, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stawell_Ball" title="Robert Stawell Ball">Robert Ball</a>, holder of the theoretical <a href="/wiki/Lowndean_Professor_of_Astronomy_and_Geometry" title="Lowndean Professor of Astronomy and Geometry">Lowndean chair</a> also died, and Eddington was named the director of the entire <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Observatory" title="Cambridge Observatory">Cambridge Observatory</a> the next year. In May 1914 he was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a>: he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Medal" title="Royal Medal">Royal Medal</a> in 1928 and delivered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakerian_Lecture" title="Bakerian Lecture">Bakerian Lecture</a> in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eddington also investigated the interior of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> through theory, and developed the first true understanding of stellar processes. He began this in 1916 with investigations of possible physical explanations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cepheid_variables" title="Cepheid variables">Cepheid variable stars</a>.  He began by extending <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schwarzschild" title="Karl Schwarzschild">Karl Schwarzschild</a>\'s earlier work on radiation pressure in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lane-Emden_equation" title="Lane-Emden equation">Emden polytropic models</a>. These models treated a star as a sphere of gas held up against gravity by internal thermal pressure, and one of Eddington\'s chief additions was to show that radiation pressure was necessary to prevent collapse of the sphere. He developed his model despite knowingly lacking firm foundations for understanding opacity and energy generation in the stellar interior. However, his results allowed for calculation of temperature, <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> at all points inside a star, and Eddington argued that his theory was so useful for further astrophysical investigation that it should be retained despite not being based on completely accepted physics. <a href="/wiki/James_Jeans" title="James Jeans">James Jeans</a> contributed the important suggestion that stellar matter would certainly be <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionized</a>, but that was the end of any collaboration between the pair, who became famous for their lively debates.\n</p><p>Eddington defended his method by pointing to the utility of his results, particularly his important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass-luminosity_relation" title="Mass-luminosity relation">mass-luminosity relation</a>. This had the unexpected result of showing that virtually all stars, including giants and dwarfs, behaved as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideal_gases" title="Ideal gases">ideal gases</a>.  In the process of developing his stellar models, he sought to overturn current thinking about the sources of stellar energy. Jeans and others defended the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_mechanism" title="Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz mechanism">Kelvin&#8211;Helmholtz mechanism</a>, which was based on classical mechanics, while Eddington speculated broadly about the qualitative and quantitative consequences of possible proton-electron annihilation and nuclear fusion processes.\n</p><p>Around 1920, he anticipated the discovery and mechanism of nuclear fusion processes in stars, in his paper <i>The Internal Constitution of the Stars</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-eddington-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-eddington2-4">[4]</a></sup> At that time, the source of stellar energy was a complete mystery; Eddington correctly speculated that the source was fusion of hydrogen into helium, liberating enormous energy according to Einstein\'s equation <i>E = mc<sup>2</sup></i>. This was a particularly remarkable development since at that time fusion and thermonuclear energy, and even the fact that stars are largely composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicity</a>), had not yet been discovered. Eddington\'s paper, based on knowledge at the time, reasoned that:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><ol><li>The leading theory of stellar energy, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Contraction_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Contraction hypothesis (page does not exist)">contraction hypothesis</a>, should cause stars\' rotation to visibly speed up due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">conservation of angular momentum</a>. But observations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cepheid" title="Cepheid">Cepheid</a> variable stars showed this was not happening.</li>\n<li>The only other known plausible source of energy was conversion of matter to energy; Einstein had shown some years earlier that a small amount of matter was equivalent to a large amount of energy.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Francis_William_Aston" title="Francis William Aston">Francis Aston</a> had also recently shown that the mass of a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> atom was about 0.8% less than the mass of the four hydrogen atoms which would, combined, form a helium atom, suggesting that if such a combination could happen, it would release considerable energy as a byproduct.</li>\n<li>If a star contained just 5% of fusible hydrogen, it would suffice to explain how stars got their energy. (We now know that most \'ordinary\' stars contain far more than 5% hydrogen)</li>\n<li>Further elements might also be fused, and other scientists had speculated that stars were the "crucible" in which light elements combined to create heavy elements, but without more accurate measurements of their <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic masses</a> nothing more could be said at the time.</li></ol></dd></dl>\n<p>All of these speculations were proven correct in the following decades.\n</p><p>With these assumptions, he demonstrated that the interior temperature of stars must be millions of degrees. In 1924, he discovered the <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">mass-luminosity relation</a> for <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> (see Lecchini in <a href="#External_links_and_references">#External links and references</a> ).  Despite some disagreement, Eddington\'s models were eventually accepted as a powerful tool for further investigation, particularly in issues of stellar evolution.  The confirmation of his estimated stellar diameters by Michelson in 1920 proved crucial in convincing astronomers unused to Eddington\'s intuitive, exploratory style. Eddington\'s theory appeared in mature form in 1926 as <i>The Internal Constitution of the Stars</i>, which became an important text for training an entire generation of astrophysicists.\n</p><p>Eddington\'s work in astrophysics in the late 1920s and the 1930s continued his work in stellar structure, and precipitated further clashes with Jeans and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Arthur_Milne" title="Edward Arthur Milne">Edward Arthur Milne</a>. An important topic was the extension of his models to take advantage of developments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>, including the use of degeneracy physics in describing dwarf stars.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_Chandrasekhar_on_existence_of_black_holes">Dispute with Chandrasekhar on existence of black holes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dispute with Chandrasekhar on existence of black holes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The topic of extension of his models precipitated his famous dispute with <a href="/wiki/Subrahmanyan_Chandrasekhar" title="Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar">Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar</a>, who was then a student at Cambridge. Chandrasekhar\'s work presaged the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>, which at the time seemed so absurdly non-physical that Eddington refused to believe that Chandrasekhar\'s purely mathematical derivation had consequences for the real world. History clearly proved Eddington wrong, but his motivation remains a matter of some controversy. Chandrasekhar\'s narrative of this incident, in which his work is harshly rejected, portrays Eddington as rather cruel, dogmatic, and racist.  Eddington\'s criticism seems to have been based on a suspicion that a purely mathematical derivation from relativity theory was not enough to explain away the seemingly daunting physical paradoxes that were inherent to degenerate stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativity">Relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Eddington was Secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a>, which meant he was the first to receive a series of letters and papers from <a href="/wiki/Willem_de_Sitter" title="Willem de Sitter">Willem de Sitter</a> regarding Einstein\'s theory of general relativity. Eddington was fortunate in being not only one of the few astronomers with the mathematical skills to understand general relativity, but owing to his internationalist and pacifist views inspired by his Quaker religious beliefs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandrasekhar1983_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chandrasekhar1983-9">[9]</a></sup> one of the few at the time who was still interested in pursuing a theory developed by a German physicist. He quickly became the chief supporter and expositor of relativity in Britain. He and <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Watson_Dyson" title="Frank Watson Dyson">Frank Watson Dyson</a> organized two expeditions to observe a <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919" title="Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919">solar eclipse in 1919</a> to make the first empirical test of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>\'s theory: the measurement of the deflection of light by the sun\'s gravitational field. In fact, Dyson\'s argument for the indispensability of Eddington\'s expertise in this test was what prevented Eddington from eventually having to enter military service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandrasekhar1983_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chandrasekhar1983-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When conscription was introduced in Britain on 2 March 1916, Eddington intended to apply for an exemption as a <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objector</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup> Cambridge University authorities instead requested and were granted an exemption on the ground of Eddington\'s work being of national interest. In 1918, this was appealed against by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Service" title="Ministry of National Service">Ministry of National Service</a>. Before the appeal tribunal in June, Eddington claimed conscientious objector status, which was not recognized and would have ended his exemption in August 1918. A further two hearings took place in June and July, respectively. Eddington\'s personal statement at the June hearing about his objection to war based on religious grounds is on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Frank_Dyson" title="Sir Frank Dyson">Sir Frank Dyson</a>, supported Eddington at the July hearing with a written statement, emphasising Eddington\'s essential role in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919" title="Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919">solar eclipse</a> expedition to Principe in May 1919. Eddington made clear his willingness to serve in the <a href="/wiki/Friends%27_Ambulance_Unit" title="Friends\' Ambulance Unit">Friends\' Ambulance Unit</a>, under the jurisdiction of the British  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a>, or as a harvest labourer. However, the tribunal\'s decision to grant a further twelve months exemption from military service was on condition of Eddington continuing his astronomy work, in particular in preparation for the Principe expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas1956_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas1956-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandrasekhar1983_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chandrasekhar1983-9">[9]</a></sup> The war ended before the end of his exemption.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1919_eclipse_positive.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="899" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="334" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1919_eclipse_positive.jpg/260px-1919_eclipse_positive.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1919_eclipse_positive.jpg/390px-1919_eclipse_positive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1919_eclipse_positive.jpg/520px-1919_eclipse_positive.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1919_eclipse_positive.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of Eddington\'s photographs of the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_29_May_1919" title="Solar eclipse of 29 May 1919">solar eclipse of 29 May 1919</a>, presented in his 1920 paper announcing its success, confirming <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>\'s theory that light "bends"</div></div></div>\n<p>After the war, Eddington travelled to the island of <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Pr&#237;ncipe">Pr&#237;ncipe</a> off the west coast of Africa to watch the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919" title="Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919">solar eclipse of 29 May 1919</a>. During the eclipse, he took pictures of the stars (several stars in the <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">Hyades cluster</a> include <a href="/wiki/Kappa_Tauri" title="Kappa Tauri">Kappa Tauri</a> of the constellation <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">Taurus</a>) in the region around the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eddington1920_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eddington1920-10">[10]</a></sup> According to the theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, stars with light rays that passed near the Sun would appear to have been slightly shifted because their light had been curved by its gravitational field. This effect is noticeable only during eclipses, since otherwise the Sun\'s brightness obscures the affected stars. Eddington showed that Newtonian gravitation could be interpreted to predict half the shift predicted by Einstein.\n</p><p>Eddington\'s observations published the next year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eddington1920_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eddington1920-10">[10]</a></sup> confirmed Einstein\'s theory, and were hailed at the time as evidence of general relativity over the Newtonian model.  The news was reported in newspapers all over the world as a major story. Afterward, Eddington embarked on a campaign to popularize relativity and the expedition as landmarks both in scientific development and international scientific relations.\n</p><p>It has been claimed that Eddington\'s observations were of poor quality, and he had unjustly discounted simultaneous observations at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sobral,_Brazil" title="Sobral, Brazil">Sobral, Brazil</a>, which appeared closer to the Newtonian model, but a 1979 re-analysis with modern measuring equipment and contemporary software validated Eddington\'s results and conclusions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The quality of the 1919 results was indeed poor compared to later observations, but was sufficient to persuade contemporary astronomers.  The rejection of the results from the Brazil expedition was due to a defect in the telescopes used which, again, was completely accepted and well understood by contemporary astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhysToday_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-PhysToday-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Throughout this period, Eddington lectured on relativity, and was particularly well known for his ability to explain the concepts in lay terms as well as scientific. He collected many of these into the <i>Mathematical Theory of Relativity</i> in 1923, which <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> suggested was "the finest presentation of the subject in any language." He was an early advocate of Einstein\'s General Relativity, and an interesting anecdote well illustrates his humour and personal intellectual investment: <a href="/wiki/Ludwik_Silberstein" title="Ludwik Silberstein">Ludwik Silberstein</a>, a physicist who thought of himself as an expert on relativity, approached Eddington at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>\'s (6 November) 1919 meeting where he had defended Einstein\'s Relativity with his Brazil-Principe Solar Eclipse calculations with some degree of skepticism, and ruefully charged Arthur as one who claimed to be one of three men who actually understood the theory (Silberstein, of course, was including himself and Einstein as the other). When Eddington refrained from replying, he insisted Arthur not be "so shy", whereupon Eddington replied, "Oh, no! I was wondering who the third one might be!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmology">Cosmology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cosmology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Eddington was also heavily involved with the development of the first generation of general relativistic cosmological models. He had been investigating the instability of the Einstein universe when he learned of both <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema&#238;tre">Lema&#238;tre\'s</a> 1927 paper postulating an expanding or contracting universe and Hubble\'s work on the recession of the spiral nebulae. He felt the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a> must have played the crucial role in the universe\'s evolution from an Einsteinian steady state to its current expanding state, and most of his cosmological investigations focused on the constant\'s significance and characteristics.  In <i>The Mathematical Theory of Relativity,</i> Eddington interpreted the cosmological constant to mean that the universe is "self-gauging".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_theory_and_the_Eddington_number">Fundamental theory and the Eddington number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fundamental theory and the Eddington number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 1920s until his death, Eddington increasingly concentrated on what he called "fundamental theory" which was intended to be a unification of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a>, cosmology, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>. At first he progressed along "traditional" lines, but turned increasingly to an almost <a href="/wiki/Numerology" title="Numerology">numerological</a> analysis of the dimensionless ratios of fundamental constants.\n</p><p>His basic approach was to combine several fundamental constants in order to produce a dimensionless number. In many cases these would result in numbers close to 10<sup>40</sup>, its square, or its square root. He was convinced that the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and the charge of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> were a <i>natural and complete specification for constructing a Universe</i> and that their values were not accidental. One of the discoverers of quantum mechanics, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a>, also pursued this line of investigation, which has become known as the <a href="/wiki/Dirac_large_numbers_hypothesis" title="Dirac large numbers hypothesis">Dirac large numbers hypothesis</a>, and some scientists even today believe it has something to it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Which scientists? What is the \'something\'? (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>A somewhat damaging statement in his defence of these concepts involved the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_structure_constant" title="Fine structure constant">fine structure constant</a>, &#945;. At the time it was measured to be very close to 1/136, and he argued that the value should in fact be exactly 1/136 for epistemological reasons. Later measurements placed the value much closer to 1/137, at which point he switched his line of reasoning to argue that one more should be added to the <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry)" title="Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)">degrees of freedom</a>, so that the value should in fact be exactly 1/137, the <a href="/wiki/Eddington_number" title="Eddington number">Eddington number</a>. Wags at the time started calling him "Arthur Adding-one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  This change of stance detracted from Eddington\'s credibility in the physics community. The <a class="external text" href="http://physics.nist.gov/cgi-bin/cuu/Value?alphinv%7Csearch_for=fine+structure+constant" rel="nofollow">current measured value</a> is estimated at 1/137.035 999 074(44).\n</p><p>Eddington believed he had identified an algebraic basis for fundamental physics, which he termed "E-numbers" (representing a certain <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">group</a>&#160;&#8211; a <a href="/wiki/Clifford_algebra" title="Clifford algebra">Clifford algebra</a>). These in effect incorporated <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> into a higher-dimensional structure. While his theory has long been neglected by the general physics community, similar algebraic notions underlie many modern attempts at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unified_theory" title="Grand unified theory">grand unified theory</a>. Moreover, Eddington\'s emphasis on the values of the fundamental constants, and specifically upon dimensionless numbers derived from them, is nowadays a central concern of physics. In particular, he predicted a number of hydrogen atoms in the Universe 136 &#215; 2<sup>256</sup>, or equivalently the half of the total number of particles protons + electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> When equalized with the non-dark energy equivalent number of hydrogen atoms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (3/10) &#215; Rc<sup>2</sup>/GmH, this corresponds to a Universe radius R = 13.8 Giga light year, a value predicted for years from universal constants using an atomic-cosmic symmetry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and compatible with <i>c</i> times the so-called age of the Universe, 13.80(4) Gyr, as determined by the <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck mission</a> in March 2003.\n</p><p>He did not complete this line of research before his death in 1944; his book <i>Fundamental Theory</i> was published posthumously in 1948.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eddington_number_for_cycling"><span id="E_number"></span>Eddington number for cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eddington number for cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Eddington is credited with devising a measure of a <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cyclist\'s</a> long-distance riding achievements. The Eddington number in the context of cycling is defined as the maximum number E such that the cyclist has cycled E miles on E days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> For example, an Eddington number of 70 would imply that the cyclist has cycled at least 70 miles in a day on 70 occasions. Achieving a high Eddington number is difficult since moving from, say, 70 to 75 will probably require more than five new long distance rides since any rides shorter than 75 miles will no longer be included in the reckoning. Eddington\'s own E-number was 84.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Eddington number for cycling is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index"><i>h</i>-index</a> that quantifies both the actual scientific productivity and the apparent scientific impact of a scientist.\n</p><p>The Eddington Number for cycling has units (indeed applying it to any physical property will result in E having units).  For example, an E of 62 miles means a cyclist has covered 62 or more miles on 62 or more days.  However, in units of kilometers the 62 miles becomes 100&#160;km. It is possible that the cyclist, while having covered 100&#160;km on 62 days or more, may <b>not</b> have covered 100&#160;km on 100 days or more. Thus the order of bicyclists may change depending on units used. Using the original miles, one cyclist may have an Eddington number of 60 &#8211; 60 miles (97&#160;km) in 60 days, another of 50 (corresponding to 80&#160;km). However, the latter may be a regular on a distance like this and get a km-Eddington of 80, while the former only had those 60 days riding, and thus stays at a km-Eddington of 60.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Idealism">Idealism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" The mind-stuff is not spread in space and time; these are part of the cyclic scheme ultimately derived out of it ... It is necessary to keep reminding ourselves that all knowledge of our environment from which the world of physics is constructed, has entered in the form of messages transmitted along the nerves to the seat of consciousness ... Consciousness is not sharply defined, but fades into subconsciousness; and beyond that we must postulate something indefinite but yet continuous with our mental nature ... It is difficult for the matter-of-fact physicist to accept the view that the substratum of everything is of mental character. But no one can deny that mind is the first and most direct thing in our experience, and all else is remote inference.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Eddington, <i>The Nature of the Physical World</i>, 276&#8211;81.</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idealist" title="Idealist">idealist</a> conclusion was not integral to his epistemology but was based on two main arguments.\n</p><p>The first derives directly from current physical theory. Briefly, mechanical theories of the ether and of the behaviour of fundamental particles have been discarded in both relativity and quantum physics. From this, Eddington inferred that a materialistic metaphysics was outmoded and that, in consequence, since the disjunction of materialism or idealism are assumed to be exhaustive, an idealistic metaphysics is required. The second, and more interesting argument, was based on Eddington\'s epistemology, and may be regarded as consisting of two parts. First, all we know of the objective world is its structure, and the structure of the objective world is precisely mirrored in our own consciousness. We therefore have no reason to doubt that the objective world too is "mind-stuff". Dualistic metaphysics, then, cannot be evidentially supported.\n</p><p>But, second, not only can we not know that the objective world is nonmentalistic, we also cannot intelligibly suppose that it could be material. To conceive of a dualism entails attributing material properties to the objective world. However, this presupposes that we could observe that the objective world has material properties. But this is absurd, for whatever is observed must ultimately be the content of our own consciousness, and consequently, nonmaterial.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ian_Barbour" title="Ian Barbour">Ian Barbour</a>, in his book <i>Issues in Science and Religion</i> (1966), p.&#160;133, cites Eddington\'s <i>The Nature of the Physical World</i> (1928) for a text that argues the <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Heisenberg</a> <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Uncertainty Principles</a> provides a scientific basis for "the defense of the idea of human freedom" and his <i>Science and the Unseen World</i> (1929) for support of philosophical idealism "the thesis that reality is basically mental".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_De_Koninck" title="Charles De Koninck">Charles De Koninck</a> points out that Eddington believed in objective reality existing apart from our minds, but was using the phrase "mind-stuff" to highlight the inherent intelligibility of the world: that our minds and the physical world are made of the same "stuff" and that our minds are the inescapable connection to the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Koninck_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Koninck-21">[21]</a></sup> As De Koninck quotes Eddington,\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There is a doctrine well known to philosophers that the moon ceases to exist when no one is looking at it. I will not discuss the doctrine since I have not the least idea what is the meaning of the word existence when used in this connection. At any rate the science of astronomy has not been based on this spasmodic kind of moon. In the scientific world (which has to fulfill functions less vague than merely existing) there is a moon which appeared on the scene before the astronomer; it reflects sunlight when no one sees it; it has mass when no one is measuring the mass; it is distant 240,000 miles from the earth when no one is surveying the distance; and it will eclipse the sun in 1999 even if the human race has succeeded in killing itself off before that date.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Eddington, <i>The Nature of the Physical World</i>, 226</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indeterminism">Indeterminism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Indeterminism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Indeterminism" title="Indeterminism">Indeterminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indeterminism#Science" title="Indeterminism">Indeterminism in science</a></div>\n<p>Against <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> and others who advocated <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">determinism</a>, indeterminism&#8212;championed by Eddington<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Koninck_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_Koninck-21">[21]</a></sup>&#8212;says that a physical object has an <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontologically</a> undetermined component that is not due to the <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemological</a> limitations of physicists\' understanding. The <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">uncertainty principle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, then, would not necessarily be due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">hidden variables</a> but to an indeterminism in nature itself.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_and_philosophical_writings">Popular and philosophical writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Popular and philosophical writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Eddington wrote a clever parody of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Rubaiyat_of_Omar_Khayyam" title="The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam">The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> recounting his 1919 solar eclipse experiment. It contained the following <a href="/wiki/Quatrain" title="Quatrain">quatrain</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<center>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">\n<p>Oh leave the Wise our measures to collate<br/>\n&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;One thing at least is certain, LIGHT has WEIGHT,<br/>\nOne thing is certain, and the rest debate&#8212;<br/>\nLight-rays, when near the Sun, DO NOT GO STRAIGHT.\n</p>\n</div>\n</blockquote>\n</center>\n<p>During the 1920s and 30s, Eddington gave numerous lectures, interviews, and radio broadcasts on relativity, in addition to his textbook <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_publications_in_physics#The_Mathematical_Theory_of_Relativity" title="List of publications in physics">The Mathematical Theory of Relativity</a></i>, and later, quantum mechanics. Many of these were gathered into books, including <i>The Nature of the Physical World</i> and <i>New Pathways in Science</i>. His skillful use of literary allusions and humour helped make these famously difficult subjects quite accessible.\n</p><p>Eddington\'s books and lectures were immensely popular with the public, not only because of Eddington\'s clear and entertaining exposition, but also for his willingness to discuss the philosophical and religious implications of the new physics. He argued for a deeply rooted philosophical harmony between scientific investigation and religious mysticism, and also that the positivist nature of modern physics (i.e., relativity and quantum physics) provided new room for personal religious experience and free will. Unlike many other spiritual scientists, he rejected the idea that science could provide proof of religious propositions.\n</p><p>He is sometimes misunderstood as having promoted the <a href="/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem" title="Infinite monkey theorem">infinite monkey theorem</a> in his 1928 book <i>The Nature of the Physical World</i>, with the phrase "If an army of monkeys were strumming on typewriters, they might write all the books in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>". It is clear from the context that Eddington is not suggesting that the probability of this happening is worthy of serious consideration. On the contrary, it was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhetorical" title="Rhetorical">rhetorical</a> illustration of the fact that below certain levels of probability, the term <i>improbable</i> is functionally equivalent to <i>impossible</i>.\n</p><p>His popular writings made him a household name in Great Britain between the world wars.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Eddington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Eddington died of cancer in the Evelyn Nursing Home, Cambridge, on 22 November 1944. He was unmarried.
