language">Indonesian</a>: <i>Jawa</i>; <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a>: &#43415;&#43438;; <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_language" title="Sundanese language">Sundanese</a>: <span lang="su" style="font-family:\'Sundanese Unicode\',\'Noto Sans Sundanese\'; font-size:18px;">&#7055;&#7069;</span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> on the south and the <a href="/wiki/Java_Sea" title="Java Sea">Java Sea</a> on the north. With a population of over 141 million (Java only) or 145&#160;million (including the inhabitants of its surrounding islands), Java is the home to 56.7 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Indonesia" title="Demographics of Indonesia">Indonesian population</a> and is the world\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_population" title="List of islands by population">most populous island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citypopulation.de_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-citypopulation.de-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> capital city, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, is located on its <a href="/wiki/West_Java" title="West Java">northwestern</a> coast. Much of Indonesian history took place on Java. It was the center of powerful Hindu-Buddhist empires, the Islamic sultanates, and the core of the colonial <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>. Java was also the center of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Indonesia" title="History of Indonesia">Indonesian struggle for independence</a> during the 1930s and 1940s. Java dominates <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> politically, economically and culturally. Four of Indonesia\'s eight UNESCO world heritage sites are located in Java: <a href="/wiki/Ujung_Kulon_National_Park" title="Ujung Kulon National Park">Ujung Kulon National Park</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borobudur_Temple" title="Borobudur Temple">Borobudur Temple</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prambanan_Temple" title="Prambanan Temple">Prambanan Temple</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sangiran_Early_Man_Site" title="Sangiran Early Man Site">Sangiran Early Man Site</a>.\n</p><p>Formed mostly as the result of volcanic eruptions from geologic subduction between <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Plate" title="Sunda Plate">Sunda Plate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Plate" title="Australian Plate">Australian Plate</a>, Java is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">13th largest island</a> in the world and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_of_Indonesia" title="List of islands of Indonesia">fifth largest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> by landmass at about 138,800 square kilometres (53,600&#160;sq&#160;mi). A chain of volcanic mountains forms an east&#8211;west spine along the island. Three main languages are spoken on the island: <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_language" title="Sundanese language">Sundanese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Madurese_language" title="Madurese language">Madurese</a>, where Javanese is the most spoken; it is the native language of about 60 million <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese people</a> in Indonesia, most of whom live on Java. Furthermore, most residents are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingual" title="Bilingual">bilingual</a>, speaking <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> (the official language of Indonesia) as their first or second language. While the majority of the people of Java are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a>, Java\'s population comprises people of diverse religious beliefs, ethnicities, and cultures.\n</p><p>Java is divided into four administrative provinces, <a href="/wiki/West_Java" title="West Java">West Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Java" title="Central Java">Central Java</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Java" title="East Java">East Java</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Banten" title="Banten">Banten</a>, and two special regions, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yogyakarta_(special_region)" title="Yogyakarta (special region)">Yogyakarta</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The origins of the name "Java" are not clear. One possibility is that the island was named after the <a href="/wiki/Foxtail_millet" title="Foxtail millet"><i>j&#225;wa-wut</i></a> plant, which was said to be common in the island during the time, and that prior to Indianization the island had different names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are other possible sources: the word <i>ja&#250;</i> and its variations mean "beyond" or "distant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raffles,_Thomas_E._1965._Page_3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raffles,_Thomas_E._1965._Page_3-3">[3]</a></sup> And, in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>yava</i> means barley, a plant for which the island was famous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raffles,_Thomas_E._1965._Page_3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raffles,_Thomas_E._1965._Page_3-3">[3]</a></sup> "Yavadvipa" is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>\'s earliest epic, the <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sugriva" title="Sugriva">Sugriva</a>, the chief of <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a>\'s army dispatched his men to Yavadvipa, the island of Java, in search of <a href="/wiki/Sita" title="Sita">Sita</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was hence referred to in India by the Sanskrit name "y&#257;vaka dv&#299;pa" (dv&#299;pa = island). Java is mentioned in the ancient Tamil text <i><a href="/wiki/Manimekalai" title="Manimekalai">Manimekalai</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Chithalai_Chathanar" title="Chithalai Chathanar">Chithalai Chathanar</a> that states that Java had a kingdom with a capital called Nagapuram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Another source states that the "Java" word is derived from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Austronesian_language" title="Proto-Austronesian language">Proto-Austronesian</a> root word, <i>Iawa</i> that meaning "home".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The great island of Iabadiu or Jabadiu was mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geographia</a></i> composed around 150 CE in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. <i>Iabadiu</i> is said to mean "barley island", to be rich in gold, and have a silver town called Argyra at the west end. The name indicates Java,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AncientGeo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AncientGeo-9">[9]</a></sup> and seems to be derived from the Sanskrit name Java-dvipa (Yavadvipa).\n</p><p>The annual news of Songshu and Liangshu referred Java as She-po (5th century CE), He-ling (640&#8211;818), then called it She-po again until the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> (1271&#8211;1368), where they began mentioning Zhao-Wa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Ma_Huan" title="Ma Huan">Ma Huan</a>\'s book (the <a href="/wiki/Yingya_Shenglan" title="Yingya Shenglan">Yingya Shenlan</a>), the Chinese call Java as Chao-Wa, and the island was called <i>She-p&#243;</i> (<i>She-b&#243;</i>) in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_de%27_Marignolli" title="Giovanni de\' Marignolli">John of Marignolli</a> returned from China to <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>, he stayed at the <a href="/wiki/Sheba" title="Sheba">Kingdom of Saba</a> for a few months, which he said had many elephants and led by a <a href="/wiki/Tribhuwana_Wijayatunggadewi" title="Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi">queen</a>; Saba may be his interpretation of She-b&#243;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes_of_Java" title="Volcanoes of Java">Volcanoes of Java</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Bromo_(2012).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG/250px-Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG/375px-Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG/500px-Mount_Bromo_%282012%29.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Bromo_(2012).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Bromo" title="Mount Bromo">Mount Bromo</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Java" title="East Java">East Java</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Java lies between <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> to the west and <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a> to the east. <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> lies to the north and <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Island" title="Christmas Island">Christmas Island</a> is to the south. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">world\'s 13th largest island</a>. Java is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Java_Sea" title="Java Sea">Java Sea</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a> to the west, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the south and <a href="/wiki/Bali_Strait" title="Bali Strait">Bali Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madura_Strait" title="Madura Strait">Madura Strait</a> in the east.\n</p><p>Java is almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> origin; it contains thirty-eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountains" title="Mountains">mountains</a> forming an east&#8211;west spine that have at one time or another been active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>. The highest volcano in Java is Mount <a href="/wiki/Semeru" title="Semeru">Semeru</a> (3,676 metres (12,060&#160;ft)). The most active volcano in Java and also in Indonesia is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Merapi" title="Mount Merapi">Mount Merapi</a> (2,930 metres (9,610&#160;ft)). In total, Java boast more than 150 mountains.\n</p><p>More mountains and highlands help to split the interior into a series of relatively isolated regions suitable for <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">wet-rice</a> cultivation; the rice lands of Java are among the richest in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RICKLEFS_p15_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-RICKLEFS_p15-13">[13]</a></sup> Java was the first place where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_coffee" title="Indonesian coffee">Indonesian coffee</a> was grown, starting in 1699. Today, <a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">Coffea arabica</a> is grown on the Ijen Plateau by small-holders and larger plantations.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="585" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg/220px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg/330px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg/440px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_De_weg_van_Buitenzorg_naar_de_Preanger_Regentschappen_TMnr_3728-429c.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parahyangan" title="Parahyangan">Parahyangan</a> highland near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buitenzorg" title="Buitenzorg">Buitenzorg</a>, c. 1865&#8211;1872</div></div></div>\n<p>The area of Java is approximately 150,000 square kilometres (58,000&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MONK_7_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MONK_7-14">[14]</a></sup> It is about 1,000&#160;km (620&#160;mi) long and up to 210&#160;km (130&#160;mi) wide. The island\'s longest <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> is the 600&#160;km long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solo_River_(Java)" title="Solo River (Java)">Solo River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The river rises from its source in central Java at the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Lawu" title="Mount Lawu">Lawu</a> volcano, then flows north and eastward to its mouth in the <a href="/wiki/Java_Sea" title="Java Sea">Java Sea</a> near the city of <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a>. Other major rivers are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brantas" title="Brantas">Brantas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citarum" title="Citarum">Citarum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuk_River" title="Manuk River">Cimanuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serayu_River" title="Serayu River">Serayu</a>.\n</p><p>The average temperature ranges from 22&#160;&#176;C (72&#160;&#176;F) to 29&#160;&#176;C (84&#160;&#176;F); average humidity is 75%. The northern coastal plains are normally hotter, averaging 34&#160;&#176;C (93&#160;&#176;F) during the day in the <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. The south coast is generally cooler than the north, and highland areas inland are even cooler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a> begins in November and ends in April. During that rain falls mostly in the afternoons and intermittently during other parts of the year. The wettest months are January and February.\n</p><p>West Java is wetter than East Java and mountainous regions receive much higher rainfall. The <a href="/wiki/Parahyangan" title="Parahyangan">Parahyangan</a> highlands of West Java receive over 4,000 millimetres (160&#160;in) annually, while the north coast of East Java receives 900 millimetres (35&#160;in) annually.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_environment">Natural environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg/220px-Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg/330px-Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg/440px-Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Banteng_at_Alas_Purwo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Banteng" title="Banteng">Banteng</a> at <a href="/wiki/Alas_Purwo_National_Park" title="Alas Purwo National Park">Alas Purwo</a>, eastern edge of Java</div></div></div><p>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> of Java is <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a>, with ecosystems ranging from coastal <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forests on the north coast, rocky coastal cliffs on the southern coast, and low-lying tropical forests to high altitude rainforests on the slopes of mountainous volcanic regions in the interior. The Javan environment and climate gradually alters from west to east; from wet and humid dense rainforest in western parts, to a dry <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> environment in the east, corresponding to the climate and rainfall in these regions.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Java-1934_-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="550" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Java-1934_-2.jpg/220px-Java-1934_-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Java-1934_-2.jpg/330px-Java-1934_-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Java-1934_-2.jpg/440px-Java-1934_-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Java-1934_-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male <a href="/wiki/Javan_rhinoceros" title="Javan rhinoceros">Javan rhino</a> shot in 1934 in West Java. Today only small numbers of Javan rhino survive in <a href="/wiki/Ujung_Kulon_National_Park" title="Ujung Kulon National Park">Ujung Kulon</a>; it is the world\'s rarest rhino.</div></div></div><p>Originally Javan wildlife supported a rich biodiversity, where numbers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic" title="Endemic">endemic</a> species of flora and fauna flourished; such as the <a href="/wiki/Javan_rhinoceros" title="Javan rhinoceros">Javan rhinoceros</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Banteng" title="Banteng">Javan banteng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javan_warty_pig" title="Javan warty pig">Javan warty pig</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nisaetus_bartelsi" title="Nisaetus bartelsi">Javan hawk-eagle</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavo_muticus" title="Pavo muticus">Javan peafowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silvery_gibbon" title="Silvery gibbon">Javan silvery gibbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javan_lutung" title="Javan lutung">Javan lutung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java_mouse-deer" title="Java mouse-deer">Java mouse-deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javan_rusa" title="Javan rusa">Javan rusa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Javan_leopard" title="Javan leopard">Javan leopard</a>. With over 450 species of birds and 37 endemic species, Java is a birdwatcher\'s paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There are about 130 freshwater fish species in Java.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There are also several <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_amphibians_of_Java" title="List of amphibians of Java">amphibian species in Java</a>, including 5 species of <a href="/wiki/Rhacophoridae" title="Rhacophoridae">tree frogs</a>.\n</p><p>Since ancient times, people have opened the rainforest, altered the ecosystem, shaped the landscapes and created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_paddy" title="Rice paddy">rice paddy</a> and terraces to support the growing population. Javan rice terraces have existed for more than a millennium, and had supported ancient agricultural kingdoms. The growing human population has put severe pressure on Java\'s wildlife, as rainforests were diminished and confined to highland slopes or isolated peninsulas. Some of Java\'s endemic species are now critically endangered, with some already extinct; Java used to have <a href="/wiki/Javan_tiger" title="Javan tiger">Javan tigers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Javan_elephant" title="Javan elephant">Javan elephants</a>, but both have been rendered extinct. Today, several national parks exist in Java that protect the remnants of its fragile wildlife, such as <a href="/wiki/Ujung_Kulon_National_Park" title="Ujung Kulon National Park">Ujung Kulon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Halimun_Salak_National_Park" title="Mount Halimun Salak National Park">Mount Halimun-Salak</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gunung_Gede_Pangrango_National_Park" title="Gunung Gede Pangrango National Park">Gede Pangrango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baluran_National_Park" title="Baluran National Park">Baluran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meru_Betiri_National_Park" title="Meru Betiri National Park">Meru Betiri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromo_Tengger_Semeru_National_Park" title="Bromo Tengger Semeru National Park">Bromo Tengger Semeru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alas_Purwo_National_Park" title="Alas Purwo National Park">Alas Purwo</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sumbing,_Java.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Sumbing%2C_Java.jpg/220px-Sumbing%2C_Java.jpg" href="/wiki/File:Sumbing,_Java.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Sumbing" title="Mount Sumbing">Mount Sumbing</a> surrounded by rice fields. Java\'s volcanic topography and rich agricultural lands are the fundamental factors in its history.</div></div></div>\n<p>Fossilised remains of <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i>, popularly known as the "<a href="/wiki/Java_Man" title="Java Man">Java Man</a>", dating back 1.7&#160;million years were found along the banks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bengawan_Solo_River" title="Bengawan Solo River">Bengawan Solo River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The island\'s exceptional fertility and rainfall allowed the development of wet-field rice cultivation, which required sophisticated levels of cooperation between villages. Out of these village alliances, small kingdoms developed. The chain of volcanic mountains and associated highlands running the length of Java kept its interior regions and peoples separate and relatively isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Before the advent of Islamic states and European colonialism, the rivers provided the main means of communication, although Java\'s many rivers are mostly short. Only the <a href="/wiki/Brantas_River" title="Brantas River">Brantas</a> and Sala rivers could provide long-distance communication, and this way their valleys supported the centres of major kingdoms. A system of roads, permanent bridges and toll gates is thought to have been established in Java by at least the mid-17th century. Local powers could disrupt the routes as could the wet season and road use was highly dependent on constant maintenance. Subsequently, communication between Java\'s population was difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Borobudur-Temple-Park_Indonesia_Stupas-of-Borobudur-04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 9th century <a title="Tarumanagara">Taruma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunda_kingdom" title="Sunda kingdom">Sunda</a> kingdoms of western Java appeared in the 4th and 7th centuries respectively, while the <a href="/wiki/Kalingga_Kingdom" title="Kalingga Kingdom">Kalingga Kingdom</a> sent embassies to China starting in 640.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>53,79</span></sup> However, the first major principality was the <a href="/wiki/Medang_Kingdom" title="Medang Kingdom">Medang Kingdom</a> that was founded in central Java at the beginning of the 8th century. Medang\'s religion centred on the Hindu god <a href="/wiki/Shiva" title="Shiva">Shiva</a>, and the kingdom produced some of Java\'s earliest Hindu temples on the <a href="/wiki/Dieng_Plateau" title="Dieng Plateau">Dieng Plateau</a>. Around the 8th century the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sailendra" title="Sailendra">Sailendra</a> dynasty rose in <a href="/wiki/Kedu_Plain" title="Kedu Plain">Kedu Plain</a> and become the patron of <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahayana</a> <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>. This ancient kingdom built monuments such as the 9th century <a href="/wiki/Borobudur" title="Borobudur">Borobudur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prambanan" title="Prambanan">Prambanan</a> in central Java.\n</p><p>Around the 10th century the centre of power shifted from central to eastern Java. The eastern Javanese kingdoms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kediri_(historical_kingdom)" title="Kediri (historical kingdom)">Kediri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singhasari" title="Singhasari">Singhasari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Majapahit" title="Majapahit">Majapahit</a> were mainly dependent on rice agriculture, yet also pursued trade within the Indonesian archipelago, and with China and India.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Majapahit" title="Majapahit">Majapahit</a> was established by <a href="/wiki/Raden_Wijaya" title="Raden Wijaya">Wijaya</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>201</span></sup> and by the end of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Hayam_Wuruk" title="Hayam Wuruk">Hayam Wuruk</a> (r. 1350&#8211;89) it claimed sovereignty over the entire Indonesian archipelago, although control was likely limited to Java, Bali and Madura. Hayam Wuruk\'s prime minister, <a href="/wiki/Gajah_Mada" title="Gajah Mada">Gajah Mada</a>, led many of the kingdom\'s territorial conquests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>234</span></sup> Previous Javanese kingdoms had their power based in agriculture, however, Majapahit took control of ports and shipping lanes and became Java\'s first commercial empire. With the death of Hayam Wuruk and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_spread_of_Islam_in_Indonesia" title="The spread of Islam in Indonesia">coming of Islam to Indonesia</a>, Majapahit went into decline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coedes_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Coedes-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>241</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread_of_Islam_and_rise_of_Islamic_sultanates">Spread of Islam and rise of Islamic sultanates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spread of Islam and rise of Islamic sultanates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> became the dominant religion in Java at the end of the 16th century. During this era, the Islamic kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Demak_Sultanate" title="Demak Sultanate">Demak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Cirebon" title="Sultanate of Cirebon">Cirebon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Banten_Sultanate" title="Banten Sultanate">Banten</a> were ascendant. The <a href="/wiki/Mataram_Sultanate" title="Mataram Sultanate">Mataram Sultanate</a> became the dominant power of central and eastern Java at the end of the 16th century. The principalities of Surabaya and Cirebon were eventually subjugated such that only Mataram and Banten were left to face the Dutch in the 17th century.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Thee-kweekbedden_zonder_afdak_Java_TMnr_10011931.jpg" with the European colonial powers began in 1522 with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luso_Sundanese_Treaty" title="Luso Sundanese Treaty">a treaty</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunda_kingdom" title="Sunda kingdom">Sunda kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Malacca" title="Portuguese Malacca">Portuguese in Malacca</a>. After its failure the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Portuguese_in_Indonesia" title="The Portuguese in Indonesia">Portuguese presence</a> was confined to Malacca, and to the eastern islands.\nIn 1596, a four-ship expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_de_Houtman" title="Cornelis de Houtman">Cornelis de Houtman</a> was the first Dutch contact with Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> By the end of the 18th century the Dutch had extended their influence over the sultanates of the interior through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company_in_Indonesia" title="Dutch East India Company in Indonesia">Dutch East India Company in Indonesia</a>. Internal conflict prevented the Javanese from forming effective alliances against the Dutch. Remnants of the Mataram survived as the Surakarta (Solo) and Yogyakarta principalities. Javanese kings claimed to rule with divine authority and the Dutch helped them to preserve remnants of a Javanese aristocracy by confirming them as regents or district officials within the colonial administration.\n</p><p>Java\'s major role during the early part of the colonial period was as a producer of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>. In spice producing islands like <a href="/wiki/Banda_Islands" title="Banda Islands">Banda</a>, rice was regularly imported from Java, to supply the deficiency in means of subsistence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleonic_wars" title="Napoleonic wars">Napoleonic wars</a> in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> fell to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, as did its colony in the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>. During the short-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daendels" title="Daendels">Daendels</a> administration, as French proxy rule on Java, the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Post_Road" title="Great Post Road">Java Great Post Road</a> was commenced in 1808. The road, spanning from <a href="/wiki/Anyer" title="Anyer">Anyer</a> in Western Java to Panarukan in East Java, served as a military supply route and was used in defending Java from British invasion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1811, Java was <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Java_(1811)" title="Invasion of Java (1811)">captured by the British</a>, becoming a possession of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, and Sir <a href="/wiki/Stamford_Raffles" title="Stamford Raffles">Stamford Raffles</a> was appointed as the island\'s Governor. In 1814, Java was returned to the Dutch under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1814)" title="Treaty of Paris (1814)">Treaty of Paris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java,_1945_SE4857.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg/220px-The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg/330px-The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg/440px-The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java%2C_1945_SE4857.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Allied_Occupation_of_Java,_1945_SE4857.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Japanese prepare to discuss surrender terms with British-allied forces in Java 1945</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1815, there may have been five million people in Java.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In the second half of the 18th century, population spurts began in districts along the north-central coast of Java, and in the 19th century population grew rapidly across the island. Factors for the great population growth include the impact of Dutch colonial rule including the imposed end to civil war in Java, the increase in the area under rice cultivation, and the introduction of food plants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casava" title="Casava">casava</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> that could sustain populations that could not afford rice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Others attribute the growth to the taxation burdens and increased expansion of employment under the <a href="/wiki/Cultivation_System" title="Cultivation System">Cultivation System</a> to which couples responded by having more children in the hope of increasing their families\' ability to pay tax and buy goods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">Cholera</a> claimed 100,000 lives in Java in 1820.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The advent of trucks and railways where there had previously only been buffalo and carts, telegraph systems, and more coordinated distribution systems under the colonial government all contributed to famine elimination in Java, and in turn, population growth. There were no significant famines in Java from the 1840s through to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Indonesia" title="Japanese occupation of Indonesia">Japanese occupation</a> in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_2003,_p._254_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2003,_p._254-32">[32]</a></sup> However, other sources claimed the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Cultivation_System#Implementation_and_effects" title="Cultivation System">Cultivation system</a> is linked to famines and epidemics in the 1840s, firstly in <a href="/wiki/Cirebon" title="Cirebon">Cirebon</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Central_Java" title="Central Java">Central Java</a>, as cash crops such as indigo and sugar had to be grown instead of rice. Furthermore, the age of first marriage dropped during the 19th century thus increasing a woman\'s child-bearing years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_2003,_p._254_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_2003,_p._254-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independence">Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Awakening" title="Indonesian National Awakening">Indonesian National Awakening</a></div>\n<p>Indonesian nationalism first took hold in Java in the early 20th century, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution" title="Indonesian National Revolution">the struggle to secure the country\'s independence</a> following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> was centered in Java. In 1949, Indonesia became independent and the island has dominated Indonesian social, political and economic life, which has been the source of resentment of those residents in other islands.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a lower classes were often involved in agriculture and fishing.  The elite class in Java has evolved over the course of history, as cultural wave after cultural wave immigrated to the island. There is evidence that South Asian emigres were among this elite, as well as Arabian and Persian immigrants during the Islamic eras.  More recently, Chinese immigrants have also become part of the economic elite of Java. Although politically the Chinese generally remain sidelined, there are notable exceptions, such as the former governor of Jakarta, <a href="/wiki/Basuki_Tjahaja_Purnama" title="Basuki Tjahaja Purnama">Basuki Tjahaja Purnama</a>. Though Java is increasingly becoming more modern and urban, only 75% of the island has electricity. Villages and their rice paddies are still a common sight. Unlike the rest of Java, the population growth in Central Java remains low. Central Java however has a younger population than the national average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The slow population growth can in part be attributed to the choice by many people to leave the more rural Central Java for better opportunities and higher incomes in the bigger cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Java\'s population continues to rapidly increase despite many Javanese leaving the island. This is somewhat due to the fact that Java is the business, academic, and cultural hub of Indonesia, which attracts millions of non-Javanese people to its cities. The population growth is most intense in the regions surrounding <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a>, which is reflected through the demographic diversity in those areas.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_development">Population development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Population development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With a combined population of 145 million in the 2015 census (including Madura\'s 3.7&#160;million),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JKTPOS_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-JKTPOS-37">[37]</a></sup> which is estimated for 2014 at 143.1&#160;million (including 3.7&#160;million for Madura), Java is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_population" title="List of islands by population">most populous island in the world</a> and is home to 57% of Indonesia\'s population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JKTPOS_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-JKTPOS-37">[37]</a></sup> At over 1,100 people per km&#178; in 2014, it is also one of the most densely populated parts of the world, on par with Bangladesh. Every region of the island has numerous volcanoes, with the people left to share the remaining flatter land. Because of this, many coasts are heavily populated and cities ring around the valleys surrounding volcanic peaks.\n</p><p>The population growth rate more than doubled in economically depressed Central Java in the latest 2010&#8211;2015 period vs 2000&#8211;2010, indicative of migration or other issues; there were significant <a href="/wiki/Mount_Merapi#2006_eruption" title="Mount Merapi">volcanic eruptions</a> during the earlier period.  Approximately 45% of the population of Indonesia is ethnically Javanese,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> while Sundanese make a large portion of Java\'s population as well.\n</p><p>The western third of the island (West Java, Banten, and DKI Jakarta) has an even higher population density, of nearly 1,500 per square kilometer and accounts for the lion\'s share of the population growth of Java.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JKTPOS_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-JKTPOS-37">[37]</a></sup> It is home to three metropolitan areas, <a the other larger islands of Indonesia, Java is comparatively homogeneous in ethnic composition. Only two ethnic groups are native to the island&#8212;the <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a>. A third group is the <a href="/wiki/Madurese_people" title="Madurese people">Madurese</a>, who inhabit the island of <a href="/wiki/Madura_Island" title="Madura Island">Madura</a> off the north east coast of Java, and have immigrated to <a href="/wiki/East_Java" title="East Java">East Java</a> in large numbers since the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus58_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus58-40">[40]</a></sup> The Javanese comprise about two-thirds of the island\'s population, while the Sundanese and Madurese account for 20% and 10% respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus58_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus58-40">[40]</a></sup> The fourth group is the <a href="/wiki/Betawi_people" title="Betawi people">Betawi people</a> that speak a dialect of <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>, they are the descendants of the people living around <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a> from around the 17th century. Betawis are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creole_people" title="Creole people">creole people</a>, mostly descended from various Indonesian archipelago ethnic groups such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_Malay" title="Ethnic Malay">Malay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balinese_people" title="Balinese people">Balinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bugis_people" title="Bugis people">Bugis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makassar_people" title="Makassar people">Makassar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambonese" title="Ambonese">Ambonese</a>, mixed with foreign ethnic groups such as <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people" title="Chinese people">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indian</a> brought to or attracted to Batavia to meet labour needs. They have a culture and language distinct from the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gamelandegung.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="344" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Gamelandegung.jpg/220px-Gamelandegung.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Gamelandegung.jpg/330px-Gamelandegung.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/Gamelandegung.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gamelandegung.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Gamelan" title="Gamelan">Gamelan</a> players</div></div></div>\n<p>The Javanese <a href="/wiki/Kakawin" title="Kakawin">kakawin</a> <a href="/wiki/Tantu_Pagelaran" title="Tantu Pagelaran">Tantu Pagelaran</a> explained the mythical origin of the island and its volcanic nature. Four major cultural areas exist on the island: the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Javanese_beliefs" title="Javanese beliefs">kejawen</a></i> or Javanese heartland, the north coast of the <i>pasisir</i> region, the <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sunda</a> lands of West Java, and the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_salient_of_Java" title="Eastern salient of Java">eastern salient</a>, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banyuwangi" title="Banyuwangi">Blambangan</a>. Madura makes up a fifth area having close cultural ties with coastal Java.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus58_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus58-40">[40]</a></sup> The <i>kejawen</i> Javanese culture is the island\'s most dominant. Java\'s remaining aristocracy are based here, and it is the region from where the majority of Indonesia\'s army, business, and political elite originate. Its language, arts, and etiquette are regarded as the island\'s most refined and exemplary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus58_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus58-40">[40]</a></sup> The territory from <a href="/wiki/Banyumas_Regency" title="Banyumas Regency">Banyumas</a> in the west through to <a href="/wiki/Blitar" title="Blitar">Blitar</a> in the east and encompasses Indonesia\'s most fertile and densely populated agricultural land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Periplus58_40-4"><a href="#cite_note-Periplus58-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ramayana_Java.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2409" data-file-width="1984" decoding="async" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ramayana_Java.jpg/220px-Ramayana_Java.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ramayana_Java.jpg/330px-Ramayana_Java.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Ramayana_Java.jpg/440px-Ramayana_Java.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ramayana_Java.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lakshmana" title="Lakshmana">Lakshmana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sita" title="Sita">Shinta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a> ballet at <a href="/wiki/Prambanan" title="Prambanan">Prambanan</a>, Java.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the southwestern part of Central Java, which is usually named the <a href="/wiki/Banyumas_Regency" title="Banyumas Regency">Banyumasan</a> region, a cultural mingling occurred; bringing together Javanese culture and Sundanese culture to create the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banyumasan_language" title="Banyumasan language">Banyumasan</a> culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the central Javanese court cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yogyakarta_(city)" title="Yogyakarta (city)">Yogyakarta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surakarta" title="Surakarta">Surakarta</a>, contemporary kings trace their lineages back to the pre-colonial Islamic kingdoms that ruled the region, making those places especially strong repositories of classical Javanese culture. Classic arts of Java include <a href="/wiki/Gamelan" title="Gamelan">gamelan</a> music and <a href="/wiki/Wayang" title="Wayang">wayang</a> puppet shows.\n</p><p>Java was the site of many influential kingdoms in the Southeast Asian region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and as a result, many literary works have been written by Javanese authors. These include <i><a href="/wiki/Ken_Arok" title="Ken Arok">Ken Arok</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ken_Dedes" title="Ken Dedes">Ken Dedes</a></i>, the story of the orphan who usurped his king, and married the queen of the ancient Javanese kingdom; and translations of <i><a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Pramoedya_Ananta_Toer" title="Pramoedya Ananta Toer">Pramoedya Ananta Toer</a> is a famous contemporary Indonesian author, who has written many stories based on his own experiences of having grown up in Java, and takes many elements from Javanese folklore and historical legends.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Java_languages.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="681" decoding="async" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Java_languages.JPG/220px-Java_languages.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Java_languages.JPG/330px-Java_languages.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Java_languages.JPG/440px-Java_languages.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Java_languages.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Languages spoken in Java (Javanese is shown in white). region), <a href="/wiki/Osing_language" title="Osing language">Osing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banyumasan_language" title="Banyumasan language">Banyumasan</a>, and <a language">Indonesian</a>, often as a second language.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Indonesia" title="Religion in Indonesia">Religion in Indonesia</a></div>\n<p>Java has been a melting pot of religions and cultures, which has created a broad range of religious belief.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">Indian</a> influences came first with <a href="/wiki/Shaivism" title="Shaivism">Shaivism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> penetrating deeply into society, blending with indigenous tradition and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup> One <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduit_(spiritualism)" title="Conduit (spiritualism)">conduit</a> for this were the <a href="/wiki/Asceticism" title="Asceticism">ascetics</a>, called <i>resi</i>, who taught mystical practices. A <i>resi</i> lived surrounded by students, who took care of their master\'s daily needs. Resi\'s authorities were merely ceremonial. At the courts, <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmin</a> clerics and <i>pudjangga</i> (sacred literati) legitimised rulers and linked <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> cosmology to their political needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup> Small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> enclaves are scattered throughout Java, but there is a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> population along the eastern coast nearest <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>, especially around the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banyuwangi" title="Banyuwangi">Banyuwangi</a>.\n</p><p>The coming of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, strengthened the status structure of this traditional religious pattern. More than 90 percent of the people of Java are Muslims, on a broad continuum between <i><a href="/wiki/Abangan" title="Abangan">abangan</a></i> (more traditional) and <i><a href="/wiki/Santri" title="Santri">santri</a></i> (more modernist). The Muslim scholar of the writ (<i><a href="/wiki/Kyai" title="Kyai">Kyai</a></i>) became the new religious elite as Hindu influences receded. Islam recognises no hierarchy of religious leaders nor a formal <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">priesthood</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch colonial government</a> established an elaborate rank order for mosque and other Islamic preaching schools. In Javanese <i><a href="/wiki/Pesantren" title="Pesantren">pesantren</a></i> (Islamic schools), The <i>Kyai</i> perpetuated the tradition of the <i>resi</i>. Students around him provided his needs, even <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasants</a> around the school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed center">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 162px"><div style="width: 162px">\n<div class="thumb" architecture</a>.\n</p>\n</div>\n</div></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Pre-Islamic Javan traditions have encouraged Islam in a mystical direction. There emerged in Java a loosely structured society of religious leadership, revolving around <i>kyais</i>, possessing various degrees of proficiency in pre-Islamic and Islamic <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">lore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belief" title="Belief">belief</a> and practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup> The kyais are the principal intermediaries between the villages masses and the realm of the <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a>. However, this very looseneess of kyai leadership structure has promoted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schism_(religion)" title="Schism (religion)">schism</a>. There were often sharp divisions between orthodox kyais, who merely instructed in Islamic law, with those who taught <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a> and those who sought reformed Islam with modern scientific concepts. As a result, there is a division between <i>santri</i>, who believe that they are more orthodox in their Islamic belief and practice, with <i><a href="/wiki/Abangan" title="Abangan">abangan</a></i>, who have mixed pre-Islamic <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animistic</a> and Hindu-Indian concepts with a superficial acceptance of Islamic belief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-4"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are also <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a> communities, mostly in the larger cities, though some rural areas of south-central Java are strongly <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> communities also exist in the major cities, primarily among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesian" title="Chinese Indonesian">Chinese Indonesian</a>. The Indonesian constitution recognises six official religions.\n</p><p>A wider effect of this division is the number of sects. In the middle of 1956, the Department of Religious Affairs in <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta" title="Yogyakarta">Yogyakarta</a> reported 63 religious sects in Java other than the official Indonesian religions. Of these, 35 were in <a href="/wiki/Central_Java" title="Central Java">Central Java</a>, 22 in <a href="/wiki/West_Java" title="West Java">West Java</a> and six in <a href="/wiki/East_Java" title="East Java">East Java</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kroef1961_43-5"><a href="#cite_note-kroef1961-43">[43]</a></sup> These include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Javanese_beliefs" title="Javanese beliefs">Kejawen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Javanese_beliefs" title="Javanese beliefs">Sumarah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subud" title="Subud">Subud</a>, etc. Their total membership is difficult to estimate as many of their adherents identify themselves with one of the official religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beatty_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beatty-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Java&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KerbauJawa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="991" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/KerbauJawa.jpg/220px-KerbauJawa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/KerbauJawa.jpg/330px-KerbauJawa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/KerbauJawa.jpg/440px-KerbauJawa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KerbauJawa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Water_buffalo" title="Water buffalo">Water buffalo</a> ploughing <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">rice fields</a> near <a href="/wiki/Salatiga" title="Salatiga">Salatiga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Java" title="Central Java">Central Java</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Initially the economy of Java relied heavily on <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> agriculture. Ancient kingdoms such as the <a href="/wiki/Tarumanagara" title="Tarumanagara">Tarumanagara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mataram_Kingdom" title="Mataram Kingdom">Mataram</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Majapahit" title="Majapahit">Majapahit</a> were dependent on rice yields and tax. Java was famous for rice surpluses and rice export since ancient times, and rice agriculture contributed to the population growth of the island. Trade with other parts of Asia such as ancient India and China flourished as early as the 4th century, as evidenced by Chinese ceramics found on the island dated to that period. Java also took part in the global trade of <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Maluku</a> <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spice</a> from ancient times in the Majapahit era, until well into the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> (VOC) era.\n</p><p>The VOC set their foothold on <a href="/wiki/Batavia,_Dutch_East_Indies" title="Batavia, Dutch East Indies">Batavia</a> in the 17th century and was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> in the 19th century. During these colonial times, the Dutch introduced the cultivation of commercial plants in Java, such as <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a>. In the 19th and early 20th century, Javanese coffee gained global popularity. Thus, the name "Java" today has become a synonym for coffee.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1275" data-file-width="1701" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg/220px-Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg/330px-Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg/440px-Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jakarta_Car_Free_Day.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Central <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Java has been Indonesia\'s most developed island since the Dutch East Indies era and continues to be so today in the modern Republic of Indonesia. The road transportation networks that have existed since ancient times were connected and perfected with the construction of <a href="/wiki/Great_Post_Road" title="Great Post Road">Java Great Post Road</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daendels" title="Daendels">Daendels</a> in the early 19th century. It became the backbone of Java\'s road infrastructure and laid the base of <a href="/wiki/North_Coast_Road_(Java)" title="North Coast Road (Java)">Java North Coast Road</a> (<a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a>: <i lang="id">Jalan Pantura, abbreviation from "Pantai Utara"</i>). The need to transport commercial produces such as coffee from plantations in the interior of the island to the harbour on the coast spurred the construction of railway networks in Java. Today, industry, business, trade and services flourished in major cities of Java, such as <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semarang" title="Semarang">Semarang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a>; while some traditional Sultanate cities such as <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta" title="Yogyakarta">Yogyakarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surakarta" title="Surakarta">Surakarta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cirebon" title="Cirebon">Cirebon</a> preserved its royal legacy and has become the centre of art, culture and tourism. Industrial estates are also growing in towns on northern coast of Java, especially around <a href="/wiki/Cilegon" title="Cilegon">Cilegon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tangerang" title="Tangerang">Tangerang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bekasi" title="Bekasi">Bekasi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karawang" title="Karawang">Karawang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gresik" title="Gresik">Gresik</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidoarjo" title="Sidoarjo">Sidoarjo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">toll road</a> highway networks was built and expanded since the New Order until the present day, connecting major urban centres and surrounding areas, such as in and around <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a>; also the ones in <a href="/wiki/Cirebon" title="Cirebon">Cirebon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semarang" title="Semarang">Semarang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surabaya" title="Surabaya">Surabaya</a>. In addition to these motorways, Java has 16 national highways.\n</p><p>Based on the statistical data by the year of 2012 released by <a href="/wiki/Statistics_Indonesia" title="Statistics Indonesia">Statistics Indonesia</a> (<i>Badan Pusat Statistik</i>), Java alone contributes at least 57.51% of Indonesia\'s GDP or equivalent to US$504&#160;billion.\n</p>\n<h2><span
