class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mars_Polar_Lander_in_aeroshell.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mars Polar Lander prep</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>lander</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> which descends toward and comes to rest on the surface of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">astronomical body</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> By contrast with an impact probe, which makes a <a href="/wiki/Hard_landing" title="Hard landing">hard landing</a> and is damaged or destroyed so ceases to function after reaching the surface, a lander makes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a> after which the probe remains functional.\n</p><p>For bodies with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmospheres</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a> occurs after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric entry</a>. In these cases, landers may employ <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachutes</a> to slow down and to maintain a low terminal velocity. Sometimes small landing <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> are fired just before <a href="/wiki/Impact_(mechanics)" title="Impact (mechanics)">impact</a> to reduce the impact velocity. Landing may be accomplished by <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">controlled descent</a> and setdown on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacecraft_lander_landing_gear" title="Spacecraft lander landing gear">landing gear</a>, with the possible addition of a <a href="/wiki/Philae_(spacecraft)" title="Philae (spacecraft)">post-landing attachment mechanism</a> for celestial bodies with low gravity. Some missions (for example, <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">Luna 9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a>), used inflatable <a href="/wiki/Airbag" title="Airbag">airbags</a> to cushion the lander\'s impact rather than a more traditional landing gear.\n</p><p>When a high velocity impact is planned not for just achieving the surface but for study of consequences of impact, the spacecraft is called an impactor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial bodies</a> have been subject of lander or impactor exploration: among them Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, the planets <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, the Saturn moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1270" data-file-width="1459" decoding="async" height="192" href="/wiki/File:Surveyor_3_on_Moon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Surveyor 3 on the Moon.</div></div></div>\n<p>Beginning with <a href="/wiki/Luna_2" title="Luna 2">Luna 2</a> in 1959, the first few spacecraft to reach the lunar surface were impactors, not landers. They were part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luna_program" title="Luna program">Luna program</a> or the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Ranger_program" title="Ranger program">Ranger program</a>.\n</p><p>In 1966, the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Luna_9" title="Luna 9">Luna 9</a> became the first spacecraft to achieve a lunar soft landing and to transmit photographic data to Earth. The American <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_program" title="Surveyor program">Surveyor program</a> (since 1966) was designed to determine where Apollo could land safely. As a result, these robotic missions required soft landers to sample the lunar soil and determine the thickness of the dust layer, which was unknown before Surveyor.\n</p><p>The U.S. manned <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Modules</a> (since 1969) with rovers (since 1971) and Soviet unmanned late big landers (since 1969), <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_programme" title="Lunokhod programme">Lunokhods</a> (since 1970) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">sample return missions</a> (since 1970) used a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> descent engine for a soft landing of astronauts and lunar rovers on the Moon.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a> spacecraft, previously known as the "Lunar Surface Access Module" or "LSAM", was the planned lander for the <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a>, prior to the cancellation of Project Constellation.\n</p><p>As of  August&#160;2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> NASA is developing vehicles that use a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> descent engine permitting them land on the Moon and other locations. These vehicles include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mighty_Eagle_lander" title="Mighty Eagle lander">Mighty Eagle lander</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morpheus_lander" title="Morpheus lander">Morpheus lander</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Project_Morpheus" title="Project Morpheus">Project Morpheus</a> lander may have sufficient thrust to propel a manned ascent stage.\n</p><p>Russia has plans for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luna-Grunt" title="Luna-Grunt">Luna-Grunt</a> mission to return samples from the Moon by 2021.\n</p><p>The Chinese <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_3" title="Chang\'e 3">Chang\'e 3</a> mission and its Jade Rabbit rover landed on 14 December 2013. Then China plans to repeat lander with rover in <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_4" title="Chang\'e 4">Chang\'e 4</a> mission after 2015 that will followed by <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5" title="Chang\'e 5">Chang\'e 5</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_6" title="Chang\'e 6">Chang\'e 6</a> sample return missions in 2017 and before 2020.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venus">Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Venus#Exploration" title="Venus">Venus &#167;&#160;Exploration</a></div>\n<p>The Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_program" title="Venera program">Venera program</a> included a number of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> landers, some of which were crushed during descent much as Galileo\'s Jupiter "lander" and others of which successfully touched down. <a href="/wiki/Venera_3" title="Venera 3">Venera 3</a> in 1966 and <a href="/wiki/Venera_7" title="Venera 7">Venera 7</a> in 1970 became the first impact and soft landing on Venus. The Soviet <a href="/wiki/Vega_program" title="Vega program">Vega program</a> also placed in 1985 two balloons in the Venusian atmosphere, they were the first aerial tools on other planets.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mars_landing" title="Mars landing">Mars landing</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">Mars rover</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_sample_return_mission" title="Mars sample return mission">Mars sample return mission</a></div>\n<p>The Soviet Union\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_1962B" title="Mars 1962B">Mars 1962B</a> was the first Earth based mission intended to reach the surface as impact on Mars in 1962. In 1971, the lander of the <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a> probe conducted the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(rocketry)" title="Soft landing (rocketry)">soft landing</a> on Mars, but communication was lost within a minute after touchdown, which occurred during one of the worst global dust storms since the beginning of telescopic observations of the Red Planet. Three other landers, <a href="/wiki/Mars_2" title="Mars 2">Mars 2</a> in 1971 and <a href="/wiki/Mars_5" title="Mars 5">Mars 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars_6" title="Mars 6">Mars 6</a> in 1973, either crashed or failed to even enter the planet\'s atmosphere. All four landers used an aeroshell-like heat shield during <a href="/wiki/Mars_atmospheric_entry" title="Mars atmospheric entry">atmospheric entry</a>. Mars 2 and Mars 3 landers carried the first small skis-walking <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">Martian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration)" title="Rover (space exploration)">rovers</a> that did not work on the planet.\n</p><p>The Soviet Union planned the heavy <a href="/wiki/Marsokhod" title="Marsokhod">Marsokhod</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_4NM" title="Mars 4NM">Mars 4NM</a> mission (in 1973) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_sample_return_mission" title="Mars sample return mission">Mars sample return</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_5NM" title="Mars 5NM">Mars 5NM</a> mission (in 1975) but they did not occur due to need of the <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1 superlauncher</a> that was never flown successfully. A double-launching Soviet <a href="/wiki/Mars_5M" title="Mars 5M">Mars 5M (Mars-79)</a> sample return mission was planned for 1979 but cancelled due to complexity and technical problems.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:auto; max-width:808px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars_Viking_12a001.png" title=\'First "clear" image ever transmitted from the surface of Mars &#8211; shows rocks near the Viking 1 Lander (July 20, 1976).\'><img alt="" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="1438" decoding="async" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Mars_Viking_12a001.png/800px-Mars_Viking_12a001.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Mars_Viking_12a001.png/1200px-Mars_Viking_12a001.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Mars_Viking_12a001.png 2x" width="800"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mars_Viking_12a001.png" title="File:Mars Viking 12a001.png"> </a></div>First "clear" image ever transmitted from the surface of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> &#8211; shows <a href="/wiki/List_of_rocks_on_Mars#Viking" title="List of rocks on Mars">rocks</a> near the <i><a href="/wiki/Viking_1" title="Viking 1">Viking 1</a></i> Lander (July 20, 1976).</div>\n</div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking 1 and 2</a> were launched respectively in August and September 1975, each comprising an orbiter vehicle and a lander. Viking 1 landed in July 1976 and Viking 2 in September 1976. The Viking rovers were the first successful, working Mars landers. The mission ended in May 1983, after both landers had died.\n</p><p>In the 1970s, the US planned the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program_(Mars)" title="Voyager program (Mars)">Voyager-Mars</a> mission. This would have consisted of two orbiters and two landers, launched by a single <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket, but the mission was cancelled.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mars_96" title="Mars 96">Mars 96</a> was the first complex post-Soviet Russian mission with an orbiter, lander and penetrators. Planned for 1996, it failed at launch. A planned repeat of this mission, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mars_98&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mars 98 (page does not exist)">Mars 98</a>, was cancelled due to lack of funding.\n</p><p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a> was launched in December 1996 and released the first acting <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">rover on Mars</a>, named <i>Sojourner</i>, in July 1997. It failed in September 1997, probably due to electronics failure caused by the cold temperatures. Mars Pathfinder was part of the cancelled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Environmental_Survey" title="Mars Environmental Survey">Mars Environmental Survey</a> program with a set of 16 landers planned for 1999&#8211;2009.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="722" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg/220px-MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg/330px-MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg/440px-MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MER_Spirit_Lander_Pan_Sol16-A18R1_br2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The lander which brought the <a href="/wiki/Spirit_(rover)" title="Spirit (rover)"><i>Spirit</i> rover</a> to the surface of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mars_Polar_Lander" title="Mars Polar Lander">Mars Polar Lander</a> ceased communication on 3 December 1999, prior to reaching the surface, and is presumed to have crashed.\n</p><p>The European <a href="/wiki/Beagle_2" title="Beagle 2">Beagle 2</a> lander deployed successfully from the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Express" title="Mars Express">Mars Express</a> spacecraft but the signal confirming a landing which should have come on 25 December 2003 was not received. Indeed, no communication was ever established and <i>Beagle 2</i> was declared lost on 6 February 2004. The proposed 2009 British <a href="/wiki/Beagle_3" title="Beagle 3">Beagle 3</a> lander mission to search for life, past or present was not adopted.\n</p><p>The French/ESA <a href="/wiki/NetLander" title="NetLander">NetLander</a> mission for 2007 or 2009, with an orbiter and 4 landers, was cancelled because it was too expensive. Its successor, <a href="/wiki/Mars_MetNet" title="Mars MetNet">Mars MetNet</a>, a multi-lander mission for 2011&#8211;2019 was not adopted by the ESA.\n</p><p>The American <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rovers</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_rover" title="Spirit rover"><i>Spirit</i></a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover"><i>Opportunity</i></a> were launched in June and July 2003. They reached the Martian surface in January 2004 using landers featuring airbags and parachutes to soften impact. <i>Spirit</i> ceased functioning in 2010, more than five years past its design lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  As of January 2017, <i>Opportunity</i> remains active, having exceeded its three-month design lifetime by well over a decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasaStatus_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasaStatus-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft)" title="Phoenix (spacecraft)">Phoenix spacecraft</a> successfully achieved soft landing on the surface of Mars on May 25, 2008, using a combination of parachutes and rocket descent engines.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> (<i>Curiosity</i>) was successfully launched by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> on November 26, 2011. It landed in the <a href="/wiki/Aeolis_Palus" title="Aeolis Palus">Aeolis Palus</a> region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gale_Crater" title="Gale Crater">Gale Crater</a> on Mars on August 6, 2012.\n</p><p>Planned for 2018, NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Mars_Astrobiology_Explorer-Cacher" title="Mars Astrobiology Explorer-Cacher">Mars Astrobiology Explorer-Cacher</a> lander mission was cancelled due to budget cuts.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Mars#Future_missions" title="Exploration of Mars">Exploration of Mars</a> including the use of landers continues to this day. Amongst them, Russia has planned a Mars sample return mission <a href="/wiki/Mars-Grunt" title="Mars-Grunt">Mars-Grunt</a> for near 2026, and China for near 2030.\n</p>\n<div Two probes under the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Phobos_program" title="Phobos program">Phobos program</a> were successfully launched in 1988, but in 1989 the intended landings on Phobos and Deimos were not conducted due to failures in the spacecraft system. The post-Soviet Russian <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">Fobos-Grunt</a> probe was an intended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sample_return_mission" title="Sample return mission">sample return mission</a> to Phobos in 2012 but failed after launch in 2011.\n</p><p>In 2007 <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EADS_Astrium" title="EADS Astrium">EADS Astrium</a> proposed and developed the mission to Phobos to 2016 with lander and sample return, but it stayed as project. Since 2007 the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">Canadian Space Agency</a> considering the mission to Phobos PRIME (Phobos Reconnaissance and International Mars Exploration) with orbiter and lander. Recent proposals include a 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Glenn_Research_Center" title="NASA Glenn Research Center">NASA Glenn Research Center</a> Phobos and Deimos sample return mission, the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Phobos_Surveyor" title="Phobos Surveyor">Phobos Surveyor</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx#OSIRIS-REx_II" title="OSIRIS-REx">OSIRIS-REx II</a> mission concept.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency">Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency</a> (JAXA) plans to launch the <a href="/wiki/Martian_Moons_Exploration" title="Martian Moons Exploration">Martian Moons Exploration</a> (MMX) in 2024, a sample return mission targeting Phobos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_Clark_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-S_Clark-5">[5]</a></sup> MMX will land and collect samples from Phobos multiple times, along with deploying a rover jointly developed by <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">CNES</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">DLR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMXroverJAXAPR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMXroverJAXAPR-6">[6]</a></sup> By using a corer sampling mechanism, the spacecraft aims to retrieve a minimum 10 g amount of samples. MMX will return to Earth in 2029.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujimoto20170111_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fujimoto20170111-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Russia plans to repeat <a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_2" title="Deep Space 2">Deep Space 2</a> impactor probe was to be the first spacecraft to penetrate below the surface of another planet. However the mission failed with the loss of its mother ship, <a href="/wiki/Mars_Polar_Lander" title="Mars Polar Lander">Mars Polar Lander</a> after communication was lost after entry into Mars atmosphere on 3 December 1999.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_Impact"><i>Deep Impact</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Deep Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Comet <a href="/wiki/Tempel_1" title="Tempel 1">Tempel 1</a> was visited by NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(spacecraft)" title="Deep Impact (spacecraft)"><i>Deep Impact</i></a> probe on 4 July 2005. The <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact crater</a> formed was approximately 200 m wide and 30&#8211;50 m deep, and detected  the presence of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smectite" title="Smectite">smectite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_carbon" title="Amorphous carbon">amorphous carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_Impact_Probe"><i>Moon Impact Probe</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Moon Impact Probe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moon_Impact_Probe" title="Moon Impact Probe">Moon Impact Probe</a></div>\n<p>The <b>Moon Impact Probe</b> (<b>MIP</b>) developed by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO)</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>\'s national space agency, was a <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> probe that was released on 14 November 2008 by ISRO\'s <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">Chandrayaan-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">lunar remote sensing</a> orbiter which in turn was launched, on 22 October 2008. It discovered the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">presence of water on the Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Water_found_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Water_found-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_first_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-India_first-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LCROSS"><i>LCROSS</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: LCROSS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">LCROSS</a></div>\n<p>The <b>Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite</b> (<b>LCROSS</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> to perform a lower-cost means of determining the nature of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> detected at the polar regions of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-11">[11]</a></sup> The main LCROSS mission objective was to explore the presence of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> ice in a permanently shadowed crater near a lunar polar region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> LCROSS was launched together with the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter</a> (LRO) on 18 June 2009, as part of the shared <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Precursor_Robotic_Program" title="Lunar Precursor Robotic Program">Lunar Precursor Robotic Program</a>. LCROSS was designed to collect and relay data from the impact and debris plume resulting from the launch vehicle\'s spent <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a> upper stage striking the crater <a href="/wiki/Cabeus_(crater)" title="Cabeus (crater)">Cabeus</a> near the south pole of the Moon. Centaur impacted successfully on 9 October 2009, at 11:31 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>. The Shepherding Spacecraft descended through Centaur\'s ejectate plume, collected and relayed data, impacting six minutes later at 11:37 UTC. The project was successful in discovering water in the southern lunar crater <a href="/wiki/Cabeus_(crater)" title="Cabeus (crater)">Cabeus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectralResult_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-spectralResult-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MESSENGER"><i>MESSENGER</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lander_(spacecraft)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: MESSENGER">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The NASA <i><a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">MESSENGER</a></i> (MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging) mission to Mercury launched on 3 August 2004 and entered orbit around the planet on 18 March 2011. Following a mapping mission, <i>MESSENGER</i> was directed to crash into Mercury\'s surface on April 30, 2015. The spacecraft\'s impact with Mercury occurred near 3:26 PM EDT on April 30, 2015, leaving a crater estimated to be 16&#160;m (52&#160;ft) in diameter.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
