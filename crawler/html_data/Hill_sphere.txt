The Hill spheres are the circular regions surrounding the two large masses.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Hill sphere</b> of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">astronomical body</a> is the region in which it dominates the attraction of <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">satellites</a>. The outer shell of that region constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Zero-velocity_surface" title="Zero-velocity surface">zero-velocity surface</a>. To be retained by a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moon</a> must have an <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> that lies within the planet\'s Hill sphere. That moon would, in turn, have a Hill sphere of its own. Any object within that distance would tend to become a satellite of the moon, rather than of the planet itself. One simple view of the extent of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is the Hill sphere of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> with respect to local stars and the <a href="/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus" title="Active galactic nucleus">galactic nucleus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chebotarev1964_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chebotarev1964-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In more precise terms, the Hill sphere approximates the <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational</a> <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)" title="Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)">sphere of influence</a> of a smaller body in the face of <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a> from a more massive body. It was defined by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/George_William_Hill" title="George William Hill">George William Hill</a>, based on the work of the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> astronomer <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Roche" title="&#201;douard Roche">&#201;douard Roche</a>. For this reason, it is also known as the <b>Roche sphere</b> (not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">Roche limit</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roche_Lobe" title="Roche Lobe">Roche Lobe</a>).\n</p><p>In the example to the right, Earth\'s Hill sphere extends between the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian points</a> <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>2</sub></a>, which lie along the line of centers of the two bodies. The region of influence of the second body is shortest in that direction, and so it acts as the limiting factor for the size of the Hill sphere. Beyond that distance, a third object in orbit around the second (e.g. the Moon) would spend at least part of its orbit outside the Hill sphere, and would be progressively perturbed by the tidal forces of the central body (e.g. the Sun), eventually ending up orbiting the latter.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" This is also convenient, because many planetary astronomers work in and remember distances in units of planetary radii.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="True_region_of_stability">True region of stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hill_sphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: True region of stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Hill sphere is only an approximation, and other forces (such as <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect" title="Yarkovsky effect">Yarkovsky effect</a>) can eventually perturb an object out of the sphere. This third object should also be of small enough mass that it introduces no additional complications through its own gravity. Detailed numerical calculations show that orbits at or just within the Hill sphere are not stable in the long term; it appears that stable satellite orbits exist only inside 1/2 to 1/3 of the Hill radius. The region of stability for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_orbit" title="Retrograde orbit">retrograde orbits</a> at a large distance from the primary, is larger than the region for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prograde_orbit" title="Prograde orbit">prograde orbits</a> at a large distance from the primary. In fact, in any <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, a spherical body must be more dense than <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> in order to fit inside its own Hill sphere, or else it will be incapable of supporting an orbit. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the planet with the largest Hill radius is <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>, with 116 million km, or 0.775 au; its great distance from the Sun amply compensates for its small mass relative to Jupiter (whose own Hill radius measures 53 million km). An <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a> will have a Hill sphere that can reach 220,000&#160;km (for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_Ceres" title="1 Ceres">1 Ceres</a>), diminishing rapidly with decreasing mass. The Hill sphere of <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(66391)_1999_KW4" title="(66391) 1999 KW4">(66391) 1999 KW<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">4</span></span></a></span>, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mercury-crossing_minor_planets" title="List of Mercury-crossing minor planets">Mercury-crossing asteroid</a> that has a moon (S/2001 (66391) 1), measures 22&#160;km in radius.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>A typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar</a> "<a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">hot Jupiter</a>", <a href="/wiki/HD_209458_b" title="HD 209458 b">HD 209458 b</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> has a Hill sphere radius of 593,000&#160;km, about eight times its physical radius of approx 71,000&#160;km. Even the smallest close-in extrasolar planet, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CoRoT-7b" title="CoRoT-7b">CoRoT-7b</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> still has a Hill sphere radius (61,000&#160;km) six times its physical radius (approx 10,000&#160;km). Therefore, these planets could have small moons close in, although not within their respective <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">Roche limits</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
