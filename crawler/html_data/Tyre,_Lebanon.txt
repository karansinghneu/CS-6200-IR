area">urban</a> centres: Tyre itself,  which was on an island just off shore, and the associated settlement of <a href="/wiki/Ushu" title="Ushu">Ushu</a> on the adjacent mainland. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> connected the island to the mainland by constructing a <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeway</a> during his <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Tyre_(332_BC)" title="Siege of Tyre (332 BC)">siege of the city</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> demolishing the old city to reuse its cut stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The original island city had two harbours, one on the south side and the other on the north side of the island. It was the two harbours that enabled Tyre to gain the maritime prominence that it did; the harbour on the north side of the island was, in fact, one of the best harbours on the eastern end of the Mediterranean. The harbour on the south side has silted over, but the harbour on the north side (see Tyre harbor photo at top of page right) is still in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In ancient times, the island-city of Tyre was heavily fortified and the mainland settlement, originally called <a href="/wiki/Ushu" title="Ushu">Ushu</a> (later called Palaetyrus, meaning "Old Tyre," by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a>) was actually more like a line of suburbs than any one city and was used primarily as a source of water and timber for the main island city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> records that the two fought against each other on occasion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> but most of the time, they supported one another because they both benefited from the island city\'s wealth from maritime trade and the mainland area\'s source of timber, water and burial grounds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_and_Iron_Ages">Bronze and Iron Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bronze and Iron Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tyre was founded around 2750 BC according to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> and was originally built as a walled city upon the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Tyre\'s name appears on monuments as early as 1300 BC. <a href="/wiki/Philo_of_Byblos" title="Philo of Byblos">Philo of Byblos</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a>) quotes the antiquarian authority <a href="/wiki/Sanchuniathon" title="Sanchuniathon">Sanchuniathon</a> as stating that it was first occupied by Hypsuranius. <a href="/wiki/Sanchuniathon" title="Sanchuniathon">Sanchuniathon\'s</a> work is said to be dedicated to "Abibalus king of Berytus"&#8212;possibly the <a href="/wiki/Abibaal" title="Abibaal">Abibaal</a> who was king of Tyre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are ten <a href="/wiki/Amarna_letters" title="Amarna letters">Amarna letters</a> dated 1350 BC from the mayor, <a href="/wiki/Abimilku" title="Abimilku">Abimilku</a>, written to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akenaten" title="Akenaten">Akenaten</a>. The subject is often water, wood and the <a href="/wiki/Habiru" title="Habiru">Habiru</a> overtaking the countryside of the mainland and how that affected the island-city.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Commerce from throughout ancient world was gathered into the warehouses of Tyre.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Tyrian merchants were the first who ventured to navigate the Mediterranean waters; and they founded their <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonies</a> on the coasts and neighbouring islands of the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>, in Greece, on the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">northern coast of Africa</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> and other places, in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, in Spain at <a href="/wiki/Tartessos" title="Tartessos">Tartessus</a> and even beyond the <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">pillars of Hercules</a> at Gadeira (<a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Tyre became one of the more powerful cities in <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenicia</a>. One of its kings, the priest <a href="/wiki/Ithobaal_I" title="Ithobaal I">Ithobaal</a> (887&#8211;856&#160;BC), ruled Phoenicia as far north as <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">Beirut</a>, and part of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> was founded in 814&#160;BC under <a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_of_Tyre" title="Pygmalion of Tyre">Pygmalion</a> of Tyre (820&#8211;774&#160;BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The collection of city-states constituting Phoenicia came to be characterized by outsiders and the Phoenicians as <i>Sidonia</i> or <i>Tyria</i>. Phoenicians and Canaanites alike were called <i>Sidonians</i> or <i>Tyrians</i>, as one Phoenician city came to prominence after another.\n</p><p>The city of Tyre was particularly known for the production of a rare and extraordinarily expensive sort of <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a> <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dye</a>, produced from the <a href="/wiki/Murex" title="Murex">murex</a> shellfish, known as <a href="/wiki/Tyrian_purple" title="Tyrian purple">Tyrian purple</a>. The colour was, in ancient cultures, <a href="/wiki/Sumptuary_law" title="Sumptuary law">reserved</a> for the use of royalty or at least the nobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Phoenicians from Tyre settled in houses around <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Egypt" title="Memphis, Egypt">Memphis in Egypt</a>, south of the temple of <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a> in a district called the Tyrian Camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tyre was often attacked by Egypt and was besieged by Assyrian king <a href="/wiki/Shalmaneser_V" title="Shalmaneser V">Shalmaneser V</a>, who was assisted by the Phoenicians of the mainland, for five years. From 586 until 573 BC, the city was besieged by <a href="/wiki/Nebuchadnezzar_II" title="Nebuchadnezzar II">Nebuchadnezzar II</a> of Babylon until it agreed to pay a tribute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_period">Persian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Persian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_(1898-1899).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2227" data-file-width="1539" decoding="async" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg/220px-A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg/330px-A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg/440px-A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_%281898-1899%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_naval_action_during_the_siege_of_Tyre_by_Andre_Castaigne_(1898-1899).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A naval action during the siege of Tyre (332 BC). Drawing by <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Castaigne" title="Andr&#233; Castaigne">Andr&#233; Castaigne</a></i>, 1888&#8211;89.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a> of King <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a> conquered the city in 539 BC and kept it under its rule until 332 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Persians then divided Phoenicia into four vassal kingdoms: <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>, Tyre, <a href="/wiki/Arwad" title="Arwad">Arwad</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Byblos" title="Byblos">Byblos</a>. They prospered, furnishing fleets for Persian kings. Phoenician influence declined after this.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_period">Hellenistic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hellenistic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> connected the island to the mainland by constructing a <a href="/wiki/Causeway" title="Causeway">causeway</a> during his <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a> of the city in 332 BC, demolishing the old city to reuse its cut stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 315 BC, Alexander\'s former general <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_I_Monophthalmus" title="Antigonus I Monophthalmus">Antigonus</a> began his own siege of Tyre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-315_B.C._&#8211;_events_and_references_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-315_B.C._&#8211;_events_and_references-21">[21]</a></sup> taking the city a year later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 126 BC, Tyre regained its independence from the <a href="/wiki/Seleucid_Empire" title="Seleucid Empire">Seleucid Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">Roman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tyre was allowed to keep much of its independence, as a "<a href="/wiki/Civitas_foederata" title="Civitas foederata">civitas foederata</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> when the area became a Roman province in 64 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Tyre continued to maintain much of its commercial importance until the <a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">Common Era</a>. The Tyrians, or "people of Tyre" during the Roman period, extended their areas of hegemony over the adjoining regions, such as in northern <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine region</a>, settling in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Kedesh" title="Kedesh">Kedesh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baca_(Galilee)" title="Baca (Galilee)">Baca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is stated in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> that <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> visited the region of Tyre and <a href="/wiki/Sidon#The_Biblical_Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a> and healed a <a href="/wiki/Gentile" title="Gentile">Gentile</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Matthew+15:21&#8211;15:21&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Matthew 15:21</a>; <a class="external text" href="http://bible.oremus.org/?passage=Mark+7:24&#8211;7:24&amp;version=nrsv" rel="nofollow">Mark 7:24</a>) and from this region many came forth to hear him preaching (Mark 3:8; <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a> 6:17, Matthew 11:21&#8211;23). A congregation was founded here soon after the death of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Stephen" title="St. Stephen">St. Stephen</a>. <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul the Apostle</a>, on his return from his third missionary journey, spent a week in conversation with the disciples there. According to <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a> of Lyon in <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Detection_and_Overthrow_of_the_So-Called_Gnosis" title="On the Detection and Overthrow of the So-Called Gnosis">On the Detection and Overthrow of the So-Called Gnosis</a></i>, the female companion of <a href="/wiki/Simon_Magus" title="Simon Magus">Simon Magus</a> came from here.\n</p><p>The famous "Arch of Hadrian" and one of the best hippodromes in the region were constructed during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">Roman empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_period">Byzantine period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Byzantine period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 395 Tyre became part of the Byzantine Empire. The city remained under Byzantine control until 638, when it was occupied by the Arabs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Muslim_period">Early Muslim period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early Muslim period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_Tyre_(996%E2%80%93998)" title="Revolt of Tyre (996&#8211;998)">Revolt of Tyre (996&#8211;998)</a>, the populace of the city rose against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatimid" title="Fatimid">Fatimid</a> rule, led by an ordinary sailor named \'Allaqa - but were brutally suppressed in May 998. In 1086 it fell into the hands of the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Empire" title="Seljuk Empire">Seljuks</a> who lost it in 1089 to the Fatimids.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crusader_period">Crusader period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Crusader period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Tyre" title="Lordship of Tyre">Lordship of Tyre</a></div>\n<p>After the first failed siege in 1111, Tyre was captured during the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/First_Crusade" title="First Crusade">First Crusade</a> on July 7, 1124 and became one of the most important cities of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Kingdom of Jerusalem</a>. It was part of the royal domain, but there were also autonomous trading colonies there for the Italian merchant cities. The city was the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Tyre" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Tyre">Roman Catholic archbishopric</a>, whose archbishop was a suffragan of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Patriarchate_of_Jerusalem" title="Latin Patriarchate of Jerusalem">Latin Patriarch of Jerusalem</a>; its archbishops often acceded to the Patriarchate. The most notable of the Latin archbishops was the historian <a href="/wiki/William_of_Tyre" title="William of Tyre">William of Tyre</a>.\n</p><p>After the loss of the First Crusader Kingdom in the wake of the 1187 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hattin" title="Battle of Hattin">Battle of Hattin</a> and the reconquest of <a href="/wiki/Acre,_Israel" title="Acre, Israel">Acre</a> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">Richard I of England</a> on July 12, 1191, the seat of the kingdom moved there, but coronations were held in Tyre. In the 13th century, Tyre was separated from the royal domain as the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Tyre" title="Lordship of Tyre">Lordship of Tyre</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mamluk_period">Mamluk period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mamluk period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1291, Tyre was retaken by the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_(Cairo)" title="Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)">Mamluk Sultanate</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_period">Ottoman period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> conquered the region in 1516-17 and held it until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Lebanon">Modern Lebanon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a of the causeway, which had increased greatly in width over the centuries because of extensive silt depositions on either side. The part of the original island not covered by the modern city of Tyre is mostly of an archaeological site showcasing remains of the city from ancient times.\n</p><p>After numerous attacks and reprisals involving the <a href="/wiki/Palestine_Liberation_Organization" title="Palestine Liberation Organization">Palestine Liberation Organization</a> (PLO), including an assassination attempt on Israeli ambassador Argov, Israel invaded, as part of the so-called <a href="/wiki/1978_South_Lebanon_conflict" title="1978 South Lebanon conflict">1978 South Lebanon conflict</a>, and Tyre was badly damaged. It was damaged again in the <a href="/wiki/1982_Lebanon_War" title="1982 Lebanon War">1982 Lebanon War</a> between <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and the PLO. The city was used as a base by the PLO and was nearly destroyed by Israeli artillery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> After the 1982 war, the city was the site of an Israeli military post. In late 1982, and again in November 1983, buildings housing Israeli headquarters were <a href="/wiki/Tyre_headquarters_bombings" title="Tyre headquarters bombings">destroyed by bombs</a>, causing dozens of deaths in both cases and known in Israel as the First and Second Tyre Catastrophes. The 1983 explosion by a <a href="/wiki/Car_bomb" title="Car bomb">suicide truck</a> happened only 10 days after similar attacks, the <a href="/wiki/1983_Beirut_barracks_bombings" title="1983 Beirut barracks bombings">1983 Beirut barracks bombings</a> of US Marines and French paratroop barracks. Israel and the US blame <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> for all explosions, but they have denied any involvement.\n</p><p>During Israel\'s invasion in the <a href="/wiki/2006_Lebanon_War" title="2006 Lebanon War">2006 Lebanon War</a>, several rocket-launching sites used by <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> to attack Israel were located in rural areas around the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> At least one village near the city was bombed by Israel as well as several sites within the city, causing civilian deaths and adding to the food shortage problem inside Tyre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shayetet_13" title="Shayetet 13">Shayetet 13</a> (Israeli naval commandos) also raided Hezbollah targets within the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coast_Nature_Reserve">Coast Nature Reserve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyre,_Lebanon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Coast Nature Reserve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tyre_Coast_Nature_Reserve" title="Tyre Coast Nature Reserve">Tyre Coast Nature Reserve</a> covers over 380 hectares (940 acres) and divided into three zones: the Tourism zone (public beaches, the old city and Souks, the ancient port), the Agricultural and Archaeological zone, and the Conservation zone that includes the Phoenician springs of <a href="/wiki/Ras_al-Ain,_Lebanon" title="Ras al-Ain, Lebanon">Ras El Ain</a>. Due to its diverse flora and fauna, the reserve is a designated <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention" title="Ramsar Convention">Ramsar Site</a>. It is an important nesting site for migratory birds and the endangered <a href="/wiki/Loggerhead_sea_turtle" title="Loggerhead sea turtle">Loggerhead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_sea_turtle"
