For information about Wikipedia\'s random article feature, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Random" title="Wikipedia:Random">Wikipedia:Random</a>.</div></div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RandomBitmap.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/RandomBitmap.png/220px-RandomBitmap.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/RandomBitmap.png/330px-RandomBitmap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/RandomBitmap.png/440px-RandomBitmap.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RandomBitmap.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pseudorandomly generated <a href="/wiki/Bitmap" title="Bitmap">bitmap</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Randomness</b> is the lack of <a href="/wiki/Pattern" title="Pattern">pattern</a> or <a href="/wiki/Predictability" title="Predictability">predictability</a> in events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  A random sequence of events, <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a> or <a href="/wiki/Procedure_(term)" title="Procedure (term)">steps</a> has no <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/order" title="wikt:order">order</a> and does not follow an intelligible pattern or combination. Individual random events are by definition unpredictable, but in many cases the frequency of different outcomes over a large number of events (or "trials") is predictable.  For example, when throwing two <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a>, the outcome of any particular roll is unpredictable, but a sum of 7 will occur twice as often as 4.  In this view, randomness is a measure of uncertainty of an outcome, rather than haphazardness, and applies to concepts of chance, <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_entropy" title="Information entropy">information entropy</a>.\n</p><p>The fields of mathematics, probability, and statistics use formal definitions of randomness.  In statistics, a <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variable</a> is an assignment of a numerical value to each possible outcome of an event space.  This association facilitates the identification and the calculation of probabilities of the events. Random variables can appear in <a href="/wiki/Random_sequence" title="Random sequence">random sequences</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_process" title="Random process">random process</a> is a sequence of random variables whose outcomes do not follow a <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">deterministic</a> pattern, but follow an evolution described by <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">probability distributions</a>.  These and other constructs are extremely useful in <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a> and the various <a href="/wiki/Applications_of_randomness" title="Applications of randomness">applications of randomness</a>.\n</p><p>Randomness is most often used in <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> to signify well-defined statistical properties. <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_method" title="Monte Carlo method">Monte Carlo methods</a>, which rely on random input (such as from <a href="/wiki/Random_number_generation" title="Random number generation">random number generators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pseudorandom_number_generator" title="Pseudorandom number generator">pseudorandom number generators</a>), are important techniques in science, as, for instance, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_computing" title="Scientific computing">computational science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> By analogy, <a href="/wiki/Quasi-Monte_Carlo_method" title="Quasi-Monte Carlo method">quasi-Monte Carlo methods</a> use <a href="/wiki/Low-discrepancy_sequence" title="Low-discrepancy sequence">quasirandom number generators</a>.\n</p><p><b>Random selection</b>, when narrowly associated with a <a href="/wiki/Simple_random_sample" title="Simple random sample">simple random sample</a>, is a method of selecting items (often called units) from a population where the probability of choosing a specific item is the proportion of those items in the population.  For example, with a bowl containing just 10 red marbles and 90 blue marbles, a random selection mechanism would choose a red marble with probability 1/10.  Note that a random selection mechanism that selected 10 marbles from this bowl would not necessarily result in 1 red and 9 blue.  In situations where a population consists of items that are distinguishable, a random selection mechanism requires equal probabilities for any item to be chosen.  That is, if the selection process is such that each member of a population, of say research subjects, has the same probability of being chosen then we can say the selection process is random.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" <a href="/wiki/History_of_randomness" title="History of randomness">History of randomness</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1159" data-file-width="1461" decoding="async" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg/220px-Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg/330px-Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg/440px-Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pompeii_-_Osteria_della_Via_di_Mercurio_-_Dice_Players.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ancient <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> of dice players in <a href="/wiki/Pompei" title="Pompei">Pompei</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In ancient history, the concepts of chance and randomness were intertwined with that of fate. Many ancient peoples threw <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a> to determine fate, and this later evolved into games of chance. Most ancient cultures used various methods of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a> to attempt to circumvent randomness and fate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Chinese of 3000 years ago were perhaps the earliest people to formalize odds and chance. The Greek philosophers discussed randomness at length, but only in non-quantitative forms. It was only in the 16th century that Italian mathematicians began to formalize the odds associated with various games of chance. The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a> had a positive impact on the formal study of randomness. In the 1888 edition of his book <i>The Logic of Chance</i> <a href="/wiki/John_Venn" title="John Venn">John Venn</a> wrote a chapter on <i>The conception of randomness</i> that included his view of the randomness of the digits of the number <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">pi</a> by using them to construct a <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a> in two dimensions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The early part of the 20th century saw a rapid growth in the formal analysis of randomness, as various approaches to the mathematical foundations of probability were introduced. In the mid- to late-20th century, ideas of <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_information_theory" title="Algorithmic information theory">algorithmic information theory</a> introduced new dimensions to the field via the concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algorithmic_randomness" title="Algorithmic randomness">algorithmic randomness</a>.\n</p><p>Although randomness had often been viewed as an obstacle and a nuisance for many centuries, in the 20th century computer scientists began to realize that the <i>deliberate</i> introduction of randomness into computations can be an effective tool for designing better algorithms. In some cases such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randomized_algorithms" title="Randomized algorithms">randomized algorithms</a> outperform the best deterministic methods.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_science">In science</span></h2>\n<p>Many scientific fields are concerned with randomness:\n</p>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 20em; -webkit-column-width: 20em; column-width: 20em;">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_probability" title="Algorithmic probability">Algorithmic probability</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">Chaos theory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">Cryptography</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">Game theory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">Information theory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">Pattern recognition</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">Probability theory</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">Quantum mechanics</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">Statistical mechanics</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">Statistics</a></li></ul>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_physical_sciences">In the physical sciences</span></h3>\n<p>In the 19th century, scientists used the idea of random motions of molecules in the development of <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a> to explain phenomena in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_laws" title="Gas laws">the properties of gases</a>.\n</p><p>According to several standard interpretations of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, microscopic phenomena are objectively random.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> That is, in an experiment that controls all causally relevant parameters, some aspects of the outcome still vary randomly. For example, if a single unstable <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> is placed in a controlled environment, it cannot be predicted how long it will take for the atom to decay&#8212;only the probability of decay in a given time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, quantum mechanics does not specify the outcome of individual experiments but only the probabilities.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory" title="Hidden variable theory">Hidden variable theories</a> reject the view that nature contains irreducible randomness: such theories posit that in the processes that appear random, properties with a certain statistical distribution are at work behind the scenes, determining the outcome in each case.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_biology">In biology</span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century)" title="Modern synthesis (20th century)">modern evolutionary synthesis</a> ascribes the observed diversity of life to random genetic <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>. The latter retains some random mutations in the <a href="/wiki/Gene_pool" title="Gene pool">gene pool</a> due to the systematically improved chance for survival and reproduction that those mutated genes confer on individuals who possess them.\n</p><p>Several authors also claim that evolution and sometimes development require a specific form of randomness, namely the introduction of qualitatively new behaviors. Instead of the choice of one possibility among several pre-given ones, this randomness corresponds to the formation of new possibilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The characteristics of an organism arise to some extent deterministically (e.g., under the influence of genes and the environment) and to some extent randomly. For example, the <i>density</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freckles" title="Freckles">freckles</a> that appear on a person\'s skin is controlled by genes and exposure to light; whereas the exact location of <i>individual</i> freckles seems random.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As far as behavior is concerned, randomness is important if an animal is to behave in a way that is unpredictable to others. For instance, insects in flight tend to move about with random changes in direction, making it difficult for pursuing predators to predict their trajectories.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mathematics">In mathematics</span></h3>\n<p>The mathematical theory of <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> arose from attempts to formulate mathematical descriptions of chance events, originally in the context of <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, but later in connection with physics. <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">Statistics</a> is used to infer the underlying <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">probability distribution</a> of a collection of empirical observations. For the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulation</a>, it is necessary to have a large supply of <a href="/wiki/Random_sequence" title="Random sequence">random numbers</a> or means to generate them on demand.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_information_theory" title="Algorithmic information theory">Algorithmic information theory</a> studies, among other topics, what constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Random_sequence" title="Random sequence">random sequence</a>. The central idea is that a string of <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bits</a> is random if and only if it is shorter than any computer program that can produce that string (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_randomness" title="Kolmogorov randomness">Kolmogorov randomness</a>)&#8212;this means that random strings are those that cannot be <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">compressed</a>. Pioneers of this field include <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andrey Kolmogorov</a> and his student <a href="/wiki/Per_Martin-L%C3%B6f" title="Per Martin-L&#246;f">Per Martin-L&#246;f</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_Solomonoff" title="Ray Solomonoff">Ray Solomonoff</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Chaitin" title="Gregory Chaitin">Gregory Chaitin</a>. For the notion of infinite sequence, one normally uses <a href="/wiki/Per_Martin-L%C3%B6f" title="Per Martin-L&#246;f">Per Martin-L&#246;f</a>\'s definition.\nThat is, an infinite sequence is random if and only if it withstands all recursively enumerable null sets. The other notions of random sequences include (but not limited to): recursive randomness and Schnorr randomness which are based on recursively computable martingales. It was shown by <a href="/wiki/Yongge_Wang" title="Yongge Wang">Yongge Wang</a> that these randomness notions are generally different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Randomness occurs in numbers such as <a href="/wiki/Binary_logarithm" title="Binary logarithm">log (2)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">pi</a>. The decimal digits of pi constitute an infinite sequence and "never repeat in a cyclical fashion." Numbers like pi are also considered likely to be <a href="/wiki/Normal_number" title="Normal number">normal</a>, which means their digits are random in a certain statistical sense.\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\nPi certainly seems to behave this way. In the first six billion decimal places of pi, each of the digits from 0 through 9 shows up about six hundred million times. Yet such results, conceivably accidental, do not prove normality even in base 10, much less normality in other number bases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_statistics">In statistics</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Statistical_randomness" title="Statistical randomness">Statistical randomness</a></div>\n<p>In statistics, randomness is commonly used to create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_random_samples" title="Simple random samples">simple random samples</a>. This lets surveys of completely random groups of people provide realistic data. Common methods of doing this include drawing names out of a hat or using a random digit chart. A random digit chart is simply a large table of random digits.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_information_science">In information science</span></h3>\n<p>In information science, irrelevant or meaningless data is considered noise. Noise consists of a large number of transient disturbances with a statistically randomized time distribution.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Communication_theory" title="Communication theory">communication theory</a>, randomness in a signal is called "noise" and is opposed to that component of its variation that is causally attributable to the source, the signal.\n</p><p>In terms of the development of random networks, for communication randomness rests on the two simple assumptions of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Erd%C5%91s" title="Paul Erd&#337;s">Paul Erd&#337;s</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfr%C3%A9d_R%C3%A9nyi" title="Alfr&#233;d R&#233;nyi">Alfr&#233;d R&#233;nyi</a> who said that there were a fixed number of nodes and this number remained fixed for the life of the network, and that all nodes were equal and linked randomly to each other.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_finance">In finance</span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Random_walk_hypothesis" title="Random walk hypothesis">random walk hypothesis</a> considers that asset prices in an organized <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> evolve at random, in the sense that the expected value of their change is zero but the actual value may turn out to be positive or negative. More generally, asset prices are influenced by a variety of unpredictable events in the general economic environment.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_politics">In politics</span></h2>\n<p>Random selection can be an official method to resolve <a href="/wiki/Tie_(draw)" title="Tie (draw)">tied</a> elections in some jurisdictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Its use in politics is very old, as office holders in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Athens" title="Ancient Athens">Ancient Athens</a> were chosen by lot, there being no voting.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Randomness_and_religion">Randomness and religion</span></h2>\n<p>Randomness can be seen as conflicting with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deterministic" title="Deterministic">deterministic</a> ideas of some religions, such as those where the universe is created by an omniscient deity who is aware of all past and future events. If the universe is regarded to have a purpose, then randomness can be seen as impossible. This is one of the rationales for religious opposition to <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, which states that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-random" title="Non-random">non-random</a> selection is applied to the results of random genetic variation.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> philosophies state that any event is the result of previous events, as reflected in the concept of <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a>, and as such there is no such thing as a random event or a first event<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.\n</p><p>In some religious contexts, procedures that are commonly perceived as randomizers are used for divination. <a href="/wiki/Cleromancy" title="Cleromancy">Cleromancy</a> uses the casting of bones or dice to reveal what is seen as the will of the gods.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Applications_of_randomness" title="Applications of randomness">Applications of randomness</a></div>\n<p>In most of its mathematical, political, social and religious uses, randomness is used for its innate "fairness" and lack of bias.\n</p><p><b>Politics</b>: <a href="/wiki/Athenian_democracy" title="Athenian democracy">Athenian democracy</a> was based on the concept of <a href="/wiki/Isonomia" title="Isonomia">isonomia</a> (equality of political rights) and used complex allotment machines to ensure that the positions on the ruling committees that ran Athens were fairly allocated. <a href="/wiki/Sortition" title="Sortition">Allotment</a> is now restricted to selecting jurors in Anglo-Saxon legal systems and in situations where "fairness" is approximated by <a href="/wiki/Randomization" title="Randomization">randomization</a>, such as selecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juror" title="Juror">jurors</a> and military <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">draft</a> lotteries.\n</p><p><b>Games</b>: Random numbers were first investigated in the context of <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, and many randomizing devices, such as <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shuffling_playing_cards" title="Shuffling playing cards">shuffling playing cards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a> wheels, were first developed for use in gambling. The ability to produce random numbers fairly is vital to electronic gambling, and, as such, the methods used to create them are usually regulated by government <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaming_Control_Board" title="Gaming Control Board">Gaming Control Boards</a>. Random drawings are also used to determine <a href="/wiki/Lottery" title="Lottery">lottery</a> winners.  Throughout history, randomness has been used for games of chance and to select out individuals for an unwanted task in a fair way (see <a href="/wiki/Drawing_straws" title="Drawing straws">drawing straws</a>).\n</p><p><b>Sports</b>: Some sports, including <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a>, use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coin_toss" title="Coin toss">coin tosses</a> to randomly select starting conditions for games or <a href="/wiki/Seed_(sports)" title="Seed (sports)">seed</a> tied teams for <a href="/wiki/Playoffs" title="Playoffs">postseason play</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> uses a weighted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Draft_Lottery" title="NBA Draft Lottery">lottery</a> to order teams in its draft.\n</p><p><b>Mathematics</b>: Random numbers are also employed where their use is mathematically important, such as sampling for <a href="/wiki/Opinion_poll" title="Opinion poll">opinion polls</a> and for statistical sampling in <a href="/wiki/Quality_control" title="Quality control">quality control</a> systems. Computational solutions for some types of problems use random numbers extensively, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_method" title="Monte Carlo method">Monte Carlo method</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_algorithm" title="Genetic algorithm">genetic algorithms</a>.\n</p><p><b>Medicine</b>: Random allocation of a clinical intervention is used to reduce bias in controlled trials (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trials" title="Randomized controlled trials">randomized controlled trials</a>).\n</p><p><b>Religion</b>: Although not intended to be random, various forms of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cleromancy" title="Cleromancy">cleromancy</a> see what appears to be a random event as a means for a divine being to communicate their will. (See also <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">Free will</a> and <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">Determinism</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">Generation</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Random_number_generation" title="Random number generation">Random number generation</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roulette_wheel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="921" decoding="async" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Roulette_wheel.jpg/200px-Roulette_wheel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Roulette_wheel.jpg/300px-Roulette_wheel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Roulette_wheel.jpg/400px-Roulette_wheel.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roulette_wheel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ball in a <a href="/wiki/Roulette" title="Roulette">roulette</a> can be used as a source of apparent randomness, because its behavior is very sensitive to the initial conditions.</div></div></div>\n<p>It is generally accepted that there exist three mechanisms responsible for (apparently) random behavior in systems:\n</p>\n<ol><li><i>Randomness</i> coming from the environment (for example, <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Hardware_random_number_generator" title="Hardware random number generator">hardware random number generators</a>)</li>\n<li><i>Randomness</i> coming from the initial conditions. This aspect is studied by <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a> and is observed in systems whose behavior is very sensitive to small variations in initial conditions (such as <a href="/wiki/Pachinko" title="Pachinko">pachinko</a> machines and <a href="/wiki/Dice" title="Dice">dice</a>).</li>\n<li><i>Randomness</i> intrinsically generated by the system. This is also called <a href="/wiki/Pseudorandomness" title="Pseudorandomness">pseudorandomness</a> and is the kind used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-random_number_generator" title="Pseudo-random number generator">pseudo-random number generators</a>. There are many algorithms (based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetics" title="Arithmetics">arithmetics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">cellular automaton</a>) to generate pseudorandom numbers. The behavior of the system can be determined by knowing the <a href="/wiki/Random_seed" title="Random seed">seed state</a> and the algorithm used. These methods are often quicker than getting "true" randomness from the environment.</li></ol>\n<p>The many <a href="/wiki/Applications_of_randomness" title="Applications of randomness">applications of randomness</a> have led to many different methods for generating random data. These methods may vary as to how unpredictable or <a href="/wiki/Statistical_randomness" title="Statistical randomness">statistically random</a> they are, and how quickly they can generate random numbers.\n</p><p>Before the advent of computational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_number_generator" title="Random number generator">random number generators</a>, generating large amounts of sufficiently random numbers (important in statistics) required a lot of work. Results would sometimes be collected and distributed as <a href="/wiki/Random_number_table" title="Random number table">random number tables</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures_and_tests">Measures and tests</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Randomness_tests" title="Randomness tests">Randomness tests</a></div>\n<p>There are many practical measures of randomness for a binary sequence. These include measures based on frequency, <a href="/wiki/Discrete_transform" title="Discrete transform">discrete transforms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>, or a mixture of these. These include tests by Kak, Phillips, Yuen, Hopkins, Beth and Dai, Mund, and Marsaglia and Zaman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Quantum Non-Locality has been used to certify the presence of genuine randomness in a given string of numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Misconceptions_and_logical_fallacies">Misconceptions and logical fallacies</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gambler%27s_fallacy" title="Gambler\'s fallacy">Gambler\'s fallacy</a></div>\n<p>Popular perceptions of randomness are frequently mistaken, based on fallacious reasoning or intuitions.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="A_number_is_.22due.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'A_number_is_"due"\'>A number is "due"</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coupon_collector%27s_problem" title="Coupon collector\'s problem">Coupon collector\'s problem</a></div>\n<p>This argument is, "In a random selection of numbers, since all numbers eventually appear,  those that have not come up yet are \'due\', and thus more likely to come up soon."  This logic is only correct if applied to a system where numbers that come up are removed from the system, such as when <a href="/wiki/Playing_card" title="Playing card">playing cards</a> are drawn and not returned to the deck. In this case, once a jack is removed from the deck, the next draw is less likely to be a jack and more likely to be some other card. However, if the jack is returned to the deck, and the deck is thoroughly reshuffled, a jack is as likely to be drawn as any other card. The same applies in any other process where objects are selected independently, and none are removed after each event, such as the roll of a die, a coin toss, or most <a href="/wiki/Lottery" title="Lottery">lottery</a> number selection schemes. Truly random processes such as these do not have memory, making it impossible for past outcomes to affect future outcomes.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="A_number_is_.22cursed.22_or_.22blessed.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'A_number_is_"cursed"_or_"blessed"\'>A number is "cursed" or "blessed"</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Benford%27s_law" title="Benford\'s law">Benford\'s law</a></div>\n<p>In a random sequence of numbers, a number may be said to be cursed because it has come up less often in the past, and so it is thought that it will occur less often in the future. A number may be assumed to be blessed because it has occurred more often than others in the past, and so it is thought likely to come up more often in the future. This logic is valid only if the randomisation is biased, for example with a loaded die. If the die is fair, then previous rolls give no indication of future events.\n</p><p>In nature, events rarely occur with perfectly equal frequency, so observing outcomes to determine which events are more probable makes sense. It is fallacious to apply this logic to systems designed to make all outcomes equally likely, such as shuffled cards, dice, and roulette wheels.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Odds_are_never_dynamic">Odds are never dynamic</span></h3>\n<p>In the beginning of a scenario, one might calculate the probability of a certain event. The fact is, as soon as one gains more information about that situation, they may need to re-calculate the probability.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monty_open_door.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="180" decoding="async" height="56" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Monty_open_door.svg/100px-Monty_open_door.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Monty_open_door.svg/150px-Monty_open_door.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Monty_open_door.svg/200px-Monty_open_door.svg.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monty_open_door.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>When the host reveals one door that contains a goat, this is new information.</div></div></div>\n<p>Say we are told that a woman has two children. If we ask whether either of them is a girl, and are told yes, what is the probability that the other child is also a girl? Considering this new child independently, one might expect the probability that the other child is female is &#189; (50%). But by building a <a href="/wiki/Probability_space" title="Probability space">probability space</a> (illustrating all possible outcomes), we see that the probability is actually only &#8531; (33%). This is because the possibility space illustrates 4 ways of having these two children: boy-boy, girl-boy, boy-girl, and girl-girl. But we were given more information. Once we are told that one of the children is a female, we use this new information to eliminate the boy-boy scenario. Thus the probability space reveals that there are still 3 ways to have two children where one is a female: boy-girl, girl-boy, girl-girl. Only &#8531; of these scenarios would have the other child also be a girl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYOdds_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYOdds-17">[17]</a></sup> Using a probability space, we are less likely to miss one of the possible scenarios, or to neglect the importance of new information. For further information, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boy_or_girl_paradox" title="Boy or girl paradox">Boy or girl paradox</a>.\n</p><p>This technique provides insights in other situations such as the <a href="/wiki/Monty_Hall_problem" title="Monty Hall problem">Monty Hall problem</a>, a game show scenario in which a car is hidden behind one of three doors, and two goats are hidden as <a href="/wiki/Booby_prize" title="Booby prize">booby prizes</a> behind the others. Once the contestant has chosen a door, the host opens one of the remaining doors to reveal a goat, eliminating that door as an option. With only two doors left (one with the car, the other with another goat), the player must decide to either keep their decision, or switch and select the other door. Intuitively, one might think the player is choosing between two doors with equal probability, and that the opportunity to choose another door makes no difference. But probability spaces reveal that the contestant has received new information, and can increase their chances of winning by changing to the other door.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYOdds_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYOdds-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<div aria-label="Portals" class="noprint portal
