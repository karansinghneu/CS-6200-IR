<b>in-space propulsion technologies</b> describe the propulsion technologies that could meet future <a href="/wiki/Space_research" title="Space research">space science and exploration</a> needs. These propulsion technologies are intended to provide effective <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">exploration</a> of our <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> and will permit mission designers to plan missions to "fly anytime, anywhere, and complete a host of science objectives at the destinations" and with greater reliability and safety. With a wide range of possible missions and candidate propulsion technologies, the question of which technologies are "best" for future missions is a difficult one. A portfolio of propulsion technologies should be developed to provide optimum solutions for a diverse set of missions and destinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mason_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mason-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leone_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-leone-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>In-space propulsion</b> begins where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_stage" title="Upper stage">upper stage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> leaves off; performing the functions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_propulsion" title="Rocket propulsion">primary propulsion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reaction_engine" title="Reaction engine">reaction control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping" title="Orbital station-keeping">station keeping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">precision pointing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orbital_maneuver" title="Orbital maneuver">orbital maneuvering</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">main engines</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> provide the primary propulsive force for <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">orbit transfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">planetary trajectories</a> and extra <a href="/wiki/List_of_landings_on_extraterrestrial_bodies" title="List of landings on extraterrestrial bodies">planetary landing</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ascent" title="wikt:ascent">ascent</a>. The reaction control and orbital maneuvering systems provide the propulsive force for orbit maintenance, position control, station keeping, and spacecraft attitude control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mason_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mason-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A large fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a> in use today are <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine#Chemically_powered" title="Rocket engine">chemical rockets</a>; that is, they obtain the energy needed to generate thrust by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a> to create a hot gas that is expanded to produce <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a>. A significant limitation of <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine#Chemically_powered" title="Rocket engine">chemical propulsion</a> is that it has a relatively low <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> (Isp), which is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of the thrust produced to the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass of propellant</a> needed at a certain <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">rate of flow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ion_Engine_Test_Firing_-_GPN-2000-000482.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1482" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Ion_Engine_Test_Firing_-_GPN-2000-000482.jpg/338px-Ion_Engine_Test_Firing_-_GPN-2000-000482.jpg 1.5x, improvement (above 30%) in specific impulse can be obtained by using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_propellant" title="Cryogenic propellant">cryogenic propellants</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>, for example. Historically, these propellants have not been applied beyond <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_stage" title="Rocket stage">upper stages</a>. Furthermore, numerous concepts for advanced propulsion technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Electrically_powered_spacecraft_propulsion" title="Electrically powered spacecraft propulsion">electric propulsion</a>, are commonly used for station keeping on commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_satellites" title="Communications satellites">communications satellites</a> and for prime propulsion on some <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">scientific space missions</a> because they have significantly higher values of specific impulse. However, they generally have very small values of thrust and therefore must be operated for long durations to provide the total impulse required by a mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tomsik_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tomsik-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oleson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-oleson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several of these technologies offer performance that is significantly better than that achievable with chemical propulsion.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metrics">Metrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In-space propulsion represents technologies that can significantly improve a number of critical aspects of the mission. <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">Space exploration</a> is about getting somewhere safely (mission enabling), getting there quickly (reduced transit times), getting a lot of mass there (increased <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a> mass), and getting there cheaply (lower cost). The simple act of "getting" there requires the employment of an in-space propulsion system, and the other metrics are modifiers to this fundamental action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leone_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-leone-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Development of technologies will result in technical solutions that improve thrust levels, Isp, power, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_mass" title="Specific mass">specific mass</a>, (or <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">specific power</a>), volume, system mass, system complexity, operational complexity, commonality with other spacecraft systems, manufacturability, durability, and cost. These types of improvements will yield decreased transit times, increased payload mass, safer spacecraft, and decreased costs. In some instances, development of technologies within this TA will result in mission- enabling breakthroughs that will revolutionize space exploration. There is no single propulsion technology that will benefit all missions or mission types. The requirements for in-space propulsion vary widely due according to their intended application. The described technologies should support everything from small <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_space_exploration" title="Deep space exploration">robotic deep space exploration</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_stations" title="Space stations">space stations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">human missions to Mars applications</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leone_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-leone-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_area_breakdown">Technology area breakdown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technology area breakdown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The technology areas are divided into four basic groups: (1) Chemical propulsion, (2) Nonchemical propulsion, (3) Advanced propulsion technologies, and (4) Supporting technologies; based on the physics of the propulsion system and how it derives thrust as well as its technical maturity. Additionally, there may be credible meritorious in-space propulsion concepts not foreseen or reviewed at the time of publication, and which may be shown to be beneficial to future mission applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_technologies">Defining technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Defining technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Furthermore, the term "mission pull" defines a technology or a performance characteristic necessary to meet a planned NASA mission requirement. Any other relationship between a technology and a mission (an alternate propulsion system, for example) is categorized as "technology push." Also, a space demonstration refers to the spaceflight of a scaled version of a particular technology or of a critical technology subsystem. On the other hand, a space validation would serve as a qualification flight for future mission implementation. A successful validation flight would not require any additional space testing of a particular technology before it can be adopted for a science or exploration mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_challenge">The challenge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The challenge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For both human and robotic exploration, traversing the solar system is a struggle against time and distance. The most distant planets are 4.5&#8211;6 billion kilometers from the Sun and to reach them in any reasonable time requires much more capable propulsion systems than conventional chemical rockets. Rapid inner solar system missions with flexible launch dates are difficult, requiring propulsion systems that are beyond today\'s current state of the art. The logistics, and therefore the total system mass required to support sustained human exploration beyond Earth to destinations such as the Moon, Mars or Near Earth Objects,\nare daunting unless more efficient in-space propulsion technologies are developed and fielded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meyer_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-meyer-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huntsb_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-huntsb-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_propulsion_technologies">Primary propulsion technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In-space_propulsion_technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Primary propulsion technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Work being performed at the <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Research_Center" title="Glenn Research Center">Glenn Research Center</a> develops primary propulsion technologies that can benefit near and mid-term science missions by reducing cost, mass and/or travel times. The In-Space Program is working to develop next generation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_propulsion" title="Electric propulsion">electric propulsion</a> technologies, including Ion and Hall thrusters.  Solar Sails, which are a form of propellantless propulsion, are also being developed. <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">Solar sails</a> rely on the naturally occurring sunlight for the propulsion energy. Other propulsion technologies being developed include advanced chemical propulsion and aerocapture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leone_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-leone-3">[3]</a></sup><sup
