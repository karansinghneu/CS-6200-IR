For the 1999 album, see <a href="/wiki/Space_Age_2.0" title="Space Age 2.0">Space Age 2.0</a>.</div>\n<p><b>NewSpace</b>, or <b>new space</b>, is a term for a <a href="/wiki/Philosophical_movement" title="Philosophical movement">movement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nagendra_1-0"><a Specifically, the term is used to refer to a global sector of new <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> companies and ventures working independently of governments and traditional major contractors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heeding_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heeding-4">[4]</a></sup> to develop faster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morring_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morring-5">[5]</a></sup> better, and cheaper access to space and spaceflight technologies, driven by commercial, as distinct from political or other, motivations to broader, more <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomics" title="Socioeconomics">socioeconomically</a>-oriented, ends.\n</p><p>Investment bank Morgan Stanley expects 2019 to show "key milestones and catalysts [in the new space sector]"\nand advises its customers to "pay attention to space companies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input href="/w/index.php?title=NewSpace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a>, which began in the mid-1950s and gave birth in earnest to spaceflight, was famously a manifestation of the then larger politico-economic competition between <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> (represented by the United States) and <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> (represented by the former Soviet Union). For this reason, from the very beginning, the American business establishment&#8212;particularly those bellwether private firms directly involved in the U.S. space program&#8212;has championed the private development of space and space activity. In 1961, writing as one of the deans of the American business establishment, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_J._Cordiner" title="Ralph J. Cordiner">Ralph J. Cordiner</a>, then chairman of <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> (a blue-chip, charter-prime contractor to NASA and the U.S. space program), contributed a chapter titled "Competitive Private Enterprise in Space" to the anthology <i>Peacetime Uses of Outer Space</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peacetime_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peacetime-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-8">[8]</a></sup> While recognizing at the time the realities of having to initially rely on the U.S. government\'s vast and convenient organization, resources, and power in order to effectively address the immediate Soviet space challenge, Cordiner nonetheless advocated <a href="/wiki/Private_sector" title="Private sector">private sector</a> dominance&#8212;ultimately&#8212;of space activity, consistent with textbook American capitalist ideals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peacetime_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peacetime-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nIn fact, <a href="/wiki/Syncom" title="Syncom">Syncom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a>\'s pioneering commercial <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellite</a> system, was originally conceived as a direct competitive response by American private industry to the Soviet\'s successful deployment of <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik</a> in 1957, the iconic <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> event that triggered the Space Race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harold_Rosen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harold_Rosen-9">[9]</a></sup> While Syncom was eventually successfully deployed in 1963, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Harold_Rosen_(electrical_engineer)" title="Harold Rosen (electrical engineer)">Harold Rosen</a>, the Hughes <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> responsible for developing, championing, and spearheading Syncom (also brother of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_M._Rosen" title="Benjamin M. Rosen">Ben Rosen</a>, a pioneering Silicon Valley venture capitalist and entrepreneur, and Wall Street technology analyst), cited a general lack of confidence in the U.S. government\'s early launch capabilities. He later explained the Syncom project\'s lengthy gestation period:</p><blockquote><p>This was not the most auspicious time [late 1950s] to propose a commercial space program...The most vivid impression most people then had of space-related activities was of rockets blowing up at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station">Cape Canaveral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harold_Rosen_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harold_Rosen-9">[9]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s:_U.S._commercial_space_policy_and_enabling_legislation">1980s: U.S. commercial space policy and enabling legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NewSpace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980s: U.S. commercial space policy and enabling legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Notwithstanding the free-enterprise sentiments and preferences of American industry, space remained a firmly government-controlled and -directed endeavor well after the capstone Apollo moon landing in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barcomb_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barcomb-10">[10]</a></sup> The term "alt.space" was first used in the early 1980s to describe companies that were at last beginning to take up Cordiner\'s mantle and make serious efforts to reach <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> without needing or relying on the cooperation of NASA or other governmental agencies (or, by extension, even their major contractors); efforts which were catalyzed by an historic shift in U.S. policy favoring private space activity, culminating in the landmark <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Space_Launch_Act_of_1984" title="Commercial Space Launch Act of 1984">Commercial Space Launch Act of 1984</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Beyond the terminology&#8212;"alt.space", "private space", "NewSpace," or "new space"&#8212;since the 1980s, the philosophy of various organizations (such as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Frontier_Foundation" title="Space Frontier Foundation">Space Frontier Foundation</a> in the United States) has been one of "extolling the virtues of Solar System settlement and operating independent of bureaucratic government programs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars20161006_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ars20161006-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s:_Post-Soviet_U.S.-Russian_private_space_ventures">1990s: Post-Soviet U.S.-Russian private space ventures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NewSpace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1990s: Post-Soviet U.S.-Russian private space ventures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The seeds of today\'s NewSpace were brought to fruition by the collapse of the former Soviet Union in 1991 and the releasing of that former rival-<a href="/wiki/Superpower" title="Superpower">superpower</a>\'s iconic, state-owned, and otherwise mature and proven space assets, technologies, capabilities, and services onto the world\'s private markets with the assistance of a handful of largely American private firms; notably these core-four: <a href="/wiki/International_Launch_Services" title="International Launch Services">International Launch Services</a> (f/k/a Lockheed-<a href="/wiki/Khrunichev_State_Research_and_Production_Space_Center" title="Khrunichev State Research and Production Space Center">Khrunichev</a>-Energia Int\'l;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">JV</a>; <a href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket_family)" title="Proton (rocket family)">Proton</a>; est. 1993);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLReport_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-QLReport-16">[16]</a></sup> Commercial Space Management Co. (CSMC; <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">Energia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(rocket_family)" title="Zenit (rocket family)">Zenit</a>, <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a>; est. 1993);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sell_Energia_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sell_Energia-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sea_Launch" title="Sea Launch">Sea Launch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> JV; Zenit; est. 1995);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLReport_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-QLReport-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_To_Make_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-How_To_Make-18">[18]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/MirCorp" title="MirCorp">MirCorp</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TM-30" title="Soyuz TM-30">Soyuz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progress_M1-5" title="Progress M1-5">Progress</a>; est. 1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foust_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foust-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stratosphere_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stratosphere-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Until that moment in world industrial history, no private business enterprise or entrepreneur could rightly conceive of, for example, leasing&#8212;or possibly owning and operating&#8212;an orbiting space station, such as Mir, or even just ordering a space launch in the ordinary course of business.\n</p><p>(Until then, even for a <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunications</a> giant, like <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a>, placing a commercial communications satellite in orbit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> for example, was a fairly monumental undertaking. Contrast that with today, when a $100 million space launch vehicle can now be specified, built, priced, ordered, and eventually even launched online through, for example, <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a>\'s <i>RocketBuilder</i> website.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once that industry-wide <i>mental block</i> was removed&#8212;once the <i>ease</i> (relatively speaking) and <a href="/wiki/Normalization_(sociology)" title="Normalization (sociology)">normalization</a> of planning and conducting space activities began to dawn on private industry&#8212;the <a href="/wiki/Animal_spirits_(Keynes)" title="Animal spirits (Keynes)">animal spirits</a> of aerospace capitalism were roused, entrepreneurial vision and imagination started to abound, and NewSpace began to take shape in earnest. This set off today\'s competitive, industry-wide, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtuous_cycle" title="Virtuous cycle">virtuous cycle</a> of "faster, better, cheaper" (a project and systems management<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> philosophy pioneered in the space field by NASA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>); and otherwise paved the way to today\'s generally far more vibrant and conducive space-business environment&#8212;whether or not involving Russian space resources, at this point&#8212;where entrepreneurs, investors, regulators, lawmakers, <a href="/wiki/Supranational_union" title="Supranational union">supranational organizations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a> (NGOs), the media, and other key ecosystem participants are now able to deal with privately conducted, for-profit space activity more rationally, practically, and cost-efficiently than ever before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barcomb_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barcomb-10">[10]</a></sup> (Wernher von Braun summed up the historical institutional-<a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucratic</a> cautiousness toward space activity in general by famously quipping, "We can lick gravity, but sometimes the paperwork is overwhelming.")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nIn his 2016 <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal"><i>Wall Street Journal</i></a> review of Julian Guthrie\'s book <i>How to Make a Spaceship: A Band of Renegades, an Epic Race, and the Birth of Private Spaceflight</i>, <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Easterbrook" title="Gregg Easterbrook">Gregg Easterbrook</a> highlighted the seminal importance of these often overlooked post-Soviet private space efforts in enabling and shaping today\'s NewSpace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renegades_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renegades-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_To_Make_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-How_To_Make-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>How to Make a Spaceship</i> centers largely around the efforts of space entrepreneur, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Diamandis" title="Peter Diamandis">Peter Diamandis</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Ansari_X_Prize" title="Ansari X Prize">Ansari X Prize</a> won in 2004 by the <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a> team led by American aerospace engineer <a href="/wiki/Burt_Rutan" title="Burt Rutan">Burt Rutan</a>, and funded by <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> co-founder and billionaire <a href="/wiki/Paul_Allen" title="Paul Allen">Paul Allen</a> (<a href="/wiki/SpaceShipTwo" title="SpaceShipTwo">SpaceShipTwo</a> was then funded by British billionaire and industrialist Sir Richard Branson and his Virgin Galactic). To set the stage, Guthrie retraces the private space industry\'s development path; however, according to Easterbrook:</p><blockquote><p>Mr. Diamandis wasn\'t the sole entrepreneur to pursue private space flight [early on]. Ms. Guthrie covers other, peculiar attempts.... Neglected in Ms. Guthrie\'s account is <a href="/wiki/Sea_Launch" title="Sea Launch">Sea Launch</a> [archetypal post-Soviet Boeing JV with Russians and others], the first private project to send heavy objects into orbit, including, in 2001, the big satellites Rock and Roll, the initial broadcast towers of XM Radio. Every bit as eccentric as the efforts that <i>How to Make a Spaceship</i> describes, Sea Launch fired large [Russian Zenit] rockets from a ship at the equator&#8212;equatorial water is the ideal position for space access&#8212;compiling a record of 32 successes, three failures and one satellite functioning but in the wrong orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_To_Make_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-How_To_Make-18">[18]</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>In 2001, the FAA/AST confirmed that NewSpace pioneer Sea Launch was indeed "[t]he first privately financed, working launch system and infrastructure...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QLReport_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-QLReport-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Near the end of the 1990s, favored by strong public policy, and spurred on by the foundational success of these post-Soviet U.S.-Russian private space ventures, there was a dramatic increase in companies engaging in this process, leading to common usage of the phrase "new space companies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joel_Achenbach_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joel_Achenbach-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Anderman_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_Anderman-32">[32]</a></sup> "NewSpace" (most prominently), "entrepreneurial space," and "commercial space" are now the most commonly used terms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sell_Energia_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sell_Energia-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daytona_Beach_Embry_Riddle_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daytona_Beach_Embry_Riddle-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Anderman_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-David_Anderman-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAA_OCST_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAA_OCST-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JF2007_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-JF2007-35">[35]</a></sup> though "alt.space" was still seen occasionally as late as 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsr20110815_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsr20110815-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s:_Silicon_Valley-style_entrepreneurial_space_initiatives">2000s: Silicon Valley-style entrepreneurial space initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NewSpace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000s: Silicon Valley-style entrepreneurial space initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Things changed further in the early 2000s as <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> formed <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> with significantly more private capital while he articulated a strong and consistent vision of the "colonization of space, beginning with Mars."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars20161006_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ars20161006-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, one company in a worldwide milieu of government-driven spaceflight activities simply did not cement a movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars20161006_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ars20161006-2">[2]</a></sup> This began to change with the increasingly public revelations and pronouncements of <a href="/wiki/Blue_Origin" title="Blue Origin">Blue Origin</a> after 2014. Even though the company was formed about the same time as <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a>, it had maintained a very low profile in its first decade-and-a-half of existence. By 2016, both of these private companies, with billion-US-dollar-plus backing by committed investors, were successfully <a href="/wiki/VTVL" title="VTVL">vertically landing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_vehicle" title="Reusable launch vehicle">reusing</a> space launch vehicles. Both companies are building large reusable orbital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_system" title="Launch system">launch systems</a> that will utilize currently-under-development rocket engines that are each at least four years along in development, and are already in use or under development test on ground test stands, all with a focus on radically lowering the price of carrying people and cargo to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars20161006_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ars20161006-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bt20120627_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-bt20120627-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beginning on November 23, 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Blue Origin successfully demonstrated the repeated reuse of a rocket for the first time ever, by completing five <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">suborbital</a>, vertical takeoff and landing (<a href="/wiki/VTOL" title="VTOL">VTOL</a>) flights of the same <a href="/wiki/New_Shepard" title="New Shepard">New Shepard</a> rocket; a feat for which Blue Origin was awarded the prestigious 2016 Robert J. <a href="/wiki/Collier_Trophy" title="Collier Trophy">Collier Trophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nOn March 30, 2017, SpaceX successfully relaunched a previously flown orbital-class rocket (<a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a>) for the first time in history, an achievement many compare in significance to that of the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a>\' <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">first flight</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Falcon_Wright_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Falcon_Wright-40">[40]</a></sup> Celebrated <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Neil_deGrasse_Tyson" title="Neil deGrasse Tyson">Neil deGrasse Tyson</a> described the underlying economic importance of SpaceX\'s otherwise technical <a href="/wiki/Milestone" title="Milestone">milestone</a>:</p><blockquote><p>Any demonstration of rocket reusability is a good thing. [...] When we fly on a Boeing 747 across great distances, we don\'t throw it away and roll out a new one. Reusability is arguably the most fundamental feature of affordable expensive things.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Echoing deGrasse Tyson\'s post-flight sentiments, former NASA official (and current engineering dean at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Boulder" title="University of Colorado Boulder">University of Colorado Boulder</a>) <a href="/wiki/Robert_D._Braun" title="Robert D. Braun">Bobby Braun</a> "compared the [Falcon 9] rocket to the first successful commercial airliner, the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a>, which ushered in the <a
