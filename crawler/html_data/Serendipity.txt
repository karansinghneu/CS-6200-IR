In recent years, the phenomenon has become a potential design principle in online activity for preventing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_bubbles" title="Filter bubbles">filter bubbles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Echo_chamber" title="Echo chamber">echo chambers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first noted use of "serendipity" in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> was by <a href="/wiki/Horace_Walpole" title="Horace Walpole">Horace Walpole</a> in 1754. In a letter he wrote to his friend <a href="/wiki/Sir_Horace_Mann,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Horace Mann, 1st Baronet">Horace Mann</a>, Walpole explained an unexpected discovery he had made about a <a href="/wiki/Lost_artworks" title="Lost artworks">lost painting</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bianca_Cappello" title="Bianca Cappello">Bianca Cappello</a> by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a> by reference to a Persian fairy tale, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Princes_of_Serendip" title="The Three Princes of Serendip">The Three Princes of Serendip</a></i>. The princes, he told his correspondent, were "always making discoveries, by accidents and sagacity, of things which they were not in quest of".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The name comes from <i><a href="/wiki/Names_of_Sri_Lanka#Ceylon,_Serendip_and_related_names" title="Names of Sri Lanka">Serendip</a></i>, an old name for <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> (Ceylon), hence <i>Sarandib</i> by Arab traders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is derived from the Sanskrit <i>Si&#7747;haladv&#299;pa&#7717;</i> (Si&#7747;hala&#7717;, Sri Lanka + dv&#299;pa&#7717;, island).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2004, a British translation company voted the word to be one of the ten English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Words_hardest_to_translate" title="Words hardest to translate">words hardest to translate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The word has been exported into many other languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inventions">Inventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "serendipity" is often applied to inventions made by chance rather than intent. Andrew Smith, editor of <i>The</i> <i>Oxford Companion to American Food and Drink</i>, has speculated that most everyday products had serendipitous roots, with many early ones related to animals. The origin of cheese, for example, possibly originated in the <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">Nomad</a> practice of storing milk in the stomach of a dead camel that was attached to the saddle of a live one, thereby mixing <a href="/wiki/Rennet" title="Rennet">rennet</a> from the stomach with the milk stored within.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-It_Note" title="Post-It Note">Post-It Note</a> emerged after a 3M scientist <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Silver" title="Spencer Silver">Spencer Silver</a> produced a weak adhesive, and a colleague used it to keep bookmarks in place on a church hymnal. <a href="/wiki/Silly_Putty" title="Silly Putty">Silly Putty</a> came from a failed attempt at <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a>. The use of sensors to prevent automobile air bags from killing children came from a chair developed by the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">MIT Media Lab</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Penn_%26_Teller" title="Penn &amp; Teller">Penn and Teller</a> magic show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> Raytheon scientist Percy Spencer first patented the idea behind the microwave oven after noticing that emissions from radar equipment had melted the candy in his pocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On a bird hunting trip, George de Mestral took a closer look at the <a href="/wiki/Xanthium" title="Xanthium">cockleburs</a> stuck to his pants and dog. Viewed under a microscope,  each burr was covered with tiny hooks. The observation eventually led to the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Velcro" title="Velcro">Velcro</a> hook-and-loop fastener.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In San Francisco, Frank Epperson, age 11, accidentally left a mix of water and soda powder outside; the frozen result became the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Popsicle_(brand)" title="Popsicle (brand)">Popsicle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discoveries">Discoveries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discoveries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Entomologist Shaun Winterton discovered <i>Semachrysa jade</i>, a new species of <a href="/wiki/Neuroptera" title="Neuroptera">lacewing</a>, finding it not in its native Malasia, but on the photo-sharing site <a href="/wiki/Flickr" title="Flickr">Flickr</a>. His discovery was aided by Flickr\'s ability to present images that are personalized to a user\'s interests, thereby increasing the odds Winterton would chance upon the photo. Computer scientist <a href="/wiki/Jaime_Teevan" title="Jaime Teevan">Jaime Teevan</a> has argued that serendipitous discovery is promoted by such personalization, writing that "people don&#8217;t know what to do with random new information. Instead, we want information that is at the fringe of what we already know, because that is when we have the cognitive structures to make sense of the new ideas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms"><span id="Zemblanity"></span><span id="Bahramdipity"></span>Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/William_Boyd_(writer)" title="William Boyd (writer)">William Boyd</a> coined the term <b>zemblanity</b> in the late twentieth century to mean somewhat the opposite of serendipity: "making unhappy, unlucky and unexpected discoveries occurring by design".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A zemblanity is, effectively, an "unpleasant unsurprise". It derives from <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> (or Nova Zembla), a cold, barren land with many features opposite to the lush Sri Lanka (Serendip). On this island <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willem_Barents" title="Willem Barents">Willem Barents</a> and his crew were stranded while searching for a new route to the east.\n</p><p><b>Bahramdipity</b> is derived directly from <a href="/wiki/Bahram_V" title="Bahram V">Bahram Gur</a> as characterized in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Princes_of_Serendip" title="The Three Princes of Serendip">The Three Princes of Serendip</a></i>.  It describes the <i>suppression</i> of serendipitous discoveries or research results by powerful individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Browse" title="Browse">Browse</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Chance_(disambiguation)" title="Chance (disambiguation)">Chance</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Coincidence" title="Coincidence">Coincidence</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Felix_culpa" title="Felix culpa">Felix culpa</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Insight" title="Insight">Insight</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lateral_thinking" title="Lateral thinking">Lateral thinking</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Multiple_discovery" title="Multiple discovery">Multiple discovery</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Role_of_chance_in_scientific_discoveries" title="Role of chance in scientific discoveries">Role of chance in scientific discoveries</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serendipity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
