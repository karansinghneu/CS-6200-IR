href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'l\' in \'lie\'">l</span><span title="/&#603;/: \'e\' in \'dress\'">&#603;</span><span title="\'v\' in \'vie\'">v</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>; July 4, 1868 &#8211; December 12, 1921) was an American <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>.  A graduate of <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a>, she worked at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College_Observatory" title="Harvard College Observatory">Harvard College Observatory</a> as a "<a href="/wiki/Human_computer" title="Human computer">computer</a>", tasked with examining <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plates</a> in order to measure and catalog the brightness of stars. This work led her to discover the relation between the <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> and the period of <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variables</a>.  Though she received little recognition in her lifetime, Leavitt\'s discovery provided astronomers with the first "<a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">standard candle</a>" with which to measure the distance to faraway <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>.  After her death, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> used Leavitt\'s luminosity&#8211;period relation, together with the galactic spectral shifts first measured by <a href="/wiki/Vesto_Slipher" title="Vesto Slipher">Vesto Slipher</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">Lowell Observatory</a>, in order to establish that the universe is expanding (see <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a>).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_and_education">Early years and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Henrietta Swan Leavitt was born in <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Massachusetts" title="Lancaster, Massachusetts">Lancaster, Massachusetts</a>, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregational church</a> minister George Roswell Leavitt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSMonitor_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSMonitor-1">[1]</a></sup> and his wife Henrietta Swan Kendrick. She was a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">Deacon</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Leavitt" title="John Leavitt">John Leavitt</a>, an English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> tailor, who settled in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony" title="Massachusetts Bay Colony">Massachusetts Bay Colony</a> in the early seventeenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Byers-2">[2]</a></sup> (In the early Massachusetts records the family name was spelled "Levett".)\n</p><p>Leavitt attended <a href="/wiki/Oberlin_College" title="Oberlin College">Oberlin College</a> before transferring to <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>\'s Society for the Collegiate Instruction of Women (later known as <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a>), receiving a bachelor\'s degree in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-3">[3]</a></sup>  At Oberlin and Harvard, Leavitt studied a broad curriculum that included classical Greek, fine arts, philosophy, analytic geometry, and calculus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnegiescience_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-carnegiescience-4">[4]</a></sup> It wasn\'t until her fourth year of college that Leavitt took a course in astronomy, in which she earned an A&#8211;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>27</span></sup>  Leavitt also began working as one of the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Computers" title="Harvard Computers">women human "computers"</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College_Observatory" title="Harvard College Observatory">Harvard College Observatory</a>, hired by its director <a href="/wiki/Edward_Charles_Pickering" title="Edward Charles Pickering">Edward Charles Pickering</a> to measure and catalog the brightness of stars as they appeared in the observatory\'s <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plate</a> collection. (In the early 1900s, women were not allowed to operate telescopes.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exploratorium_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Exploratorium-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1893, Leavitt obtained credits toward a graduate degree in astronomy for her work at the Harvard College Observatory, but she never completed that degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kidwell_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kidwell-7">[7]</a></sup>  Leavitt left the observatory to make two trips to Europe and complete a stint as an art assistant at <a href="/wiki/Beloit_College" title="Beloit College">Beloit College</a> in Wisconsin.  At this time she contracted an illness that caused her increasingly to lose her hearing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hockey_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hockey-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamblin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_career">Astronomical career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Astronomical career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg"><img alt="Woman sitting at desk writing, with short hair, long-sleeved white blouse and vest" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="519" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg/270px-Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg/405px-Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg 2x" width="270"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leavitt_henrietta_b1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Henrietta Swan Leavitt working at her desk in the Harvard College Observatory<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamblin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-9">[9]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Leavitt returned to the Harvard College Observatory in 1903.  Because Leavitt had independent means, Pickering initially did not have to pay her. Later, she received $0.30 an hour for her work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>32</span></sup> being paid only $10.50 per week. She was reportedly "hard-working, serious-minded &#8230;, little given to frivolous pursuits and selflessly devoted to her family, her church, and her career".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnegiescience_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-carnegiescience-4">[4]</a></sup>  One of the women that Leavitt worked with in the Harvard Observatory was <a href="/wiki/Annie_Jump_Cannon" title="Annie Jump Cannon">Annie Jump Cannon</a>, who shared the experience of being deaf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sobel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sobel-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pickering assigned Leavitt to the study of variable stars of the <a href="/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Small Magellanic Cloud">Small</a> and <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">Large Magellanic Clouds</a>, as recorded on photographic plates taken with the Bruce Astrograph of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boyden_Station" title="Boyden Station">Boyden Station</a> of the Harvard Observatory in <a href="/wiki/Arequipa" title="Arequipa">Arequipa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. She identified 1777 variable stars.  In 1908 she published her results in the <i>Annals of the Astronomical Observatory of Harvard College</i>, noting that the brighter variables had the longer period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leavitt1908_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leavitt1908-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In another paper published in 1912, Leavitt looked carefully at the relation between the periods and the brightness of a sample of 25 of the Cepheids variables in the Small Magellanic Cloud.  This paper was communicated and signed by Edward Pickering, but the first sentence indicates that it was "prepared by Miss Leavitt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leavitt1912_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leavitt1912-12">[12]</a></sup> Leavitt made a graph of magnitude versus logarithm of period and determined that, in her own words,\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="767" data-file-width="725" decoding="async" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg/250px-HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg/375px-HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg/500px-HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HSLeavittHSCr13Fig2_1912.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plot from a paper prepared by Leavitt in 1912.  The horizontal axis is the logarithm of the period of the corresponding Cepheid, and the vertical axis is its <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">magnitude</a>.  The lines drawn connect points corresponding to the stars\' minimum and maximum brightness, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leavitt1912_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leavitt1912-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A straight line can be readily drawn among each of the two series of points corresponding to maxima and minima, thus showing that there is a simple relation between the brightness of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cepheid" title="Cepheid">Cepheid</a> variables and their periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leavitt1912_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leavitt1912-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>She then used the simplifying assumption that all of the Cepheids within the Small Magellanic Cloud were at approximately the same distance, so that their <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_brightness" title="Intrinsic brightness">intrinsic brightness</a> could be deduced from their apparent brightness as registered in the photographic plates, up to a scale factor since the distance to the Magellanic Clouds were as yet unknown. She expressed the hope that parallaxes to some Cepheids would be measured, which eventually happened thereby allowing her period-luminosity scale to be calibrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leavitt1912_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Leavitt1912-12">[12]</a></sup>  This reasoning allowed Leavitt to establish that the <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">period</a> is linearly related to the logarithm of the star\'s average intrinsic optical <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> (which is the amount of power radiated by the star in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leavitt also developed, and continued to refine, the Harvard Standard for photographic measurements, a logarithmic scale that orders stars by brightness over 17 magnitudes. She initially analyzed 299 plates from 13 telescopes to construct her scale, which was accepted by the International Committee of Photographic Magnitudes in 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leavitt was a member of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa_Society" title="Phi Beta Kappa Society">Phi Beta Kappa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_University_Women" title="American Association of University Women">American Association of University Women</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical and Astrophysical Society</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a>, and an honorary member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Variable_Star_Observers" title="American Association of Variable Star Observers">American Association of Variable Star Observers</a>.  In 1921, when <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a> took over as director of the observatory, Leavitt was made head of stellar <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometry</a>. By the end of that year she had succumbed to cancer and was buried in the Leavitt family plot at Cambridge Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>89</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illness_and_death">Illness and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Illness and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg/200px-Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg/300px-Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg/400px-Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt_marker.agr.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leavitt family monument in Cambridge Cemetery</div></div></div>\n<p>Leavitt\'s scientific work at Harvard was frequently interrupted by illness and family obligations.  Her early death, at the age of 53, was seen as a tragedy by her colleagues for reasons that went beyond her scientific achievements. Her colleague <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solon_I._Bailey" title="Solon I. Bailey">Solon I. Bailey</a> wrote in her obituary that "she had the happy faculty of appreciating all that was worthy and lovable in others, and was possessed of a nature so full of sunshine that, to her, all of life became beautiful and full of meaning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>28</span></sup>\n</p><p>"Sitting at the top of a gentle hill," writes George Johnson in his biography of Leavitt, "the spot is marked by a tall hexagonal monument, on top of which sits a globe cradled on a draped marble pedestal. Her uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erasmus_Darwin_Leavitt,_Jr." title="Erasmus Darwin Leavitt, Jr.">Erasmus Darwin Leavitt</a> and his family also are buried there, along with other Leavitts. ..." A plaque memorializing Henrietta and her two siblings, Mira and Roswell, is mounted on one side of the monument. Nearby are the graves of <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">William James</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>90</span></sup> There is no <a href="/wiki/Epitaph" title="Epitaph">epitaph</a> at the gravesite memorializing Henrietta Leavitt\'s achievements in astronomy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_impact">Scientific impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scientific impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to science writer <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Bernstein" title="Jeremy Bernstein">Jeremy Bernstein</a>, "variable stars had been of interest for years, but when she was studying those plates, I doubt Pickering thought she would make a significant discovery&#8212;one that would eventually change astronomy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  The period&#8211;luminosity relationship for Cepheids, now known as "Leavitt\'s law" made the stars the first "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_candle" title="Standard candle">standard candle</a>" in astronomy, allowing scientists to compute the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">distances</a> to galaxies too remote for <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">stellar parallax</a> observations to be useful. One year after Leavitt reported her results, <a href="/wiki/Ejnar_Hertzsprung" title="Ejnar Hertzsprung">Ejnar Hertzsprung</a> determined the distance of several Cepheids in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and that, with this calibration, the distance to any Cepheid could be accurately determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cepheids were soon detected in other galaxies, such as <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda</a> (notably by Edwin Hubble in 1923&#8211;24), and they became an important part of the evidence that "spiral nebulae" are independent galaxies located far outside of our own <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. Thus, Leavitt\'s discovery would forever change our picture of the universe, as it prompted <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a> to move our Sun from the center of the galaxy in the "<a href="/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy)" title="Great Debate (astronomy)">Great Debate</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> to move our galaxy from the center of the universe.\n</p><p>Leavitt\'s discovery of a way to accurately measure distances on an inter-galactic scale, paved the way for modern astronomy\'s understanding of the structure and scale of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carnegiescience_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-carnegiescience-4">[4]</a></sup>  The accomplishments of <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a>, the American astronomer who established that the universe is expanding, also were made possible by Leavitt\'s groundbreaking research. Hubble often said that Leavitt deserved the Nobel Prize for her work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Mathematician <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6sta_Mittag-Leffler" title="G&#246;sta Mittag-Leffler">G&#246;sta Mittag-Leffler</a>, a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Swedish Academy of Sciences">Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>, tried to nominate her for that prize in 1924, only to learn that she had died of cancer three years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singh-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>118</span></sup> (The Nobel Prize is not awarded posthumously.)\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_honors">Posthumous honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Posthumous honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5383_Leavitt" title="5383 Leavitt">5383 Leavitt</a> and the crater <a href="/wiki/Leavitt_(crater)" title="Leavitt (crater)">Leavitt</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> are named after her to honor deaf men and women who have worked as astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>\n<li>One of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASAS-SN" title="ASAS-SN">ASAS-SN</a> telescopes, located in the McDonald Observatory in Texas, is named in her honor.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books_and_plays">Books and plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Books and plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Lauren_Gunderson" title="Lauren Gunderson">Lauren Gunderson</a> wrote a play, <i>Silent Sky</i>, which followed Leavitt\'s journey from her acceptance at Harvard to her death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/George_Johnson_(writer)" title="George Johnson (writer)">George Johnson</a> wrote a biography, <i>Miss Leavitt\'s Stars</i>, which showcases the triumphs of women\'s progress in science through the story of Leavitt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a wrote the biography <i>Look Up!: Henrietta Leavitt, Pioneering Woman Astronomer</i> for a younger audience. It is written for four to eight year olds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_female_scientists" title="List of female scientists">List of female scientists</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_deaf_people" title="List of deaf people">List of deaf people</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Women_in_science" title="Women in science">Women in science</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_women_in_science" title="Timeline of women in science">Timeline of women in science</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Human_computer" title="Human computer">Human computer</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-CSMonitor-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-CSMonitor_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation news">Gregory M.
