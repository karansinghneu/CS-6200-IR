Part of a larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantheon_(gods)" title="Pantheon (gods)">pantheon</a>, he was the god of the daylit <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a>, and his position may have mirrored the position of the <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_society" title="Proto-Indo-European society">Proto-Indo-European society</a>.\n</p><p>This deity is not directly attested; rather, scholars have reconstructed this deity from the languages and cultures of later Indo-European peoples such as the Greeks, Latins, and Indo-Aryans. According to this scholarly reconstruction, Dyeus was known as <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space:nowrap;" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Dy&#7703;us Ph<sub>2</sub>t&#7703;r</span>, literally "<a href="/wiki/Sky_father" title="Sky father">sky father</a>" or "shining father", as reflected in Latin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_(god)" title="Jupiter (god)">I&#363;piter</a>, <i>Di&#275;spiter</i>, possibly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dis_Pater" title="Dis Pater">Dis Pater</a> and <i>deus pater</i>, Greek <i>Zeu Pater</i>, Vedic <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyaus_(deity)" title="Dyaus (deity)"><i lang="sa-Latn" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration">Dy&#225;u&#7779; Pit&#7771;&#769;</i></a></i>. As the pantheons of the individual mythologies related to Proto-Indo-European religion evolved, attributes of Dyeus seem to have been redistributed to other deities. In Greek and Roman mythology, Dyeus remained the chief god; however, in <a href="/wiki/Vedic_mythology" title="Vedic mythology">Vedic mythology</a>, the etymological continuant of Dyeus became a very abstract god, and his original attributes and dominance over other gods appear to have been transferred to gods such as <a href="/wiki/Agni" title="Agni">Agni</a> or <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Indra</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a>.\n</p><p>Although some of the more iconic <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_reconstruction#Reflex" title="Linguistic reconstruction">reflexes</a> of Dyeus are storm deities, such as <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>, this is thought to be a late development exclusive to Mediterranean traditions, probably derived from syncretism with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaanite_religion" title="Canaanite religion">Canaanite</a> deities and <a href="/wiki/Perkwunos" title="Perkwunos">Perkwunos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The deity\'s original domain was over the daylight sky, and indeed reflexes emphasise this connection to light: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Istanu" title="Istanu">Istanu</a> (<i>Tiyaz</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Solar_deity" title="Solar deity">solar deity</a> (though this name may actually refer to a female sun goddess<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Helios" title="Helios">Helios</a> is often referred to as the "eye of Zeus",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> in Romanian paganism the Sun is similarly called "God\'s eye"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and in Indo-Iranian tradition <a href="/wiki/Surya" title="Surya">Surya</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hvare-khshaeta" title="Hvare-khshaeta">Hvare-khshaeta</a> is similarly associated with <a href="/wiki/Ahura_Mazda" title="Ahura Mazda">Ahura Mazda</a>. Even in Roman tradition, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> often is only associated with diurnal lightning at most, while <a href="/wiki/Summanus" title="Summanus">Summanus</a> is a deity responsible for nocturnal lightning or storms as a whole.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_ordinary_noun">As an ordinary noun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dyeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As an ordinary noun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section  <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyeus&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>.
