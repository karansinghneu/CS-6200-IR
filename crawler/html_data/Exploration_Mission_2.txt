2022 mission of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a> and planned to be the first crewed mission of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-launch2022_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-launch2022-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Originally, the crewed mission was intended to collect samples from a captured asteroid in <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">lunar orbit</a> by the now cancelled robotic <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_Redirect_Mission" title="Asteroid Redirect Mission">Asteroid Redirect Mission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-claw_2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-claw_2015-6">[6]</a></sup> The plan is for a crewed Orion spacecraft to perform a lunar flyby test and return to Earth. As of 2019 the last crewed spacecraft to leave low Earth orbit was <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a> in 1972.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" not exist)">Human mission to an asteroid</a></div>\n<p>Until 2017, EM-2 was a projected single-launch mission of a <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a> Block IB with an <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Upper_Stage" title="Exploration Upper Stage">Exploration Upper Stage</a>, lunar Block 1 Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle (MPCV), and a payload insertion of 50.7&#160;t. The plan was to rendezvous with an asteroid previously placed in lunar orbit by the robotic <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_Redirect_Mission" title="Asteroid Redirect Mission">Asteroid Redirect Mission</a> and have astronauts perform space-walks and gather samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wall-2013_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wall-2013-7">[7]</a></sup>  After the cancellation of the Asteroid Redirect Mission, it was proposed in 2017 to fly an eight-day mission with a crew of four astronauts, sent on a free return trajectory around the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Another proposal suggested in 2017 was to take four astronauts aboard Orion on an 8-to-21-day trip around the Moon to deliver the first element of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbital_Platform-Gateway" title="Lunar Orbital Platform-Gateway">Lunar Orbital Platform-Gateway</a> (LOP-G).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf2017-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a 32)</div></div></div>\n<p>In March 2018 it was decided to launch the first Lunar Gateway module on a commercial launch vehicle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> because of delays in building the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_launch_platform" title="Mobile launch platform">mobile launch platform</a> needed to hold the more powerful \'<a href="/wiki/Exploration_Upper_Stage" title="Exploration Upper Stage">Exploration Upper Stage</a>\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As of  2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exploration_Mission-2&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the EM-2 mission plan is to send four astronauts in the first crewed <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> capsule into a lunar flyby for a maximum of 21 days. The mission profile is a multi-translunar injection (MTLI), or multiple departure burns, and includes a free return trajectory from the Moon. Basically, the spacecraft will orbit Earth twice while periodically firing its engines to build up enough velocity to push it toward the Moon before looping back to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_missions">Similar missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration_Mission-2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Similar missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1968, the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> mission, crewed by 3 astronauts, was designed to test-fly a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">Command/Service Module</a> beyond low Earth orbit. Although similar to EM-2 in that it was crewed and did not land on the Moon, it differed by entering lunar orbit for an extended stay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2005, the company <a href="/wiki/Space_Adventures" title="Space Adventures">Space Adventures</a> announced plans to <a href="/wiki/Tourism_on_the_Moon" title="Tourism on the Moon">take two tourists</a> within 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) of the lunar surface using a <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz spacecraft</a> piloted by a professional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a>. The mission, named <a href="/wiki/DSE-Alpha" title="DSE-Alpha">DSE-Alpha</a>, has been priced at <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$150 million</span> per seat and is expected to last 8&#8211;9 days when scheduled. Company CEO Eric Anderson stated in 2011 that one seat had been sold, but the launch date has continually slipped since the second seat remains unsold as of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exploration_Mission-2&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popmec20110426_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-popmec20110426-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceX_lunar_tourism_mission" title="SpaceX lunar tourism mission">SpaceX lunar tourism mission</a> was initially proposed for late 2018 and would have been similar to EM-2 in crew size, with two space tourists paying for a free-return loop around the Moon and back to Earth, using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crew_Dragon" title="Crew Dragon">Crew Dragon</a> capsule and launched on the <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heavy" title="Falcon Heavy">Falcon Heavy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> After the first flight of Falcon Heavy in 2018, SpaceX announced that Falcon Heavy would not be used for crewed flights to focus their future development on <a href="/wiki/BFR_(rocket)" title="BFR (rocket)">BFR</a> and indicated that the lunar mission would more likely be carried out with the BFR system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On September 14, 2018, SpaceX officially announced that it had signed one of the paying passengers, <a href="/wiki/Yusaku_Maezawa" title="Yusaku Maezawa">Yusaku Maezawa</a>, for the mission using the BFR, now rescheduled for 2023, and that he would invite 6 to 8 artists to join him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teslarati_moonflight_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-teslarati_moonflight-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grush_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-grush-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
