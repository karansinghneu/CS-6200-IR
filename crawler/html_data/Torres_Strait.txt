class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="/&#594;r/: \'or\' in \'moral\'">&#594;r</span><span title="/&#618;/: \'i\' in \'kit\'">&#618;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">strait</a> which lies between <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesian</a> island of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. It is approximately 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) wide at its narrowest extent. To the south is <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a>, the northernmost extremity of the Australian mainland. To the north is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Province,_Papua_New_Guinea" title="Western Province, Papua New Guinea">Western Province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>. It is named after navigator <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres" title="Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres">Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres</a>, who passed through the Strait in 1606.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torres_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The strait links the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a> to the east with the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a> in the west. Although it is an important international sea lane, it is very shallow (7 to 15&#160;m water depth),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the maze of reefs and islands can make it hazardous to navigate. In the south the <a href="/wiki/Endeavour_Strait" title="Endeavour Strait">Endeavour Strait</a> is located between <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Island_(Queensland)" title="Prince of Wales Island (Queensland)">Prince of Wales Island</a> (Muralug) and the mainland.  Shipping enters Torres Strait via the <a href="/wiki/Adolphus_Channel" title="Adolphus Channel">Adolphus Channel</a> which joins to the Great Barrier Reef lagoon to the southeast.  Strong tidal currents occur in the narrow channels between islands and reefs, and large submarine sand dunes migrate across the seafloor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Some 580 coral reefs, including the Warrior Reefs and Eastern Patch Reefs, cover a total area of 2,400&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (930&#160;sq&#160;mi) in the region, as well as some of the most extensive seagrass beds in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nSeveral clusters of islands lie in the Strait, collectively called the <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait_Islands" title="Torres Strait Islands">Torres Strait Islands</a>. There are at least 274 of these islands, of which 17 have present-day permanent settlements. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg/220px-Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg/330px-Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg/440px-Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Torres_Strait_Islands_air_photo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Torres Strait Islands air photo</div></div></div><p> Over 6,800 Torres Strait Islanders live on the Islands and 42,000 live on the mainland.\n</p><p>These islands have a variety of <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and formation history. Several of those closest to the New Guinea coastline are low-lying, formed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alluvial" title="Alluvial">alluvial</a> sedimentary deposits borne by the outflow of the local rivers into the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many of the western islands are hilly and steep, formed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, and are peaks of the northernmost extension of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dividing_Range" title="Great Dividing Range">Great Dividing Range</a> now turned into islands when sea levels rose at the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The central islands are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> <a href="/wiki/Cay" title="Cay">cays</a>, and those of the east are of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> origins. The islands are considered Australian territory and are administered from <a href="/wiki/Thursday_Island" title="Thursday Island">Thursday Island</a>. There are several major policy and institutional frameworks in the Torres Strait region that support the sustainable use and management of marine resources while also protecting habitats, biodiversity and the traditional islander way of life. Most important of these is the Torres Strait Treaty entered into by Australia and Papua New Guinea in February 1985. The Treaty defines sovereignty and maritime boundaries in the area between the two countries. It guides decision makers on protecting the way of life and livelihood of traditional inhabitants, on managing the protection of habitats, and on sharing the commercial and traditional fisheries resources. The Treaty established a Torres Strait Protected Zone within which both nations manage access to fisheries resources.  Each country exercises sovereign jurisdiction for resources on either side of the agreed jurisdiction lines.\n</p><p>The islands\' <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous</a> inhabitants are the <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait_Islanders" title="Torres Strait Islanders">Torres Strait Islanders</a>, who are distinct from both the Papuans of adjoining New Guinea and from Aboriginal groups on the nearby Australian mainland but related to both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The various Torres Strait Islander communities have a unique culture and long-standing history with the islands and nearby coastlines. Their maritime-based trade and interactions with the Papuans to the north and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" title="Australian Aborigines">Australian Aboriginal</a> communities have maintained a steady <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cultural_diffusion" title="Cultural diffusion">cultural diffusion</a> between the three societal groups, dating back thousands of years at least.\n</p><p>Two indigenous languages are spoken on the Torres Strait Islands: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kala_Lagaw_Ya" title="Kala Lagaw Ya">Kala Lagaw Ya</a>/Kalaw Kawaw Ya/Kawalgau Ya/Muwalgau Ya/Kulkalgau Ya, and Miriam Mir, as well as Brokan [Broken], otherwise called <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait_Creole" title="Torres Strait Creole">Torres Strait Creole</a>. In the 2001 Australian national census, the population of the islands was recorded as 8,089, though many more live outside of Torres Strait in Australia.\n</p><p>Environmental issues facing the region include the risk of mining waste from the <a href="/wiki/Fly_River" title="Fly River">Fly River</a> in southern Papua New Guinea, the impacts of global climate change and the sustainable management of natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torres_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The islands of the Torres Strait have been inhabited for at least 2,500 years and possibly much longer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_TSRA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_TSRA-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first recorded European navigation of the strait was by <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres" title="Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres">Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres</a>, a pilot who was second-in-command on the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> expedition led by navigator <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s">Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</a> who sailed from <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">South Pacific</a> in 1605. After Queir&#243;s\'s ship returned to <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, Torres resumed the intended voyage to <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Maluku Islands</a>. He sailed along the south coast of New Guinea, and may also have sighted the northernmost extremity of the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> mainland, however no specific records exist that indicate he did so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1769 the <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dalrymple" title="Alexander Dalrymple">Alexander Dalrymple</a>, whilst translating some Spanish documents captured in the Philippines in 1762, had found Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres\' testimony proving a passage south of New Guinea now known as Torres Strait. This discovery led Dalrymple to publish the <i>Historical Collection of the Several Voyages and Discoveries in the South Pacific Ocean</i> in 1770&#8211;1771, which aroused widespread interest in his claim of the existence of an unknown continent. It was Dalrymple who named the strait after Torres. Dalrymple was bitterly disappointed that it was <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> and not he who was appointed commander of the expedition that eventually led in 1770 to the British encounter and charting of the eastern coastline of Australia.\n</p><p>In 1770 Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> rounded Cape York, turned south-west and landed on <a href="/wiki/Possession_Island_(Queensland)" title="Possession Island (Queensland)">Possession Island</a>. From the top of a hill, he signalled down to the ship that he could see a navigable passage through the dangerous Strait. Later in <a href="/wiki/Batavia,_Dutch_East_Indies" title="Batavia, Dutch East Indies">Batavia</a>, where he learnt that the French had preceded him across the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a>, Cook re-wrote this signalling drill as a possession ceremony <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>, saying he had claimed Australia\'s east coast for the British Crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1823 Captain John Lihou, Master of the 550-ton merchant ship <i>Zenobia</i> of Calcutta, was on passage from <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> to <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and chose a route through Torres Strait. Remarkably, this would become the first time for a ship to be navigated through the Torres Strait from west to east. According to the <i>Sydney Gazette</i> of April 1823: \'This essay of nautical skill was accomplished after the loss of four anchors and the rudder.\' It was also the first occasion a ship was navigated through the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a> from Torres Strait, south-eastward to the southward of <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>. Lihou saw Sir James Saumarez\' Shoal (now <a href="/wiki/Saumarez_Reefs" title="Saumarez Reefs">Saumarez Reefs</a>) on 27 February and named the reef system after Vice-Admiral <a href="/wiki/James_Saumarez,_1st_Baron_de_Saumarez" title="James Saumarez, 1st Baron de Saumarez">James Saumarez</a>. On this same trip, Lihou discovered the <a href="/wiki/Lihou_Reef_National_Nature_Reserve" title="Lihou Reef National Nature Reserve">Lihou Reef and Cays</a> and Port Lihou (originally named Port Yarborough, on the southern shore of <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Island_(Queensland)" title="Prince of Wales Island (Queensland)">Prince of Wales Island</a>). Please note that Lihou\'s ship should not be confused with the 385-ton <a href="/wiki/HMS_Zenobia" title="HMS Zenobia">HMS Zenobia</a>, built at Kings Lynn in 1807.  \n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/London_Missionary_Society" title="London Missionary Society">London Missionary Society</a> arrived on Erub (Darnley Island) in 1871.  Although some of the Torres Strait islands lie just off the coast of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, they were annexed in 1879 by <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, then a British colony. There was an important <a href="/wiki/Pearl_hunting" title="Pearl hunting">pearling</a> industry from the 1860s until about 1970 when it collapsed in the face of competition from the plastics industry. Pearl-shelling was responsible for the arrival of experienced divers from many countries, notably <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1978 an agreement between Australia and <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> determined the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_boundary" title="Maritime boundary">maritime border</a> in the Torres Strait.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Torres Strait is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Twenty_Thousand_Leagues_Under_the_Sea" title="Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea">Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> as a dangerous strait where the submarine, the <a href="/wiki/Nautilus_(Verne)" title="Nautilus (Verne)"><i>Nautilus</i></a>, is briefly stranded.\n</p><p>The people of the Torres Strait have a unique indigenous culture which has drawn the interest of a range of anthropological, historical, archaeological and folklorist researchers (both professional and amateur). This includes an expedition from Cambridge University led by the early ethnographer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Haddon" title="Alfred Haddon">Alfred Haddon</a> in 1898 and the more contemporary regional work of Australian anthropologist Jeremy Beckett. Accounts of local Indigenous narrative traditions can be found in the work of Nonie Sharp and <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Lawrie" title="Margaret Lawrie">Margaret Lawrie</a>.\n</p><p>Due to proximity to the <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> mainland, the northern Torres Strait islands experience occasional <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seeker" title="Asylum seeker">asylum seeker</a> arrivals from across the Strait. A total of ten asylum seekers from Papua New Guinea were detected in each of 2012 and 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016 the Australian Federal Police were tipped off by residents of Prince of Wales Island that men from Sydney were attempting to buy a boat to reach Papua New Guinea in order to leave Australia without a passport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipping_routes">Shipping routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torres_Strait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shipping routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="772" data-file-width="1082" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png/220px-Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png/330px-Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png/440px-Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Torres_Strait_Chart_%26_Passages.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Navigation Routes through Torres Strait</div></div></div>\n<p>The two routes through the Strait are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Endeavour_Strait" title="Endeavour Strait">Endeavour Strait</a> (purple line on chart) &#8211; for small vessels.</li>\n<li>Prince of Wales Channel: Larger ships transiting Torres Strait enter the Prince of Wales Channel from the West just north of <a href="/wiki/Booby_Island_(Queensland)" title="Booby Island (Queensland)">Booby Island</a> by way of the Gannet or Varzin Passages. The minimum depths for deep draught shipping in the Great Barrier Reef pilotage area are found here  (10.3m &#8211; Nov 2011). Shipping with a 12.2m static draught or less are permitted to transit the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n<ul><li>Channels to the East of Prince of Wales Channel\n<ul><li>Great North East Channel: East of Prince of Wales Channel at Wednesday Island the Route becomes The Great North East Channel (green line on chart). The Great North East Channel (GNEC) links the Prince of Wales Channel to the northernmost entrance to the Great Barrier Reef, 120 NM away at Bligh Entrance. The GNEC route runs North or South of Alert Patches and East to under Twin Island then Northeast  to Dalrymple Island (the end of Pilotage requirement) then on to Bramble Quay, Bligh Entrance and the open sea. This passage routes shipping to the Coral Sea, the Pacific Ocean and the outside of the Great Barrier Reef. Another channel (brown line on chart) runs down towards Cape York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Inner Route Pilotage Area: The Inner Route Pilotage Area runs from near Cape York to near Cairns. This channel, named the \'Inner Route\' runs between the Australian mainland and the Great Barrier Reef <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p><br/>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
