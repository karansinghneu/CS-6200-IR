In the case of a data path, these effects can range from an increase in error rate to a total loss of the data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Both man-made and natural sources generate changing electrical currents and voltages that can cause EMI: <a href="/wiki/Ignition_system" title="Ignition system">ignition systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">cellular network</a> of mobile phones, <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">auroras</a> (Northern/Southern Lights). EMI frequently  affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AM_radio" title="AM radio">AM radios</a>. It can also affect <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FM_radio" title="FM radio">FM radios</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">televisions</a>, as well as observations for <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>.\n</p><p>EMI can be used intentionally for <a href="/wiki/Radio_jamming" title="Radio jamming">radio jamming</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_warfare" title="Electronic warfare">electronic warfare</a>.  \n</p>\n<table class="mbox-small noprint" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><img alt="" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="160" decoding="async" height="50" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/50px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/75px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg/100px-Gnome-mime-sound-openclipart.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></div></div></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist" style="line-height:1.1em"><div class="haudio">\n<div style="padding:4px 0"><a href="/wiki/File:Buzzing_Speakers.ogg" title="File:Buzzing Speakers.ogg">EMI sound sample 1</a></div>\n<div><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="17.29306122449" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Buzzing_Speakers.ogg" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_0" preload="none" style="width:220px"><source data-bandwidth="104496" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-title="Original Ogg file (104 kbps)" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/Buzzing_Speakers.ogg" type=\'audio/ogg; class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since the earliest days of radio communications, the negative effects of interference from both intentional and unintentional transmissions have been felt and the need to manage the radio frequency spectrum became apparent.\n</p><p>In 1933, a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> (IEC) in Paris recommended the International Special Committee on Radio Interference (<a href="/wiki/CISPR" title="CISPR">CISPR</a>) be set up to deal with the emerging problem of EMI. CISPR subsequently produced technical publications covering measurement and test techniques and recommended emission and immunity limits.  These have evolved over the decades and form the basis of much of the world\'s <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_compatibility" title="Electromagnetic compatibility">EMC</a> regulations today.\n</p><p>In 1979, legal limits were imposed on electromagnetic emissions from all digital equipment by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a> in the USA in response to the increased number of digital systems that were interfering with wired and radio communications. Test methods and limits were based on CISPR publications, although similar limits were already enforced in parts of Europe.\n</p><p>In the mid 1980s, the European Union member states adopted a number of "new approach" directives with the intention of standardizing technical requirements for products so that they do not become a barrier to trade within the EC.  One of these was the EMC Directive (89/336/EC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and it applies to all equipment placed on the market or taken into service. Its scope covers all apparatus "liable to cause electromagnetic disturbance or the performance of which is liable to be affected by such disturbance".\n</p><p>This was the first time there was a legal requirement on immunity, as well as emissions on apparatus intended for the general population.  And although there may be additional costs involved for some products to give them a known level of immunity, it increases their perceived quality as they are able to co-exist with apparatus in the active EM environment of modern times and with fewer problems.\n</p><p>Many countries now have similar requirements for  products to meet some level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_Compatibility" title="Electromagnetic Compatibility">Electromagnetic Compatibility</a> (EMC) regulation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Electromagnetic interference can be categorized as follows:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Narrowband" title="Narrowband">Narrowband</a> EMI or RFI interference typically emanates from intended transmissions, such as <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">radio and TV stations</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phones</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">Broadband</a> EMI or RFI interference is unintentional radiation from sources such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric power transmission</a> lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Conducted electromagnetic interference is caused by the physical contact of the conductors as opposed to radiated EMI, which is caused by induction (without physical contact of the conductors). Electromagnetic disturbances in the EM field of a conductor will no longer be confined to the surface of the conductor and will radiate away from it. This persists in all conductors and mutual inductance between two radiated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_fields" title="Electromagnetic fields">electromagnetic fields</a> will result in EMI.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ITU_definition">ITU definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: ITU definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>Interference</b> with the meaning of <i>electromagnetic interference</i>, also <i>radio-frequency interference</i> (short: <i>EMI</i> | <i>RFI</i>) is  &#8211; according to <i>Article 1.166</i> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s (ITU) <a href="/wiki/ITU_Radio_Regulations" title="ITU Radio Regulations">Radio Regulations</a> (RR)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> &#8211; defined as &#171;<i>The effect of unwanted energy due to one or a combination of <a href="/wiki/Emission_(radiocommunications)" title="Emission (radiocommunications)">emissions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">radiations</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">inductions</a> upon reception in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocommunication" title="Radiocommunication">radiocommunication</a> system, manifested by any performance degradation, misinterpretation, or loss of information which could be extracted in the absence of such unwanted energy</i>&#187;.\n</p><p>This is also a definition used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_administration" title="Frequency administration">frequency administration</a> to provide <a href="/wiki/Frequency_assignment" title="Frequency assignment">frequency assignments</a> and assignment of frequency channels to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio stations</a> or systems, as well as to analyze <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_compatibility" title="Electromagnetic compatibility">electromagnetic compatibility</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocommunication_service" title="Radiocommunication service">radiocommunication services</a>.\n</p><p>In accordance with ITU RR (article 1) variations of interference are classified as follows:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Permissible interference</li>\n<li>Acceptable interference</li>\n<li>Harmful interference</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conducted_interference">Conducted interference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conducted interference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Conducted EMI is caused by the physical contact of the conductors as opposed to radiated EMI which is caused by <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">induction</a> (without physical contact of the conductors).\n</p><p>For lower frequencies, EMI is caused by conduction and, for higher frequencies, by radiation.\n</p><p>EMI through the ground wire is also very common in an electrical facility.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Susceptibilities_of_different_radio_technologies">Susceptibilities of different radio technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Susceptibilities of different radio technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Interference tends to be more troublesome with older radio technologies such as analogue <a href="/wiki/Amplitude_modulation" title="Amplitude modulation">amplitude modulation</a>, which have no way of distinguishing unwanted in-band signals from the intended signal, and the omnidirectional antennas used with broadcast systems.\nNewer radio systems incorporate several improvements that enhance the <a href="/wiki/Selectivity_(electronic)" title="Selectivity (electronic)">selectivity</a>.\nIn digital radio systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Error-correction" title="Error-correction">error-correction</a> techniques can be used. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spread-spectrum" title="Spread-spectrum">Spread-spectrum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_hopping" title="Frequency hopping">frequency-hopping</a> techniques can be used with both analogue and digital signalling to improve resistance to interference.\nA highly <a href="/wiki/Directivity" title="Directivity">directional</a> receiver, such as a <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antenna</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Antenna_diversity" title="Antenna diversity">diversity receiver</a>, can be used to select one signal in space to the exclusion of others.\n</p><p>The most extreme example of digital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spread-spectrum" title="Spread-spectrum">spread-spectrum</a> signalling to date is ultra-wideband (<a href="/wiki/Ultra-wideband" title="Ultra-wideband">UWB</a>), which proposes the use of large sections of the radio spectrum at low amplitudes to transmit high-bandwidth digital data. UWB, if used exclusively, would enable very efficient use of the spectrum, but users of non-UWB technology are not yet prepared to share the spectrum with the new system because of the interference it would cause to their receivers (the regulatory implications of UWB are discussed in the <a href="/wiki/Ultra-wideband" title="Ultra-wideband">ultra-wideband</a> article).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interference_to_consumer_devices">Interference to consumer devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interference to consumer devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the 1982 Public Law 97-259 allowed the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) to regulate the susceptibility of consumer electronic equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Potential sources of RFI and EMI include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> various types of <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a>, doorbell transformers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toaster_oven" title="Toaster oven">toaster ovens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_blanket" title="Electric blanket">electric blankets</a>, ultrasonic pest control devices, electric <a href="/wiki/Bug_zapper" title="Bug zapper">bug zappers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heating_pad" title="Heating pad">heating pads</a>, and touch controlled lamps. Multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT</a> computer monitors or televisions sitting too close to one another can sometimes cause a "shimmy" effect in each other, due to the electromagnetic nature of their picture tubes, especially when one of their <a href="/wiki/Degaussing" title="Degaussing">de-gaussing</a> coils is activated.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interference_at_2.4_GHz" title="Electromagnetic interference at 2.4 GHz">Electromagnetic interference at 2.4 GHz</a> can be caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11b" title="802.11b">802.11b</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/802.11g" title="802.11g">802.11g</a> wireless devices, <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> devices, <a href="/wiki/Baby_monitor" title="Baby monitor">baby monitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cordless_telephone" title="Cordless telephone">cordless telephones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_sender" title="Video sender">video senders</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave ovens</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Switch" title="Switch">Switching</a> loads (<a href="/wiki/Inductance" title="Inductance">inductive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance_and_conductance" title="Electrical resistance and conductance">resistive</a>), such as electric motors, transformers, heaters, lamps, ballast, power supplies, etc., all cause electromagnetic interference especially at currents above 2 <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">A</a>. The usual method used for suppressing EMI is by connecting a <a href="/wiki/Snubber" title="Snubber">snubber</a> network, a resistor in series with a <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitor</a>, across a pair of contacts. While this may offer modest EMI reduction at very low currents, snubbers do not work at currents over 2 A with <a href="/wiki/Electromechanics" title="Electromechanics">electromechanical</a> contacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another method for suppressing EMI is the use of ferrite core noise suppressors, which are inexpensive and which clip on to the power lead of the offending device or the compromised device.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Switched-mode_power_supply" title="Switched-mode power supply">Switched-mode power supplies</a> can be a source of EMI, but have become less of a problem as design techniques have improved, such as integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_factor_correction" title="Power factor correction">power factor correction</a>.\n</p><p>Most countries have legal requirements that mandate <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_compatibility" title="Electromagnetic compatibility">electromagnetic compatibility</a>: electronic and electrical hardware must still work correctly when subjected to certain amounts of EMI, and should not emit EMI, which could interfere with other equipment (such as radios).\n</p><p>Radio frequency signal quality has declined throughout the 21st century by roughly one decibel per year as the spectrum becomes increasingly crowded.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs additional references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">additional citation(s) needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This has inflicted a <a href="/wiki/Red_Queen%27s_race" title="Red Queen\'s race">Red Queen\'s race</a> on the mobile phone industry as companies have been forced to put up more cellular towers (at new frequencies) that then cause more interference thereby requiring more investment by the providers and frequent upgrades of mobile phones to match.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards">Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The  International Special Committee for Radio Interference or CISPR (French acronym for "Comit&#233; International Sp&#233;cial des Perturbations Radio&#233;lectriques"), which is a committee of the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) sets international standards for radiated and conducted electromagnetic interference. These are civilian standards for domestic, commercial, industrial and automotive sectors. These standards form the basis of other national or regional standards, most notably the European Norms (EN) written by CENELEC (European committee for electrotechnical standardisation). US organizations include the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), the American National Standards Institute (ANSI), and the US Military (MILSTD).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EMI_in_integrated_circuits">EMI in integrated circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: EMI in integrated circuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_compatibility" title="Electromagnetic compatibility">Electromagnetic compatibility</a></div>\n<p>Integrated circuits are often a source of EMI, but they must usually couple their energy to larger objects such as heatsinks, circuit board planes and cables to radiate significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, important means of reducing EMI are: the use of bypass or <a href="/wiki/Decoupling_capacitor" title="Decoupling capacitor">decoupling capacitors</a> on each active device (connected across the power supply, as close to the device as possible), <a href="/wiki/Rise_time" title="Rise time">rise time</a> control of high-speed signals using series resistors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IC_power_supply_pin" title="IC power supply pin">IC power supply pin</a> filtering. Shielding is usually a last resort after other techniques have failed, because of the added expense of shielding components such as conductive gaskets.\n</p><p>The efficiency of the radiation depends on the height above the <a href="/wiki/Ground_plane" title="Ground plane">ground plane</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plane" title="Power plane">power plane</a> (at <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">RF</a>, one is as good as the other) and the length of the conductor in relation to the wavelength of the signal component (<a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">fundamental frequency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">harmonic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transient_(oscillation)" title="Transient (oscillation)">transient</a> such as overshoot, undershoot or ringing). At lower frequencies, such as 133&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHz" title="MHz">MHz</a>, radiation is almost exclusively via I/O cables; RF noise gets onto the power planes and is coupled to the line drivers via the VCC and GND pins. The RF is then coupled to the cable through the line driver as <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_interference" title="Common-mode interference">common-mode noise</a>. Since the noise is common-mode, shielding has very little effect, even with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_pair" title="Differential pair">differential pairs</a>. The RF energy is <a href="/wiki/Capacitive_coupling" title="Capacitive coupling">capacitively coupled</a> from the signal pair to the shield and the shield itself does the radiating. One cure for this is to use a <a href="/wiki/Braid-breaker" title="Braid-breaker">braid-breaker</a> or <a href="/wiki/Choke_(electronics)" title="Choke (electronics)">choke</a> to reduce the common-mode signal.\n</p><p>At higher frequencies, usually above 500&#160;MHz, traces get electrically longer and higher above the plane. Two techniques are used at these frequencies: wave shaping with series resistors and embedding the traces between the two planes. If all these measures still leave too much EMI, shielding such as RF gaskets and copper tape can be used. Most digital equipment is designed with metal or conductive-coated plastic cases.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RF_immunity_and_testing">RF immunity and testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: RF immunity and testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Any unshielded semiconductor (e.g. an integrated circuit) will tend to act as a detector for those radio signals commonly found in the domestic environment (e.g. mobile phones).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Such a detector can demodulate the high frequency mobile phone carrier (e.g., GSM850 and GSM1900, GSM900 and GSM1800) and produce low-frequency (e.g., 217&#160;Hz) demodulated signals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfdesign_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rfdesign-17">[17]</a></sup>  This demodulation manifests itself as unwanted audible buzz in audio appliances such as <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> amplifier, <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">speaker</a> amplifier, car radio, telephones etc. Adding onboard EMI filters or special layout techniques can help in bypassing EMI or improving RF immunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\nSome ICs are designed (e.g., LMV831-LMV834,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> MAX9724<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) to have integrated RF filters or a special design that helps reduce any demodulation of high-frequency carrier.\n</p><p>Designers often need to carry out special tests for RF immunity of parts to be used in a system. These tests are often done in an <a href="/wiki/Anechoic_chamber" title="Anechoic chamber">anechoic chamber</a> with a controlled RF environment where the test vectors produce a RF field similar to that produced in an actual environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rfdesign_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-rfdesign-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RFI_in_radio_astronomy">RFI in radio astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: RFI in radio astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Interference in <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>, where it is commonly referred to as radio-frequency interference (RFI), is any source of transmission that is within the observed frequency band other than the celestial sources themselves. Because transmitters on and around the Earth can be many times stronger than the astronomical signal of interest, RFI is a major concern for performing radio astronomy. Natural sources of interference, such as lightning and the Sun, are also often referred to as RFI.\n</p><p>Some of the frequency bands that are very important for radio astronomy, such as the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_line" title="Hydrogen line">21-cm HI line</a> at 1420&#160;MHz, are protected by regulation. This is called <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_management" title="Spectrum management">spectrum management</a>. However, modern radio-astronomical observatories such as <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Array" title="Very Large Array">VLA</a>, <a href="/wiki/LOFAR" title="LOFAR">LOFAR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">ALMA</a> have a very large bandwidth over which they can observe. Because of the limited spectral space at radio frequencies, these frequency bands cannot be completely allocated to radio astronomy. Therefore, observatories need to deal with RFI in their observations.\n</p><p>Techniques to deal with RFI range from filters in hardware to advanced algorithms in software. One way to deal with strong transmitters is to filter out the frequency of the source completely. This is for example the case for the LOFAR observatory, which filters out the FM radio stations between 90-110&#160;MHz. It is important to remove such strong sources of interference as soon as possible, because they might "saturate" the highly sensitive receivers (<a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">analog-to-digital converters</a>), which means that the received signal is stronger than the receiver can handle. However, filtering out a frequency band implies that these frequencies can never be observed with the instrument.\n</p><p>A common technique to deal with RFI within the observed frequency bandwidth, is to employ RFI detection in software. Such software can find samples in time, frequency or time-frequency space that are contaminated by an interfering source. These samples are subsequently ignored in further analysis of the observed data. This process is often referred to as <i>data flagging</i>. Because most transmitters have a small bandwidth and are not continuously present such as lightning or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizens%27_Band_radio" title="Citizens\' Band radio">citizens\' band</a> (CB) radio devices, most of the data remains available for the astronomical analysis. However, data flagging can not solve issues with continuous broad-band transmitters, such as windmills, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Video_Broadcast" title="Digital Video Broadcast">digital video</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_Audio_Broadcast" title="Digital Audio Broadcast">digital audio</a> transmitters.\n</p><p>Another way to manage RFI is to establish a <a href="/wiki/Radio_quiet_zone" title="Radio quiet zone">radio quiet zone</a> (RQZ). RQZ is a well-defined area surrounding receivers that has special regulations to reduce RFI in favor of radio astronomy observations within the zone. The regulations may include special management of spectrum and power flux or power flux-density limitations. The controls within the zone may cover elements other than radio transmitters or radio devices. These include aircraft controls and control of unintentional radiators such as industrial, scientific and medical devices, vehicles, and power lines. The first RQZ for radio astronomy is <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Radio_Quiet_Zone" title="United States National Radio Quiet Zone">United States National Radio Quiet Zone</a> (NRQZ), established in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">Electromagnetic radiation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Faraday_cage" title="Faraday cage">Faraday cage</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Interference_(communication)" title="Interference (communication)">Interference (communication)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Power_integrity" title="Power integrity">Power integrity</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">Radio receiver</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Signal_integrity" title="Signal integrity">Signal integrity</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signal_noise" title="Signal noise">Signal noise</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Twisted_pair" title="Twisted pair">Twisted pair</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_interference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
