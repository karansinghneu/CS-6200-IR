Despite his prominent role, his identity was kept a secret from the public for most of his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bond_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bond-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blair_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blair-2">[2]</a></sup> He was one of the lead architects behind the string of Soviet successes that stunned the world from the late 1950s &#8211; from the launch of the first <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> in 1957, and the first <a class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2011</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Kerim Kerimov was born on November 14, 1917 in a family of an engineer-technologist in <a href="/wiki/Baku" title="Baku">Baku</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> (then part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Republic" title="Russian Republic">Russian Republic</a>). After graduation from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azerbaijan_Industrial_Institute" title="Azerbaijan Industrial Institute">Azerbaijan Industrial Institute</a> in 1942, Kerimov continued his education at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_military_academies#Dzerzhinsky_Military_Academy_of_Rocket_Forces" title="Soviet military academies">Dzerzhinsky Artillery Academy</a>, where he committed himself to design and development of rocket systems.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adulthood">Adulthood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerim_Kerimov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adulthood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An expert in rocket technology, he worked during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> on the inspection and acceptance of the famous <a href="/wiki/Katyusha_rocket_launcher" title="Katyusha rocket launcher">Katyusha rocket launchers</a>. His work was honoured with the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Star" title="Order of the Red Star">Order of the Red Star</a>. Kerim Kerimov has been involved in Soviet aeronautics from its inception. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Kerimov worked on the Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inter-continental_ballistic_missile" title="Inter-continental ballistic missile">inter-continental ballistic missile</a> program, rising by 1960 to head the Third Directorate of the Main Directorate of Missile Weapons (GURVO) of the USSR Ministry of Defense that oversaw secret test launches. Along with other rocketry experts, he was sent to Germany in 1946 to collect information on the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-2" title="V-2">V-2</a> rocket.\n</p><p>In 1964 he became head of the newly formed Central Directorate of the Space Forces (TsUKOS) of the USSR Ministry of Defense. Following the death of <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a> in 1966, Kerimov was appointed Chairman of the State Commission on Piloted Flights and headed it for 25 years (1966&#8211;1991). He supervised every stage of development and operation of both manned space complexes as well as unmanned interplanetary stations for former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Kerimov was also the Head of Chief Directorate of the Ministry of General Machine Building in 1965-1974, which was engaged in creation of rocket systems.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerim_Kerimov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After his 1991 retirement, Kerimov was a Consultant to the Main Space Flights Control Centre of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">Russian Federal Space Agency</a>, and wrote <i>The Way to Space</i>, a history of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a>. Kerim Kerimov was a <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Socialist_Labour" title="Hero of Socialist Labour">Hero of Socialist Labour</a>, laureate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalin_Prize" title="Stalin Prize">Stalin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lenin_Prize" title="Lenin Prize">Lenin</a> and <a href="/wiki/USSR_State_Prize" title="USSR State Prize">State</a> prizes of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general" title="Lieutenant-general">lieutenant-general</a> of <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">Soviet Army</a>. General Kerim Kerimov died March 29, 2003 in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, at the age of 85.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerim_Kerimov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wladys%C5%82aw_J%C3%B3zef_Marian_Turowicz" title="Wladys&#322;aw J&#243;zef Marian Turowicz">Wladys&#322;aw J&#243;zef Marian Turowicz</a></li>\n<li>"Bank of the Universe" - edited by Boltenko A.
