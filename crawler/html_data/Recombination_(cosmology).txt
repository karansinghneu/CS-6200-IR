href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>, <b>recombination</b> refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoch_(cosmology)" title="Epoch (cosmology)">epoch</a> at which charged <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> first became <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bound</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electrically neutral</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Recombination occurred about 378,000 years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> (at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_redshift" title="Cosmic redshift">redshift</a> of <i>z</i>&#160;=&#160;<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003110000000000000&#9824;</span>1100</span>). The word "recombination" is misleading, since the big bang theory doesn\'t posit that protons and electrons had been combined before, but the name exists for historical reasons since it was named before the Big Bang hypothesis became the primary theory of the creation of the universe.\n</p><p>Immediately after the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>, the universe was a hot, dense <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lepton" title="Lepton">leptons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Quark_epoch" title="Quark epoch">Quark epoch</a>. At 10<sup>&#8722;6</sup> seconds, the Universe had expanded and cooled sufficiently to allow for the formation of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Hadron_epoch" title="Hadron epoch">Hadron epoch</a>. This plasma was effectively opaque to electromagnetic radiation due to <a href="/wiki/Thomson_scattering" title="Thomson scattering">Thomson scattering</a> by free electrons, as the <a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">mean free path</a> each photon could travel before encountering an electron was very short. This is the current state of the interior of the Sun. As the universe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expanded</a>, it also cooled. Eventually, the universe cooled to the point that the formation of neutral hydrogen was energetically favored, and the fraction of free electrons and protons as compared to neutral hydrogen decreased to a few parts in 10,000.\n</p><p>Recombination involves electrons binding to protons (hydrogen nuclei) to form neutral <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Because direct recombinations to the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> (lowest energy) of hydrogen are very inefficient, these hydrogen atoms generally form with the electrons in a high energy state, and the electrons quickly transition to their low energy state by emitting <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>. Two main pathways exist: from the <i>2p</i> state by emitting a <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman-a photon</a> - these photons will almost always be reabsorbed by another hydrogen atom in its ground state - or from the <i>2s</i> state by emitting two photons, which is very slow. \n</p><p>This production of photons is known as <a href="/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology)" title="Decoupling (cosmology)">decoupling</a>, which leads to recombination sometimes being called <b>photon decoupling</b>, but recombination and photon decoupling are distinct events. Once photons decoupled from matter, they <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_stream" title="Free stream">traveled freely</a> through the universe without interacting with matter and constitute what is observed today as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> (in that sense, the cosmic background radiation is <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> [and some red] <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a> emitted when the universe was at a temperature of some 4000&#160;K, <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifted</a> by a factor of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003110000000000000&#9824;</span>1100</span> from the visible spectrum to the <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> spectrum).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" The basic elements of the model are the following.\n</p>\n<ul><li>Direct recombinations to the ground state of hydrogen are very inefficient: each such event leads to a photon with energy greater than 13.6 eV, which almost immediately re-ionizes a neighboring hydrogen atom.</li>\n<li>Electrons therefore only efficiently recombine to the excited states of hydrogen, from which they cascade very quickly down to the first excited state, with <a href="/wiki/Principal_quantum_number" title="Principal quantum number">principal quantum number</a> <i>n</i> = 2.</li>\n<li>From the first excited state, electrons can reach the ground state <i>n</i> =1 through  two pathways:\n<ul><li>Decay from the <i>2p</i> state by emitting a <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman-&#945; photon</a>. This photon will almost always be reabsorbed by another hydrogen atom in its ground state. However, cosmological redshifting systematically decreases the photon frequency, and there is a small chance that it escapes reabsorption if it gets redshifted far enough from the Lyman-&#945; line resonant frequency before encountering another hydrogen atom.</li>\n<li>Decay from the <i>2s</i> state by emitting two photons. This <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Two-photon_decay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Two-photon decay (page does not exist)">two-photon decay</a> process is very slow, with a rate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> of 8.22 s<sup>&#8722;1</sup>. It is however competitive with the slow rate of Lyman-&#945; escape in producing ground-state hydrogen.</li></ul></li>\n<li>Atoms in the first excited state may also be re-ionized by the ambient <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">CMB</a> photons before they reach the ground state. When this is the case, it is as if the recombination to the excited state did not happen in the first place. To account for this possibility, Peebles defines the factor <i>C</i> as the probability that an atom in the first excited state reaches the ground state through either of the two pathways described above before being photoionized.</li></ul>\n<p>This model is usually described as an "effective three-level atom" as it requires keeping track of hydrogen under three forms: in its ground state, in its first excited state (assuming all the higher excited states are in <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution" title="Boltzmann distribution">Boltzmann equilibrium</a> with it), and in its ionized state.\n</p><p>Accounting for these processes, the recombination history is then described by the <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equation</a>\n</p><p><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\frac {dx_{\\text{e}}}{dt}}=-C\\left(\\alpha _{\\text{B}}(T)n_{\\text{p}}x_{e}-4(1-x_{\\text{e}})\\beta _{\\text{B}}(T)e^{-E_{21}/T}\\right),}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mrow>\n<mi>d</mi>\n<msub>\n<mi>x</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mtext>e</mtext>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mrow>\n<mrow>\n<mi>d</mi>\n<mi>t</mi>\n</mrow>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; The equilibrium result given in the previous section would be recovered by setting the left-hand side to zero, i.e. assuming that the net rates of recombination and photoionization are large in comparison to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_expansion" title="Hubble expansion">Hubble expansion</a> rate, which sets the overall evolution timescale for the temperature and density. However, <span class="texhtml"><i>C</i> &#945;<sub>B</sub> <i>n</i><sub>p</sub></span> is comparable to the Hubble expansion rate, and even gets significantly lower at low redshifts, leading to an evolution of the free electron fraction much slower than what one would obtain from the Saha equilibrium calculation. With modern values of cosmological parameters, one finds that the universe is 90% neutral at <i>z</i> &#8776; 1070.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_developments">Modern developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recombination_(cosmology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The simple effective three-level atom model described above accounts for the most important physical processes. However it does rely on approximations which lead to errors on the predicted recombination history at the level of 10% or so. Due to the importance of recombination for the precise prediction of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> anisotropies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> several research groups have revisited the details of this picture over the last two decades.\n</p><p>The refinements to the theory can be divided into two categories:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Accounting for the non-equilibrium populations of the highly excited states of hydrogen. This effectively amounts to modifying the recombination coefficient <i>&#945;</i><sub>B</sub>.</li>\n<li>Accurately computing the rate of Lyman-&#945; escape and the effect of these photons on the <i>2s-1s</i> transition. This requires solving a time-dependent <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer</a> equation. In addition, one needs to account for higher-order <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman transitions</a>. These refinements effectively amount to a modification of Peebles\' <i>C</i> factor.</li></ul>\n<p>Modern recombination theory is believed to be accurate at the level of 0.1%, and is implemented in publicly available fast recombination codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primordial_helium_recombination">Primordial helium recombination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Recombination_(cosmology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Primordial helium recombination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> nuclei are produced during <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">Big Bang nucleosynthesis</a>, and make up about 24% of the total mass of <a href="/wiki/Baryon" title="Baryon">baryonic matter</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ionization_energy" title="Ionization energy">ionization energy</a> of helium is larger than that of hydrogen and it therefore recombines earlier. This scattering causes a loss of information, and "there is therefore a photon barrier at a redshift" near that of recombination that prevents us from using photons directly to learn about the universe at larger redshifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Once recombination had occurred, however, the mean free path of photons greatly increased due to the lower number of free electrons. Shortly after recombination, the photon mean free path became larger than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_sphere" title="Hubble sphere">Hubble length</a>, and photons traveled freely without interacting with matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p115_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-p115-12">[12]</a></sup> For this reason, recombination is closely associated with the last scattering surface, which is the name for the last time at which the photons in the cosmic microwave background interacted with matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, these two events are distinct, and in a universe with different values for the baryon-to-photon ratio and matter density, recombination and photon decoupling need not have occurred at the same epoch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p115_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-p115-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
