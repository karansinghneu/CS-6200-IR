class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Archaeological site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg" title="This map shows the locations of archeological sites associated with Oc Eo culture. It is located at the Museum of Vietnamese History, Ho Chi Minh City."><img alt="This map shows the locations of archeological sites associated with Oc Eo culture. It is located at the Museum of Vietnamese History, Ho Chi Minh City." data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/150px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/225px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg/300px-Sites_of_Oc_Eo_Culture_in_Southern_Vietnam.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a></div>\n<p>Excavation at &#211;c Eo began on February 10, 1942, after French archaeologists had discovered the site through the use of aerial photography. The first excavations were led by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Malleret" title="Louis Malleret">Louis Malleret</a>.  The site covers 450 ha.\n</p><p>&#211;c Eo is situated within a network of ancient canals that crisscross the low flatland of the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a>.  One of the canals connects &#211;c Eo to  the town\'s seaport while another goes 68 kilometres (42&#160;mi) north-northeast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angkor_Borei" title="Angkor Borei">Angkor Borei</a>.  &#211;c Eo is longitudinally bisected by a canal, and there are four transverse canals along which pile-supported houses were perhaps ranged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Archaeological sites reflecting the material culture of &#211;c Eo are spread throughout southern Vietnam, but are most heavily concentrated in the area of the Mekong Delta to the south and west of <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>.  The most significant site, aside from &#211;c Eo itself, is at Th&#225;p Mu&#7901;i north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tien_Giang" title="Tien Giang">Tien Giang</a> River, where among other remains a stele with a 6th-century <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> text has been discovered.\n</p><p>Aerial photography in 1958 revealed that during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Funan</a> period a distributary of the Mekong entered the Gulf of Thailand in the vicinity of Ta Keo, which was then on the shore but since then become separated by some distance from the sea as a result of siltation. At that time, Ta Keo was connected by a canal with Oc Eo, allowing it access to the Gulf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The distributary of the Mekong revealed in the aerial photography was probably <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Sinus" title="Magnus Sinus">the <i>Saenus</i></a> mentioned in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>&#8217;s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_Geography" title="Ptolemy\'s Geography">Geography</a></i> as the western branch of the Mekong, which Ptolemy called the <i>Cottiaris</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Cattigara in Ptolemy\'s Geography could be derived from a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_grammar#Phonology" title="Sanskrit grammar">Sanskrit word</a>, either <i>Kottinagara</i> (Strong City) or <i>Kirtinagara</i> (Renowned City).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remains">Remains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Among the finds are gold jewellery imitating coins from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonine" title="Antonine">Antonine</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>279</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Roman golden <a href="/wiki/Medal" Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, have been discovered at &#211;c Eo, which was near Chinese-controlled <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhou_(region)" title="Jiaozhou (region)">Jiaozhou</a> and the region where <a href="#cite_note-young_2001_p_29-8">[8]</a></sup> Many of the remains have been collected and are on exhibition in <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Vietnamese_History" title="Museum of Vietnamese History">Museum of Vietnamese History</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Ho Chi Minh City</a>.\n</p><p>Among the coins found at &#211;c Eo by Malleret were eight made of silver bearing the image of the <a href="/wiki/Hamsa_(bird)" title="Hamsa (bird)">hamsa</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crested_argus" title="Crested argus">crested argus</a>, apparently minted in Funan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id=".C3.93c_Eo_and_Funan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&#211;c_Eo_and_Funan">&#211;c Eo and Funan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &#211;c Eo and Funan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Kingdom of Funan</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" decoding="async" height="165" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg/330px-Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg 1.5x, class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Di_t%C3%ADch_G%C3%B2_C%C3%A2y_Th%E1%BB%8B_A.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The archeological site of Go Cay Thi, Ba The Oc Eo</div></div></div>\n<p>&#211;c Eo has been regarded as belonging to the historical kingdom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Funan</a> (&#25206;&#21335;) that flourished in the Mekong Delta between the 1st and the 6th century CE.  The kingdom of Funan is known to us from the works of ancient Chinese historians, especially writers of dynastic histories, who in turn drew from the testimony of Chinese diplomats and travellers, and of foreign (including Funanese) embassies to the Chinese imperial courts.  Indeed, the name "Funan" itself is an artifact of the Chinese histories, and does not appear in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleography" title="Paleography">paleographic</a> record of ancient Vietnam or Cambodia.  From the Chinese sources, however, it can be determined that a polity called "Funan" by the Chinese was the dominant polity located in the Mekong Delta region.  As a result, archeological discoveries in that region that can be dated to the period of Funan have been identified with the historical polity of Funan.  The discoveries at &#211;c Eo and related sites are our primary source for the <a href="/wiki/Material_culture" title="Material culture">material culture</a> of Funan.\n</p><p>The Vietnamese archaeologist and historian <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_V%C4%83n_T%E1%BA%A5n" title="H&#224; V&#259;n T&#7845;n">H&#224; V&#259;n T&#7845;n</a> has written that at the present stage of knowledge, it was impossible to demonstrate the existence of a Funan culture, widely spread from the Mekong Delta through the Chao Praya delta to Burma, with Oc Eo as the typical representative: the presence of similar artefacts such as jewelry and seals from sites in those areas was simply the result of trade and exchange, while each of the sites bore the signs of their own separate cultural development. He supported the view of Claude Jacques that, in view of the complete lack of any <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Khmer</a> records relating to a kingdom by the name of Funan, use of this name should be abandoned in favour of the names, such as <i>Aninditapura</i>, <i>Bhavapura</i>, <i>Shresthapura</i> and <i>Vyadhapura</i>, which are known from inscriptions to have been used at the time for cities in the region and provide a more accurate idea of the true geography of the ancient Khmer territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> H&#224; V&#259;n T&#7845;n argued that, from the late neolithic or early metal age, &#211;c Eo gradually emerged as an economic and cultural centre of the Mekong Delta and, with an important position on the Southeast Asian sea routes, became a meeting place for craftsmen and traders, which provided adequate conditions for urbanization, receiving foreign influences, notably from India, which in turn stimulated internal development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Funan was part of the region of Southeast Asia referred to in ancient Indian texts as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suwannaphum" title="Suwannaphum">Suvarnabhumi</a>, and may have been the part to which the term was first applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kattigara_of_Ptolemy">Kattigara of Ptolemy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oceo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ancient canal linking &#211;c Eo to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angkor_Borei" title="Angkor Borei">Angkor Borei</a></div></div></div>\n<p>&#211;c Eo may have been the port known to the Romans as Kattigara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Kattigara was the name given by the 2nd-century <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandrian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_geographers" title="Greek geographers">geographer</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> to the land on the easternmost shore of the <a id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name "Kattigara" was probably derived from the Sanskrit <i>Kirti-nagara</i> &#2325;&#2368;&#2352;&#2381;&#2340;&#2367;- &#2344;&#2327;&#2352; &#8220;Renowned City&#8221; or <i>Kotti-nagara</i> &#2325;&#2379;&#2335;&#2367;-&#2344;&#2327;&#2352; &#8220;Strong City&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scholarship has now determined that Ptolemy\'s Kattigara was at 8&#189;&#176; north of the Equator and was the forerunner of <a class="mw-redirect" Caverhill deduced in 1767 that Cattigara was the Mekong Delta port Banteaymeas (now <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A0_Ti%C3%AAn" title="H&#224; Ti&#234;n">H&#224; Ti&#234;n</a>),<sup class="reference" still remains unanswered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The eighteenth-century French geographer, <a class="mw-redirect" (<i>Cottiaris</i>) River, where it is shown on his map, <i>Orbis Veteribus Notus</i> (The World Known to the Ancients).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DAnville_Cattigara_1794.jpg"><img alt="" World Known to the Ancients).</div></div></div>\n<p>The Swedish yachtsman and writer Bjorn Landstr&#246;m also concluded, from the sailing directions given by the ancient merchant and seafarer Alexander, that Cattigara lay at the mouth of the Mekong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The &#8220;father of Early Southeast Asian History&#8221;, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Coed%C3%A8s" title="George Coed&#232;s">George Coed&#232;s</a>, has said: &#8220;By the middle of the 3rd century Fu-nan had already established relations with China and India, and it is doubtless on the west coast of the Gulf of Siam that the furthest point reached by Hellenistic navigators is to be found, that is the harbour of Kattigara mentioned by Ptolemy&#8221;.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The On the 1489 map of the world made by <a href="/wiki/Henricus_Martellus_Germanus" title="Henricus Martellus Germanus">Henricus Martellus Germanus</a>, revising Ptolemy&#8217;s work, Asia terminated in its southeastern point in a cape, the Cape of Cattigara. Writing of his 1499 voyage, <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a> said he had hoped to reach <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">Malacca</a> (Melaka) by sailing westward from Spain across the Western Ocean (the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a>) around the Cape of Cattigara into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinus_Magnus" title="Sinus Magnus">Sinus Magnus</a> ("Great Gulf") that lay to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Chersonese" title="Golden Chersonese">Golden Chersonese</a> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>), of which the Cape of  Cattigara formed the southeastern point. The Sinus Magnus was the actual <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, on his fourth and last voyage of 1502&#8211;1503, planned to follow the coast of <a href="/wiki/Champa" title="Champa">Champa</a> southward around the Cape of Cattigara and sail through the strait separating Cattigara from the New World, into the Sinus Magnus to Malacca. This was the route he thought <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> had gone from China to India in 1292.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Columbus planned to meet up with the expedition sent at the same time from <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a> under <a href="/wiki/Vasco_da_Gama" title="Vasco da Gama">Vasco da Gama</a>, and carried letters of credence from the Spanish monarchs to present to da Gama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On reaching Cariay on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, Columbus thought he was close to the gold mines of Champa. On July 7, 1503, he wrote from <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>: &#8220;I reached the land of Cariay...Here I received news of the gold mines of Ciamba [Champa] which I was seeking&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%93c_Eo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Paul L&#233;vy, "Recent Archaeological Researches by the &#201;cole Fran&#231;ais d&#8217;Extr&#234;me Orient, French Indo-China, 1940&#8211;1945", in Kalidas Nag (ed.), <i>Sir William Jones: Bicentenary of his Birth Commemoration Volume, 1746&#8211;1946</i>, Calcutta, Royal Asiatic Society of Bengal, 1948, pp.118-19; paraphrased in <a href="/wiki/R._C._Majumdar" title="R.
