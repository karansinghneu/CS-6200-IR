class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/State_of_the_art_(disambiguation)" title="State of the art (disambiguation)">State of the art (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>State-of-the-art</b> (sometimes <b>cutting edge</b>) refers to the highest level of general development, as of a device, technique, or scientific field achieved at a particular time. It also refers to such a level of development reached at any particular time as a result of the common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodologies" title="Methodologies">methodologies</a> employed at the time. \n</p><p>The term has been used since 1910, and has become both a common term in <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a>, and a legally significant phrase with respect to both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patent_law" title="Patent law">patent law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">liability</a>. \n</p><p>In advertising, the phrase is often used to convey that a product is made with the best possible technology, but  it has been noted that "the term \'state-of-the-art\' requires little proof on the part of advertisers", as it is considered mere <a href="/wiki/Puffery" title="Puffery">puffery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The use of the term in patent law, by contrast, "does not connote even superiority, let alone the superlative quality the ad writers would have us ascribe to the term".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_history">Origin and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The origin of the concept of "state-of-the-art" took place in the beginning of the twentieth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LM-3">[3]</a></sup> The earliest use of the term "state-of-the-art" documented by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> dates back to 1910, from an engineering manual by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Harrison_Suplee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Harrison Suplee (page does not exist)">Henry Harrison Suplee</a> (1856-post 1943), an engineering graduate (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, 1876), titled <i>Gas Turbine: progress in the design and construction of turbines operated by gases of combustion</i>.  The relevant passage reads: "In the present state-of-the-art this is all that can be done". The term "art" refers to technics, rather than <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fine_art" title="Fine art">fine arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over time, use of the term increased in all fields where this kind of art has a significant role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Anderson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Anderson-5">[5]</a></sup> In this relation it has been quoted by the author that "<i>Although eighteenth century writers did not use the term, there was indeed in existence a collection of scientific and engineering knowledge and expertise that can be identified as the state-of-the-art for that time</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Anderson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-John_Anderson-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite its actual meaning, which does not convey technology that is ahead of the industry, the phrase became so widely used in advertising that a 1985 article described it as "overused", stating that "[i]t has no punch left and actually sounds like a lie".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A 1994 essay listed it among "the same old tired <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Clich&#233;">clich&#233;s</a>" that should be avoided in advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_importance">Legal importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_law">Patent law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patent law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prior_art" title="Prior art">Prior art</a></div>\n<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">European</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, the term "state of the art" is a concept used in the process of assessing and asserting <a href="/wiki/Novelty_(patent)" title="Novelty (patent)">novelty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inventive_step_and_non-obviousness" title="Inventive step and non-obviousness">inventive step</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and is a synonym of the expression "<a href="/wiki/Prior_art" title="Prior art">prior art</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  In the <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">European Patent Convention</a> (EPC), "[the] state-of-the-art shall be held to comprise everything made available to the public by means of a written or oral description, by use, or in any other way, before the date of filing of the European patent application" according to <a class="external text" href="https://www.epo.org/law-practice/legal-texts/html/epc/2013/e/ar54.html" rel="nofollow">Article 54(2)</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">EPC</a>. Due account should be taken of <a class="external text" href="https://www.epo.org/law-practice/legal-texts/html/epc/2013/e/ar54.html" rel="nofollow">Article 54(3)</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">EPC</a> as well, but merely for the examination of novelty.\n</p><p>The expression "background art" is also used in certain legal provisions, such as <a class="external text" href="https://www.epo.org/law-practice/legal-texts/html/epc/2016/e/r42.html" rel="nofollow">Rule 42(1)(b) and(c)</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">EPC</a> (previously <a class="external text" href="https://www.epo.org/law-practice/legal-texts/html/epc/1973/e/r27.html" rel="nofollow">Rule 27(1)(b) and (c)</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Convention" title="European Patent Convention">EPC 1973</a>), and has the same meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tort_liability">Tort liability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tort liability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The state-of-the-art is important in the law of <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">liability</a>, specifically in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Products_liability" title="Products liability">products liability</a>. With respect to negligence, "an engineer may defend against a claim of negligence by contending that he met the standards of his profession and the state-of-the-art".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> With respect to products liability, manufacturers generally have <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a> for any injury caused by defects in their products. However, in some jurisdictions a manufacturer may raise as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_defense" title="Legal defense">legal defense</a> the assertion that their product represents the "state-of-the-art", and that the manufacturer therefore could not have made the product any safer in light of the knowledge available at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, "[u]nder German law, the producer can also raise the state-of-the-art defense: general tort law does not hold him liable if he could not know or discover the defect for lack of fault, and the Product Liability Statute expressly provides for this defense".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This defense is available throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Community" title="European Community">European Community</a> under the Product Liability Directive, art. 7(e). Pursuant to this article:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The state-of-the-art defense allows a defendant to be absolved of liability if he can prove that the state of technical and scientific knowledge, at the time when he put the product into circulation, was not such as to enable the existence of the defect to be discovered. The Directive allows Member States to eliminate the state-of-the-art defense, but only Luxembourg, which has little manufacturing industry, has done so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, in those states that follow the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, the state of an industry is "merely evidence of due care rather than a controlling factor",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandall_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vandall-15">[15]</a></sup> but a number of states have State-of-the-Art statutes that "make a manufacturer\'s compliance with technological feasibility an absolute defense to a products liability suit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandall_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vandall-15">[15]</a></sup> Because the state-of-the-art is constantly advancing, the ability of manufacturers to claim that their products are "state-of-the-art" tracks their potential liability when these products are defective. As an industry journal explained in the 1980s:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Remote control rear view mirrors, disc brakes, automatic slack adjusters for drum brakes and sealed lighting systems are just a few examples of products that have advanced the state-of-the-art. When one of these gains a degree of industry acceptance, it begins to bridge a legal gap between what is state-of-the-art from a design standpoint, and what is state-of-the-art from a usage standpoint. This could place a carrier in a vulnerable position in the not too distant future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleeding_edge" title="Bleeding edge">Bleeding edge</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_end" title="High end">High end</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">Innovation</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_of_the_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
