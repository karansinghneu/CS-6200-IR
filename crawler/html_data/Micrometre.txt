<b>micrometre</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">International spelling</a> as used by the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> symbol: <b>&#956;m</b>) or <b>micrometer</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">American spelling</a>), also <a href="#Continuing_informal_usage_of_micron">commonly known</a> by the previous name <b>micron</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> equaling <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6994100000000000000&#9824;</span>1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;6</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a></span> (SI standard prefix "<a href="/wiki/Micro-" title="Micro-">micro-</a>" = 10<sup>&#8722;6</sup>); that is, one millionth of a <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> (or one thousandth of a <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">millimetre</a>, 0.001&#160;mm, or about 0.000039&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inch</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The micrometre is a common unit of <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> for <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> as well as sizes of biological <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop&#230;dia_Britannica_Online-1">[1]</a></sup> and for grading <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> by the diameter of the fibres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW_Department_of_Education_and_Communities_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSW_Department_of_Education_and_Communities-2">[2]</a></sup> The width of a single human hair ranges from approximately 10 to 200&#160;&#956;m. were officially accepted for use in isolation to denote the micrometre in 1879, but officially revoked by the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI) in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-7">[7]</a></sup> This became necessary because the older usage was incompatible with the official adoption of the unit prefix <i>micro-</i>, denoted &#956;, during the creation of the SI in 1960. \n</p><p>In the SI, the systematic name <i>micrometre</i> became the official name of the unit, and &#956;m became the official unit symbol.\n</p><p><span id="Continuing_informal_usage_of_micron"></span> Nevertheless, in practice, "micron" remains a widely used term in preference to "micrometre" in many English-speaking countries, both in \nacademic science (including <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>) and in applied science and industry (including <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machining</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_industry" title="Semiconductor industry">semiconductor industry</a>, and plastics manufacturing).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Additionally, in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, the use of "micron" helps differentiate the unit from the <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">micrometer</a>, a measuring device, because the unit\'s name in mainstream <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re.2C_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">American spelling</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Homograph" title="Homograph">homograph</a> of the device\'s name. In spoken English, they may be distinguished by pronunciation, as the name of the measuring device is invariably stressed on the second syllable, whereas the systematic pronunciation of the unit name, in accordance with the convention for pronouncing SI units in English, places the stress on the first syllable.\n</p><p>The plural of <i>micron</i> is normally "microns", though "micra" was occasionally used before 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol">Symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micrometre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Micro-#Symbol_encoding_in_character_sets" title="Micro-">Micro- &#167;&#160;Symbol encoding in character sets</a></div>\n<p>The official symbol for the <a href="/wiki/Metric_prefix" title="Metric prefix">SI prefix</a> <i>micro-</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Mu_(letter)" title="Mu (letter)">Greek lowercase mu</a> (&#956;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>, there is also a micro sign with the <a href="/wiki/Code_point" title="Code point">code point</a> U+00B5 (&#181;), distinct from the code point U+03BC (&#956;) of the Greek letter lowercase mu. According to the <a href="/wiki/Unicode_Consortium" title="Unicode Consortium">Unicode Consortium</a>, the Greek letter character is preferred,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but implementations must recognize the micro sign as well.
