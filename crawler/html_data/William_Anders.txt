Lerner</a></td></tr><tr style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr><tr style="display:none"><td colspan="2">\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>William Alison Anders</b> (born October 17, 1933), is a retired <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major general</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_engineer" title="Electrical engineer">electrical engineer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_engineer" title="Nuclear engineer">nuclear engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Businessman" title="Businessman">businessman</a>. He is known for being one of the first three persons to leave <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> and travel to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> along with fellow astronauts <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">Jim Lovell</a> as well as for photographing the iconic image, <i><a href="/wiki/Earthrise" title="Earthrise">Earthrise</a></i>. Apart from his professional and military careers, Anders was the U.S. Ambassador to Norway from 1976 to 1977 under <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Gerald_Ford" title="Presidency of Gerald Ford">President Gerald Ford</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anders was born on October 17, 1933, in <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British Hong Kong</a>, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> Lieutenant Arthur Anders and his wife Muriel (n&#233;e Adams) Anders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The Anders family practiced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>.  Following Anders\' birth, the family relocated from Hong Kong to <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> where Anders\' father began teaching <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Postgraduate_School" title="Naval Postgraduate School">U.S. Naval Postgraduate School</a>.  After a time, the family returned to China where Anders and his mother escaped to the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> following the Japanese attack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanking" title="Nanking">Nanking</a>.  With his mother, Anders started his journey to the Philippine Islands by troop train to <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Canton</a>.  While there, they stayed in a hotel 200 yards from a river the Japanese were bombing, the same river they were soon forced to travel in their escape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airportjournals_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-airportjournals-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While in the United States, Anders was active in the <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts</a>, achieving the organization\'s second-highest rank, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Scout" title="Life Scout">Life Scout</a>. As a teen, Anders attended St. Martin\'s Academy and graduated in 1951 from <a href="/wiki/Grossmont_High_School" title="Grossmont High School">Grossmont High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/La_Mesa,_California" title="La Mesa, California">La Mesa</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following high school, he received an appointment to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy</a>, receiving a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> in 1955.  Upon graduation, he chose to be commissioned a Second Lieutenant in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>.  He then continued his studies, receiving a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_engineering" title="Nuclear engineering">nuclear engineering</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Institute_of_Technology" title="Air Force Institute of Technology">Air Force Institute of Technology</a> in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritageflight_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-heritageflight-4">[4]</a></sup> He attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>\'s six-week <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School#Advanced_Management_Program" title="Harvard Business School">Advanced Management Program</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Force_service_and_NASA_career">Air Force service and NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Air Force service and NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Air_Force">United States Air Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States Air Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following graduation from the U.S. Naval Academy, Anders took his commission in the U.S. Air Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritageflight_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-heritageflight-4">[4]</a></sup> After receiving his <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_aeronautical_rating" title="U.S. Air Force aeronautical rating">pilot wings</a> in 1956, he served as a <a href="/wiki/Fighter_pilot" title="Fighter pilot">fighter pilot</a> in all-weather interceptor squadrons of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Defense_Command" title="Air Defense Command">Air Defense Command</a> in California and in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, where he participated in early intercepts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet" title="Soviet">Soviet</a> heavy bombers who at the time were challenging America\'s air defense borders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritageflight_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-heritageflight-4">[4]</a></sup> While at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Weapons_Laboratory" title="Air Force Weapons Laboratory">Air Force Weapons Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, he was responsible for technical management of nuclear power reactor shielding and radiation effects programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA">NASA</span><span href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">Frank Borman</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1963, Anders was selected by NASA in the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Astronaut_Group_3" title="NASA Astronaut Group 3">third group</a> of astronauts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>  While at NASA, he became involved in <a href="/wiki/Dosimetry" title="Dosimetry">dosimetry</a>, radiation effects, and environmental controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup> He was the backup pilot for the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_11" title="Gemini 11">Gemini 11</a> mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup> Then in December 1968, he flew as <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> Pilot for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> mission (although no lunar module was carried by the .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We came all this way to explore the Moon, and the most important thing is that we discovered the Earth.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>William Anders <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nmspacemuseum_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nmspacemuseum-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-NASA_career">Post-NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From 1969 to 1973, Anders served as Executive Secretary for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Council" title="National Aeronautics and Space Council">National Aeronautics and Space Council</a>, which was responsible to the President, Vice President and Cabinet-level members of the Council for developing policy options concerning research, development, operations and planning of aeronautical and space systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On August 6, 1973, he was appointed to the five-member <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a>, where he was lead commissioner for nuclear and non-nuclear power <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>. He was also named as U.S. Chairman of the joint U.S./<a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> technology exchange program for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">fission</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the reorganization of national nuclear regulatory and developmental activities on January 19, 1975, he was named by President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Ford" title="Gerald R. Ford">Ford</a> to become the first chairman of the newly established <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a>, which is responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_safety" title="Nuclear safety">nuclear safety</a> and environmental compatibility. At the completion of his term as NRC chairman, he was appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_Norway" title="United States Ambassador to Norway">Ambassador to Norway</a> and held that position until 1977. He then ended his career with the federal government after 26 years and began work in the private sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>He served briefly as a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Enterprise_Institute" title="American Enterprise Institute">American Enterprise Institute</a>, then joined <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> (GE) in September 1977. As Vice President and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Manager" title="General Manager">General Manager</a> of GE\'s Nuclear Products Division in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>, he was responsible for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>, reactor internal equipment, and control and instrumentation for GE <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">boiling-water reactors</a> at facilities located in San Jose and <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. He also oversaw GE\'s partnership with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago_Bridge_and_Iron" title="Chicago Bridge and Iron">Chicago Bridge and Iron</a> for making large steel pressure vessels in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>. In August 1979, he attended Harvard Business School\'s six-week Advanced Management Program. On the first day of 1980, he was appointed General Manager of the GE Aircraft Equipment Division. Headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Utica,_New_York" title="Utica, New York">Utica, New York</a>, the division included more than 8,500 employees in five locations in the northeastern U.S. Its products included aircraft flight and weapon control systems, cockpit instruments, aircraft electrical generating systems, airborne radars and data processing systems, electronic countermeasures, space command systems, and aircraft/surface multi-barrel armament systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1984, he left GE to join <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a> as Executive Vice President for aerospace, and two years later became Senior Executive Vice President for operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1990, he became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_Chairman" title="Vice Chairman">Vice Chairman</a> of <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a>, and on January 1, 1991, its chairman and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer">Chief Executive Officer</a> (CEO). He retired in 1993, but remained Chairman until May 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anders was a consultant to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="Office of Science and Technology Policy">Office of Science and Technology Policy</a>, and was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Science_Board" title="Defense Science Board">Defense Science Board</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Advisory_Council" title="NASA Advisory Council">NASA Advisory Council</a>. He is a retired <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">USAF Reserve</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg/220px-Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg/330px-Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg/440px-Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bergen_Air_Show_075.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bill Anders taxiing a P-51 Mustang at <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a> Air Show in 2005</div></div></div>\n<p>He established the William A. Anders Foundation, a philanthropic organization dedicated to supporting educational and environmental issues. The foundation was a primary sponsor of the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Experience" title="American Experience">American Experience</a></i> television episode "Race to the Moon." The foundation also founded the <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Flight_Museum" title="Heritage Flight Museum">Heritage Flight Museum</a> in 1996  in <a href="/wiki/Bellingham,_Washington" title="Bellingham, Washington">Bellingham, Washington</a>; Museum moved to Skagit Regional Airport in Burlington, Washington in 2014; he serves as its President and until 2008 was an active participant in its air shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museumofflight_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-museumofflight-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Anders_(crater)" title="Anders (crater)">Anders crater</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is named in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-museumofflight_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-museumofflight-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2011, he spoke at the first <a href="/wiki/Starmus_Festival" title="Starmus Festival">Starmus Festival</a> in the Canary Islands, delivering a lecture on the early American space program. His talk was published in the book <i>Starmus: 50 Years of Man in Space</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anders is a member of <a href="/wiki/Tau_Beta_Pi" title="Tau Beta Pi">Tau Beta Pi</a> National Engineering Honor Society, <a href="/wiki/American_Nuclear_Society" title="American Nuclear Society">American Nuclear Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Aeronautics_and_Astronautics" title="American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics">American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Experimental_Test_Pilots" title="Society of Experimental Test Pilots">Society of Experimental Test Pilots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Anders&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anders married Valerie Hoard in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasabio_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-nasabio-5">[5]</a></sup> Together, they have six children: Alan (born in 1957), Glen (born in 1958), Gayle (born in 1960), Gregory (born in 1962), Eric (born in 1964), and Diana (born in 1972). From his Air Force career on, he has logged more than 8,000 hours of flight time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritageflight_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-heritageflight-4">[4]</a></sup> He and his wife reside in <a href="/wiki/Anacortes,_Washington" title="Anacortes, Washington">Anacortes, Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airportjournals_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-airportjournals-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anacortes_home_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-anacortes_home-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Anders stated that the <i>Earthrise</i> photograph "really undercut my religious beliefs.
