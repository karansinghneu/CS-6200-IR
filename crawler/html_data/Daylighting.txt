//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Daylighting_-_Skylight.jpg/440px-Daylighting_-_Skylight.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Daylighting_-_Skylight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Skylight" title="Skylight">skylight</a> providing internal illumination</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Daylighting</b> is the practice of placing <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">windows</a>, skylights, other openings, and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflective</a> surfaces so that <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> (direct or indirect) can provide effective internal <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">lighting</a>. Particular attention is given to daylighting while designing a building when the aim is to maximize visual comfort or to reduce energy use. Energy savings can be achieved from the reduced use of artificial (electric) lighting or from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">passive solar</a> heating. Artificial lighting energy use can be reduced by simply installing fewer electric lights where daylight is present or by automatically <a href="/wiki/Dimmer" title="Dimmer">dimming</a>/switching off electric lights in response to the presence of daylight &#8211; a process known as <a href="/wiki/Daylight_harvesting" title="Daylight harvesting">daylight harvesting</a>.\n</p><p>The amount of daylight received in an internal space can be analyzed by measuring <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> on a grid or undertaking a <a href="/wiki/Daylight_factor" title="Daylight factor">daylight factor</a> calculation. Computer programs such as <a href="/wiki/Radiance_(software)" title="Radiance (software)">Radiance</a> allow an architect or engineer to quickly calculate benefits of a particular design. The human eye\'s response to light <a href="/wiki/Weber%E2%80%93Fechner_law#Vision" title="Weber&#8211;Fechner law">is non-linear</a>, so a more even distribution of the same amount of light makes a room appear brighter. \n</p><p>The source of all <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The proportion of direct to diffuse light impacts the amount and quality of daylight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daylighting_Handbook_1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daylighting_Handbook_1-1">[1]</a></sup> "Direct <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>" reaches a site without being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">scattered</a> within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>. Light that is scattered in the atmosphere is  <i><a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">diffused daylight</a></i>. Ground reflected light also contributes to the daylight. Each climate has different composition of these daylights and different <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud coverage</a>, so daylighting strategies vary with site locations and climates. There is no direct sunlight on the polar-side wall (north-facing wall in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and south-facing wall in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>) of a building from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" title="Autumnal equinox">autumnal equinox</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_equinox" title="Spring equinox">spring equinox</a> at latitudes north of the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and south of the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Science_of_Renewable_Energy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Science_of_Renewable_Energy-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Traditionally, houses were designed with minimal windows on the polar side, but more and larger windows on the equatorial-side (south-facing wall in the Northern Hemisphere and north-facing wall in the Southern Hemisphere). Equatorial-side windows receive at least some direct sunlight on any sunny day of the year (except in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summertime</a>), so they are effective at daylighting areas of the house adjacent to the windows. In  higher latitudes during <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">midwinter</a>, light incidence is highly directional and casts long shadows. This may be partially ameliorated through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_diffusion" title="Light diffusion">light diffusion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light_tube" title="Light tube">light pipes or tubes</a>, and through somewhat reflective internal surfaces. In fairly low latitudes in summertime, windows that face east and west and sometimes those that face toward the pole receive more sunlight than windows facing toward the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Science_of_Renewable_Energy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Science_of_Renewable_Energy-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" title="Window">Window</a></div>\n<p>Windows are the most common way to admit daylight into a space. Their vertical orientation means that they selectively admit sunlight and diffuse daylight at different times of the day and year. Therefore, windows on multiple orientations must usually be combined to produce the right mix of light for the building, depending on the climate and latitude. There are three ways to improve the amount of light available from a window:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Building_for_Energy_Independence:_Sun/Earth_Buffering_and_Superinsulation_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Building_for_Energy_Independence:_Sun/Earth_Buffering_and_Superinsulation-3">[3]</a></sup> (a) placing the window close to a light colored wall, (b) slanting the sides of window openings so the inner opening is larger than the outer opening, or (c) using a large light colored window-sill to project light into the room.\n</p><p>Different types and grades of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and different window treatments can also affect the amount of light transmission through the windows. The type of <a href="/wiki/Glazing_(window)" title="Glazing (window)">glazing</a> is an important issue, expressed by its VT coefficient (Visual Transmittance),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> also known as visual light transmittance (VLT). As the name suggests, this coefficient measures how much visible light is admitted by the window. A low VT (below 0.4) can reduce by half or more the light coming into a room. But be also aware of high VT glass: high VT numbers (say, above 0.60) can be a cause of glare. On the other hand, you should also take into account the undesirable effects of large windows.\n</p><p>Windows grade into translucent walls (below).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clerestory_windows">Clerestory windows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clerestory windows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Another important element in creating daylighting is the use of <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">clerestory</a> windows. These are high, vertically placed windows. They can be used to increase direct solar gain when oriented towards the equator. When facing toward the sun, clerestories and other windows may admit unacceptable <a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">glare</a>. In the case of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">passive solar</a> house, clerestories may provide a direct light path to polar-side (north in the northern hemisphere; south in the southern hemisphere) rooms that otherwise would not be illuminated. Alternatively, clerestories can be used to admit diffuse daylight (from the north in the northern hemisphere) that evenly illuminates a space such as a classroom or office.\n</p><p>Often, clerestory windows also shine onto interior wall surfaces painted white or another light color. These walls are placed so as to reflect indirect light to interior areas where it is needed. This method has the advantage of reducing the directionality of light to make it softer and more diffuse, reducing shadows.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sawtooth_roof">Sawtooth roof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sawtooth roof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Another roof-angled glass alternative is a <a href="/wiki/Saw-tooth_roof" title="Saw-tooth roof">sawtooth roof</a> (found on older factories). Sawtooth roofs have vertical roof glass facing away from the equator side of the building to capture diffused light (not harsh direct equator-side solar gain).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LG10_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-LG10-5">[5]</a></sup> The angled portion of the glass-support structure is opaque and well insulated with a cool roof and radiant barrier. The sawtooth roof\'s lighting concept partially reduces the summer "solar furnace" skylight problem, but still allows warm interior air to rise and touch the exterior roof glass in the cold winter, with significant undesirable heat transfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skylights">Skylights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Skylights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1338" data-file-width="1800" decoding="async" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg/180px-Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg/270px-Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg/360px-Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Skylight_w_cuppola.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern <a href="/wiki/Skylight" title="Skylight">skylight</a></div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Skylight" title="Skylight">Skylight</a></div>\n<p>Skylights are light transmitting fenestration (products filling openings in a building envelope which also includes windows, doors, etc.) forming all, or a portion of, the roof of a building space. Skylights are widely used in daylighting design in residential and commercial buildings, mainly because they are the most effective source of daylight on a unit area basis.\n</p><p>\nAn alternative to a skylight is a <a href="/wiki/Roof_lantern" title="Roof lantern">roof lantern</a>. A roof lantern is a daylighting <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">cupola</a> that sits above a roof, as opposed to a skylight which is fitted into a roof\'s construction. Roof lanterns serve as both an architectural feature and a method of introducing natural light into a space, and are typically wooden or metal structures with a number of glazed glass panels.</p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atrium">Atrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(architecture)" title="Atrium (architecture)">Atrium (architecture)</a></div>\n<p>An atrium is a large open space located within a building. It is often used to light a central circulation or public area by daylight admitted through a glass roof or wall. Atria provide some daylight to adjacent working areas, but the amount is often small and does not penetrate very far.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook-7">[7]</a></sup> The main function of an <a href="/wiki/Atrium_(architecture)" title="Atrium (architecture)">atrium</a> is to provide a visual experience and a degree of contact with the outside for people in the working areas. The daylighting of successive storeys of rooms adjoining an atrium is interdependent and requires a balanced approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daylighting_in_atrium_buildings_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daylighting_in_atrium_buildings-8">[8]</a></sup> Light from the sky can easily penetrate the upper storeys but not the lower, which rely primarily on light reflected from internal surfaces of the atrium such as floor-reflected light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The upper stories need less window area than the lower ones, and if the atrium walls are light in color the upper walls will reflect light toward the lower stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LG10_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-LG10-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span made of <a href="/wiki/Glass_brick" title="Glass brick">glass brick</a> are translucent to transparent. Traditionally, they are hollow and <a href="/wiki/Grout" title="Grout">grouted</a> with a fine concrete grout, but some modern glass brick walls are solid cast glass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> grouted with a transparent glue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> If the glue matches the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of the glass, the wall can be fairly transparent.\n</p><p>Increasing the amount of concrete, bottle walls embed bottles that run right through the wall, transmitting light. Concrete walls with glass prisms running through them have also been made.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With the advent of cheaper <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fibers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Translucent_concrete" title="Translucent concrete">fiber-optic concrete</a> walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Daylight (and shadow images) can then pass directly through a solid concrete wall, making it translucent; fiber optics will lead light around bends and over tens of meters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Typically only a few percent of the light is transmitted (the percent transmittance is about half the percent of the surface that is fibers, and usually only ~5% fibers are used).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both glass and concrete conduct heat fairly well, when solid, so none of these walls <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insulation_value" title="Insulation value">insulate well</a>. They are therefore often used outdoors, as a divider between two heated spaces (see images), or in very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_climates" title="Temperate climates">temperate climates</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">Greenhouse</a> walls (and rooves) are made to transmit as much light and as little heat as possible. They use a variety of materials, and may be transparent or translucent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_distribution">Remote distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Remote distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>See <a href="/wiki/Anidolic_lighting" title="Anidolic lighting">anidolic lighting</a></i>\n</p><p>It is possible to provide some daylight into spaces that have low possibility of windows or skylights through remote distribution devices such as mirrors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prism_(optics)" title="Prism (optics)">prisms</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Light_tube" title="Light tube">light tubes</a>. This is called <a href="/wiki/Anidolic_lighting" title="Anidolic lighting">anidolic lighting</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Nonimaging_optics" title="Nonimaging optics">anidolic (non-image-forming) optics</a>. The non-linear response of the human eye to light means that spreading light to a broader area of a room makes the room appear brighter, and makes more of it usefully lit.\n</p><p>Remote daylight distribution systems have losses, and the further they have to transmit the daylight and the more convoluted the path, the greater the inefficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The efficiency of many remote distribution systems can also vary dramatically from clear to overcast skies. Nonetheless, where there is no other possibility of providing daylight to a space, remote distribution systems can be appreciated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_reflectors_and_shelves">Light reflectors and shelves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Early thick, slow-cooling cast glass prism tiles were often known as "luxfer tiles" after a major manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glassian_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-glassian-19">[19]</a></sup> They were and are used in the upper portions of windows, and some believe that they contributed to the trend from dark, subdivided Victorian interiors to open-plan, light-coloured ones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. placed into a roof and admits light to a focused area of the interior. These somewhat resemble recessed ceiling light fixtures. They do not allow as much heat transfer as skylights because they have less surface area.\n</p><p>TDDs use modern technology to transmit visible light through opaque walls and roofs. The tube itself is a passive component consisting of either a simple reflective interior coating or a light conducting fiber optic bundle. It is frequently capped with a transparent, roof-mounted dome "light collector" and terminated with a diffuser assembly that admits the daylight into interior spaces and distributes the available light energy evenly (or else efficiently if the use of the lit space is reasonably fixed, and the user desired one or more "bright-spots").\n</p><p>The tubular daylighting device was invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solatube" title="Solatube">Solatube International</a> in 1986 and brought to market first in Australia in 1991.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2017)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Daylighting#Dubious" title="Talk:Daylighting">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smart_glass">Smart glass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Smart glass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Smart_glass" title="Smart glass">Smart glass</a></div>\n<p>Smart glass is the name given to a class of materials and devices that can be switched between a transparent state and a state which is opaque, translucent, reflective, or retro-reflective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The switching is done by applying a voltage to the material, or by performing some simple mechanical operation. Windows, skylights, etc., that are made of smart glass can be used to adjust indoor lighting, compensating for changes of the brightness of the light outdoors and of the required brightness indoors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_lighting">Solar lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span id="Heliostats">Heliostats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Heliostats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Heliostat" title="Heliostat">heliostats</a>, mirrors which are moved automatically to reflect sunlight in a constant direction as the sun moves across the sky, is gaining popularity as an energy-efficient method of lighting. A heliostat can be used to shine sunlight directly through a window or skylight, or into any arrangement of optical elements, such as light tubes, that distribute the light where it is needed. The image shows a mirror that rotates on a computer-controlled, motor-driven <a href="/wiki/Altazimuth_mount" title="Altazimuth mount">altazimuth mount</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_street_lights">Solar street lights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Solar street lights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Solar_street_light" title="Solar street light">Solar street lights</a> raised light sources which are powered by photovoltaic panels generally mounted on the lighting structure. The solar array of such off-grid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PV_system" title="PV system">PV system</a> charges a <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable battery</a>, which powers a fluorescent or <a href="/wiki/LED_lamp" title="LED lamp">LED lamp</a> during the night. Solar street lights are <a href="/wiki/Stand-alone_power_system" title="Stand-alone power system">stand-alone power systems</a>, and have the advantage of savings on trenching, landscaping, and maintenance costs, as well as on the electric bills, despite their higher initial cost compared to conventional street lighting. They are designed with sufficiently large batteries to ensure operation for at least a week and even in the worst situation, they are expected to dim only slightly.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_solar_lighting">Hybrid solar lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hybrid solar lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_solar_lighting" title="Hybrid solar lighting">Hybrid solar lighting</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> (ORNL) has developed a new alternative to skylights called hybrid solar lighting. This design uses a roof-mounted light collector, large-diameter <a href="/wiki/Light_tube#Optical_fiber" title="Light tube">optical fiber</a>, and modified efficient fluorescent lighting fixtures that have transparent rods connected to the optical fiber cables. Essentially no electricity is needed for daytime natural interior lighting.\n</p><p>Field tests conducted in 2006 and 2007 of the new HSL technology were promising, but the low-volume equipment production is still expensive. HSL should become more cost effective in the near future. A version that can withstand windstorms could begin to replace conventional commercial fluorescent lighting systems with improved implementations in 2008 and beyond. The U.S. 2007 Energy Bill provides funding for HSL R&amp;D, and multiple large commercial buildings are ready to fund further HSL application development and deployment.\n</p><p>At night, ORNL HSL uses variable-intensity fluorescent lighting electronic control ballasts. As the sunlight gradually decreases at sunset, the fluorescent fixture is gradually turned up to give a near-constant level of interior lighting from daylight until after it becomes dark outside.\n</p><p>HSL may soon become an option for commercial interior lighting. It can transmit about half of the direct sunlight it receives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hybrid_lighting_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-hybrid_lighting-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solarium">Solarium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Solarium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sunroom" title="Sunroom">Sunroom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">Greenhouse</a></div>\n<p>In a well-designed isolated solar gain building with a solarium, sunroom, greenhouse, etc., there is usually significant glass on the equator side. A large area of glass can also be added between the sun room and the interior living quarters. Low-cost, high-volume-produced patio door safety glass is an inexpensive way to accomplish this goal.\n</p><p>The doors used to enter a room should be opposite the sun room interior glass, so that a user can see outside immediately when entering most rooms. Halls should be minimized with open spaces used instead. If a hall is necessary for privacy or room isolation, inexpensive patio door safety glass can be placed on both sides of the hall. Drapes over the interior glass can be used to control lighting. Drapes can optionally be automated with sensor-based electric motor controls that are aware of room occupancy, daylight, interior temperature, and time of day. Passive solar buildings with no central air conditioning system need control mechanisms for hourly, daily, and seasonal, temperature-and-daylight variations. If the temperature is correct, and a room is unoccupied, the drapes can automatically close to reduce heat transfer in either direction.\n</p><p>To help distribute sun room daylight to the sides of rooms that are farthest from the equator, inexpensive ceiling-to-floor mirrors can be used.\n</p><p>Building codes require a second means of egress, in case of fire. Most designers use a door on one side of bedrooms, and an outside window, but west-side windows provide very-poor summer thermal performance. Instead of a west-facing window, designers use an R-13 foam-filled solid energy-efficient exterior door. It may have a glass storm door on the outside so that light can pass through when the inner door is opened. East/west glass doors and windows should be fully shaded top-to-bottom or a spectrally selective coating can be used to reduce solar gain.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Architects and interior designers often use daylighting as one of design elements. Good daylighting requires attention to both qualitative and quantitative aspects of design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-The_SLL_Lighting_Handbook-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualitative">Qualitative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Qualitative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Utilizing natural light is one of the design aspects in architecture; In 1929, the French architect, <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> said that "The history of architectural material... has been the endless struggle for light... in other words, the history of windows." As he emphasized in his architecture (such as <a href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_du_Haut" title="Notre Dame du Haut">Notre Dame du Haut</a>), daylighting has been a major architectural design elements (See <a href="/wiki/MIT_Chapel" title="MIT Chapel">MIT Chapel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Light" title="Church of the Light">Church of the Light</a> for examples). Not only the aesthetic aspects, the impact of daylighting on <a href="/wiki/Healthy_building" title="Healthy building">human health</a> and work performance is also considered as qualitative daylighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The current studies show that lighting conditions in workplaces contribute to a variety of factors related to work satisfaction, productivity and well-being and significantly higher visual acceptance scores under daylighting than electrical lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Studies have also shown that light has a direct effect on human health because of the way it influences the <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantitative">Quantitative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Quantitative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A well daylit space needs both adequate lighting levels and light that is well distributed. In the current building industry, daylighting is considered a building performance measure in green building certification programs such as <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a>. Illumination Engineering Society (IES) and the society of Light and Lighting (SLL) provide illuminance recommendation for each space type. How much daylighting contributes to the recommended lighting level determines daylighting performance of a building. There are two metrics that IES has approved to evaluate daylighting performance:  Spatial Daylight Autonomy(sDA) and Annual Sunlight Exposure (ASE). sDA is a metric describing annual sufficiency of ambient daylight levels in interior environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM-83-12_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-LM-83-12-27">[27]</a></sup> See Daylight autonomy and LEED documentation sections for more details.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation_method">Evaluation method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Evaluation method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Field_measurements">Field measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Field measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In existing buildings, field measurements can be undertaken to evaluate daylighting performance. <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">Illuminance</a> measurements on a grid is a basic level to derive an average illuminance of a space. The spacing of the measurement points vary with project purposes. The height of these points depends on where the primary task is performed. In most office spaces, desk level (0.762m above the floor) will be measured. Based on measurements, average illuminance, maximum-to-minimum uniformity ratio, and average-to-minimum uniformity ratio will be calculated and compared to the recommended lighting level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Performance_Measurement_Protocols_for_Commercial_Buildings_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Performance_Measurement_Protocols_for_Commercial_Buildings-28">[28]</a></sup>  A diagnostic survey specific to lighting can be conducted to analyse the satisfaction of building occupants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Performance_Measurement_Protocols_for_Commercial_Buildings_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Performance_Measurement_Protocols_for_Commercial_Buildings-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_simulations">Computational simulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Computational simulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Computational simulations can predict daylighting condition of a space much faster and more detailed than hand calculations or scale model testing.  The simulations allow for the effects of climate with hourly weather data from <a href="/wiki/Typical_meteorological_year" title="Typical meteorological year">typical meteorological year</a>. Computer models are available which can predict variations in internally reflected light. <a href="/wiki/Radiosity_(computer_graphics)" title="Radiosity (computer graphics)">Radiosity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray-tracing" title="Ray-tracing">ray-tracing</a> are methods can deal with complex geometry, allow complex sky distributions and potentially produce photorealistic images. Radiosity methods assume all surfaces are perfectly diffusing to reduce computational times. Ray-tracing techniques have accuracy and image rendering capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LG10_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-LG10-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomy">Autonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Autonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Daylight autonomy is the percentage of time that daylight levels are above a specified target <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> within a physical space or building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinhart_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reinhart-29">[29]</a></sup> The calculation is based on annual data and the predetermined lighting levels. The goal of the calculation is to determine how long an individual can work in a space without requiring electrical lighting, while also providing optimal visual and physical comfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinhart_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reinhart-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Daylight autonomy is beneficial when determining how daylight enters and illuminates a space. The drawback, however, is that there is no upper limit on <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">luminance</a> levels. Therefore, a space with a high internal heat gain deemed uncomfortable by occupants, would still perform well in the analysis. Achieving daylight autonomy requires an <a href="/wiki/Integrated_design" title="Integrated design">integrated design</a> approach that guides the building form, siting, climate considerations, building components, lighting controls, and lighting design criteria.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous">Continuous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Continuous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Continuous daylight autonomy, is similar to daylight autonomy but partial credit is attributed to time steps when the daylight illuminance lies below the minimum illuminance level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup> For example, if the target illuminance is 400 <a href="/wiki/Lux" title="Lux">lux</a> and the calculated value is 200 lux, daylight autonomy would give zero credit, while continuous daylight autonomy would give 0.5 credit (200/400 = 0.5). The benefit of continuous daylight autonomy is that it does not give a hard threshold of acceptable illuminance. Instead, it addresses the transition area&#8212;allowing for realistic preferences within any given space. For example, office occupants usually prefer to work at daylight below the illuminance threshold since this level avoids potential <a href="/wiki/Glare_(vision)" title="Glare (vision)">glare</a> and excessive contrast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Useful_illuminance">Useful illuminance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Useful illuminance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Useful daylight illuminance focuses on the direct sunlight that falls into a space. The useful daylight illuminance calculation is based on three factors&#8212;the percentage of time a point is below, between, or above an illuminance value. The range for these factors is typically 100-2,000 lux. Useful daylight illuminance is similar to daylight autonomy but has the added benefit of addressing glare and thermal discomfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The upper threshold is used to determine when glare or thermal discomfort is occurring and may need resolution.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_documentation">LEED documentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daylighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: LEED documentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> 2009 daylighting standards were intended to connect building occupants with the outdoors through use of optimal daylighting techniques and technologies. According to these standards, the maximum value of 1 point can be achieved through four different approaches.  The first approach is a computer simulation to demonstrate, in clear sky conditions, the daylight illuminance levels 108-5,400 lux on, September 21 between 9:00&#160;a.m. and 3:00&#160;p.m. Another prescriptive approach is a method that uses two types of side-lighting, and three types of top-lighting to determine if a minimum of 75% daylighting is achieved in the occupied spaces. A third approach uses indoor light measurements showing that between 108-5,400 lux have been achieved in the space. The last approach is a combination of the other three calculation methods to prove that the daylight illumination requirements are achieved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The LEED 2009 documentation is based upon the <a href="/wiki/Daylight_factor" title="Daylight factor">daylight factor</a> calculation. The daylight factor calculation is based on uniform overcast skies. It is most applicable in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IESNA_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-IESNA-33">[33]</a></sup> Daylight factor is &#8220;the ratio of the illuminance at a point on a plane, generally the horizontal work plane, produced by the luminous flux received directly or indirectly at that point from a sky whose luminance distribution is known, to the illuminance on a horizontal plane produced by an unobstructed hemisphere of this same sky."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IESNA_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-IESNA-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>LEED v4 daylighting standards are the most current as of 2014. The new standards are similar to the old standards, but also intend to &#8220;reinforce circadian rhythms, and reduce the use of electrical lighting by introducing daylight in the space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGBCV4_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGBCV4-34">[34]</a></sup> Two options exist for achieving the maximum value of these two most recent points.  One option is to use a computer simulation to demonstrate that a spatial daylight autonomy of 300 lux for at least 50% of the time, and an annual sunlight exposure of 1,000 lux for 250 occupied hours per year, exists in the space. Another option is to show that illuminance levels are between 300 lux and 3,000 lux between 9:00&#160;a.m. and 3:00&#160;p.m. on a clear day at the equinox for 75% or 90% of the floor area in the space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGBCV4_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGBCV4-34">[34]</a></sup> The overall goal of the LEED v4 daylighting metrics is to analyze both the quantity and quality of the light, as well as to balance the use of glazing to ensure more light and less cooling load.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
