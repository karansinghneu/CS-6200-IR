href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">unit of length</a> used to express <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_distance" title="Astronomical distance">astronomical distances</a> and measures about 9.46 <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">trillion</a> <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a> (9.46 x 10<sup>12</sup> km) or 5.88 trillion <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a> (5.88 x 10<sup>12</sup> mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[note 1]</a></sup> As defined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU), a light-year is the distance that <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">light travels in vacuum</a> in one <a href="/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy)" title="Julian year (astronomy)">Julian year</a> (365.25 days).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUgen_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAUgen-2">[2]</a></sup> Because it includes the word "<a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>", the term light-year is sometimes misinterpreted as a unit of time.\n</p><p>The light-year is most often used when expressing distances to stars and other distances on a <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galactic</a> scale, especially in nonspecialist and <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> publications. The unit most commonly used in professional <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometry</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a> (symbol: pc, about 3.26 light-years; the distance at which one <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a> <a href="/wiki/Subtended_angle" title="Subtended angle">subtends</a> an angle of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_of_arc" title="Second of arc">second of arc</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUgen_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-IAUgen-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div The star was <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">61 Cygni</a>, and he used a 6.2-inch (160&#160;mm) <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">heliometer</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">Joseph von Fraunhofer</a>. The largest unit for expressing distances across space at that time was the astronomical unit, equal to the radius of the Earth\'s orbit <span class="nowrap">(<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011150000000000000&#9824;</span>1.50<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>8</sup>&#160;km</span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7011149668992000000&#9824;</span>9.30<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>7</sup>&#160;mi</span>).</span> In those terms, trigonometric calculations based on 61 Cygni\'s <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> of 0.314 arcseconds, showed the distance to the star to be <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005660000000000000&#9824;</span>660<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> astronomical units (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016990000000000000&#9824;</span>9.9<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>13</sup>&#160;km</span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016981699840000000&#9824;</span>6.1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>13</sup>&#160;mi</span>). Bessel added that light employs 10.3 years to traverse this distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup> He recognized that his readers would enjoy the mental picture of the approximate transit time for light, but he refrained from using the light-year as a unit. He may have resented expressing distances in light-years because it would deteriorate the accuracy of his parallax data due to multiplying with the uncertain parameter of the speed of light. The speed of light was not yet precisely known in 1838; its value changed in 1849 (<a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau" title="Hippolyte Fizeau">Fizeau</a>) and 1862 (<a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault" title="L&#233;on Foucault">Foucault</a>). It was not yet considered to be a fundamental constant of nature, and the propagation of light through the <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">aether</a> or space was still enigmatic. The light-year unit appeared, however, in 1851 in a German popular astronomical article by <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Eduard_Vincenz_Ule" title="de:Otto Eduard Vincenz Ule">Otto Ule</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup> The paradox of a distance unit name ending on "year"\' was explained by Ule by comparing it to a hiking road hour (<i>Wegstunde</i>). A contemporary German popular astronomical book also noticed that light-year is an odd name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup> In 1868 an English journal labelled the light-year as a unit used by the Germans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Eddington</a> called the light-year an inconvenient and irrelevant unit, which had sometimes crept from popular use into technical investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although modern astronomers often prefer to use the <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a>, light years are also popularly used to gauge the expanses of interstellar and intergalactic space.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_term">Usage of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light-year&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Distances expressed in light-years include those between <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in the same general area, such as those belonging to the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_arm" title="Spiral arm">spiral arm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a>. However, smaller units of length can similarly be formed usefully by multiplying units of time by the speed of light. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Light-second" title="Light-second">light-second</a>, useful in astronomy, telecommunications and relativistic physics, is exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7008299792458000000&#9824;</span>299<span style="margin-left:.25em;">792</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">458</span></span> metres or &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7007315576000000000&#9824;</span>31<span style="margin-left:.25em;">557</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">600</span></span></sub></span> of a light-year. Units such as the light-minute, light-hour and light-day are sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> publications. The light-month, roughly one-twelfth of a light-year, is also used occasionally for approximate measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayden_Planetarium" title="Hayden Planetarium">Hayden Planetarium</a> specifies the light month more precisely as 30 days of light travel time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Light travels approximately one <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">foot</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Nanosecond" title="Nanosecond">nanosecond</a>; the term "light-foot" is sometimes used as an informal measure of time.<sup
