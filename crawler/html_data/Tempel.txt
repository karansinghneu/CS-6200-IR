2022</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Tempel 1</b> (official designation: <b>9P/Tempel</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_periodic_comets" title="List of periodic comets">periodic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter-family_comet" title="Jupiter-family comet">Jupiter-family</a> <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> discovered by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Tempel" title="Wilhelm Tempel">Wilhelm Tempel</a> in 1867. It completes an orbit of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> every 5.5 years. Tempel 1 was the target of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(spacecraft)" title="Deep Impact (spacecraft)"><i>Deep Impact</i></a> space mission, which photographed a deliberate high-speed impact upon the comet in 2005. It was re-visited by the <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)"><i>Stardust</i></a> spacecraft on February 14, 2011 and came back to perihelion in August 2016.\n</p>\n<div At the time of discovery, it approached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> once every 5.68 years (designations 9P/1867 G1 and 1867 II). It was subsequently observed in 1873 (9P/1873 G1, 1873 I, 1873a) and in 1879 (1879 III, 1879b).\n</p><p>Photographic attempts during 1898 and 1905 failed to recover the comet, and astronomers surmised that it had <a href="/wiki/Lost_comet" title="Lost comet">disintegrated</a>. In fact, its orbit had changed. Tempel 1\'s orbit occasionally brings it sufficiently close to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> to be altered, with a consequent change in the comet\'s orbital period. This occurred in 1881 (closest approach to Jupiter of 0.55 AU), lengthening the orbital period to 6.5 years. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">Perihelion</a> also changed, increasing by 50 million kilometres, rendering the comet far less visible from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comet_Temple_1_detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="245" decoding="async" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Comet_Temple_1_detail.jpg/220px-Comet_Temple_1_detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Comet_Temple_1_detail.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comet_Temple_1_detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of crater-like features on Comet Tempel 1 in image taken by Deep Impact\'s impactor</div></div></div>\n<p>Tempel 1 was rediscovered 13 orbits later in 1967 (as 9P/1967 L1, 1966 VII), after British astronomer <a href="/wiki/Brian_G._Marsden" title="Brian G. Marsden">Brian G. Marsden</a> performed precise calculations of the comet\'s orbit that took into account Jupiter\'s <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a>. Marsden found that further close approaches to Jupiter in 1941 (0.41 AU) and 1953 (0.77 AU) had decreased both the perihelion distance and the orbital period to values smaller than when the comet was initially discovered (5.84 and 5.55 years, respectively). These approaches moved Tempel 1 into its present <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">libration</a> around the 1:2 resonance with Jupiter. Despite an unfavorable 1967 return, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Roemer" title="Elizabeth Roemer">Elizabeth Roemer</a> of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Catalina_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Catalina Observatory (page does not exist)">Catalina Observatory</a> took several photographs. Initial inspection revealed nothing, but in late 1968 she found a June 8, 1967 exposure (Tempel 1 had passed perihelion in January) that held the image of an 18th magnitude diffuse object very close to where Marsden had predicted the comet to be. A single image does not allow an orbit computation, so the next return had to be awaited.\n</p><p>\nRoemer and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L._M._Vaughn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L. M. Vaughn (page does not exist)">L. M. Vaughn</a> recovered the comet on January 11, 1972, from <a href="/wiki/Steward_Observatory" title="Steward Observatory">Steward Observatory</a> (9P/1972 A1, 1972 V, 1972a). The comet became widely observed, reached a maximum brightness of magnitude 11 during May, and was last seen on July 10. Since that time the comet has been seen at every apparition, in 1978 (1978 II, 1977i), 1983 (1983 XI, 1982j), 1989 (1989 I, 1987e1), 1994 (1994 XIUX, 1993c), 2000 and 2005. Its orbital period is 5.515 years.</p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tempel_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA02118.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="458" decoding="async" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/PIA02118.jpg/220px-PIA02118.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/PIA02118.jpg/330px-PIA02118.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/PIA02118.jpg/440px-PIA02118.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA02118.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tempel 1 in X-ray light by <a href="/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory" title="Chandra X-ray Observatory">Chandra</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Tempel 1 is not a bright comet; its brightest <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> since discovery has been 11, far below naked-eye visibility. Its nucleus measures 7.6&#160;km &#215;&#160;4.9&#160;km (4.7&#160;mi &#215;&#160;3.0&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tempel1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tempel1-3">[3]</a></sup> Measurements taken by the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> in visible light and the <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">Spitzer Space Telescope</a> in infrared light suggest a low <a href="/wiki/Albedo" The low-cost mission utilized the already existing <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)"><i>Stardust</i> spacecraft</a>, which had studied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Wild_2" title="Comet Wild 2">Comet Wild 2</a> in 2004. Stardust was placed into a new orbit so that it approached Tempel 1. It passed at a distance of approximately 181&#160;km (112&#160;mi) on February 15, 2011, 04:42 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This was the first time that a comet was visited twice.\n</p><p>On February 15, NASA scientists identified the crater formed by <i>Deep Impact</i> in images from <i>Stardust</i>. This kinetic energy is generated by the combination of the mass of the impactor (370 kg; 816 lbs) and its velocity when it impacts (~10.2 km/s)". Also according to NASA, <a class="external text" href="https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/deepimpact/spacecraft/index.html" rel="nofollow">Expected crater dimensions</a> "The energy from the impact will excavate a crater approximately 100m wide and 28m deep". (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a> on November 11, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-11">[11]</a></sup>  Then, as a Jupiter-family comet, it will spend
