<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin"><b>H&#224;n H&#250;an D&#236;</b></span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile"><b>Han Huan-ti</b></span></i>; 132 &#8211; 25 January 168)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was the 27th <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">emperor</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> after he was enthroned by the Empress Dowager and her brother Liang Ji on 1 August 146.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was a great-grandson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Zhang_of_Han_China" title="Emperor Zhang of Han China">Emperor Zhang</a>.\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Zhi_of_Han" title="Emperor Zhi of Han">Emperor Zhi</a> was poisoned to death by the powerful official <a href="/wiki/Liang_Ji" title="Liang Ji">Liang Ji</a> in 146, Liang Ji persuaded his sister, the regent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Liang_Na" title="Empress Liang Na">Empress Dowager Liang</a> to make the 14-year-old Liu Zhi, the Marquess of Liwu, who was betrothed to their sister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Liang_N%C3%BCying" title="Empress Liang N&#252;ying">Liang N&#252;ying</a> (&#26753;&#22899;&#29801;), emperor.  As the years went by, Emperor Huan, offended by Liang Ji\'s autocratic and violent nature, became determined to eliminate the Liang family with the help of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eunuch_(court_official)" title="Eunuch (court official)">eunuchs</a>.  Emperor Huan succeeded in removing Liang Ji in 159 but this only caused an increase in the influence of <a href="/wiki/Ten_Attendants" title="Ten Attendants">these eunuchs</a> over all aspects of the government. Corruption during this period had reached a boiling point. In 166, university students rose up in protest against the government and called on Emperor Huan to eliminate all corrupt officials. Instead of listening, Emperor Huan ordered the arrest of all students involved. Emperor Huan has largely been viewed as an emperor who might have had some intelligence but lacked wisdom in governing his empire; and his reign contributed greatly to the downfall of the Eastern Han Dynasty.\n</p><p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hou_Hanshu" title="Hou Hanshu">Hou Hanshu</a></i> (<i>History of the Later Han</i>) recounts that one <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">Roman envoy</a> (perhaps sent by emperor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>) reached the Chinese capital <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a> in 166 and was greeted by Emperor Huan.\n</p><p>Emperor Huan died in 168 after reigning for 22 years; he was 36. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Huan_of_Han&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family background and ascension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Liu Zhi was born in 132, to Liu Yi (&#21129;&#32764;), the Marquess of Liwu, and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concubine" title="Concubine">concubine</a> <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager_Yan_Ming" title="Empress Dowager Yan Ming">Yan Ming</a> (&#21309;&#26126;).\n</p><p>Liu Yi was the son of Liu Kai (&#21129;&#38283;) the Prince of Hejian (and therefore a grandson of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Zhang_of_Han" title="Emperor Zhang of Han">Emperor Zhang</a>), and he had initially been made the Prince of Pingyuan as the heir of his cousin Liu Sheng (&#21129;&#21213;) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Deng_Sui" title="Empress Deng Sui">Empress Dowager Deng Sui</a>, the regent for <a href="/wiki/Emperor_An_of_Han" title="Emperor An of Han">Emperor An</a>, who was impressed with his abilities.  That led to rumors that Empress Deng was looking to replace Emperor An, Prince Yi\'s cousin, with Prince Yi.  After Empress Dowager Deng died in 121, Emperor An, bearing a grudge against Prince Yi, demoted him to the rank of Marquess of Duxiang and exiled him to his father\'s principality.  During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Shun_of_Han" title="Emperor Shun of Han">Emperor Shun</a>, Prince Kai requested that he be allowed to give Liwu County, part of his principality, to his son, and Emperor Shun permitted it. Marquess Yi thus became the Marquess of Liwu.\n</p><p>By 146, Liu Zhi had inherited his father\'s title and was betrothed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Liang_N%C3%BCying" title="Empress Liang N&#252;ying">Liang N&#252;ying</a>, the younger sister of the regent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Liang_Na" title="Empress Liang Na">Empress Dowager Liang Na</a> and her violent and corrupt brother, Grand Marshal <a href="/wiki/Liang_Ji" title="Liang Ji">Liang Ji</a>.  That year, Liang Ji, bearing a grudge against the eight-year-old <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Zhi_of_Han" title="Emperor Zhi of Han">Emperor Zhi</a> for calling him an "arrogant general," murdered the young emperor by poison.  The officials largely favored Emperor Zhi\'s first cousin Liu Suan (&#21129;&#33948;) the Prince of Qinghe, who was described as a solemn and proper man.  Prince Suan\'s age was not given in history, though he appeared to be an adult by this time.  However, Liang Ji was hesitant to yield authority to an able emperor. Because Marquess Zhi was betrothed to his sister and relatively young, Liang Ji felt that he could control him and insisted on making him emperor.  Marquess Zhi took the throne later that year as Emperor Huan.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Early_reign:_under_Liang_Ji.27s_shadow"></span><span class="mw-headline" &#25171;&#34382;&#20141;&#27721;&#22675;, Pinyin: <i>Dahuting Han mu</i>) of the late <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Han_dynasty" title="History of the Han dynasty">Eastern Han Dynasty</a> (25-220 AD), located in <a href="/wiki/Zhengzhou" title="Zhengzhou">Zhengzhou</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henan_province" title="Henan province">Henan province</a>, China</div></div></div>\n<p>After Emperor Huan\'s ascension at age 14, Empress Dowager Liang continued to serve as regent.  However, her brother Liang Ji came to take on more and more control, even more than the empress dowager.  Emperor Huan posthumously honored his grandfather and father as emperors but, because the empress dowager was regent, did not honor his mother Yan Ming as an empress dowager; rather, she was given the title of an imperial consort.  His father\'s wife, Lady Ma, was belatedly honored as an imperial consort as well in 148.  In 147, he married Empress Dowager Liang and Liang Ji\'s sister Liang N&#252;ying, making her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress" title="Empress">empress</a>.  It appeared that, while the Liangs were in control, Emperor Huan was not a complete puppet. Instead, as a sign of bad things to come, he trusted eunuchs in his decision-making.\n</p><p>That same year, Liang Ji, in conjunction with the eunuchs Tang Heng (&#21776;&#34913;) and Zuo Guan (&#24038;&#24762;), but with Emperor Huan\'s clear approval, falsely accused the honest officials Li Gu (&#26446;&#22266;) and Du Qiao (&#26460;&#21932;) of conspiring to overthrow Emperor Huan and replace him with Prince Suan.  Li and Du were executed, while Prince Suan was demoted to marquess status and committed suicide.\n</p><p>In 150, Empress Dowager Liang announced that she was retiring and returning imperial authority to Emperor Huan.  Later that year, she died.  Emperor Huan then honored his mother as an empress dowager.  However, without his sister to curb his power, Liang Ji came to be even more powerful than before.  His violence and corruption increased and he stamped out all dissent with threats of death.  He even threw his humble and peace-loving brother Liang Buyi (&#26753;&#19981;&#30097;) out of the government.\n</p><p>In 152, Empress Dowager Yan died.  Because Emperor Huan had inherited the throne through a collateral line, he was not permitted by customs to be the mourner. Instead, his brother Liu Shi (&#21129;&#30707;) the Prince of Pingyuan served as chief mourner.\n</p><p>In 153, the first major public confrontation between an official and a powerful eunuch occurred&#8212;foreshadowing many to come.  Zhu Mu (&#26417;&#31302;), the governor of Ji Province (modern central and northern <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>) had found out that the father of the powerful eunuch Zhao Zhong (&#36249;&#24544;) had been improperly buried in a jade vest, an honor that was reserved to imperial princes. He ordered an investigation:  Zhao\'s father was exhumed and the jade vest was stripped away, an act that angered Zhao and Emperor Huan.  Zhu was not only removed from his post but was sentenced to hard labor.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Coup_d.27etat_against_Liang_Ji"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Coup_d\'etat_against_Liang_Ji">Coup d\'etat against Liang Ji</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Huan_of_Han&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coup d\'etat against Liang Ji">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the years went by, Emperor Huan became increasingly disgruntled at Liang Ji\'s control of the government and was also angered by Empress Liang\'s behavior.  Because of her position as Empress Dowager Liang and Liang Ji\'s sister, Empress Liang was wasteful in her luxurious living, far exceeding any past empress, and was exceedingly jealous.  She did not have a son and, because she did not want any other imperial consorts to have sons, she would find some way to murder pregnant consorts.  Emperor Huan did not dare to react to her due to Liang Ji\'s power, but rarely had sexual relations with her.  In 159, angry and depressed that she had lost her husband\'s favor, Empress Liang died.\n</p><p>This death started a chain of events that led to Liang Ji\'s downfall later that year.  Liang, in order to continue to control Emperor Huan, had adopted his wife\'s beautiful cousin (the stepdaughter of her uncle Liang Ji (&#26753;&#32000;&#8212;note different characters despite the same pronunciation)), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Deng_Mengn%C3%BC" title="Empress Deng Mengn&#252;">Deng Mengn&#252;</a> (&#37159;&#29467;&#22899;), as his own daughter, changing her family name to Liang. He and Sun gave Liang Mengn&#252; to Emperor Huan as an imperial consort. After Empress Liang\'s death, they hoped that she would eventually be made empress. To completely control her, Liang Ji planned to have her mother, Lady Xuan (&#23459;), killed: he sent assassins against her but the assassination was foiled by the powerful eunuch Yuan She (&#34945;&#36198;), a neighbor of Lady Xuan.\n</p><p>Lady Xuan reported the assassination attempt to Emperor Huan, who was greatly angered. He entered into a conspiracy with eunuchs Tang Heng, Zuo Guan, Dan Chao (&#21934;&#36229;), Xu Huang (&#24464;&#29852;), and Ju Yuan (&#20855;&#29783;) to overthrow Liang&#8212;sealing the oath by biting open Dan\'s arm and swearing by his blood. Liang Ji had some suspicions about what Emperor Huan and the eunuchs were up to, and began an investigation. The five eunuchs quickly reacted. They had Emperor Huan openly announce that he was taking back power from Liang Ji and mobilized the imperial guards to defend the palace against a counterattack by Liang. They surrounded Liang\'s house and forced him to surrender. Liang and Sun were unable to respond and committed suicide. The entire Liang and Sun clans (except for Liang Ji\'s brothers Li Buyi and Liang Meng [&#26753;&#33945;], who had previously already died) were arrested and slaughtered. A large number of officials were executed or deposed for close association with Liang&#8212;so many that the government was almost unable to function for some time. Liang and Sun\'s properties were confiscated by the imperial treasury, which allowed the taxes to be reduced by 50% for one year. The people greatly celebrated Liang Ji\'s death.  \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_reign:_empowerment_of_the_eunuchs">Late reign: empowerment of the eunuchs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emperor_Huan_of_Han&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late reign: empowerment of the eunuchs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After Liang Ji\'s death, Emperor Huan made Liang Mengn&#252; empress. However, he disliked her family name and therefore ordered her to take the family name Bo (&#34180;).  He later found out that her original family name was actually Deng, and therefore had her family name restored.\n</p><p>The people had great expectations for Emperor Huan\'s administration after the death of Liang Ji.  However, having been unable to overthrow Liang Ji without the five eunuchs\' help, Emperor Huan greatly rewarded them, making them and several other eunuchs who participated in the coup d\'&#233;tat marquesses and further giving them governmental posts that conferred tremendous power.  Further, the five eunuch-marquesses openly engaged in massive corruption and became extremely wealthy with Emperor Huan\'s approval.  A song written about the four remaining eunuch-marquesses following Dan\'s death described them in this way:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>Zuo can reverse heaven\'s decision.  Ju sits by himself without match.  Xu is a lying wolf.  Tang\'s power is as prevalent as the falling rain.</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Emperor Huan himself was also corrupt and unwilling to accept any criticism.  In 159, when the honest county magistrate Li Yun (&#26446;&#38642;) submitted a petition urging him to curb the power of the eunuchs, Emperor Huan was deeply offended that he included the phrase, "Is the emperor turning blind?" and, despite intercessions by a number of officials and even some fairly-minded eunuchs, had Li and his friend Du Zhong (&#26460;&#30526;) both executed.\n</p><p>In 161, apparently in reaction to spending due to renewed <a href="/wiki/Qiang_people" title="Qiang people">Qiang</a> rebellions and new agrarian revolts, Emperor Huan issued an edict offering minor offices for sale&#8212;including imperial guard officer positions.  This practice set a bad precedent and would become even more prevalent and problematic under Emperor Huan\'s successor, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Ling_of_Han" title="Emperor Ling of Han">Emperor Ling</a>.  While Emperor Huan actually was able to find competent generals to suppress the rebellions or to persuade the rebels to surrender, the rampant corruption came to cause new rebellions as soon as the old ones are quelled.\n</p><p>In 165, perhaps finally fed up with the eunuchs\' excess, Emperor Huan demoted Ju, the only eunuch remaining of the original five.  Several other corrupt eunuchs were also demoted or deposed.  However, soon thereafter, the eunuchs\' powers were restored again.  For the rest of Emperor Huan\'s reign, there would be a cycle of rise and fall of power of the eunuchs after conflicts with officials, but inexorably the eunuchs would return, becoming more powerful than before.\n</p><p>Later that year, apparently tired of Empress Deng and sick of her disputes with a favorite consort of his, Consort Guo, Emperor Huan deposed and imprisoned her.  She died in anger, and several of her family members were executed.  He wanted to make another consort, Tian Sheng (&#30000;&#32854;) empress, but officials opposed this on the basis that she was of lowly birth. They recommended that he make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Dou_Miao" title="Empress Dou Miao">Consort Dou Miao</a> (&#31431;&#22937;), the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Dou_Wu" title="Dou Wu">Dou Wu</a> (&#31431;&#27494;), a Confucian scholar and a descendant of Dou Rong (&#31431;&#34701;), who had contributed much to the establishment of the Eastern Han Dynasty, empress.  Even though he did not favor Consort Dou, Emperor Huan gave in to pressure and made her empress.\n</p><p>In 166, a major public confrontation between university students and eunuchs evolved into a major incident.  The governor of the capital province (modern western <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> and central <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>), Li Ying, had arrested and executed a fortuneteller named Zhang Cheng (&#24373;&#25104;), who had had his son kill a man, having predicted that a general pardon was coming.  Li was arrested and 200 some university students signed a petition requesting his release. This further angered Emperor Huan, who had the students arrested.  Only after about a year and Dou Wu\'s intercession were Li and the university students released, but all of them had their citizenship rights stripped.  This incident was later known as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disasters_of_Partisan_Prohibitions" title="Disasters of Partisan Prohibitions">Disaster of Partisan Prohibition</a>.\n</p><p>In 168, Emperor Huan died without a son.  Empress Dou, jealous of how he favored Consort Tian, had her immediately put to death.  She conducted a survey among the members of the imperial clan and decided on the 11-year-old Liu Hong (&#21129;&#23439;), the Marquess of Jieduting, who then ascended the throne as Emperor Ling.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Era_names">Era names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
