Such suits may be either full-pressure (i.e. a <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">space suit</a>) or partial-pressure (as used by <a href="/wiki/Aircrew" title="Aircrew">aircrew</a>). Partial-pressure suits work by providing mechanical counter-pressure to assist breathing at altitude.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The region from sea level to around 3,000&#160;m (10,000&#160;ft) is known as the <b>physiological-efficient zone</b>. Oxygen levels are usually high enough for humans to function without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplemental_oxygen" title="Supplemental oxygen">supplemental oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> is rare.\n</p><p>The <b>physiological-deficient</b> zone extends from 3,600&#160;m (12,000&#160;ft) to about 15,000&#160;m (50,000&#160;ft). There is an increased risk of problems such as <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>, trapped-gas <a href="/wiki/Dysbarism" title="Dysbarism">dysbarism</a> (where gas trapped in the body expands), and evolved-gas dysbarism (where dissolved gases such as nitrogen may form in the tissues, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Above approximately 10,000&#160;m (33,000&#160;ft) oxygen-rich <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing mixture</a> is required to approximate the oxygen available in the lower atmosphere, while above 12,000&#160;m (40,000&#160;ft) oxygen must be under positive pressure.  Above 15,000&#160;m (49,000&#160;ft), respiration is not possible because the pressure at which the lungs excrete carbon dioxide (approximately 87 mmHg) exceeds outside air pressure.  Above 19,000&#160;m (62,000&#160;ft), also known as the <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_limit" title="Armstrong limit">Armstrong limit</a>, fluids in the throat and lungs will boil away. Generally, 100% oxygen is used to maintain an equivalent altitude of 3,000&#160;m (10,000&#160;ft).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_pressure">Partial pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Partial pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Partial pressure suits only pressurize certain parts of the body. They can only provide protection up to a certain altitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They do not provide protection for extended periods of time at low ambient pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_pressure">Full pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Full pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Full pressure suits pressurize the entire body. These suits have no altitude limit.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_to_space_without_a_spacesuit">Exposure to space without a spacesuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exposure to space without a spacesuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_exposure" title="Space exposure">Space exposure</a></div>\n<p>The human body can briefly survive the hard <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> of space unprotected, despite contrary depictions in much popular <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>. Human flesh expands to about twice its size in such conditions, giving the visual effect of a body builder<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> rather than an overfilled balloon. Consciousness is retained for up to 15 seconds as the effects of <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">oxygen starvation</a> set in. No snap freeze effect occurs because all heat must be lost through <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> of liquids, and the blood does not boil because it remains pressurized within the body. The greatest danger is in attempting to hold one\'s breath before exposure, as the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression#Explosive_decompression" title="Uncontrolled decompression">explosive decompression</a> can damage the lungs. These effects have been confirmed through various accidents (including in very high altitude conditions, outer space and training vacuum chambers).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Human skin does not need to be protected from vacuum and is gas-tight by itself. Instead it only needs to be mechanically compressed to retain its normal shape. This can be accomplished with a tight-fitting elastic body suit and a helmet for containing breathing gases, known as a <a href="/wiki/Space_activity_suit" title="Space activity suit">space activity suit</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In Russia, the first full pressure suit was designed by engineer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ciann_Downes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ciann Downes (page does not exist)">Ciann Downes</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad" title="Leningrad">Leningrad</a> in 1931. The CH-1 was a simple pressure-tight suit with a helmet which did not have joints, thus requiring substantial force to move the arms and legs when pressurised. This was remedied in later suits. Work on full pressure suits was carried out during 1936-41 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TsAGI" title="TsAGI">Central Aerohydrodynamic Institute</a> (TsAGI), with similar work being carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Gromov_Flight_Research_Institute" title="Gromov Flight Research Institute">Gromov Flight Research Institute</a> (LII) after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The LII produced four experimental full pressure suits for aircrews, and in 1959 began work on full pressure suits for spaceflight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Chertovskiy used the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skafander" title="Skafander">skafander</a> for full pressure suits, from the French <i>scaphandre</i> ("diving suit"); <i>skafander</i> has since become the term used by Russians to refer to standard <a href="/wiki/Diving_suit" title="Diving suit">diving dresses</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_suits" title="Space suits">space suits</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haldane-Davis">Haldane-Davis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Haldane-Davis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:199px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caproni_Ca.161_pilot.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="197" decoding="async" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/61/Caproni_Ca.161_pilot.jpg" width="197"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caproni_Ca.161_pilot.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italian aviator <a href="/wiki/Mario_Pezzi_(aviator)" title="Mario Pezzi (aviator)">Mario Pezzi</a> in his high-altitude pressure suit, circa 1937</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1931, American Mark Ridge became obsessed with breaking the world altitude record in an open gondola balloon. Recognizing that the flight would require specialised protective clothing, he visited the UK in 1933 where he met with <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiologist" title="Physiologist">physiologist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Haldane" title="John Scott Haldane">John Scott Haldane</a>, who had published a concept for a fabric full pressure suit in the 1920s. The two sought the assistance of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Davis_(inventor)" title="Robert Davis (inventor)">Robert Henry Davis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Siebe_Gorman" title="Siebe Gorman">Siebe Gorman</a>, the inventor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis_Escape_Set" title="Davis Escape Set">Davis Escape Set</a>, and with Haldane\'s and Davis\' resources a prototype suit was constructed. Ridge tested it in a low-pressure chamber to a simulated altitude of 50,000 feet. However, he received no support for further work and never made his attempt on the world record.\n</p><p>On 28 September 1936 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squadron_Leader" title="Squadron Leader">Squadron Leader</a> F.R.D. Swain of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> set the official world altitude record at 49,967 feet in a <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Type_138#Operational_history" title="Bristol Type 138">Bristol Type 138</a> wearing a similar suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wiley_Post">Wiley Post</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wiley Post">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1934, aviator <a href="/wiki/Wiley_Post" title="Wiley Post">Wiley Post</a>, working with Russell S. Colley of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BF_Goodrich" title="BF Goodrich">B.F. Goodrich Company</a>, produced the world\'s first practical pressure suit. The suit\'s body had three layers: long underwear, a rubber air pressure bladder, and an outer suit of rubberized parachute fabric which was attached to a frame with arm and leg joints that allowed Post to operate aircraft controls and to walk to and from the aircraft. Attached to the frame were pigskin gloves, rubber boots, and an aluminum and plastic helmet with a removable faceplate that could accommodate earphones and a throat microphone. In the first flight using the suit on September 5, 1934, Post reached an altitude of 40,000 feet above <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and in later flights reached 50,000 feet.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the US, a large amount of effort was put into the development of pressure suits during World War II. While B.F. Goodrich led the field, other companies involved in such research included the Arrowhead Rubber Co., <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company">Goodyear</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Rubber_Company" title="United States Rubber Company">US Rubber</a>. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> worked with <a href="/wiki/Bell_Aircraft" title="Bell Aircraft">Bell Aircraft</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">US National Bureau of Standards</a>. The Bureau of Standards and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a> acted as clearing houses to distribute information to all the companies involved. No effective fully mobile pressure suits were produced in World War II but the effort provided a valuable basis for later development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2478" data-file-width="2724" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg/200px-Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg/300px-Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg/400px-Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joe_Walker_X-1E.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Walker" title="Joseph A. Walker">Joe Walker</a> in an early Air Force partial pressure suit</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Clark_Company">David Clark Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: David Clark Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following the war, the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> caused continued funding of aviation development, which included high altitude, high speed research such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NACA" title="NACA">NACA</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Bell_X-1" title="Bell X-1">X-1</a>. James Henry of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a> devised a partial pressure suit using an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">oxygen mask</a> to provide pressurised oxygen, with gas pressure also inflating rubber tubes called capstans to tighten the suit and provide sufficient mechanical counterpressure to just balance the breathing pressure necessary to prevent hypoxia at a particular altitude. The <a href="/wiki/David_Clark_Company" title="David Clark Company">David Clark Company</a> supplied technical support and resources, and a prototype suit was tested to a simulated 90,000 feet at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright_Field" title="Wright Field">Wright Field</a> in 1946. Henry\'s design was subsequently developed by the David Clark Company into the S-1 and T-1 flight suit used by X-1 pilots. The X-1 was succeeded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_Skyrocket" title="Douglas Skyrocket">Douglas Skyrocket</a>, whose objective was to exceed Mach 2, and an improved pressure suit was required. David Clark won the contract in 1951 with their first full pressure suit, the Model 4 Full Pressure Suit; it was first flown in 1953 by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">USMC</a> aviator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marion_E._Carl" title="Marion E. Carl">Marion E. Carl</a> who became the first US military aviator to wear a full pressure suit, at the same time setting an unofficial worlds altitude record in the Skyrocket.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="2409" decoding="async" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg/200px-Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg/300px-Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg/400px-Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gordon_Cooper_in_Helmet_and_Pressure_Suit_-_GPN-2000-001000.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Astronaut <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Cooper" title="Gordon Cooper">Gordon Cooper</a> in helmet and pressure suit</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goodrich_Mk_III_and_IV">Goodrich Mk III and IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Goodrich Mk III and IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>US requirements for high-altitude reconnaissance aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a>, and fighters to intercept high-altitude Soviet aircraft caused the US Navy to be tasked with the development of a full pressure suit in the 1950s. Working with B.F. Goodrich and Arrowhead Rubber, the USN produced a series of designs which culminated in the Goodrich Mk III and IV. While intended for aircraft use, the Mk IV was later used by NASA with modifications for <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navy_Mark_V" title="Navy Mark V">Navy Mark V</a>. At the same time, David Clark won the contract to produce suits for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">X-15</a> project; its XMC-2 suits qualified as the first US spacesuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RAF">RAF</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pressure_suit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: RAF">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/RAF_Institute_of_Aviation_Medicine" title="RAF Institute of Aviation Medicine">RAF Institute of Aviation Medicine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Establishment" title="Royal Aircraft Establishment">Royal Aircraft Establishment</a> developed a partial-pressure helmet which was used with a capstan type suit purchased from the US. It was worn by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Gibb" title="Walter Gibb">Walter Gibb</a> and his navigator to set a world altitude record on 29 August 1955 in an <a href="/wiki/English_Electric_Canberra" title="English Electric Canberra">English Electric Canberra</a>. However, evaluation of the suit showed that it encumbered the wearer and did not integrate well with RAF escape systems. Instead, the RAF IAM proposed a minimal-coverage suit which would provide "get-me-down" protection. The RAF never issued a partial-pressure suit, preferring instead to use anti-g trousers in conjunction with pressure jerkins (which applied mechanical counter-pressure to the wearer\'s chest).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
