the Earth moves through a "medium" of aether that carries light</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Luminiferous aether</b> or <b>ether</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> ("luminiferous", meaning "light-bearing"), was the postulated <a href="/wiki/Transmission_medium" title="Transmission medium">medium</a> for the propagation of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was invoked to explain the ability of the apparently <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">wave</a>-based light to propagate through empty space, something that waves should not be able to do. The assumption of a spatial plenum of luminiferous aether, rather than a spatial vacuum, provided the theoretical medium that was required by wave theories of light.\n</p><p>The aether hypothesis was the topic of considerable debate throughout its history, as it required the existence of an invisible and infinite material with no interaction with physical objects. As the nature of light was explored, especially in the 19th century, the physical qualities required of an aether became increasingly contradictory. By the late 1800s, the existence of the aether was being questioned, although there was no physical theory to replace it.\n</p><p>The negative outcome of the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment">Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment</a> (1887) suggested that the aether did not exist, a finding that was confirmed in subsequent experiments through the 1920s. This led to considerable theoretical work to explain the propagation of light without an aether. A major breakthrough was the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">theory of relativity</a>, which could explain why the experiment failed to see aether, but was more broadly interpreted to suggest that it was not needed. The Michelson-Morley experiment, along with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody_radiator" title="Blackbody radiator">blackbody radiator</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">photoelectric effect</a>, was a key experiment in the development of <a href="/wiki/Modern_physics" title="Modern physics">modern physics</a>, which includes both relativity and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>, the latter of which explains the wave-like nature of light.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input history of light and aether</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The history of light and aether">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_luminiferous_aether" title="Timeline of luminiferous aether">Timeline of luminiferous aether</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particles_vs._waves">Particles vs. waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Particles vs. waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wave-particle_duality" title="Wave-particle duality">Wave-particle duality</a></div>\n<p>In the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a> was a proponent of an aether hypothesis. According to Boyle, the aether consists of subtle particles, one sort of which explains the absence of vacuum and the mechanical interactions between bodies, and the other sort of which explains phenomena such as magnetism (and possibly gravity) that are, otherwise, inexplicable on the basis of purely mechanical interactions of macroscopic bodies, "though in the ether of the ancients there was nothing taken notice of but a diffused and very subtle substance; yet we are at present content to allow that there is always in the air a swarm of steams moving in a determinate course between the north pole and the south".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> hypothesized that light is a wave propagating through an aether. He and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> could only envision light waves as being <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">longitudinal</a>, propagating like sound and other <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_wave" title="Mechanical wave">mechanical waves</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a>. However, longitudinal waves necessarily have only one form for a given propagation direction, rather than two <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarizations</a> like <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse wave</a>. Thus, longitudinal waves can not explain <a href="/wiki/Birefringence" title="Birefringence">birefringence</a>, in which two polarizations of light are refracted differently by a crystal. In addition, Newton rejected light as waves in a medium because such a medium would have to extend everywhere in space, and would thereby "disturb and retard the Motions of those great Bodies" (the planets and comets) and thus "as it [light\'s medium] is of no use, and hinders the Operation of Nature, and makes her languish, so there is no evidence for its Existence, and therefore it ought to be rejected".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Isaac Newton contended that light is made up of numerous small particles. This can explain such features as light\'s ability to travel in straight lines and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflect</a> off surfaces. Newton imagined that light particles as non-spherical "corpuscles", with different "sides" that give rise to birefringence. But the particle theory of light can not satisfactorily explain <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> To explain refraction, Newton\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Opticks" title="Opticks">Opticks</a></i> (1704) postulated an "Aethereal Medium" transmitting vibrations faster than light, by which light, when overtaken, is put into "Fits of easy Reflexion and easy Transmission", which caused refraction and diffraction. Newton believed that these vibrations were related to heat radiation:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Is not the Heat of the warm Room convey\'d through the vacuum by the Vibrations of a much subtiler Medium than Air, which after the Air was drawn out remained in the Vacuum?  And is not this Medium the same with that Medium by which Light is refracted and reflected, and by whose Vibrations Light communicates Heat to Bodies, and is put into Fits of easy Reflexion and easy Transmission?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-newton-5">[A 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>349</span></sup></p></blockquote><p>\nIn contrast to the modern understanding that heat radiation and light are both <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, Newton viewed heat and light as two different phenomena. He believed heat vibrations to be excited "when a Ray of Light falls upon the Surface of any pellucid Body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-newton-5">[A 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>348</span></sup> He wrote, "I do not know what this Aether is", but that if it consists of particles then they must be </p><blockquote><p>exceedingly smaller than those of Air, or even than those of Light: The exceeding smallness of its Particles may contribute to the greatness of the force by which those Particles may recede from one another, and thereby make that Medium exceedingly more rare and elastic than Air, and by consequence exceedingly less able to resist the motions of Projectiles, and exceedingly more able to press upon gross Bodies, by endeavoring to expand itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-newton-5">[A 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>352</span></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bradley_suggests_particles">Bradley suggests particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bradley suggests particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1720, <a href="/wiki/James_Bradley" title="James Bradley">James Bradley</a> carried out a series of experiments attempting to measure <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax" title="Stellar parallax">stellar parallax</a> by taking measurements of stars at different times of the year. As the Earth moves around the sun, the apparent angle to a given distant spot changes. By measuring those angles the distance to the star can be calculated based on the known orbital circumference of the Earth around the sun. He failed to detect any parallax, thereby placing a lower limit on the distance to stars.\n</p><p>During these experiments, Bradley also discovered a related effect; the apparent positions of the stars did change over the year, but not as expected. Instead of the apparent angle being maximized when the Earth was at either end of its orbit with respect to the star, the angle was maximized when the Earth was at its fastest sideways velocity with respect to the star. This effect is now known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_aberration" title="Stellar aberration">stellar aberration</a>.\n</p><p>Bradley explained this effect in the context of Newton\'s corpuscular theory of light, by showing that the aberration angle was given by simple vector addition of the Earth\'s orbital velocity and the velocity of the corpuscles of light, just as  vertically falling raindrops strike a moving object at an angle. Knowing the Earth\'s velocity and the aberration angle, this enabled him to estimate the speed of light.\n</p><p>Explaining stellar aberration in the context of an aether-based theory of light was regarded as more problematic. As the aberration relied on relative velocities, and the measured velocity was dependent on the motion of the Earth, the aether had to be remaining stationary with respect to the star as the Earth moved through it. This meant that the Earth could travel through the aether, a physical medium, with no apparent effect &#8211; precisely the problem that led Newton to reject a wave model in the first place.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waves_theory_triumphs">Waves theory triumphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Waves theory triumphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A century later, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist)" title="Thomas Young (scientist)">Thomas Young</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Jean_Fresnel" title="Augustin-Jean Fresnel">Augustin-Jean Fresnel</a> revived the wave theory of light when they pointed out that light could be a <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse wave</a> rather than a longitudinal wave &#8211; the polarization of a transverse wave (like Newton\'s "sides" of light) could explain birefringence, and in the wake of a series of experiments on diffraction the particle model of Newton was finally abandoned. <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">Physicists</a> assumed, moreover, that like mechanical waves, light waves required a medium for <a href="/wiki/Wave_propagation" title="Wave propagation">propagation</a>, and thus required Huygens\'s idea of an aether "gas" permeating all space.\n</p><p>However, a transverse wave apparently required the propagating medium to behave as a solid, as opposed to a gas or fluid. The idea of a solid that did not interact with other matter seemed a bit odd, and <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy" title="Augustin-Louis Cauchy">Augustin-Louis Cauchy</a> suggested that perhaps there was some sort of "dragging", or "entrainment", but this made the aberration measurements difficult to understand. He also suggested that the <i>absence</i> of longitudinal waves suggested that the aether had negative compressibility. <a href="/wiki/George_Green_(mathematician)" title="George Green (mathematician)">George Green</a> pointed out that such a fluid would be unstable. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gabriel_Stokes" title="George Gabriel Stokes">George Gabriel Stokes</a> became a champion of the entrainment interpretation, developing a model in which the aether might be (by analogy with pine pitch) rigid at very high frequencies and fluid at lower speeds. Thus the Earth could move through it fairly freely, but it would be rigid enough to support light.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electromagnetism">Electromagnetism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electromagnetism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1856, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Eduard_Weber" title="Wilhelm Eduard Weber">Wilhelm Eduard Weber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Kohlrausch" title="Rudolf Kohlrausch">Rudolf Kohlrausch</a> measured the numerical value of the ratio of the electromagnetic unit of charge to the electrostatic unit of charge. They found that the ratio equals the product of the speed of light and the square root of two. The following year, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> wrote a paper in which he showed that the speed of a signal along an electric wire was equal to the speed of light. These are the first recorded historical links between the speed of light and electromagnetic phenomena.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> began working on <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lines_of_force" title="Lines of force">lines of force</a>. In his 1861 paper <i><a href="/wiki/File:On_Physical_Lines_of_Force.pdf" title="File:On Physical Lines of Force.pdf">On Physical Lines of Force</a></i> he modelled these magnetic lines of force using a sea of molecular vortices that he considered to be partly made of aether and partly made of ordinary matter. He derived expressions for the dielectric constant and the magnetic permeability in terms of the transverse elasticity and the density of this elastic medium. He then equated the ratio of the dielectric constant to the magnetic permeability with a suitably adapted version of Weber and Kohlrausch\'s result of 1856, and he substituted this result into Newton\'s equation for the speed of sound. On obtaining a value that was close to the speed of light as measured by <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau" title="Hippolyte Fizeau">Hippolyte Fizeau</a>, Maxwell concluded that light consists in undulations of the same medium that is the cause of electric and magnetic phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitt_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitt-6">[B 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janb_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-janb-7">[B 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darrigol_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-darrigol-8">[B 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schaffner_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-schaffner-9">[B 4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Maxwell had, however, expressed some uncertainties surrounding the precise nature of his molecular vortices and so he began to embark on a purely dynamical approach to the problem. He wrote another paper in 1864, entitled "<a href="/wiki/A_Dynamical_Theory_of_the_Electromagnetic_Field" title="A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field">A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field</a>", in which the details of the luminiferous medium were less explicit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxb_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-maxb-10">[A 2]</a></sup> Although Maxwell did not explicitly mention the sea of molecular vortices, his derivation of <a href="/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law" title="Amp&#232;re\'s circuital law">Amp&#232;re\'s circuital law</a> was carried over from the 1861 paper and he used a dynamical approach involving rotational motion within the electromagnetic field which he likened to the action of flywheels. Using this approach to justify the electromotive force equation (the precursor of the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">Lorentz force</a> equation), he derived a wave equation from a set of eight equations which appeared in the paper and which included the electromotive force equation and <a href="/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law" title="Amp&#232;re\'s circuital law">Amp&#232;re\'s circuital law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxb_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-maxb-10">[A 2]</a></sup> Maxwell once again used the experimental results of Weber and Kohlrausch to show that this wave equation represented an electromagnetic wave that propagates at the speed of light, hence supporting the view that light is a form of electromagnetic radiation.\n</p><p>The apparent need for a propagation medium for such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Rudolf_Hertz" title="Heinrich Rudolf Hertz">Hertzian waves</a> can be seen by the fact that they consist of orthogonal electric (E) and magnetic (B or H) waves. The E waves consist of undulating dipolar electric fields, and all such dipoles appeared to require separated and opposite electric charges. Electric charge is an inextricable property of <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>, so it appeared that some form of matter was required to provide the alternating current that would seem to have to exist at any point along the propagation path of the wave. Propagation of waves in a true vacuum would imply the existence of <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric fields</a> without associated <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, or of electric charge without associated matter. Albeit compatible with Maxwell\'s equations, <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction" title="Electromagnetic induction">electromagnetic induction</a> of electric fields could not be demonstrated in vacuum, because all methods of detecting electric fields required electrically charged matter.\n</p><p>In addition, Maxwell\'s equations required that all electromagnetic waves in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> propagate at a fixed speed, <i><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">c</a></i>. As this can only occur in one <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">reference frame</a> in Newtonian physics (see <a href="/wiki/Galilean_invariance" title="Galilean invariance">Galilean-Newtonian relativity</a>), the aether was hypothesized as the absolute and unique frame of reference in which Maxwell\'s equations hold. That is, the aether must be "still" universally, otherwise <i>c</i> would vary along with any variations that might occur in its supportive medium. Maxwell himself proposed several mechanical models of aether based on wheels and gears, and <a href="/wiki/George_Francis_FitzGerald" title="George Francis FitzGerald">George Francis FitzGerald</a> even constructed a working model of one of them. These models had to agree with the fact that the electromagnetic waves are <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">transverse</a> but never <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">longitudinal</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By this point the mechanical qualities of the aether had become more and more magical: it had to be a <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> in order to fill space, but one that was millions of times more rigid than steel in order to support the high frequencies of light waves. It also had to be massless and without <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, otherwise it would visibly affect the orbits of planets. Additionally it appeared it had to be completely transparent, non-dispersive, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompressible_fluid" title="Incompressible fluid">incompressible</a>, and continuous at a very small scale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for these properties (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Maxwell wrote in <i>Encyclop&#230;dia Britannica</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxa_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-maxa-11">[A 3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Aethers were invented for the planets to swim in, to constitute electric atmospheres and magnetic effluvia, to convey sensations from one part of our bodies to another, and so on, until all space had been filled three or four times over with aethers. ... The only aether which has survived is that which was invented by Huygens to explain the propagation of light.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Contemporary scientists were aware of the problems, but aether theory was so entrenched in physical law by this point that it was simply assumed to exist. In 1908 <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Lodge" title="Oliver Lodge">Oliver Lodge</a> gave a speech on behalf of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[5]</a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institution" title="Royal Institution">Royal Institution</a> on this topic, in which he outlined its physical properties, and then attempted to offer reasons why they were not impossible. Nevertheless, he was also aware of the criticisms, and quoted <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gascoyne-Cecil,_3rd_Marquess_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Gascoyne-Cecil, 3rd Marquess of Salisbury">Lord Salisbury</a> as saying that "aether is little more than a nominative case of the verb <i>to undulate</i>". Others criticized it as an "English invention", although Rayleigh jokingly stated it was actually an invention of the Royal Institution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the early 20th century, aether theory was in trouble. A series of <a href="#Negative_aether-drift_experiments">increasingly complex experiments</a> had been carried out in the late 19th century to try to detect the motion of the Earth through the aether, and had failed to do so. A range of proposed aether-dragging theories could explain the null result but these were more complex, and tended to use arbitrary-looking coefficients and physical assumptions. Lorentz and FitzGerald offered within the framework of <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">Lorentz ether theory</a> a more elegant solution to how the motion of an absolute aether could be undetectable (length contraction), but if their equations were correct, the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity" title="Special theory of relativity">special theory of relativity</a> (1905) could generate the same mathematics without referring to an aether at all. Aether fell to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occam%27s_Razor" title="Occam\'s Razor">Occam\'s Razor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitt_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitt-6">[B 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janb_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-janb-7">[B 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darrigol_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-darrigol-8">[B 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schaffner_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-schaffner-9">[B 4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_motion_between_the_Earth_and_aether">Relative motion between the Earth and aether</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relative motion between the Earth and aether">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_drag">Aether drag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aether drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aether_drag_hypothesis" title="Aether drag hypothesis">Aether drag hypothesis</a></div>\n<p>The two most important models, which were aimed to describe the relative motion of the Earth and aether, were <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Jean_Fresnel" title="Augustin-Jean Fresnel">Augustin-Jean Fresnel</a>\'s (1818) model of the (nearly) stationary aether including a partial aether drag determined by Fresnel\'s dragging coefficient,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fresnel_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fresnel-14">[A 4]</a></sup>\nand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gabriel_Stokes" title="George Gabriel Stokes">George Gabriel Stokes</a>\' (1844)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stokes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-stokes-15">[A 5]</a></sup>\nmodel of complete aether drag. The latter theory was not considered as correct, since it was not compatible with the <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">aberration of light</a>, and the auxiliary hypotheses developed to explain this problem were not convincing. Also, subsequent experiments as the <a href="/wiki/Sagnac_effect" title="Sagnac effect">Sagnac effect</a> (1913) also showed that this model is untenable. However, the most important experiment supporting Fresnel\'s theory was <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau" title="Hippolyte Fizeau">Fizeau</a>\'s 1851 experimental confirmation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresnel" title="Fresnel">Fresnel</a>\'s 1818 prediction that a medium with <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> <i>n</i> moving with a velocity <i>v</i> would increase the speed of light travelling through the medium in the same direction as <i>v</i> from <i>c</i>/<i>n</i> to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fizeau1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fizeau1-16">[E 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michel2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-michel2-17">[E 2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\frac {c}{n}}+\\left(1-{\\frac {1}{n^{2}}}\\right)v.}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mi>c</mi>\n<mi>n</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mrow>\n<mo>(</mo>\n<mrow>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<msup>\n<mi>n</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mrow>\n</msup>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n</mrow>\n<mo>)</mo>\n</mrow>\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mo>.</mo>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle {\\frac {c}{n}}+\\left(1-{\\frac {1}{n^{2}}}\\right)v.}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\frac{c}{n} + \\left( 1 - \\frac{1}{n^2} \\right) v." aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3ddbd2c9efd7f677821ad043613f04bb5578d2a" style="vertical-align: -2.505ex; width:17.942ex; height:6.176ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>That is, movement adds only a fraction of the medium\'s velocity to the light (predicted by Fresnel in order to make <a href="/wiki/Snell%27s_law" title="Snell\'s law">Snell\'s law</a> work in all frames of reference, consistent with stellar aberration).  This was initially interpreted to mean that the medium drags the aether along, with a <i>portion</i> of the medium\'s velocity, but that understanding became very problematic after <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wilhelm_Veltmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wilhelm Veltmann (page does not exist)">Wilhelm Veltmann</a> demonstrated that the index <i>n</i> in Fresnel\'s formula depended upon the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of light, so that the aether could not be moving at a wavelength-independent speed. This implied that there must be a separate aether for each of the infinitely many frequencies.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_aether-drift_experiments">Negative aether-drift experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Negative aether-drift experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The key difficulty with Fresnel\'s aether hypothesis arose from the juxtaposition of the two well-established theories of Newtonian dynamics and Maxwell\'s electromagnetism. Under a <a href="/wiki/Galilean_transformation" title="Galilean transformation">Galilean transformation</a> the equations of Newtonian dynamics are  <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(physics)" title="Invariant (physics)">invariant</a>, whereas those of electromagnetism are not. Basically this means that while physics should remain the same in non-accelerated experiments, light would not follow the same rules because it is travelling in the universal "aether frame". Some effect caused by this difference should be detectable.\n</p><p>A simple example concerns the model on which aether was originally built: sound. The speed of propagation for mechanical waves, the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>, is defined by the mechanical properties of the medium. Sound travels 4.3 times faster in water than in air.  This explains why a person hearing an explosion underwater and quickly surfacing can hear it again as the slower travelling sound arrives through the air.  Similarly, a traveller on an <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a> can still carry on a conversation with another traveller because the sound of words is travelling along with the air inside the aircraft. This effect is basic to all Newtonian dynamics, which says that everything from sound to the trajectory of a thrown baseball should all remain the same in the aircraft flying (at least at a constant speed) as if still sitting on the ground. This is the basis of the Galilean transformation, and the concept of frame of reference.\n</p><p>But the same was not supposed to be true for light, since Maxwell\'s mathematics demanded a single universal speed for the propagation of light, based, not on local conditions, but on two measured properties, the <a href="/wiki/Permittivity" title="Permittivity">permittivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism)" title="Permeability (electromagnetism)">permeability</a> of free space, that were assumed to be the same throughout the universe. If these numbers did change, there should be noticeable effects in the sky; stars in different directions would have different colours, for instance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (June 2011)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Thus at any point there should be one special coordinate system, "at rest relative to the aether". Maxwell noted in the late 1870s that detecting motion relative to this aether should be easy enough&#8212;light travelling along with the motion of the Earth would have a different speed than light travelling backward, as they would both be moving against the unmoving aether. Even if the aether had an overall universal flow, changes in position during the day/night cycle, or over the span of seasons, should allow the drift to be detected.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_order_experiments">First order experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: First order experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Although the aether is almost stationary according to Fresnel, his theory predicts a positive outcome of aether drift experiments only to <i>second</i> order in <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v/c}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>/</mo>\n</mrow>\n<mi>c</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v/c}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v/c" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd5fbfe05e197127354e233f6c813e6b5d846672" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:3.297ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, because Fresnel\'s dragging coefficient would cause a negative outcome of all optical experiments capable of measuring effects to <i>first</i> order in <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle v/c}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>v</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>/</mo>\n</mrow>\n<mi>c</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle v/c}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="v/c" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd5fbfe05e197127354e233f6c813e6b5d846672" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:3.297ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>. This was confirmed by the following first-order experiments, which all gave negative results. The following list is based on the description of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wien" title="Wilhelm Wien">Wilhelm Wien</a> (1898), with changes and additional experiments according to the descriptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmund_Taylor_Whittaker" title="Edmund Taylor Whittaker">Edmund Taylor Whittaker</a> (1910) and <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Laub" title="Jakob Laub">Jakob Laub</a> (1910):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wien_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-wien-18">[B 5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitt_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-whitt-6">[B 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laub_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-laub-19">[B 6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago" title="Fran&#231;ois Arago">Fran&#231;ois Arago</a> (1810), to confirm whether refraction, and thus the aberration of light, is influenced by Earth\'s motion. Similar experiments were conducted by <a href="/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy" title="George Biddell Airy">George Biddell Airy</a> (1871) by means of a telescope filled with water, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89leuth%C3%A8re_Mascart" title="&#201;leuth&#232;re Mascart">&#201;leuth&#232;re Mascart</a> (1872).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arago_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arago-20">[E 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Airy_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Airy-21">[E 4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masc1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-masc1-22">[E 5]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of Fizeau (1860), to find whether the rotation of the polarization plane through glass columns is changed by Earth\'s motion. He obtained a positive result, but Lorentz could show that the results have been contradictory. <a href="/wiki/DeWitt_Bristol_Brace" title="DeWitt Bristol Brace">DeWitt Bristol Brace</a> (1905) and Strasser (1907) repeated the experiment with improved accuracy, and obtained negative results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fizeau2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fizeau2-23">[E 6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brace2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brace2-24">[E 7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strasser_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strasser-25">[E 8]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hoek" title="Martin Hoek">Martin Hoek</a> (1868). This experiment is a more precise variation of the <a href="/wiki/Fizeau_experiment" title="Fizeau experiment">Fizeau experiment (1851)</a>. Two light rays were sent in opposite directions &#8211; one of them traverses a path filled with resting water, the other one follows a path through air. In agreement with Fresnel\'s dragging coefficient, he obtained a negative result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoek_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoek-26">[E 9]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Klinkerfues" title="Wilhelm Klinkerfues">Wilhelm Klinkerfues</a> (1870) investigated whether an influence of Earth\'s motion on the absorption line of sodium exists. He obtained a positive result, but this was shown to be an experimental error, because a repetition of the experiment by <a href="/wiki/Hermanus_Haga" title="Hermanus Haga">Haga</a> (1901) gave a negative result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinkerfues_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klinkerfues-27">[E 10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haga_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haga-28">[E 11]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of Ketteler (1872), in which two rays of an interferometer were sent in opposite directions through two mutually inclined tubes filled with water. No change of the interference fringes occurred. Later, Mascart (1872) showed that the interference fringes of polarized light in calcite remained uninfluenced as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ketteler_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ketteler-29">[E 12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masc2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-masc2-30">[E 13]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89leuth%C3%A8re_Mascart" title="&#201;leuth&#232;re Mascart">&#201;leuth&#232;re Mascart</a> (1872) to find a change of rotation of the polarization plane in quartz. No change of rotation was found when the light rays had the direction of Earth\'s motion and then the opposite direction. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh" title="Lord Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> conducted similar experiments with improved accuracy, and obtained a negative result as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masc1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-masc1-22">[E 5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masc2_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-masc2-30">[E 13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayleigh1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rayleigh1-31">[E 14]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Besides those optical experiments, also electrodynamic first-order experiments were conducted, which should have led to positive results according to Fresnel. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hendrik_Antoon_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Antoon Lorentz">Hendrik Antoon Lorentz</a> (1895) modified Fresnel\'s theory and showed that those experiments can be explained by a stationary aether as well:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lorb_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-lorb-32">[A 6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm R&#246;ntgen">Wilhelm R&#246;ntgen</a> (1888), to find whether a charged condenser produces magnetic forces due to Earth\'s motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roentgen_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roentgen-33">[E 15]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Theodor_des_Coudres" title="Theodor des Coudres">Theodor des Coudres</a> (1889), to find whether the inductive effect of two wire rolls upon a third one is influenced by the direction of Earth\'s motion. Lorentz showed that this effect is cancelled to first order by the electrostatic charge (produced by Earth\'s motion) upon the conductors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coudres_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coudres-34">[E 16]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of K&#246;nigsberger (1905). The plates of a condenser are located in the field of a strong electromagnet. Due to Earth\'s motion, the plates should have become charged. No such effect was observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koenigsberger_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koenigsberger-35">[E 17]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The experiment of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Thomas_Trouton" title="Frederick Thomas Trouton">Frederick Thomas Trouton</a> (1902). A condenser was brought parallel to Earth\'s motion, and it was assumed that momentum is produced when the condenser is charged. The negative result can be explained by Lorentz\'s theory, according to which the electromagnetic momentum compensates the momentum due to Earth\'s motion. Lorentz could also show, that the sensitivity of the apparatus was much too low to observe such an effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trouton1_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trouton1-36">[E 18]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_order_experiments">Second order experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Second order experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="655" data-file-width="690" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png/220px-Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png/330px-Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png/440px-Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Michelson-Morley_experiment_conducted_with_white_light.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment compared the time for light to reflect from mirrors in two orthogonal directions.</div></div></div>\n<p>While the <i>first</i>-order experiments could be explained by a modified stationary aether, more precise <i>second</i>-order experiments were expected to give positive results, however, no such results could be found.\n</p><p>The famous <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment" title="Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment">Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment</a> compared the source light with itself after being sent in different directions, looking for changes in phase in a manner that could be measured with extremely high accuracy. In this experiment, their goal was to determine the velocity of the Earth through the aether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michel1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-michel1-37">[E 19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michel3_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-michel3-38">[E 20]</a></sup> The publication of their result in 1887, the <a href="/wiki/Null_result" title="Null result">null result</a>, was the first clear demonstration that something was seriously wrong with the aether hypothesis (Michelson\'s first experiment in 1881 was not entirely conclusive). In this case the MM experiment yielded a shift of the fringing pattern of about 0.01 of a <a href="/wiki/Fringe_shift" title="Fringe shift">fringe</a>, corresponding to a small velocity. However, it was incompatible with the expected aether wind effect due to the Earth\'s (seasonally varying) velocity which would have required a shift of 0.4 of a fringe, and the error was small enough that the value may have indeed been zero.  Therefore, the <a href="/wiki/Null_hypothesis" title="Null hypothesis">null hypothesis</a>, the hypothesis that there was no aether wind, could not be rejected.  More modern experiments have since reduced the possible value to a number very close to zero, about 10<sup>&#8722;17</sup>.\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is obvious from what has gone before that it would be hopeless to attempt to solve the question of the motion of the solar system by observations of optical phenomena at the surface of the earth.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>A. Michelson and E. Morley. "On the Relative Motion of the Earth and the Luminiferous &#198;ther". <i>Phil. Mag.</i> S. 5. Vol. 24. No. 151. Dec. 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>A series of experiments using similar but increasingly sophisticated apparatuses all returned the null result as well. Conceptually different experiments that also attempted to detect the motion of the aether were the <a href="/wiki/Trouton%E2%80%93Noble_experiment" title="Trouton&#8211;Noble experiment">Trouton&#8211;Noble experiment</a>&#160;(1903),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trouton2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trouton2-40">[E 21]</a></sup> whose objective was to detect <a href="/wiki/Torsion_(mechanics)" title="Torsion (mechanics)">torsion</a> effects caused by electrostatic fields, and <a href="/wiki/Experiments_of_Rayleigh_and_Brace" title="Experiments of Rayleigh and Brace">the experiments of Rayleigh and Brace</a> (1902, 1904),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayleigh2_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rayleigh2-41">[E 22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brace1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brace1-42">[E 23]</a></sup> to detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_refraction" title="Double refraction">double refraction</a> in various media. However, all of them obtained a null result, like Michelson&#8211;Morley (MM) previously did.\n</p><p>These "aether-wind" experiments led to a flurry of efforts to "save" aether by assigning to it ever more complex properties, while only few scientists, like <a href="/wiki/Emil_Cohn" title="Emil Cohn">Emil Cohn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Bucherer" title="Alfred Bucherer">Alfred Bucherer</a>, considered the possibility of the abandonment of the aether hypothesis. Of particular interest was the possibility of "aether entrainment" or "aether drag", which would lower the magnitude of the measurement, perhaps enough to explain the results of the Michelson-Morley experiment. However, as noted earlier, aether dragging already had problems of its own, notably aberration. In addition, the interference experiments of <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Lodge" title="Oliver Lodge">Lodge</a> (1893, 1897) and <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Zehnder" title="Ludwig Zehnder">Ludwig Zehnder</a> (1895), aimed to show whether the aether is dragged by various, rotating masses, showed no aether drag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lodge_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lodge-43">[E 24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lodge2_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lodge2-44">[E 25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zehnder_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zehnder-45">[E 26]</a></sup> A more precise measurement was made in the <a href="/wiki/Hammar_experiment" title="Hammar experiment">Hammar experiment</a> (1935), which ran a complete MM experiment with one of the "legs" placed between two massive lead blocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hammar_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hammar-46">[E 27]</a></sup> If the aether was dragged by mass then this experiment would have been able to detect the drag caused by the lead, but again the null result was achieved. The theory was again modified, this time to suggest that the entrainment only worked for very large masses or those masses with large magnetic fields. This too was shown to be incorrect by the <a href="/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Gale%E2%80%93Pearson_experiment" title="Michelson&#8211;Gale&#8211;Pearson experiment">Michelson&#8211;Gale&#8211;Pearson experiment</a>, which detected the Sagnac effect due to Earth\'s rotation (see <a href="/wiki/Aether_drag_hypothesis" title="Aether drag hypothesis">Aether drag hypothesis</a>).\n</p><p>Another, completely different attempt to save "absolute" aether was made in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentz%E2%80%93FitzGerald_contraction_hypothesis" title="Lorentz&#8211;FitzGerald contraction hypothesis">Lorentz&#8211;FitzGerald contraction hypothesis</a>, which posited that <i>everything</i> was affected by travel through the aether. In this theory the reason the Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment "failed" was that the apparatus contracted in length in the direction of travel. That is, the light was being affected in the "natural" manner by its travel though the aether as predicted, but so was the apparatus itself, cancelling out any difference when measured. FitzGerald had inferred this hypothesis from a paper by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">Oliver Heaviside</a>. Without referral to an aether, this physical interpretation of relativistic effects was <a href="/wiki/Kennedy%E2%80%93Thorndike_experiment" title="Kennedy&#8211;Thorndike experiment">shared by Kennedy and Thorndike</a> in 1932 as they concluded that the interferometer\'s arm contracts and also the frequency of its light source "very nearly" varies in the way required by relativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenn_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-kenn-47">[E 28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Similarly the <a href="/wiki/Sagnac_effect" title="Sagnac effect">Sagnac effect</a>, observed by G. Sagnac in 1913, was immediately seen to be fully consistent with special relativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagnac1_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagnac1-49">[E 29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagnac2_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagnac2-50">[E 30]</a></sup> In fact, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelson-Gale-Pearson_experiment" title="Michelson-Gale-Pearson experiment">Michelson-Gale-Pearson experiment</a> in 1925 was proposed specifically as a test to confirm the relativity theory, although it was also recognized that such tests, which merely measure absolute rotation, are also consistent with non-relativistic theories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1920s, the experiments pioneered by Michelson were repeated by <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Miller" title="Dayton Miller">Dayton Miller</a>, who publicly proclaimed positive results on several occasions, although they were not large enough to be consistent with any known aether theory. However, other researchers were unable to duplicate Miller\'s claimed results. Over the years the experimental accuracy of such measurements has been raised by many orders of magnitude, and no trace of any violations of Lorentz invariance has been seen. (A later re-analysis of Miller\'s results concluded that he had underestimated the variations due to temperature.)\n</p><p>Since the Miller experiment and its unclear results there have been many more experimental attempts to detect the aether. Many experimenters have claimed positive results. These results have not gained much attention from mainstream science, since they contradict a large quantity of high-precision measurements, all the results of which were consistent with special relativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lorentz_aether_theory">Lorentz aether theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lorentz aether theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">Lorentz ether theory</a></div>\n<p>Between 1892 and 1904, <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">Hendrik Lorentz</a> developed an electron-aether theory, in which he introduced a strict separation between matter (electrons) and aether. In his model the aether is completely motionless, and won\'t be set in motion in the neighborhood of ponderable matter. Contrary to earlier electron models, the electromagnetic field of the aether appears as a mediator between the electrons, and changes in this field cannot propagate faster than the speed of light. A fundamental concept of Lorentz\'s theory in 1895 was the "theorem of corresponding states" for terms of order v/c.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lorb_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-lorb-32">[A 6]</a></sup> This theorem states that an observer moving relative to the aether makes the same observations as a resting observer, after a suitable change of variables. Lorentz noticed that it was necessary to change the space-time variables when changing frames and introduced concepts like physical <a href="/wiki/Length_contraction" title="Length contraction">length contraction</a> (1892)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lora_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-lora-53">[A 7]</a></sup> to explain the Michelson&#8211;Morley experiment, and the mathematical concept of <a href="/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity" title="Relativity of simultaneity">local time</a> (1895) to explain the <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">aberration of light</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fizeau_experiment" title="Fizeau experiment">Fizeau experiment</a>. This resulted in the formulation of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformation" title="Lorentz transformation">Lorentz transformation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Larmor" title="Joseph Larmor">Joseph Larmor</a> (1897, 1900)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lara_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-lara-54">[A 8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-larb_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-larb-55">[A 9]</a></sup> and Lorentz (1899, 1904),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lorc_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-lorc-56">[A 10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lord_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-lord-57">[A 11]</a></sup> whereby (it was noted by Larmor) the complete formulation of local time is accompanied by some sort of <a href="/wiki/Time_dilation" title="Time dilation">time dilation</a> of electrons moving in the aether. As Lorentz later noted (1921, 1928), he considered the time indicated by clocks resting in the aether as "true" time, while local time was seen by him as a heuristic working hypothesis and a mathematical artifice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lore_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-lore-58">[A 12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lorf_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-lorf-59">[A 13]</a></sup> Therefore, Lorentz\'s theorem is seen by modern authors as being a mathematical transformation from a "real" system resting in the aether into a "fictitious" system in motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-miller-60">[B 7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darrigol_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-darrigol-8">[B 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jana_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-jana-61">[B 8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The work of Lorentz was mathematically perfected by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9" title="Henri Poincar&#233;">Henri Poincar&#233;</a>, who formulated on many occasions the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principle_of_Relativity" title="Principle of Relativity">Principle of Relativity</a> and tried to harmonize it with electrodynamics. He declared simultaneity only a convenient convention which depends on the speed of light, whereby the constancy of the speed of light would be a useful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postulate" title="Postulate">postulate</a> for making the laws of nature as simple as possible. In 1900 and 1904<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poinca_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-poinca-62">[A 14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poincb_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-poincb-63">[A 15]</a></sup> he physically interpreted Lorentz\'s local time as the result of clock synchronization by light signals. In June and July 1905<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poincc_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-poincc-64">[A 16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-poincd_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-poincd-65">[A 17]</a></sup> he declared the relativity principle a general law of nature, including gravitation. He corrected some mistakes of Lorentz and proved the Lorentz covariance of the electromagnetic equations. However, he used the notion of an aether as a perfectly undetectable medium and distinguished between apparent and real time, so most historians of science argue that he failed to invent special relativity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-miller-60">[B 7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pais_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-pais-66">[B 9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-darrigol_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-darrigol-8">[B 3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_aether">End of aether</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: End of aether">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_relativity">Special relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Special relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Aether theory was dealt another blow when the Galilean transformation and Newtonian dynamics were both modified by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special theory of relativity</a>, giving the mathematics of <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">Lorentzian electrodynamics</a> a new, "non-aether" context.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einselektro_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-einselektro-67">[A 18]</a></sup> Unlike most major shifts in scientific thought, special relativity was adopted by the scientific community remarkably quickly, consistent with Einstein\'s later comment that the laws of physics described by the Special Theory were "ripe for discovery" in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-born-68">[B 10]</a></sup> Max Planck\'s early advocacy of the special theory, along with the elegant formulation given to it by <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski" title="Hermann Minkowski">Hermann Minkowski</a>, contributed much to the rapid acceptance of special relativity among working scientists.\n</p><p>Einstein based his theory on Lorentz\'s earlier work. Instead of suggesting that the mechanical properties of objects changed with their constant-velocity motion through an undetectable aether, Einstein proposed to deduce the characteristics that any successful theory must possess in order to be consistent with the most basic and firmly established principles, independent of the existence of a hypothetical aether. He found that the Lorentz transformation must transcend its connection with Maxwell\'s equations, and must represent the fundamental relations between the space and time coordinates of <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">inertial frames of reference</a>. In this way he demonstrated that the laws of physics remained invariant as they had with the Galilean transformation, but that light was now invariant as well.\n</p><p>With the development of the special theory of relativity, the need to account for a single universal <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> had disappeared &#8211; and acceptance of the 19th century theory of a luminiferous aether disappeared with it. For Einstein, the Lorentz transformation implied a conceptual change: that the concept of position in space or time was not absolute, but could differ depending on the observer\'s location and velocity.\n</p><p>Moreover, in another paper published the same month in 1905, Einstein made several observations on a then-thorny problem, the <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">photoelectric effect</a>. In this work he demonstrated that light can be considered as particles that have a "wave-like nature". Particles obviously do not need a medium to travel, and thus, neither did light. This was the first step that would lead to the full development of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, in which the wave-like nature <i>and</i> the particle-like nature of light are both considered as valid descriptions of light. A summary of Einstein\'s thinking about the aether hypothesis, relativity and light quanta may be found in his 1909 (originally German) lecture "The Development of Our Views on the Composition and Essence of Radiation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einsdev_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-einsdev-69">[A 19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lorentz on his side continued to use the aether hypothesis. In his lectures of around 1911 he pointed out that what "the theory of relativity has to say ... can be carried out independently of what one thinks of the aether and the time". He commented that "whether there is an aether or not, electromagnetic fields certainly exist, and so also does the energy of the electrical oscillations" so that, "if we do not like the name of \'aether\', we must use another word as a peg to hang all these things upon". He concluded that "one cannot deny the bearer of these concepts a certain substantiality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-miller-60">[B 7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_models">Other models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aether_theories" title="Aether theories">Aether theories</a></div>\n<p>In later years there have been a few individuals who advocated a neo-Lorentzian approach to physics, which is Lorentzian in the sense of positing an absolute true state of rest that is undetectable and which plays no role in the predictions of the theory. (No violations of <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_covariance" title="Lorentz covariance">Lorentz covariance</a> have ever been detected, despite strenuous efforts.) Hence these theories resemble the 19th century aether theories in name only. For example, the founder of quantum field theory, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a>, stated in 1951 in an article in Nature, titled "Is there an Aether?" that "we are rather forced to have an aether".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirac_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-dirac-72">[A 20]</a></sup> However, Dirac never formulated a complete theory, and so his speculations found no acceptance by the scientific community.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Einstein.27s_views_on_the_aether"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Einstein\'s_views_on_the_aether">Einstein\'s views on the aether</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luminiferous_aether&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Einstein\'s views on the aether">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When Einstein was still a student in the Zurich Polytechnic in 1900, he was very interested in the idea of aether. His initial proposal of research thesis was to do an experiment to measure how fast the Earth was moving through the aether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[13]</a></sup>  "The velocity of a wave is proportional to the square root of the elastic forces which cause [its] propagation, and inversely proportional to the mass of the aether moved by these forces."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1916, after Einstein completed his foundational work on <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, Lorentz wrote a letter to him in which he speculated that within general relativity the aether was re-introduced. In his response Einstein wrote that one can actually speak about a "new aether", but one may not speak of motion in relation to that aether. This was further elaborated by Einstein in some semi-popular articles (1918, 1920, 1924, 1930).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einsta_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-einsta-75">[A 21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstb_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-einstb-76">[A 22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstc_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-einstc-77">[A 23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstd_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-einstd-78">[A 24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kosta_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-kosta-79">[B 11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stach_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-stach-80">[B 12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kostb_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-kostb-81">[B 13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1918 Einstein publicly alluded to that new definition for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einsta_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-einsta-75">[A 21]</a></sup> Then, in the early 1920s, in a lecture which he was invited to give at Lorentz\'s university in Leiden, Einstein sought to reconcile the theory of relativity with <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory" title="Lorentz ether theory">Lorentzian aether</a>. In this lecture Einstein stressed that special relativity took away the last mechanical property of the aether: immobility. However, he continued that special relativity does not necessarily rule out the aether, because the latter can be used to give physical reality to acceleration and rotation. This concept was fully elaborated within <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, in which physical properties (which are partially determined by matter) are attributed to space, but no substance or state of motion can be attributed to that "aether" (by which he meant curved space-time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kostb_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-kostb-81">[B 13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstb_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-einstb-76">[A 22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In another paper of 1924, named "Concerning the Aether", Einstein argued that Newton\'s absolute space, in which acceleration is absolute, is the "Aether of Mechanics". And within the electromagnetic theory of Maxwell and Lorentz one can speak of the "Aether of Electrodynamics", in which the aether possesses an absolute state of motion. As regards special relativity, also in this theory acceleration is absolute as in Newton\'s mechanics. However, the difference from the electromagnetic aether of Maxwell and Lorentz lies in the fact, that "because it was no longer possible to speak, in any absolute sense, of simultaneous states at different locations in the aether, the aether became, as it were, four dimensional, since there was no objective way of ordering its states by time alone". Now the "aether of special relativity" is still "absolute", because matter is affected by the properties of the aether, but the aether is not affected by the presence of matter. This asymmetry was solved within general relativity. Einstein explained that the "aether of general relativity" is not absolute, because matter is influenced by the aether, just as matter influences the structure of the aether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-einstc_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-einstc-77">[A 23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The only similarity of this relativistic aether concept with the <a href="/wiki/Aether_(classical_element)" title="Aether (classical element)">classical aether</a> models lies in the presence of physical properties in space, which can be identified through <a href="/wiki/Geodesics_in_general_relativity" title="Geodesics in general relativity">geodesics</a>. As historians such as <a href="/wiki/John_Stachel" title="John Stachel">John Stachel</a> argue, Einstein\'s views on the "new aether" are not in conflict with his abandonment of the aether in 1905. As Einstein himself pointed out, no "substance" and no state of motion can be attributed to that new aether. Einstein\'s use of the word "aether" found little support in the scientific community, and played no role in the continuing development of modern physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kosta_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-kosta-79">[B 11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stach_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-stach-80">[B 12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kostb_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-kostb-81">[B 13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aether_concepts">Aether concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
