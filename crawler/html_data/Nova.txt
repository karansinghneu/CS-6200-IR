id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Nova_(disambiguation)" title="Nova (disambiguation)">Nova (disambiguation)</a>, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Novas_(disambiguation)" title="Novas (disambiguation)">Novas (disambiguation)</a>, and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Supernova_(disambiguation)" title="Supernova (disambiguation)">Supernova (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Making_a_Nova.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="169" data-file-width="250" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Making_a_Nova.jpg" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Artist\'s conception of a white dwarf, right, <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accreting</a> hydrogen from the <a href="/wiki/Roche_lobe" title="Roche lobe">Roche lobe</a> of its larger companion star</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>nova</b> (plural <b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nova" title="wiktionary:nova">novae</a></b> or <b>novas</b>) or <b>classical nova</b> (<b>CN</b>, plural <b>CNe</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Transient_astronomical_event" title="Transient astronomical event">transient astronomical event</a> that causes the sudden appearance of a bright, apparently "new" star, that slowly fades over several weeks or many months. Novae involve an interaction between two stars that cause the flareup that is perceived as a new entity that is much brighter than the stars involved.\n</p><p>Causes of the dramatic appearance of a nova vary, depending on the circumstances of the two progenitor stars.  All observed novae involve closely located <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_stars" title="Binary stars">binary stars</a> (the progenitors), either a pair of <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarfs</a> in the process of merging, or a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a> and another star.\n</p><p>The main sub-classes of novae are classical novae, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recurrent_nova" title="Recurrent nova">recurrent novae</a> (RNe), and <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_nova" title="Dwarf nova">dwarf novae</a>.  They are all considered to be <a href="/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable_star" title="Cataclysmic variable star">cataclysmic variable stars</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Luminous_red_nova" title="Luminous red nova">Luminous red novae</a> share the name and are also cataclysmic variables, but are a different type of event caused by a stellar merger.  Also with similar names are the much more energetic <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> (SNe) and <a href="/wiki/Kilonova" title="Kilonova">kilonovae</a>.\n</p><p>Classical nova eruptions are the most common type of nova. They are likely created in a close binary star system consisting of a white dwarf and either a main sequence, sub-giant, or red giant star. When the orbital period falls in the range of several days to one day, the white dwarf is close enough to its companion star to start drawing <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accreted</a> matter onto the surface of the white dwarf, which creates a dense but shallow atmosphere. This atmosphere is mostly <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and is thermally heated by the hot white dwarf, which eventually reaches a critical temperature causing rapid runaway ignition by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a>.\n</p><p>From the dramatic and sudden energies created, the now hydrogen-burnt atmosphere is then dramatically expelled into interstellar space, and its brightened envelope is seen as the visible light created from the nova event, and previously was mistaken as a "new" star. A few novae produce short-lived <a href="/wiki/Nova_remnant" title="Nova remnant">nova remnants</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liimets2014_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liimets2014-1">[1]</a></sup> lasting for perhaps several centuries. Recurrent nova processes are the same as the classical nova, except that the fusion ignition may be repetitive because the companion star can again feed the dense atmosphere of the white dwarf.\n</p><p>Novae most often occur in the sky along the path of the Milky Way, especially near the observed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_centre" title="Galactic centre">galactic centre</a> in Sagittarius; however, they can appear anywhere in the sky. They occur far more <a href="/wiki/List_of_novae_in_the_Milky_Way_galaxy" title="List of novae in the Milky Way galaxy">frequently</a> than galactic supernovae, averaging about ten per year. Most are found telescopically, perhaps only one every year to eighteen months reaching naked-eye visibility. Novae reaching first or second <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">magnitude</a> occur only several times per century. The last bright nova was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V1369_Centauri" title="V1369 Centauri">V1369 Centauri</a> reaching 3.3 magnitude on 14 December 2013.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" tocsection-13"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the sixteenth century, astronomer <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> observed the <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> <a href="/wiki/SN_1572" title="SN 1572">SN 1572</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation)" title="Cassiopeia (constellation)">Cassiopeia</a>. He described it in his book <i>De nova stella</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "concerning the new star"), giving rise to the adoption of the name <i>nova</i>. In this work he argued that a nearby object should be seen to move relative to the fixed stars, and that the nova had to be very far away. Although this event was a supernova and not a nova, the terms were considered interchangeable until the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> After this, novae were classified as <i>classical novae</i> to distinguish them from supernovae, as their causes and energies were thought to be different, based solely in the observational evidence.\n</p><p>Ironically, despite the term "stella nova" meaning "new star", novae most often take place as a result of <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarfs</a>: remnants of extremely <i>old</i> stars.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_evolution_of_novae">Stellar evolution of novae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stellar evolution of novae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Evolution of potential novae begins with two main sequence stars in a binary system. One of the two evolves into a <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a>, leaving its remnant white dwarf core in orbit with the remaining star. The second star&#8212;which may be either a main sequence star or an aging giant&#8212;begins to shed its envelope onto its white dwarf companion when it overflows its <a href="/wiki/Roche_lobe" title="Roche lobe">Roche lobe</a>. As a result, the white dwarf steadily captures matter from the companion\'s outer atmosphere in an accretion disk, and in turn, the accreted matter falls into the atmosphere. As the white dwarf consists of <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">degenerate matter</a>, the accreted hydrogen does not inflate, but its temperature increases. Runaway fusion occurs when the temperature of this atmospheric layer reaches ~20&#160;million <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>, initiating nuclear burning, via the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2802" data-file-width="3600" decoding="async" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg/250px-GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg/375px-GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg/500px-GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GKPersei-MiniSuperNova-20150316.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/GK_Persei" title="GK Persei">GK Persei</a>: Nova of 1901</div></div></div>\n<p>Hydrogen fusion may occur in a stable manner on the surface of the white dwarf for a narrow range of accretion rates, giving rise to a <a href="/wiki/Super_soft_X-ray_source" title="Super soft X-ray source">super soft X-ray source</a>, but for most binary system parameters, the hydrogen burning is unstable thermally and rapidly converts a large amount of the hydrogen into other, heavier <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">runaway</a> reaction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> liberating an enormous amount of energy. This blows the remaining gases away from the surface of the white dwarf surface and produces an extremely bright outburst of light.\n</p><p>The rise to peak brightness may be very rapid, or gradual. This is related to the speed class of the nova; yet after the peak, the brightness declines steadily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The time taken for a nova to decay by around 2 or 3&#160;magnitudes from maximum optical brightness is used for classification, via its speed class. Fast novae typically will take fewer than 25&#160;days to decay by 2&#160;magnitudes, while slow novae will take more than 80&#160;days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In spite of their violence, usually the amount of material ejected in novae is only about &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>10,000</sub></span> of a <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a>, quite small relative to the mass of the white dwarf. Furthermore, only five percent of the accreted mass is fused during the power outburst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> Nonetheless, this is enough energy to accelerate nova ejecta to velocities as high as several thousand kilometers per second&#8212;higher for fast novae than slow ones&#8212;with a concurrent rise in <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> from a few times solar to 50,000&#8211;100,000&#160;times solar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2010 scientists using NASA&#8217;s <a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope</a> discovered that a nova also can emit gamma-rays (&gt;100&#160;MeV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Potentially, a white dwarf can generate multiple novae over time as additional hydrogen continues to accrete onto its surface from its companion star. An example is <a href="/wiki/RS_Ophiuchi" title="RS Ophiuchi">RS Ophiuchi</a>, which is known to have flared six times (in 1898, 1933, 1958, 1967, 1985, and 2006). Eventually, the white dwarf could explode as a Type&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ia_supernova" title="Ia supernova">Ia supernova</a> if it approaches the <a href="/wiki/Chandrasekhar_limit" title="Chandrasekhar limit">Chandrasekhar limit</a>.\n</p><p>Occasionally, novae are bright enough and close enough to Earth to be conspicuous to the unaided eye. The brightest recent example was <a href="/wiki/V1500_Cygni" title="V1500 Cygni">Nova Cygni 1975</a>. This nova appeared on 29&#160;August 1975, in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation)" title="Cygnus (constellation)">Cygnus</a> about five degrees north of <a href="/wiki/Deneb" title="Deneb">Deneb</a>, and reached <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a>&#160;2.0 (nearly as bright as <a href="/wiki/Deneb" title="Deneb">Deneb</a>). The most recent were <a href="/wiki/V1280_Scorpii" title="V1280 Scorpii">V1280 Scorpii</a>, which reached magnitude&#160;3.7 on 17&#160;February 2007, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nova_Delphini_2013" title="Nova Delphini 2013">Nova Delphini 2013</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nova_Centauri_2013" title="Nova Centauri 2013">Nova Centauri 2013</a> was discovered 2&#160;December 2013 and so far, is the brightest nova of this millennium, reaching magnitude&#160;3.3.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helium_novae">Helium novae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Helium novae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A helium nova (undergoing a helium flash) is a proposed category of nova events that lacks hydrogen lines in its spectrum. This may be caused by the explosion of a helium shell on a white dwarf. The theory was first proposed in 1989, and the first candidate helium nova to be observed was <a href="/wiki/V445_Puppis" title="V445 Puppis">V445 Puppis</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Since then, four other novae have been proposed as helium novae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_rate_and_astrophysical_significance">Occurrence rate and astrophysical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence rate and astrophysical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Astronomers estimate that the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> experiences roughly 30 to 60 novae per year, but a recent examination has found the likely improved rate of about 50&#177;27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shafter2017_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shafter2017-10">[10]</a></sup> The number of novae discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> each year is much lower, about 10,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nova_list_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nova_list-11">[11]</a></sup> probably due to distant novae being obscured by gas and dust absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nova_list_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nova_list-11">[11]</a></sup> Roughly 25 novae brighter than about the twentieth magnitude are discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a> each year and smaller numbers are seen in other nearby galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M31-Nova_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-M31-Nova-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopic</a> observation of nova ejecta <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nebulae" title="Nebulae">nebulae</a> has shown that they are enriched in elements such as helium, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, neon, and magnesium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> The contribution of novae to the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> is not great; novae supply only &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>50</sub></span> as much material to the Galaxy as do supernovae, and only &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>200</sub></span> as much as <a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">supergiant</a> stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Recurrent novae such as <a href="/wiki/RS_Ophiuchi" title="RS Ophiuchi">RS Ophiuchi</a> (those with periods on the order of decades), are rare. Astronomers theorize, however, that most, if not all, novae are recurrent, albeit on time scales ranging from 1,000 to 100,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The recurrence interval for a nova is less dependent on the accretion rate of the white dwarf, than on its mass; with their powerful gravity, massive white dwarfs require less accretion to fuel an outburst than lower-mass ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup> Consequently, the interval is shorter for high-mass white dwarfs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyc_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtypes">Subtypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subtypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Novae are classified according to the light curve development speed, so in \n</p>\n<ul><li><b>NA</b>: fast novae, with a rapid brightness increase, followed by a brightness decline of 3 magnitudes &#8212; to about &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>16</sub></span> brightness &#8212; within 100 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heasarc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-heasarc-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>NB</b>: slow novae, with magnitudes of 3, decline in 150 days or more.</li>\n<li><b>NC</b>: very slow novae, also known as <a href="/wiki/Symbiotic_nova" title="Symbiotic nova">symbiotic novae</a>, staying at maximum light for a decade or more and then fading very slowly.</li>\n<li><b>NR</b>/<b>RN</b>: recurrent novae, novae with two or more outbursts separated by 10&#8211;80 years have been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Novae_as_distance_indicators">Novae as distance indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
