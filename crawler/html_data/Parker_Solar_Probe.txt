mission\n&#160;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>Parker Solar Probe</b></i> (previously <i><b>Solar Probe</b></i>, <i><b>Solar Probe Plus</b></i>, or <i><b>Solar Probe+</b></i>, abbreviated <b>PSP</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc22jl18_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc22jl18-9">[8]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">robotic spacecraft</a> launched in 2018 and currently <i>en route</i> to probe the outer <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20180811_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20180811-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170531_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170531-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-feasible_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-feasible-6">[6]</a></sup> It will approach to within 9.86 <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">solar radii</a> (6.9 million kilometers or 4.3 million miles)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressKit_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-PressKit-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> from the center of the Sun and by 2025 will travel, at closest approach, as fast as 690,000&#160;km/h (430,000&#160;mph), or 0.064% the speed of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressKit_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-PressKit-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-201180809_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-201180809-13">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The project was announced in the fiscal 2009 budget year. The cost of the project is US$1.5 billion. <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> <a href="/wiki/Applied_Physics_Laboratory" title="Applied Physics Laboratory">Applied Physics Laboratory</a> designed and built the spacecraft,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> which was launched on August 12, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20180812-kc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20180812-kc-2">[2]</a></sup> It became the first NASA spacecraft  named after a living person, honoring physicist <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">Eugene Parker</a>, professor emeritus at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Memory_card" title="Memory card">memory card</a> containing the names of over 1.1 million people was mounted on a plaque and installed below the spacecraft\'s high-gain antenna on May 18, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The card also contains photos of Parker and a copy of his 1958 scientific paper predicting important aspects of solar physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 October 2018 at about 1:04&#160;p.m. EDT, the spacecraft became the closest ever man-made object to the Sun.  The previous record, 26.55 million miles from the Sun\'s surface, was set by the <i><a href="/wiki/Helios_(spacecraft)" title="Helios (spacecraft)">Helios 2</a></i> spacecraft in April 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" href="/wiki/File:NASA%27s_Parker_Solar_Probe_Mission_Launches_to_Touch_the_Sun.webm" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch of the probe</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Parker Solar Probe</i> concept originates from a predecessor <i>Solar Orbiter</i> project conceived in the 1990s. Similar in design and objectives, the <i>Solar Probe</i> mission served as one of the centerpieces of the <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponymous</a> Outer Planet/Solar Probe (OPSP) program formulated by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>. The first three missions of the program were planned to be: the <i>Solar Orbiter</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> reconnaissance mission <i><a href="/wiki/Pluto_Kuiper_Express" title="Pluto Kuiper Express">Pluto Kuiper Express</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Europa_Orbiter" title="Europa Orbiter">Europa Orbiter</a></i> astrobiology mission focused on <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opsp_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-opsp-20">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The original <i>Solar Probe</i> design used a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a> from <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> to enter a polar orbit which dropped almost directly toward the Sun. While this explored the important solar poles and came even closer to the surface (3&#160;<var>R</var><sub>&#9737;</sub>, a perihelion of 4&#160;<var>R</var><sub>&#9737;</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opsp_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-opsp-20">[19]</a></sup> the extreme variation in solar irradiance made for an expensive mission and required a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermal_generator" title="Radioisotope thermal generator">radioisotope thermal generator</a> for power. The trip to Jupiter also made for a long mission (&#8203;<span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> years to first solar perihelion, 8 years to second).\n</p><p>Following the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Keefe" title="Sean O\'Keefe">Sean O\'Keefe</a> as <a href="/wiki/List_of_Administrators_and_Deputy_Administrators_of_NASA" title="List of Administrators and Deputy Administrators of NASA">Administrator of NASA</a>, the entirety of the OPSP program was canceled as part of President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>\'s request for the <a href="/wiki/2003_United_States_federal_budget" title="2003 United States federal budget">2003 United States federal budget</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com-1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-space.com-1-21">[20]</a></sup> Administrator O\'Keefe cited a need for a restructuring of NASA and its projects, falling in line with the Bush Administration\'s wish for NASA to refocus on "research and development, and addressing management shortcomings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com-1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-space.com-1-21">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The cancellation of the program also resulted in the initial cancellation of <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i>, the mission that eventually won the competition to replace <i>Pluto Kuiper Express</i> in the former OPSP program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> That mission, which would eventually be launched as the first mission of the <a href="/wiki/New_Frontiers_program" title="New Frontiers program">New Frontiers program</a>, a conceptual successor to the OPSP program, would undergo a lengthy political battle to secure funding for its launch, which occurred in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the early 2010s, plans for the <i>Solar Probe</i> mission were incorporated into a lower-cost <i>Solar Probe Plus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup>  The redesigned mission uses multiple Venus gravity assists for a more direct flight path, which can be powered by <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panels</a>. It also has a higher perihelion, reducing the demands on the thermal protection system.\n</p><p>In May 2017, the spacecraft was renamed <i>Parker Solar Probe</i> in honor of astrophysicist <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">Eugene Parker</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20180810kc_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20180810kc-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> coiner of the term "<a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>". The solar probe cost NASA US$1.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> The launch rocket bore a dedication in memory of APL engineer Andrew A. Dantzler who had worked on the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_2" style="width:220px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:854px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_3" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Parker_Solar_Probe.webm/854px--Parker_Solar_Probe.webm.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:854px;height:480px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="55.859" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Parker_Solar_Probe.webm" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/b/b3/Parker_Solar_Probe.webm/Parker_Solar_Probe.webm.480p.vp9.webm" type="video/webm; It will assess the structure and dynamics of the Sun\'s coronal plasma and magnetic field, the energy flow that heats the solar corona and impels the solar wind, and the mechanisms that accelerate energetic particles.\n</p><p>The spacecraft\'s systems are protected from the extreme heat and radiation near the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> by a solar shield. Incident solar radiation at perihelion is approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005650000000000000&#9824;</span>650&#160;kW/m<sup>2</sup></span>, or 475 times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">intensity at Earth orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extreme_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-extreme-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap">:<span>31</span></sup> The solar shield is hexagonal, mounted on the Sun-facing side of the spacecraft, 2.3&#160;m (7.5&#160;ft) in diameter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jhuapl_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-jhuapl-31">[30]</a></sup> 11.4&#160;cm (4.5&#160;in) thick, and is made of <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_carbon%E2%80%93carbon" title="Reinforced carbon&#8211;carbon">reinforced carbon&#8211;carbon</a> composite, which is designed to withstand temperatures outside the spacecraft of about 1,370&#160;&#176;C (2,500&#160;&#176;F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extreme_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-extreme-1">[1]</a></sup>  A white reflective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> surface layer minimizes absorption. The spacecraft systems and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_instruments" title="Scientific instruments">scientific instruments</a> are located in the central portion of the shield\'s shadow, where direct radiation from the Sun is fully blocked. If the shield were not between the spacecraft and the Sun, the probe would be damaged and become inoperative within tens of seconds. As radio communication with Earth will take about eight minutes, the <i>Parker Solar Probe</i> will have to act autonomously and rapidly to protect itself. This will be done using four light sensors to detect the first traces of direct sun light coming from the shield limits and engaging movements from fly wheels to reposition the spacecraft within the shadow again. According to project scientist Nicky Fox, the team describe it as "the most autonomous spacecraft that has ever flown".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc22jl18_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc22jl18-9">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The primary power for the mission is a dual system of <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panels</a> (<a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic arrays</a>). A primary photovoltaic array, used for the portion of the mission outside <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010373994676750000&#9824;</span>0.25&#160;AU</span>, is retracted behind the shadow shield during the close approach to the Sun, and a much smaller secondary array powers the spacecraft through closest approach. This secondary array uses pumped-fluid cooling to maintain <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a> of the solar panels and instrumentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trajectory">Trajectory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trajectory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="560" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif/220px-Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif/330px-Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif/440px-Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Animation_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_trajectory.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of <i>Parker Solar Probe</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> trajectory from August 7, 2018 to August 29, 2025 <br/><span style="margin:0; The velocity decreases by 2.9&#160;km/s to 26&#160;km/s (red circle), shifting the spacecraft to a new orbit closer to the Sun.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Parker Solar Probe</i> mission design uses repeated <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assists</a> at Venus to incrementally decrease its orbital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> to achieve a final altitude (above the surface) of approximately 8.5 solar radii, or about 6<span style="margin-left:0.2em">&#215;<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;km (3.7<span style="margin-left:0.2em">&#215;<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>6</sup>&#160;mi; 0.040&#160;AU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jhuapl_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-jhuapl-31">[30]</a></sup> The spacecraft trajectory will include seven Venus flybys over nearly seven years to gradually shrink its elliptical orbit around the Sun, for a total of 24 orbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extreme_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-extreme-1">[1]</a></sup> The near Sun radiation environment is predicted to cause spacecraft charging effects, radiation damage in materials and electronics, and communication interruptions, so the orbit will be highly elliptical with short times spent near the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-30">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The trajectory requires high launch energy, so the probe was launched on a <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV_Heavy" title="Delta IV Heavy">Delta IV Heavy</a> class <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> and an upper stage based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STAR-48" title="STAR-48">STAR 48BV</a> solid rocket motor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-30">[29]</a></sup> Interplanetary <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assists</a> will provide further deceleration relative to its <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>, which will result in a heliocentric speed record at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delta_4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Delta_4-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> As the probe passes around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, it will achieve a velocity of up to 200&#160;km/s (120&#160;mi/s), which will temporarily make it the fastest manmade object, almost three times as fast as the current record holder, <a href="/wiki/Helios_(spacecraft)#Helios-B" title="Helios (spacecraft)">Helios-B</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Like every object in an orbit, due to gravity the spacecraft will accelerate as it nears perihelion, then slow down again afterward until it reaches its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Within each orbit of the Parker Solar Probe around the Sun, the portion within 0.25&#160;AU will be the Science Phase, in which the probe will be actively and autonomously making observations.  Communication with the probe will be largely cut off in that phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_design_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-mission_design-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup>  These science phases will run for a few days both before and after each perihelion.  They will last 11.6 days for the earliest perihelion, and drop to 9.6 days for the final, closest perihelion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_design_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-mission_design-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8</span></sup>\n</p><p>Much of the rest of each orbit will be devoted to transmitting data from the science (observation and measurement) phase.  But during this part of each orbit, there will still be periods when communication is impeded or not possible.  First, the heat shield of the probe must be pointed towards the Sun; there will be times when that will put the heat shield between the antenna and Earth.  Secondly, even when the probe is not particularly near the Sun, when the angle between the probe and the Sun, as seen from Earth, is too small, the Sun\'s radiation will overwhelm the communication link.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_design_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-mission_design-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11&#8211;14</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_goals">Science goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-30">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Trace the flow of energy that heats the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> and accelerates the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</li>\n<li>Determine the structure and dynamics of the <a href="/wiki/Sun#Magnetic_field" title="Sun">magnetic fields</a> at the sources of solar wind.</li>\n<li>Determine what mechanisms accelerate and transport energetic particles.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Investigations">Investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Investigations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To achieve these goals, the mission will perform five major experiments or investigations:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-30">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Electromagnetic Fields Investigation (<b><a href="/wiki/FIELDS" title="FIELDS">FIELDS</a></b>)&#160;&#8211; This investigation will make direct measurements of <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, radio waves, <a href="/wiki/Poynting_vector" title="Poynting vector">Poynting flux</a>, absolute <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> density, and <a href="/wiki/Electron_temperature" title="Electron temperature">electron temperature</a>. It consists of two <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer#Fluxgate_magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">flux-gate magnetometers</a>, a search-coil magnetometer, and 5 plasma voltage sensors. The Principal investigator is Stuart Bale, at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>.</li>\n<li>Integrated Science Investigation of the Sun (<b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IS%E2%98%89IS" title="IS&#9737;IS">IS&#9737;IS</a></b>)&#160;&#8211; This investigation will measure  energetic electrons, protons and heavy ions. The instrument suite is composed of two independent instruments, EPI-Hi and EPI-Lo. The Principal investigator is David McComas, at the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.</li>\n<li>Wide-field Imager for Solar Probe (<b><a href="/wiki/WISPR" title="WISPR">WISPR</a></b>)&#160;&#8211; These optical telescopes will acquire  images of the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> and inner <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">heliosphere</a>. The Principal Investigator is Russell Howard, at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a>.</li>\n<li>Solar Wind Electrons Alphas and Protons (<b><a href="/wiki/SWEAP" title="SWEAP">SWEAP</a></b>)&#160;&#8211; This investigation will count the  electrons, protons and helium ions, and measure their properties such as velocity, density, and temperature. Its main instruments are the Solar Probe Analyzers (SPAN, two <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_analyzer" title="Electrostatic analyzer">electrostatic analyzers</a>) and the Solar Probe Cup (SPC, a <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cup" title="Faraday cup">Faraday cup</a>). The Principal Investigator is Justin Kasper at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory</a>.</li>\n<li>Heliospheric Origins with Solar Probe Plus (<b>HeliOSPP</b>)&#160;&#8211; A theory and modeling investigation to maximize the scientific return from the mission. The Principal Investigator is Marco Velli at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">Jet Propulsion Laboratory</a> (JPL).</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the first Venus <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">flyby</a>, the probe will be in an elliptical orbit with a period of 150 days (two-thirds the period of Venus), making three orbits while Venus makes two. On the second flyby, the period shortens to 130 days. After less than two orbits (only 198 days later) it encounters Venus a third time at a point earlier in the orbit of Venus. This encounter shortens its period to half of that of Venus, or about 112.5 days. After two orbits it meets Venus a fourth time at about the same place, shortening its period to about 102 days. After 237 days it meets Venus for the fifth time and its period is shortened to about 96 days, three-sevenths that of Venus. It then makes seven orbits while Venus makes three. The sixth encounter, almost two years after the fifth, brings its period down to 92 days, two-fifths that of Venus. After five more orbits (two orbits of Venus) it meets Venus for the seventh and last time, decreasing its period to 88 or 89 days and allowing it to approach closer to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the_mission_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-the_mission-39">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:center ; max-width:908px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Velocity_of_Parker_Solar_Probe_wide.svg" title="Speed of the probe and distance from the Sun, from launch until 2026. The spacecraft operated nominally after launching. During its first week in space it deployed its high-gain antenna, magnetometer boom, and electric field antennas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> The spacecraft performed its first scheduled trajectory correction on 20 August 2018, while it was 5.5 million miles from Earth, and travelling at 63,569&#160;km/h (39,500&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Instrument activation and testing began in early September 2018. test, transmitting wide-angle images of the background sky towards the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">galactic center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The probe successfully performed the first of the seven planned Venus flybys on 3 October 2018, where it came within about 1,500 miles (2,400&#160;km) of Venus in order to reduce the probe\'s speed and orbit closer to the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The first scientific
