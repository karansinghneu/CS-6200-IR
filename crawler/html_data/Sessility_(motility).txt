tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydras_(8).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2051" decoding="async" href="/wiki/File:Hydras_(8).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Generally sessile <i>Hydra</i> attached to a substrate</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Sessility</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> property of an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> describing its lack of a means of self-locomotion.  Absent natural <i><a href="/wiki/Motility" title="Motility">motility</a></i> sessile organisms are normally immobile. This is distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(botany)" title="Sessility (botany)">botanical</a> meaning of sessility, which refers to an organism or biological structure attached directly by its base without a stalk.\n</p><p>Sessile organisms can move via external forces (such as water currents), but are usually permanently attached to something. Organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> lay down their own <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(biology)" title="Substrate (biology)">substrate</a> from which they grow.  Other sessile organisms grow from a solid such as a rock, dead tree trunk, or a manmade object such as a buoy or ship\'s hull.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobility">Mobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sessility_(motility)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Sessile animals typically have a motile phase in their development. <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">Sponges</a> have a motile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larval" title="Larval">larval</a> stage, which becomes sessile at maturity. In contrast, many <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> develop as sessile <a href="/wiki/Polyp" title="Polyp">polyps</a> early in their life cycle. In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Cochineal" title="Cochineal">cochineal</a>, it is in the nymph stage (also called the crawler stage) that the cochineal disperses. The juveniles move to a feeding spot and produce long wax filaments. Later they move to the edge of the cactus pad where the wind catches the wax filaments and carries the tiny larval cochineals to a new host.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sessility_(motility)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many sessile animals, including sponges, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(genus)" title="Hydra (genus)">hydra</a>, are capable of <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a> <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> by the process of <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a>.  Sessile organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tunicate" title="Tunicate">tunicates</a> need some mechanism to move their young into new territory. This is why the most widely accepted theory explaining the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larval</a> stage is the need for long-distance dispersal ability. Biologist <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Sousa" title="Wayne Sousa">Wayne Sousa</a>\'s 1979 study in intertidal disturbance added support for the theory of nonequilibrium community structure, &#8220;suggesting that open space is necessary for the maintenance of diversity in most communities of sessile organisms.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clumping">Clumping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a exhibit clumping</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Clumping</b> is a behavior in sessile organisms, in which individuals of a particular species group closely to one another for beneficial purposes, and can be seen in <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> and cochineal populations. This allows for faster reproduction and better protection from predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predominance_in_coastal_environments">Predominance in coastal environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sessility_(motility)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Predominance in coastal environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">circalittoral zone</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal</a> environments and <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a> are dominated by sessile organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_platform" title="Carbonate platform">Carbonate platforms</a> grow due to the buildup of skeletal remains of sessile organisms, usually <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, which induce carbonate precipitation through their metabolism.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Botanical_sessility">Botanical sessility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sessility_(motility)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Botanical sessility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In anatomy and botany, <i>sessility</i> refers to an organism or biological structure that has no <i>peduncle</i> or stalk. See <a href="/wiki/Peduncle_(anatomy)" title="Peduncle (anatomy)">peduncle (anatomy)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peduncle_(botany)" title="Peduncle (botany)">peduncle (botany)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(botany)" title="Sessility (botany)">sessility (botany)</a>.
