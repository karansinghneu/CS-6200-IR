2017</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>rover</b> (or sometimes <b>planetary rover</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Space_Exploration_Vehicle" title="Space Exploration Vehicle">space exploration vehicle</a> designed to move across the surface of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a>. Some rovers have been designed to transport members of a <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> crew; others have been partially or fully <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_robot" title="Autonomous robot">autonomous robots</a>. Rovers usually arrive at the planetary surface on a <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">lander</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Rovers are created to land on another <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, besides <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, to find out information and to take samples. They can collect dust, rocks, and even take pictures. They are very useful for exploring the universe.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div title="Lander (spacecraft)">landers</a>: they examine more territory and they can be directed to interesting features. If they are solar powered, they can place themselves in sunny positions to weather winter months. They can also advance the knowledge of how to perform very remote <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">robotic</a> vehicle control which is necessarily semi-autonomous due to the finite speed of light.\n</p><p>Their advantages over <a href="/wiki/Orbiter" title="Orbiter">orbiting spacecraft</a> are that they can make observations to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">microscopic</a> level and can conduct physical <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimentation</a>. Disadvantages of rovers compared to orbiters are the higher chance of failure, due to landing and other risks, and that they are limited to a small area around a landing site which itself is only approximately anticipated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a spacecraft and are used in conditions very distinct from those on the Earth, which makes some demands on their design.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span usually packed for placing in a spacecraft, because it has limited capacity, and has to be deployed. They are also attached to a spacecraft, so devices for removing these connections are installed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autonomy">Autonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rover_(space_exploration)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Autonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rovers which land on celestial bodies far from the Earth, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rovers" title="Mars Exploration Rovers">Mars Exploration Rovers</a>, cannot be remotely controlled in real-time since the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed at which radio signals travel</a> is far too slow for <i>real time</i> or <i>near-real time</i> communication. For example, sending a signal from Mars to Earth takes between 3 and 21 minutes. These rovers are thus capable of operating <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_robot" title="Autonomous robot">autonomously</a> with little assistance from ground control as far as <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_robot#Outdoor_autonomous_position-sensing_and_navigation" title="Autonomous robot">navigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_acquisition" title="Data acquisition">data acquisition</a> are concerned, although they still require human input for identifying promising targets in the distance to which to drive, and determining how to position itself to maximize solar energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schirber_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schirber-2">[2]</a></sup> Giving a rover some rudimentary visual identification capabilities to make simple distinctions can allow engineers to speed up the reconnaissance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schirber_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schirber-2">[2]</a></sup> During the NASA Sample Return Robot Centennial Challenge, a rover, named <i>Cataglyphis</i>, successfully demonstrated autonomous navigation, decision-making, and sample detection, retrieval, and return capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-wheeled_approaches">Non-wheeled approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rover_(space_exploration)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-wheeled approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Other rover designs that do not use wheeled approaches are possible.  Mechanisms that utilize "walking" on <a href="/wiki/Robotic_arm" title="Robotic arm">robotic legs</a>, hopping, rolling, etc. are possible. From November 17, 1970 to November 22, 1970 the rover drove 197 m, and during 10 communication sessions returned 14 close up pictures of the Moon and 12 panoramic views. It also analyzed the lunar soil. The last successful communications session with Lunokhod 1 was on September 14, 1971. <i>Opportunity</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rover_(space_exploration)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover">Opportunity rover</a></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover"><i>Opportunity</i></a> is a <a href="/wiki/Mars_rover" title="Mars rover">robotic rover on the planet Mars</a>, active from 2004 to 2018. Launched from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> on July 7, 2003, it landed on the <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Martian</a> <a href="/wiki/Meridiani_Planum" title="Meridiani Planum">Meridiani Planum</a> on January 25, 2004 at 05:05 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_UTC" title="Ground UTC">Ground UTC</a> (about 13:15 <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars" title="Timekeeping on Mars">local time</a>), three weeks after its twin <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_rover" title="Spirit rover">Spirit</a></i> (MER-A) touched down on the other side of the planet. On July 28, 2014, NASA announced that <i>Opportunity</i>, after having traveled over 40&#160;km (25&#160;mi) on the planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, has set a new "off-world" record as the rover having driven the greatest distance, surpassing the previous record held by the Soviet Union\'s <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">Lunokhod 2 rover</a> that had traveled 39&#160;km (24&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140728a_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140728a-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FRB-20140729_25-0"><a title="Chandrayaan-2">Chandrayaan-2</a></div>\n<p>The Chandrayaan-2 is a mission by India, consisting of a lunar orbiter, a lander, and a rover. An opportunity was given to students to design this rover. 150 students submitted their designs but only six were selected. The Indian-designed rover weighs 20&#160;kg, will have six wheels and will run on solar power. It will land near one of the lunar poles and will operate for two weeks. The proposed launch is in April 2019.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ExoMars_Rosalind_Franklin">ExoMars Rosalind Franklin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rover_(space_exploration)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: ExoMars Rosalind Franklin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Franklin_(rover)" title="Rosalind Franklin (rover)">Rosalind Franklin (rover)</a></div>\n<p>The European Space Agency (ESA) has designed and carried out early prototyping and testing of the <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Franklin_(rover)" title="Rosalind Franklin (rover)">Rosalind Franklin</a> rover.
