The mission succeeded in closing in with the asteroid and orbited it several times, finally terminating by touching down on the asteroid on 12 February 2001.\n</p><p>The primary scientific objective of NEAR was to return data on the bulk properties, composition, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineralogy</a>, morphology, internal <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> distribution and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> of Eros. Secondary objectives include studies of <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> properties, interactions with the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, possible current activity as indicated by dust or gas, and the asteroid spin state. This data will be used to help understand the characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> in general, their relationship to <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>, and the conditions in the early Solar System. To accomplish these goals, the spacecraft was equipped with an <a href="/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy" title="X-ray spectroscopy">X-ray</a>/<a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_spectrometer" title="Gamma-ray spectrometer">gamma-ray spectrometer</a>, a near-infrared imaging spectrograph, a multi-spectral camera fitted with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCD_imaging" title="CCD imaging">CCD imaging</a> detector, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_range-finder" title="Laser range-finder">laser rangefinder</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>. A radio science experiment was also performed using the NEAR tracking system to estimate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational" title="Gravitational">gravity</a> field of the asteroid. The total mass of the instruments was 56&#160;kg, and they required 80 W power.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" spacecraft.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The primary goal of the mission was to study the near-Earth asteroid <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">433 Eros</a> from orbit for approximately one year. Eros is an <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">S-type asteroid</a> approximately 13 &#215; 13 &#215; 33&#160;km in size, the second largest near-Earth asteroid. Initially the orbit was circular with a radius of 200&#160;km. The radius of the orbit was brought down in stages to a 50 &#215; 50&#160;km orbit on 30 April 2000 and decreased to 35 &#215; 35&#160;km on July 14, 2000. The orbit was raised over succeeding months to a 200 &#215; 200&#160;km orbit and then slowly decreased and altered to a 35 &#215; 35&#160;km retrograde orbit on December 13, 2000. The mission ended with a touchdown in the "saddle" region of Eros on February 12, 2001.\n</p><p>Some scientists claim that the ultimate goal of the mission was to link Eros, an asteroidal body, to meteorites recovered on Earth. With sufficient data on chemical composition, a causal link could be established between Eros and other S-type asteroids, and those meteorites believed to be pieces of S-type asteroids (perhaps Eros itself). Once this connection is established, meteorite material can be studied with large, complex, and evolving equipment, and the results extrapolated to bodies in space. NEAR-Shoemaker did not prove or disprove this link to the satisfaction of scientists.\n</p><p>Between December 1999 and February 2001 NEAR Shoemaker used its gamma-ray spectrometer to detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_bursts" title="Gamma-ray bursts">gamma-ray bursts</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/InterPlanetary_Network" title="InterPlanetary Network">InterPlanetary Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trombka_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trombka-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_journey_to_Mathilde">The journey to Mathilde</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The journey to Mathilde">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NEARlaunch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2186" data-file-width="2175" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/NEARlaunch.jpg/220px-NEARlaunch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/NEARlaunch.jpg/330px-NEARlaunch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/NEARlaunch.jpg/440px-NEARlaunch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NEARlaunch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch of the NEAR spacecraft, February 1996.</div></div></div>\n<p>After launch on a Delta 7925-8 (a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> launch vehicle with nine strap-on solid-rocket boosters and a Star 48 (PAM-D) third stage) and exit from Earth orbit, NEAR entered the first part of its cruise phase. NEAR spent most of the cruise phase in a minimal activity "hibernation" state, which ended a few days before the flyby of the 61&#160;km diameter asteroid <a href="/wiki/253_Mathilde" title="253 Mathilde">253 Mathilde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:(253)_mathilde_crop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="284" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/%28253%29_mathilde_crop.jpg/220px-%28253%29_mathilde_crop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/%28253%29_mathilde_crop.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:(253)_mathilde_crop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the images from the flyby of <a href="/wiki/253_Mathilde" title="253 Mathilde">253 Mathilde</a></div></div></div>\n<p>On 27 June 1997, NEAR flew by Mathilde within 1200&#160;km at 12:56 UT at 9.93&#160;km/s, returning imaging and other instrument data. The flyby produced over 500 images, covering 60% of Mathilde\'s surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flyby_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-flyby-5">[5]</a></sup> as well as gravitational data allowing calculations of Mathilde\'s dimensions and mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yeomans_1997_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yeomans_1997-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_journey_to_Eros">The journey to Eros</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The journey to Eros">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On July 3, 1997, NEAR executed the first major deep space maneuver, a two-part burn of the main 450&#160;N thruster. This decreased the velocity by 279&#160;m/s and lowered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> from 0.99&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> to 0.95&#160;AU. The Earth <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assist</a> swingby occurred on January 23, 1998 at 7:23 UT. The closest approach was 540&#160;km, altering the orbital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> from 0.5 to 10.2&#160;degrees, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a> distance from 2.17 to 1.77&#160;AU, nearly matching those of Eros. Instrumentation was active at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_of_first_attempt_at_orbital_insertion">Failure of first attempt at orbital insertion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Failure of first attempt at orbital insertion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first of four scheduled rendezvous burns was attempted on December 20, 1998 at 22:00 UT. The burn sequence was initiated but immediately aborted. The spacecraft subsequently entered <a href="/wiki/Safe_mode_(spacecraft)" title="Safe mode (spacecraft)">safe mode</a> and began tumbling. The spacecraft\'s <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">thrusters</a> fired thousands of times during the anomaly, which expended 29&#160;kg of propellant reducing the program\'s propellant margin to zero. This anomaly almost resulted in the loss of the spacecraft due to lack of solar orientation and subsequent battery drain. Contact between the spacecraft and mission control could not be established for over 24 hours. The root cause of this incident has not been determined, but software and operational errors contributed to the severity of the anomaly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The original mission plan called for the four burns to be followed by an orbit insertion burn on January 10, 1999, but the abort of the first burn and loss of communication made this impossible. A new plan was put into effect in which NEAR flew by Eros on December 23, 1998 at 18:41:23 UT at a speed of 965&#160;m/s and a distance of 3827&#160;km from the center of mass of Eros. Images of Eros were taken by the camera, data were collected by the near <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">IR</a> spectrograph, and radio tracking was performed during the flyby. A rendezvous maneuver was performed on January 3, 1999 involving a thruster burn to match NEAR\'s orbital speed to that of Eros. A <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> thruster burn took place on January 20 to fine-tune the trajectory. On August 12 a two-minute thruster burn slowed the spacecraft velocity relative to Eros to 300&#160;km/h.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_insertion">Orbital insertion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Eros</a></span></div></div></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_insertion" title="Orbital insertion">Orbital insertion</a> around Eros occurred on 14 February 2000 at 15:33 UT (10:33 AM EST) after NEAR completed a 13-month heliocentric orbit which closely matched the orbit of Eros. A rendezvous maneuver was completed on February 3 at 17:00 UT, slowing the spacecraft from 19.3 to 8.1&#160;m/s relative to Eros. Another maneuver took place on February 8 increasing the relative velocity slightly to 9.9&#160;m/s. Searches for satellites of Eros took place on January 28, and 4 and 9 February; none were found. The scans were for scientific purposes and to mitigate any chances of collision with a satellite. NEAR went into a 321&#215;366&#160;km elliptical orbit around Eros on February 14. The orbit was slowly decreased to a 35&#160;km circular polar orbit by July 14. NEAR remained in this orbit for 10 days and then was backed out in stages to a 100&#160;km circular orbit by September 5, 2000. Maneuvers in mid-October led to a flyby of Eros within 5.3&#160;km of the surface at 07:00 UT on 26 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbits_and_landing">Orbits and landing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Orbits and landing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erosregolith.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Erosregolith.jpg/200px-Erosregolith.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Erosregolith.jpg/300px-Erosregolith.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Erosregolith.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erosregolith.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eros asteroid from approximately 250&#160;meters altitude (area in image is roughly 12&#160;meters across <a class="external autonumber" href="http://near.jhuapl.edu/iod/20010214/index.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>). This image was taken during NEAR\'s descent to the surface of the asteroid.</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the flyby NEAR moved to a 200&#160;km circular orbit and shifted the orbit from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_motion" title="Direct motion">prograde</a> near-polar to a retrograde near-equatorial orbit. By December 13, 2000 the orbit was shifted back to a circular 35&#160;km low orbit. Starting on January 24, 2001 the spacecraft began a series of close passes (5 to 6&#160;km) to the surface and on January 28 passed 2 to 3&#160;km from the asteroid. The spacecraft then made a slow controlled descent to the surface of Eros ending with a touchdown just to the south of the saddle-shaped feature Himeros on February 12, 2001 at approximately 20:01 UT (3:01&#160;p.m. EST). To the surprise of the controllers, the spacecraft was undamaged and operational after the landing at an estimated speed of 1.5 to 1.8&#160;meters per second (thus becoming the first spacecraft to soft-land on an asteroid). After receiving an extension of antenna time on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Network" title="Deep Space Network">Deep Space Network</a>, the spacecraft\'s gamma-ray spectrometer was reprogrammed to collect data on Eros\' composition from a vantage point about 4 inches (100&#160;mm) from the surface where it was ten times more sensitive than when it was used in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phone_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-phone-8">[8]</a></sup> This increase in sensitivity was in part due to the increased ratio of the signal from Eros compared to noise generated by the probe itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trombka_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trombka-3">[3]</a></sup> The impact of cosmic rays on the sensor was also reduced by about 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trombka_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Trombka-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At 7 p.m. EST on February 28, 2001 the last data signals were received from NEAR Shoemaker before it was shut down. A final attempt to communicate with the spacecraft on December 10, 2002 was unsuccessful. This was likely due to the extreme &#8722;279&#160;&#176;F (&#8722;173&#160;&#176;C, 100 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) conditions the probe experienced while on Eros.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-silent_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-silent-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_and_subsystems">Spacecraft and subsystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NEAR_Shoemaker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spacecraft and subsystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NEARCraft.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="606" data-file-width="458" decoding="async" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/NEARCraft.jpg/200px-NEARCraft.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/NEARCraft.jpg/300px-NEARCraft.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/NEARCraft.jpg/400px-NEARCraft.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NEARCraft.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NEAR spacecraft inside its <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> rocket.</div></div></div>\n<p>The spacecraft has the shape of an octagonal prism, approximately 1.7 m on a side, with four fixed <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_module" title="Photovoltaic module">solar panels</a> in a windmill arrangement, a fixed 1.5 m <a href="/wiki/X_band" title="X band">X-band</a> high-gain radio antenna with a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> mounted on the antenna feed, and an X-ray solar monitor on one end (the forward deck), with the other instruments fixed on the opposite end (the aft deck). Most electronics were mounted on the inside of the decks. The propulsion module was contained in the interior. The decision to mount instruments on the body of the spacecraft rather than using booms resulted in the gamma-ray spectrometer needing to be shielded from noise generated by the craft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trombka_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Trombka-3">[3]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_germanate" title="Bismuth germanate">bismuth germanate</a> shield was used although this proved only moderately effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trombka_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Trombka-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The craft was three-axis stabilized and used a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipropellant_rocket" title="Bipropellant rocket">bipropellant</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a>) 450 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> (N) main thruster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and four 21&#160;N and seven 3.5&#160;N hydrazine thrusters for propulsion, for a total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta-V" title="Delta-V">delta-V</a> potential of 1450&#160;m/s. Attitude control was achieved using the hydrazine thrusters and four reaction wheels. The propulsion system carried 209&#160;kg of hydrazine and 109&#160;kg of NTO oxidizer in two oxidizer and three fuel tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Power was provided by four 1.8 by 1.2&#160;meter <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> solar panels which could produce 400&#160;<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> at 2.2&#160;<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (329,000,000&#160;km), NEAR\'s maximum distance from the Sun, and 1800 W at one AU (150,000,000&#160;km). Power was stored in a nine ampere-hour, 22-cell rechargeable super <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel-cadmium" title="Nickel-cadmium">nickel-cadmium</a> battery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="613" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg/220px-NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg/330px-NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg/440px-NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NEAR_Spacecraft_Configuration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing location of NEAR science instruments.</div></div></div>\n<p>Spacecraft guidance was achieved through the use of a sensor suite of five digital solar attitude detectors, an inertial measurement unit (IMU), and a star tracker camera pointed opposite the instrument pointing direction. The IMU contained hemispherical resonator <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscopes</a> and accelerometers. Four <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">reaction wheels</a> (arranged so that any three can provide complete three-axis control) were used for normal attitude control. The thrusters were used to dump <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> from the reaction wheels, as well as for rapid slew and propulsive maneuvers. Attitude control was to 0.1 degree, line-of-sight pointing stability is within 50 microradians over one second, and post-processing attitude knowledge is to 50 microradians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The command and data handling subsystem was composed of two redundant command and telemetry processors and solid state recorders, a power switching unit, and an interface to two redundant <a href="/wiki/MIL-STD-1553" title="MIL-STD-1553">1553 standard data buses</a> for communications with other subsystems.\nNEAR is the first APL spacecraft to use significant numbers of plastic encapsulated microcircuits (PEMs).\nNEAR is the first APL spacecraft to use solid-state data recorders for mass storage&#8212;previous APL spacecraft used magnetic tape recorders or magnetic cores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The solid state recorders are constructed from 16 Mbit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Business_Machines" title="International Business Machines">IBM</a> Luna-C <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DRAM" title="DRAM">DRAMs</a>. One recorder has 1.1 <a href="/wiki/Gigabit" title="Gigabit">gigabits</a> of storage, the other has 0.67 gigabits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSSDC_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-NSSDC-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The NEAR mission was the first launch of NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">Discovery Program</a>, a series of small-scale spacecraft designed to proceed from development to flight in under three years for a cost of less than $150 million. The construction, launch, and 30-day cost for this mission is estimated at $122 million. The final total mission cost was $224 million which consisted of $124.9 million for spacecraft development, $44.6 million for launch support and tracking, and $54.6 million for mission operations and data analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span
