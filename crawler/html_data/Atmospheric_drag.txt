resistance</b>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, or <b>fluid resistance</b>, another type of friction or fluid friction) is a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> acting opposite to the relative motion of any object moving with respect to a surrounding fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This can exist between two fluid layers (or surfaces) or a fluid and a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> surface. Unlike other resistive forces, such as dry <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, which are nearly independent of velocity, drag forces depend on velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAdrag_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdrag-3">[3]</a></sup>\nDrag force is proportional to the velocity for a <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> and the squared velocity for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbulent_flow" title="Turbulent flow">turbulent flow</a>. Even though the ultimate cause of a drag is viscous friction, the turbulent drag is independent of <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Drag forces always decrease fluid velocity relative to the solid object in the fluid\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathline" title="Pathline">path</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples of drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Examples of drag include the component of the <a href="/wiki/Net_force" title="Net force">net</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_force" title="Aerodynamic force">aerodynamic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamics" title="Hydrodynamics">hydrodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> acting opposite to the direction of movement of a solid object such as cars, aircraft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAdrag_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdrag-3">[3]</a></sup> and boat hulls; or acting in the same geographical direction of motion as the solid, as for sails attached to a down wind sail boat, or in intermediate directions on a sail depending on points of sail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eiffel_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eiffel-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchaj1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marchaj1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fossati1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fossati1-7">[7]</a></sup>  In the case of viscous drag of <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">fluid in a pipe</a>, drag force on the immobile pipe decreases fluid velocity relative to the pipe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the physics of sports, the drag force is necessary to explain the performance of runners, particularly of sprinters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_drag">Types of drag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Types of drag are generally divided into the following categories:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Parasitic_drag" title="Parasitic drag">parasitic drag</a>, consisting of\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Parasitic_drag#Form_drag" title="Parasitic drag">form drag</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Parasitic_drag#Skin_friction" title="Parasitic drag">skin friction</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Parasitic_drag#Interference_drag" title="Parasitic drag">interference drag</a>,</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lift-induced_drag" title="Lift-induced drag">lift-induced drag</a>, and</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wave_drag" title="Wave drag">wave drag</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Wave-making_resistance" title="Wave-making resistance">wave resistance</a> (ship hydrodynamics).</li></ul>\n<p>The phrase <i>parasitic drag</i> is mainly used in aerodynamics, since for Terminal velocity for very small objects (pollen, etc.) at low Reynolds numbers is determined by Stokes law.\n</p><p>Terminal velocity is higher for larger creatures, and thus potentially more deadly.  A creature such as a mouse falling at its terminal velocity is much more likely to survive impact with the ground than a human falling at its terminal velocity. Using 10<sup>&#8722;3</sup> Pa&#183;s as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_viscosity" title="Dynamic viscosity">dynamic viscosity</a> of water in SI units,\nwe find a drag force of 0.09 pN. This is about the drag force that a bacterium experiences as it swims through water.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aerodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, <b>aerodynamic drag</b> is the fluid drag force that acts on any moving solid body in the direction of the fluid <a href="/wiki/Freestream" title="Freestream">freestream</a> flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> From the body\'s perspective (near-field approach), the drag results from forces due to pressure distributions over the body surface, symbolized <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{pr}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>p</mi>\n<mi>r</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{pr}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{{pr}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ada07c1562f0e00738c3b9049d954a2b3e602f03" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.725ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, and forces due to skin friction, which is a result of viscosity, denoted <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{f}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>f</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{f}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{{f}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13b8420d715315c8e8b57f178a1b3a8512bd0959" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.061ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>. Alternatively, calculated from the flowfield perspective (far-field approach), the drag force results from three natural phenomena: <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a>, vortex sheet, and <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> distribution acting on a body\'s surface exerts normal forces on the body. Those forces can be summed and the component of that force that acts downstream represents the drag force, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{pr}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>p</mi>\n<mi>r</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{pr}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{{pr}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ada07c1562f0e00738c3b9049d954a2b3e602f03" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.725ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, due to pressure distribution acting on the body. The nature of these normal forces combines shock wave effects, vortex system generation effects, and wake viscous mechanisms.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the fluid has a major effect on drag. In the absence of viscosity, the pressure forces acting to retard the vehicle are canceled by a pressure force further aft that acts to push the vehicle forward; this is called pressure recovery and the result is that the drag is zero. That is to say, the work the body does on the airflow, is reversible and is recovered as there are no frictional effects to convert the flow energy into heat. Pressure recovery acts even in the case of viscous flow. Viscosity, however results in pressure drag and it is the dominant component of drag in the case of vehicles with regions of separated flow, in which the pressure recovery is fairly ineffective.\n</p><p>The friction drag force, which is a tangential force on the aircraft surface, depends substantially on <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> configuration and viscosity. The net friction drag, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{f}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>f</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{f}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{f}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13b8420d715315c8e8b57f178a1b3a8512bd0959" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.061ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, is calculated as the downstream projection of the viscous forces evaluated over the body\'s surface.\n</p><p>The sum of friction drag and pressure (form) drag is called viscous drag. This drag component is due to viscosity. In a thermodynamic perspective, viscous effects represent irreversible phenomena and, therefore, they create entropy. The calculated viscous drag <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{v}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>v</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{v}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{v}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a7721f1f570d606715d4c046488254a537c85b1" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.954ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> use entropy changes to accurately predict the drag force.\n</p><p>When the airplane produces lift, another drag component results. <a href="/wiki/Lift-induced_drag" title="Lift-induced drag">Induced drag</a>, symbolized <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{i}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>i</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{i}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{i}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f07b53d3212e08ca316a536c8aac0bbefa79ee1" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.724ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>, is due to a modification of the pressure distribution due to the trailing vortex system that accompanies the lift production. An alternative perspective on lift and drag is gained from considering the change of momentum of the airflow.  The wing intercepts the airflow and forces the flow to move downward. This results in an equal and opposite force acting upward on the wing which is the lift force. The change of momentum of the airflow downward results in a reduction of the rearward momentum of the flow which is the result of a force acting forward on the airflow and applied by the wing to the air flow; an equal but opposite force acts on the wing rearward which is the induced drag. Induced drag tends to be the most important component for airplanes during take-off or landing flight. Another drag component, namely <a href="/wiki/Wave_drag" title="Wave drag">wave drag</a>, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle D_{w}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>D</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>w</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle D_{w}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="D_{w}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fca99aff837a1f07cd9562cd371a10bef91ca3be" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:3.333ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>, results from shock waves in transonic and supersonic flight speeds. The shock waves induce changes in the boundary layer and pressure distribution over the body surface.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The idea that a moving body passing through air or another fluid encounters resistance had been known since the time of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>. <a href="/wiki/Louis_Charles_Breguet" title="Louis Charles Breguet">Louis Charles Breguet</a>\'s paper of 1922 began efforts to reduce drag by streamlining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-23">[23]</a></sup> Breguet went on to put his ideas into practice by designing several record-breaking aircraft in the 1920s and 1930s. <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Prandtl" title="Ludwig Prandtl">Ludwig Prandtl</a>\'s boundary layer theory in the 1920s provided the impetus to minimise skin friction. A further major call for streamlining was made by Sir <a href="/wiki/Melvill_Jones" title="Melvill Jones">Melvill Jones</a> who provided the theoretical concepts to demonstrate emphatically the importance of streamlining in <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biography-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-26">[26]</a></sup> \nIn 1929 his paper &#8216;The Streamline Airplane&#8217; presented to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aeronautical_Society" title="Royal Aeronautical Society">Royal Aeronautical Society</a> was seminal. He proposed an ideal aircraft that would have minimal drag which led to the concepts of a \'clean\' monoplane and retractable <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">undercarriage</a>. The aspect of Jones\'s paper that most shocked the designers of the time was his plot of the horse power required versus velocity, for an actual and an ideal plane. By looking at a data point for a given aircraft and extrapolating it horizontally to the ideal curve, the velocity gain for the same power can be seen. When Jones finished his presentation, a member of the audience described the results as being of the same level of importance as the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a> in thermodynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift-induced_drag">Lift-induced drag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lift-induced drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lift-induced_drag" title="Lift-induced drag">Lift-induced drag</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Induced_drag_r.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Induced_drag_r.svg/150px-Induced_drag_r.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Induced_drag_r.svg/225px-Induced_drag_r.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Induced_drag_r.svg/300px-Induced_drag_r.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Induced_drag_r.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Induced drag <i>vs.</i> lift<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><b>Lift-induced drag</b> (also called <b>induced drag</b>) is drag which occurs as the result of the creation of <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> on a three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Lifting_body" title="Lifting body">lifting body</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> or fuselage of an airplane. Induced drag consists primarily of two components: drag due to the creation of trailing vortices (<b>vortex drag</b>); and the presence of additional viscous drag (<b>lift-induced viscous drag</b>) that is not present when lift is zero. The trailing vortices in the flow-field, present in the wake of a lifting body, derive from the turbulent mixing of air from above and below the body which flows in slightly different directions as a consequence of creation of <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>.\n</p><p>With other parameters remaining the same, as the <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> generated by a body increases, so does the lift-induced drag. This means that as the wing\'s <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a> increases (up to a maximum called the stalling angle), the <a href="/wiki/Lift_coefficient" title="Lift coefficient">lift coefficient</a> also increases, and so too does the lift-induced drag. At the onset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)" title="Stall (flight)">stall</a>, lift is abruptly decreased, as is lift-induced drag, but viscous pressure drag, a component of parasite drag, increases due to the formation of turbulent unattached flow in the wake behind the body.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parasitic_drag">Parasitic drag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parasitic drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_drag" title="Parasitic drag">parasitic drag</a></div>\n<p><b>Parasitic drag</b> is drag caused by moving a solid object through a fluid. Parasitic drag is made up of multiple components including viscous pressure drag (<b>form drag</b>), and drag due to surface roughness (<b>skin friction drag</b>). Additionally, the presence of multiple bodies in relative proximity may incur so called <b>interference drag</b>, which is sometimes described as a component of parasitic drag.\n</p><p>In aviation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Induced_drag" title="Induced drag">induced drag</a> tends to be greater at lower speeds because a high <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a> is required to maintain lift, creating more drag. However, as speed increases the angle of attack can be reduced and the induced drag decreases. Parasitic drag, however, increases because the fluid is flowing more quickly around protruding objects increasing friction or drag. At even higher speeds (<a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">transonic</a>), <a href="/wiki/Wave_drag" title="Wave drag">wave drag</a> enters the picture. Each of these forms of drag changes in proportion to the others based on speed. The combined overall drag curve therefore shows a minimum at some airspeed - an aircraft flying at this speed will be at or close to its optimal efficiency. Pilots will use this speed to maximize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endurance_(aircraft)" title="Endurance (aircraft)">endurance</a> (minimum fuel consumption), or maximize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glide_ratio" title="Glide ratio">gliding range</a> in the event of an engine failure.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_curve_in_aviation">Power curve in aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Power curve in aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="707" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/220px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/330px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/440px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>power curve</i>: form and induced drag <i>vs.</i> airspeed</div></div></div>\n<p>The interaction of parasitic and induced drag <i>vs.</i> airspeed can be plotted as a characteristic curve, illustrated here. In aviation, this is often referred to as the <i>power curve</i>, and is important to pilots because it shows that, below a certain airspeed, maintaining airspeed counterintuitively requires <i>more</i> thrust as speed decreases, rather than less. The consequences of being "behind the curve" in flight are important and are taught as part of pilot training. At the subsonic airspeeds where the "U" shape of this curve is significant, wave drag has not yet become a factor, and so it is not shown in the curve.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_drag_in_transonic_and_supersonic_flow">Wave drag in transonic and supersonic flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Wave drag in transonic and supersonic flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="450" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png/220px-Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png/330px-Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png/440px-Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Qualitive_variation_of_cd_with_mach_number.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Qualitative variation in Cd factor with Mach number for aircraft</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wave_drag" title="Wave drag">wave drag</a></div>\n<p><b>Wave drag</b> (also called <b>compressibility drag</b>) is drag that is created when a body moves in a compressible fluid and at speeds that are close to the speed of sound in that fluid. In <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, wave drag consists of multiple components depending on the speed regime of the flight.\n</p><p>In transonic flight (Mach numbers greater than about 0.8 and less than about 1.4), wave drag is the result of the formation of shockwaves in the fluid, formed when local areas of supersonic (Mach number greater than 1.0) flow are created. In practice, supersonic flow occurs on bodies traveling well below the speed of sound, as the local speed of air increases as it accelerates over the body to speeds above Mach 1.0. However, full supersonic flow over the vehicle will not develop until well past Mach 1.0. Aircraft flying at transonic speed often incur wave drag through the normal course of operation. In transonic flight, wave drag is commonly referred to as <b>transonic compressibility drag</b>. Transonic compressibility drag increases significantly as the speed of flight increases towards Mach 1.0, dominating other forms of drag at those speeds.\n</p><p>In supersonic flight (Mach numbers greater than 1.0), <b>wave drag</b> is the result of shockwaves present in the fluid and attached to the body, typically <b>oblique shockwaves</b> formed at the leading and trailing edges of the body. In highly supersonic flows, or in bodies with turning angles sufficiently large, <b>unattached shockwaves</b>, or <b>bow waves</b> will instead form. Additionally, local areas of transonic flow behind the initial shockwave may occur at lower supersonic speeds, and can lead to the development of additional, smaller shockwaves present on the surfaces of other lifting bodies, similar to those found in transonic flows. In supersonic flow regimes, <b>wave drag</b> is commonly separated into two components, <b>supersonic lift-dependent wave drag</b> and <b>supersonic volume-dependent wave drag</b>.\n</p><p>The closed form solution for the minimum wave drag of a body of revolution with a fixed length was found by Sears and Haack, and is known as the <b>Sears-Haack Distribution</b>. Similarly, for a fixed volume, the shape for minimum wave drag is the <b>Von Karman Ogive</b>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Busemann%27s_Biplane" title="Busemann\'s Biplane">Busemann\'s Biplane</a> is not, in principle, subject to wave drag at all when operated at its design speed, but is incapable of generating lift.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="d.27Alembert.27s_paradox"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="d\'Alembert\'s_paradox">d\'Alembert\'s paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drag_(physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: d\'Alembert\'s paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_paradox" title="D\'Alembert\'s paradox">d\'Alembert\'s paradox</a></div>\n<p>In 1752 <a href="/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert" title="Jean le Rond d\'Alembert">d\'Alembert</a> proved that <a href="/wiki/Potential_flow" title="Potential flow">potential flow</a>, the 18th century state-of-the-art <a href="/wiki/Inviscid_flow" title="Inviscid flow">inviscid flow</a> theory amenable to mathematical solutions, resulted in the prediction of zero drag.  This was in contradiction with experimental evidence, and became known as d\'Alembert\'s paradox.  In the 19th century the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier&#8211;Stokes equations">Navier&#8211;Stokes equations</a> for the description of <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> flow were developed by <a href="/wiki/Adh%C3%A9mar_Jean_Claude_Barr%C3%A9_de_Saint-Venant" title="Adh&#233;mar Jean Claude Barr&#233; de Saint-Venant">Saint-Venant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claude-Louis_Navier" title="Claude-Louis Navier">Navier</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gabriel_Stokes" title="George Gabriel Stokes">Stokes</a>.  Stokes derived the drag around a sphere at very low <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds numbers</a>, the result of which is called <a href="/wiki/Stokes%27_law" title="Stokes\' law">Stokes\' law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the limit of high Reynolds numbers, the Navier&#8211;Stokes equations approach the inviscid <a href="/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Euler equations (fluid dynamics)">Euler equations</a>, of which the potential-flow solutions considered by d\'Alembert are solutions.  However, all experiments at high Reynolds numbers showed there is drag. Attempts to construct inviscid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_flow" title="Steady flow">steady flow</a> solutions to the Euler equations, other than the potential flow solutions, did not result in realistic results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The notion of <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layers</a>&#8212;introduced by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Prandtl" title="Ludwig Prandtl">Prandtl</a> in 1904, founded on both theory and experiments&#8212;explained the causes of drag at high Reynolds numbers.  The boundary layer is the thin layer of fluid close to the object\'s boundary, where viscous effects remain important even when the viscosity is very small (or equivalently the Reynolds number is very large).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
