Wikidata</a>]</div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Glasses</b>, also known as <b>eyeglasses</b> or <b>spectacles</b>, are devices consisting of glass or hard plastic <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a> mounted in a frame that holds them in front of a person\'s <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">eyes</a>, typically using a bridge over the nose and arms which rest over the ears.\n</p><p>Glasses are typically used for <a href="/wiki/Corrective_lens" title="Corrective lens">vision correction</a>, such as with reading glasses and glasses used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nearsightedness" title="Nearsightedness">nearsightedness</a>.\n</p><p>Safety glasses provide <a href="/wiki/Eye_protection" title="Eye protection">eye protection</a> against flying debris for construction workers or lab technicians; these glasses may have protection for the sides of the eyes as well as in the lenses. Some types of safety glasses are used to protect against visible and near-visible <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> or <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. Glasses are worn for eye protection in some sports, such as <a href="/wiki/Squash_(sport)" title="Squash (sport)">squash</a>.\n</p><p>Glasses wearers may use a strap to prevent the glasses from falling off during movement or sports. Wearers of glasses that are used only part of the time may have the glasses attached to a cord that goes around their neck, to prevent the loss of the glasses and breaking.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">Sunglasses</a> allow for better vision in bright daylight, and may protect one\'s eyes against damage from excessive levels of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>. Typical sunglasses lenses are tinted for protection against bright light or polarized to remove glare; Photochromatic glasses are clear in dark or indoor conditions, but turn into sunglasses when in they come in contact with Ultraviolet light. Most over the counter sunglasses do not have corrective power in the lenses; however, special prescription sunglasses can be made.\n</p><p>Specialized glasses may be used for viewing specific visual information, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3D_glasses" title="3D glasses">3D glasses</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3D_films" title="3D films">3D films</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">stereoscopy</a>). Sometimes glasses are worn purely for fashion or aesthetic purposes. Even with glasses used for vision correction, a wide range of fashions are available, using plastic, metal, wire, and other materials.\n</p><p>People are more likely to need glasses the older they get with 93% of people between the ages of 65 and 75 wearing corrective lenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Glasses can be marked or found by their primary function, but also appear in combinations such as prescription sunglasses or safety glasses with enhanced magnification.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrective">Corrective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corrective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Corrective_lens" title="Corrective lens">Corrective lens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Refractive_error" title="Refractive error">Refractive error</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg/220px-Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg/330px-Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg/440px-Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Refraction_through_glasses_090306.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> <a href="/wiki/Skyline" title="Skyline">skyline</a> as seen through a corrective lens, showing the effect of <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Corrective lenses are used to correct <a href="/wiki/Refractive_error" title="Refractive error">refractive errors</a> by bending the light entering the eye in order to alleviate the effects of conditions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">nearsightedness (myopia)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypermetropia" title="Hypermetropia">farsightedness (hypermetropia)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism" title="Astigmatism">astigmatism</a>. The ability of one\'s eyes to accommodate their focus to near and distant focus alters over time. A common condition in people over forty years old is <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">presbyopia</a>, which is caused by the eye\'s <a href="/wiki/Lens_(anatomy)" title="Lens (anatomy)">crystalline lens</a> losing elasticity, progressively reducing the ability of the lens to <a href="/wiki/Accommodation_(eye)" title="Accommodation (eye)">accommodate</a> (i.e. to focus on objects close to the eye). Few people have a pair of eyes that show exactly equal refractive characteristics; one eye may need a "stronger" (i.e. more refracting) lens than the other.\n</p><p>Corrective lenses bring the image back into focus on the retina. They are made to conform to the <a href="/wiki/Eyeglass_prescription" title="Eyeglass prescription">prescription</a> of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophthalmologist" title="Ophthalmologist">ophthalmologist</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optometrist" title="Optometrist">optometrist</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Lensmeter" title="Lensmeter">lensmeter</a> can be used to verify the specifications of an existing pair of glasses. Corrective eyeglasses can significantly improve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_quality" title="Life quality">life quality</a> of the wearer. Not only do they enhance the wearer\'s visual experience, but can also reduce problems that result from eye strain, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headaches" title="Headaches">headaches</a> or squinting.\n</p><p>The most common type of corrective lens is "single vision", which has a uniform <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>. For people with <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">presbyopia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperopia" title="Hyperopia">hyperopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bifocals" title="Bifocals">bifocal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trifocal" title="Trifocal">trifocal</a> glasses provide two or three different refractive indices, respectively, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_lenses" title="Progressive lenses">progressive lenses</a> have a continuous gradient.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reading_glasses" title="Reading glasses">Reading glasses</a> provide a separate set of glasses for focusing on close-by objects. Reading glasses are available without prescription from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugstore" title="Drugstore">drugstores</a>, and offer a cheap, practical solution, though these have a pair of simple lenses of equal power, so will not correct refraction problems like <a href="/wiki/Astigmatism" title="Astigmatism">astigmatism</a> or refractive or prismatic variations between the left and right eye. For total correction of the individual\'s sight, glasses complying to a recent ophthalmic prescription are required.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Adjustable-focus_eyeglasses" title="Adjustable-focus eyeglasses">Adjustable-focus eyeglasses</a> might be used to replace bifocals or trifocals, or might be used to produce cheaper single-vision glasses (since they don\'t have to be custom-manufactured for every person).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pinhole_glasses" title="Pinhole glasses">Pinhole glasses</a> are a type of corrective glasses that do not use a lens. Pinhole glasses do not actually refract the light or change focal length. Instead, they create a <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> limited system, which has an increased depth of field, similar to using a small <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>.  This form of correction has many limitations that prevent it from gaining popularity in everyday use. Pinhole glasses can be made in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DIY" title="DIY">DIY</a> fashion by making small holes in a piece of card which is then held in front of the eyes with a strap or cardboard arms.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SafetyGlassesShield.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1416" data-file-width="1917" decoding="async" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SafetyGlassesShield.jpg/220px-SafetyGlassesShield.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SafetyGlassesShield.jpg/330px-SafetyGlassesShield.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SafetyGlassesShield.jpg/440px-SafetyGlassesShield.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SafetyGlassesShield.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Eye_protection" title="Eye protection">Safety glasses</a> with side shields</div></div></div>\n<p>Safety glasses are worn to protect the eyes in various situations. They are made with break-proof plastic lenses to protect the eye from flying debris or other matter. Construction workers, factory workers, machinists and lab technicians are often required to wear safety glasses to shield the eyes from flying debris or hazardous splatters such as blood or chemicals. As of 2017, dentists and surgeons in Canada and other countries are required to wear safety glasses to protect against infection from patients\' blood or other body fluids. There are also safety glasses for <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a>, which are styled like wraparound sunglasses, but with much darker lenses, for use in welding where a full sized <a href="/wiki/Welding_helmet" title="Welding helmet">welding helmet</a> is inconvenient or uncomfortable. These are often called "flash goggles", because they provide protection from welding flash. <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">Nylon</a> frames are usually used for protection eyewear for sports because of their lightweight and flexible properties. Unlike most regular glasses, safety glasses often include protection beside the eyes as well as in front of the eyes.\n</p>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Eye_protection" title="Eye protection">Eye protection</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunglasses">Sunglasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sunglasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">Sunglasses</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1742" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg/220px-Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg/330px-Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg/440px-Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Woman_wearing_sunglasses.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woman wearing <a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">sunglasses</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Sunglasses provide more comfort and protection against bright <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">light</a> and often against <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV) light. To properly protect the eyes from the dangers of UV light, sunglasses should have UV-400 blocker to provide good coverage against the entire light spectrum that poses a danger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Photochromic_lens" title="Photochromic lens">Photochromic lenses</a>, which are <a href="/wiki/Photosensitivity" title="Photosensitivity">photosensitive</a>, darken when struck by UV light.  The dark tint of the lenses in a pair of sunglasses blocks the transmission of light through the lens.\n</p><p>Light <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a> is an added feature that can be applied to sunglass lenses. Polarization filters are positioned to remove horizontally polarized rays of light, which eliminates glare from horizontal surfaces (allowing wearers to see into water when reflected light would otherwise overwhelm the scene). Polarized sunglasses may present some difficulties for pilots since reflections from water and other structures often used to gauge altitude may be removed.  <a href="/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display" title="Liquid-crystal display">Liquid-crystal displays</a> often emit polarized light making them sometimes difficult to view with polarized sunglasses. Sunglasses may be worn just for aesthetic purposes, or simply to hide the eyes. Examples of sunglasses that were popular for these reasons include teashades and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirrorshades_(fashion)" title="Mirrorshades (fashion)">mirrorshades</a>. Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blind</a> people wear nearly opaque glasses to hide their eyes for cosmetic reasons.\n</p><p>Sunglasses may also have corrective lenses, which requires a prescription.  Clip-on sunglasses or sunglass clips can be attached to another pair of glasses.  Some wrap-around sunglasses are large enough to be worn over top of another pair of glasses.  Otherwise, many people opt to wear <a href="/wiki/Contact_lens" title="Contact lens">contact lenses</a> to correct their vision so that standard sunglasses can be used.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_doubleframe">Mixed doubleframe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mixed doubleframe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="449" data-file-width="387" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif/220px-Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif/330px-Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doubleframe_uplifting_eyewear_-_Nella.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Doubleframe eyewear with one set of lenses on the moving frame and another pair of lenses on fixed frame (optional).</div></div></div>\n<p>The doubleframe uplifting glasses have one moving frame with one pair of lenses and the basic fixed frame with another pair of lenses (optional), that are connected by <a href="/wiki/Four-bar_linkage" title="Four-bar linkage">four-bar linkage</a>. For example, sunlenses could be easily lifted up and down while mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">myopia</a> lenses that always stay on. <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">Presbyopia</a> lenses could be also combined and easily removed from field of view if needed without taking off glasses.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3D_glasses">3D glasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 3D glasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy#3D_viewers" title="Stereoscopy">3D Viewers</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">illusion of three dimensions</a> on a two dimensional surface can be created by providing each eye with different visual information. 3D glasses create the illusion of three dimensions by filtering a signal containing information for both eyes.  The signal, often light reflected off a movie screen or emitted from an electronic display, is filtered so that each eye receives a slightly different image. The filters only work for the type of signal they were designed for.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Anaglyph_3D" title="Anaglyph 3D">Anaglyph 3D</a> glasses have a different colored filter for each eye, typically red and blue or red and green. A <a href="/wiki/Polarized_3D_system" title="Polarized 3D system">polarized 3D system</a> on the other hand uses <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarized</a> filters. Polarized 3D glasses allow for color 3D, while the red-blue lenses produce an image with distorted coloration. An <a href="/wiki/Active_shutter_3D_system" title="Active shutter 3D system">active shutter 3D system</a> uses electronic <a href="/wiki/Shutter_(photography)" title="Shutter (photography)">shutters</a>. <a href="/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">Head-mounted displays</a> can filter the signal electronically and then transmit light directly into the viewers eyes.\n</p><p>Anaglyph and polarized glasses are distributed to audiences at <a href="/wiki/3D_film" title="3D film">3D movies</a>.  Polarized and active shutter glasses are used with many home theaters.  Head-mounted displays are used by a single person, but the input signal can be shared between multiple units.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Magnification_.28bioptics.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnification_(bioptics)">Magnification (bioptics)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Magnification (bioptics)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Glasses can also provide magnification that is useful for people with vision impairments or specific occupational demands. For lower power lenses, there are few restrictions which allows for many trendy and fashionable shapes.  Higher power lenses can cause distortion of peripheral vision and may become thick and heavy if a large lens shape is used.  However, if the lens becomes too small, the field of view can be drastically reduced.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bifocals" title="Bifocals">Bifocal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trifocal_lenses" title="Trifocal lenses">trifocal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Progressive_lens" title="Progressive lens">progressive lenses</a> generally require a taller lens shape to leave room for the different segments while preserving an adequate field of view through each segment. Frames with rounded edges are the most efficient for correcting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">myopic</a> prescriptions, with perfectly round frames being the most efficient. Before the advent of eyeglasses as a fashion item, when frames were constructed with only functionality in mind, virtually all eyeglasses were either round, oval, or curved octagons. It was not until glasses began to be seen as an accessory that different shapes were introduced to be more aesthetically pleasing than functional.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors">Precursors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Precursors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Reading_stone" title="Reading stone">Reading stone</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:164px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hugh_specs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="186" data-file-width="162" decoding="async" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4c/Hugh_specs.jpg" width="162"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hugh_specs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of a portrait of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> Cardinal and renowned biblical scholar <a href="/wiki/Hugh_of_Saint-Cher" title="Hugh of Saint-Cher">Hugh of Saint-Cher</a> painted by <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_da_Modena" title="Tommaso da Modena">Tommaso da Modena</a> in 1352</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:El_Greco_048.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2360" data-file-width="1576" decoding="async" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/El_Greco_048.jpg/170px-El_Greco_048.jpg" 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:El_Greco_048.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of cardinal <a with temples passing over and beyond the ears</div></div></div>\n<p>Scattered evidence exists for use of vision aid devices in Greek and Roman times, most prominently the use of an emerald by emperor Nero as mentioned by Pliny the Elder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The use of a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">convex lens</a> to form an enlarged/magnified image was most likely described in <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i>Optics</i> (which however only survives in a poor Arabic translation). Ptolemy\'s description of lenses was commented upon and improved by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Sahl_(mathematician)" title="Ibn Sahl (mathematician)">Ibn Sahl</a> (10th century) and most notably by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Alhazen</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i>, ca. 1021). <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">Latin translations</a> of Ptolemy\'s <i>Optics</i> and of Alhazen became available in Europe in the 12th century, coinciding with the development of "<a href="/wiki/Reading_stone" title="Reading stone">reading stones</a>".\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Grosseteste" title="Robert Grosseteste">Robert Grosseteste</a>\'s treatise <i>De iride</i> ("On the Rainbow"), written between 1220 and 1235, mentions using optics to "read the smallest letters at incredible distances". A few years later in 1262, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a> is also known to have written on the magnifying properties of lenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\nThe development of the first eyeglasses took place in Northern Italy in the second half of the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kriss_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kriss-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Independently of the development of optical lenses, some cultures developed "<a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">sunglasses</a>" for eye protection, without any corrective properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\nThus,  flat panes of <a href="/wiki/Smoky_quartz" title="Smoky quartz">smoky quartz</a>, were used in 12th-century China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[a]</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> have used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inuit_snow_goggles" title="Inuit snow goggles">snow goggles</a> for eye protection.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invention">Invention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Invention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Conrad_von_Soest%2C_%27Brillenapostel%27_%281403%29.jpg/255px-Conrad_von_Soest%2C_%27Brillenapostel%27_%281403%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Conrad_von_Soest%2C_%27Brillenapostel%27_%281403%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Conrad_von_Soest,_%27Brillenapostel%27_(1403).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Glasses Apostle</i> by <a href="/wiki/Conrad_von_Soest" title="Conrad von Soest">Conrad von Soest</a> (1403)</div></div></div>\n<p>The first eyeglasses were made in Northern Italy, most likely in <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, by about 1290:\nIn a sermon delivered on 23 February 1306, the Dominican friar <a href="/wiki/Jordan_of_Pisa" title="Jordan of Pisa">Giordano da Pisa</a> (ca. 1255&#8211;1311) wrote "It is not yet twenty years since there was found the art of making eyeglasses, which make for good vision... And it is so short a time that this new art, never before extant, was discovered.&#160;... I saw the one who first discovered and practiced it, and I talked to him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIlardi2007[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpeIL7hVQUmwCpgPA5vonepageqffalse_5]_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIlardi2007[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpeIL7hVQUmwCpgPA5vonepageqffalse_5]-21">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Giordano\'s colleague Friar Alessandro della Spina of Pisa (d. 1313) was soon making eyeglasses. The <i>Ancient Chronicle of the Dominican Monastery of St. Catherine in Pisa</i> records: "Eyeglasses, having first been made by someone else, who was unwilling to share them, he [Spina] made them and shared them with everyone with a cheerful and willing heart."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIlardi20076_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIlardi20076-22">[21]</a></sup> By 1301, there were guild regulations in Venice governing the sale of eyeglasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIlardi20079_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIlardi20079-23">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Medieval_Spectacles.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="443" decoding="async" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Medieval_Spectacles.jpg/220px-Medieval_Spectacles.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Medieval_Spectacles.jpg/330px-Medieval_Spectacles.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Medieval_Spectacles.jpg/440px-Medieval_Spectacles.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Medieval_Spectacles.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seated apostle holding lenses in position for reading. Detail from <i><a href="/wiki/Death_of_the_Virgin" title="Death of the Virgin">Death of the Virgin</a></i>, by the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Heiligenkreuz" title="Master of Heiligenkreuz">Master of Heiligenkreuz</a>, ca. 1400&#8211;30 (<a href="/wiki/Getty_Center" title="Getty Center">Getty Center</a>).</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scissors_glasses.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scissors_glasses.jpg/220px-Scissors_glasses.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scissors_glasses.jpg/330px-Scissors_glasses.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Scissors_glasses.jpg/440px-Scissors_glasses.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scissors_glasses.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>French Empire gilt <a href="/wiki/Scissors-glasses" title="Scissors-glasses">scissors glasses</a> (with one lens missing), c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1805</span></div></div></div>\n<p>The earliest pictorial evidence for the use of eyeglasses is <a href="/wiki/Tommaso_da_Modena" title="Tommaso da Modena">Tommaso da Modena</a>\'s 1352 portrait of the cardinal Hugh de Provence reading in a <a href="/wiki/Scriptorium" title="Scriptorium">scriptorium</a>. Another early example would be a depiction of eyeglasses found north of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> in an altarpiece of the church of <a href="/wiki/Bad_Wildungen" title="Bad Wildungen">Bad Wildungen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, in 1403. These early glasses had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convex_lenses" title="Convex lenses">convex lenses</a> that could correct both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperopia" title="Hyperopia">hyperopia</a> (farsightedness), and the <a href="/wiki/Presbyopia" title="Presbyopia">presbyopia</a> that commonly develops as a symptom of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging" title="Aging">aging</a>. It was not until 1604 that <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Johannes Kepler</a> published the first correct explanation as to why convex and concave lenses could correct presbyopia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myopia" title="Myopia">myopia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[b]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early frames for glasses consisted of two magnifying glasses <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">riveted</a> together by the handles so that they could grip the nose. These are referred to as "rivet spectacles". The earliest surviving examples were found under the floorboards at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kloster_Wienhausen" title="Kloster Wienhausen">Kloster Wienhausen</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a> near <a href="/wiki/Celle" title="Celle">Celle</a> in Germany; they have been dated to <i>circa</i> 1400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_claims">Other claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Other claims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Claims that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvino_degli_Armati" title="Salvino degli Armati">Salvino degli Armati</a> of Florence invented eyeglasses have been exposed as hoaxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIlardi2007[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpeIL7hVQUmwCpgPA13vonepageqffalse_13-18]_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIlardi2007[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpeIL7hVQUmwCpgPA13vonepageqffalse_13-18]-29">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> is sometimes claimed to have encountered eyeglasses during his travels in China in the 13th century. However, no such statement appears in his accounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeedham1962[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidoJ9nayZZ2oECpg119vonepageqffalse_119,_footnote_c]_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeedham1962[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidoJ9nayZZ2oECpg119vonepageqffalse_119,_footnote_c]-30">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirschberg_1911_265_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschberg_1911_265-31">[29]</a></sup> Indeed, the earliest mentions of eyeglasses in China occur in the 15th century and those Chinese sources state that eyeglasses were imported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeedham1962119_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeedham1962119-32">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is also sometimes claimed that glasses were first invented in India. In 1907 Professor <a href="/wiki/Berthold_Laufer" title="Berthold Laufer">Berthold Laufer</a> stated in his history of glasses that <i>"the opinion that spectacles originated in India is of the greatest probability and that spectacles must have been known in India earlier than in Europe"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> However, Joseph Needham showed that the mention of glasses in the manuscript Laufer used to justify the prior invention of them in Asia did not exist in older versions of that manuscript, and the reference to them in later versions was added during the Ming dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> In a 1971 article in the British Journal of Ophthalmology it was argued that it: <i>"...is therefore most likely that the use of lenses reached Europe via the Arabs, as did Hindu mathematics and the ophthalmological works of the ancient Hindu surgeon Susruta"</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> but all dates given are well after the existence of eyeglasses in Italy was established, and there had been significant shipments of eye glasses from Italy to the Middle East, with one shipment as large as 24,000 glasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span title="Francisco de Quevedo y Villegas">Francisco de Quevedo y Villegas</a>, 1580&#8211;1645</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" Serious historians have from time to time produced evidence to suggest that others may have preceded him in the invention; however, a correspondence between <a href="/wiki/George_Whatley" title="George Whatley">George Whatley</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Fenno" title="John Fenno">John Fenno</a>, editor of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gazette_of_the_United_States" title="The Gazette of the United States">The Gazette of the United States</a></i>, suggested that Franklin had indeed invented bifocals, and perhaps 50 years earlier than had been originally thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> The first lenses for correcting astigmatism were designed by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Airy" title="George Airy">George Airy</a> in 1825.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Over time, the construction of frames for glasses also evolved. Early eyepieces were designed to be either held in place by hand or by exerting pressure on the nose (<i><a href="/wiki/Pince-nez" title="Pince-nez">pince-nez</a></i>). <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">Girolamo Savonarola</a> suggested that eyepieces could be held in place by a ribbon passed over the wearer\'s head, this in turn secured by the weight of a <a href="/wiki/Hat" title="Hat">hat</a>. The modern style of glasses, held by temples passing over the ears, was developed some time before 1727, possibly by the British optician <a href="/wiki/Edward_Scarlett" title="Edward Scarlett">Edward Scarlett</a>. These designs were not immediately successful, however, and various styles with attached handles such as "<a href="/wiki/Scissors-glasses" title="Scissors-glasses">scissors-glasses</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Lorgnette" title="Lorgnette">lorgnettes</a> were also fashionable from the second half of the 18th century and into the early 19th century.\n</p><p>In the early 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Moritz_von_Rohr" title="Moritz von Rohr">Moritz von Rohr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">Zeiss</a> (with the assistance of H. Boegehold and A. Sonnefeld<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup>), developed the Zeiss Punktal spherical point-focus lenses that dominated the eyeglass lens field for many years. In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Silver" title="Joshua Silver">Joshua Silver</a> designed eyewear with adjustable corrective glasses. They work by silicone liquid, a syringe, and a pressure mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the increasing popularity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_lenses" title="Contact lenses">contact lenses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> <a href="/wiki/Refractive_surgery" title="Refractive surgery">corrective eye surgery</a>, glasses remain very common, as their technology has improved. For instance, it is now possible to purchase frames made of special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memory_metal" title="Memory metal">memory metal</a> alloys that return to their correct shape after being bent. Other frames have spring-loaded hinges. Either of these designs offers dramatically better ability to withstand the stresses of daily wear and the occasional accident. Modern frames are also often made from strong, light-weight materials such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> alloys, which were not available in earlier times.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_fashion">In fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: In fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 1930s, "spectacles" were described as "medical appliances."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup> Wearing spectacles was sometimes considered socially humiliating. \nIn the 1970s, fashionable glasses started to become available through manufacturers, and governments also recognized the demand for stylized eyewear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Graham Pullin describes how devices for disability, like glasses, have traditionally been designed to camouflage against the skin and restore ability without being visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup> In the past, design for disability has "been less about projecting a positive image as about trying not to project an image at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup> Pullin uses the example of spectacles, traditionally categorized as a medical device for "patients", and outlines how they are now described as eyewear: a fashionable accessory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup> Much like other fashion designs and accessories, eyewear is created by designers, has reputable labels, and comes in collections, by season and designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-5"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup> It is becoming more common for consumers purchase eyewear with clear, non-prescription lenses, illustrating that glasses are no longer a social stigma, but a fashionable accessory that "frames your face."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pullin_41-6"><a href="#cite_note-pullin-41">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Market">Market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The market for spectacles has been characterized as having highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inelastic_demand" title="Inelastic demand">inelastic demand</a>, and advertising restrictions in the USA have correlated with higher prices, suggesting that adverts make the spectacles market more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_competition" title="Price competition">price-competitive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> It may also be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monopolistically_competitive" title="Monopolistically competitive">monopolistically competitive</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>There are claims that insufficiently free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Market_competition" title="Market competition">market competition</a> inflates the prices of frames, which cost an average of $25-$50 US to make, to an average retail price of $300 in the United States. This claim is disputed by some in the industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snopes_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-43">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIXTYMINUTES2012_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIXTYMINUTES2012-45">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Luxottica#Criticism" title="Luxottica">Luxottica &#167;&#160;Criticism</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redistribution">Redistribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Redistribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some organizations like <a href="/wiki/Lions_Clubs_International" title="Lions Clubs International">Lions Clubs International</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Unite_For_Sight" title="Unite For Sight">Unite For Sight</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> ReSpectacle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/New_Eyes_for_the_Needy" title="New Eyes for the Needy">New Eyes for the Needy</a> provide a way to donate glasses and sunglasses. Unite For Sight has redistributed more than 200,000 pairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion">Fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_(19).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg/220px-Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg/330px-Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg/440px-Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_%2819%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Demo_Day_2016_-_Design_Center_(19).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Glasses - Decoration, Presi HQ, Budapest</div></div></div>\n<p>Many people require glasses for the reasons listed above.  There are many shapes, colors, and materials that can be used when designing frames and lenses that can be utilized in various combinations.  Oftentimes, the selection of a frame is made based on how it will affect the appearance of the wearer.  Some people with good natural eyesight like to wear eyeglasses as a style accessory.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_image">Personal image</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Personal image">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>For most of their history, eyeglasses were seen as unfashionable, and carried several potentially negative connotations: wearing glasses caused individuals to be stigmatized and stereotyped as pious clergymen (as those in religious vocation were the most likely to be literate and therefore the most likely to need reading glasses), elderly, or physically weak and passive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mount_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-mount-50">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-har_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-har-51">[49]</a></sup> The stigma began to fall away in the early 1900s when the popular <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> was regularly photographed wearing eyeglasses, and in the 1910s when popular comedian <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lloyd" title="Harold Lloyd">Harold Lloyd</a> began wearing a pair of <a href="/wiki/Horn-rimmed_glasses" title="Horn-rimmed glasses">horn-rimmed glasses</a> as the "Glasses" character in his films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mount_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-mount-50">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-har_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-har-51">[49]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BarryGoldwater.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="180" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b7/BarryGoldwater.jpg" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BarryGoldwater.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former United States senator <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Horn-rimmed_glasses" title="Horn-rimmed glasses">horn-rimmed glasses</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Since, eyeglasses have become an acceptable fashion item and often act as a key component in individuals\' personal image. Musicians <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a> became synonymous with the styles of eye-glasses they wore to the point that thick, black horn-rimmed glasses are often called "Buddy Holly glasses" and perfectly round metal eyeglass frames called "John Lennon (or <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_(character)" title="Harry Potter (character)">Harry Potter</a>) Glasses." <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> comedic actor <a href="/wiki/Eric_Sykes" title="Eric Sykes">Eric Sykes</a> was known in the United Kingdom for wearing thick, square, horn-rimmed glasses, which were in fact a sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Hearing_aid" title="Hearing aid">hearing aid</a> that alleviated his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafness" title="Deafness">deafness</a> by allowing him to "hear" vibrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eric_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-eric-52">[50]</a></sup> Some celebrities have become so associated with their eyeglasses that they continued to wear them even after taking alternate measures against vision problems: United States Senator <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> and comedian <a href="/wiki/Drew_Carey" title="Drew Carey">Drew Carey</a> continued to wear non-prescription glasses after being fitted for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contacts" title="Contacts">contacts</a> and getting <a href="/wiki/LASIK" title="LASIK">laser eye surgery</a>, respectively.\n</p><p>Other celebrities have used glasses to differentiate themselves from the characters they play, such as <a href="/wiki/Anne_Kirkbride" title="Anne Kirkbride">Anne Kirkbride</a>, who wore oversized, 1980s-style round horn-rimmed glasses as <a href="/wiki/Deirdre_Barlow" title="Deirdre Barlow">Deirdre Barlow</a> in the soap opera <i><a href="/wiki/Coronation_Street" title="Coronation Street">Coronation Street</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Masaharu_Morimoto" title="Masaharu Morimoto">Masaharu Morimoto</a>, who wears glasses to separate his professional persona as a <a href="/wiki/Chef" title="Chef">chef</a> from his stage persona as <a href="/wiki/Iron_Chef" title="Iron Chef">Iron Chef</a> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a>. In 2012 some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA" title="NBA">NBA</a> players wear <a href="/wiki/Lensless_glasses" title="Lensless glasses">lensless glasses</a> with thick plastic frames like <a href="/wiki/Horn-rimmed_glasses" title="Horn-rimmed glasses">horn-rimmed glasses</a> during <a href="/wiki/Post-game_show" title="Post-game show">post-game interviews</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geek_chic" title="Geek chic">geek chic</a> that draws comparisons to actor <a href="/wiki/Jaleel_White" title="Jaleel White">Jaleel White\'s</a> infamous styling as TV character <a href="/wiki/Steve_Urkel" title="Steve Urkel">Steve Urkel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wsj-nba_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-nba-54">[52]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> fiction, eyeglasses have become a standard component of various heroes\' disguises (as masks), allowing them to adopt a nondescript demeanor when they are not in their superhero personae: <a href="/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a> is well known for wearing 1950s style horn-rimmed glasses as <a href="/wiki/Clark_Kent" title="Clark Kent">Clark Kent</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Wonder_Woman" title="Wonder Woman">Wonder Woman</a> wears either round, Harold Lloyd style glasses or 1970s style <a href="/wiki/Bug-eye_glasses" title="Bug-eye glasses">bug-eye glasses</a> as <a href="/wiki/Diana_Prince" title="Diana Prince">Diana Prince</a>. An example of the <a href="/wiki/Halo_effect" title="Halo effect">halo effect</a> is seen in the <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotype</a> that those who wear glasses are intelligent.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles">Styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
