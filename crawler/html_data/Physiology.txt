</i>&#966;&#973;&#963;&#953;&#962;<i> (physis)</i>, meaning \'nature, origin\', and <i> </i>-&#955;&#959;&#947;&#943;&#945;<i> (-logia)</i>, meaning \'study of\'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineEtDict_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtDict-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the scientific study of the <a href="/wiki/Function_(biology)" title="Function (biology)">functions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mechanism_(biology)" title="Mechanism (biology)">mechanisms</a> which work within a <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prosser_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prosser-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a <a href="/wiki/Branches_of_science" title="Branches of science">sub-discipline</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, the focus of physiology is on how <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organ_system" title="Organ system">organ systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a> carry out the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a> functions that exist in a living system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Central to an understanding of physiological functioning is the investigation of the fundamental biophysical and biochemical phenomena, the coordinated <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostatic</a> control mechanisms, and the continuous <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">communication</a> between cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <b>physiologic</b> state is the condition occurring from normal body function, while the <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathological</a> state is centered on the abnormalities that occur in animal diseases, including humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medicalnewstoday.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-medicalnewstoday.com-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the type of investigated organisms, the field can be divided into, animal physiology (including that of <a href="/wiki/Homo_sapiens" title="Homo sapiens">humans</a>), <a href="/wiki/Plant_physiology" title="Plant physiology">plant physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_physiology" title="Cell physiology">cellular physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_metabolism" title="Microbial metabolism">microbial physiology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> is awarded to those who make significant achievements in this discipline by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal The principal level of focus of physiology is at the level of organs and systems within systems. The endocrine and nervous systems play major roles in the reception and transmission of signals. that integrate function in animals. Homeostasis is a major aspect with regard to such interactions within plants as well as animals. The biological basis of the study of physiology, integration refers to the overlap of many functions of the systems of the human body, as well as its accompanied form. It is achieved through communication that occurs in a variety of ways, both electrical and chemical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Changes in physiology can impact the mental functions of individuals. Examples of this would be the effects of certain medications or toxic levels of substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Change in <a href="/wiki/Behavior_change_(individual)" title="Behavior change (individual)">behavior</a> as a result of these substances is often used to assess the health of individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis2017-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zolDavis2017_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-zolDavis2017-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Much of the foundation of knowledge in human physiology was provided by animal experimentation. Due to the frequent connection between form and function, physiology and <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a> are intrinsically linked and are studied in tandem as part of a medical curriculum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH2013_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2013-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plant_physiology" title="Plant physiology">Plant physiology</a></div><p>Plant physiology is a subdiscipline of <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> concerned with the functioning of plants. Closely related fields include <a href="/wiki/Plant_morphology" title="Plant morphology">plant morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant_ecology" title="Plant ecology">plant ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phytochemistry" title="Phytochemistry">phytochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>, genetics, <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>. Fundamental processes of plant physiology include <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiration_in_plant" title="Respiration in plant">respiration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant_nutrition" title="Plant nutrition">plant nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropism" title="Tropism">tropisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nastic_movements" title="Nastic movements">nastic movements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photoperiodism" title="Photoperiodism">photoperiodism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photomorphogenesis" title="Photomorphogenesis">photomorphogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germination" title="Germination">seed germination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dormancy" title="Dormancy">dormancy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a> function and transpiration. Absorption of water by roots, production of food in the leaves, and growth of shoots towards light are examples of plant physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cells">Cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cell_physiology" title="Cell physiology">Cell physiology</a></div><p>Although there are differences between animal, plant, and microbial cells, the basic physiological functions of cells can be divided into the processes of <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">cell division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">cell signaling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">cell growth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">cell metabolism</a>.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microorganisms">Microorganisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microorganisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microorganism</a></div><p>Microorganisms can be found almost everywhere on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Types of microorganisms include archaea, bacteria, eukaryotes, protists, fungi, and micro-plants. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes_in_human_culture" title="Microbes in human culture">Microbes are important in human culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> in many ways, serving to <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">ferment foods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">treat sewage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae_fuel" title="Algae fuel">produce fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioactive_compound" title="Bioactive compound">bioactive compounds</a>. They are essential tools in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> as <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organisms</a> and have been put to use in <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bioterrorism" title="Bioterrorism">bioterrorism</a>. They are a vital <a href="/wiki/Soil_microbiology" title="Soil microbiology">component of fertile soils</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> microorganisms make up the <a href="/wiki/Human_microbiota" title="Human microbiota">human microbiota</a> including the essential <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a>. They are the <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> responsible for many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> and as such are the target of <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene measures</a>. Most microorganisms can reproduce rapidly, and bacteria are also able to freely exchange genes through <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_conjugation" title="Bacterial conjugation">conjugation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)" title="Transformation (genetics)">transformation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transduction_(genetics)" title="Transduction (genetics)">transduction</a>, even between widely divergent species.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viruses">Viruses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Viruses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">Virus</a></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_classical_era">The classical era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The classical era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The study of human physiology as a medical field originates in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">classical Greece</a>, at the time of <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a> (late 5th century BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> \nOutside of Western tradition, early forms of physiology or anatomy can be reconstructed as having been present at around the same time in China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> India<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and elsewhere.\nHippocrates incorporated his belief system called the theory of humours, which consisted of four basic substance: earth, water, air and fire. Each substance is known for having a corresponding humour: black bile, phlegm, blood and yellow bile, respectively. Hippocrates also noted some emotional connections to the four humours, which Claudius Galenus would later expand on. The critical thinking of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and his emphasis on the relationship between structure and function marked the beginning of physiology in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a>. Like Hippocrates, Aristotle took to the humoral theory of disease, which also consisted of four primary qualities in life: hot, cold, wet and dry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Claudius Galenus (c. 130&#8211;200&#160;AD), known as <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> of Pergamum, was the first to use experiments to probe the functions of the body. Unlike Hippocrates, Galen argued that humoral imbalances can be located in specific organs, including the entire body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-18">[18]</a></sup> His modification of this theory better equipped doctors to make more precise diagnoses. Galen also played off of Hippocrates idea that emotions were also tied to the humours, and added the notion of temperaments: sanguine corresponds with blood; phlegmatic is tied to phlegm; yellow bile is connected to choleric; and black bile corresponds with melancholy. Galen also saw the human body consisting of three connected systems: the brain and nerves, which are responsible for thoughts and sensations; the heart and arteries, which give life; and the liver and veins, which can be attributed to nutrition and growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-18">[18]</a></sup> Galen was also the founder of experimental physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> And for the next 1,400 years, Galenic physiology was a powerful and influential tool in medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Jean_Fernel" title="Jean Fernel">Jean Fernel</a> (1497&#8211;1558), a French physician, introduced the term "physiology".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Galen, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Nafis" title="Ibn al-Nafis">Ibn al-Nafis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">Michael Servetus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Realdo_Colombo" title="Realdo Colombo">Realdo Colombo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amato_Lusitano" title="Amato Lusitano">Amato Lusitano</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>, are credited as making important discoveries in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circulation_of_the_blood" title="Circulation of the blood">circulation of the blood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Santorio_Santorio" title="Santorio Santorio">Santorio Santorio</a> in 1610s was the first to use a device to measure the <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a> rate (the <i>pulsilogium</i>), and a <a href="/wiki/Thermoscope" title="Thermoscope">thermoscope</a> to measure temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1791 <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Galvani" title="Luigi Galvani">Luigi Galvani</a> described the role of electricity in nerves of dissected frogs. In 1811, Julien Jean C&#233;sar Legallois studied respiration in animal dissection and lesions and found the center of respiration in the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla oblongata</a>. In the same year, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bell" title="Charles Bell">Charles Bell</a> finished work on what would later become known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell-Magendie_law" title="Bell-Magendie law">Bell-Magendie law</a>, which compared functional differences between dorsal and ventral roots of the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>. In 1824, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Magendie" title="Fran&#231;ois Magendie">Fran&#231;ois Magendie</a> described the sensory roots and produced the first evidence of the cerebellum&#8217;s role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">equilibration</a> to complete the Bell-Magendie law.\n</p><p>In the 1820s, the French physiologist <a href="/wiki/Henri_Milne-Edwards" title="Henri Milne-Edwards">Henri Milne-Edwards</a> introduced the notion of physiological division of labor, which allowed to "compare and study living things as if they were machines created by the industry of man." Inspired in the work of <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, Milne-Edwards wrote that the "body of all living beings, whether animal or plant, resembles a factory ... where the organs, comparable to workers, work incessantly to produce the phenomena that constitute the life of the individual." In more differentiated organisms, the functional labor could be apportioned between different instruments or <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">systems</a> (called by him as <i>appareils</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brain_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-brain-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1858, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Lister,_1st_Baron_Lister" title="Joseph Lister, 1st Baron Lister">Joseph Lister</a> studied the cause of blood coagulation and inflammation that resulted after previous injuries and surgical wounds. He later discovered and implemented <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptics</a> in the operating room, and as a result decreased death rate from surgery by a substantial amount.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medicalnewstoday.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-medicalnewstoday.com-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physiologyinfo.org_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-physiologyinfo.org-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Physiological_Society" title="The Physiological Society">The Physiological Society</a> was founded in London in 1876 as a dining club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_American_Physiological_Society" title="The American Physiological Society">The American Physiological Society</a>  (APS) is a nonprofit organization that was founded in 1887. The Society is, "devoted to fostering education, scientific research, and dissemination of information in the physiological sciences."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1891, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Pavlov" title="Ivan Pavlov">Ivan Pavlov</a> performed research on "conditional responses" that involved dogs\' saliva production in response to a bell and visual stimuli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physiologyinfo.org_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-physiologyinfo.org-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 19th century, physiological knowledge began to accumulate at a rapid rate, in particular with the 1838 appearance of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_theory" title="Cell theory">Cell theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Jakob_Schleiden" title="Matthias Jakob Schleiden">Matthias Schleiden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Theodor Schwann</a>. It radically stated that organisms are made up of units called cells. <a href="/wiki/Claude_Bernard" title="Claude Bernard">Claude Bernard</a>\'s (1813&#8211;1878) further discoveries ultimately led to his concept of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milieu_interieur" title="Milieu interieur">milieu interieur</a></i> (internal environment),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which would later be taken up and championed as "<a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>" by American physiologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_B._Cannon" title="Walter B. Cannon">Walter B. Cannon</a> in 1929. By homeostasis, Cannon meant "the maintenance of steady states in the body and the physiological processes through which they are regulated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In other words, the body\'s ability to regulate its internal environment. William Beaumont was the first American to utilize the practical application of physiology.\n</p><p>Nineteenth century physiologists such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foster_(physiologist)" title="Michael Foster (physiologist)">Michael Foster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Verworn" title="Max Verworn">Max Verworn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Binet" title="Alfred Binet">Alfred Binet</a>, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haeckel" title="Haeckel">Haeckel</a>\'s ideas, elaborated what came to be called "general physiology", a unified science of life based on the cell actions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brain_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-brain-23">[23]</a></sup> later renamed in the 20th century as <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_modern_period">Late modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Late modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 20th century, biologists  became interested in how organisms other than human beings function, eventually spawning the fields of <a href="/wiki/Comparative_physiology" title="Comparative physiology">comparative physiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecophysiology" title="Ecophysiology">ecophysiology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Major figures in these fields include <a href="/wiki/Knut_Schmidt-Nielsen" title="Knut Schmidt-Nielsen">Knut Schmidt-Nielsen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Bartholomew_(biologist)" title="George Bartholomew (biologist)">George Bartholomew</a>. Most recently, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_physiology" title="Evolutionary physiology">evolutionary physiology</a> has become a distinct subdiscipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/August_Krogh" title="August Krogh">August Krogh</a> won the Nobel Prize for discovering how, in capillaries, blood flow is regulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physiologyinfo.org_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-physiologyinfo.org-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1954, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Huxley" title="Andrew Huxley">Andrew Huxley</a> and Hugh Huxley, alongside their research team, discovered the sliding filaments in <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscle" title="Skeletal muscle">skeletal muscle</a>, known today as the sliding filament theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physiologyinfo.org_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-physiologyinfo.org-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Recently, there have been intense debates about the vitality of physiology as a discipline (Is it dead or alive?).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-34">[34]</a></sup> If physiology is perhaps less visible nowadays than during the golden age of the 19th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> it is in large part because the field has given birth to some of the most active domains of today\'s biological sciences, such as <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrinology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Immunology" title="Immunology">immunology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Furthermore, physiology is still often seen as an integrative discipline, which can put together into a coherent framework data coming from various different domains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_physiologists">Notable physiologists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notable physiologists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_physiologists" title="List of physiologists">List of physiologists</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women_in_physiology">Women in physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Women in physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Initially, women were largely excluded from official involvement in any physiological society. The <a href="/wiki/American_Physiological_Society" title="American Physiological Society">American Physiological Society</a>, for example, was founded in 1887 and included only men in its ranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 1902, the American Physiological Society elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ida_Hyde" title="Ida Hyde">Ida Hyde</a> as the first female member of the society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_1981_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker_1981-40">[40]</a></sup> Hyde, a representative of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_University_Women" title="American Association of University Women">American Association of University Women</a> and a global advocate for gender equality in education,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> attempted to promote gender equality in every aspect of science and medicine.\n</p><p>Soon thereafter, in 1913, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.S._Haldane" title="J.S. Haldane">J.S. Haldane</a> proposed that women be allowed to formally join <a href="/wiki/The_Physiological_Society" title="The Physiological Society">The Physiological Society</a>, which had been founded in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On 3 July 1915, six women were officially admitted: <a href="/wiki/Florence_Buchanan" title="Florence Buchanan">Florence Buchanan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winifred_Cullis" title="Winifred Cullis">Winifred Cullis</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_C._Skelton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ruth C. Skelton (page does not exist)">Ruth C. Skelton</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sarah_C._M._Sowton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sarah C. M. Sowton (page does not exist)">Sarah C. M. Sowton</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Constance_Leetham_Terry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Constance Leetham Terry (page does not exist)">Constance Leetham Terry</a>, and  <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Enid_M._Tribe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Enid M. Tribe (page does not exist)">Enid M. Tribe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physoc.org_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-physoc.org-43">[43]</a></sup>  The centenary of the election of women was celebrated in 2015 with the publication of the book "Women Physiologists: Centenary Celebrations And Beyond For The Physiological Society." href="/wiki/Gerty_Cori" title="Gerty Cori">Gerty Cori</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> along with husband <a class="mw-redirect" <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> mechanisms for energy production. Moreover, they discovered the <a href="/wiki/Cori_cycle" title="Cori cycle">Cori cycle</a>, also known as the Lactic acid cycle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> which describes how muscle tissue converts glycogen into lactic acid via <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Barbara_McClintock" title="Barbara McClintock">Barbara McClintock</a> was rewarded the 1983 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transposition_(horizontal_gene_transfer)" title="Transposition (horizontal gene transfer)">genetic transposition</a> McClintock is the only female recipient who has won an unshared Nobel Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gertrude_Elion" title="Gertrude Elion">Gertrude Elion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Hitchings" title="George Hitchings">George Hitchings</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Black_(pharmacologist)" title="James Black (pharmacologist)">Sir James Black</a>, received the Nobel Prize for Physiology or Medicine in 1988 for their development of drugs employed in the treatment of several major diseases, such as <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disorders" title="Autoimmune disorders">autoimmune disorders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpes" title="Herpes">viral herpes</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Linda_B._Buck" title="Linda B. Buck">Linda B. Buck</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nobelprize.org_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-nobelprize.org-48">[48]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Axel" title="Richard Axel">Richard Axel</a>, received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2004 for their discovery of <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">odorant receptors</a> and the complex organization of the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Barr%C3%A9-Sinoussi" title="Fran&#231;oise Barr&#233;-Sinoussi">Fran&#231;oise Barr&#233;-Sinoussi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Luc_Montagnier" title="Luc Montagnier">Luc Montagnier</a>, received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2008 for their work on the identification of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Immunodeficiency_Virus" title="Human Immunodeficiency Virus">Human Immunodeficiency Virus</a> (HIV), the cause of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acquired_Immunodeficiency_Syndrome" title="Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome">Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome</a> (AIDS).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Blackburn" title="Elizabeth Blackburn">Elizabeth Blackburn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Carol_W._Greider" title="Carol W. Greider">Carol W. Greider</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_W._Szostak" title="Jack W. Szostak">Jack W. Szostak</a>, was awarded the 2009 Nobel Prize for Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of the genetic composition and function of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telomeres" title="Telomeres">telomeres</a> and the enzyme called <a href="/wiki/Telomerase" title="Telomerase">telomerase</a>.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
