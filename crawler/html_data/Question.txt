Questions can thus be understood as a kind of <a href="/wiki/Illocutionary_act" title="Illocutionary act">illocutionary act</a> in the field of <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a> or as special kinds of propositions in frameworks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_(logic)" title="Formal semantics (logic)">formal semantics</a> such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_semantics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alternative semantics (page does not exist)">alternative semantics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inquisitive_semantics" title="Inquisitive semantics">inquisitive semantics</a>. The information requested is expected to be provided in the form of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Answer_(response)" title="Answer (response)">answer</a>. Questions are often conflated with <a href="/wiki/Interrogative" title="Interrogative">interrogatives</a>, which are the <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatical</a> forms typically used to achieve them. <a href="/wiki/Rhetorical_question" title="Rhetorical question">Rhetorical questions</a>, for example, are interrogative in form but may not be considered true questions as they are not expected to be answered. Conversely, non-interrogative grammatical structures may be considered questions as in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a> sentence "tell me your name".\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BBC_World_Questions_-_Hungary.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/BBC_World_Questions_-_Hungary.jpg/440px-BBC_World_Questions_-_Hungary.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BBC_World_Questions_-_Hungary.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jonathan Dimbleby questioning - BBC World Service</div></div></div>\n<p>The principal use of questions is to elicit information from the person being addressed by indicating the information which the speaker (or writer) desires <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>. However, questions can also be used for a number of other purposes. Questions may be asked for the purpose of testing someone\'s knowledge, as in a <a href="/wiki/Quiz" title="Quiz">quiz</a> or <a href="/wiki/Test_(assessment)" title="Test (assessment)">examination</a>. These are termed <a href="/wiki/Display_question" title="Display question">display questions</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>.  Raising a question may guide the questioner along an avenue of <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Socratic_method" title="Socratic method">Socratic method</a>).\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Research_question" title="Research question">research question</a> is an interrogative statement that manifests the objective or line of scholarly or scientific inquiry designed to address a specific gap in knowledge.  Research questions are expressed in a language that is appropriate for the academic community that has the greatest interest in answers that would address said gap.  These interrogative statements serve as launching points for the academic pursuit of new knowledge by directing and delimiting an investigation of a topic, a set of studies, or an entire program of research.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Rhetorical_question" title="Rhetorical question">rhetorical question</a> is asked to make a point, and does not expect an answer (often the answer is implied or obvious). As such, it is not a true question. Similarly, requests for things other than information, as with "Would you pass the salt?" are interrogative in form, but are not true questions.\n</p><p>Pre-suppositional or <a href="/wiki/Loaded_question" title="Loaded question">loaded questions</a>, such as "Have you stopped beating your wife?" may be used as a joke or to embarrass an audience, because any answer a person could give would imply more information than he was willing to affirm.\n</p><p>Questions can also be used as titles of works of literature, art and scholarship. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>\'s short story <i><a href="/wiki/How_Much_Land_Does_a_Man_Need%3F" title="How Much Land Does a Man Need?">How Much Land Does a Man Need?</a></i>, the painting <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/And_When_Did_You_Last_See_Your_Father%3F_(picture)" title="And When Did You Last See Your Father? (picture)">And When Did You Last See Your Father?</a></i>, the movie <i><a href="/wiki/What_About_Bob%3F" title="What About Bob?">What About Bob?</a></i>, and the academic work <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Who_Asked_the_First_Question%3F" title="Who Asked the First Question?">Who Asked the First Question?</a></i>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_purpose">By purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: By purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Various categorizations of questions have been proposed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> With regard to research projects, one system distinguishes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>descriptive questions</i>, used primarily with the aim of describing the existence of some thing or process</li>\n<li><i>relational questions</i>, designed to look at the relationships between two or more variables</li>\n<li><i>causal questions</i>, designed to determine whether certain variables affect one or more outcome variables</li></ul>\n<p>For the purpose of surveys, one type of question asked is the <a href="/wiki/Closed-ended_question" title="Closed-ended question">closed-ended</a> (also <i>closed</i> or <i>dichotomous</i>) question, usually requiring a yes/no answer or the choice of an option(s) from a list (see also <a href="/wiki/Multiple_choice" title="Multiple choice">multiple choice</a>). There are also <i>nominal questions</i>, designed to inquire about a level of quantitative measure, usually making connections between a number and a concept (as in "1 = Moderate; 2 = Severe; 3 = ...").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Open-ended_question" title="Open-ended question">Open-ended</a> or <i>open</i> questions give the respondent greater freedom to provide information or opinions on a topic. (The distinction between closed and open questions is applied in a variety of other contexts too, such as <a href="/wiki/Job_interview" title="Job interview">job interviewing</a>.) Surveys also often contain <i>qualifying questions</i> (also called <i>filter questions</i> or <i>contingency questions</i>), which serve to determine whether the respondent needs to continue on to answer subsequent questions.\n</p><p>Some types of questions that may be used in an educational context are listed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloom%27s_Taxonomy" title="Bloom\'s Taxonomy">Bloom\'s Taxonomy</a> of educational objectives. These include questions designed to test and promote:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Knowledge: <a href="/wiki/Five_Ws" title="Five Ws">Who, what, when, where, why, how</a>&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? Describe&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;?</li>\n<li>Comprehension: Retell&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;</li>\n<li>Application: How is&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;an example of&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;?; How is&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;related to&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;?; Why is&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;significant?</li>\n<li>Analysis: What are the parts or features of&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? Classify&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;according to&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;;</li>\n<li>Synthesis: What would you infer from&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;?  What ideas can you add to&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? How would you design a new&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? What would happen if you combined&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? What solutions would you suggest for&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;?</li>\n<li>Evaluation: Do you agree that&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? What do you think about&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? What is the most important&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? Place the following in order of priority&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? How would you decide about&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? What criteria would you use to assess&#160;.&#160;.&#160;.&#160;? <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>McKenzie\'s "Questioning Toolkit"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> lists 17 types of questions, and suggests that thinkers need to orchestrate and combine these types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Examples of these question types include the <i>irreverent question</i>, the <i>apparently irrelevant question</i>, the <i>hypothetical question</i> and the <i>unanswerable question</i>. Questions can also be <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/felicitous" title="wikt:felicitous">infelicitous</a>, being based on incorrect and illogical premises (e.g. "Why do cats have green wings?") or ill-placed premises (e.g. "When did you start beating your wife?").\n</p><p>Strategic studies also took into consideration the questioning process. In Humint (Human Intelligence), a taxonomy of questions includes:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Direct questions: basic questions normally beginning with an interrogative (who, what, where, when, how, or why) and requiring a narrative answer. They are brief, precise, and simply worded to avoid confusion.</li>\n<li>Initial questions: directed toward obtaining the basic information on the topic. In other words, they are the &#8220;who, what, where, when, how, and why&#8221; of each topic.</li>\n<li>Follow-up questions: used to expand on and complete the information obtained from the initial questions.</li>\n<li>Nonpertinent questions: questions that do not pertain to the collection objectives. They are used to conceal the collection objectives or to strengthen rapport with the source.</li>\n<li>Repeat questions: ask the source for the same information obtained in response to earlier questions.</li>\n<li>Control questions: developed from recently confirmed information from other sources that is not likely to have changed.</li>\n<li>Prepared questions developed by the HUMINT collector, normally in writing, prior to the questioning.</li>\n<li>Prepared questions: used primarily when dealing with information of a technical nature or specific topic.</li>\n<li>Negative questions: questions that contain a negative word in the question itself such as, "Didn&#8217;t you go to the pick-up point?&#8221;</li>\n<li>Compound questions: consist of two questions asked at the same time; for example,  &#8220;Where were you going after work and who were you to meet there?&#8221;</li>\n<li>Vague questions: do not have enough information for the source to understand exactly what the HUMINT collector is asking. They may be incomplete, general, or otherwise nonspecific.</li>\n<li>Elicitation: is the gaining of information through direct interaction with a human source where the source is not aware of the specific purpose for the conversation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Softball question: question on easy, not serious or not important topic</li>\n<li>Hardball question: confronting question, pressing the answerer to explain e.g. inconsistencies or discrepancies with their previous stances</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gotcha_question" title="Gotcha question">Gotcha question</a></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_grammatical_form">By grammatical form <span id="wh"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By grammatical form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Questions that ask whether or not some statement is true are called <a href="/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question" title="Yes&#8211;no question">yes&#8211;no questions</a> (or <i>polar questions</i>, or <i>general questions</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm-10">[10]</a></sup>), since they can in principle be answered by a <a href="/wiki/Yes_and_no" title="Yes and no">"yes" or "no"</a> (or similar words or expressions in other languages). Examples include "Do you take sugar?", "Should they be believed?" and "Am I the loneliest person in the world?"\n</p><p>A type of question that is similar in form to a yes&#8211;no question, but is not intended to be answered with a "yes" or "no", is the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_question" title="Alternative question">alternative question</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> (or <i>choice question</i>). This presents two or more alternative answers, as in "Do you want fish or lamb?", or "Are you supporting England, Ireland or Wales?" The expected response is one of the alternatives, or some other indication such as "both" or "neither" (questionnaire forms sometimes contain an option "none of the above" or similar for such questions). Because of their similarity in form to yes&#8211;no questions, they may sometimes be answered "yes" or "no", possibly humorously or as a result of misunderstanding.\n</p><p>The other main type of question (other than yes&#8211;no questions) is those called <b><i>wh</i>-questions</b> (or <i>non-polar questions</i>, or <i>special questions</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chisholm_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chisholm-10">[10]</a></sup>). These use <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">interrogative words</a> (<i>wh</i>-words) such as <i>when</i>, <i>which</i>, <i>who</i>, <i>how</i>, etc. to specify the information that is desired. (In some languages the formation of such questions may involve <a href="/wiki/Wh-movement" title="Wh-movement"><i>wh</i>-movement</a> &#8211; see the section below for grammatical description.) The name derives from the fact that most of the English interrogative words (with the exception of <i>how</i>) begin with the letters <i>wh</i>. These are the types of question sometimes referred to in journalism and other investigative contexts as the <a href="/wiki/Five_Ws" title="Five Ws">Five Ws</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tag_question" title="Tag question">Tag questions</a> are a grammatical structure in which a declarative statement or an imperative is turned into a question by adding an interrogative fragment (the "tag"), such as <i>right</i> in "You remembered the eggs, right?", or <i>isn\'t it</i> in "It\'s cold today, isn\'t it?" Tag questions may or may not be answerable with a yes or no.\n</p><p>As well as direct questions (such as <i>Where are my keys?</i>), there also exist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indirect_question" title="Indirect question">indirect questions</a> (also called <i>interrogative content clauses</i>), such as <i>where my keys are</i>. These are used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subordinate_clause" title="Subordinate clause">subordinate clauses</a> in sentences such as "I wonder where my keys are" and "Ask him where my keys are." Indirect questions do not necessarily follow the same rules of grammar as direct questions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, in English and some other languages, indirect questions are formed without inversion of subject and verb (compare the word order in "where are they?" and "(I wonder) where they are"). Indirect questions may also be subject to the changes of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tense</a> and other changes that apply generally to <a href="/wiki/Indirect_speech" title="Indirect speech">indirect speech</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interrogative" title="Interrogative">Interrogative</a></div>\n<p>Languages may use both <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">prosody</a> to distinguish interrogative sentences (which pose questions) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declarative_sentence" title="Declarative sentence">declarative sentences</a> (which state propositions). Syntax refers to grammatical changes, such as moving words around or adding question words; prosody refers here to changes in <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">intonation</a> while speaking.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/English_grammar" title="English grammar">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_grammar" title="German grammar">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_grammar" title="French grammar">French</a> and various other languages, questions are marked by a distinct <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(linguistics)" title="Inversion (linguistics)">inversion</a> &#8211; the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> is placed after the <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> rather than before it: "You are cold" becomes "Are you cold?" However, English allows such inversion only with a particular class of verbs (called auxiliary or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_verb" title="Special verb">special verbs</a>), and thus sometimes requires the addition of an auxiliary <i>do</i>, <i>does</i> or <i>did</i> before inversion can take place ("He sings" &#8594; "Does he sing?") &#8211; for details see <a href="/wiki/Do-support" title="Do-support"><i>do</i>-support</a>.\n</p><p>In some languages, yes&#8211;no questions are marked by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interrogative_particle" title="Interrogative particle">interrogative particle</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> <span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">&#12363;</span> <i>ka</i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin" title="Standard Mandarin">Mandarin</a> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">&#21527;</span> <i>ma</i> and <a href="/wiki/Polish_grammar" title="Polish grammar">Polish</a> <i>czy</i>. Also, in languages generally, <i>wh</i>-questions are marked by an <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">interrogative word</a> (<i>wh</i>-word) such as <i>what</i>, <i>where</i> or <i>how</i>. In languages such as English this word generally moves to the front of the sentence (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wh-fronting" title="Wh-fronting"><i>wh</i>-fronting</a>), and subject&#8211;verb inversion occurs as in yes&#8211;no questions, but in some other languages these changes in word order are not necessary (e.g. Mandarin <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">&#20320;&#35201;&#20160;&#20040;&#65311;</span> <i>n&#464; y&#224;o sh&#233;nme</i>, meaning "what do you want?" is literally "you want what?").\n</p><p>Intonation patterns characteristic of questions often involve a raised pitch near the end of the sentence. In English this occurs especially for yes&#8211;no questions; it may also be used for sentences that do not have the grammatical form of questions, but are nonetheless intended to elicit information (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declarative_question" title="Declarative question">declarative questions</a>), as in "You\'re not using this?"\n</p><p>In languages written in <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> or certain other scripts, a <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">question mark</a> at the end of a sentence identifies questions in writing. (In <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> an additional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%BF" title="&#191;">inverted mark</a> is placed at the beginning: <i>&#191;C&#243;mo est&#225; usted?</i> "How are you?") As with intonation, this feature is not restricted to sentences having the grammatical form of questions &#8211; it may also indicate a sentence\'s <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatic</a> function.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responses">Responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most typical response to a question is an answer that provides the information indicated as being sought by the questioner. This may range from a simple <a href="/wiki/Yes_and_no" title="Yes and no"><i>yes</i> or <i>no</i></a> (in the case of yes&#8211;no questions) to a more complex or detailed answer. (An answer may be <i>correct</i> or <i>incorrect</i>, depending on whether the information it presents is true or <a href="/wiki/False_(logic)" title="False (logic)">false</a>.) An indication of inability or unwillingness to provide an answer is the other response to a question.\n</p><p>"Negative questions" are interrogative sentences which contain negation in their phrasing, such as "Shouldn\'t you be working?" These can have different ways of expressing affirmation and denial from the standard form of question, and they can be confusing, since it is sometimes unclear whether the answer should be the opposite of the answer to the non-negated question. For example, if one does not have a passport, both "Do you have a passport?" and "Don\'t you have a passport?" are properly answered with "No", despite apparently asking opposite questions. The Japanese and Korean languages avoid this ambiguity. Answering "No" to the second of these in Japanese or Korean would mean, "I <i>do</i> have a passport".\n</p><p>A similar ambiguous question in English is "Do you mind if...?" The responder may reply unambiguously "Yes, I do mind," if they do mind, or "No, I don\'t mind," if they don\'t, but a simple "No" or "Yes" answer can lead to confusion, as a single "No" can seem like a "Yes, I do mind" (as in "No, please don\'t do that"), and a "Yes" can seem like a "No, I don\'t mind" (as in "Yes, go ahead"). An easy way to bypass this confusion would be to ask a non-negative question, such as "Is it all right with you if...?"\n</p><p>Some languages have different particles (for example the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> "<i>si</i>", the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> "<i>doch</i>" or the <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> "<i>jo</i>") to answer negative questions (or negative statements) in an affirmative way; they provide a means to express contradiction.\n</p><p>More information on these issues can be found in the articles <a href="/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question" title="Yes&#8211;no question">yes&#8211;no question</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yes_and_no" title="Yes and no">yes and no</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Answer_ellipsis" title="Answer ellipsis">answer ellipsis</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning">Learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Learning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Questions are used from the most elementary stage of learning to original research.  In the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>, a question often forms the basis of the investigation and can be considered a transition between the observation and hypothesis stages.  Students of all ages use questions in their learning of topics, and the skill of having learners creating "investigatable" questions is a central part of <a href="/wiki/Inquiry_education" title="Inquiry education">inquiry education</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Socratic_method" title="Socratic method">Socratic method</a> of questioning student responses may be used by a teacher to lead the student towards the truth without direct instruction, and also helps students to form logical conclusions.\n</p><p>A widespread and accepted use of questions in an educational context is the assessment of students\' knowledge through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exam" title="Exam">exams</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span There are questions that are not fully answered by any other. Philosophy deals with questions that arise when people reflect on their lives and their world. Some philosophical questions are practical: for example, "Is <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a> justifiable?", "Does the state have the right to censor pornography or restrict tobacco advertising?", "To what extent are <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="M&#257;ori people">M&#257;ori</a> and <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81keh%C4%81" title="P&#257;keh&#257;">P&#257;keh&#257;</a> today responsible for decisions made by their ancestors?"\n</p><p>Other philosophical questions are more <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theoretical</a>, although they often arise through thinking about practical issues. The questions just listed, for example, may prompt more general philosophical questions about the circumstances under which it may be morally justifiable to take a life, or about the extent to which the state may restrict the liberty of the individual. Some "classic" questions of philosophy are speculative and theoretical and concern the nature of knowledge, reality and human existence: for example, "What, if anything, can be known with certainty?", "Is the mind essentially non-physical?", "Are values absolute or relative?", "Does the universe need explanation in terms of a Supreme Intelligence?", "What, if anything, is the meaning or purpose of human existence?" Finally, the philosophical questions are typically about conceptual issues; they are often questions about our concepts and the relation between our concepts and the world they represent. Every question implies a statement and every statement implies a question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Question&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Enculturated apes <a href="/wiki/Kanzi" title="Kanzi">Kanzi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washoe_(chimpanzee)" title="Washoe (chimpanzee)">Washoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_(chimpanzee)" title="Sarah (chimpanzee)">Sarah</a> and a few others who underwent extensive language training programs (with the use of gestures and other visual forms of communications) successfully learned to <i>answer</i> quite complex questions and requests (including question words "who" what", "where"), although so far they failed to learn how to <i>ask questions themselves</i>. For example, <a href="/wiki/David_Premack" title="David Premack">David and Anne Premack</a> wrote: "Though she [Sarah] understood the question, she did not herself ask any questions &#8212; unlike the child who asks interminable questions, such as What that? Who making noise? When Daddy come home? Me go Granny\'s house? Where puppy? Sarah never delayed the departure of her trainer after her lessons by asking where the trainer was going, when she was returning, or anything else".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The ability to ask questions is often assessed in relation to comprehension of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic_structure" title="Syntactic structure">syntactic structures</a>. It is widely accepted, that the first questions are asked by humans during their early infancy, at the pre-syntactic, one word stage of <a href="/wiki/Language_development" title="Language development">language development</a>, with the use of question <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">intonation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a
