(1 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> and 1 parsec are not to scale, 1 parsec = ~206265 AU)</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Stellar parallax</b> is the apparent shift of position of any nearby star (or other object) against the background of distant objects. Created by the different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_positions_of_Earth" title="Orbital positions of Earth">orbital positions of Earth</a>, the extremely small observed shift is largest at time intervals of about six months, when Earth arrives at exactly opposite sides of the Sun in its orbit, giving a baseline distance of about two <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a> between observations. The <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a> itself is considered to be half of this maximum, about equivalent to the observational shift that would occur due to the different positions of Earth and the Sun, a baseline of one <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a> (AU).\n</p><p>Stellar parallax is so difficult to detect that its existence was the subject of much debate in astronomy for hundreds of years. It was first observed in 1806 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giuseppe_Calandrelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giuseppe Calandrelli (page does not exist)">Giuseppe Calandrelli</a> who reported parallax in &#945;-Lyrae in his work "<i>Osservazione e riflessione sulla parallasse annua dall&#8217;alfa della Lira</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Then in 1838 <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">Friedrich Bessel</a> made the first successful parallax measurement, for the star <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">61&#160;Cygni</a>, using a Fraunhofer <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">heliometer</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Observatory" title="K&#246;nigsberg Observatory">K&#246;nigsberg Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZG44_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZG44-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hirschgoogle_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hirschgoogle-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once a star\'s parallax is known, its distance from Earth can be computed <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometrically</a>. But the more distant an object is, the smaller its parallax. Even with 21st-century techniques in <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometry</a>, the limits of accurate measurement make distances farther away than about 100 <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> (or roughly 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a>) too approximate to be useful when obtained by this technique. This limits the applicability of parallax as a measurement of distance to objects that are relatively close on a galactic scale. Other techniques, such as <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectral</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-shift" title="Red-shift">red-shift</a>, are required to measure the distance of more remote objects.\n</p><p>Stellar parallax measures are given in the tiny units of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a>, or even in thousandths of arcseconds (milliarcseconds). The distance unit parsec is defined as the length of the <a href="/wiki/Cathetus" title="Cathetus">leg</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Right_triangle" title="Right triangle">right triangle</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjacent_side_(right_triangle)" title="Adjacent side (right triangle)">adjacent to</a> the angle of one arcsecond at one <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">vertex</a>, where the other leg is 1 AU long. Because stellar parallaxes and distances all involve such <a href="/wiki/Skinny_triangle" title="Skinny triangle">skinny right triangles</a>, a convenient trigonometric approximation can be used to convert parallaxes (in arcseconds) to distance (in parsecs). reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_theory_and_attempts">Early theory and attempts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early theory and attempts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Stellar parallax is so small (as to be unobservable until the 19th century) that its apparent absence was used as a scientific argument against <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">heliocentrism</a> during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_modern_age" title="Early modern age">early modern age</a>. It is clear from <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> that the effect would be undetectable if the stars were far enough away, but for various reasons such gigantic distances involved seemed entirely implausible: it was one of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a>\'s principal objections to <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">Copernican heliocentrism</a> that in order for it to be compatible with the lack of observable stellar parallax, there would have to be an enormous and unlikely void between the orbit of Saturn and the eighth sphere (the fixed stars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_Bradley" title="James Bradley">James Bradley</a> first tried to measure stellar parallaxes in 1729. The stellar movement proved too insignificant for his <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>, but he instead discovered the <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">aberration of light</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_nutation" title="Astronomical nutation">nutation</a> of Earth\'s axis, and catalogued 3222 stars.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_and_20th_centuries">19th and 20th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19th and 20th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Koenigsberg_helio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="299" decoding="async" height="328" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Koenigsberg_helio.jpg/220px-Koenigsberg_helio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/Koenigsberg_helio.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Koenigsberg_helio.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bessel\'s heliometer</div></div></div>\n<p>Stellar parallax is most often measured using <i>annual parallax</i>, defined as the difference in position of a star as seen from Earth and Sun, i.&#160;e. the angle subtended at a star by the mean radius of Earth\'s orbit around the Sun. The <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a> (3.26 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a>) is defined as the distance for which the annual parallax is 1&#160;<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcsecond</a>. Annual parallax is normally measured by observing the position of a star at different times of the year as Earth moves through its orbit. Measurement of annual parallax was the first reliable way to determine the distances to the closest stars. The first successful measurements of stellar parallax were made by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">Friedrich Bessel</a> in 1838 for the star <a href="/wiki/61_Cygni" title="61 Cygni">61&#160;Cygni</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Heliometer" title="Heliometer">heliometer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZG44_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZG44-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Being very difficult to measure, only about 60 stellar parallaxes had been obtained by the end of the 19th century, mostly by use of the <a href="/wiki/Filar_micrometer" title="Filar micrometer">filar micrometer</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Astrograph" title="Astrograph">Astrographs</a> using astronomical <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">photographic plates</a> sped the process in the early 20th century.  Automated plate-measuring machines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and more sophisticated computer technology of the 1960s allowed more efficient compilation of <a href="/wiki/Star_catalogue" title="Star catalogue">star catalogues</a>. In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled devices</a> (CCDs) replaced photographic plates and reduced optical uncertainties to one milliarcsecond.\n</p><p>Stellar parallax remains the standard for calibrating other measurement methods (see <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">Cosmic distance ladder</a>). Accurate calculations of distance based on stellar parallax require a measurement of the distance from Earth to the Sun, now known to exquisite accuracy based on <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> reflection off the surfaces of planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The angles involved in these calculations are very small and thus difficult to measure. The nearest star to the Sun (and also the star with the largest parallax), <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">Proxima Centauri</a>, has a parallax of 0.7687&#160;&#177;&#160;0.0003&#160;arcsec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj118_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-apj118-4">[4]</a></sup> This angle is approximately that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtended" title="Subtended">subtended</a> by an object 2 centimeters in diameter located 5.3 kilometers away.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_astrometry_for_parallax">Space astrometry for parallax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Space astrometry for parallax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_stretches_the_stellar_tape_measure_ten_times_further.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3150" data-file-width="2400" decoding="async" height="289" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hubble_stretches_the_stellar_tape_measure_ten_times_further.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hubble precision stellar distance measurement has been extended 10 times further into the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1989 the satellite <a href="/wiki/Hipparcos" title="Hipparcos">Hipparcos</a> was launched primarily for obtaining parallaxes and <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motions</a> of nearby stars, increasing the number of stellar parallaxes measured to milliarcsecond accuracy a thousandfold.  Even so, Hipparcos is only able to measure parallax angles for stars up to about 1,600 <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">light-years</a> away, a little more than one percent of the diameter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a>.\n</p><p>The Hubble telescope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WFC3" title="WFC3">WFC3</a> now has a precision of 20 to 40 microarcseconds, enabling reliable distance measurements up to 3,066 parsecs (10,000&#160;ly) for a small number of stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This gives more accuracy to the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder" title="Cosmic distance ladder">Cosmic distance ladder</a> and improves the knowledge of distances in the Universe, based on the dimensions of the Earth\'s orbit.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_mission" title="Gaia mission">Gaia mission</a>, launched 19 December 2013, is expected to measure parallax angles to an accuracy of 10 micro<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">arcseconds</a> for all moderately bright stars, thus mapping nearby stars (and potentially planets) up to a distance of tens of thousands of light-years from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Data Release 2 in 2018 claims mean errors for the parallaxes of 15th magnitude and brighter stars of 20 &#8211; 40 microarcseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dr2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dr2-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_baselines">Other baselines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other baselines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_parallax">Statistical parallax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statistical parallax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two related techniques can determine the mean distances of stars by modelling the motions of stars.  Both are referred to as statistical parallaxes, or individual called secular parallaxes and classical statistical parallaxes.\n</p><p>The motion of the Sun through space provides a longer baseline that will increase the accuracy of parallax measurements, known as secular parallax. For stars in the Milky Way disk, this corresponds to a mean baseline of 4 AU per year, whereas for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halo_star" title="Halo star">halo stars</a> the baseline is 40 AU per year. After several decades, the baseline can be orders of magnitude greater than the Earth&#8211;Sun baseline used for traditional parallax. However, secular parallax introduces a higher level of uncertainty because the relative velocity of other stars is an additional unknown. When applied to samples of multiple stars, the uncertainty can be reduced; the precision is inversely proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Square_root" title="Square root">square root</a> of the sample size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popowski_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-popowski-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mean parallaxes and distances of a large group of stars can be estimated from their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_velocities" title="Radial velocities">radial velocities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motions</a>.  This is known as a classical statistical parallax.  The motions of the stars are modelled to statistically reproduce the velocity dispersion based on their distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popowski_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-popowski-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-layden_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-layden-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_parallax_in_astronomy">Other parallax in astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_parallax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other parallax in astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Other uses of the term parallax in astronomy, with different meanings are the <a href="/wiki/Photometric_parallax_method" title="Photometric parallax method">photometric parallax method</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_parallax" title="Spectroscopic parallax">spectroscopic parallax</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_parallax" title="Dynamical parallax">dynamical parallax</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
