mw-body-content">\n</div>\n<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Stellar nucleosynthesis</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">\n<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>\n<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>\n<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Stellar nucleosynthesis</b> is the theory explaining the <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">creation (nucleosynthesis)</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> by nuclear fusion reactions between atoms within stars. Stellar nucleosynthesis has occurred continuously since the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis" title="Big Bang nucleosynthesis">original creation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>. It is a highly predictive theory that today yields excellent agreement between calculations based upon it and the observed abundances of the elements. It explains why the observed abundances of elements in the universe grow over time and why some elements and their isotopes are much more abundant than others. The theory was initially proposed by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> in 1946,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyle1946_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyle1946-1">[1]</a></sup> who later refined it in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyle1954_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyle1954-2">[2]</a></sup> Further advances were made, especially to nucleosynthesis by neutron capture of the elements heavier than iron, by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge" title="Margaret Burbidge">Margaret Burbidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge" title="Geoffrey Burbidge">Geoffrey Burbidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler" title="William Alfred Fowler">William Alfred Fowler</a> and Hoyle in their famous 1957 <a href="/wiki/B2FH_paper" title="B2FH paper">B<sup>2</sup>FH paper</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B2FH_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-B2FH-3">[3]</a></sup> which became one of the most heavily cited papers in astrophysics history.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">Stars evolve</a> because of changes in their composition (the abundance of their constituent elements) over their lifespans, first by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_burning" title="Hydrogen burning">burning hydrogen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> star), then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helium_burning" title="Helium burning">helium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Red_giant" title="Red giant">red giant</a> star), and progressively burning higher elements. However, this does not by itself significantly alter the abundances of elements in the universe as the elements are contained within the star. Later in its life, a low-mass star will slowly eject its atmosphere via <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a>, forming a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a>, while a higher&#8211;mass star will eject mass via a sudden catastrophic event called a <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a>. The term <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">supernova nucleosynthesis</a> is used to describe the creation of elements during the evolution and explosion of a pre-supernova massive star (12&#8211;35 times the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">mass of the sun</a>). Those massive stars are the most prolific source of new isotopes from carbon (<span class="nowrap"><i><a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">Z</a></i> = 6</span>) to nickel (<span class="nowrap"><i>Z</i> = 28</span>).\n</p><p>The advanced sequence of burning fuels is driven by <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">gravitational collapse</a> and its associated heating, resulting in the subsequent burning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_burning" title="Carbon burning">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-burning_process" title="Oxygen-burning process">oxygen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_burning" title="Silicon burning">silicon</a>. However, most of the nucleosynthesis in the mass range <span class="nowrap"><i><a href="/wiki/Mass_number" title="Mass number">A</a></i> = 28&#8211;56</span> (from silicon to nickel) is actually caused by the upper layers of the star <a href="/wiki/Supernova#Core_collapse" title="Supernova">collapsing onto the core</a>, creating a compressional <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> rebounding outward. The shock front briefly raises temperatures by roughly 50%, thereby causing furious burning for about a second. This final burning in massive stars, called <i>explosive nucleosynthesis</i> or <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">supernova nucleosynthesis</a>, is the final epoch of stellar nucleosynthesis.\n</p><p>A stimulus to the development of the theory of nucleosynthesis was the discovery of variations in the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements#Universe" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">abundances of elements found in the universe</a>. The need for a physical description was already inspired by the relative abundances of isotopes of the chemical elements in the solar system. Those abundances, when plotted on a graph as a function of atomic number of the element, have a jagged sawtooth shape that varies by factors of tens of millions (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_nucleosynthesis_theory" title="History of nucleosynthesis theory">history of nucleosynthesis theory</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This suggested a natural process that is not random. A second stimulus to understanding the processes of stellar nucleosynthesis occurred during the 20th century, when it was realized that the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> released from nuclear fusion reactions accounted for the longevity of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> as a source of heat and light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayton1968_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clayton1968-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_nucleosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="688" data-file-width="538" decoding="async" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg/250px-Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg/375px-Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg/500px-Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a> proposed that stars obtained their energy from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion#Astrophysical_reaction_chains" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and also raised the possibility that the heavier elements are produced in stars.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a>, on the basis of the precise measurements of atomic masses by <a href="/wiki/Francis_William_Aston" title="Francis William Aston">F.W. Aston</a> and a preliminary suggestion by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Perrin" title="Jean Perrin">Jean Perrin</a>, proposed that stars obtained their energy from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and raised the possibility that the heavier elements are produced in stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This was a preliminary step toward the idea of <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a>. In 1928, <a href="/wiki/George_Gamow" title="George Gamow">George Gamow</a> derived what is now called the <a href="/wiki/Gamow_factor" title="Gamow factor">Gamow factor</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum-mechanical</a> formula that gave the probability of bringing two nuclei sufficiently close for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_nuclear_force" title="Strong nuclear force">strong nuclear force</a> to overcome the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb_barrier" title="Coulomb barrier">Coulomb barrier</a>. The Gamow factor was used in the decade that followed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_d%27Escourt_Atkinson" title="Robert d\'Escourt Atkinson">Atkinson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Houtermans" title="Fritz Houtermans">Houtermans</a> and later by Gamow himself and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Teller" title="Edward Teller">Edward Teller</a> to derive the rate at which nuclear reactions would proceed at the high temperatures believed to exist in stellar interiors.\n</p><p>In 1939, in a paper entitled "Energy Production in Stars", <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a> analyzed the different possibilities for reactions by which hydrogen is fused into helium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He defined two processes that he believed to be the sources of energy in stars. The first one, the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton&#8211;proton chain reaction">proton&#8211;proton chain reaction</a>, is the dominant energy source in stars with masses up to about the mass of the Sun. The second process, the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">carbon&#8211;nitrogen&#8211;oxygen cycle</a>, which was also considered by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker" title="Carl Friedrich von Weizs&#228;cker">Carl Friedrich von Weizs&#228;cker</a> in 1938, is more important in more massive main-sequence stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These works concerned the energy generation capable of keeping stars hot. A clear physical description of the proton&#8211;proton chain and of the CNO cycle appears in a 1968 textbook<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayton1968_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clayton1968-5">[5]</a></sup>. Bethe\'s two papers did not address the creation of heavier nuclei, however. That theory was begun by <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> in 1946 with his argument that a collection of very hot nuclei would assemble thermodynamically into <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyle1946_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyle1946-1">[1]</a></sup>. Hoyle followed that in 1954 with a large paper describing how advanced fusion stages within massive stars would synthesize the elements from carbon to iron in mass<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoyle1954_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoyle1954-2">[2]</a></sup>. This is the first work of stellar nucleosynthesis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>. It and Hoyle\'s 1954 paper provided the roadmap to how the most abundant elements on Earth had been synthesized within stars from their initial hydrogen and helium, making clear how those abundant elements increased their galactic abundances as the galaxy aged.\n</p><p>Hoyle\'s theory was expanded to other processes, beginning with the publication of a review paper in 1957 by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge" title="Margaret Burbidge">Burbidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge" title="Geoffrey Burbidge">Burbidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler" title="William Alfred Fowler">Fowler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Hoyle</a> (commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/B2FH_paper" title="B2FH paper">B<sup>2</sup>FH paper</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B2FH_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-B2FH-3">[3]</a></sup> This review paper collected and refined earlier research into a heavily cited picture that gave promise of accounting for the observed relative abundances of the elements; but it did not itself enlarge Hoyle\'s 1954 picture for the origin of primary nuclei as much as many assumed, except in the understanding of nucleosynthesis of those elements heavier than iron by neutron capture. Significant improvements were made by <a href="/wiki/Alastair_G._W._Cameron" title="Alastair G. W. Cameron">Alastair G. W. Cameron</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Donald_D._Clayton" title="Donald D. Clayton">Donald D. Clayton</a>. Cameron presented his own independent approach<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (following Hoyle\'s approach for the most part) of nucleosynthesis. He introduced computers into time-dependent calculations of evolution of nuclear systems. Clayton calculated the first time-dependent models of the <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">S-process</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and of the <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">R-process</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seeger1965_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seeger1965-14">[14]</a></sup> as well as of the burning of silicon into the abundant alpha-particle nuclei and iron-group elements,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodansky1968a_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bodansky1968a-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bodansky1968b_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bodansky1968b-16">[16]</a></sup> and discovered radiogenic chronologies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> for determining the age of the elements. The entire research field expanded rapidly in the 1970s.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton&#8211;proton chain reaction">Proton&#8211;proton chain .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:304px;max-width:304px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:302px;max-width:302px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FusionintheSun.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="966" data-file-width="678" reaction</b></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:302px;max-width:302px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CNO_Cycle.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1000" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/CNO_Cycle.svg/450px-CNO_Cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/CNO_Cycle.svg/600px-CNO_Cycle.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left"><b>CNO-I cycle</b><br/>The helium nucleus is released at the top-left step.</div></div></div></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fusion" title="Hydrogen fusion">Hydrogen fusion</a> (nuclear fusion of four protons to form a <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">helium-4</a> nucleus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones2009-18">[18]</a></sup>) is the dominant process that generates energy in the cores of <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main-sequence</a> stars. It is also called "hydrogen burning", which should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen#Combustion" title="Hydrogen">combustion of hydrogen</a> in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizing" title="Oxidizing">oxidizing</a> atmosphere. There are two predominant processes by which stellar hydrogen fusion occurs: proton-proton chain and the carbon-nitrogen-oxygen (CNO) cycle. Ninety percent of all stars, with the exception of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_dwarfs" title="White dwarfs">white dwarfs</a>, are fusing hydrogen by these two processes.\n</p><p>In the cores of lower-mass main-sequence stars such as the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, the dominant energy production process is the <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton&#8211;proton chain reaction">proton&#8211;proton chain reaction</a>. This creates a helium-4 nucleus through a sequence of chain reactions that begin with the fusion of two protons to form a <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> nucleus (one proton plus one neutron) along with an ejected positron and neutrino.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohm_vitense1992_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-19">[19]</a></sup> In each complete fusion cycle, the proton&#8211;proton chain reaction releases about 26.2&#160;MeV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohm_vitense1992_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-19">[19]</a></sup> The proton&#8211;proton chain reaction cycle is relatively insensitive to temperature; a 10% rise of temperature would increase energy production by this method by 46%, hence, this hydrogen fusion process can occur in up to a third of the star\'s radius and occupy half the star\'s mass. For stars above 35% of the Sun\'s mass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaa496_3_787_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaa496_3_787-20">[20]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Energy_flux" title="Energy flux">energy flux</a> toward the surface is sufficiently low and energy transfer from the core region remains by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiative_heat_transfer" title="Radiative heat transfer">radiative heat transfer</a>, rather than by <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">convective heat transfer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deloore_doom1992_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-deloore_doom1992-21">[21]</a></sup> As a result, there is little mixing of fresh hydrogen into the core or fusion products outward.\n</p><p>In higher-mass stars, the dominant energy production process is the <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a>, which is a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_cycle" title="Catalytic cycle">catalytic cycle</a> that uses nuclei of carbon, nitrogen and oxygen as intermediaries and in the end produces a helium nucleus as with the proton-proton chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohm_vitense1992_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-19">[19]</a></sup> During a complete CNO cycle, 25.0&#160;MeV of energy is released. The difference in energy production of this cycle, compared to the proton&#8211;proton chain reaction, is accounted for by the energy lost through <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bohm_vitense1992_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-19">[19]</a></sup> The CNO cycle is very temperature sensitive, a 10% rise of temperature would produce a 350% rise in energy production. About 90% of the CNO cycle energy generation occurs within the inner 15% of the star\'s mass, hence it is strongly concentrated at the core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey2010_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey2010-22">[22]</a></sup> This results in such an intense outward energy flux that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective" title="Convective">convective</a> energy transfer become more important than does <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer</a>. As a result, the core region becomes a <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">convection zone</a>, which stirs the hydrogen fusion region and keeps it well mixed with the surrounding proton-rich region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karttunen_oja2007_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-karttunen_oja2007-23">[23]</a></sup> This core convection occurs in stars where the CNO cycle contributes more than 20% of the total energy. As the star ages and the core temperature increases, the region occupied by the convection zone slowly shrinks from 20% of the mass down to the inner 8% of the mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey2010_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey2010-22">[22]</a></sup> Our Sun produces 10% of its energy from the CNO cycle.\n</p><p>The type of hydrogen fusion process that dominates in a star is determined by the temperature dependency differences between the two reactions. The proton&#8211;proton chain reaction starts at temperatures about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006400000000000000&#9824;</span>4<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>6</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reid_hawley2005_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-reid_hawley2005-24">[24]</a></sup> making it the dominant fusion mechanism in smaller stars. A self-maintaining CNO chain requires a higher temperature of approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7007160000000000000&#9824;</span>16<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>6</sup>&#160;K</span>, but thereafter it increases more rapidly in efficiency as the temperature rises, than does the proton-proton reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salaris_cassini2005_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-salaris_cassini2005-25">[25]</a></sup> Above approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7007170000000000000&#9824;</span>17<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>6</sup>&#160;K</span>, the CNO cycle becomes the dominant source of energy. This temperature is achieved in the cores of main sequence stars with at least 1.3 times the mass of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apj701_1_837_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-apj701_1_837-26">[26]</a></sup> The Sun itself has a core temperature of about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7007157000000000000&#9824;</span>15.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>6</sup>&#160;K</span>. As a main sequence star ages, the core temperature will rise, resulting in a steadily increasing contribution from its CNO cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey2010_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey2010-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helium_fusion">Helium fusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_nucleosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Helium fusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Triple-alpha_process" title="Triple-alpha process">Triple-alpha process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alpha_process" title="Alpha process">Alpha process</a></div>\n<p>Main sequence stars accumulate helium in their cores as a result of hydrogen fusion, but the core does not become hot enough to initiate helium fusion.  Helium fusion first begins when a star leaves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giant_branch" title="Red giant branch">red giant branch</a> after accumulating sufficient helium in its core to ignite it. In stars around the mass of the sun, this begins at the tip of the red giant branch with a <a href="/wiki/Helium_flash" title="Helium flash">helium flash</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">degenerate</a> helium core and the star moves to the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_branch" title="Horizontal branch">horizontal branch</a> where it burns helium in its core. More massive stars ignite helium in their cores without a flash and execute a <a href="/wiki/Blue_loop" title="Blue loop">blue loop</a> before reaching the <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a>. Despite the name, stars on a blue loop from the red giant branch are typically not blue in color, but are rather yellow giants, possibly <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">Cepheid variables</a>. They fuse helium until the core is largely <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The most massive stars become supergiants when they leave the main sequence and quickly start helium fusion as they become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_supergiant" title="Red supergiant">red supergiants</a>. After helium is exhausted in the core of a star, it will continue in a shell around the carbon-oxygen core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones2009_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-jones2009-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deloore_doom1992_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-deloore_doom1992-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In all cases, helium is fused to carbon via the triple-alpha process. This can then form oxygen, neon, and heavier elements via the alpha process. In this way, the alpha process preferentially produces elements with even numbers of protons by the capture of helium nuclei.
