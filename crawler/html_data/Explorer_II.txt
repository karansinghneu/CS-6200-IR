Launched at 8:00 am from the <a href="/wiki/Stratobowl" title="Stratobowl">Stratobowl</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, the helium balloon carried a two-man crew consisting of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">U.&#160;S. Army Air Corps</a> Captains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_W._Stevens" title="Albert W. Stevens">Albert W. Stevens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orvil_A._Anderson" title="Orvil A. Anderson">Orvil A. Anderson</a> inside a sealed, spherical cabin. The crew landed safely near <a href="/wiki/White_Lake,_South_Dakota" title="White Lake, South Dakota">White Lake, South Dakota</a> at 4:13 pm and both were acclaimed as national heroes. Scientific instruments carried on the gondola returned useful information about the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. The mission was funded by the membership of the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmExp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmExp-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In January 1934, the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> (NGS) and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">U.&#160;S. Army Air Corps</a> decided to collaborate on a program to build and launch a manned balloon to the then record altitude of 24&#160;km (15&#160;mi). This vehicle would be capable of carrying a crew of three in an airtight capsule, along with a laboratory of instruments. The hydrogen balloon, named <i>Explorer</i>, was completed by July at a cost of around $60,000; equivalent to $1,123,731 in 2018 currency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The balloon was launched from a canyon in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hills" title="Black Hills">Black Hills</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>&#8212;dubbed the <a href="/wiki/Stratobowl" title="Stratobowl">Stratobowl</a>&#8212;on July 28, 1934 and reached a near-record altitude of 18,475&#160;m (60,613&#160;ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shayler2000_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shayler2000-4">[4]</a></sup> before tears in the fabric led the crew to begin reducing their altitude. A rupture in the balloon resulted in a precipitous descent, followed by a spark that caused the hydrogen to ignite and destroy what was left of the balloon, leaving the capsule to plummet toward the ground at <a href="/wiki/Terminal_velocity" title="Terminal velocity">terminal velocity</a>. The crew just managed to escape using their parachutes, with the last man bailing out at 500 feet (150&#160;m) above the ground. Their capsule was almost completely destroyed upon impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmExp_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AmExp-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The crash resulted in a national embarrassment, leading Captain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_W._Stevens" title="Albert W. Stevens">Albert W. Stevens</a>, the scientific observer on board the <i>Explorer</i> balloon, to lobby for another attempt with an improved balloon to be named <i>Explorer II</i>. But news of the <a href="/wiki/Osoaviakhim-1" title="Osoaviakhim-1">fatal crash of a Russian stratospheric attempt</a> in 1934 left President <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Hovey_Grosvenor" title="Gilbert Hovey Grosvenor">Gilbert H. Grosvenor</a> of the NGS sobered by the risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup> A review of the crash by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" title="National Bureau of Standards">National Bureau of Standards</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup> (NBS) was held between July and September, revealing that the balloon had not opened symmetrically during the ascent, which caused stresses that led to the fabric tears. A month-long delay prior to launch had allowed the rubberized cotton to stick together, which created the uneven expansion. The hydrogen explosion followed when the gas in the bag mixed with the oxygen in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shayler2000_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shayler2000-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Despite the concerns, in 1935 the NGS and Army Air Corps decided to make another attempt. To eliminate the hazard of the hydrogen lifting gas, it was decided instead to use helium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmExp_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-AmExp-2">[2]</a></sup>&#8212;to which the United States had a monopoly. The lower lifting efficiency of helium gas meant that a larger balloon would be needed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup> so <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company">Goodyear-Zeppelin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> increased the volume to 100,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (3,700,000&#160;cu&#160;ft). <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a> assembled a larger, lighter gondola<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maurer1987_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maurer1987-6">[6]</a></sup> made of "Dowmetal"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup>&#8212;a magnesium-aluminium alloy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup>&#8212; that would carry a two-man crew with a reduced amount of scientific instrumentation. The cabin was 2.7&#160;m (9&#160;ft) in diameter with a mass of 290&#160;kg (640&#160;lb) and could transport a 680&#160;kg (1,500&#160;lb) payload.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup> It was manufactured from a single, large plate that was cut up into form that could be reshaped and welded into a sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Froes2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Froes2006-8">[8]</a></sup> In order to make crew escape easier, the portholes were made wider than on the <i>Explorer I</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup> The atmosphere in the interior of the capsule was supplied from liquid air instead of liquid oxygen in order to reduce the fire hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis2008_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis2008-9">[9]</a></sup> The modified balloon was ready by the spring of 1935 and the first launch occurred July 10, 1935. Unfortunately, this too proved a failure with the balloon rupturing at liftoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following a review by the NBS, the balloon was prepared for another attempt after Goodyear strengthened the material. Examination of climatological data for the Stratobowl collected over the previous fifteen years was examined, and it was determined that the month of October typically had periods of good weather that would last sufficiently long for a flight attempt. A team of meteorologists was assembled at the Stratobowl in early September and they proceeded to put together a weather station. The meteorological requirements for the launch was for clear skies&#8212;with no precipitation&#8212;lasting for the duration of the flight, as well as surface wind speeds that were not to exceed 23&#160;km/h (14&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Potter2010-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>With a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> approaching, on the night of November 10, 1935 the balloon was prepared for launch. The temperature dropped to &#8722;14&#160;&#176;C (6&#160;&#176;F) overnight, so the 10,762.4&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (115,845&#160;sq&#160;ft) of fabric was kept warm and pliable through the use of stoves. The task of inflating the balloon with helium from the 1,685 steel cylinders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> took eight hours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter2010_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Potter2010-10">[10]</a></sup> during which the team needed to repair a 5.2&#160;m (17&#160;ft) long tear that formed in the fabric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maurer1987_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maurer1987-6">[6]</a></sup> Once inflated, the balloon stood 96&#160;m (316&#160;ft) tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FWP2008_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FWP2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The gondola was kept anchored to the earth by a team of more than 100 soldiers holding cables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> Preparations were complete by 7:01&#160;am the following morning<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter2010_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Potter2010-10">[10]</a></sup> and the conditions were deemed suitable for a launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Explorer II</i> gondola at the landing site</div></div></div>\n<p>The crew of the <i>Explorer II</i> consisted of Captain Albert W. Stevens, in command of the mission, and Captain <a href="/wiki/Orvil_A._Anderson" title="Orvil A. Anderson">Orvil A. Anderson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis2008_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davis2008-9">[9]</a></sup> A crowd of around 20,000 viewers gathered to watch the event. (The local residents had raised and contributed $13,000 for the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup>) Lift-off occurred at precisely 8:00 am with the release of 34&#160;kg (75&#160;lb) of ballast made of fine lead shot. A few moments after liftoff, wind shear propelled the balloon into a side canyon, but thereafter it ascended normally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Explorer II</i> reached a peak altitude of 22,066&#160;m (72,395&#160;ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bilstein2001_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bilstein2001-11">[11]</a></sup> at 12:30 pm and remained there for 80 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> This set a new world altitude record, and one that would last for nearly two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bilstein2001_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bilstein2001-11">[11]</a></sup> The crew became the first humans to witness the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of the Earth. Unfortunately, the fan that was to be used to rotate the gondola proved ineffective at that altitude, so they were unable to avoid the Sun\'s glare. This made viewing from one side of the capsule nearly useless. Despite this, Captain Stevens reported seeing details of the Earth\'s surface for hundreds of miles. They were too high up to be able to view any movement on the ground,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> but their photography showed the potential of high-altitude reconnaissance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Explorer II</i> included communications equipment, and constant radio contact was maintained throughout the flight with the signal being broadcast across the U.S. and in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> The onboard instruments collected data on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, the ozone distribution and electrical conductivity of the atmosphere at different altitudes, the atmospheric composition of the stratosphere, and the luminosity of the Sun, Moon and Earth. In addition, microorganisms were collected from the stratosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maurer1987_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Maurer1987-6">[6]</a></sup> Mold samples were carried along to determine the effects of cosmic ray exposure. Stevens took along a camera to take pictures, including the first ever motion pictures shot from the stratosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup> The collected data showed that the ozone in the upper atmosphere was effective at blocking most of the ultraviolet radiation from the Sun. It was also found that the percentage of oxygen at the peak altitude was about the same as that at sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis2008_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davis2008-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Finally, the descent was begun and it proceeded normally. At an altitude of 300&#160;m (1,000&#160;ft) the crew began releasing scientific instruments that would descend by their own parachutes. This was done to protect the data in case the gondola had a rough landing. The precautions proved unnecessary as the balloon landed gently in an open field near the town of <a href="/wiki/White_Lake,_South_Dakota" title="White Lake, South Dakota">White Lake, South Dakota</a> at 4:13 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ryan2003_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan2003-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explorer_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The success of the mission was much celebrated in the press and the aeronauts were invited to an audience with U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>. They became national heroes and both men were presented with the <a href="/wiki/Hubbard_Medal" title="Hubbard Medal">Hubbard Medal</a> of the National Geographic Society by General <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">John J. Pershing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT19351212_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT19351212-12">[12]</a></sup> The Air Corps awarded them the <a href="/wiki/Mackay_Trophy" title="Mackay Trophy">Mackay Trophy</a> for the most meritorious flight of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fredriksen2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fredriksen2011-13">[13]</a></sup> Both men were also awarded the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Flying_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Flying Cross (United States)">Distinguished Flying Cross</a> for each of the Explorer flights.\n</p><p>Scientific observations made during the mission were highly successful and much data was collected,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeVorkin1991_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-DeVorkin1991-5">[5]</a></sup> with the results appearing in scientific journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weightman1937_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weightman1937-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup> The data and the crew experiences were later used when designing flight crew equipment and methods for high-altitude combat operations during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bilstein2001_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bilstein2001-11">[11]</a></sup> The balloon used for the <i>Explorer II</i> expedition was cut up into a million strips and distributed as commemorative bookmarks among the NGS members who supported the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shayler2000_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shayler2000-4">[4]</a></sup> The gondola is on display at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-SI-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
