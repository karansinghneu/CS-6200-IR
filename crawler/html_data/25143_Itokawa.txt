class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=25143_Itokawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The asteroid was discovered in 1998 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LINEAR" title="LINEAR">LINEAR</a> project at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lincoln_Laboratory%27s_Experimental_Test_Site" title="Lincoln Laboratory\'s Experimental Test Site">Lincoln Laboratory\'s Experimental Test Site</a> in Socorro, New Mexico. It was given the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_designation_in_astronomy" title="Provisional designation in astronomy">provisional designation</a> <span class="nowrap">1998 SF<sub>36</sub></span>. This <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> was named after <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Itokawa" title="Hideo Itokawa">Hideo Itokawa</a> (1912&#8211;1999), a Japanese rocket scientist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The official naming citation was published in the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a> on 6 August 2003 (<span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Circulars" title="Minor Planet Circulars">M.P.C.</a> 49281</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Circulars-Archive_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-Circulars-Archive-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=25143_Itokawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Itokawa is an <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">S-type asteroid</a>. <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">Radar</a> imaging by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldstone_Observatory" title="Goldstone Observatory">Goldstone</a> in 2001 observed an ellipsoid 630 &#177; 60 m long and 250 &#177; 30 m wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OstroItokawa_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-OstroItokawa-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Hayabusa mission confirmed these findings and also suggested that Itokawa may be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_binary_(asteroid)" title="Contact binary (asteroid)">contact binary</a> formed by two or more smaller asteroids that have gravitated toward each other and stuck together. The Hayabusa images show a surprising lack of <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact craters</a> and a very rough surface studded with boulders, described by the mission team as a \'rubble pile\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ItokawaBeckons_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ItokawaBeckons-27">[27]</a></sup>  Furthermore, the density of the asteroid is too low for it to be made from solid rock. This would mean that Itokawa is not a <a href="/wiki/Monolith" title="Monolith">monolith</a> but rather a \'<a href="/wiki/Rubble_pile" title="Rubble pile">rubble pile</a>\' formed from fragments that have cohered over time. Based on <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky%E2%80%93O%27Keefe%E2%80%93Radzievskii%E2%80%93Paddack_effect" title="Yarkovsky&#8211;O\'Keefe&#8211;Radzievskii&#8211;Paddack effect">Yarkovsky&#8211;O\'Keefe&#8211;Radzievskii&#8211;Paddack effect</a> measurements, a small section of Itokawa is estimated to have a density of 2.9 g/cm&#179;, whereas a larger section is estimated to have a density of 1.8 g/cm&#179;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eso1405_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-eso1405-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table>\n<tbody><tr>\n<td><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif/300px-Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif/450px-Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif/600px-Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Animation_of_25143_Itokawa_orbit.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of 25143 Itokawa orbit<br/><span style="background-color:lime; The probe arrived in the vicinity of Itokawa on 12 September 2005 and initially "parked" in an asteroid&#8211;Sun line at 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi), and later 7&#160;km (4.3&#160;mi), from the asteroid (Itokawa\'s gravity was too weak to provide an orbit, so the spacecraft adjusted its orbit around the Sun until it matched the asteroid\'s). Hayabusa landed on 20 November for thirty minutes, but it failed to operate a device designed to collect soil samples. On 25 November, a second landing and sampling sequence was attempted. The sample capsule was returned to Earth and landed at <a href="/wiki/Woomera,_South_Australia" title="Woomera, South Australia">Woomera, South Australia</a> on 13 June 2010, around 13:51 UTC (23:21 local). On 16 November 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency</a> reported that dust collected during Hayabusa\'s voyage was indeed from the asteroid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features">Surface features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=25143_Itokawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Names of major <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">surface features</a> were proposed by Hayabusa scientists and accepted by the Working Group for Planetary System Nomenclature of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a>.
