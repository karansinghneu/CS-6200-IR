<b>night sky</b>, usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, refers to the <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">nighttime</a> appearance of <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">celestial objects</a> like <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, which are visible in a clear <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> between <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is below the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>.\n</p><p>Natural light sources in a night sky include <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">moonlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starlight" title="Starlight">starlight</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Airglow" title="Airglow">airglow</a>, depending on location and timing. <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">Aurorae</a> light up the skies above the <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">polar circles</a>. Occasionally, a large <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejection</a> from the Sun or simply high levels of <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> may extend the phenomenon toward the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Northern_Lights_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Northern_Lights-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The night sky and studies of it have a historical place in both ancient and modern cultures. In the past, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmers</a> have used the status of the night sky as a <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a> to determine when to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">plant crops</a>. Many cultures have drawn <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a> between <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> in the sky, using them in association with <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a> about their <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deities</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_astrology" title="History of astrology">anciently developed</a> belief of <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a> is generally based on the belief that relationships between heavenly bodies influence or convey information about events on Earth. The <i>scientific</i> study of celestial objects visible at night takes place in the science of <a href="/wiki/Observational_astronomy" title="Observational astronomy">observational astronomy</a>.\n</p><p>The visibility of celestial objects in the night sky is affected by <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>. The presence of the Moon in the night sky has historically hindered astronomical observation by increasing the amount of <a href="/wiki/Sky_brightness" title="Sky brightness">ambient brightness</a>. With the advent of artificial light sources, however, light pollution has been a growing problem for viewing the night sky. <a href="/wiki/Optical_filter" title="Optical filter">Optical filters</a> and modifications to light fixtures can help to alleviate this problem, but for optimal views, both professional and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">amateur astronomers</a> seek locations far from <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> <a href="/wiki/Skyglow" title="Skyglow">skyglow</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" During daytime when the sun is above the horizon direct scattering of sunlight (<a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>) is the overwhelmingly dominant source of light. In twilight, the period of time between sunset and sunrise, the situation is more complicated and a further differentiation is required. Twilight is divided in three segments according to how far the sun is below the horizon in segments of 6&#176;.\n</p><p>After sunset the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_twilight" title="Civil twilight">civil twilight</a> sets in, and ends when the sun drops more than 6&#176; below the horizon. This is followed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_twilight" title="Nautical twilight">nautical twilight</a>, when the sun reaches heights of -6&#176; and -12&#176;, after which comes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_twilight" title="Astronomical twilight">astronomical twilight</a> defined as the period from -12&#176; to -18&#176;. When the sun drops more than 18&#176; below the horizon the sky generally attains its minimum brightness.\n</p><p>Several sources can be identified as the source of the intrinsic brightness of the sky, namely <a href="/wiki/Airglow" title="Airglow">airglow</a>,  indirect scattering of sunlight, scattering of starlight, and artificial <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light title="Noctcaelador">noctcaelador</a> tackles the aesthetic perception of the night sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Depending on local sky cloud cover, pollution, humidity, and light pollution levels, the stars visible to the unaided naked eye appear as hundreds, thousands or tens of thousands of white pinpoints of light in an otherwise near black sky together with some faint nebulae or clouds of light .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  In ancient times the stars were often assumed to be equidistant on a dome above the earth because they are much too far away for <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">stereopsis</a> to offer any depth cues.  Visible stars range in color from blue (hot) to red (cold), but with such small points of faint light, most look white because they stimulate the <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rod cells</a> without triggering the <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cone cells</a>.  If it is particularly dark and a particularly faint celestial object is of interest, <a href="/wiki/Averted_vision" title="Averted vision">averted vision</a> may be helpful.\n</p><p>The stars of the night sky cannot be counted unaided because they are so numerous and there is no way to track which have been counted and which have not.  Further complicating the count, fainter stars may appear and disappear depending on exactly where the observer is looking.  The result is an impression of an extraordinarily vast star field.\n</p><p>Because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stargazing" title="Stargazing">stargazing</a> is best done from a dark place away from city lights, dark <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_(eye)" title="Adaptation (eye)">adaptation</a> is important to achieve and maintain.  It takes several minutes for eyes to adjust to the darkness necessary for seeing the most stars, and surroundings on the ground are hard to discern.  A red <a href="/wiki/Flashlight" title="Flashlight">flashlight</a> (torch) can be used to illuminate star charts, telescope parts, and the like without undoing the dark adaptation. Constellations are prominent because their stars tend to be brighter than other nearby stars in the sky.  Different cultures have created different groupings of constellations based on differing interpretations of the more-or-less random patterns of dots in the sky.  Constellations were identified without regard to distance to each star, but instead as if they were all dots on a dome.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a> is among the most prominent and recognizable constellations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper" title="Big Dipper">Big Dipper</a> (which has a wide variety of other names) is helpful for navigation in the northern hemisphere because it points to <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">Polaris</a>, the north star.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pole_star" title="Pole star">pole stars</a> are special because they are approximately in line with the Earth\'s axis of rotation so they appear to stay in one place while the other stars rotate around them through the course of a night (or a year).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">Planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Night_sky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">Planets</a>, named for the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word for "wanderer," process through the star field a little each day, executing loops with time scales dependent on the length of the planet\'s year or orbital period around solar system.  Planets, to the naked eye, appear as points of light in the sky with variable brightness. Planets shine due to sunlight reflecting or scattering from the planets\' surface or atmosphere. Thus the relative sun-planet-earth positions determine the planet\'s brightness. With a telescope or good binoculars, the planets appear as discs demonstrating finite size, and it is possible to observe orbiting moons which cast shadows onto the host planet\'s surface. <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> is the most prominent planet, often called the "morning star" or "evening star" because it is brighter than the stars and often the only "star" visible near sunrise or sunset, depending on its location in its orbit. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> are also visible to the naked eye.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon">The Moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Night_sky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is a grey disc in the sky with cratering visible to the naked eye.  It spans, depending on its exact location, 29-33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">arcminutes</a> - which is about the size of a thumbnail at arm\'s length, and is readily identified.  Over 28 days, the moon goes through a full cycle of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phases</a>.  People can generally identify phases within a few days by looking at the moon.  Unlike stars and most planets, the light reflected from the moon is bright enough to be seen during the day. (Venus can sometimes be seen even after sunrise.)\n</p><p>Some of the most spectacular moons come during the full moon phase near sunset or sunrise.  The moon on the horizon benefits from the <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">moon illusion</a> which makes it appear larger.  The light reflected from the moon traveling through the atmosphere also colors the moon orange and/or red.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comets">Comets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Night_sky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">Comets</a> come to the night sky only rarely.  Comets are illuminated by the sun, and their tails extend away from the sun.  A comet with visible tail is quite unusual - a <a href="/wiki/Great_comet" title="Great comet">great comet</a> appears about once a decade.  They tend to be  visible only shortly before sunrise or after sunset because those are the times they are close enough to the sun to show a tail.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clouds">Clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Night_sky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">Clouds</a> obscure the view of other objects in the sky, though varying thicknesses of cloudcover have differing effects.  A very thin <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus cloud</a> in front of the moon might produce a rainbow-colored ring around the moon.  Stars and planets are too small or dim to take on this effect, and are instead only dimmed (often to the point of invisibility).  Thicker cloudcover obscures celestial objects entirely, making the sky black or reflecting city lights back down.  Clouds are often close enough to afford some depth perception, though they are hard to see without moonlight or light pollution.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_objects">Other objects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Night_sky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other objects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:auto; max-width:508px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deathvalleysky_nps_big.jpg" title="The Milky Way can be seen as a very large streak or arc across the night sky if the visibility conditions are good enough. href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>On clear dark nights in unpolluted areas, when the moon is thin or below the horizon, the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, a band of what looks like white dust, can be seen.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_Clouds" title="Magellanic Clouds">Magellanic Clouds</a> of the southern sky are easily mistaken to be Earth-based clouds (hence the name) but are in fact collections of stars found outside the Milky Way known as <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy" title="Dwarf galaxy">dwarf galaxies</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">Zodiacal light</a> is a glow that appears near the points where the sun rises and sets, and is caused by sunlight interacting with <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud" title="Interplanetary dust cloud">interplanetary dust</a>.\n</p><p>Shortly after sunset and before sunrise, artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> often look like stars&#8212;similar in brightness and size&#8212;but move relatively quickly. Those that fly in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> cross the sky in a couple of minutes. Some satellites, including <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a>, appear to blink or have a periodic fluctuation in brightness because they are rotating. <a href="/wiki/Satellite_flare" title="Satellite flare">Satellite flares</a> can appear brighter than <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, with notable examples including the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS) and <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium satellite constellation">Iridium Satellites</a>.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">Meteors</a> (commonly known as shooting stars) streak across the sky very infrequently. During a <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor shower</a>, they may average one a minute at irregular intervals, but otherwise their appearance is a random surprise. The occasional meteor will make a bright, fleeting streak across the sky, and they can be very bright in comparison to the night sky.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">Aircraft</a> are also visible at night, distinguishable at a distance from other objects because their lights blink.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
