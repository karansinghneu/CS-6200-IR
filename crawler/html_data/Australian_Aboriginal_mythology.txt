mythology</b> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Dreamtime" title="Dreamtime">Dreamtime</a> or Dreaming stories, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songlines" title="Songlines">songlines</a>, or Aboriginal <a href="/wiki/Oral_literature" title="Oral literature">oral literature</a>) are the stories <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">traditionally performed</a> by <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Aboriginal peoples</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> within each of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_group" title="Language group">language groups</a> across Australia.\n</p><p>All such <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myths</a> variously "tell significant truths within each Aboriginal group\'s local <a href="/wiki/Cultural_landscape" title="Cultural landscape">landscape</a>. They effectively layer the whole of the Australian continent\'s <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> with cultural nuance and deeper meaning, and empower selected audiences with the accumulated wisdom and knowledge of Australian Aboriginal ancestors back to <a href="/wiki/Time_immemorial" title="Time immemorial">time immemorial</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>David Horton\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_of_Aboriginal_Australia" title="Encyclopaedia of Aboriginal Australia">Encyclopaedia of Aboriginal Australia</a></i> contains an article on Aboriginal mythology observing:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BERN001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BERN001-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\n"A mythic map of Australia would show thousands of characters, varying in their importance, but all in some way connected with the land. Some emerged at their specific sites and stayed spiritually in that vicinity. Others came from somewhere else and went somewhere else."</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"Many were shape changing, transformed from or into human beings or natural species, or into natural features such as rocks but all left something of their spiritual essence at the places noted in their stories."</p></blockquote><p>Australian Aboriginal mythologies have been characterized as "at one and the same time fragments of a <a href="/wiki/Catechism" title="Catechism">catechism</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgical</a> manual, a history of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> textbook, and to a much smaller extent a manual of <a href="/wiki/Cosmography" title="Cosmography">cosmography</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p><br/>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An Australian <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R._M._W._Dixon" title="R. M. W. Dixon">R. M. W. Dixon</a>, recording Aboriginal myths in their original languages, encountered coincidences between some of the landscape details being told about within various myths, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_science" title="Hard science">scientific</a> discoveries being made about the same landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Atherton_Tableland" title="Atherton Tableland">Atherton Tableland</a>, myths tell of the origins of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eacham" title="Lake Eacham">Lake Eacham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Barrine" title="Lake Barrine">Lake Barrine</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Euramo" title="Lake Euramo">Lake Euramo</a>. Geological research dated the formative volcanic explosions described by Aboriginal myth tellers as having occurred more than 10,000 years ago. <a href="/wiki/Paleobotany" title="Paleobotany">Pollen fossil</a> sampling from the silt which had settled to the bottom of the craters confirmed the Aboriginal myth-tellers\' story. When the craters were formed, <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> forests dominated rather than the current wet tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forests" title="Rain forests">rain forests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dixon,_R._M._W._1996_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dixon,_R._M._W._1996-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Euramo" title="Lake Euramo">Lake Euramo</a> for an excerpt of the original myth, translated.)\n</p><p>Dixon observed from the evidence available that Aboriginal myths regarding the origin of the Crater Lakes might be dated as accurate back to 10,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dixon,_R._M._W._1996_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dixon,_R._M._W._1996-6">[6]</a></sup> Further investigation of the material by the Australian Heritage Commission led to the Crater Lakes myth being listed nationally on the <a href="/wiki/Register_of_the_National_Estate" title="Register of the National Estate">Register of the National Estate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and included within Australia\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage" title="World Heritage">World Heritage</a> nomination of the <a href="/wiki/Wet_Tropics_of_Queensland" title="Wet Tropics of Queensland">wet tropical forests</a>, as an "unparalleled human record of events dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> era."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since then, Dixon has assembled a number of similar examples of Australian Aboriginal myths that accurately describe landscapes of an ancient past. He particularly noted the numerous myths telling of previous sea levels, including:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Port_Phillip" title="Port Phillip">Port Phillip</a> myth (recorded as told to Robert Russell in 1850), describing Port Phillip Bay as once dry land, and the course of the <a href="/wiki/Yarra_River" title="Yarra River">Yarra River</a> being once different, following what was then Carrum Carrum swamp.</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> coastline myth (told to Dixon) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarrabah" title="Yarrabah">Yarrabah</a>, just south of <a href="/wiki/Cairns" title="Cairns">Cairns</a>, telling of a past coastline (since flooded) which stood at the edge of the current Great Barrier Reef, and naming places now completely submerged after the forest types and trees that once grew there.</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> myths (recorded by J. W. Gregory in 1906), telling of the deserts of <a href="/wiki/Central_Australia" title="Central Australia">Central Australia</a> as once having been fertile, well-watered plains, and the deserts around present Lake Eyre having been one continuous garden. This oral story matches geologists\' understanding that there was a wet phase to the early <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> when the lake would have had permanent water.</li></ul>\n<p>Other volcanic eruptions in Australia may also be recorded in Aboriginal myths, including <a href="/wiki/Mount_Gambier,_South_Australia" title="Mount Gambier, South Australia">Mount Gambier</a> in South Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and Kinrara in northern Queensland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aboriginal_mythology:_whole_of_Australia">Aboriginal mythology: whole of Australia</span><span Australia</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversity_across_a_continent">Diversity across a continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diversity across a continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are 900 distinct Aboriginal groups across Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> each distinguished by unique names usually identifying particular <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a>, or distinctive <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">speech mannerisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Each language was used for original myths, from which the distinctive words and names of individual myths derive.\n</p><p>With so many distinct Aboriginal groups, languages, beliefs and practices, scholars cannot attempt to characterise, under a single heading, the full range and diversity of all myths being variously and continuously told, developed, elaborated, performed, and experienced by group members across the entire continent. (See external link<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> for one indicative spatial map of Australian Aboriginal groups, and see <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20080316044231/http://www.samuseum.sa.gov.au/page/default.asp?site=2&amp;page=TIN_Tribal&amp;level=1" rel="nofollow">here</a> for an earlier <a href="/wiki/Norman_Tindale" title="Norman Tindale">Tindale</a> map of Aboriginal groups.)\n</p><p><i>The Encyclopaedia of Aboriginal Australia</i> nevertheless observes: "One intriguing feature [of Aboriginal Australian mythology] is the mixture of diversity and similarity in myths across the entire continent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BERN001_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BERN001-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_education_about_Aboriginal_perspectives">Public education about Aboriginal perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public education about Aboriginal perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reconciliation_Australia" title="Reconciliation Australia">Council for Aboriginal Reconciliation</a>\'s booklet, <i>Understanding Country</i>, formally seeks to introduce non-indigenous Australians to Aboriginal perspectives on the environment. It makes the following generalisation about Aboriginal myths and mythology:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\n"...they generally describe the journeys of ancestral beings, often giant animals or people, over what began as a featureless domain. Mountains, rivers, waterholes, animal and plant species, and other natural and cultural resources came into being as a result of events which took place during these Dreamtime journeys. Their existence in present-day landscapes is seen by many indigenous peoples as confirmation of their creation beliefs..."</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"The routes taken by the Creator Beings in their Dreamtime journeys across land and sea... link many <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_sacred_sites" title="Australian Aboriginal sacred sites">sacred sites</a> together in a web of Dreamtime tracks criss-crossing the country. Dreaming tracks can run for hundreds, even thousands of kilometres, from desert to the coast [and] may be shared by peoples in countries through which the tracks pass..."</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="An_anthropological_generalisation">An anthropological generalisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: An anthropological generalisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Australian <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropologists</a> willing to generalise suggest Aboriginal myths still being performed across Australia by Aboriginal peoples serve an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Functionalism_(sociology)" title="Functionalism (sociology)">social function</a> amongst their intended audiences: justifying the received ordering of their daily lives;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> helping shape peoples\' ideas; and assisting to influence others\' behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In addition, such performance often continuously incorporates and "mythologises" historical events in the service of these social purposes in an otherwise rapidly changing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_world" title="Modern world">modern world</a>. As R.M. W. Dixon writes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\n"It is always integral and common... that the Law (<a href="/wiki/Dreamtime#The_Dreaming.2C_Tribal_Law_.26_Songlines" title="Dreamtime">Aboriginal law</a>) is something derived from ancestral peoples or Dreamings and is passed down the generations in a continuous line. While... entitlements of particular human beings may come and go, the underlying relationships between foundational Dreamings and certain landscapes are theoretically eternal&#160;... the entitlements of people to places are usually regarded strongest when those people enjoy a relationship of identity with one or more Dreamings of that place. This is an identity of spirit, a <a href="/wiki/Consubstantiality" title="Consubstantiality">consubstantiality</a>, rather than a matter of mere belief...: the Dreaming pre-exists and persists, while its human incarnations are temporary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="An_Aboriginal_generalization">An Aboriginal generalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: An Aboriginal generalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Aboriginal specialists willing to generalise believe all Aboriginal myths across Australia, in combination, represent a kind of unwritten (<a href="/wiki/Orality" title="Orality">oral</a>) library within which Aboriginal peoples learn about the world and perceive a peculiarly Aboriginal \'reality\' dictated by concepts and values vastly different from those of <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">western societies</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"Aboriginal people learned from their stories that a society must not be human-centred but rather land centred, otherwise they forget their source and purpose&#160;... humans are prone to exploitative behaviour if not constantly reminded they are interconnected with the rest of creation, that they as individuals are only temporal in time, and past and future generations must be included in their perception of their purpose in life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_C_1995_p_71_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morris_C_1995_p_71-22">[22]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"People come and go but the Land, and stories about the Land, stay. This is a wisdom that takes lifetimes of listening, observing and experiencing&#160;... There is a deep understanding of human nature and the environment... sites hold \'feelings\' which cannot be described in physical terms... subtle feelings that resonate through the bodies of these people... It is only when talking and being with these people that these \'feelings\' can truly be appreciated. This is... the intangible reality of these people..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morris_C_1995_p_71_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morris_C_1995_p_71-22">[22]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-Australian_mythology">Pan-Australian mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainbow_Serpent">Rainbow Serpent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Serpent</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Australian-Carpet-Python.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Australian-Carpet-Python.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpet_python" title="Carpet python">carpet python</a>, one of the forms the \'Rainbow Serpent\' character may take in \'Rainbow Serpent\' myths</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1926 a British anthropologist specialising in Australian Aboriginal <a href="/wiki/Ethnology" title="Ethnology">ethnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnography</a>, Professor <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Radcliffe-Brown" title="Alfred Radcliffe-Brown">Alfred Radcliffe-Brown</a>, noted many Aboriginal groups widely distributed across the Australian continent all appeared to share variations of a single (common) myth telling of an unusually powerful, often creative, often dangerous snake or serpent of sometimes enormous size closely associated with the rainbows, rain, rivers, and deep waterholes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RADC1926_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-RADC1926-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Radcliffe-Brown coined the term \'Rainbow Serpent\' to describe what he identified to be a common, recurring myth. Working in the field in various places on the Australian continent, he noted the key character of this myth (the \'Rainbow Serpent\') is variously named:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RADC1926_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-RADC1926-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><i>Kanmare</i> (<a href="/wiki/Boulia,_Queensland" title="Boulia, Queensland">Boulia, Queensland</a>); <i>Tulloun</i>: (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Isa,_Queensland" title="Mount Isa, Queensland">Mount Isa, Queensland</a>); <i>Andrenjinyi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pennefather_River" title="Pennefather River">Pennefather River</a>, Queensland), <i>Takkan</i> (<a href="/wiki/Maryborough,_Queensland" title="Maryborough, Queensland">Maryborough, Queensland</a>); <i>Targan</i> (<a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, Queensland); <i>Kurreah</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_Hill,_New_South_Wales" title="Broken Hill, New South Wales">Broken Hill, New South Wales</a>);<i>Wawi</i> (<a href="/wiki/Riverina" title="Riverina">Riverina</a>, New South Wales), <i>Neitee &amp; Yeutta</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilcannia,_New_South_Wales" title="Wilcannia, New South Wales">Wilcannia, New South Wales</a>), <i>Myndie</i> (<a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, Victoria); <i>Bunyip</i> (Western <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)#Geology_and_geography" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>); <i><a href="/wiki/The_Arkaroo" title="The Arkaroo">Arkaroo</a></i> (Flinders Ranges, South Australia); <i><a href="/wiki/Wagyl" title="Wagyl">Wogal</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>, Western Australia); <i>Wanamangura</i> (<a href="/wiki/Shire_of_Laverton" title="Shire of Laverton">Laverton, Western Australia</a>); <i>Kajura</i> (<a href="/wiki/Shire_of_Carnarvon" title="Shire of Carnarvon">Carnarvon, Western Australia</a>); <i>Numereji</i> (<a href="/wiki/Kakadu_National_Park" title="Kakadu National Park">Kakadu, Northern Territory</a>).</dd></dl>\n<p>This \'Rainbow Serpent\' is generally and variously identified by those who tell \'Rainbow Serpent\' myths, as a snake of some enormous size often living within the deepest waterholes of many of Australia\'s waterways; descended from that larger being visible as a dark streak in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, it reveals itself to people in this world as a rainbow as it moves through water and rain, shaping landscapes, naming and singing of places, swallowing and sometimes drowning people; strengthening the knowledgeable with rainmaking and healing powers; blighting others with sores, weakness, illness, and death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RADC1926_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-RADC1926-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even Australia\'s \'<a href="/wiki/Bunyip" title="Bunyip">Bunyip</a>\' was identified as a \'Rainbow Serpent\' myth of the above kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The term coined by Radcliffe-Brown is now commonly used and familiar to broader Australian and international audiences, as it is increasingly used by government agencies, museums, art galleries, Aboriginal organisations and the media to refer to the pan-Australian Aboriginal myth specifically, and as a shorthand allusion to Australian Aboriginal mythology generally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Captain_Cook">Captain Cook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Captain Cook">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" decoding="async" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG/150px-James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG/225px-James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG/300px-James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:James_Cook_statue_closeup_574.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Captain James Cook at <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_Arch" title="Admiralty Arch">Admiralty Arch</a>, London</div></div></div>\n<p>A number of linguists, anthropologists and others have formally documented another common Aboriginal myth occurring across Australia. Predecessors of the myth tellers encounter a mythical, exotic (most often English) character who arrives from the sea, bringing western <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a>, either offering gifts to the performer\'s predecessors or bringing great harm upon the performer\'s predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This key mythical character is most often named \'Captain Cook\', this being a \'mythical\' character shared with the broader Australian community, who also attribute <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> with playing a key role in colonising Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MADD001_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-MADD001-27">[27]</a></sup> The Aboriginal \'Captain Cook\' is attributed with bringing British rule to Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> but his arrival is not celebrated. More often within the Aboriginal telling, he proves to be a <a href="/wiki/Villain" title="Villain">villain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MADD001_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-MADD001-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The many Aboriginal versions of this \'Captain Cook\' are rarely oral recollections of encounters with the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> who first navigated and mapped Australia\'s east coast on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HM_Bark_Endeavour" title="HM Bark Endeavour">HM Bark <i>Endeavour</i></a> in 1770. <a href="/wiki/Guugu_Yimithirr_people" title="Guugu Yimithirr people">Guugu Yimidhirr</a> predecessors, along the <a href="/wiki/Endeavour_River" title="Endeavour River">Endeavour River</a>, did encounter James Cook during a 7-week period beached at the site of the present town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooktown" title="Cooktown">Cooktown</a> while the Endeavour was being repaired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> From this time the Guugu Yimidhirr did receive present-day names for places occurring in their local landscape; and the Guugu Yimmidhir may recollect this encounter.\n</p><p>The pan-Australian Captain Cook myth, however, tells of a generic, largely symbolic British character who arrives from across the oceans sometime after the Aboriginal world was formed and the original social order founded. This Captain Cook is a harbinger of dramatic transformations in the social order, bringing change and a different social order, into which present-day audiences have been born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_mythology#An_Anthropological_generalisation" title="Australian Aboriginal mythology">above</a> regarding this social function played by Aboriginal myths)\n</p><p>In 1988 Australian anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Maddock" title="Kenneth Maddock">Kenneth Maddock</a> assembled several versions of this \'Captain Cook\' myth as recorded from a number of Aboriginal groups around Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Included in his assemblage are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batemans_Bay,_New_South_Wales" title="Batemans Bay, New South Wales">Batemans Bay, New South Wales</a>:</b> Percy Mumbulla told of Captain Cook\'s arriving on a large ship which anchored at <a href="/wiki/Snapper_Island_(New_South_Wales)" title="Snapper Island (New South Wales)">Snapper Island</a>, from which he disembarked to give the myth-teller\'s predecessors clothes (to wear) and hard biscuits (to eat). Then he returned to his ship and sailed away. Mumbulla told how his predecessors rejected Captain Cook\'s gifts, throwing them into the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cardwell,_Queensland" title="Cardwell, Queensland">Cardwell, Queensland</a>:</b> Chloe Grant and Rosie Runaway told of how Captain Cook and his group seemed to stand up out of the sea with the white skin of ancestral spirits, returning to their descendants. Captain Cook arrived first offering a pipe and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> to smoke (which was dismissed as a \'burning thing... stuck in his mouth\'), then boiling a billy of <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> (which was dismissed as <a href="/wiki/Scalding" title="Scalding">scalding</a> \'dirty water\'), next baking <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> on the coals (which was rejected as smelling \'stale\' and thrown away untasted), finally boiling <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a> (which smelled well, and tasted okay, once the salty skin was wiped off). Captain Cook and group then left, sailing away to the north, leaving Chloe Grant and Rosie Runaway\'s predecessors beating the ground with their fists, fearfully sorry to see the spirits of their ancestors depart in this way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>South-eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>:</b> Rolly Gilbert told of how Captain Cook and others sailed the oceans in a boat, and decided to come to see Australia. There he encountered a couple of Rolly\'s predecessors whom he first intended to shoot, but instead tricked them into revealing the local population\'s main camping area, after which they:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<blockquote><p>"set up the people [cattle industry] to go down the countryside and shoot people down, just like animal, they left them lying there for the hawks and crows... So a lot of old people and young people were struck by the head with the end of a gun and left there. They wanted to get the people wiped out because Europeans in Queensland had to run their stock: horses and cattle."</p></blockquote>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Victoria_River_(Northern_Territory)" title="Victoria River (Northern Territory)">Victoria River (Northern Territory)</a>:</b> it is told in a Captain Cook <a href="/wiki/Saga" title="Saga">saga</a> that Captain Cook sailed from London to <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> to acquire land. Admiring the country, he landed <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">bullocks</a> and men with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firearms" title="Firearms">firearms</a>, following which local Aboriginal peoples in the Sydney area were <a href="/wiki/List_of_events_named_massacres" title="List of events named massacres">massacred</a>. Captain Cook made his way to <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin</a>, where he sent armed horsemen to hunt down the Aborigines in the Victoria River country, founding the city of Darwin and giving police plus cattle station managers orders on how to treat Aborigines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>\n<li><b> <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_(Western_Australia)" title="Kimberley (Western Australia)">Kimberley (Western Australia)</a>:</b> Numerous Aboriginal myth-tellers say that Captain Cook is a European culture hero who landed in Australia. Using gunpowder, he set a precedent for the treatment of Aboriginal peoples throughout Australia, including the Kimberley. On returning to his home, he claimed he had not seen any Aboriginal peoples, and advised that the country was a vast and empty land which settlers could come and claim for themselves. In this myth, Captain Cook introduced \'Cook\'s Law\', upon which the settlers rely. The Aboriginals note, however, that this is a recent, unjust and false law compared to Aboriginal law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_death">Views on death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Views on death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Death in Aboriginal religion in some aspects may seem like it has some Western traditions in regard to having a ceremony and mourning the loss of the person that is deceased. However, that is really the only thing that this religion has in common with Western religion as far as death is concerned. "For Aboriginal people when a person dies some form of the persons spirit and also their bones go back to the country they were born in".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-37">[37]</a></sup> "Aborigine people believe that they share their being with their country and all that is within it". "So when a person dies their country suffers, trees die and become scarred because it is believed that came into being because of the deceased person".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When an Aborigine person dies the families have death ceremonies called the "Sorry Business". "During this time the entire community and family mourns the loss of the person for days". "They are expected to cry together and share grief as a community".  If someone was out of town and arrives after they have had a ceremony for the deceased, the entire community stops what they are doing and  goes and tells them and mourns with them. "The family of the deceased all stay in one room and mourn for their loved one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>"Naming a person after they have died is not allowed in the Aborigine religion". "To say someone\'s name after they die would be to disturb their spirit ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-38">[38]</a></sup> Photos of the deceased are not allowed for fear of disturbing the spirit also. Many Aborigine families will not have any photographs of their loved ones after they die. "A smoking ceremony is also conducted when someone dies". "The community uses smoke on the  belongings and also the residence of the deceased to help release the spirit". "Identifying the cause of death is determined by elders who hold the cultural authority to do so, and the causes in question are usually of a spiritual nature". "The ceremonies are likened to an autopsy of Western practice".\n</p><p>Ceremonies and mourning periods last days, weeks and even sometimes months depending upon the social status of the deceased person.  It is culturally inappropriate for a non-Aboriginal person to contact and inform the next of kin of a person&#8217;s passing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-38">[38]</a></sup> When someone passes away, the family of the deceased move out of their house and another family then moves in. Some families will move to "sorry camps" which are usually further away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-38">[38]</a></sup>  \nMourning includes the recital of symbolic chants, the singing of songs, dance, body paint, and cuts on the bodies of the mourners. The body is placed on a raised platform  for several months, covered in native plants. Sometimes a cave or a tree is used instead. "When nothing but bones are left, family and friends will scatter them in a variety of ways. They sometimes wrap the bones in a hand-knitted fabric and place them in a cave for eventual disintegration or place them in a naturally hollowed out log".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_38-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Aboriginals believe in a place called the "Land of the Dead". This place was also commonly known as the  "sky-world", which is really just the sky. As long as certain rituals were carried out during their life and at the time of their death, the deceased is allowed to enter The Land of the Dead in the "Sky World".  The spirit of the dead is also a part of different lands and sites and then those areas become <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_sacred_sites" title="Australian Aboriginal sacred sites">sacred sites</a>. This explains why the Aboriginals are very protective of sites they call sacred. The Aboriginals believe that life is a never-ending cycle. You are born, you die, you are born again as an animal, human or other life form .\n</p><p>The rituals that are performed enable the aborigine to return to the womb of all time which is another word for "Dream Time". It allows the spirit to be  connected once more to all nature, to all their ancestors, and to their own personal meaning and place within the scheme of things. "The Dreamtime is a return to the real existence for the aborigine". "Life in time is simply a passing phase &#8211; a gap in eternity". It has a beginning and it has an end. "The experience of Dreamtime, whether through ritual or from dreams, flowed through into the life in time in practical ways". "The individual who enters the Dreamtime feels no separation between themselves and their ancestors". "The strengths and resources of the timeless enter into what is needed in the life of the present". "The future is less uncertain because the individual feels their life as a continuum linking past and future in unbroken connection". Through Dreamtime the limitations of time and space are overcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-39">[39]</a></sup> \nFor the Aborigine people dead relatives are very much a part of continuing life. It is believed that in dreams dead relatives communicate their presence." At times they may bring healing if the dreamer is in pain". "Death is seen as part of a cycle of life in which one emerges from Dreamtime through birth, and eventually returns to the timeless, only to emerge again. It is also a common belief that a person leaves their body during sleep, and temporarily enters the Dreamtime".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p><br/>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Group-specific_mythology">Group-specific mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Group-specific mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Murrinh-Patha_people">Murrinh-Patha people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Murrinh-Patha people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table align="right">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td>\n<div class="floatnone noresize" style="position: relative; width: 250px;"><div style="position: absolute; left: 100px; top: 25px; padding: 0;"><img alt="" data-file-height="8" data-file-width="8" decoding="async" height="8" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Locator_Dot.png" title="Wadeye" width="8"/></div><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Australia_Locator_Blank.svg" title="Australia"><img alt="" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="290" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Australia_Locator_Blank.svg/250px-Australia_Locator_Blank.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Australia_Locator_Blank.svg/375px-Australia_Locator_Blank.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Australia_Locator_Blank.svg/500px-Australia_Locator_Blank.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>\n<p>Murrinh-Patha people\'s country<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deBr001_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-deBr001-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Murrinh-Patha" title="Murrinh-Patha">Murrinh-Patha</a> people (whose country is the saltwater country immediately inland from the town of <a href="/wiki/Wadeye,_Northern_Territory" title="Wadeye, Northern Territory">Wadeye</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deBr001_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-deBr001-40">[40]</a></sup>) describe a <a href="/wiki/Dreamtime" title="Dreamtime">Dreamtime</a> in their myths which anthropologists believe is a religious belief equivalent to, though wholly different from, most of the world\'s other significant religious beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YENG001_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-YENG001-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In particular, scholars suggest the Murrinh-Patha have a oneness of thought, belief, and expression unequalled within <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, as they see all aspects of their lives, thoughts and culture as under the continuing influence of their Dreaming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YENG001_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-YENG001-41">[41]</a></sup> Within this Aboriginal religion, no distinction is drawn between things spiritual/ideal/mental and things material; nor is any distinction drawn between things sacred and things profane: rather all life is \'sacred\', all conduct has \'moral\' implication, and all life\'s meaning arises out of this eternal, everpresent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dreaming_(story)" title="Dreaming (story)">Dreaming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YENG001_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-YENG001-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"In fact, the isomorphic fit between the natural and supernatural means that all nature is coded and charged by the <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacred</a>, while the sacred is everywhere within the physical landscape. Myths and mythic tracks cross over.. thousands of miles, and every particular form and feature of the terrain has a well-developed \'story\' behind it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Animating and sustaining this Murrinh-patha mythology is an underlying philosophy of life that has been characterised by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Stanner" title="Bill Stanner">Stanner</a> as a belief that life is "...&#160;a joyous thing with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maggots" title="Maggots">maggots</a> at its centre."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YENG001_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-YENG001-41">[41]</a></sup> Life is good and benevolent, but throughout life\'s journey, there are numerous painful sufferings that each individual must come to understand and endure as he grows. This is the underlying message repeatedly being told within the Murrinh-patha myths. It is this philosophy that gives Murrinh-patha people motive and meaning in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YENG001_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-YENG001-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The following Murrinh-patha myth, for instance, is performed in Murrinh-patha ceremonies to initiate young men into adulthood.  \n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"A woman, <i>Mutjinga</i> (the \'Old Woman\'), was in charge of young children, but instead of watching out for them during their parents\' absence, she swallowed them and tried to escape as a giant snake. The people followed her, spearing her and removing the undigested children from the body."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Within the myth and in its performance, young, unadorned children must first be swallowed by an ancestral being (who transforms into a <a href="#Rainbow_Serpent">giant snake</a>), then regurgitated before being accepted as young adults with all the rights and privileges of young adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pintupi_people">Pintupi people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pintupi people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table align="right">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td>\n<div class="floatnone noresize" style="position: relative; width: 250px;"><div style="position: absolute; Desert" width="8"/></div><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Australia_Locator_Blank.svg" title="Australia"><img alt="" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="290" decoding="async" height="226" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Australia_Locator_Blank.svg/500px-Australia_Locator_Blank.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>\n<p>Pintupi people\'s country\n</p>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Scholars of the <a href="/wiki/Pintupi" title="Pintupi">Pintupi</a> peoples (from within Australia\'s <a href="/wiki/Gibson_Desert" title="Gibson Desert">Gibson Desert</a> region) believe they have a predominantly \'mythic\' form of <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> within which events occur and are explained by the preordained social structures and orders told of, sung about, and performed within their superhuman mythology, rather than by reference to the possible accumulated political actions, decisions and influences of local individuals (i.e. this understanding effectively \'erases\' history).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"The Dreaming.. provides a <a href="/wiki/Moral_authority" title="Moral authority">moral authority</a> lying outside the individual will and outside human creation.. although the Dreaming as an ordering of the cosmos is presumably a product of historical events, such an origin is denied."</p></blockquote>\n<blockquote><p>"These human creations are objectified &#8211; thrust out &#8211; into principles or precedents for the immediate world.. Consequently, current action is not understood as the result of human alliances, creations, and choices, but is seen as imposed by an embracing, cosmic order."</p></blockquote>\n<p>Within this Pintupi <a href="/wiki/World_view" title="World view">world view</a>, three long geographical tracks of named places dominate, being interrelated strings of significant places named and created by mythic characters on their routes through the Pintupi desert region during the Dreaming. It is a complex mythology of narratives, songs and ceremonies known to the Pintupi as <a href="/wiki/Tingari" title="Tingari"><i>Tingarri</i></a>. It is most completely told and performed by Pintupi peoples at larger gatherings within Pintupi country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cultural_landscape" title="Cultural landscape">Cultural landscape</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians#Belief_systems" title="Indigenous Australians">Indigenous Australians &#167;&#160;Belief systems</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian_literature" title="Indigenous Australian literature">Indigenous Australian literature</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibliography">Bibliography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_religion_and_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bibliography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Beckett, J.
