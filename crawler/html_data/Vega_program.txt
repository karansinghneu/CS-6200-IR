1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Vega-mission.jpg/440px-Vega-mission.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vega-mission.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vega mission description</div></div></div><p>The <b>Vega program</b> (Cyrillic: &#1042;&#1077;&#1043;&#1072;) was a series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venus_missions" title="Venus missions">Venus missions</a> that also took advantage of the appearance of comet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1P/Halley" title="1P/Halley">1P/Halley</a> in 1986. <i><a href="/wiki/Vega_1" title="Vega 1">Vega 1</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vega_2" title="Vega 2">Vega 2</a></i> were unmanned <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> launched in a cooperative effort among the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (who also provided the spacecraft and launch vehicle) and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> in December 1984. They had a two-part mission to investigate <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> and also flyby <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">Halley\'s Comet</a>.\n</p><p>The flyby of Halley\'s Comet had been a late mission change in the <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a> program following on from the cancellation of the American Halley mission in 1981. A later Venera mission was cancelled and the Venus part of the <i>Vega 1</i> mission was reduced. Because of this, the craft was designated VeGa, a contraction of <i>Venera</i> and <i>Gallei</i> (&#1042;&#1077;&#1085;&#1077;&#1088;&#1072; and &#1043;&#1072;&#1083;&#1083;&#1077;&#1103; respectively, the Russian words for "Venus" and "Halley"). The spacecraft design was based on the previous <a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venera_10" title="Venera 10">Venera 10</a> missions.\n</p><p>The two spacecraft were launched on December 15 and 21, 1984, respectively. With their redesignated dual missions, the Vega probes became part of the <a href="/wiki/Halley_Armada" title="Halley Armada">Halley Armada</a>, a group of space probes that studied Halley\'s Comet during its 1985/1986 perihelion.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" href="/wiki/Vega_1" title="Vega 1">Vega 1</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vega_2" title="Vega 2">Vega 2</a></i> were identical sister ships. The spacecraft was a development of the earlier <i><a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a></i> craft. They were designed by Babakin Space Center and constructed as 5VK by <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin" title="Lavochkin">Lavochkin</a> at <a href="/wiki/Khimki" title="Khimki">Khimki</a>. The craft was powered by twin large solar panels and instruments included an antenna dish, cameras, <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a>, infrared sounder, <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometers</a> (MISCHA), and plasma probes. The 4,920&#160;kg craft was launched by a <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton 8K82K rocket</a> from <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kazakh_SSR" title="Kazakh SSR">Kazakh SSR</a> (current <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>). Both <i>Vega 1</i> and <i>Vega 2</i> were three-axis stabilized spacecraft. The units were released some days before each arrived at Venus and entered the atmosphere without active inclination changes. Each contained a lander and a balloon explorer.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descent_craft">Descent craft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vega_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Descent craft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The landers were identical to that of the previous five <i><a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a></i> missions and were to study the atmosphere and surface, each had instruments to study temperature, pressure, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV" title="UV">UV</a> spectrometer, a water concentration meter, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas-liquid_chromatography" title="Gas-liquid chromatography">gas-phase chromatograph</a>, an <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometer" title="Mass spectrometer">mass spectrometer</a> and a surface sampling device.\n</p><p>The <i>Vega 1</i> lander\'s surface experiments were inadvertently activated at 20&#160;km from the surface by an especially hard wind jolt, and so failed to provide results. It landed at 7.5&#176;N, 177.7&#176;E.\n</p><p>The <i>Vega 2</i> lander touched down at 03:00:50 UT on June 15, 1985 at 8.5&#176; S, 164.5&#176; E, in eastern <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite_Terra" title="Aphrodite Terra">Aphrodite Terra</a>. The altitude of the touchdown site was 0.1&#160;km above the planetary mean radius. The measured pressure at the landing site was 91 atm and the temperature was 736&#160;K (463&#160;&#176;C). The surface sample was found to be an <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">anorthosite</a>-<a href="/wiki/Troctolite" title="Troctolite">troctolite</a>. The lander transmitted data from the surface for 56 minutes.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Payload">Payload</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Landis.</div></div></div>\n<p>The two balloon <a href="/wiki/Aerobot" title="Aerobot">aerobots</a> were designed to float  at 54&#160;km from the surface, in the most active layer of the Venusian cloud system. The instrument pack had enough battery power for sixty hours of operation and measured temperature, pressure, wind speed and aerosol density. The balloon envelopes were surfaced with <a href="/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene" title="Polytetrafluoroethylene">polytetrafluoroethylene</a> to resist attack by the corrosive atmosphere.   Both Vega-1 and Vega-2 balloons operated for more than 46 hrs from injection to the final transmission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The balloons were spherical superpressure types with a diameter of 3.54 metres (11.6&#160;ft) and filled with <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. A gondola assembly weighing 6.9 kilograms (15.2 pounds) and 1.3 meters (4.26&#160;ft) long was connected to the balloon envelope by a tether 13 metres (42.6&#160;ft) long. Total mass of the entire assembly was 21 kilograms (46 pounds).\n</p><p>The top section of the gondola assembly was capped by a conical antenna 37 centimetres (14.6&#160;inches) tall and 13 centimetres (5 <sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub> inches) wide at the base. Beneath the antenna was a module containing the radio transmitter and system control electronics. The lower section of the gondola assembly carried the instrument payload and batteries.\n</p><p>The instruments consisted of:\n</p>\n<ul><li>An arm carrying thin-film resistance thermometers and a <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">velocity anemometer</a>. The anemometer consisted of a free-spinning plastic propeller whose spin was measured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a>-photodetector optointerrupters.</li>\n<li>A module containing a <a href="/wiki/PIN_diode" title="PIN diode">PIN diode</a> <a href="/wiki/Photodetector" title="Photodetector">photodetector</a> to measure light levels and a vibrating <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> beam <a href="/wiki/Pressure_sensor" title="Pressure sensor">pressure sensor</a>.</li>\n<li>A package at the bottom carrying the batteries and a <a href="/wiki/Nephelometer" title="Nephelometer">nephelometer</a> to measure cloud density through light reflection.</li></ul>\n<p>The small low-power transmitter only allowed a data transmission rate of 2,048 bits/sec, though the system performed data compression to squeeze more information through the narrow bandwidth. Nonetheless, the sampling rate for most of the instruments was only once every 75 seconds. The balloons were tracked by two networks of 20 radio telescopes in total back on Earth: the Soviet network, coordinated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">Academy of Sciences of the Soviet Union</a> and the international network, coordinated by <i>Centre national d\'&#233;tudes spatiales</i> of France (<a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">CNES</a>).\n</p><p>The balloons were dropped onto the planet\'s darkside and deployed at an altitude of about 50 kilometres (31&#160;mi). They then floated upward a few kilometres to their equilibrium altitude. At this altitude, pressure and temperature conditions of Venus are similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, though the planet\'s winds moved at hurricane velocity and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> atmosphere is laced with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, along with smaller concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>.\n</p><p>The balloons moved swiftly across the night side of the planet into the light side, where their batteries finally ran down and contact was lost. Tracking indicated that the motion of the balloons included a surprising vertical component, revealing vertical motions of air masses that had not been detected by earlier probe missions.\n</p>\n<div logo.</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Halley_mission">The Halley mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vega_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Halley mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After their encounters, the Vega motherships were redirected by Venus\'s gravity to intercept <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Halley" title="Comet Halley">Comet Halley</a>.\n</p><p>Vega 1 made its closest approach on March 6, around 8,890&#160;km from the nucleus, and <i>Vega 2</i> made its closest approach on March 9 at 8,030&#160;km. The data intensive examination of the comet covered only the three hours around closest approach. They were intended to measure the physical parameters of the nucleus, such as dimensions, shape, temperature and surface properties, as well as to study the structure and dynamics of the <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a>, the gas composition close to the nucleus, the dust particles\' composition and mass distribution as functions of distance to the nucleus and the cometary-<a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> interaction.\n</p><p>In total Vega 1 and Vega 2 returned about 1,500 images of Comet Halley. Spacecraft operations were discontinued a few weeks after the Halley encounters.\n</p><p>The on-board TV system was created in international cooperation of the scientific and industrial facilities from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a>, Hungary, France and <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>. TV data were processed by international team, including scientists from Soviet Union, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, France, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The basic steps of data acquisition and preprocessing were performed in <a href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Research_Institute" title="Russian Space Research Institute">IKI</a> using an image processing computer system based on a <a href="/wiki/PDP-11" title="PDP-11">PDP-11/40</a> compatible host.\n</p><p><i>Vega 1</i> and <i>Vega 2</i> are currently in <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbits</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vega_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus_Orbiter" title="Pioneer Venus Orbiter">Pioneer Venus Orbiter</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_program" title="Venera program">Venera program</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vega_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Preston;  et al.
