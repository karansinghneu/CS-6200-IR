<i>Voyager</i>.</li></ul>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>heliosphere</b> is the vast, bubble-like <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Interplanetary_space" title="Outer space">region of space</a> which surrounds and is created by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. In plasma physics terms, this is the cavity formed by the Sun in the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. The "bubble" of the heliosphere is continuously "inflated" by <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> originating from the Sun, known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. Outside the heliosphere, this solar plasma gives way to the interstellar plasma permeating <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">our galaxy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">Radiation</a> levels inside and outside the heliosphere differ; in particular, the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">galactic cosmic rays</a> are less abundant inside the heliosphere, so that the planets inside (including <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>) are partly shielded from their impact. The word "heliosphere" is said to have been coined by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_J._Dessler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexander J. Dessler (page does not exist)">Alexander J. Dessler</a>, who is credited with first use of the word in scientific literature in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dessler-revgeophys_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dessler-revgeophys-1">[1]</a></sup> The scientific study of the heliosphere is <b><a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">heliophysics</a></b>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_climate" title="Space climate">space climate</a>.\n</p><p>Flowing unimpeded through the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> for billions of kilometres, the solar wind extends far beyond even the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiper_Belt" title="Kuiper Belt">region of Pluto</a>, until it encounters the <b>termination shock</b>, where its motion slows abruptly due to the outside <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of the interstellar medium. Beyond the shock lies the <b>heliosheath</b>, a broad transitional region between the inner heliosphere and the external environment. The outermost edge of the heliosphere is called the <b>heliopause</b>. The overall shape of the heliosphere resembles that of a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> - being approximately spherical on one side, with a long trailing tail opposite, known as the heliotail.\n</p><p>The two <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program"><i>Voyager</i> spacecraft</a> have explored the outer reaches of the heliosphere, passing through the termination shock and the heliosheath. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> announced in 2013 that <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> had encountered the heliopause on August 25, 2012, when the spacecraft measured a sudden increase in plasma <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of about forty times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager-historic_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-voyager-historic-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2018, NASA announced that <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> had traversed the heliopause on November 5 of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-v2-heliopause_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-v2-heliopause-3">[3]</a></sup> Because the heliopause <a href="/wiki/Solar_System#Boundaries" title="Solar System">marks the boundary</a> between matter originating from the Sun and matter originating from the rest of the galaxy, spacecraft such as the two Voyagers, which have departed the heliosphere, can be said to have reached <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Interstellar_space" title="Outer space">interstellar space</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" Voyager 2</i> has since crossed the heliopause into interstellar space.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="539" data-file-width="659" decoding="async" height="409" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg/500px-Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg/750px-Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg/1000px-Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_speed_and_distance_from_Sun.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Voyager 1 and 2 speed and distance from Sun</div></div></div>\n<p>The Heliosphere is the area under the influence of the Sun; the two major components to determining its edge are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_field_lines" title="Magnetic field lines">magnetic field lines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> from the Sun. Three major sections from the beginning of the Heliosphere to its edge are the termination shock, the heliosheath, and the heliopause. Five spacecraft have returned much of the data about its furthest reaches, including <i>Pioneer 10</i> (1972-1997; data to 67 AU), <i>Pioneer 11</i> (1973-1995;44 AU), <i>Voyager 1</i> and <i>Voyager 2\'</i> (launched 1977, ongoing), and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i> (Launched 2006). A type of particle called an <a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">energetic neutral atom</a> (ENA) has also been observed to have been produced from its edges. \n</p><p>There are a host of spacecraft that observe the Sun and interplanetary space.  One of the latest to explore the Sun, from nearer than ever before, is the <a href="/wiki/Parker_Solar_Probe" title="Parker Solar Probe">Parker Solar Probe</a> launched in 2018. Solar observations, such as during a Solar eclipse, allow observation of the Sun\'s <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">Corona</a>.  While various types of space observatories also provide data on the Sun and its influences. The study of other Stars can also allow insights indirectly.\n</p><p>Except for <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">regions</a> near obstacles such as <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> or <a href="/wiki/Comet#Coma_and_tail" title="Comet">comets</a>, the heliosphere is dominated by material emanating from the Sun, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atoms" title="Energetic neutral atoms">fast-moving neutral atoms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> can penetrate the heliosphere from the outside. Originating at the extremely hot surface of the <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>, solar wind particles reach <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">escape velocity</a>, streaming outwards at 300 to 800&#160;km/s (671 thousand to 1.79 million mph or 1 to 2.9 million km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As it begins to interact with the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>, its velocity slows to a stop. The point where the solar wind becomes slower than the speed of sound is called the <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere#Termination_shock" title="Heliosphere">termination shock</a>; the solar wind continues to slow as it passes through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliosheath" title="Heliosheath">heliosheath</a> leading to a boundary called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)#Heliopause" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a>, where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance. The termination shock was traversed by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> in 2004,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-voyager-5">[5]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It was thought that beyond the heliopause there was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bow_shock" title="Bow shock">bow shock</a>, but data from <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">Interstellar Boundary Explorer</a> suggested the velocity of the Sun through the interstellar medium is too low for it to form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">[7]</a></sup> It may be a more gentle "bow wave".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zank_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-zank-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Starting in May 2012 at 120&#160;au (1.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup>10</sup>&#160;km; 1.1<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup>10</sup>&#160;mi), <i>Voyager 1</i> detected a sudden increase in cosmic rays, an apparent signature of approach to the heliopause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rays-9">[9]</a></sup> In the summer of 2013, NASA announced that<i> Voyager 1</i> had reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">interstellar space</a> as of August 25, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a></i> was launched in March 1972, and within 10 hours passed by the Moon; over the next 35 years or so the mission would be the first out laying out many firsts of discoveries about the nature of heliosphere as well as Jupiter\'s impact on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>Pioneer 10</i> was the first spacecraft to detect sodium and aluminum ions in the Solar Wind, as well as helium in the inner Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In November of 1972, <i>Pioneer 10</i> encountered Jupiter\'s enormous (compared to Earth) magnetosphere, and would pass in and out of it and heliosphere 17 times charting its interaction with the Solar Wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Pioneer 10</i> returned scientific data until March 1997, including data on the Solar wind out to about 67 AU at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It was also contacted in 2003, when it was a distance of 7.6 billion miles from Earth (82 AU), but no instrument data about the Solar Wind was returned then.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Voyager 1</i> surpassed the radial distance from Sun of <i>Pioneer 10</i> at 69.4 AU on 17 February 1998, because it was traveling faster gaining about 1.02 AU per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>Pioneer 11</i>, launched a year after <i>Pioneer 10</i>, took similar data as Pioneer out to 44.7 AU in 1995 when that mission was concluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Pioneer 11 had a similar instrument suite as 10, but also had a Flux-Gate Magnetometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Pioneer and Voyager spacecraft were on different trajectories, and thus recorded data on the heliosphere in different overall directions away  from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Data obtained from Pioneer and Voyager spacecraft helped corroborate the detection of a hydrogen wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Voyager 1</i> and <i>2</i> were launched in 1977 and operated continuously to at least the late 2010s, and encountered various aspects of the heliosphere past Pluto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2012 <i>Voyager</i> 1 is thought to have passed through the Heliopause, and in 2018 there is some indications <i>Voyager 2</i> may be experiencing the same threshold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While its twin spacecraft, <i>Voyager 1</i>, crossed the boundary known as the Heliopause in 2012, <i>Voyager 2</i> carries instruments that will provide new observations into the gateway of interstellar space. The twin Voyagers are the only man-made objects to have entered interstellar space. However, while they have left the Heliosphere, they have not yet left the boundary of the Solar System which is considered to be the outer edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oort_Cloud" title="Oort Cloud">Oort Cloud</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Upon passing the Heliopause, <i>Voyager 2</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> Plasma Science Experiment (PLS) observed a sharp decline in the speed of solar wind particles on 5 November and there has been no sign of it since. The three other instruments on board measuring cosmic rays, low-energy charged particles and magnetic fields also recorded the transition. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The observations complement data from NASA\'s IBEX mission. NASA is also preparing an additional mission, Interstellar Mapping and Acceleration Probe (<a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Mapping_and_Acceleration_Probe" title="Interstellar Mapping and Acceleration Probe">IMAP</a>) which is due to launch in 2024 to capitalise on Voyager<span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> observations. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Despite its name, the heliosphere\'s shape is not a perfect sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-6">[6]</a></sup> Its shape is determined by three factors: the interstellar medium (ISM), the solar wind, and the overall motion of the Sun and heliosphere as it passes through the ISM. Because the solar wind and the ISM are both fluids, the heliosphere\'s shape and size are also fluid. Changes in the solar wind, however, more strongly alter the fluctuating position of the boundaries on short timescales (hours to a few years). The solar wind\'s pressure varies far more rapidly than the outside pressure of the ISM at any given location. In particular, the effect of the 11-year <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a>, which sees a distinct maximum and minimum of solar wind activity, is thought to be significant.\n</p><p>On a broader scale, the motion of the heliosphere through the fluid medium of the ISM results in an overall comet-like shape. The solar wind plasma which is moving roughly "upstream" (in the same direction as the Sun\'s motion through the galaxy) is compressed into a nearly-spherical form, whereas the plasma moving "downstream" (opposite the Sun\'s motion) flows out for a much greater distance before giving way to the ISM, defining the long, trailing shape of the heliotail.\n</p><p>The limited data available and unexplored nature of these structures have resulted in many theories as to their form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scientificamerican-voyager_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican-voyager-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>)</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_wind">Solar wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">Solar wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_medium" title="Interplanetary medium">Interplanetary medium</a></div>\n<p>The solar wind consists of particles (<a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionized</a> atoms from the solar <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>) and fields like the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> that are produced from the Sun and stream out into space. Because the Sun <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotates</a> once approximately every 25 days, the magnetic field transported by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> gets wrapped into a spiral. The Solar wind affects many other systems in the Solar System; for example, variations in the Sun\'s own magnetic field are carried outward by the solar wind, producing <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storms</a> in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="676" data-file-width="866" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif/200px-Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif/300px-Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif/400px-Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The heliospheric current sheet out to the orbit of Jupiter</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliospheric_current_sheet">Heliospheric current sheet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heliospheric current sheet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet" title="Heliospheric current sheet">Heliospheric current sheet</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet" title="Heliospheric current sheet">heliospheric current sheet</a> is a ripple in the heliosphere created by the rotating magnetic field of the Sun. Extending throughout the heliosphere, it is considered the largest structure in the Solar System and is said to resemble a "ballerina\'s skirt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_structure">Outer structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Outer structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The outer structure of the heliosphere is determined by the interactions between the solar wind and the winds of interstellar space. The solar wind streams away from the Sun in all directions at speeds of several hundred km/s in the Earth\'s vicinity. At some distance from the Sun, well beyond the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>, this supersonic wind must slow down to meet the gases in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. This takes place in several stages:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The solar wind is traveling at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> speeds within the Solar System. At the termination shock, a standing <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>, the solar wind falls below the speed of sound and becomes <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">subsonic</a>.</li>\n<li>It was previously thought that, once subsonic, the solar wind would be shaped by the ambient flow of the interstellar medium, forming blunt nose on one side and comet-like heliotail behind, a region called the heliosheath. However, observations in 2009 showed that this model is incorrect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-30">[30]</a></sup> As of 2011, it is thought to be filled with a magnetic bubble "foam".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surprise_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-surprise-31">[31]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The outer surface of the heliosheath, where the heliosphere meets the interstellar medium, is called the heliopause. This is the edge of the entire heliosphere. Observations in 2009 led to changes to this model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-2009_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-30">[30]</a></sup></li>\n<li>In theory, the heliopause causes turbulence in the interstellar medium as the Sun orbits the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a>. Outside the heliopause, would be a turbulent region caused by the pressure of the advancing heliopause against the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. However, the velocity of Solar wind relative to the interstellar medium is probably too low for a bow shock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Termination_shock">Termination shock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Termination shock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heliosphere-washbasin.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="790" data-file-width="1052" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/220px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/330px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Heliosphere-washbasin.svg/440px-Heliosphere-washbasin.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heliosphere-washbasin.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A termination shock in a sink basin</div></div></div>\n<p>The termination shock is the point in the heliosphere where the solar wind slows down to <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound#Speed_of_sound_in_plasma" title="Speed of sound">subsonic</a> speed (relative to the Sun) because of interactions with the local <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. This causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_(physical)" title="Compression (physical)">compression</a>, heating, and a change in the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the termination shock is believed to be 75 to 90 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical units</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod-heliosphere_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-apod-heliosphere-32">[32]</a></sup> from the Sun. In 2004, <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a></i> crossed the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>\'s termination shock, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy1-term2004_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voy1-term2004-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy1-term2005_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voy1-term2005-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager-historic_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-voyager-historic-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voy2-term2007_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voy2-term2007-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-voyager-proves_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-voyager-proves-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The shock arises because solar wind particles are emitted from the Sun at about 400&#160;km/s, while the speed of sound (in the interstellar medium) is about 100&#160;km/s. (The exact speed depends on the density, which fluctuates considerably.) The interstellar medium, although very low in density, nonetheless has a constant pressure associated with it; the pressure from the solar wind decreases with the square of the distance from the Sun. As one moves far enough away from the Sun, the pressure of the solar wind drops to where it can no longer maintain supersonic flow against the pressure of the interstellar medium, at which point the solar wind slows to below its speed of sound, causing a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>. Further from the Sun, the termination shock is followed by the heliopause, where the two pressures become equal and solar wind particles are stopped by the interstellar medium.\n</p><p>Other termination shocks can be seen in terrestrial systems; perhaps the easiest may be seen by simply running a water <a href="/wiki/Tap_(valve)" title="Tap (valve)">tap</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Sink" title="Sink">sink</a> creating a <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_jump" title="Hydraulic jump">hydraulic jump</a>. Upon hitting the floor of the sink, the flowing water spreads out at a speed that is higher than the local <a href="/wiki/Phase_velocity" title="Phase velocity">wave speed</a>, forming a disk of shallow, rapidly diverging flow (analogous to the tenuous, supersonic solar wind). Around the periphery of the disk, a shock front or wall of water forms; outside the shock front, the water moves slower than the local wave speed (analogous to the subsonic interstellar medium).\n</p><p>Evidence presented at a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> in May 2005 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ed_Stone" title="Ed Stone">Ed Stone</a> suggests that the <i>Voyager 1</i> spacecraft passed the termination shock in December 2004, when it was about 94 AU from the Sun, by virtue of the change in magnetic readings taken from the craft. In contrast, <i>Voyager 2</i> began detecting returning particles when it was only 76 AU from the Sun, in May 2006. This implies that the heliosphere may be irregularly shaped, bulging outwards in the Sun\'s northern hemisphere and pushed inward in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager_II_cnn_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-voyager_II_cnn-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliosheath">Heliosheath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heliosheath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The heliosheath is the region of the heliosphere beyond the termination shock. Here the wind is slowed, compressed and made turbulent by its interaction with the interstellar medium. At its closest point, the inner edge of the heliosheath lies approximately 80 to 100 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> from the Sun. A proposed model hypothesizes that the heliosheath is shaped like the <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a> of a comet, and trails several times that distance in the direction opposite to the Sun\'s path through space. At its windward side, its thickness is estimated to be between 10 and 100 AU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandt-2007_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandt-2007-40">[40]</a></sup> Voyager project scientists have determined that the heliosheath is not "smooth" - it is rather a "foamy zone" filled with magnetic bubbles, each about 1 AU wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surprise_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-surprise-31">[31]</a></sup>. These magnetic bubbles are created by the impact of the solar wind and the interstellar medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rayl_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rayl-42">[42]</a></sup> <i>Voyager 1</i> and <i>Voyager 2</i> began detecting evidence for the bubbles in 2007 and 2008, respectively. The probably sausage-shaped bubbles are formed by <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">magnetic reconnection</a> between oppositely oriented sectors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">solar magnetic field</a> as the solar wind slows down. They probably represent self-contained structures that have detached from the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a>.\n</p><p>At a distance of about 113 AU, <i>Voyager 1</i> detected a \'stagnation region\' within the heliosheath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stag_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-stag-43">[43]</a></sup> In this region, the solar wind slowed to zero,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amos_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amos-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.Com_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space.Com-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brumfiel_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brumfiel-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krimigis_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krimigis-47">[47]</a></sup> the magnetic field intensity doubled and high-energy electrons from the galaxy increased 100-fold. At about 122 AU, the spacecraft entered a new region that Voyager project scientists called the "magnetic highway", an area still under the influence of the Sun but with some dramatic differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voyager_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-voyager-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Transitional_regions.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="730" data-file-width="973" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/220px-Transitional_regions.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/330px-Transitional_regions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Transitional_regions.jpg/440px-Transitional_regions.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Transitional_regions.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This picture about the heliosphere was released on June 28, 2013 and incorporates results from the Voyager spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliopause">Heliopause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Heliopause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The heliopause is the theoretical boundary where the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> is stopped by the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>; where the solar wind\'s strength is no longer great enough to push back the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar winds</a> of the surrounding stars. This is the boundary where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance. The crossing of the heliopause should be signaled by a sharp drop in the temperature of charged particles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.Com_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Space.Com-45">[45]</a></sup> a change in the direction of the magnetic field, and an increase in the number of galactic cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-rays-9">[9]</a></sup> In May 2012, <i>Voyager 1</i> detected a rapid increase in such cosmic rays (a 9% increase in a month, following a more gradual increase of 25% from Jan. 2009 to Jan. 2012), suggesting it was approaching the heliopause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rays_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-rays-9">[9]</a></sup> Between late August and early September 2012, <i>Voyager 1</i> witnessed a sharp drop in protons from the sun, from 25 particles per sec in late August, to about 2 particles per second by early October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbc-voyager_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbc-voyager-49">[49]</a></sup>. In September of 2013, NASA announced that <i>Voyager 1</i> had crossed the heliopause as of August 25, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup> This was at a distance of 121 AU (18 billion km) from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowen2013_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cowen2013-50">[50]</a></sup>  Contrary to predictions, data from <i>Voyager 1</i> indicates the magnetic field of the galaxy is aligned with the solar magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tnone" style="margin-left: auto; margin-right:auto; width:auto; max-width:783px;">\n<div class="thumbinner"><div class="noresize overflowbugx" style="overflow:auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg" title=\'"..solar wind and the interstellar medium interact to create a region known as the inner heliosheath, bounded on the inside by the termination shock, and on the outside by the heliopause."-NASA\'><img alt="" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="446" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg/775px-Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg/1163px-Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg/1550px-Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg 2x" width="775"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg" title="File:Ibexheliosphererevised.jpg"> </a></div>"..solar wind and the interstellar medium interact to create a region known as the inner heliosheath, bounded on the inside by the termination shock, and on the outside by the heliopause."-NASA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></div>\n</div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heliotail">Heliotail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heliotail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The heliotail is the tail of the heliosphere, and thus the Solar System\'s tail. It can be compared to the tail of a comet (however, a comet\'s tail does not stretch behind it as it moves; it is always pointing away from the Sun). The tail is a region where the Sun\'s  solar wind slows down and ultimately escapes the heliosphere, slowly evaporating because of charge exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>\nThe shape of the heliotail (newly found by NASA\'s Interstellar Boundary Explorer - IBEX), is that of a four-leaf clover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-54">[54]</a></sup> The particles in the tail do not shine, therefore it cannot be seen with conventional optical instruments. IBEX made the first observations of the heliotail by measuring the energy of "energetic neutral atoms", neutral particles created by collisions in the Solar System\'s boundary zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tail has been shown to contain fast and slow particles; the slow particles are on the side and the fast particles are encompassed in the center. The shape of the tail can be linked to the sun sending out fast solar winds near its poles and slow solar wind near its equator more recently. The clover-shaped tail moves further away from the sun, which makes the charged particles begin to morph into a new orientation.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini&#8211;Huygens">Cassini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">IBEX</a> data challenged the "heliotail" theory in 2009.<sup href="#cite_note-IBEX-2009-30">[30]</a></sup> In July 2013, IBEX results revealed a 4-lobed tail on the Solar System\'s heliosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a interstellar pressure was detected as early as 2004, some of the Sun\'s material seeps into the interstellar medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interstellar_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-interstellar-58">[58]</a></sup> The heliosphere is thought to reside in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud" title="Local Interstellar Cloud">Local Interstellar Cloud</a> inside the <a href="/wiki/Local_Bubble" title="Local Bubble">Local Bubble</a>, which is a region in the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Arm" title="Orion Arm">Orion Arm</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" One paper released in 2013 studied the concept of a bow wave and hydrogen wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zank_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-zank-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another hypothesis suggests that the heliopause could be smaller on the side of the Solar System facing the Sun\'s orbital motion through the galaxy. It may also vary depending on the current velocity of the solar wind and the local density of the interstellar medium. It is known to lie far outside the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. The current mission of the <i>Voyager&#160;1</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">2</a></i> spacecraft is to find and study the termination shock, heliosheath, and heliopause. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">Interstellar Boundary Explorer</a> (IBEX) mission is attempting to image the heliopause from Earth orbit within two years of its 2008 launch. Initial results (October 2009) from IBEX suggest that previous assumptions are insufficiently cognisant of the true complexities of the heliopause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In August 2018, long-term studies about the hydrogen wall by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i> spacecraft confirmed results first detected in 1992 by the two <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRL-20180807_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRL-20180807-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LS-20180809_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20180809-64">[64]</a></sup> Although the hydrogen is detected by extra ultraviolet light (which may come from another source), the detection by <i>New Horizons</i> corroborates the earlier detections by <i>Voyager</i> at a much higher level of sensitivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bow_shock">Bow shock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bow shock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics" title="Bow shocks in astrophysics">Bow shocks in astrophysics</a></div>\n<p>It was long hypothesized that the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> produces a "shock wave" in its travels within the interstellar medium. It would occur if the interstellar medium is moving supersonically "toward" the Sun, since its solar wind moves "away" from the Sun supersonically. When the interstellar wind hits the heliosphere it slows and creates a region of turbulence. A bow shock was thought to possibly occur at about 230&#160;AU,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod-heliosphere_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-apod-heliosphere-32">[32]</a></sup> but in 2012 it was determined it probably does not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-No-Bow-Shock_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-No-Bow-Shock-7">[7]</a></sup> This conclusion resulted from new measurements: The velocity of the LISM (Local Interstellar Medium) relative to the Sun\'s was previously measured to be 26.3&#160;km/s by <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">Ulysses</a>, whereas IBEX measured it at 23.2&#160;km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This phenomenon has been observed outside the Solar System, around stars other than the Sun, by NASA\'s now retired orbital <a href="/wiki/GALEX" title="GALEX">GALEX</a> telescope. The red giant star <a href="/wiki/Mira" title="Mira">Mira</a> in the constellation <a href="/wiki/Cetus" title="Cetus">Cetus</a> has been shown to have both a debris tail of ejecta from the star and a distinct shock in the direction of its movement through space (at over 130 kilometers per second).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observational_methods">Observational methods</span><span 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PioneerH2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_H" title="Pioneer href="#cite_note-Pioneer-Swingby-67">[67]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_by_spacecraft">Detection by spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Detection by spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The precise distance to, and shape of the heliopause is still uncertain. Interplanetary/interstellar <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">Pioneer 11</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a> are traveling outward through the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> and will eventually pass through the heliopause. Contact to <i>Pioneer 10</i> and <i>11</i> has been lost.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cassini_results">Cassini results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cassini results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Rather than a comet-like shape, the heliosphere appears to be bubble-shaped according to data from <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini&#8211;Huygens">Cassini</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s Ion and Neutral Camera (MIMI / INCA).  Rather than being dominated by the collisions between the solar wind and the interstellar medium, the INCA (<a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">ENA</a>) maps suggest that the interaction is controlled more by particle pressure and magnetic field energy density.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassini-shape-solar-system_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cassini-shape-solar-system-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photojournal_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-photojournal-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span
