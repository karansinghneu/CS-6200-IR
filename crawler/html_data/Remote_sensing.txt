Remote sensing is used in numerous fields, including geography, land surveying and most Earth Science disciplines (for example, hydrology, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, meteorology, oceanography, glaciology, geology); it also has military, intelligence, commercial, economic, planning, and humanitarian applications.\n</p><p>In current usage, the term "remote sensing" generally refers to the use of satellite- or aircraft-based sensor technologies to detect and classify objects on Earth, including on the surface and in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Wave_propagation" title="Wave propagation">propagated signals</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>). It may be split into "active" remote sensing (i.e., when a signal is emitted by a satellite or aircraft and its reflection by the object is detected by the sensor) and "passive" remote sensing (i.e., when the reflection of sunlight is detected by the sensor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ran_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a Active collection, on the other hand, emits energy in order to scan objects and areas whereupon a sensor then detects and measures the radiation that is reflected or backscattered from the target. <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">RADAR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">LiDAR</a> are examples of active remote sensing where the time delay between emission and return is measured, establishing the location, speed and direction of an object.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1217" data-file-width="1302" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg/220px-Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg/330px-Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg/440px-Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Remote_Sensing_Illustration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of remote sensing</div></div></div>\n<p>Remote sensing makes it possible to collect data of dangerous or inaccessible areas. Remote sensing applications include monitoring <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in areas such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> features in Arctic and Antarctic regions, and <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">depth sounding</a> of coastal and ocean depths. Military collection during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> made use of stand-off collection of data about dangerous border areas. Remote sensing also replaces costly and slow data collection on the ground, ensuring in the process that areas or objects are not disturbed.\n</p><p>Orbital platforms collect and transmit data from different parts of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, which in conjunction with larger scale aerial or ground-based sensing and analysis, provides researchers with enough information to monitor trends such as <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni&#241;o">El Ni&#241;o</a> and other natural long and short term phenomena. Other uses include different areas of the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth sciences</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a>, agricultural fields such as land usage and conservation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and national security and overhead, ground-based and stand-off collection on border areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_data_acquisition_techniques">Types of data acquisition techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of data acquisition techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The basis for multispectral collection and analysis is that of examined areas or objects that reflect or emit radiation that stand out from surrounding areas. For a summary of major remote sensing satellite systems see the <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing_satellite_and_data_overview" title="Remote sensing satellite and data overview">overview table</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_remote_sensing">Applications of remote sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications of remote sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing_(geology)" title="Remote sensing (geology)">Remote sensing (geology)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing_(archaeology)" title="Remote sensing (archaeology)">Remote sensing (archaeology)</a></div>\n<ul><li>Conventional radar is mostly associated with aerial traffic control, early warning, and certain large scale meteorological data. <a href="/wiki/Doppler_radar" title="Doppler radar">Doppler radar</a> is used by local law enforcements&#8217; monitoring of speed limits and in enhanced <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">meteorological collection</a> such as wind speed and direction within weather systems in addition to precipitation location and intensity. Other types of active collection includes <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasmas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interferometric_synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Interferometric synthetic aperture radar">Interferometric synthetic aperture radar</a> is used to produce precise <a href="/wiki/Digital_elevation_model" title="Digital elevation model">digital elevation models</a> of large scale terrain (See <a href="/wiki/RADARSAT" title="RADARSAT">RADARSAT</a>, <a href="/wiki/TerraSAR-X" title="TerraSAR-X">TerraSAR-X</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magellan_probe" title="Magellan probe">Magellan</a>).</li>\n<li>Laser and <a href="/wiki/Radar_altimeter" title="Radar altimeter">radar</a> <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeters</a> on satellites have provided a wide range of data. By measuring the bulges of water caused by gravity, they map features on the seafloor to a resolution of a mile or so. By measuring the height and wavelength of ocean waves, the altimeters measure wind speeds and direction, and surface ocean currents and directions.</li>\n<li>Ultrasound (acoustic) and radar tide gauges measure sea level, tides and wave direction in coastal and offshore tide gauges.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_detection_and_ranging" title="Light detection and ranging">Light detection and ranging</a> (LIDAR) is well known in examples of weapon ranging, laser illuminated homing of projectiles. LIDAR is used to detect and measure the concentration of various chemicals in the atmosphere, while airborne LIDAR can be used to measure heights of objects and features on the ground more accurately than with radar technology. Vegetation remote sensing is a principal application of LIDAR.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">Radiometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photometer" title="Photometer">photometers</a> are the most common instrument in use, collecting reflected and emitted radiation in a wide range of frequencies. The most common are visible and infrared sensors, followed by microwave, gamma ray and rarely, ultraviolet. They may also be used to detect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_spectra" title="Emission spectra">emission spectra</a> of various chemicals, providing data on chemical concentrations in the atmosphere.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Polarimetry#Imaging" title="Polarimetry">Spectropolarimetric Imaging</a> has been reported to be useful for target tracking purposes by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Research_Laboratory" title="United States Army Research Laboratory">U.S. Army Research Laboratory</a>. They determined that manmade items possess polarimetric signatures that are not found in natural objects. These conclusions were drawn from the imaging of military trucks, like the <a href="/wiki/Humvee" title="Humvee">Humvee</a>, and trailers with their <a href="/wiki/Acousto-optic_modulator" title="Acousto-optic modulator">acousto-optic tunable filter</a> dual hyperspectral and spectropolarimetric VNIR Spectropolarimetric Imager.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Stereoscopy" title="Stereoscopy">Stereographic pairs</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_photograph" title="Aerial photograph">aerial photographs</a> have often been used to make <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic maps</a> by imagery and terrain analysts in trafficability and highway departments for potential routes, in addition to modelling terrestrial habitat features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millsetal1997_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Millsetal1997-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Twissetal2001_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Twissetal2001-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stewartetal2014_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stewartetal2014-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Simultaneous multi-spectral platforms such as Landsat have been in use since the 1970s. These thematic mappers take images in multiple wavelengths of electro-magnetic radiation (multi-spectral) and are usually found on <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a>, including (for example) the <a href="/wiki/Landsat_program" title="Landsat program">Landsat program</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IKONOS" title="IKONOS">IKONOS</a> satellite. Maps of land cover and land use from thematic mapping can be used to prospect for minerals, detect or monitor land usage, detect invasive vegetation, deforestation, and examine the health of indigenous plants and crops, including entire farming regions or forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-guo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ran_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ran-1">[1]</a></sup> Prominent scientists using remote sensing for this purpose include <a href="/wiki/Janet_Franklin" title="Janet Franklin">Janet Franklin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ruth_DeFries" title="Ruth DeFries">Ruth DeFries</a>. Landsat images are used by regulatory agencies such as KYDOW to indicate water quality parameters including Secchi depth, chlorophyll a density and total phosphorus content. <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Weather satellites</a> are used in meteorology and climatology.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hyperspectral_imaging" title="Hyperspectral imaging">Hyperspectral imaging</a> produces an image where each pixel has full spectral information with imaging narrow spectral bands over a contiguous spectral range. Hyperspectral imagers are used in various applications including mineralogy, biology, defence, and environmental measurements.</li>\n<li>Within the scope of the combat against <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, remote sensing allows researchers to follow up and monitor risk areas in the long term, to determine desertification factors, to support decision-makers in defining relevant measures of environmental management, and to assess their impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geodetic">Geodetic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geodetic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">Geodetic</a> remote sensing can be <a href="/wiki/Gravimetry" title="Gravimetry">gravimetric</a> or geometric. Overhead gravity data collection was first used in aerial submarine detection. This data revealed minute perturbations in the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">gravitational field</a> that may be used to determine changes in the mass distribution of the Earth, which in turn may be used for geophysical studies, as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRACE_(satellite)" title="GRACE (satellite)">GRACE</a>. Geometric remote sensing includes position and deformation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_imaging" title="Radar imaging">imaging</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InSAR" title="InSAR">InSAR</a>, LIDAR, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_and_near-acoustic">Acoustic and near-acoustic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acoustic and near-acoustic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">Sonar</a>: <i>passive sonar</i>, listening for the sound made by another object (a vessel, a whale etc.); <i>active sonar</i>, emitting pulses of sounds and listening for echoes, used for detecting, ranging and measurements of underwater objects and terrain.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismograph" title="Seismograph">Seismograms</a> taken at different locations can locate and measure <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> (after they occur) by comparing the relative intensity and precise timings.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a>: Ultrasound sensors, that emit high frequency pulses and listening for echoes, used for detecting water waves and water level, as in tide gauges or for towing tanks.</li></ul>\n<p>To coordinate a series of large-scale observations, most sensing systems depend on the following: platform location and the orientation of the sensor. High-end instruments now often use positional information from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation_system" title="Satellite navigation system">satellite navigation systems</a>. The rotation and orientation is often provided within a degree or two with electronic compasses. Compasses can measure not just azimuth (i. e. degrees to magnetic north), but also altitude (degrees above the horizon), since the magnetic field curves into the Earth at different angles at different latitudes. More exact orientations require <a href="/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system" title="Inertial navigation system">gyroscopic-aided orientation</a>, periodically realigned by different methods including navigation from stars or known benchmarks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_processing">Data processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Data processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Inverse_problem" title="Inverse problem">Inverse problem</a></div>\n<p>Generally speaking, remote sensing works on the principle of the <i>inverse problem</i>. While the object or phenomenon of interest (the <b>state</b>) may not be directly measured, there exists some other variable that can be detected and measured (the <b>observation</b>) which may be related to the object of interest through a calculation. The common analogy given to describe this is trying to determine the type of animal from its footprints. For example, while it is impossible to directly measure temperatures in the upper atmosphere, it is possible to measure the spectral emissions from a known chemical species (such as carbon dioxide) in that region. The frequency of the emissions may then be related via <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> to the temperature in that region.\n</p><p>The quality of remote sensing data consists of its spatial, spectral, radiometric and temporal resolutions.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Spatial resolution</dt>\n<dd>The size of a <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a> that is recorded in a <a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">raster image</a> &#8211; typically pixels may correspond to square areas ranging in side length from 1 to 1,000 metres (3.3 to 3,280.8&#160;ft).</dd>\n<dt>Spectral resolution</dt>\n<dd>The wavelength of the different frequency bands recorded &#8211; usually, this is related to the number of frequency bands recorded by the platform. Current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landsat" title="Landsat">Landsat</a> collection is that of seven bands, including several in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> spectrum, ranging from a spectral resolution of 0.7 to 2.1 &#956;m. The Hyperion sensor on Earth Observing-1 resolves 220 bands from 0.4 to 2.5 &#956;m, with a spectral resolution of 0.10 to 0.11 &#956;m per band.</dd>\n<dt>Radiometric resolution</dt>\n<dd>The number of different intensities of radiation the sensor is able to distinguish. Typically, this ranges from 8 to 14 bits, corresponding to 256 levels of the gray scale and up to 16,384 intensities or "shades" of colour, in each band. It also depends on the instrument <a href="/wiki/Noise" title="Noise">noise</a>.</dd>\n<dt>Temporal resolution</dt>\n<dd>The frequency of flyovers by the satellite or plane, and is only relevant in time-series studies or those requiring an averaged or mosaic image as in deforesting monitoring. This was first used by the intelligence community where repeated coverage revealed changes in infrastructure, the deployment of units or the modification/introduction of equipment. Cloud cover over a given area or object makes it necessary to repeat the collection of said location.</dd></dl>\n<p>In order to create sensor-based maps, most remote sensing systems expect to extrapolate sensor data in relation to a reference point including distances between known points on the ground. This depends on the type of sensor used. For example, in conventional photographs, distances are accurate in the center of the image, with the distortion of measurements increasing the farther you get from the center. Another factor is that of the platen against which the film is pressed can cause severe errors when photographs are used to measure ground distances. The step in which this problem is resolved is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georeference" title="Georeference">georeferencing</a>, and involves computer-aided matching of points in the image (typically 30 or more points per image) which is extrapolated with the use of an established benchmark, "warping" the image to produce accurate spatial data. As of the early 1990s, most satellite images are sold fully georeferenced.\n</p><p>In addition, images may need to be radiometrically and atmospherically corrected.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Radiometric correction</dt>\n<dd>Allows avoidance of radiometric errors and distortions. The illumination of objects on the Earth surface is uneven because of different properties of the relief. This factor is taken into account in the method of radiometric distortion correction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>    Radiometric correction gives a scale to the pixel values, e. g. the monochromatic scale of 0 to 255 will be converted to actual radiance values.</dd>\n<dt>Topographic correction (also called terrain correction)</dt>\n<dd>In rugged mountains, as a result of terrain, the effective illumination of pixels varies considerably. In a remote sensing image, the pixel on the shady slope receives weak illumination and has a low radiance value, in contrast, the pixel on the sunny slope receives strong illumination and has a high radiance value. For the same object, the pixel radiance value on the shady slope will be different from that on the sunny slope. Additionally, different objects may have similar radiance values. These ambiguities seriously affected remote sensing image information extraction accuracy in mountainous areas. It became the main obstacle to further application of remote sensing images. The purpose of topographic correction is to eliminate this effect, recovering the true reflectivity or radiance of objects in horizontal conditions. It is the premise of quantitative remote sensing application.</dd>\n<dt><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_correction" title="Atmospheric correction">Atmospheric correction</a></dt>\n<dd>Elimination of atmospheric haze by rescaling each frequency band so that its minimum value (usually realised in water bodies) corresponds to a pixel value of 0. The digitizing of data also makes it possible to manipulate the data by changing gray-scale values.</dd></dl>\n<p>Interpretation is the critical process of making sense of the data. The first application was that of aerial photographic collection which used the following process; spatial measurement through the use of a <a href="/wiki/Light_table" title="Light table">light table</a> in both conventional single or stereographic coverage, added skills such as the use of photogrammetry, the use of photomosaics, repeat coverage, Making use of objects&#8217; known dimensions in order to detect modifications. Image Analysis is the recently developed automated computer-aided application which is in increasing use.\n</p><p>Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) is a sub-discipline of GIScience devoted to partitioning remote sensing (RS) imagery into meaningful image-objects, and assessing their characteristics through spatial, spectral and temporal scale.\n</p><p>Old data from remote sensing is often valuable because it may provide the only long-term data for a large extent of geography. At the same time, the data is often complex to interpret, and bulky to store. Modern systems tend to store the data digitally, often with <a href="/wiki/Lossless_compression" title="Lossless compression">lossless compression</a>. The difficulty with this approach is that the data is fragile, the format may be archaic, and the data may be easy to falsify. One of the best systems for archiving data series is as computer-generated machine-readable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultrafiche" title="Ultrafiche">ultrafiche</a>, usually in typefonts such as <a href="/wiki/OCR-B" title="OCR-B">OCR-B</a>, or as digitized half-tone images. Ultrafiches survive well in standard libraries, with lifetimes of several centuries. They can be created, copied, filed and retrieved by automated systems. They are about as compact as archival magnetic media, and yet can be read by human beings with minimal, standardized equipment.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_processing_levels">Data processing levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Data processing levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To facilitate the discussion of data processing in practice, several processing "levels" were first defined in 1986 by NASA as part of its <a href="/wiki/Earth_Observing_System" title="Earth Observing System">Earth Observing System</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and steadily adopted since then, both internally at NASA (e. g.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>) and elsewhere (e. g.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>); these definitions are:\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Level\n</th>\n<th>Description\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>0\n</td>\n<td>Reconstructed, unprocessed instrument and payload data at full resolution, with any and all communications artifacts (e. g., synchronization frames, communications headers, duplicate data) removed.\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>1a\n</td>\n<td>Reconstructed, unprocessed instrument data at full resolution, time-referenced, and annotated with ancillary information, including radiometric and geometric calibration coefficients and georeferencing parameters (e. g., platform ephemeris) computed and appended but not applied to the Level 0 data (or if applied, in a manner that level 0 is fully recoverable from level 1a data).\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>1b\n</td>\n<td>Level 1a data that have been processed to sensor units (e. g., radar backscatter cross section, brightness temperature, etc.); not all instruments have Level 1b data; level 0 data is not recoverable from level 1b data.\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>2\n</td>\n<td>Derived geophysical variables (e. g., ocean wave height, soil moisture, ice concentration) at the same resolution and location as Level 1 source data.\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>3\n</td>\n<td>Variables mapped on uniform spacetime grid scales, usually with some completeness and consistency (e. g., missing points interpolated, complete regions mosaicked together from multiple orbits, etc.).\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>4\n</td>\n<td>Model output or results from analyses of lower level data (i. e., variables that were not measured by the instruments but instead are derived from these measurements).\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A Level 1 data record is the most fundamental (i. e., highest reversible level) data record that has significant scientific utility, and is the foundation upon which all subsequent data sets are produced. Level 2 is the first level that is directly usable for\nmost scientific applications; its value is much greater than the lower levels. Level 2 data sets tend to be less voluminous than Level 1 data because they have been reduced temporally, spatially, or spectrally. Level 3 data sets are generally smaller than lower level data sets and thus can be dealt with without incurring a great deal of data handling overhead. These data tend to be generally more useful for many applications. The regular spatial and temporal organization of Level 3 datasets makes it feasible to readily combine data from different sources.\n</p><p>While these processing levels are particularly suitable for typical satellite data processing pipelines, other data level vocabularies have been defined and may be appropriate for more heterogeneous workflows.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. and imagers to hunt for evidence of past or present water and volcanic activity on Mars.</div></div></div>\n<p>The modern discipline of remote sensing arose with the development of flight. The balloonist G. Tournachon (alias <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nadar_(photographer)" title="Nadar (photographer)">Nadar</a>) made photographs of Paris from his balloon in 1858. Messenger pigeons, kites, rockets and unmanned balloons were also used for early images. With the exception of balloons, these first, individual images were not particularly useful for map making or for scientific purposes.\n</p><p>Systematic <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a> was developed for military surveillance and reconnaissance purposes beginning in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and reaching a climax during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> with the use of modified combat aircraft such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-51" title="P-51">P-51</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-38" title="P-38">P-38</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RB-66" title="RB-66">RB-66</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-4C" title="F-4C">F-4C</a>, or specifically designed collection platforms such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U2/TR-1</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SR-71" title="SR-71">SR-71</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-5_Vigilante" title="A-5 Vigilante">A-5</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OV-1" title="OV-1">OV-1</a> series both in overhead and stand-off collection. A more recent development is that of increasingly smaller sensor pods such as those used by law enforcement and the military, in both manned and unmanned platforms. The advantage of this approach is that this requires minimal modification to a given airframe. Later imaging technologies would include infrared, conventional, Doppler and synthetic aperture radar.\n</p><p>The development of artificial satellites in the latter half of the 20th century allowed remote sensing to progress to a global scale as of the end of the Cold War. Instrumentation aboard various Earth observing and weather satellites such as <a href="/wiki/Landsat_program" title="Landsat program">Landsat</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_program" title="Nimbus program">Nimbus</a> and more recent missions such as <a href="/wiki/RADARSAT" title="RADARSAT">RADARSAT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Atmosphere_Research_Satellite" title="Upper Atmosphere Research Satellite">UARS</a> provided global measurements of various data for civil, research, and military purposes. Space probes to other planets have also provided the opportunity to conduct remote sensing studies in extraterrestrial environments, synthetic aperture radar aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magellan_probe" title="Magellan probe">Magellan</a> spacecraft provided detailed topographic maps of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, while instruments aboard <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> allowed studies to be performed on the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, just to name a few examples.\n</p><p>Recent developments include, beginning in the 1960s and 1970s with the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_processing" title="Image processing">image processing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a>. Several research groups in <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Ames_Research_Center" title="NASA Ames Research Center">NASA Ames Research Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/GTE" title="GTE">GTE</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESL_Inc." title="ESL Inc.">ESL Inc.</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform" title="Fourier transform">Fourier transform</a> techniques leading to the first notable enhancement of imagery data. In 1999 the first commercial satellite (IKONOS) collecting very high resolution imagery was launched.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_and_education">Training and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Training and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Remote Sensing has a growing relevance in the modern information society. It represents a key technology as part of the aerospace industry and bears increasing economic relevance &#8211; new sensors e.g. <a href="/wiki/TerraSAR-X" title="TerraSAR-X">TerraSAR-X</a> and <a href="/wiki/RapidEye" title="RapidEye">RapidEye</a> are developed constantly and the demand for skilled labour is increasing steadily. Furthermore, remote sensing exceedingly influences everyday life, ranging from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_forecasts" title="Weather forecasts">weather forecasts</a> to reports on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_disasters" title="Natural disasters">natural disasters</a>. As an example, 80% of the German students use the services of <a href="/wiki/Google_Earth" title="Google Earth">Google Earth</a>; in 2006 alone the software was downloaded 100 million times. But studies have shown that only a fraction of them know more about the data they are working with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> There exists a huge <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_gap_hypothesis" title="Knowledge gap hypothesis">knowledge gap</a> between the application and the understanding of satellite images.\nRemote sensing only plays a tangential role in schools, regardless of the political claims to strengthen the support for teaching on the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A lot of the computer software explicitly developed for school lessons has not yet been implemented due to its complexity. Thereby, the subject is either not at all integrated into the curriculum or does not pass the step of an interpretation of analogue images. In fact, the subject of remote sensing requires a consolidation of physics and mathematics as well as <a href="/wiki/Competence_(human_resources)" title="Competence (human resources)">competences</a> in the fields of media and methods apart from the mere visual interpretation of satellite images.\n</p><p>Many teachers have great interest in the subject "remote sensing", being motivated to integrate this topic into teaching, provided that the curriculum is considered. In many cases, this encouragement fails because of confusing information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In order to integrate remote sensing in a sustainable manner organizations like the <a href="/wiki/European_Geosciences_Union" title="European Geosciences Union">EGU</a> or <a href="/wiki/Digital_Earth" title="Digital Earth">Digital Earth</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> encourage the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-learning" title="E-learning">learning modules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Learning_management_system" title="Learning management system">learning portals</a>. Examples include: <i>FIS &#8211; Remote Sensing in School Lessons</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Geospektiv</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Ychange</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> or Spatial Discovery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> to promote media and method qualifications as well as independent learning.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing_software" title="Remote sensing software">Remote sensing software</a></div>\n<p>Remote sensing data are processed and analyzed with computer software, known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remote_sensing_application" title="Remote sensing application">remote sensing application</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Remote_sensing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>First Satellite UV/VIS observations simply showed pictures of the\nEarth\'s surface and atmosphere. Such satellite images are still used,\nfor instance as input for numerical weather forecast. The first\nspectroscopic UV/VIS observations started in 1970 on board of the US\nresearch satellite <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_4" title="Nimbus 4">Nimbus 4</a>. These measurements (backscatter\nultraviolet, BUV, later also called Solar BUV, SBUV) operated in <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>\ngeometry, i.e., they measured the solar light reflected from the ground\nor scattered from the atmosphere. Like for the Dobson instruments also\nthe BUV/SBUV instruments measure the intensity in different narrow\nspectral intervals. The intention of these BUV/SBUV observations was to\ndetermine information on the atmospheric O<sub>3</sub> profile, since the\npenetration depth into the atmosphere strongly depends on wavelength.\nFor example, the light at the shortest wavelengths has only \'seen\' the\nhighest parts of the O<sub>3</sub> layer whereas the longest wavelengths have seen\nthe total column. While in principle the BUV/SBUV measurements worked\nwell, they suffered from instrumental instabilities.\n</p><p>The big breakthrough in UV/VIS satellite remote sensing of the\natmosphere took place in 1979 with the launch the Total Ozone Mapping\nSpectrometer (TOMS) on <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_7" title="Nimbus 7">Nimbus 7</a>. TOMS is similar to the BUV/SBUV\ninstrument but measures light at longer wavelengths. Thus it is only\nsensitive to the total O<sub>3</sub> column (instead of the\nO<sub>3</sub> profile). However, compared to the BUV/SBUV instruments\nthe TOMS instruments were much more stable. The TOMS instrument on board\nof Nimbus 7 yielded the so far longest global data set on O<sub>3</sub>\n(1979 - 1992). This period in particular includes the discovery of the\nozone hole. Several further TOMS instruments have been launched on other\nsatellites. Like the Dobson instruments on the ground they yield very\naccurate O<sub>3</sub> total column densities using a relatively simple\nmethod. Besides events of very strong atmospheric SO<sub>2</sub>\nabsorption and aerosols they are, however, only sensitive to\nO<sub>3</sub>.\n</p><p>Since April 1995 the first <a href="/wiki/Differential_optical_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Differential optical absorption spectroscopy">DOAS</a> instrument is operating from space. The\nGlobal Ozone Monitoring Experiment (GOME) was launched on the European\nresearch satellite ERS-2. Like SBUV and TOMS also GOME is a nadir\nviewing instrument; unlike its predecessor instruments it covers a large\nspectral range (240 - 790&#160;nm) at a total of 4096 wavelengths arranged in\nfour \'channels\' with a spectral resolution between 0.2 and 0.4&#160;nm. Its\nnormal ground pixel size in 320 x 40&#160;km<sup>2</sup>. Global coverage is achieved\nafter three days. For O<sub>3</sub> profile measurements the intensities\nat short wavelengths are observed (BUV/SBUV instruments). For the\ndetermination of the total atmospheric O<sub>3</sub> column the\nintensities at larger wavelengths are used (TOMS instruments). In\ncontrast to the limited spectral information of BUV/SBUV and TOMS\ninstruments, GOME spectra yield a surplus of spectral information. By\napplying the DOAS method to these measurements it is thus possible to\nretrieve a large variety of atmospheric trace gases, the majority of\nwhich are very weak absorbers (O<sub>3</sub>, NO<sub>2</sub>, BrO, OClO,\nHCHO, H<sub>2</sub>O, O<sub>2</sub>, O<sub>4</sub>, SO<sub>2</sub>). In\naddition also other quantities like aerosol absorptions, the ground\nalbedo or indices characterising the solar cycle can be analysed.\nBecause of the high sensitivity of GOME it is in particular possible to\nmeasure various tropospheric trace gases (NO<sub>2</sub>, BrO, HCHO,\nH2O, SO<sub>2</sub>). A further important advantage is that the GOME\nspectra can be analysed with respect to a spectrum of direct sun light,\nwhich contains no atmospheric absorptions. Therefore, in contrast to\nground based DOAS measurements the DOAS analysis of GOME spectra yields\ntotal atmospheric column densities rather than the difference between\ntwo atmospheric spectra.\n</p><p>In March 2002 a second DOAS satellite instrument, the SCanning Imaging\nAbsorption SpectroMeter for Atmospheric ChartographY (<a href="/wiki/SCIAMACHY" title="SCIAMACHY">SCIAMACHY</a>) was\nlaunched on board of the European research satellite <a href="/wiki/Envisat" title="Envisat">Envisat</a>. In\naddition to GOME it measures over a wider wavelength range (240&#160;nm -\n2380) including also the absorption of several greenhouse gases (CO<sub>2</sub>,\nCH<sub>4</sub>, N<sub>2</sub>O) and CO in the IR. It also operated in\nadditional viewing modes (nadir, limb, <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultation</a>), which allows to\nderive stratospheric trace gas profiles. Another advantage is that the\nground pixel size for the nadir viewing mode was significantly reduced\nto 30 x 60&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (in a special mode even to 15 x 30&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). Especially for the observation of tropospheric trace\ngases this is very important because of the strong spatial gradients\noccurring for such species. The first tropospheric results of SCIAMACHY\nshowed that it was now possible to identify pollution plumes of\nindividual cities or other big sources.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
