href="#cite_note-jplssd-4">[4]</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Miranda</b>, also designated <b>Uranus&#160;V</b>, is the smallest and innermost of <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>\'s five round <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">satellites</a>. It was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">Gerard Kuiper</a> on 16 February 1948 at <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">McDonald Observatory</a>, and named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miranda_(Shakespeare)" title="Miranda (Shakespeare)">Miranda</a> from <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>\'s play <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">The Tempest</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">[5]</a></sup> Like the other large <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">moons of Uranus</a>, Miranda orbits close to its planet\'s equatorial plane. Because Uranus orbits the Sun on its side, Miranda\'s orbit is perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> and shares Uranus\'s extreme seasonal cycle.\n</p><p>At just 470&#160;km in diameter, Miranda is one of the smallest closely observed objects in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> that might be in <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">hydrostatic equilibrium</a> (spherical under its own gravity). The only close-up images of Miranda are from the <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> probe, which made observations of Miranda during its Uranus flyby in January 1986. During the flyby, Miranda\'s southern hemisphere pointed towards the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, so only that part was studied.\n</p><p>Miranda probably formed from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">accretion disc</a> that surrounded the planet shortly after its formation, and, like other large moons, it is likely <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">differentiated</a>, with an inner core of rock surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> of ice. Miranda has one of the most extreme and varied topographies of any object in the Solar System, including <a href="/wiki/Verona_Rupes" title="Verona Rupes">Verona Rupes</a>, a 20-kilometer-high scarp that is the highest cliff in the Solar System,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin2001b_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin2001b-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa2016_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa2016-7">[7]</a></sup> and chevron-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> features called <i><a href="/wiki/Corona_(planetary_geology)" title="Corona (planetary geology)">coronae</a></i>. The origin and evolution of this varied geology, the most of any Uranian satellite, are still not fully understood, and multiple hypotheses exist regarding Miranda\'s evolution.\n</p>\n<div class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Miranda was discovered on 16 February 1948 by planetary astronomer <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">Gerard Kuiper</a> using the McDonald Observatory\'s 82-inch (2,080&#160;mm) <a href="/wiki/Otto_Struve_Telescope" title="Otto Struve Telescope">Otto Struve Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otto_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Otto-8">[8]</a></sup> Its motion around Uranus was confirmed on 1 March 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">[5]</a></sup> It was the first satellite of Uranus discovered in nearly 100 years. Kuiper elected to name the object "Miranda" after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miranda_(Shakespeare)" title="Miranda (Shakespeare)">character</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">The Tempest</a></i>, because the four previously discovered moons of Uranus, <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">Ariel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">Umbriel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">Titania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">Oberon</a>, had all been named after characters of Shakespeare or <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>. However, the previous moons had been named specifically after fairies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-namesof_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-namesof-9">[9]</a></sup> whereas Miranda was a human. Subsequently, discovered satellites of Uranus were named after characters from Shakespeare and Pope, whether fairies or not. The moon is also designated <b>Uranus V</b>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">Orbit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Of Uranus\'s five round satellites, Miranda orbits closest to it, at roughly 129,000&#160;km from the surface; about a quarter again as far as its most distant <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Uranus" title="Rings of Uranus">ring</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> is 34 hours, and, like that of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, is synchronous with its <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">rotation period</a>, which means it always shows the same face to Uranus, a condition known as <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">tidal lock</a>. Miranda\'s orbital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> (4.34&#176;) is unusually high for a body so close to its planet, and roughly ten times that of the other major Uranian satellites. The reason for this is still uncertain; there are no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean-motion_resonance" title="Mean-motion resonance">mean-motion resonances</a> between the moons that could explain it, leading to the hypothesis that the moons occasionally pass through <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Secondary_resonance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Secondary resonance (page does not exist)">secondary resonances</a>, which at some point in the past led to Miranda being locked for a time into a 3:1 resonance with <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">Umbriel</a>, before chaotic behaviour induced by the secondary resonances moved it out of it again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-umbriel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-umbriel-10">[10]</a></sup> In the Uranian system, due to the planet\'s lesser degree of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">oblateness</a>, and the larger relative size of its satellites, escape from a mean-motion resonance is much easier than for satellites of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra_Dermott_1990_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malhotra_Dermott_1990-12">[12]</a></sup> Miranda\'s orbit is the most <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclined</a> of any of Uranus\'s large satellites, at 4.232&#176;, it is 10-20 times that of <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">Titania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">Ariel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">Umbriel</a> and 73 times that of <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">Oberon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_scarp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1002" data-file-width="1016" decoding="async" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/200px-Miranda_scarp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/300px-Miranda_scarp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/400px-Miranda_scarp.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_scarp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up of <a href="/wiki/Verona_Rupes" title="Verona Rupes">Verona Rupes</a>, a large fault scarp on Miranda possibly 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) high,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA00044_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-PIA00044-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chaikin2001b_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-chaikin2001b-6">[6]</a></sup> taken by <i>Voyager 2</i> in January 1986</div></div></div>\n<p>At 1.2&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>, Miranda is the least dense of Uranus\'s round satellites. That density suggests a composition of more than 60% water ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-water_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-water-16">[16]</a></sup> Miranda\'s surface may be mostly water ice, though it is far rockier than its corresponding satellites in the Saturn system, indicating that heat from <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> may have led to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_differentiation" title="Internal differentiation">internal differentiation</a>, allowing  <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> rock and <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> to settle in its interior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup> Miranda is too small for any internal heat to have been retained over the age of the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup> Miranda is the least spherical of Uranus\'s satellites, with an equatorial diameter 3% wider than its polar diameter. Only water has been detected so far on Miranda\'s surface, though it has been speculated that methane, ammonia, carbon monoxide or nitrogen may also exist at 3% concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miner_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-miner-20">[20]</a></sup> These bulk properties are similar to Saturn\'s moon <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">Mimas</a>, though Mimas is smaller, less dense, and more oblate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miner_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-miner-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to Uranus\'s near-sideways orientation, only Miranda\'s southern hemisphere was visible to <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> when it arrived. The observed surface has patchwork regions of broken terrain, indicating intense geological activity in Miranda\'s past, and is criss-crossed by huge canyons, believed to be the result of <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">extensional tectonics</a>; as liquid water froze beneath the surface, it expanded, causing the surface ice to split, creating <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">graben</a>. The canyons are hundreds of kilometers long and tens of kilometers wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup> Miranda also has the largest known <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliff</a> in the Solar System, Verona Rupes, which has a height of 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa2016_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa2016-7">[7]</a></sup> Some of Miranda\'s terrain is possibly less than 100&#160;million years old based on crater counts, while sizeable regions possess crater counts that indicate ancient terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usra_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-usra-21">[21]</a></sup> Precisely how a body as small as Miranda could have enough internal energy to produce the myriad geological features seen on its surface is not established with certainty,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup> though the currently favoured hypothesis is that it was driven by <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">tidal heating</a> during a past time when it was in 3:1 orbital resonance with Umbriel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-22">[22]</a></sup> The resonance would have increased Miranda\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> to 0.1, and generated tidal friction due to the varying <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal forces</a> from Uranus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-23">[23]</a></sup> As Miranda approached Uranus, tidal force increased; as it retreated, tidal force decreased, causing flexing that would have warmed Miranda\'s interior by 20 K, enough to trigger melting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra_Dermott_1990_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Malhotra_Dermott_1990-12">[12]</a></sup> The period of tidal flexing could have lasted for up to 100 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-23">[23]</a></sup> Also, if <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> existed within Miranda, as has been hypothesised for the satellites of Uranus, it may have acted as an insulator, since it has a lower conductivity than water, increasing Miranda\'s temperature still further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-23">[23]</a></sup> Miranda may have also once been in a 5:3 resonance with <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">Ariel</a>, which would have also contributed to its internal heating. However, the maximum heating attributable to the resonance with Umbriel was likely about three times greater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1400" data-file-width="1362" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/33/Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg/200px-Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/33/Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg/300px-Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/33/Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg/400px-Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_elsinorinverness.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The fault scarps around Elsinore (top right) and the chevrons of Inverness Corona (bottom left)</div></div></div>\n<p>While <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> counts suggest that the majority of Miranda\'s surface is old, with a similar geological history to the other Uranian satellites,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miner309_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miner309-24">[24]</a></sup> few of those craters are particularly large, indicating that most must have formed after a major resurfacing event in its distant past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-17">[17]</a></sup> Craters on Miranda also appear to possess softened edges, which could be the result either of ejecta or of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanism" title="Cryovolcanism">cryovolcanism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miner309_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miner309-24">[24]</a></sup> The temperature at Miranda\'s south pole is roughly 85 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvin_scale" title="Kelvin scale">K</a>, a temperature at which pure water ice adopts the properties of rock. Also, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanic" title="Cryovolcanic">cryovolcanic</a> material responsible for the surfacing is too viscous to have been pure liquid water, but too fluid to have been solid water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miner1990_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-miner1990-25">[25]</a></sup> Rather, it is believed to have been a viscous, lava-like mixture of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, which freezes at 176 K (-97 &#176;C), or perhaps <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mirandaanicy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="515" decoding="async" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Mirandaanicy.jpg/200px-Mirandaanicy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Mirandaanicy.jpg/300px-Mirandaanicy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/Mirandaanicy.jpg/400px-Mirandaanicy.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mirandaanicy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The three coronae imaged on Miranda by <i>Voyager 2</i></div></div></div>\n<p>Miranda\'s observed hemisphere contains three giant \'racetrack\'-like grooved structures called <a href="/wiki/Corona_(planetary_geology)" title="Corona (planetary geology)">coronae</a>, each at least 200&#160;km (120&#160;mi) wide and up to 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) deep, named Arden, Elsinore and Inverness after locations in Shakespeare\'s plays. Inverness is lower in altitude than the surrounding terrain (though domes and ridges are of comparable elevation), while Elsinore is higher,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup> The relative sparsity of craters on their surfaces means they overlay the earlier cratered terrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanton_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-tanton-19">[19]</a></sup> The coronae, which are unique to Miranda, initially defied easy explanation; one early hypothesis was that Miranda, at some time in its distant past, (prior to any of the current cratering)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup> had been completely torn to pieces, perhaps by a massive impact, and then reassembled in a random jumble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-space-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-15">[15]</a></sup> The heavier core material fell through the crust, and the coronae formed as the water re-froze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blom_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-blom-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up of the ring of concentric fault scarps around Elsinore Corona.</div></div></div>\n<p>However, the current favoured hypothesis is that they formed formed via <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">extensional</a> processes at the tops of <a href="/wiki/Diapir" title="Diapir">diapirs</a>, or upwellings of warm ice from within Miranda itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-space-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teachastronomy_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-teachastronomy-28">[28]</a></sup> The coronae are surrounded by rings of concentric faults with a similar low-crater count, suggesting they played a role in their formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miner1990_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-miner1990-25">[25]</a></sup> If the coronae formed through downwelling from a catastrophic disruption, then the concentric faults would present as <a href="/wiki/Compression_(geology)" title="Compression (geology)">compressed</a>. If they formed through upwelling, such as by diapirism, then they would be <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">extensional</a> tilt blocks, and present extensional features, as current evidence suggests they do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997-27">[27]</a></sup> The concentric rings would have formed as ice moved away from the heat source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hammond_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hammond-29">[29]</a></sup> The diapirs may have changed the density distribution within Miranda, which could have caused Miranda to reorient itself,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pappalardo_Greeley_1993_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pappalardo_Greeley_1993-30">[30]</a></sup> similar to a process believed to have occurred at <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>\'s geologically active moon <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a>. Evidence suggests the reorientation would have been as extreme as 60 degrees from the sub-Uranian point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hammond_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-hammond-29">[29]</a></sup> The positions of all the coronae require a tidal heating pattern consistent with Miranda being solid, and lacking an internal liquid ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hammond_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-hammond-29">[29]</a></sup> It is believed through computer modelling that Miranda may have an additional corona on the unimaged hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_eclipse.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" Miranda</div></div></div>\n<p>Miranda\'s apparent magnitude is +16.6, making it invisible to many amateur telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michigan_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-michigan-32">[32]</a></sup> Virtually all known information regarding its geology and geography was obtained during the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">flyby</a> of Uranus made by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> on Jan 25 1986,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-17">[17]</a></sup> The closest approach of <i>Voyager 2</i> to Miranda was 29,000&#160;km (18,000&#160;mi)&#8212;significantly less than the distances to all other Uranian moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_1987_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stone_1987-33">[33]</a></sup> Of all the Uranian satellites, Miranda had the most visible surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miner_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-miner-20">[20]</a></sup> The discovery team had expected Miranda to resemble <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">Mimas</a>, and found themselves at a loss to explain the moon\'s unique geography in the 24-hour window before releasing the images to the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miner309_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Miner309-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2017, as part of its <a href="/wiki/Planetary_Science_Decadal_Survey" title="Planetary Science Decadal Survey">Planetary Science Decadal Survey</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> evaluated the possibility of an orbiter to return to Uranus some time in the 2020s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Uranus was the preferred destination over Neptune due to favourable planetary alignments meaning shorter flight times.<sup class="reference"
