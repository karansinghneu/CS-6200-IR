Beginning in 1961 with the flight of <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> occurs when a human crew flies a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> into <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>.  Human spaceflight is distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> generally, which entails both crewed and uncrewed spacecraft.\n</p><p>There are two definitions of spaceflight.  The <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale">F&#233;d&#233;ration A&#233;ronautique Internationale</a> (FAI), an international record-keeping body, defines the boundary between Earth\'s atmosphere and outer space at 100 kilometers above sea level.  This boundary is known as the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="K&#225;rm&#225;n line">K&#225;rm&#225;n line</a>.  Additionally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_military" title="United States military">United States military</a> awards <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_wings" title="Astronaut wings">astronaut wings</a> to qualified personnel who pilot a spaceflight above an altitude of 50 miles (80 km).  Thirteen flights of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">North American X-15</a> met the latter criteria, while only two met the former.  This article is primarily concerned with the former international convention, and also lists flights which only satisfied the latter convention.  Unless otherwise specified, "spaceflight" and related terms only apply to flights which went beyond the K&#225;rm&#225;n line.\n</p><p>As of the launch of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_MS-11" title="Soyuz MS-11">Soyuz MS-11</a> on 3 December 2018, there have been 324 human spaceflight launch attempts, including three failed attempts which did not cross the K&#225;rm&#225;n line.  These were the fatal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Challenger_disaster" title="Challenger disaster">Challenger disaster</a>, and two non-fatal aborted Soyuz missions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_T-10a" title="Soyuz T-10a">T-10a</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_MS-10" title="Soyuz MS-10">MS-10</a>.  Another non-fatal aborted Soyuz mission, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_18a" title="Soyuz 18a">18a</a>, nevertheless crossed the K&#225;rm&#225;n line and therefore qualified as a <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">sub-orbital spaceflight</a>.  Three missions successfully achieved human spaceflight, yet ended as fatal failures as their crews died during the return.  These were <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_1" title="Soyuz 1">Soyuz 1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Columbia_disaster" title="Columbia disaster">Columbia disaster</a>.  Uniquely, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_34" title="Soyuz 34">Soyuz 34</a> was launched uncrewed to the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">Salyut 6</a> space station, to provide a successful return vehicle for the crew of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_32" title="Soyuz 32">Soyuz 32</a>.  Including Soyuz 34 gives a total of 325 attempted human spaceflights.  13 flights reached an apogee beyond 50 miles, but failed to go beyond 100 kilometers.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input
