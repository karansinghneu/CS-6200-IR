//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Augustine_Commission_logo.svg/500px-Augustine_Commission_logo.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a></div>\n<p>The <b>Review of United States Human Space Flight Plans Committee</b> (also known as the <b>HSF Committee</b>, <b>Augustine Commission</b> or <b>Augustine Committee</b>) was a group reviewing the <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> plans of the United States.  Their goal was to ensure the nation is on "a vigorous and sustainable path to achieving its boldest aspirations in space."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostp090507_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ostp090507-1">[1]</a></sup> The review was announced by the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="Office of Science and Technology Policy">Office of Science and Technology Policy</a> (OSTP) on May 7, 2009. It covered human spaceflight options after the time <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> had planned to retire the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostp090507_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ostp090507-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  A summary report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Summary_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Summary-4">[4]</a></sup> was provided to the OSTP Director <a href="/wiki/John_Holdren" title="John Holdren">John Holdren</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_House_Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="White House Office of Science and Technology Policy">White House Office of Science and Technology Policy</a> (OSTP), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASA_Administrator" title="NASA Administrator">NASA Administrator</a> on September 8, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-status-5">[5]</a></sup> The estimated cost associated with the review was expected to be US$3 million.  The committee was scheduled to be active for 180 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charter_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-charter-6">[6]</a></sup> The report was released on October 22, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" href="/w/index.php?title=Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The review was commissioned to take into account several objectives. These included support for the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, development of missions beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">Near-Earth objects</a>) and use of commercial space industry. These objectives must fit within a defined budget profile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charter_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-charter-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Among the parameters that were considered in the course of the review were "crew and mission safety, life-cycle costs, development time, national space industrial base impacts, potential to spur innovation and encourage competition, and the implications and impacts of transitioning from current human space flight systems".  The review considered the appropriate amounts of research and development and "complementary robotic activity necessary to support various human space flight activities".  It was tasked to also "explore options for extending International Space Station operations beyond 2016".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_limits">Budget limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Budget limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Statement of Task defines the <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 2010&#8211;2014 budget profile (in millions of US$) for NASA\'s Exploration program as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statement_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-statement-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table border="1" class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Year\n</th>\n<td>2010\n</td>\n<td>2011\n</td>\n<td>2012\n</td>\n<td>2013\n</td>\n<td>2014\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Budget\n</th>\n<td>3,963.1\n</td>\n<td>6,092.9\n</td>\n<td>6,077.4\n</td>\n<td>6,047.7\n</td>\n<td>6,274.6\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The fiscal year 2009 budget projection for Exploration had been:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table border="1" class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Year\n</th>\n<td>2010\n</td>\n<td>2011\n</td>\n<td>2012\n</td>\n<td>2013\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<th>Budget\n</th>\n<td>3,737.7\n</td>\n<td>7,048.2\n</td>\n<td>7,116.8\n</td>\n<td>7,666.8\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A subcommittee in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> has announced a plan to cut the 2010 budget from US3,963.1 million to $3,293.2 million, a cut of $669.9 million or 16.9%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Mollohan" title="Alan Mollohan">Alan Mollohan</a> stated the cut was a "pause" and "time-out" caused by the review of human space flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Findings">Findings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Findings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Committee has concluded that, "the ultimate goal of human exploration is to chart a path for human expansion into the solar system."  It also observed that "destinations should derive from goals," and "human spaceflight objectives should broadly align with key national objectives."  Destinations beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> that were considered by the Committee include the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_objects" title="Near-Earth objects">near-Earth objects</a> as well as the moons of Mars, <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">Deimos</a>.  Among these, the Committee felt that "Mars stands prominently above all other opportunities for exploration" because "if humans are ever to live for long periods on another planetary surface, it is likely to be on Mars."\n</p><p>The Committee\'s final report mentions the possibility of evaluating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_objects" title="Near-Earth objects">near-Earth objects</a> for "their utility as sites for mining of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">in-situ resources</a>."\n</p><p>The Committee judged the 9-year old <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a> to be so behind schedule, underfunded and over budget that meeting any of its goals would not be possible.  President Obama removed the program from the 2010 budget effectively canceling the program.  One component of the program, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_crew_capsule" title="Orion crew capsule">Orion crew capsule</a> was added back to plans but as a rescue vehicle to complement the Russian <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> in returning Station crews to Earth in the event of an emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The proposed "ultimate goal" for human space flight would appear to require two basic objectives: (1) physical sustainability and (2) economic sustainability.  The Committee adds a third objective:  to meet key national objectives.  These might include international cooperation, developing new industries, energy independence, reducing climate change, national prestige, etc.  Therefore, the ideal destination should contain resources such as water to sustain life (also providing oxygen for breathing, and hydrogen to combine with oxygen for rocket fuel), and precious and industrial metals and other resources that may be of value for space construction and perhaps in some cases worth returning to Earth (e.g., see <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">asteroid mining</a>).\n</p><p>Some of these resources are available on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, and perhaps on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, but the Committee report noted the cost and difficulty of "travel into the deep gravity wells of the lunar and Martian surface."  It did not emphasize options such as <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">asteroid mining</a> (other than the one mention noted above) or <a href="/wiki/Space-based_solar_power" title="Space-based solar power">space-based solar power</a>. The Committee report did favor strengthening the private space launch industry, and increased international collaboration.\n</p><p>In its final report, the Committee proposed three basic options for exploration beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, and appeared to favor the third option:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Mars First, with a Mars landing, perhaps after a brief test of equipment and procedures on the Moon.</li>\n<li>Moon First, with lunar surface exploration focused on developing the capability to explore Mars.</li>\n<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexible_Path" title="Flexible Path">Flexible Path</a> to inner solar system locations, such as lunar orbit, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_points" title="Lagrange points">Lagrange points</a>, near-Earth objects and the moons of Mars, followed by exploration of the lunar surface and/or Martian surface, optionally involving the development of a <a href="/wiki/Propellant_depot" title="Propellant depot">propellant depot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee_Final_Report_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Committee_Final_Report-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span id="Obama.27s_choice_for_the_future_of_the_U.S._Program"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Obama\'s_choice_for_the_future_of_the_U.S._Program">Obama\'s choice for the future of the U.S. Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Obama\'s choice for the future of the U.S. Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The review aimed to "examine ongoing and planned National Aeronautics and Space Administration (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>) development activities, as well as potential alternatives, and present options for advancing a safe, innovative, affordable, and sustainable human space flight program in the years following Space Shuttle retirement". The panel was to "work closely with NASA and will seek input" from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, "the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, the public, industry, and international partners as it develops its options". "It is to present its results in time to support an Administration decision on the way forward by August 2009."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostp090507_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ostp090507-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On April 15, 2010, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_space_policy_speech_at_Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Barack Obama space policy speech at Kennedy Space Center">President Obama spoke at the Kennedy Space Center</a> announcing the administration\'s plans for NASA. None of the three plans outlined in the Committees final report were completely selected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee_Final_Report_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Committee_Final_Report-15">[15]</a></sup>  The President promised:\n</p>\n<ol><li>$6 billion in additional funding over five years</li>\n<li>continued investment in private space to provide low earth orbit access</li>\n<li>continued funding for a solar atmosphere probe (became the <a href="/wiki/Parker_Solar_Probe" title="Parker Solar Probe">Parker Solar Probe</a>), further Mars robotic missions, and a follow-on orbital telescope (<a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a>)</li>\n<li>extend the life of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> to 2020, allowing it to be used for scientific research</li>\n<li>design of a <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">new heavy lift rocket</a> to be ready to start construction in 2015, in order to launch astronauts and material for beyond earth orbit access</li>\n<li>manned flights using the new launcher in the early 2020s</li>\n<li>a manned mission to an asteroid in the mid-2020s</li>\n<li>manned missions to Mars orbit by the mid-2030s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aw20110422_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-aw20110422-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>Obama specifically rejected going back to the moon, saying "frankly, we\'ve been there." Instead a set a series of ever-more-challenging goals to drive technological development.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Members">Members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Ralph_Augustine" title="Norman Ralph Augustine">Norman Augustine</a> (chairman), former CEO of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>, former chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Advisory_Committee_on_the_Future_of_the_United_States_Space_Program" title="Advisory Committee on the Future of the United States Space Program">Advisory Committee on the Future of the United States Space Program</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Wanda_Austin" title="Wanda Austin">Wanda Austin</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/The_Aerospace_Corporation" title="The Aerospace Corporation">The Aerospace Corporation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bohdan_Bejmuk" title="Bohdan Bejmuk">Bohdan Bejmuk</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> manager</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Leroy_Chiao" title="Leroy Chiao">Leroy Chiao</a>, former NASA <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Chyba" title="Christopher Chyba">Christopher Chyba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> professor</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Edward_F._Crawley" title="Edward F.
