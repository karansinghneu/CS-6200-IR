The term most commonly applies to a <b>spacewalk</b> made outside a craft orbiting Earth (such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>), but also has applied to <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> surface exploration (commonly known as <b>moonwalks</b>) performed by six pairs of American astronauts in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> from 1969 to 1972. On each of the last three of these missions, astronauts also performed deep-space EVAs on the return to Earth, to retrieve film canisters from the outside of the spacecraft. Astronauts also used EVA in 1973 to repair launch damage to <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a>, the United States\' first space station.\n</p><p>A "Stand-up" EVA (SEVA) is when an astronaut does not fully leave a spacecraft, but is completely reliant on the spacesuit for environmental support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-standup_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-standup-1">[1]</a></sup> Its name derives from the astronaut "standing up" in the open hatch, usually to record or assist a spacewalking astronaut.\n</p><p>EVAs may be either tethered (the astronaut is connected to the spacecraft; oxygen and electrical power can be supplied through an <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical cable</a>; no propulsion is needed to return to the spacecraft), or untethered. Untethered spacewalks were only performed on three missions in 1984 using the <a href="/wiki/Manned_Maneuvering_Unit" title="Manned Maneuvering Unit">Manned Maneuvering Unit</a> (MMU), and on a flight test in 1994 of the <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Aid_For_EVA_Rescue" title="Simplified Aid For EVA Rescue">Simplified Aid For EVA Rescue</a> (SAFER), a safety device worn on tethered U.S. EVAs.\n</p><p>The Soviet Union/Russia, the United States, and China have conducted EVAs.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> planners invented the term <i>extravehicular activity</i> (abbreviated with the acronym EVA) in the early 1960s for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> to land men on the Moon, because the astronauts would leave the spacecraft to collect <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">lunar material samples</a> and deploy scientific experiments. To support this, and other Apollo objectives, the <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini program</a> was spun off to develop the capability for astronauts to work outside a two-man Earth orbiting spacecraft. However, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> was fiercely competitive in holding the early lead it had gained in manned spaceflight, so the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Soviet Communist Party</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>, ordered the conversion of its single-pilot <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">Vostok</a> capsule into a two- or three-person craft named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voskhod_spacecraft" title="Voskhod spacecraft">Voskhod</a>, in order to compete with Gemini and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(spacecraft)" title="Apollo (spacecraft)">Apollo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siddiqi_384-6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siddiqi_384-6-2">[2]</a></sup> The Soviets were able to launch two Voskhod capsules before U.S. was able to launch its first manned Gemini.\n</p><p>The Voskhod\'s <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> required cooling by cabin air to prevent overheating, therefore an <a href="/wiki/Airlock" title="Airlock">airlock</a> was required for the spacewalking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a> to exit and re-enter the cabin while it remained pressurized. By contrast, the Gemini avionics did not require air cooling, allowing the spacewalking astronaut to exit and re-enter the depressurized cabin through an open hatch. Because of this, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> space programs developed different definitions for the duration of an EVA. The Soviet (now <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a>) definition begins when the outer airlock hatch is open and the cosmonaut is in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>. An American EVA began when the astronaut had at least his head outside the spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The USA has changed its EVA definition since.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_spacewalk">First spacewalk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First spacewalk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FirstSpaceWalk.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="267" data-file-width="374" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/FirstSpaceWalk.png/220px-FirstSpaceWalk.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/47/FirstSpaceWalk.png/330px-FirstSpaceWalk.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/47/FirstSpaceWalk.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FirstSpaceWalk.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Leonov" title="Alexei Leonov">Alexei Leonov</a> performs the first spacewalk during Voskhod 2 (March 1965)</div></div></div>\n<p>The first EVA was performed on March 18, 1965, by Soviet cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/Alexey_Leonov" title="Alexey Leonov">Alexey Leonov</a>, who spent 12 minutes outside the <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_2" title="Voskhod 2">Voskhod 2</a> spacecraft. Carrying a white metal backpack containing 45 minutes worth of breathing and pressurization oxygen, Leonov had no means to control his motion other than pulling on his 15.35&#160;m (50.4&#160;ft) tether. After the flight, he claimed this was easy, but his <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">space suit</a> ballooned from its internal pressure against the vacuum of space, stiffening so much that he could not activate the shutter on his chest-mounted camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of his space walk, the suit stiffening caused a more serious problem: Leonov had to re-enter the capsule through the inflatable cloth airlock, 1.2&#160;m (3&#160;ft 11&#160;in) in diameter and 2.5&#160;m (8&#160;ft 2&#160;in) long. He improperly entered the airlock head-first and got stuck sideways. He could not get back in without reducing the pressure in his suit, risking "<a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">the bends</a>". This added another 12 minutes to his time in vacuum, and he was overheated by 1.8&#160;&#176;C (3.2&#160;&#176;F) from the exertion. It would be almost four years before the Soviets tried another EVA. They misrepresented to the press how difficult Leonov found it to work in <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a> and concealed the problems encountered until after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-First-Spacewalk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-First-Spacewalk-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Gemini">Project Gemini</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EdWhiteFirstAmericanSpacewalker.1965.ws.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3094" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="227" White</a> performs the first American spacewalk during Gemini IV (June 1965).</div></div></div>\n<p>The first American spacewalk was performed on June 3, 1965, by <a href="/wiki/Ed_White_(astronaut)" title="Ed White (astronaut)">Ed White</a> from the second manned Gemini flight, <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">Gemini 4</a>, for 21 minutes. White was tethered to the spacecraft, and his oxygen was supplied through a 25-foot (7.6&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical</a>, which also carried communications and biomedical instrumentation. He was the first to control his motion in space with a <a href="/wiki/Hand-Held_Maneuvering_Unit" title="Hand-Held Maneuvering Unit">Hand-Held Maneuvering Unit</a>, which worked well but only carried enough propellant for 20 seconds. White found his tether useful for limiting his distance from the spacecraft but difficult to use for moving around, contrary to Leonov\'s claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup> However, a defect in the capsule\'s hatch latching mechanism caused difficulties opening and closing the hatch, which delayed the start of the EVA and put White and his crewmate at risk of not getting back to Earth alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcdivittoh_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcdivittoh-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>No EVAs were planned on the next three Gemini flights. The next EVA was planned to be made by <a href="/wiki/David_Scott" title="David Scott">David Scott</a> on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_8" title="Gemini 8">Gemini 8</a>, but that mission had to be aborted due to a critical spacecraft malfunction before the EVA could be conducted. Astronauts on the next three Gemini flights (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(astronaut)" title="Michael Collins (astronaut)">Michael Collins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Richard_F._Gordon_Jr." title="Richard F. Gordon Jr.">Richard Gordon</a>), performed several EVAs, but none was able to successfully work for long periods outside the spacecraft without tiring and overheating. Cernan attempted but failed to test an Air Force <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Maneuvering_Unit" title="Astronaut Maneuvering Unit">Astronaut Maneuvering Unit</a> which included a self-contained oxygen system.\n</p><p>On November 13, 1966, <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin</a> became the first to successfully work in space without tiring, on the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_12" title="Gemini 12">Gemini 12</a> last flight. Aldrin worked outside the spacecraft for 2 hours and 6 minutes, in addition to two stand-up EVAs in the spacecraft hatch for an additional 3 hours and 24 minutes. Aldrin\'s interest in <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba diving</a> inspired the use of <a href="/wiki/Neutral_buoyancy_simulation_as_a_training_aid" title="Neutral buoyancy simulation as a training aid">underwater EVA training</a> to simulate weightlessness, which has been used ever since to allow astronauts to practice techniques of avoiding wasted muscle energy.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_EVA_crew_transfer">First EVA crew transfer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First EVA crew transfer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On January 16, 1969, Soviet cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Aleksei_Yeliseyev" title="Aleksei Yeliseyev">Aleksei Yeliseyev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Khrunov" title="Yevgeny Khrunov">Yevgeny Khrunov</a> transferred from <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_5" title="Soyuz 5">Soyuz 5</a> to <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_4" title="Soyuz 4">Soyuz 4</a>, which were docked together. This was the second Soviet EVA, and it would be almost another nine years before the Soviets performed their third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_lunar_EVA">Apollo lunar EVA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Apollo lunar EVA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3936" data-file-width="3912" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/220px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/330px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/440px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> walks on the Moon during the pioneering <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> mission in 1969</div></div></div>\n<p>American astronauts <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> performed the first EVA on the lunar surface on July 21, 1969 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>), after landing their <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Lunar Module</a> spacecraft. This first Moon walk, using self-contained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portable_life_support_system" title="Portable life support system">portable life support systems</a>, lasted 2 hours 36 minutes. A total of fifteen Moon walks were performed among six Apollo crews, including <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">Charles "Pete" Conrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bean" title="Alan Bean">Alan Bean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">Alan Shepard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Mitchell" title="Edgar Mitchell">Edgar Mitchell</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Scott" title="David Scott">David Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Irwin" title="James Irwin">James Irwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">John Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Duke" title="Charles Duke">Charles Duke</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">Harrison "Jack" Schmitt</a>. Cernan was the last Apollo astronaut to step off the surface of the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:220px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:320px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Astronaut_Charles_Duke_with_a_hammer_on_the_lunar_surface_-_pone.0006614.s003.ogv/320px--Astronaut_Charles_Duke_with_a_hammer_on_the_lunar_surface_-_pone.0006614.s003.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:320px;height:240px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="51.833333333333" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Astronaut_Charles_Duke_with_a_hammer_on_the_lunar_surface_-_pone.0006614.s003.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/1/15/Astronaut_Charles_Duke_with_a_hammer_on_the_lunar_surface_-_pone.0006614.s003.ogv/Astronaut_Charles_Duke_with_a_hammer_on_the_lunar_surface_-_pone.0006614.s003.ogv.240p.vp9.webm" type="video/webm; He was assisted by Lunar Module Pilot James Irwin standing up in the Command Module hatch. This procedure was repeated by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Mattingly" title="Ken Mattingly">Ken Mattingly</a> and Charles Duke on <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">Apollo 16</a>, and by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Evans_(astronaut)" title="Ronald Evans (astronaut)">Ronald Evans</a> and Harrison Schmitt on <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Apollo_EVAs">Post-Apollo EVAs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-Apollo EVAs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first EVA repairs of a spacecraft were made by <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">Charles "Pete" Conrad</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Kerwin" title="Joseph Kerwin">Joseph Kerwin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_J._Weitz" title="Paul J. Weitz">Paul J. Weitz</a> on May 26, June 7, and June 19, 1973, on the <a href="/wiki/Skylab_2" title="Skylab 2">Skylab 2</a> mission. They rescued the functionality of the launch-damaged <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> by freeing a stuck <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panel</a>, deploying a solar heating shield, and freeing a stuck circuit breaker relay. The Skylab 2 crew made three EVAs, and a total of ten EVAs were made by the three Skylab crews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup> They found that activities in weightlessness required about 2&#189; times longer than on Earth because many astronauts suffered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesickness" title="Spacesickness">spacesickness</a> early in their flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reusestudy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-reusestudy-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Skylab, no more EVAs were made by the United States until the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program in the early 1980s. In this period, the Soviets resumed EVAs, making four from the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">Salyut 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_7" title="Salyut 7">Salyut 7</a> space stations between December 20, 1977, and July 30, 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portree_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-portree-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When the United States resumed EVAs on April 7, 1983, astronauts started using an <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_Mobility_Unit" title="Extravehicular Mobility Unit">Extravehicular Mobility Unit</a> (EMU) for self-contained life support independent of the spacecraft. STS-6 was the first Space Shuttle mission during which a spacewalk was conducted. Also, for the first time, American astronauts used an airlock to enter and exit the spacecraft like the Soviets. Accordingly, the American definition of EVA start time was redefined to when the astronaut switches the EMU to battery power.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_EVA">Chinese EVA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chinese EVA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>China became the third country to independently carry out an EVA on September 27, 2008 during the <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_7" title="Shenzhou 7">Shenzhou 7</a> mission. Chinese astronaut <a href="/wiki/Zhai_Zhigang" title="Zhai Zhigang">Zhai Zhigang</a> completed a spacewalk wearing the Chinese-developed <a href="/wiki/Feitian_space_suit" title="Feitian space suit">Feitian space suit</a>, with astronaut <a href="/wiki/Liu_Boming_(astronaut)" title="Liu Boming (astronaut)">Liu Boming</a> wearing the Russian-derived <a href="/wiki/Orlan_space_suit" title="Orlan space suit">Orlan space suit</a> to help him. Zhai completely exited the craft, while Liu stood by at the airlock, straddling the portal.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Milestones">Milestones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Milestones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Refimprove_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Capability milestones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>The first <b>untethered spacewalk</b> was made by American <a href="/wiki/Bruce_McCandless_II" title="Bruce McCandless II">Bruce McCandless II</a> on February 7, 1984, during <i>Challenger</i> mission <a href="/wiki/STS-41-B" title="STS-41-B">STS-41-B</a>, using the <a href="/wiki/Manned_Maneuvering_Unit" title="Manned Maneuvering Unit">Manned Maneuvering Unit</a>. He was subsequently joined by <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Stewart" title="Robert L. Stewart">Robert L. Stewart</a> during the 5 hour 55 minute spacewalk. A self-contained spacewalk was first attempted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">Eugene Cernan</a> in 1966 on <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">Gemini 9A</a>, but Cernan could not reach the maneuvering unit without tiring.</li>\n<li>The first <b>metalwork in open space</b>, consisting of welding, brazing and metal spraying, was conducted by Soviet cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Svetlana_Savitskaya" title="Svetlana Savitskaya">Svetlana Savitskaya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Dzhanibekov" title="Vladimir Dzhanibekov">Vladimir Dzhanibekov</a> on July 25, 1984. A specially designed multipurpose tool was used to perform these activities during a 3-hour, 30 minute EVA outside the Salyut 7 space station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyut7Welding1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salyut7Welding1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyut7Welding2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salyut7Welding2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salyut7Welding3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salyut7Welding3-10">[10]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The first <b>three-person EVA</b> was performed on May 13, 1992, as the third EVA of  <a href="/wiki/STS-49" title="STS-49">STS-49</a>, the maiden flight of <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Endeavour" title="Space Shuttle Endeavour">Endeavour</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sts49_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sts49-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_J._Thuot" title="Pierre J. Thuot">Pierre Thuot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hieb" title="Richard Hieb">Richard Hieb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Akers" title="Thomas Akers">Thomas Akers</a> conducted the EVA to hand-capture and repair a non-functional <a href="/wiki/Intelsat" title="Intelsat">Intelsat</a> VI-F3 <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>. As of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> it was the only three-person EVA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The first EVA to perform an <b><a href="/wiki/STS-114#In-flight_repair" title="STS-114">in-flight repair of the Space Shuttle</a></b> was by American <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Robinson" title="Stephen Robinson">Steve Robinson</a> on August 3, 2005, during "Return to Flight" mission <a href="/wiki/STS-114" title="STS-114">STS-114</a>. Robinson was sent to remove two protruding gap fillers from <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a>\'s heat shield, after engineers determined there was a small chance they could affect the shuttle upon re-entry. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>NASA "spacewalkers" during the space shuttle program were designated as EV-1, EV-2, EV-3 and EV-4 (assigned to mission specialists for each mission, if applicable).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Camp-out_procedure">Camp-out procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extravehicular_activity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Camp-out procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For EVAs from the International Space Station, NASA now routinely employs a <a href="/wiki/Quest_Joint_Airlock#Camp-out_procedure" title="Quest Joint Airlock"><i>camp-out</i> procedure</a> to reduce the risk of decompression sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanner_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanner-19">[19]</a></sup> This was first tested by the Expedition 12 crew. During a camp out, astronauts sleep overnight in the <a href="/wiki/Quest_Joint_Airlock" title="Quest Joint Airlock">airlock</a> prior to an EVA, lowering the air pressure to 10.2&#160;psi (70&#160;kPa), compared to the normal station pressure of 14.7&#160;psi (101&#160;kPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanner_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-tanner-19">[19]</a></sup> Spending a night at the lower air pressure helps flush nitrogen from the body, thereby preventing "<a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">the bends</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> More recently astronauts have been using the In-Suit Light Exercise protocol rather than camp-out to prevent decompression sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
