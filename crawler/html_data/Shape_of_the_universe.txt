universe</b> is the <a href="#Curvature_of_the_universe">local</a> and <a href="#Global_universe_structure">global geometry</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. The local features of the geometry of the universe are primarily described by its <a href="/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds" title="Curvature of Riemannian manifolds">curvature</a>, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Spacetime_topology" title="Spacetime topology">topology</a> of the universe describes general global properties of its shape as of a continuous object. The shape of the universe is related to <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, which describes how <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> is curved and bent by mass and energy.\n</p><p>Cosmologists distinguish between the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a> and the global universe. The observable universe consists of the part of the universe that can, in principle, be observed by light reaching Earth within the age of the universe. It encompasses a region of space that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">currently</a> forms a ball centered at Earth of estimated radius 46.5&#160;billion light-years (4.40<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">&#215;</span>10<sup>26</sup>&#160;m). This does not mean the universe is 46.5 billion years old; in fact, the universe <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">is 13.8 billion years old</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe" title="Expansion of the universe">space itself has also expanded</a>, causing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Size_of_the_universe" title="Size of the universe">size of the observable universe</a> to be as stated. (However, it is possible to observe these distant areas only in their very distant past, when the distance light had to travel was much less). Assuming an <a href="/wiki/Isotropy#Physics" title="Isotropy">isotropic</a> nature, the observable universe is similar for all contemporary vantage points.\n</p><p>The global shape of the universe can be described with three attributes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>Finite or infinite</li>\n<li>Flat (no <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a>), open (negative curvature), or closed (positive curvature)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Connected_space" title="Connected space">Connectivity</a>, how the universe is put together, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Simply_connected_space" title="Simply connected space">simply connected space</a> or multiply connected.</li></ol>\n<p>There are certain logical connections among these properties. For example, a universe with positive curvature is necessarily finite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis98_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis98-2">[2]</a></sup> Although it is usually assumed in the literature that a flat or negatively curved universe is infinite, this need not be the case if the topology is not the trivial one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis98_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis98-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The exact shape is still a matter of debate in <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>, but experimental data from various independent sources (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMAP" title="WMAP">WMAP</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BOOMERanG" title="BOOMERanG">BOOMERanG</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck</a> for example) confirm that the observable universe is flat with only a 0.4% margin of error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Shape_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Shape-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fermi_Flat_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fermi_Flat-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Theorists have been trying to construct a formal mathematical model of the shape of the universe. In formal terms, this is a <a href="/wiki/3-manifold" title="3-manifold">3-manifold</a> model corresponding to the spatial section (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_coordinates" title="Comoving coordinates">comoving coordinates</a>) of the 4-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">spacetime</a> of the universe. The model most theorists currently use is the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker metric">Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker</a> (FLRW) model. Arguments have been put forward that the observational data best fit with the conclusion that the shape of the global universe is infinite and flat,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> but the data are also consistent with other possible shapes, such as the so-called <a href="/wiki/Homology_sphere#Cosmology" title="Homology sphere">Poincar&#233; dodecahedral space</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat03_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nat03-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBSG08_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RBSG08-8">[8]</a></sup> and the Sokolov&#8211;Starobinskii space (quotient of the <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_half-plane_model" title="Poincar&#233; half-plane model">upper half-space model</a> of hyperbolic space by 2-dimensional lattice).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aurich0403597_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aurich0403597-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape_of_the_observable_universe">Shape of the observable universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shape of the observable universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">Observable universe</a></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Distance_measures_(cosmology)" title="Distance measures (cosmology)">Distance measures (cosmology)</a></div>\n<p>As stated in the introduction, there are two aspects to consider:\n</p>\n<ol><li>its <i>local</i> geometry, which predominantly concerns the curvature of the universe, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Observable_universe" title="Observable universe">observable universe</a>, and</li>\n<li>its <i>global</i> geometry, which concerns the topology of the universe as a whole.</li></ol>\n<p>The observable universe can be thought of as a sphere that extends outwards from any observation point for 46.5 billion light years, going farther back in time and more <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshifted</a> the more distant away one looks. Ideally, one can continue to look back all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a>; in practice, however, the farthest away one can look using light and other <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">cosmic microwave background</a> (CMB), as anything past that was opaque. Experimental investigations show that the observable universe is very close to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homogeneous" title="Homogeneous">homogeneous</a>.\n</p><p>If the observable universe encompasses the entire universe, we may be able to determine the structure of the entire universe by observation. However, if the observable universe is smaller than the entire universe, our observations will be limited to only a part of the whole, and we may not be able to determine its global geometry through measurement. From experiments, it is possible to construct different mathematical models of the global geometry of the entire universe all of which are consistent with current observational data and so it is currently unknown whether the observable universe is identical to the global universe or it is instead many orders of magnitude smaller than it. The universe may be small in some dimensions and not in others (analogous to the way a <a href="/wiki/Cuboid" title="Cuboid">cuboid</a> is longer in the dimension of length than it is in the dimensions of width and depth). To test whether a given mathematical model describes the universe accurately, scientists look for the model\'s novel implications&#8212;what are some phenomena in the universe that we have not yet observed, but that must exist if the model is correct&#8212;and they devise experiments to test whether those phenomena occur or not. For example, if the universe is a small closed loop, one would expect to see multiple images of an object in the sky, although not necessarily images of the same age.\n</p><p>Cosmologists normally work with a given <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space-like" title="Space-like">space-like</a> slice of spacetime called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comoving_distance" title="Comoving distance">comoving coordinates</a>, the existence of a preferred set of which is possible and widely accepted in present-day physical cosmology. The section of spacetime that can be observed is the backward <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">light cone</a> (all points within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_light_horizon" title="Cosmic light horizon">cosmic light horizon</a>, given time to reach a given observer), while the related term <a href="/wiki/Hubble_volume" title="Hubble volume">Hubble volume</a> can be used to describe either the past light cone or comoving space up to the surface of last scattering. To speak of "the shape of the universe (at a point in time)" is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontological" title="Ontological">ontologically</a> naive from the point of view of <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> alone: due to the <a href="/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity" title="Relativity of simultaneity">relativity of simultaneity</a> we cannot speak of different points in space as being "at the same point in time" nor, therefore, of "the shape of the universe at a point in time". However, the comoving coordinates (if well-defined) provide a strict sense to those by using the time since the Big Bang (measured in the reference of CMB) as a distinguished universal time.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curvature_of_the_universe">Curvature of the universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Curvature of the universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Curvature#Space" title="Curvature">Curvature &#167;&#160;Space</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds" title="Curvature of Riemannian manifolds">curvature</a> is a quantity describing how the geometry of a space differs locally from the one of the <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">flat space</a>. The curvature of any locally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic_space" title="Isotropic space">isotropic space</a> (and hence of a locally isotropic universe) falls into one of the three following cases:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Zero curvature (flat); a drawn triangle\'s angles add up to 180&#176; and the <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean theorem</a> holds; such 3-dimensional space is locally modeled by <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a> <span class="texhtml"><b>E</b><sup><i>3</i></sup></span>.</li>\n<li>Positive curvature; a drawn triangle\'s angles add up to more than 180&#176;; such 3-dimensional space is locally modeled by a region of a <a href="/wiki/N-sphere" title="N-sphere">3-sphere</a> <span class="texhtml"><b>S</b><sup><i>3</i></sup></span>.</li>\n<li>Negative curvature; a drawn triangle\'s angles add up to less than 180&#176;; such 3-dimensional space is locally modeled by a region of a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_space" title="Hyperbolic space">hyperbolic space</a> <span class="texhtml"><b>H</b><sup><i>3</i></sup></span>.</li></ol>\n<p>Curved geometries are in the domain of <a href="/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry" title="Non-Euclidean geometry">Non-Euclidean geometry</a>. An example of a positively curved space would be the surface of a sphere such as the Earth. A triangle drawn from the equator to a pole will have at least two angles equal 90&#176;, which makes the sum of the 3 angles greater than 180&#176;. An example of a negatively curved surface would be the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Saddle" title="Saddle">saddle</a> or mountain pass. A triangle drawn on a saddle surface will have the sum of the angles adding up to less than 180&#176;.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:End_of_universe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="501" data-file-width="557" decoding="async" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/End_of_universe.jpg/275px-End_of_universe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/End_of_universe.jpg/413px-End_of_universe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/End_of_universe.jpg/550px-End_of_universe.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:End_of_universe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The local geometry of the universe is determined by whether the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Density_parameter#Density_parameter" title="Density parameter">density parameter <span class="texhtml">&#937;</span></a> is greater than, less than, or equal to 1.<br/> From top to bottom: a <a href="/wiki/Spherical_geometry" title="Spherical geometry">spherical universe</a> with <span class="texhtml">&#937; &gt; 1</span>, a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">hyperbolic universe</a> with <span class="texhtml">&#937; &lt; 1</span>, and a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">flat universe</a> with <span class="texhtml">&#937; = 1</span>. These depictions of two-dimensional surfaces are merely easily visualizable analogs to the 3-dimensional structure of (local) space.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">General relativity</a> explains that mass and energy bend the curvature of spacetime and is used to determine what curvature the universe has by using a value called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Density_parameter" title="Density parameter">density parameter</a>, represented with Omega (<span class="texhtml">&#937;</span>). The density parameter is the average density of the universe divided by the critical energy density, that is, the mass energy needed for a universe to be flat. Put another way,\n</p>\n<ul><li>If <span class="texhtml">&#937; = 1</span>, the universe is flat</li>\n<li>If <span class="texhtml">&#937; &gt; 1</span>, there is positive curvature</li>\n<li>if <span class="texhtml">&#937; &lt; 1</span> there is negative curvature</li></ul>\n<p>One can experimentally calculate this <span class="texhtml">&#937;</span> to determine the curvature two ways. One is to count up all the mass-energy in the universe and take its average density then divide that average by the critical energy density. Data from <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe" title="Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe">Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe</a> (WMAP) as well as the <a href="/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)" title="Planck (spacecraft)">Planck spacecraft</a> give values for the three constituents of all the mass-energy in the universe &#8211; normal mass (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baryonic_matter" title="Baryonic matter">baryonic matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>), relativistic particles (<a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>&#937;<sub>mass</sub> &#8776; 0.315&#177;0.018\n</p><p>&#937;<sub>relativistic</sub> &#8776; 9.24&#215;10<sup>&#8722;5</sup>\n</p><p>&#937;<sub>&#923;</sub> &#8776; 0.6817&#177;0.0018\n</p><p>&#937;<sub>total</sub>=  &#937;<sub>mass</sub> + &#937;<sub>relativistic</sub> + &#937;<sub>&#923;</sub>=  1.00&#177;0.02\n</p><p>The actual value for critical density value is measured as &#961;<sub>critical</sub>=  9.47&#215;10<sup>&#8722;27</sup> kg m<sup>&#8722;3</sup>. From these values, within experimental error, the universe seems to be flat.\n</p><p>Another way to measure &#937; is to do so geometrically by measuring an angle across the observable universe. We can do this by using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CMB" title="CMB">CMB</a> and measuring the power spectrum and temperature anisotropy. For an intuition, one can imagine finding a gas cloud that is not in thermal equilibrium due to being so large that light speed cannot propagate the thermal information. Knowing this propagation speed, we then know the size of the gas cloud as well as the distance to the gas cloud, we then have two sides of a triangle and can then determine the angles. Using a method similar to this, the <a href="/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment" title="BOOMERanG experiment">BOOMERanG experiment</a> has determined that the sum of the angles to 180&#176; within experimental error, corresponding to an &#937;<sub>total</sub> &#8776; 1.00&#177;0.12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These and other astronomical measurements constrain the spatial curvature to be very close to zero, although they do not constrain its sign. This means that although the local geometries of spacetime are generated by the theory of relativity based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacetime_interval" title="Spacetime interval">spacetime intervals</a>, we can approximate <i>3-space</i> by the familiar <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker metric">Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker (FLRW) model</a> using <a href="/wiki/Friedmann_equations" title="Friedmann equations">Friedmann equations</a> is commonly used to model the universe. The FLRW model provides a curvature of the universe based on the mathematics of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, that is, modeling the matter within the universe as a perfect fluid. Although stars and structures of mass can be introduced into an "almost FLRW" model, a strictly FLRW model is used to approximate the local geometry of the observable universe. Another way of saying this is that if all forms of <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">dark energy</a> are ignored, then the curvature of the universe can be determined by measuring the average density of matter within it, assuming that all matter is evenly distributed (rather than the distortions caused by \'dense\' objects such as galaxies). This assumption is justified by the observations that, while the universe is "weakly" <a href="/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics)" title="Homogeneity (physics)">inhomogeneous</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anisotropic" title="Anisotropic">anisotropic</a> (see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos" title="Large-scale structure of the cosmos">large-scale structure of the cosmos</a>), it is on average homogeneous and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_universe_structure">Global universe structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global universe structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Global structure covers the <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a> of the whole universe&#8212;both the observable universe and beyond. While the local geometry does not determine the global geometry completely, it does limit the possibilities, particularly a geometry of a constant curvature. The universe is often taken to be a <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_manifold" title="Geodesic manifold">geodesic manifold</a>, free of <a href="/wiki/Topological_defect#Cosmological_defects" title="Topological defect">topological defects</a>; relaxing either of these complicates the analysis considerably. A global geometry is a local geometry plus a topology. It follows that a topology alone does not give a global geometry: for instance, Euclidean 3-space and <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">hyperbolic 3-space</a> have the same topology but different global geometries.\n</p><p>As stated in the introduction, investigations within the study of the global structure of the universe include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Whether the universe is <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinite</a> or finite in extent</li>\n<li>Whether the geometry of the global universe is flat, positively curved, or negatively curved</li>\n<li>Whether the topology is <a href="/wiki/Simply_connected_space" title="Simply connected space">simply connected</a> like a sphere or multiply connected, like a torus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infinite_or_finite">Infinite or finite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Infinite or finite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One of the presently unanswered questions about the universe is whether it is infinite or finite in extent. For intuition, it can be understood that a finite universe has a finite volume that, for example, could be in theory filled up with a finite amount of material, while an infinite universe is unbounded and no numerical volume could possibly fill it. Mathematically, the question of whether the universe is infinite or finite is referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bounded_metric_space" title="Bounded metric space">boundedness</a>. An infinite universe (unbounded metric space) means that there are points <i>arbitrarily</i> far apart: for any distance <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">d</span>, there are points that are of a distance at least <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">d</span> apart. A finite universe is a bounded metric space, where there is some distance <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">d</span> such that all points are within distance <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">d</span> of each other. The smallest such <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">d</span> is called the diameter of the universe, in which case the universe has a well-defined "volume" or "scale."\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="With_or_without_boundary">With or without boundary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: With or without boundary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Assuming a finite universe, the universe can either have an edge or no edge. Many finite mathematical spaces, e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Disk_(mathematics)" title="Disk (mathematics)">disc</a>, have an edge or boundary. Spaces that have an edge are difficult to treat, both conceptually and mathematically. Namely, it is very difficult to state what would happen at the edge of such a universe. For this reason, spaces that have an edge are typically excluded from consideration.\n</p><p>However, there exist many finite spaces, such as the <a href="/wiki/3-sphere" title="3-sphere">3-sphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Three-torus" title="Three-torus">3-torus</a>, which have no edges. Mathematically, these spaces are referred to as being <a href="/wiki/Compact_space" title="Compact space">compact</a> without boundary. The term compact basically means that it is finite in extent ("bounded") and <a href="/wiki/Complete_metric_space" title="Complete metric space">complete</a>. The term "without boundary" means that the space has no edges. Moreover, so that calculus can be applied, the universe is typically assumed to be a <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">differentiable manifold</a>. A mathematical object that possesses all these properties, compact without boundary and differentiable, is termed a <a href="/wiki/Closed_manifold" title="Closed manifold">closed manifold</a>. The 3-sphere and 3-torus are both closed manifolds.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curvature">Curvature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Curvature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The curvature of the universe places constraints on the topology. If the spatial geometry is <a href="/wiki/Spherical_3-manifold" title="Spherical 3-manifold">spherical</a>, i.e., possess positive curvature, the topology is compact. For a flat (zero curvature) or a hyperbolic (negative curvature) spatial geometry, the topology can be either compact or infinite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luminet1995_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luminet1995-14">[14]</a></sup> Many textbooks erroneously state that a flat universe implies an infinite universe; however, the correct statement is that a flat universe that is also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simply_connected" title="Simply connected">simply connected</a> implies an infinite universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luminet1995_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luminet1995-14">[14]</a></sup> For example, Euclidean space is flat, simply connected, and infinite, but the <a href="/wiki/Torus#Flat_torus" title="Torus">torus</a> is flat, multiply connected, finite, and compact.\n</p><p>In general, <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_geometry#Local_to_global_theorems" title="Riemannian geometry">local to global theorems</a> in <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_geometry" title="Riemannian geometry">Riemannian geometry</a> relate the local geometry to the global geometry. If the local geometry has constant curvature, the global geometry is very constrained, as described in <a href="/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture" title="Geometrization conjecture">Thurston geometries</a>.\n</p><p>The latest research shows that even the most powerful future experiments (like SKA, Planck..) will not be able to distinguish between flat, open and closed universe if the true value of cosmological curvature parameter is smaller than 10<sup>&#8722;4</sup>. If the true value of the cosmological curvature parameter is larger than 10<sup>&#8722;3</sup> we will be able to distinguish between these three models even now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Results of the <i>Planck</i> mission released in 2015 show the cosmological curvature parameter, &#937;<sub><i>K</i></sub>, to be 0.000&#177;0.005, consistent with a flat universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe_with_zero_curvature"><span id="Flat_universe"></span> Universe with zero curvature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Universe with zero curvature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In a universe with zero curvature, the local geometry is <a href="/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture#Euclidean_geometry_E3" title="Geometrization conjecture">flat</a>. The most obvious global structure is that of <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>, which is infinite in extent. Flat universes that are finite in extent include the <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Klein_bottle" title="Klein bottle">Klein bottle</a>. Moreover, in three dimensions, there are 10 finite closed flat 3-manifolds, of which 6 are orientable and 4 are non-orientable. These are the <a href="/wiki/Flat_manifold" title="Flat manifold">Bieberbach manifolds</a>. The most familiar is the aforementioned <a href="/wiki/Three-torus_model_of_the_universe" title="Three-torus model of the universe">3-torus universe</a>.\n</p><p>In the absence of dark energy, a flat universe expands forever but at a continually decelerating rate, with expansion asymptotically approaching zero. With dark energy, the expansion rate of the universe initially slows down, due to the effect of gravity, but eventually increases. The <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe" title="Ultimate fate of the universe">ultimate fate of the universe</a> is the same as that of an open universe.\n</p><p>A flat universe can have <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_universe" title="Zero-energy universe">zero total energy</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe_with_positive_curvature">Universe with positive curvature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Universe with positive curvature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Universecolor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="220" href="/wiki/File:Universecolor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Universe in an expanding sphere.</i> The galaxies farthest away are moving fastest and hence experience length contraction and so become smaller to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Center_of_the_Universe" title="History of the Center of the Universe">observer in the centre</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>A positively curved universe is described by <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_geometry" title="Elliptic geometry">elliptic geometry</a>, and can be thought of as a three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Hypersphere" title="Hypersphere">hypersphere</a>, or some other <a href="/wiki/Spherical_3-manifold" title="Spherical 3-manifold">spherical 3-manifold</a> (such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homology_sphere#Cosmology" title="Homology sphere">Poincar&#233; dodecahedral space</a>, a positively curved space, colloquially described as "soccerball-shaped", as it is the quotient of the 3-sphere by the <a href="/wiki/Binary_icosahedral_group" title="Binary icosahedral group">binary icosahedral group</a>, which is very close to <a href="/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry" This was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Luminet" title="Jean-Pierre Luminet">Jean-Pierre Luminet</a> and colleagues in 2003<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat03_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nat03-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physwebLum03_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-physwebLum03-17">[17]</a></sup> and an optimal orientation on the sky for the model was estimated in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RBSG08_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-RBSG08-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe_with_negative_curvature">Universe with negative curvature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Universe with negative curvature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A hyperbolic universe, one of a negative spatial curvature, is described by <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry" title="Hyperbolic geometry">hyperbolic geometry</a>, and can be thought of locally as a three-dimensional analog of an infinitely extended saddle shape. There are a great variety of <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_3-manifold" title="Hyperbolic 3-manifold">hyperbolic 3-manifolds</a>, and their classification is not completely understood. Those of finite volume can be understood via the <a href="/wiki/Mostow_rigidity_theorem" title="Mostow rigidity theorem">Mostow rigidity theorem</a>. For hyperbolic local geometry, many of the possible three-dimensional spaces are informally called "horn topologies", so called because of the shape of the <a href="/wiki/Pseudosphere" title="Pseudosphere">pseudosphere</a>, a canonical model of hyperbolic geometry. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Picard_horn" title="Picard horn">Picard horn</a>, a negatively curved space, colloquially described as "funnel-shaped".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aurich0403597_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aurich0403597-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Curvature:_open_or_closed">Curvature: open or closed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Curvature: open or closed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>When cosmologists speak of the universe as being "open" or "closed", they most commonly are referring to whether the curvature is negative or positive. These meanings of open and closed are different from the mathematical meaning of open and closed used for sets in topological spaces and for the mathematical meaning of open and closed manifolds, which gives rise to ambiguity and confusion. In mathematics, there are definitions for a <a href="/wiki/Closed_manifold" title="Closed manifold">closed manifold</a> (i.e., compact without boundary) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_manifold" title="Open manifold">open manifold</a> (i.e., one that is not compact and without boundary). A "closed universe" is necessarily a closed manifold. An "open universe" can be either a closed or open manifold. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker metric">Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker</a> (FLRW) model the universe is considered to be without boundaries, in which case "compact universe" could describe a universe that is a closed manifold.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Milne_model_.28.22spherical.22_expanding.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'Milne_model_("spherical"_expanding)\'>Milne model ("spherical" expanding)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title=\'Edit section: Milne model ("spherical" expanding)\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milne_model" title="Milne model">Milne model</a></div>\n<p>If one applies <a href="/wiki/Minkowski_space" title="Minkowski space">Minkowski space</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> to expansion of the universe, without resorting to the concept of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curved_spacetime" title="Curved spacetime">curved spacetime</a>, then one obtains the Milne model. Any spatial section of the universe of a constant age (the <a href="/wiki/Proper_time" title="Proper time">proper time</a> elapsed from the Big Bang) will have a negative curvature; this is merely a <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space" title="Pseudo-Euclidean space">pseudo-Euclidean</a> geometric fact analogous to one that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentric" title="Concentric">concentric</a> spheres in the <i>flat</i> <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a> are nevertheless curved.\nSpatial geometry of this model is an unbounded <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_space" title="Hyperbolic space">hyperbolic space</a>.\nThe entire universe is contained within a <a href="/wiki/Light_cone" title="Light cone">light cone</a>, namely the future cone of the Big Bang. For any given moment <span class="texhtml"><i>t</i>&gt; 0</span> of <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_time" title="Coordinate time">coordinate time</a> (assuming the Big Bang has <span class="texhtml"><i>t</i> = 0</span>), the entire universe is bounded by a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> of radius exactly <span class="texhtml"><i><a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">c</a>&#8201;t</i></span>.\nThe apparent paradox of an infinite universe contained within a sphere is explained with <a href="/wiki/Length_contraction" title="Length contraction">length contraction</a>: the galaxies farther away, which are travelling away from the observer the fastest, will appear thinner.\n</p><p>This model is essentially a <a href="/wiki/Degeneracy_(mathematics)" title="Degeneracy (mathematics)">degenerate</a> FLRW for <span class="texhtml">&#937; = 0</span>. It is incompatible with observations that definitely rule out such a large negative spatial curvature. However, as a background in which gravitational fields (or gravitons) can operate, due to diffeomorphism invariance, the space on the macroscopic scale, is equivalent to any other (open) solution of Einstein\'s field equations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shape_of_the_universe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
