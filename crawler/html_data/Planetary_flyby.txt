1974.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>planetary flyby</b> is the act of sending a <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">space probe</a> past a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a> close enough to record scientific data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This is a subset of the overall concept of a <a href="/wiki/Flyby_(spaceflight)" title="Flyby (spaceflight)">flyby</a> in spaceflight.\n</p><p>Flybys commonly use <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">gravity assists</a> to "slingshot" a space probe on its journey to its primary objective, but may themselves be used as primary means.\n</p><p>The first flyby of another planet with a functioning spacecraft took place on December 14, 1962, when <a href="/wiki/Mariner_2" title="Mariner 2">Mariner 2</a> zoomed by the planet <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">New Horizons</a></i> performed flyby maneuvers of Jupiter, Pluto and its moons in the 21st century. This type of maneuver allowed it to reach Pluto at high velocity without the complications of slowing down, after which it proceeded into the Kuiper belt on a solar system escape trajectory. \n</p><p>For comparison, New Horizons reached Jupiter in just over year (launched in January 2006) it flewby in February 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In contrast, Galileo spacecraft took about six years (launched 1989, arrive at Jupiter 1995) and when it got there it had expend fuel to slow down to enter orbit, and was overall much heavier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The atmospheric entry probe did not slow down but required an advanced entry shield and the atmosphere of Jupiter to slow down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (See also <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">Aerobraking</a>)\n</p><p>There also an even closer version of flyby, a sort of fly in where the spacecraft actually enters and passes through a bodies atmosphere, called a <a href="/wiki/Aerogravity_assist" title="Aerogravity assist">Aerogravity assist</a>.
