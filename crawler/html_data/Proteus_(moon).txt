class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'p\' in \'pie\'">p</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/o&#650;/: \'o\' in \'code\'">o&#650;</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="/i&#720;/: \'ee\' in \'fleece\'">i&#720;</span><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">PROH</span>-tee-&#601;s</i></a>; Greek: <i>&#928;&#961;&#969;&#964;&#949;&#973;&#962;</i>), also known as <b>Neptune&#160;VIII</b>, is the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptunian</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moon</a>, and Neptune\'s largest <a href="/wiki/Inner_moon" title="Inner moon">inner satellite</a>. Discovered by <i><a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a></i> spacecraft in 1989, it is named after <a href="/wiki/Proteus" title="Proteus">Proteus</a>, the shape-changing sea god of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. Proteus orbits Neptune in a nearly equatorial orbit at the distance of about 4.75 equatorial radii of the planet.\n</p><p>Despite being a predominantly icy body more than 400&#160;km in diameter, Proteus\'s shape deviates significantly from an <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a>. It is shaped more like an irregular <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> with several slightly concave facets and relief as high as 20&#160;km. Its surface is dark, neutral in color, and heavily cratered. Proteus\'s largest crater is Pharos, which is more than 230&#160;km in diameter. There are also a number of <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">scarps</a>, grooves, and <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a> related to large craters.\n</p><p>Proteus is probably not an original body that formed with Neptune. It received the temporary designation <b>S/1989&#160;N&#160;1</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithSoderblom1989_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SmithSoderblom1989-11">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_P._Synnott" title="Stephen P. Synnott">Stephen P. Synnott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bradford_A._Smith" title="Bradford A. Smith">Bradford A. Smith</a> announced its discovery on July 7, 1989, speaking only of "17 frames taken over 21 days", which gives a discovery date of sometime before June 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_4806_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC_4806-12">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 16 September 1991 S/1989&#160;N&#160;1 was named after <a href="/wiki/Proteus" title="Proteus">Proteus</a>, the shape-changing sea god of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_5347_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAUC_5347-13">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Proteus orbits Neptune at the distance approximately equal to 4.75 equatorial radii of the planet. Its orbit has a small <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> and is inclined by about 0.5&#176; to the planet\'s equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson2004_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Proteus is the largest of the regular prograde satellites of Neptune. It rotates <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">synchronously</a> with the orbital motion, which means that one face always points to the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proteus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Proteus is the second-largest moon of Neptune. It is about 420 kilometres in diameter, larger than <a href="/wiki/Nereid_(moon)" title="Nereid (moon)">Nereid</a>, the second to be discovered. It was not discovered by <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>-based telescopes because it is so close to Neptune that it is lost in the glare of reflected sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithSoderblom1989_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SmithSoderblom1989-11">[7]</a></sup> The surface of Proteus is dark&#8212;its <a href="/wiki/Geometric_albedo" title="Geometric albedo">geometrical albedo</a> is about 10%. The color of its surface is neutral as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> does not change appreciably with the wavelength from violet to green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithSoderblom1989_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-SmithSoderblom1989-11">[7]</a></sup> In the near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> around 2&#160;&#956;m Proteus\'s surface becomes less reflective, pointing to a possible presence of complex organic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanides</a>. These compounds may be responsible for the low albedo of the inner Neptunian moons. Although Proteus is usually thought to contain significant amounts of water ice, it has not been detected spectroscopically on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DumasSmithTerrile2003_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DumasSmithTerrile2003-14">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The shape of Proteus is close to a sphere with a radius of about 210&#160;km, although deviations from the spherical shape are large&#8212;up to 20&#160;km; scientists believe it is about as large as a body of its density can be without being pulled into a perfect <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_satellites" title="List of natural satellites">spherical shape</a> by its own <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1992_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Croft1992-4">[4]</a></sup> Saturn\'s moon <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">Mimas</a> has an <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoidal</a> shape despite being slightly less massive than Proteus, perhaps due to the higher temperature near Saturn or tidal heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1992_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Croft1992-4">[4]</a></sup> Proteus is slightly elongated in the direction of Neptune, although its overall shape is closer to an irregular <a href="/wiki/Polyhedron" title="Polyhedron">polyhedron</a> than to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triaxial_ellipsoid" title="Triaxial ellipsoid">triaxial ellipsoid</a>. The surface of Proteus shows several flat or slightly concave facets measuring from 150 to 200&#160;km in diameter. They are probably degraded <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact craters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Proteus is heavily cratered, showing no sign of any geological modification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SmithSoderblom1989_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-SmithSoderblom1989-11">[7]</a></sup> The largest crater, Pharos, has a diameter from <span class="nowrap">230 to 260 km</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1992_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Croft1992-4">[4]</a></sup> Its depth is about 10&#8211;15&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup> The crater has a central dome on its floor a few kilometres high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup> Pharos is the only named surface feature on this moon: the name is <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> and refers to the <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse_of_Alexandria" title="Lighthouse of Alexandria">island</a> where Proteus reigned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgsPharos_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgsPharos-15">[11]</a></sup> In addition to Pharos there are several craters 50&#8211;100&#160;km in diameter and many more with diameters less than 50&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The second <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landform</a> found on Proteus is linear features such as <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">scarps</a>, valleys, and grooves. The most prominent one runs parallel to the equator to the west of Pharos. These features likely formed as a result of the giant impacts, which formed Pharos and other large craters or as a result of tidal stresses from Neptune.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1992_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Croft1992-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proteus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Proteus, like the other inner satellites of Neptune, is unlikely to be an original body that formed with it, and is more likely to have accreted from the rubble that was produced after Triton\'s capture. Triton\'s orbit upon capture would have been highly eccentric, and would have caused chaotic perturbations in the orbits of the original inner Neptunian satellites, causing them to collide and reduce to a disc of rubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldreich1989_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldreich1989-16">[12]</a></sup> Only after Triton\'s orbit became circularised did some of the rubble disc re-accrete into the present-day satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BanfieldMurray1992_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-BanfieldMurray1992-17">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-earlier_papers-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a Thomas and Veverka in 1991 reported 440&#215;416&#215;404&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2008-nssdc_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2008-nssdc-3">[3]</a></sup> Croft in 1992 reported 430&#215;424&#215;410&#160;km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft1992_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Croft1992-4">[4]</a></sup> The difference is caused by the use of different sets of images and by the fact that the shape of Proteus is not described well by a triaxial ellipsoid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup> </span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-mass_calc-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-mass_calc_7-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"> The mass was calculated by multiplying the volume from Stooke (1994)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stooke1994_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stooke1994-2">[2]</a></sup> by the assumed density of 1,300&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup>.
