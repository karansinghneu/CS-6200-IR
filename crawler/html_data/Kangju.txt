href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#24247;&#23621;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Kangju</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">K\'ang-ch&#252;</span></i>) was the Chinese name of an ancient kingdom in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> which became for a couple of centuries the second greatest power in <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Its people, the K&#257;ng (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#24247;</span>) were an <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European</a> semi-nomadic people probably identical to the <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdians" title="Sogdians">Sogdians</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinor174_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinor174-2">[2]</a></sup> or other Iranian groups closely related to them, such as the <a href="/wiki/Asii" title="Asii">Asii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGolden199253_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGolden199253-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="toclevel-2 tocsection-9"><a href="#Sources"><span class="tocnumber">5.2</span> <span class="toctext">Sources</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<blockquote>\n<p>"Kangju (W-G: K\'ang-ch&#252;) &#24247;&#23621; = <b>the Talas basin, Tashkent and Sogdiana</b>. It is not clear whether the Chinese name &#24247;&#23621; Kangju was intended to transcribe an ethnic name, or to be descriptive, or both. &#23621; <i>ju</i> can mean: \'seat\', \'central place of activity or authority; \'to settle down,\' \'residence,\' or \'to occupy (militarily).\'...  The term, therefore, could simply mean "the abode of the Kang," or "territory occupied by the Kang." ...  As <i>kang</i> &#24247; means \'well-being\', \'peaceful,\' \'happy;\' \'settle\', \'stability,\' Kangju can be translated as the \'Peaceful Land,\' or \'Abode of the Peaceful (People).\' ... Even if the name Kangju was originally an attempt to transcribe the sounds of a foreign name, it would still have carried the sense of a peaceful place to Chinese speakers, and the name \'Kang\' would have had overtones of a peaceful people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Kangju was referred to as the State of Kang (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">&#24247;&#22269;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">&#24247;&#22283;</span>) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">Sui</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang</a> dynasties, though by that time the area was ruled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G%C3%B6kt%C3%BCrk" title="G&#246;kt&#252;rk">G&#246;kt&#252;rk</a> Khaganate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Canadian sinologist <a href="/wiki/Edwin_G._Pulleyblank" title="Edwin G. Pulleyblank">Edwin Pulleyblank</a> linked Kangju to the <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian A</a> work <i>k&#257;&#7749;ka-</i>, probably meaning "stone" and proposed that the Kangju were originally <a href="/wiki/Tocharians" title="Tocharians">Tocharians</a> who had migrated westward into Sogdia and established themselves in &#268;a&#269; (modern <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a>). Pulleyblank also suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Jie_people" title="Jie people">Ji&#233;</a> (&#32687;) tribe Qi&#257;ngq&#250; (&#32652;&#28192;) might be Kangju people who had been incorporated into the Xiongnu tribal confederation. Pulleyblank further connected Kangju to K&#224;nji&#233; &#30640;&#32687; (*<i>Kamkar</i>?) and the name <i>Kankar</i> given to the lower <a href="/wiki/Syr_Darya" title="Syr Darya">Yaxartes</a> by Persian geographer <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khordadbeh" title="Ibn Khordadbeh">ibn Khordadbeh</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pulleyblank_(1963)_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pulleyblank_(1963)-6">[6]</a></sup> Orientalists <a href="/wiki/Josef_Markwart" title="Josef Markwart">Marquart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omeljan_Pritsak" title="Omeljan Pritsak">Pritsak</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_B._Golden" title="Peter B. Golden">Golden</a> also noted phonetic similarities between Kangju and <b>Kengeres</b> mentioned in the Orkhon isncriptions, the <b>Kangar&#226;y&#234;</b> in Transcaucasia, the city of <b>Keng&#252; Tarban</b>, and the three <a href="/wiki/Pechenegs" title="Pechenegs">Pecheneg</a> tribes collectively known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kangar_Union" title="Kangar Union">Kangar</a> mentioned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_VII_Porphyrogenitus" title="Constantine VII Porphyrogenitus">Constantine VII Porphyrogenitus</a>. Nevertheless, all those connections remain hypothetical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGolden1992264-265_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGolden1992264-265-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdia</a></div>\n<p>According to 2nd century BC <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> sources, Kangju lay north of the <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> and west of the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a>, bordering the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> in the south. Their territory covered the region of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana_Valley" title="Ferghana Valley">Ferghana Valley</a> and the area between the <a href="/wiki/Amu_Darya" title="Amu Darya">Amu Darya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syr_Darya" title="Syr Darya">Syr Darya</a> rivers, with the core territory along the middle Syr Darya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Since historians of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> do not mention the existence of any political power in the area except the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khwarezm" title="Khwarezm">Khwarezmians</a>, the Kangju must have appeared a little later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> It is likely that the state of the Kangju emerged during the great upheaval in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> following the withdrawal of the <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> from <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> and then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ili_Valley" title="Ili Valley">Ili Valley</a> after their defeat by the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> and Wusun respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Chinese sources state that the Kangju were tributiaries of the Yuezhi in the south and the Xiongnu in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="968" decoding="async" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png/250px-Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png/375px-Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png/500px-Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coin_of_Vanvan_of_Chach.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kangju coin: obverse: ruler Vanvan of Chach; reverse: Kangju tamga</div></div></div>\n<p>Kangju was mentioned by the Chinese traveller and diplomat <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> who visited the area c. 128 BCE, whose travels are documented in Chapter 123 of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i> (whose author, <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>, died c. 90 BC):\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>"Kangju is situated some 2,000 li [832 kilometers] northwest of Dayuan. Its people are nomads and resemble the Yuezhi in their customs. They have 80,000 or 90,000 skilled archers. The country is small, and borders Dayuan (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>). It acknowledges sovereignty to the Yuezhi people in the South and the Xiongnu in the East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson234_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson234-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Qian also visited a land known to the Chinese as <a href="/wiki/Yancai" title="Yancai">Yancai</a> &#22852;&#34081; (literally "vast steppe"), which lay north-west of the Kangju. The people of Yancai and were said to resemble the Kangju in their customs:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>Yancai lies some 2,000 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_(Chinese_unit)" title="Li (Chinese unit)">li</a></i> (832 km) northwest of Kangju (centered on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazrat-e_Turkestan" title="Hazrat-e Turkestan">Turkestan</a> at Beitian). The people are nomads and their customs are generally similar to those of the people of Kangju. The country has over 100,000 archer warriors, and borders a great shoreless lake, perhaps what is now known as the Northern Sea (<a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, distance between <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aralsk" title="Aralsk">Aralsk</a> is about 866 km\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>By the time of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanshu" title="Hanshu">Hanshu</a></i> (which covers the period from 206 BCE to 23 CE), Kangju had expanded considerably to a nation of some 600,000 individuals, with 120,000 men able to bear arms. Kangju was clearly now a major power in its own right. By this time it had gained control of Dayuan and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdiana" title="Sogdiana">Sogdiana</a> in which it controlled &#8220;five lesser kings&#8221; (&#23567;&#29579;&#20116;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 101 BCE, the Kangju allied themselves with the Dayuan, helping them preserve their independence against the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The account on  the \'<a href="/wiki/Western_Regions" title="Western Regions">Western Regions</a>\' in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> Chinese chronicle, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Later_Han" title="Book of Later Han">Hou Hanshu</a></i>, 88 (covering the period 25&#8211;220 and completed in the 5th century), based on a report to the Chinese emperor c. 125 CE, mentions that, at that time, Liyi &#26647;&#24331; (= Suyi &#31903;&#24331;) = Sogdiana, and both the "old" Yancai (which had changed its name to <i>Alanliao</i> and seems here to have expanded its territory to the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>), and <i>Yan</i>, a country to Yancai\'s north, as well as the strategic city of "Northern Wuyi" &#21271;&#28879;&#20234; (<a href="/wiki/Alexandria_Eschate" title="Alexandria Eschate">Alexandria Eschate</a>, or modern <a href="/wiki/Khujand" title="Khujand">Khujand</a>), were all dependent on Kangju.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FH129_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FH129-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Y. A. Zadneprovskiy suggests that the Kangju subjection of Yancai occurred in the 1st century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy465_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy465-12">[12]</a></sup> Yancai is identified with the <a href="/wiki/Aorsi" title="Aorsi">Aorsi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Scholars have connected name Alanliao to <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">Alans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy465_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy465-12">[12]</a></sup> The Yan people of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urals" title="Urals">Urals</a>, paid tribute to the Kangju in <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">furs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> The Kangju established close connections with the <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a>, their western neighbors. The westward expansion of the Kangju obliged many of the Sarmatians to migrate further west, and it may therefore be concluded that the Kangju played a major in the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">great migrations of the time</a>, which played a major role in world history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Through this expansion the Kangju gained control over key parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silk_Route" title="Silk Route">Silk Route</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> The Kangju state came to unite a number of regions which had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedentary" title="Sedentary">sedentary</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic" title="Nomadic">nomadic</a> populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Although their territory was small, the fertility of the land and their sophisticated civilization enabled the Kangju to maintain a large population, becoming a major military power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_Marshak_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin_Marshak-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Kangju were in frequent struggles with the Wusun, during which they in the mid 1st century BCE allied themselved with the northern Xiongnu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> The Kangju ruler gave his daughter in marriage to the northern Xiongnu ruler Chih-Chih (Zhizhi?) while Chih-Chih married the daughter of the Kangju ruler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy468_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy468-14">[14]</a></sup> The Xiongnu and Kangju were initially successful, besieging the Wusun in 42 BCE. The Han however intervened, defeating and killing the northern Xiongnu ruler in at Talas in 36 BCE (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Zhizhi" title="Battle of Zhizhi">Battle of Zhizhi</a>). The Kangju ruler was subsequently forced to send his son as a <a href="/wiki/Hostage" title="Hostage">hostage</a> to the Han court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the Kangju continued to send embassies to the Han court and pursued an independent policy, which they were able to maintain until the 3rd century AD. Evidence of Kangju independence can be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coinage</a> issued in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD, during which they issued their own <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> which was similar to that of Khwarezm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The biography of the Chinese General <a href="/wiki/Ban_Chao" title="Ban Chao">Ban Chao</a> in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hou_Hanshu" title="Hou Hanshu">Hou Hanshu</a></i> says in 94 CE that the Yuezhi were arranging a marriage of their king with a Kangju princess. The Chinese then sent "considerable presents of silks" to the Yuezhi successfully gaining their help in pressuring the Kangju to stop supporting the king of <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a> against them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 3rd century <i><a href="/wiki/Weil%C3%BCe" title="Weil&#252;e">Weil&#252;e</a></i> states that Kangju was among a number of countries that "had existed previously and neither grown nor shrunk."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Kangju subsequently declined. Around 270 AD they were subdued by the <a href="/wiki/Xionites" title="Xionites">Xionites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harmatta21_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harmatta21-18">[18]</a></sup> Like other Central Asian peoples, the Kangju probably became subsumed into the <a href="/wiki/Hephthalites" title="Hephthalites">Hephthalites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kangju was later known as  the State of Kang (&#24247;&#22269;) during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sui_Dynasty" title="Sui Dynasty">Sui</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang</a> dynasties. In the 8th century, some of them seem to have been adherents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manicheanism" title="Manicheanism">Manicheanism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a> describes the way of life of the Kangju elite. Its ruler spent his winter in the <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> of Pi-t\'ien, and his summers at his <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe" title="Eurasian Steppe">steppe</a> headquarters, which was a seven days\' journey away on horseback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Kangju are regarded as an <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European</a> people, and are generally held to have been an <a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Iranian</a> people identical with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdians" title="Sogdians">Sogdians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_Marshak_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin_Marshak-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kyzlasov_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kyzlasov-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinor153_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinor153-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinor174_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinor174-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sinology" title="Sinology">Sinologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Edwin_G._Pulleyblank" title="Edwin G. Pulleyblank">Edwin G. Pulleyblank</a> has however suggested that the Kangju could have been <a href="/wiki/Tocharians" title="Tocharians">Tocharians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The ruling elite of the Kangju consisted of <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_nomads" title="Eurasian nomads">nomadic</a> tribes whose customs were very similar to those of the Yuezhi. Kangju burials of the early period have been excavatated at Berk-kara and Tamd&#238;, in which the dead were placed in pit-graves, often covered with logs, under <a href="/wiki/Kurgan" title="Kurgan">kurgan</a> mounds. These graves often contain hand-made pots, iron swords, arrow-heads and jewellery. The burials show that the traditional culture of the Kangju resembled characteristics of the <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Saka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> From the beginning of the Christian era "catacomb graves" (in shaft and chamber tombs) became widespread. This is seen from the burials of the Kaunchi and Dzhun cultures of the 1st to the 4th centuries AD, which are generally accepted as having belonged to the Kangju.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-19"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> The Kangju regarded the <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">ram</a> as a noble animal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mukhamedjanov277_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mukhamedjanov277-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>References from written sources and archaeological finds show that the Kangju reached a considerable level of agricultural sophistication. Much of the population consisted of a sedentary farming population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zadneprovskiy_1-20"><a href="#cite_note-Zadneprovskiy-1">[1]</a></sup> Wide canals from the Kangju period have been discovered, with the land area under <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> of the Amu Darya and Syr Darya being four times greater than today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mukhamedjanov270_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mukhamedjanov270-25">[25]</a></sup> The irrigation systems of Central Asia reached their highest levels of development under the Kangju-<a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushans</a> and was in fact superior to those fully developed in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mukhamedjanov270_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mukhamedjanov270-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kaunchi_culture">Kaunchi culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kaunchi culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kangju appears to be a civilisation known to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet" title="Soviet">Soviet</a> archaeologists as the "Kaunchi Culture", dating from the 2nd century BCE to the early 8th century CE, and centred on the middle course of the Syr Darya and its tributaries: the <a href="/wiki/Angren_River" title="Angren River">Angren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chirchiq_River" title="Chirchiq River">Chirchik</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Keles_River" title="Keles River">Keles</a>. The culture was named after an ancient townsite now known as Kaunchi-Tepe, which was first studied by G. V. Grigoriev in 1934&#8211;37.\n</p><p>Settlements of the Kaunchi culture were typically located in proximity to water and usually have monumental oval buildings in the center, at times with a defensive wall. The largest settlement was a 150 hectare city known apparently as Kang (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>Kanka</i>), south of modern <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a> and founded in the 1st century CE. Kang had a square layout, encircled by a wall with inner passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The settlements were surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Kurgan" title="Kurgan">kurgan</a> burials of a catacomb type with long <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#D" title="Glossary of architecture">dromoses</a>, crypts, and burial vaults, with horse bone trappings and rites typical of nomads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The people predominantly practiced cattle husbandry and nonirrigated agriculture (grain cultures of millet, barley, wheat, and rice, cotton, melons, and fruits).\n</p><p>Materials typical of the culture are typical hand-formed pottery: <i>khums</i> (large bowls for water and produce), pots, pitchers, and cups adorned with ram\'s head on the handles. In the 1st century CE ceramics made on a <a href="/wiki/Potter%27s_wheel" title="Potter\'s wheel">potter\'s wheel</a> became more common. A <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">ram</a>\'s head motif at first common was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Bull" title="Bull">bull</a>\'s head during the late 3rd and early 4th centuries. At that period weapons started appearing in the kurgans.\n</p><p>Kaunchi-type sites apparently spread from the <a href="/wiki/Otrar" title="Otrar">Otrar</a> region along Syr Darya to the south of Tashkent. The Kaunchi culture significantly impacted the archeological cultures in the vast territories of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Asia" title="Middle Asia">Middle Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inscriptions">Inscriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kangju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inscriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some important inscriptions were discovered recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> that provide information about Kangju and its contacts with China.\n</p>\n<ul><li>A dozen wooden slips with Chinese writing were found at the Xuanquan site in <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a>, China. They are dated to the late Western Han Dynasty (206 BCE-24 CE).</li>\n<li>A set of Sogdian inscriptions from Kultobe in Kazakhstan; they were analyzed and deciphered by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Sims-Williams" title="Nicholas Sims-Williams">Nicholas Sims-Williams</a>. They complement the existing Chinese historical records about  Kangju. Sims-Williams also assigned a likely date to these inscriptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Several fragmentary Sogdian inscriptions discovered by A. N. Podushkin in his excavations at Kultobe. They contain archaic features which shed light on the development of the Sogdian script and language.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span
