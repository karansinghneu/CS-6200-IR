<div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zodiacal_Light.webm" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Astronomers at <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">ESO</a>\'s sites often see zodiacal light.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Zodiacal light</b> (also called <b>false dawn</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> when seen <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">before sunrise</a>) is a faint, <a href="/wiki/Photon_diffusion" title="Photon diffusion">diffuse</a>, and roughly triangular white glow that is visible in the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a> and appears to extend from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>\'s direction and along the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a>, straddling the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">Sunlight</a> <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles" title="Light scattering by particles">scattered</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">interplanetary dust</a> causes this phenomenon. Zodiacal light is best seen during <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> after <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunset</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spring_(season)" title="Spring (season)">spring</a> and before <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> in <a href="/wiki/Autumn" title="Autumn">autumn</a>, when the zodiac is at a steep angle to the horizon. However, the glow is so faint that <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">moonlight</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> outshine it, rendering it invisible.\n</p><p>The brightness of zodiacal light decreases with distance from the Sun. In naturally dark night skies, the glow is visible as a band along the entire zodiac, completely straddling the ecliptic. In fact, zodiacal light spans the entire sky and largely<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> contributes to the total natural light in a clear and moonless night sky. Another phenomenon &#8211; a faint but slightly brighter oval glow &#8211; directly <a href="/wiki/Antisolar_point" title="Antisolar point">opposite</a> of the Sun\'s direction is the <a href="/wiki/Gegenschein" title="Gegenschein">gegenschein</a>, which is caused by <a href="/wiki/Backscatter" title="Backscatter">backscattered</a> sunlight.\n</p><p>The interplanetary dust in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> collectively forms a thick, pancake-shaped cloud called the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud" title="Interplanetary dust cloud">zodiacal cloud</a>, which straddles the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> plane. The <a href="/wiki/Particle_size" title="Particle size">particle sizes</a> range between 10 and 300 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a>, most with a mass around 150 <a href="/wiki/Microgram" title="Microgram">micrograms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" href="/w/index.php?title=Zodiacal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Viewing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2976" data-file-width="4167" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg/220px-Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg/440px-Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Submillimeter_Array_Night.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zodiacal light seen behind the <a href="/wiki/Submillimeter_Array" title="Submillimeter Array">Submillimeter Array</a> from the summit of <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the mid-latitudes, the zodiacal light is best observed in the western sky in the spring after the evening <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> has completely disappeared, or in the eastern sky in the autumn just before the morning twilight appears.  The zodiacal light appears as a column, brighter at the horizon, tilted at the angle of the ecliptic.  The light scattered from extremely small dust particles is strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_scattering" title="Forward scattering">forward scattering</a>, although the zodiacal light actually extends all the way around the sky, hence it is brightest when observing at a small angle with the Sun.  This is why it is most clearly visible near sunrise or sunset, when the sun is blocked, but the dust particles nearest the line of sight to the sun are not. The dust band that causes the zodiacal light is uniform across the whole ecliptic.\n</p><p>The dust further from the ecliptic is almost undetectable except when viewed at a small angle with the sun.  Thus it is possible to see more of the width at small angles toward the sun, and it appears wider near the horizon, closer to the sun under the horizon.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zodiacal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2395" data-file-width="3529" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg/220px-Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg/330px-Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg/440px-Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moonlight_and_Zodiacal_Light_Over_La_Silla_Observatory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moonlight and zodiacal light over <a href="/wiki/La_Silla_Observatory" title="La Silla Observatory">La Silla Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The source of the dust has been long debated. Until recently, it was thought that the dust originated from the tails of active <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> and from collisions between <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">asteroid belt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Many of our meteor showers have no known active comet parent bodies.  Over 85 percent of the dust is attributed to occasional fragmentations of Jupiter-family comets that are nearly <a href="/wiki/Extinct_comet" title="Extinct comet">dormant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Jupiter-family comets have orbital periods of less than 20 years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and are considered dormant when not actively outgassing, but may do so in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The first fully dynamical model of the zodiacal cloud demonstrated that only if the dust was released in orbits that approach Jupiter, is it stirred up enough to explain the thickness of the zodiacal dust cloud. The dust in meteoroid streams is much larger, 300 to 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a> in diameter, and falls apart into smaller zodiacal dust grains over time.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5741" data-file-width="4054" decoding="async" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg/220px-Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg/330px-Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg/440px-Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Romantic_Sunset_over_the_VLT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colorful center of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and the zodiacal light above the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Poynting%E2%80%93Robertson_effect" title="Poynting&#8211;Robertson effect">Poynting&#8211;Robertson effect</a> forces the dust into more circular (but still elongated) orbits, while spiralling slowly into the Sun. Hence a continuous source of new particles is needed to maintain the zodiacal cloud. <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">Cometary</a> dust and dust generated by collisions among the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> are believed to be mostly responsible for the maintenance of the dust cloud producing the zodiacal light and the <a href="/wiki/Gegenschein" title="Gegenschein">gegenschein</a>.\n</p><p>Particles can be reduced in size by collisions or by space weathering. When ground down to sizes less than 10 micrometres, the grains are removed from the inner Solar System by solar radiation pressure. The dust is then replenished by the infall from comets. Zodiacal dust around nearby stars is called <a href="/wiki/Exozodiacal_dust" title="Exozodiacal dust">exozodiacal dust</a>; it is a potentially important source of noise for directly imaging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planets" title="Extrasolar planets">extrasolar planets</a>. It has been pointed out that this exozodiacal dust, or hot debris disks, can be an indicator of planets, as planets tend to scatter the comets to the inner Solar System.\n</p><p>In 2015, new results from the secondary ion dust spectrometer COSIMA on board the <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)"><i>ESA/Rosetta</i></a> orbiter confirmed that the parent bodies of interplanetary dust are most probably Jupiter-family comets such as comet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko" title="67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko">67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zodiacal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:False_Dawn.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="329" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="57" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/False_Dawn.jpg/220px-False_Dawn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/False_Dawn.jpg/330px-False_Dawn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/False_Dawn.jpg/440px-False_Dawn.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:False_Dawn.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>False Dawn<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3573" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg/170px-Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg/255px-Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg 1.5x, class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zodiacal_Light_Seen_from_Paranal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zodiacal light seen from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paranal" title="Paranal">Paranal</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Zodiacal light is produced by sunlight reflecting off dust particles in the Solar System known as <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a>. Consequently, its <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectrum</a> is the same as the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> spectrum. The material producing the zodiacal light is located in a lens-shaped volume of space centered on the sun and extending well out beyond the <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> of Earth. This material is known as the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud" title="Interplanetary dust cloud">interplanetary dust cloud</a>. Since most of the material is located near the plane of the Solar System, the zodiacal light is seen along the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>. The amount of material needed to produce the observed zodiacal light is quite small. If it were in the form of 1&#160;mm particles, each with the same <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> (reflecting power) as Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, each particle would be 8&#160;km from its neighbors. The <a href="/wiki/Gegenschein" title="Gegenschein">gegenschein</a> may be caused by particles directly opposite the Sun as seen from Earth, which would be in full <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">phase</a>.\n</p><p>According to Nesvorn&#253; and Jenniskens, when the dust grains are as small as about 150 micrometres in size, they will hit the Earth at an average speed of 14.5&#160;km/s, many as slowly as 12&#160;km/s. If so, they pointed out, this <a href="/wiki/Comet_dust" title="Comet dust">comet dust</a> can survive entry in partially molten form, accounting for the unusual attributes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometeorites" title="Micrometeorites">micrometeorites</a> collected in Antarctica, which do not resemble the larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorites" title="Meteorites">meteorites</a> known to originate from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">asteroids</a>. In recent years, observations by a variety of spacecraft have shown significant structure in the zodiacal light including dust bands associated with debris from particular <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">asteroid families</a> and several cometary trails.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_significance">Cultural significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zodiacal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>\'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kosmos_(Humboldt)" title="Kosmos (Humboldt)">Kosmos</a></i>, Mexicans were aware of the zodiacal light before 1500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196104_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196104-15">[15]</a></sup> It was perhaps first reported in print by <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Childrey" title="Joshua Childrey">Joshua Childrey</a> in 1661. The phenomenon was investigated by the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">Giovanni Domenico Cassini</a> in 1683. According to some sources, he explained it by dust particles around the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Other sources state that it was first explained this way by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Fatio_de_Duillier" title="Nicolas Fatio de Duillier">Nicolas Fatio de Duillier</a>, in 1684,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> whom Cassini advised to study the zodiacal light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196104_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ley196104-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_to_Islam">Importance to Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Muslim oral tradition preserves numerous sayings, or <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a>, in which Muhammad describes the difference between the light of false dawn, appearing in the sky long after sunset, and the light of the first band of horizontal light at sunrise, the "true dawn" (<span dir="rtl" lang="ar" title="Arabic language text">&#1575;&#1604;&#1601;&#1580;&#1585; &#1575;&#1604;&#1589;&#1575;&#1583;&#1602;</span> <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language transliteration">al-fajr al-s&#257;diq</i></i>). According to the vast majority of Muslim scholars, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_dawn" title="Astronomical dawn">astronomical dawn</a> is considered the true dawn. Practitioners of Islam use Muhammad\'s descriptions of zodiacal light to avoid errors in determining the timing of daily prayers. Such practical descriptions and applications of astronomical observations were vital to the golden age of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_astronomy" title="Islamic astronomy">Islamic astronomy</a>.\n</p><p>Use of the term "false dawn" in this context should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/False_sunrise" title="False sunrise">false sunrise</a>, which is a different, unrelated optical phenomenon.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brian_May">Brian May</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zodiacal_light&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brian May">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a>, lead guitarist with the band <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>, completed his <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a>, <i>A Survey of Radial Velocities in the Zodiacal Dust Cloud</i>, thirty-six years after abandoning it to pursue a career in music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He was able to submit it only because of the minimal amount of research on the topic undertaken during the intervening years.
