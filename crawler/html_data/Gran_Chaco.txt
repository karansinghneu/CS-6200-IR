<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowland" title="Lowland">lowland</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_region" title="Natural region">natural region</a> of the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and a portion of the Brazilian states of <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso" title="Mato Grosso">Mato Grosso</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Mato Grosso do Sul">Mato Grosso do Sul</a>, where it is connected with the <a href="/wiki/Pantanal" title="Pantanal">Pantanal</a> region. It is located west of the <a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay River</a> and east of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>, and is mostly an alluvial sedimentary plain shared among Paraguay, Bolivia and Argentina. It stretches from about <a href="/wiki/17th_parallel_south" title="17th parallel south">17&#176;</a> to <a href="/wiki/33rd_parallel_south" title="33rd parallel south">33&#176; South</a> latitude and between <a href="/wiki/65th_meridian_west" title="65th meridian west">65&#176;</a> and <a href="/wiki/60th_meridian_west" title="60th meridian west">60&#176; West</a> longitude, though estimates differ.\n</p><p>Historically the Chaco has been divided in three main parts: the <i>Chaco Austral</i> or Southern Chaco, south of the <a href="/wiki/Bermejo_River" title="Bermejo River">Bermejo River</a> and inside Argentinian territory, blending into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pampa" title="Pampa">Pampa</a> region in its southernmost end; the <i>Chaco Central</i> or Central Chaco between the Bermejo and the <a href="/wiki/Pilcomayo_River" title="Pilcomayo River">Pilcomayo River</a> to the north, also now in Argentinian territory; and the <i>Chaco Boreal</i> or Northern Chaco, north of the Pilcomayo up to the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Pantanal" title="Pantanal">Pantanal</a>, inside Paraguayan territory and sharing some area with Bolivia.\n</p><p>Locals sometimes divide it today by the political borders, giving rise to the terms Argentinian Chaco, Paraguayan Chaco and Bolivian Chaco. (Inside Paraguay, people sometimes use the expression Central Chaco for the area roughly in the middle of the Chaco Boreal, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">Mennonite</a> colonies are established.)\n</p><p>The Chaco Boreal may be divided in two: closer to the mountains in the west, the <i>Alto Chaco</i> (Upper Chaco), sometimes known as <i>Chaco Seco</i> (or Dry Chaco), is very dry and sparsely vegetated. To the east, less arid conditions combined with favorable soil characteristics permit a seasonally dry higher-growth thorn tree forest, and further east still higher rainfall combined with improperly drained lowland soils result in a somewhat swampy plain called the <i>Bajo Chaco</i> (Lower Chaco), sometimes known as <i>Chaco H&#250;medo</i> (Humid Chaco). It has a more open <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> vegetation consisting of palm trees, <a href="/wiki/Schinopsis_lorentzii" title="Schinopsis lorentzii">quebracho trees</a> and tropical high-grass areas, with a wealth of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. The landscape is mostly flat and slopes at a 0.004 degree gradient to the east. This area is also one of the distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiographic" title="Physiographic">physiographic</a> provinces of the Parana-Paraguay Plain division.\n</p><p>The areas more hospitable to development are along the <a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermejo_River" title="Bermejo River">Bermejo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pilcomayo_River" title="Pilcomayo River">Pilcomayo Rivers</a>. It is a great source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a>, which is derived from the native <i>quebracho</i> tree. Special tannin factories have been constructed there. The wood of the <a href="/wiki/Bulnesia_sarmientoi" title="Bulnesia sarmientoi">palo santo</a> from the Central Chaco is the source of <a href="/wiki/Oil_of_guaiac" title="Oil of guaiac">oil of guaiac</a> (a fragrance for <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>). Paraguay also cultivates <a href="/wiki/Yerba_mate" title="Yerba mate">mate</a> in the lower part of the Chaco.\n</p><p>Large tracts of the central and northern Chaco have high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_(soil)" title="Fertility (soil)">soil fertility</a>, sandy alluvial soils with elevated levels of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and a topography that is favorable for agricultural development. Other aspects are challenging for farming: a semi-arid to semi-humid climate (600&#8211;1300&#160;mm annual rainfall) with a six-month dry season and sufficient fresh groundwater restricted to roughly one third of the region, two thirds being without groundwater or with groundwater of high salinity. Soils are generally erosion prone once the forest has been cleared. In the central and northern Paraguay Chaco, occasional dust storms have caused major top soil loss.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Chaco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Chaco was occupied by nomadic peoples, notably the various groups making up the <a href="/wiki/Guaycuru_peoples" title="Guaycuru peoples">Guaycuru</a> who resisted Spanish control, often with success, of the Chaco from the 16th until the early 20th century.  \n</p><p>Prior to national independence of the nations that compose the Chaco, the entire area was a separate colonial region named by the Spaniards as <i>Chiquitos</i>.\n</p><p>The Gran Chaco had been a disputed territory since 1810. Officially, it was supposed to be part of Argentina, Bolivia and Paraguay, although a bigger land portion west of the Paraguay River had belonged to Paraguay since its independence. Argentina claimed territories south of the <a href="/wiki/Bermejo_River" title="Bermejo River">Bermejo River</a> until Paraguay\'s defeat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Triple_Alliance" title="War of the Triple Alliance">War of the Triple Alliance</a> in 1870 established its current border with Argentina.\n</p><p>Over the next few decades, Bolivia began to push the natives out and settle in the Gran Chaco, while Paraguay ignored it. Bolivia sought the Paraguay River for shipping oil out into the sea (it had become a land-locked country after the loss of its Pacific coast in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific" title="War of the Pacific">War of the Pacific</a>), and Paraguay claimed ownership of the land. This became the backdrop to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gran_Chaco_War" title="The Gran Chaco War">The Gran Chaco War</a> (1932&#8211;1935) between Paraguay and Bolivia over supposed oil in the Chaco Boreal (the aforementioned region north of the <a href="/wiki/Pilcomayo_River" title="Pilcomayo River">Pilcomayo River</a> and to the west of the <a href="/wiki/Paraguay_River" title="Paraguay River">Paraguay River</a>).  Eventually, Argentine Foreign Minister <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Saavedra_Lamas" title="Carlos Saavedra Lamas">Carlos Saavedra Lamas</a> mediated a cease fire and subsequent treaty signed in 1938, which gave Paraguay three quarters of the Chaco Boreal and gave Bolivia a corridor to the Paraguay River with the ability to use the Puerto Casado and the right to construct their own port. In the end, no oil was found in the region.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="546" data-file-width="916" decoding="async" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg/220px-Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg/330px-Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg/440px-Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aereal_view_of_Km_75_Ruins.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Road construction in the deep Gran Chaco during the 1960s</div></div></div> <p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mennonite" title="Mennonite">Mennonites</a> immigrated into the Paraguayan part of the region from Canada in the 1920s; more came from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">USSR</a> in the 1930s and immediately following World War II. These immigrants created some of the largest and most prosperous municipalities in the deep Gran Chaco.\n</p><p>The region is home to over nine million people, divided about evenly among Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, and including around 100,000 in Paraguay. The area remains relatively underdeveloped, In the 1960s, the Paraguayan authorities constructed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trans-Chaco_Highway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trans-Chaco Highway (page does not exist)">Trans-Chaco Highway</a> and the Argentine National Highway Directorate, National Routes 16 and 81, in an effort to encourage access and development. All three highways extend about 700&#160;km (430&#160;mi) from east to west and are now completely paved, as are a network of nine Brazilian highways in <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Mato Grosso do Sul">Mato Grosso do Sul</a> State.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">Flora and fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Chaco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prosopis_alba.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1007" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Prosopis_alba.jpg/220px-Prosopis_alba.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Prosopis_alba.jpg/330px-Prosopis_alba.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Prosopis_alba.jpg/440px-Prosopis_alba.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prosopis_alba.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Prosopis" title="Prosopis">Algarrobo</a>, white carob tree, in the Gran Chaco area of Argentina. This prized shade tree is common to the area.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Gran Chaco has some of the highest temperatures on the continent. \n</p><p>It has high <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, containing around 3,400 plant species, 500 birds, 150 mammals and 220 reptiles and amphibians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The floral characteristics of the Gran Chaco are varied given the large geographical span of the region. The dominant vegetative structure is xerophytic deciduous forests with multiple layers including a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canopy_(trees)" title="Canopy (trees)">canopy (trees)</a>, sub-canopy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrub_layer" title="Shrub layer">shrub layer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous_layer" title="Herbaceous layer">herbaceous layer</a>. There are ecosystems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riverine_forest" title="Riverine forest">riverine forests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Savannas" title="Savannas">savannas</a>, and cactus stands as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prado_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prado-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At higher elevations of the eastern zone of the Humid/Sub-humid Chaco there are transitional mature forests from the wet forests of Southern Brazil. These woodlands are dominated by canopy trees such as <i><a href="/wiki/Handroanthus_impetiginosus" title="Handroanthus impetiginosus">Handroanthus impetiginosus</a></i> and characterized by frequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lianas" title="Lianas">lianas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphytes" title="Epiphytes">epiphytes</a>. This declines to seasonally flooded forests, at lower elevations, that are dominated by <i><a href="/wiki/Schinopsis" title="Schinopsis">Schinopsis</a></i> spp., a common plains tree genus often harvested for its <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a> content and dense wood. The understory comprises bromeliad and cactus species as well as hardy shrubs like <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Schinus_fasciculatus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Schinus fasciculatus (page does not exist)">Schinus fasciculatus</a></i>. These lower areas lack lianas but have abundant epiphytic species like <i><a href="/wiki/Tillandsia" title="Tillandsia">Tillandsia</a></i>.\nThe river systems that flow through the area, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Paraguay" title="Rio Paraguay">Rio Paraguay</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Parana" title="Rio Parana">Rio Parana</a> allow for seasonally flooded semi evergreen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallery_forests" title="Gallery forests">gallery forests</a> that hold riparian species such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tessaria_integrifolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tessaria integrifolia (page does not exist)">Tessaria integrifolia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Salix_humboldtiana" title="Salix humboldtiana">Salix humboltiana</a></i>. Other seasonally flooded ecosystems of this area include palm dominated (<i><a href="/wiki/Copernicia_alba" title="Copernicia alba">Copernicia alba</a></i>) savannas with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunch_grass" title="Bunch grass">bunch grass</a> dominated herbaceous layer.\n</p><p>To the west, in the Semi-Arid/Arid Chaco, there are medium-sized forests consisting of <i><a href="/wiki/Aspidosperma_quebracho-blanco" title="Aspidosperma quebracho-blanco">Aspidosperma quebracho-blanco</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Schinopsis_quebracho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Schinopsis quebracho (page does not exist)">Schinopsis quebracho</a></i> with a slightly shorter subcanopy made up of several species from the Fabaceae family as well as several arboreal cacti species that distinguish this area of the Chaco. There is a scrub-like shrub and herbaceous layer. On sandy soils, the thick woodlands turn into savannas where the aforementioned species prevail as well as species like <i><a href="/wiki/Jacaranda_mimosifolia" title="Jacaranda mimosifolia">Jacaranda mimosifolia</a></i>. The giant <i><a href="/wiki/Stetsonia_coryne" title="Stetsonia coryne">Stetsonia coryne</a></i>, found throughout the western Semi-Arid/Arid region becomes very conspicuous in these sandy savannas. \nThere are various upland systems of plant associations that occur throughout the Gran Chaco. The Highlands of the Argentinian Chaco are made up of, on the dry, sunny side (up to 1800m), <i><a href="/wiki/Schinopsis_haenkeana" title="Schinopsis haenkeana">Schinopsis haenkeana</a></i> woodlands. The cooler side of the uplands hosts <i><a href="/wiki/Zanthoxylum_coco" title="Zanthoxylum coco">Zanthoxylum coco</a></i> (locally referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fagara_coco" title="Fagara coco">Fagara coco</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schinus_molleoides" title="Schinus molleoides">Schinus molleoides</a> (locally referred to as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lithrea_molleoides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lithrea molleoides (page does not exist)">Lithrea molleoides</a></i>) as the predominant species. Other notable species include <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bougainvillea_stipitata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bougainvillea stipitata (page does not exist)">Bougainvillea stipitata</a></i>, and several spp. from <a href="/wiki/Fabaceae" title="Fabaceae">Fabaceae</a>. The Paraguyan uplands have other woodland slope ecosystems, notably, those dominated by <i><a href="/wiki/Anadenanthera_colubrina" title="Anadenanthera colubrina">Anadenanthera colubrina</a></i> on moist slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prado_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prado-3">[3]</a></sup> Both of these upland systems, as well as numerous other Gran Chaco areas, are rich with <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a>.\n</p><p>Faunal diversity in the Gran Chaco is high as well.The Gran Chaco has high levels of biodiversity, containing around 3,400 plant species, 500 birds, 150 mammals and 220 reptiles and amphibians.  Animals typically associated with tropical and subtropical forests are often found throughout the eastern humid Chaco, including jaguars, howler monkeys, peccaries, deer, and tapirs. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edentate" title="Edentate">Edentate</a></i> species, including anteaters and armadillos, are readily seen here as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez-Duque_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-Duque-4">[4]</a></sup>  Being home to at least ten species, the Argentinian Chaco is the location of the peak diversity for the armadillo, including species such as the <a href="/wiki/Nine-banded_armadillo" title="Nine-banded armadillo">nine-banded armadillo</a> (<i>Dasypus novemcinctus</i>) whose range extends north to the southern US, and the <a href="/wiki/Southern_three-banded_armadillo" title="Southern three-banded armadillo">southern three-banded armadillo</a> (<i>Tolypeutes matacus</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bolkovic_and_Zuleta_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bolkovic_and_Zuleta-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pink_fairy_armadillo" title="Pink fairy armadillo">pink fairy armadillo</a> (<i>Chlamyphrous truncatus</i>), is found nowhere else in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lucero_and_Olrog_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lucero_and_Olrog-6">[6]</a></sup> The giant armadillo (<i>Priodontes maximus</i>), while not found in the eastern humid Chaco, can be seen in the drier arid Chaco of the west.  Some other notable endemics of the region include the <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_tuco-tuco" title="San Luis tuco-tuco">San Luis tuco-tuco</a> (<i>Ctenomys pontifex</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez-Duque_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez-Duque-4">[4]</a></sup> This small rodent is only found in the Argentinian Chaco. All of 60 species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ctenomys" title="Ctenomys">Ctenomys</a> are endemic to the continent of South America. The Chacoan peccary (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catagonus_wagneri" title="Catagonus wagneri">Catagonus wagneri</a></i>), locally known as tauga, is the largest of the three peccary species found in the area. This species was thought to be extinct by scientists until 1975, when it was recorded by Dr. Ralph Wetzel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wetzel,_Dubos,_Martin,_and_Myers_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wetzel,_Dubos,_Martin,_and_Myers-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to the climatic regime of the Gran Chaco, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpetofauna" title="Herpetofauna">herpetofauna</a> are restricted to moist <a href="/wiki/Refugium_(population_biology)" title="Refugium (population biology)">refugia</a> in various places throughout the chaco. Rotting logs, debris piles, old housing settlement, wells, and seasonal farm ponds are examples of such refugia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talbot_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talbot-8">[8]</a></sup> \nThe <a href="/wiki/Black-legged_seriema" title="Black-legged seriema">black-legged seriema</a> (<i>Chunga burmeisteri</i>), <a href="/wiki/Blue-crowned_parakeet" title="Blue-crowned parakeet">blue-crowned parakeet</a> (<i>Aratinga acuticadauta</i>), <a href="/wiki/Picui_ground_dove" title="Picui ground dove">Picui ground dove</a> (<i>Columbina picui</i>), <a href="/wiki/Guira_cuckoo" title="Guira cuckoo">guira cuckoo</a> (<i>Guira guira</i>), <a href="/wiki/Little_thornbird" title="Little thornbird">little thornbird</a> (<i>Phacellodomus sibilatrix</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Many-colored_Chaco_finch" title="Many-colored Chaco finch">many-colored Chaco finch</a> (<i>Saltaitricula multicolor</i>) are notable of the 409 bird species that are resident or breed in the Gran Chaco. 252 of these Chaco species are endemic to South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Short-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Kaa-Iya_del_Gran_Chaco_National_Park_and_Integrated_Management_Natural_Area" title="Kaa-Iya del Gran Chaco National Park and Integrated Management Natural Area">Kaa-Iya del Gran Chaco National Park and Integrated Management Natural Area</a> was established in an area of the Chaco in Bolivia. It is administered and was established solely by the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guarani_people" title="Guarani people">Izoce&#241;o Guaran&#237;</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayoreode" title="Ayoreode">Ayoreode</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chiquitano" title="Chiquitano">Chiquitano</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_issues">Conservation issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gran_Chaco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1017" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg/220px-Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg/330px-Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg/440px-Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sorghum_linea_14_Alto_Paraguay.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sorghum harvest 2008, Linea 14, Agua Dulce Region, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alto_Paraguay" title="Alto Paraguay">Alto Paraguay</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The Chaco is one of South America\'s last agricultural frontiers. Very sparsely populated and lacking sufficient all-weather roads and basic infrastructure (the Argentinian part is more developed than the Paraguayan or Bolivian part), it has long been too remote for crop planting. The central Chaco\'s <a href="/wiki/Mennonites_in_Paraguay" title="Mennonites in Paraguay">Mennonite colonies</a> are a notable exception.\n</p><p>Two factors may substantially change the Chaco in the near future: low land valuations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\nand the region\'s suitability to grow <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">fuel crops</a>.\nSuitability for the cultivation of <i><a href="/wiki/Jatropha" title="Jatropha">Jatropha</a></i> has been proven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n<a href="/wiki/Sweet_sorghum" title="Sweet sorghum">Sweet sorghum</a> as an ethanol plant may prove viable, too, since sorghum is a traditional local crop for domestic and feedstock use. The feasibility of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Switchgrass" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a> is currently being studied by Argentina\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instituto_Nacional_de_Tecnolog%C3%ADa_Agropecuaria" title="Instituto Nacional de Tecnolog&#237;a Agropecuaria">INTA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> as is the <a href="/wiki/Copernicia_alba" title="Copernicia alba">Karanda&#8217;y palm tree</a> in the Paraguayan Chaco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>While advancements in agriculture can bring some improvements in infrastructure and employment for the region, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loss_of_habitat" title="Loss of habitat">loss of habitat</a> / virgin forest is substantial and will likely increase <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. Paraguay, after having lost more than 90% of its Atlantic Rainforest between 1975 and 2005, is now losing its <a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">xerophytic forest</a> (dry forests) in the Chaco at an annual rate of 220,000 hectares (540,000 acres) (2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\nIn mid-2009 a projected law, initiated by the <a href="/wiki/Authentic_Radical_Liberal_Party" title="Authentic Radical Liberal Party">Liberal Party</a>, that would have outlawed <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in the Paraguayan Chaco altogether, "Deforestacion Zero en el Chaco" did not get a majority in the parliament.\n</p><p>Deforestation in the Argentinian part of the Chaco amounted to an average of 100,000 hectares (250,000 acres) per year between 2001 and 2007\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\nAccording to <a href="/wiki/Fundaci%C3%B3n_Avina" title="Fundaci&#243;n Avina">Fundaci&#243;n Avina</a>, a local NGO, on average, 1130 hectares are cut down per day &#8211; this equals 2300 football fields. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soy" title="Soy">soy</a> plantations not only eliminate the forest, but also other types of agriculture as well. Indigenous communities are losing their land to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agrobusiness" title="Agrobusiness">agrobusinesses</a>. Since 2007, there is a law which is supposed to regulate and control the cutting of timber in the Gran Chaco, but <a href="/wiki/Illegal_logging" title="Illegal logging">illegal logging</a> continues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Among the profiteers of the newly created agricultural areas in the Paraguayan Gran Chaco are U.S.-based agribusinesses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cargill_Inc." title="Cargill Inc.">Cargill Inc.</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunge_Ltd." title="Bunge Ltd.">Bunge Ltd.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archer_Daniels_Midland" title="Archer Daniels Midland">Archer Daniels Midland</a> Co..<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacDonald_2014_19-0"><a
