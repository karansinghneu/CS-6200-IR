2007</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Sunset</b> or <b>sundown</b> is the daily disappearance of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> below the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">Earth\'s rotation</a>. As viewed from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a> Sun sets exactly due west in both spring and fall. As viewed from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a>, the local <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> Sun sets to the northwest for the <a Hemisphere</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Twilight_subcategories.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_disc_just_before_sunset_(Namibia).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar disc just before sunset in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The time of sunset is defined in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> as the moment when the upper limb of the Sun disappears below the horizon. Near the horizon, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a> causes  <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> rays to be distorted to such an extent that geometrically the solar disk is already about one diameter below the horizon when a sunset is observed.\n</p><p>Sunset is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>, which is divided into three stages, the first being <i>civil</i> twilight, which begins once the Sun has disappeared below the horizon, and continues until it descends to 6 degrees below the horizon; the second phase is <i>nautical</i> twilight, between 6 and 12 degrees below the horizon; and the third is <i>astronomical</i> twilight, which is the period when the Sun is between 12 and 18 degrees below the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNO-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">Dusk</a></i> is at the very end of astronomical twilight, and is the darkest moment of twilight just before <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Night occurs when the Sun reaches 18 degrees below the horizon and no longer illuminates the sky.\n</p><p>Locations further north than the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> and further south than the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a> experience no full sunset or sunrise on at least one day of the year, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_day" title="Polar day">polar day</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">polar night</a> persists continuously for 24 hours, but full polar night occurs only at a latitude of more than about 72.5 degrees.\n</p><p>Sunset creates unique atmospheric conditions such as the often intense orange and red colors of the Sun and the surrounding sky.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" All along the terminator, the rays from the sun hit Earth horizontally, neglecting any atmospheric effects and Earth\'s orbital motion.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2525" data-file-width="4228" decoding="async" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg/220px-Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg/330px-Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg/440px-Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anatomy_of_a_Sunset-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The full cycle of a sunset on the High Plains of the <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The time of sunset varies throughout the year, and is determined by the viewer\'s position on Earth, specified by longitude and latitude, and elevation. Small daily changes and noticeable semi-annual changes in the timing of sunsets are driven by the axial tilt of Earth, daily rotation of the Earth, the planet\'s movement in its annual elliptical orbit around the Sun, and the Earth and Moon\'s paired revolutions around each other. During winter and spring, the days get longer and sunsets occur later every day until the day of the latest sunset, which occurs after the summer solstice. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, the latest sunset occurs late in June or in early July, but not on the summer solstice of June 21. This date depends on the viewer\'s latitude (connected with the Earth\'s slower movement around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">aphelion</a> around July 4). Likewise, the earliest sunset does not occur on the winter solstice, but rather about two weeks earlier, again depending on the viewer\'s latitude. In the Northern Hemisphere, it occurs in early December or late November (influenced by the Earth\'s faster movement near its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>, which occurs around January 3).\n</p><p>Likewise, the same phenomenon exists in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, but with the respective dates reversed, with the earliest sunsets occurring some time before June 21 in winter, and latest sunsets occurring some time after December 21 in summer, again depending on one\'s southern latitude. For a few weeks surrounding both solstices, both sunrise and sunset get slightly later each day. Even on the equator, sunrise and sunset shift several minutes back and forth through the year, along with solar noon. These effects are plotted by an <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">analemma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Neglecting atmospheric refraction and the Sun\'s non-zero size, whenever and wherever sunset occurs, it is always in the northwest quadrant from the <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">March equinox</a> to the <a href="/wiki/September_equinox" title="September equinox">September equinox</a>, and in the southwest quadrant from the September equinox to the March equinox. Sunsets occur almost exactly due west on the equinoxes for all viewers on Earth. Exact calculations of the <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuths</a> of sunset on other dates are complex, but they can be estimated with reasonable accuracy by using the <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">analemma</a>.\n</p><p>As sunrise and sunset are calculated from the leading and trailing edges of the Sun, respectively, and not the center, the duration of a daytime is slightly longer than nighttime (by about 10 minutes, as seen from temperate latitudes). Further, because the light from the Sun is refracted as it passes through the Earth\'s atmosphere, the Sun is still visible after it is geometrically below the horizon. Refraction also affects the apparent shape of the Sun when it is very close to the horizon. It makes things appear higher in the sky than they really are. Light from the bottom edge of the Sun\'s disk is refracted more than light from the top, since refraction increases as the angle of elevation decreases. This raises the apparent position of the bottom edge more than the top, reducing the apparent height of the solar disk. Its width is unaltered, so the disk appears wider than it is high. (In reality, the Sun is almost exactly spherical.) The Sun also appears larger on the horizon, an optical illusion, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">moon illusion</a>.\n</p><p>Locations north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> and south of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a> experience no sunset or sunrise at least one day of the year, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_day" title="Polar day">polar day</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">polar night</a> persist continuously for 24 hours.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colors">Colors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Majestic_Twilight.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4056" data-file-width="2848" decoding="async" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Majestic_Twilight.jpg/340px-Majestic_Twilight.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Majestic_Twilight.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evening <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a> in <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Tree,_California" title="Joshua Tree, the blue components scattered from the surrounding sky</div></div></div>\n<p>As a ray of white sunlight travels through the atmosphere to an observer, some of the colors are scattered out of the beam by air molecules and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">airborne particles</a>, changing the final color of the beam the viewer sees.\nBecause the shorter wavelength components, such as blue and green, scatter more strongly, these colors are preferentially removed from the beam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saha_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-saha-5">[5]</a></sup>\nAt <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> and sunset, when the path through the atmosphere is longer, the blue and green components are removed almost completely, leaving the longer wavelength orange and red <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hues</a> we see at those times. The remaining reddened sunlight can then be scattered by cloud droplets and other relatively large particles to light up the horizon red and orange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guenther_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-guenther-6">[6]</a></sup>  The removal of the shorter wavelengths of light is due to <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> by air molecules and particles much smaller than the wavelength of visible light (less than 50&#160;nm in diameter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohren_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bohren-8">[8]</a></sup>  The scattering by cloud droplets and other particles with diameters comparable to or larger than the sunlight\'s wavelengths (&gt; 600&#160;nm) is due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mie_theory" title="Mie theory">Mie scattering</a> and is not strongly wavelength-dependent.  Mie scattering is responsible for the light scattered by clouds, and also for the daytime halo of white light around the Sun (forward scattering of white light).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-11">[11]</a></sup>\nSunset colors are typically more brilliant than <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrise</a> colors, because the evening air contains more particles than morning air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saha_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-saha-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guenther_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-guenther-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohren_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bohren-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ash from volcanic eruptions, trapped within the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, tends to mute sunset and sunrise colors, while volcanic ejecta that is instead lofted into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> (as thin clouds of tiny sulfuric acid droplets), can yield beautiful post-sunset colors called <a href="/wiki/Afterglow" title="Afterglow">afterglows</a> and pre-sunrise glows. A number of eruptions, including those of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in 1991 and <a href="/wiki/Krakatoa" title="Krakatoa">Krakatoa</a> in 1883, have produced sufficiently high stratospheric sulfuric acid clouds to yield remarkable sunset afterglows (and pre-sunrise glows) around the world. The high altitude clouds serve to reflect strongly reddened sunlight still striking the stratosphere after sunset, down to the surface. Sometimes just before sunrise or after sunset a <a href="/wiki/Green_flash" title="Green flash">green flash</a> can be seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg/220px-Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg/330px-Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg/440px-Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Downdraft_Wind_shear-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illuminated downdraft wind shear <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> in the eastern sky near <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a> mimic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">aurora borealis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Some of the most varied colors at sunset can be found in the opposite or eastern sky after the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> has set during <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>. Depending on weather conditions and the types of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> present, these colors have a wide spectrum, and can produce unusual results.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_compass_points">Names of compass points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Names of compass points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In some languages, <a href="/wiki/Points_of_the_compass" title="Points of the compass">points of the compass</a> bear names etymologically derived from words for sunrise and sunset. The English words "<a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">orient</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">occident</a>", meaning "east" and "west", respectively, are descended from Latin words meaning "sunrise" and "sunset". The word "levant", related e.g. to French "<i>(se) lever</i>" meaning "lift" or "rise" (and also to English "elevate"), is also used to describe the east. In <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, the word for <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">east</a> <i>wsch&#243;d</i> (<i>vskhud</i>), is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morpheme</a> "ws" &#8211; meaning "up", and "ch&#243;d" &#8211; signifying "move" (from the verb <i>chodzi&#263;</i> &#8211; meaning "walk, move"), due to the act of the Sun coming up from behind the horizon. The Polish word for <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">west</a>, <i>zach&#243;d</i> (<i>zakhud</i>), is similar but with the word "za" at the start, meaning "behind", from the act of the Sun going behind the horizon. In <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, the word for west, <i>&#1079;&#1072;&#1087;&#1072;&#1076;</i> (<i>zapad</i>), is derived from the words <i>&#1079;&#1072;</i> &#8211; meaning "behind", and <i>&#1087;&#1072;&#1076;</i> &#8211; signifying "fall" (from the verb <i>&#1087;&#1072;&#1076;&#1072;&#1090;&#1100;</i> &#8211; <i>padat\'</i>), due to the act of the Sun falling behind the horizon. In Hebrew, the word for east is \'&#1502;&#1494;&#1512;&#1495;\', which derives from the word for rising, and the word for west is \'&#1502;&#1506;&#1512;&#1489;\', which derives from the word for setting.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_view">Historical view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a> was the first to present to the world a detailed and eventually widely accepted mathematical model supporting the premise that the Earth is moving and the Sun actually stays still, despite the impression from our point of view of a moving Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">Planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MarsSunset.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1914" data-file-width="2486" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/MarsSunset.jpg/220px-MarsSunset.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/MarsSunset.jpg/330px-MarsSunset.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/MarsSunset.jpg/440px-MarsSunset.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MarsSunset.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sunset on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Sunsets on other planets appear different because of differences in the distance of the planet from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and non-existent or differing atmospheric compositions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, the setting Sun appears about two-thirds the size it appears on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> because of its greater distance from the Sun, but some Martian sunsets last significantly longer and appear far redder than is typical on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-apod-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The colors of the Martian sunset differ from those on Earth. Mars has a thin <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, lacking <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, so the light scattering is not dominated by a <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh Scattering</a> process. Instead, the air is full of <a href="/wiki/Martian_soil" title="Martian soil">red dust</a>, blown into the atmosphere by high winds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-apod-15">[15]</a></sup> so its sky color is mainly determined by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mie_theory" title="Mie theory">Mie Scattering</a> process. One study also reported that Martian dust high in the atmosphere can reflect sunlight up to two hours after the sun has set, casting a diffuse glow across the surface of Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apod_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-apod-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gallery">Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
