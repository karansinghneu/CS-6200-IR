1</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">&#1042;&#1077;&#1085;&#1077;&#1088;&#1072;-1</span> meaning <i>Venus 1</i>), also known as <b>Venera-1VA No.2</b> and occasionally in the West as <i><b>Sputnik 8</b></i> was the first spacecraft to fly past <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Venera" title="Venera">Venera</a> programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1961-003A_1-0"><a returning no data.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Venera 1</i> was a 643.5-kilogram (1,419&#160;lb) probe consisting of a cylindrical body 1.05 metres (3&#160;ft 5&#160;in) in diameter topped by a dome, totalling 2.035 metres (6&#160;ft 8.1&#160;in) in height. This was pressurized to 1.2 standard atmospheres (120&#160;kPa) with dry nitrogen, with internal fans to maintain even distribution of heat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Two solar panels extended from the cylinder, charging a bank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silver-zinc_battery" title="Silver-zinc battery">silver-zinc batteries</a>. A 2-metre parabolic wire-mesh antenna was designed to send data from Venus to Earth on a frequency of 922.8&#160;MHz. A 2.4-metre antenna boom was used to transmit short-wave signals during the near-Earth phase of the mission. Semidirectional <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">quadrupole</a> antennas mounted on the solar panels provided routine telemetry and telecommand contact with Earth during the mission, on a circularly-polarized decimetre radio band.\n</p><p>The probe was equipped with scientific instruments including a flux-gate <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> attached to the antenna boom, two <a href="/wiki/Ion_trap" title="Ion trap">ion traps</a> to measure solar wind, <a href="/wiki/Micrometeorite" title="Micrometeorite">micrometeorite</a> detectors, and <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a> tubes and a sodium iodide <a href="/wiki/Scintillator" title="Scintillator">scintillator</a> for measurement of cosmic radiation. An experiment attached to one solar panel measured temperatures of experimental coatings. Infrared and/or ultraviolet radiometers may have been included. The dome contained a KDU-414 engine used for mid-course corrections. Temperature control was achieved by motorized thermal shutters.\n</p><p>During most of its flight, <i>Venera 1</i> was spin stabilized. It was the first spacecraft designed to perform mid-course corrections, by entering a mode of 3-axis stabilization, fixing on the Sun and the star <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a>. Had it reached Venus, it would have entered another mode of 3-axis stabilization, fixing on the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and using for the first time a parabolic antenna to relay data.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Venera 1</i> was the second of two attempts to launch a probe to Venus in February 1961, immediately following the launch of its sister ship <a href="/wiki/Tyazhely_Sputnik" title="Tyazhely Sputnik"><i>Venera-1VA No.1</i></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chronology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chronology-2">[2]</a></sup> which failed to leave Earth orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soviet_Failures_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soviet_Failures-3">[3]</a></sup> Soviet experts launched Venera-1 using a <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya</a> carrier rocket from the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>. The launch took place at 00:34:36 UTC on 12 February 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The spacecraft, along with the rocket\'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blok-L&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blok-L (page does not exist)">Blok-L</a> upper stage, were initially placed into a 229 &#215; 282&#160;km <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1961-003A_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-1961-003A-1">[1]</a></sup> before the upper stage fired to place <i>Venera 1</i> into a <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">heliocentric orbit</a>, directed towards Venus. The 11D33 engine was the world\'s first staged-combustion-cycle rocket engine, and also the first use of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ullage_engine" title="Ullage engine">ullage engine</a> to allow a liquid-fuel rocket engine to start in space.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure">Failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Three successful telemetry sessions were conducted, gathering solar-wind and cosmic-ray data near Earth, at the Earth\'s magnetopause, and on  February 19 at a distance of 1,900,000&#160;km (1,200,000&#160;mi). After discovering the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> with <i>Luna 2</i>, <i>Venera 1</i> provided the first verification that this plasma was uniformly present in deep space. Seven days later, the next scheduled telemetry session failed to occur.  On May 19, 1961, <i>Venera 1</i> passed within 100,000&#160;km (62,000&#160;mi) of Venus. With the help of the British radio telescope at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank" title="Jodrell Bank">Jodrell Bank</a>, some weak signals from <i>Venera 1</i> may have been detected in June. Soviet engineers believed that Venera-1 failed due to the overheating of a solar-direction sensor.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
