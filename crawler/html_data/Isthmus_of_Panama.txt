class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pm-map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Isthmus of Panama</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Isthmus of Panama</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Istmo de Panam&#225;</i>), also historically known as the <b>Isthmus of Darien</b> (<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Istmo de Dari&#233;n</i>), is the narrow strip of land that lies between the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, linking <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. It contains the country of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a>. Like many <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">isthmuses</a>, it is a location of great strategic value.\n</p><p>The isthmus formed around 2.8 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origins_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-1">[1]</a></sup>  This major geological event separated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and Pacific Oceans and caused the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>.  This was first suggested in 1910 by North American paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn">Henry Fairfield Osborn</a>.  He based the proposal on the fossil record of mammals in Central America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  This conclusion provided a foundation for <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">Alfred Wegener</a> when he proposed the theory of <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" Panama</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Balboa_Voyage_1513.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="316" N&#250;&#241;ez de Balboa</a> heard of the South Sea from natives while sailing along the Caribbean coast. On 25 September 1513 he saw the Pacific. In 1519 the town of Panam&#225; was founded near a small indigenous settlement on the Pacific coast. After the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, it developed into an important port of trade and became an administrative centre. In 1671 the Welsh pirate <a href="/wiki/Henry_Morgan" title="Henry Morgan">Henry Morgan</a> crossed the Isthmus of Panam&#225; from the Caribbean side and destroyed the city. The town was relocated some kilometers to the west at a small peninsula. The ruins of the old town, <a href="/wiki/Panam%C3%A1_Viejo" title="Panam&#225; Viejo">Panam&#225; Viejo</a>, are preserved and were declared a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a> in 1997.\n</p><p>Silver and gold from the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">viceroyalty of Peru</a> were transported overland across the isthmus by <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Silver_Train" title="Spanish Silver Train">Spanish Silver Train</a> to <a href="/wiki/Portobelo,_Col%C3%B3n" title="Portobelo, Col&#243;n">Porto Bello</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Spanish_treasure_fleet" title="Spanish treasure fleet">Spanish treasure fleets</a> shipped them to <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> and <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="C&#225;diz">C&#225;diz</a> from 1707.  <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Wafer" title="Lionel Wafer">Lionel Wafer</a> spent four years between 1680 and 1684 among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuna_Indians" title="Cuna Indians">Cuna Indians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Scotland tried to establish a settlement in 1698 through the <a href="/wiki/Darien_scheme" title="Darien scheme">Darien scheme</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/California_Gold_Rush" title="California Gold Rush">California Gold Rush</a>, starting in 1849, brought a large increase in the transportation of people from the Atlantic to the Pacific. Steamships brought gold diggers from eastern US ports, who trekked across the isthmus by foot, horse, and later rail. On the Pacific side, they boarded  <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Mail_Steamship_Company" title="Pacific Mail Steamship Company">Pacific Mail Steamship Company</a> vessels headed for <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Lesseps" title="Ferdinand de Lesseps">Ferdinand de Lesseps</a>, the man behind the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Canal" title="Suez Canal">Suez Canal</a>, started a Panama Canal Company in 1880 that went bankrupt in 1889 in the <a href="/wiki/Panama_scandals" title="Panama scandals">Panama scandals</a>.  In 1902&#8211;4, the United States forced <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> to grant independence to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Isthmus" title="Department of the Isthmus">Department of the Isthmus</a>, bought the remaining assets of the Panama Canal Company, and <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal#History" title="Panama Canal">finished the canal</a> in 1914.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isthmus_of_Panama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isthmus_of_Panama_(closure)_-_Speciation_of_marine_organisms_(w_annot).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1151" data-file-width="946" decoding="async" height="365" 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isthmus_of_Panama_(closure)_-_Speciation_of_marine_organisms_(w_annot).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The closure of the Isthmus led to <a href="/wiki/Allopatric_speciation" title="Allopatric speciation">allopatric speciation</a> events of marine organisms isolated on each side (blue and green). Terrestrial species also migrated between the two continents (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_American_Biotic_Interchange" title="Great American Biotic Interchange">Great American Biotic Interchange</a>) upon the formation of a passable land bridge.</div></div></div>\n<p>A significant body of water (referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Seaway" title="Central American Seaway">Central American Seaway</a>) once separated the continents of North and South America, allowing the waters of the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans to mix freely. Beneath the surface, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">plates</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth\'s crust">Earth\'s crust</a> were slowly colliding, forcing the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos Plate</a> to slide under the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Plate" title="Caribbean Plate">Caribbean Plate</a>. The pressure and heat caused by this collision led to the formation of underwater <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, some of which grew large enough to form <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> as early as 15 million years ago. Meanwhile, movement of the two tectonic plates was also pushing up the sea floor, eventually forcing some areas above sea level.\n</p><p>Over time, massive amounts of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> (sand, soil, and mud) from North and South America filled the gaps between the newly forming islands. Over millions of years, the sediment deposits added to the islands until the gaps were completely filled. By no later than 4.5 million years ago, an isthmus had formed between North and South America. However, an article in <i>Science Magazine</i> stated that <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> crystals in middle <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> bedrock from northern Colombia indicated that by 10 million years ago, it is likely that instead of islands, a full isthmus between the North and South American continents had already likely formed where the <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Seaway" title="Central American Seaway">Central American Seaway</a> had been previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Evidence also suggests that the creation of this land mass and the subsequent warm, wet weather over <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a> resulted in the formation of a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_ice_cap" title="Arctic ice cap">Arctic ice cap</a> and contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">current ice age</a>. That warm currents can lead to glacier formation may seem counterintuitive, but heated air flowing over the warm Gulf Stream can hold more moisture. The result is increased precipitation that contributes to snow pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The formation of the Isthmus of Panama also played a major role in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> on the planet. The bridge made it easier for animals and plants to migrate between the two continents. This event is known in <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontology</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Great_American_Interchange" title="Great American Interchange">Great American Interchange</a>. For instance, in North America today, the <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">opossum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armadillo" title="Armadillo">armadillo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porcupine" title="Porcupine">porcupine</a> all trace back to ancestors that came across the land bridge from South America. Likewise, bears, cats, dogs, horses, llamas, and raccoons all made the trek south across the isthmus.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosphere">Biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isthmus_of_Panama&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the connecting bridge between two vast land masses, the Panamanian biosphere is filled with overlapping fauna and flora from both North and South America. There are, for example, over 978 species of birds in the isthmus area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The tropical climate also encourages a myriad of large and brightly colored species, insects, amphibians, birds, fish, and reptiles. Divided along its length by a mountain range, the isthmus\'s weather is generally wet on the Atlantic (Caribbean) side but has a clearer division into wet and dry seasons on the Pacific side.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
