title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[&#712;h&#603;rman &#712;ju:l&#618;&#650;s &#712;o:b&#592;t]</a></span>; 25 June 1894 &#8211; 28 December 1989) was an  <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian</a>-born <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>. href="/wiki/Robert_Esnault-Pelterie" title="Robert Esnault-Pelterie">Robert Esnault-Pelterie</a>, the Russian <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a> and the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Goddard" title="Robert Goddard">Robert Goddard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Oberth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="408" decoding="async" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg/220px-Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg/330px-Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_in_his_early_years.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hermann Oberth as a young boy, c. 1901</div></div></div>\n<p>Oberth was born to a Transylvanian Saxon family in Hermannstadt (Nagyszeben), Austria-Hungary, today Sibiu in Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At the age of 11 years, Oberth became fascinated with the field in which he was to make his mark through reading the writings of <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>, especially <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">Around the Moon</a></i>, re-reading them to the point of memorization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Influenced by Verne\'s books and ideas, Oberth constructed his first <a href="/wiki/Model_rocket" title="Model rocket">model rocket</a> as a school student at the age of 14. In his youthful experiments, he arrived independently at the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">multistage rocket</a>, but he lacked then the resources to pursue his idea on any but a pencil-and-paper level.\n</p><p>In 1912, Oberth began the study of medicine in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Germany, but at the outbreak of World War I, he was drafted into the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Imperial German</a> Army, assigned to an infantry battalion, and sent to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Eastern Front (World War I)">Eastern Front</a> against Russia. In 1915, Oberth was moved into a medical unit at a hospital in <a href="/wiki/Sighi%C8%99oara" title="Sighi&#537;oara">Sighi&#537;oara</a> (German Sch&#228;&#223;burg, Hungarian Segesv&#225;r), Transylvania, in <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> (today Romania).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ianzer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ianzer-8">[8]</a></sup> There he found the spare time to conduct a series of experiments concerning <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">weightlessness</a>, and later resumed his rocketry designs. By 1917, he showed designs of a missile using liquid propellant with a range of 180 miles to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Stein_(1854-1927)" title="Hermann von Stein (1854-1927)">Hermann von Stein</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prussian_Minister_of_War" title="Prussian Minister of War">Prussian Minister of War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monde_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monde-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 6 July 1918, Oberth married Mathilde Hummel, with whom he had four children. Among these were a son who died as a soldier in World War II, and a daughter who also died during the war when there was an accidental explosion at a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> plant where she was in August 1944. In 1919, Oberth once again moved to Germany, this time to study physics, initially in Munich and later in <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="G&#246;ttingen">G&#246;ttingen</a>.\n</p><p>In 1922, Oberth\'s proposed doctoral dissertation on rocket science was rejected as "utopian". He next had his 92-page work published privately in June 1923 as the somewhat controversial book, <i>Die Rakete zu den Planetenr&#228;umen</i> ("The Rocket into Planetary Space"). By 1929, Oberth had expanded this work to a 429-page book titled <i>Wege zur Raumschiffahrt</i> ("Ways to Spaceflight"). Oberth commented later that he made the deliberate choice not to write another doctoral dissertation. He wrote, "I refrained from writing another one, thinking to myself: Never mind, I will prove that I am able to become a greater scientist than some of you, even without the title of Doctor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiosek_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiosek-10">[10]</a></sup> Oberth criticized the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Germany" title="Education in Germany">German system of education</a>, saying "Our educational system is like an automobile which has strong rear lights, brightly illuminating the past. But looking forward, things are barely discernible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiosek_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-kiosek-10">[10]</a></sup> Hermann Oberth was finally awarded his licence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> in physics with the same rocketry paper that he had written before, by the <a href="/wiki/Babe%C8%99-Bolyai_University" title="Babe&#537;-Bolyai University">University of Cluj</a>, Romania, under professor <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Maior" title="Augustin Maior">Augustin Maior</a>, on 23 May 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ianzer_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ianzer-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Oberth became a member of the <i><a href="/wiki/Verein_f%C3%BCr_Raumschiffahrt" title="Verein f&#252;r Raumschiffahrt">Verein f&#252;r Raumschiffahrt</a></i> (VfR) &#8211; the "Spaceflight Society" &#8211; an amateur rocketry group that had taken great inspiration from his book, and Oberth acted as something of a mentor to the enthusiasts who joined the Society. Oberth lacked the opportunities to work or to teach at the college or university level, as did many well-educated experts in the physical sciences and engineering in the time period of the 1920s through the 1930s &#8211; with the situation becoming much worse during the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> that started in 1929. Therefore, from 1924 through 1938, Oberth supported himself and his family by teaching <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Stephan_Ludwig_Roth" title="Stephan Ludwig Roth">Stephan Ludwig Roth</a> High School in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media%C5%9F" title="Media&#351;">Media&#351;</a>, Romania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ianzer_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ianzer-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rocketry_and_space_flight">Rocketry and space flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Oberth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rocketry and space flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="857" decoding="async" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg/220px-Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg/330px-Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg/440px-Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_Enterprise_stamp_Paraguay1979.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Paraguayan stamp celebrating the 85th anniversary of Oberth\'s birth featuring <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/USS_Enterprise_(NCC-1701)" title="USS Enterprise (NCC-1701)">USS <i>Enterprise</i> (NCC-1701)</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>In parts of 1928 and 1929, Oberth also worked in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin,_Germany" title="Berlin, Germany">Berlin, Germany</a> as a scientific consultant on the film, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frau_im_Mond" title="Frau im Mond">Frau im Mond</a></i> ("The Woman in the Moon"), which was directed and produced by the great film pioneer <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">Fritz Lang</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universum_Film_AG" title="Universum Film AG">Universum Film AG</a> company. This film was of enormous value in popularizing the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocketry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>. One of Oberth\'s main assignments was to build and launch a rocket as a publicity event just before the film\'s premiere. He also designed the model of the <i>"<a href="/wiki/Friede" title="Friede">Friede</a>"</i>, the main rocket portrayed in the film.\n</p><p>On 5 June 1929, Oberth won the first (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Esnault-Pelterie" title="Robert Esnault-Pelterie">Robert Esnault-Pelterie</a> - Andr&#233;-Louis Hirsch) <a href="/wiki/Robert_Esnault-Pelterie#Prix_REP-Hirsch" title="Robert Esnault-Pelterie">Prix REP-Hirsch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_astronomique_de_France" title="Soci&#233;t&#233; astronomique de France">French Astronomical Society</a> for the encouragement of astronautics in his book <i>Wege zur Raumschiffahrt</i> ("Ways to Spaceflight") that had expanded <i>Die Rakete zu den Planetenr&#228;umen</i> to a full-length book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The book <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Lang" title="Fritz Lang">Fritz Lang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thea_von_Harbou" title="Thea von Harbou">Thea von Harbou</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoI1929_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoI1929-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the autumn of 1929, Oberth conducted a static firing of his first liquid-fueled rocket motor, which he named the <i>Kegeld&#252;se</i>. The engine was built by Klaus Riedel in a workshop space provided by the Reich Institution of Chemical Technology, and although it lacked a cooling system, it did run briefly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He was helped in this experiment by an 18-year-old student <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>, who would later become a giant in both German and American rocket engineering from the 1940s onward, culminating with the gigantic <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rockets that made it possible for men to land on the Moon in 1969 and in several following years. Indeed, Von Braun said of him:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Hermann Oberth was the first, who when thinking about the possibility of spaceships grabbed a slide-rule and presented mathematically analyzed concepts and designs.... I, myself, owe to him not only the guiding-star of my life, but also my first contact with the theoretical and practical aspects of rocketry and space travel. A place of honor should be reserved in the history of science and technology for his ground-breaking contributions in the field of astronautics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VonBraun_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-VonBraun-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In 1938, the Oberth family left <a href="/wiki/Sibiu" title="Sibiu">Sibiu</a>, Romania, for good, to first settle in Austria, then in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, then in the United States, and finally back to a free Germany. Oberth himself moved on first to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_University_of_Technology" title="Vienna University of Technology">Technische Hochschule</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna,_Austria" title="Vienna, Austria">Vienna, Austria</a>, then to the Technische Hochschule in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dresden,_Germany" title="Dresden, Germany">Dresden, Germany</a>. (A Technische Hochschule at that time was a technical college offering advanced professional training in selected fields, rather than an institution also engaged in basic research as a university.)\n</p><p>Oberth moved to <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenem&#252;nde">Peenem&#252;nde</a>, Germany, in 1941 to work on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggregate_(rocket_family)" title="Aggregate (rocket family)">Aggregate</a> rocket program. Around September 1943, he was awarded the <i><a href="/wiki/War_Merit_Cross" title="War Merit Cross">Kriegsverdienstkreuz I Klasse mit Schwertern</a></i> (War Merit Cross 1st Class, with Swords) for his "outstanding, courageous behavior ... during the attack" on Peenem&#252;nde by <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hydra_(1943)" title="Operation Hydra (1943)">Operation Hydra</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Crossbow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Later he worked on solid-propellant anti-aircraft rockets at the German WASAG military organization near <a href="/wiki/Wittenberg" title="Wittenberg">Wittenberg</a>. Around the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in Europe, the Oberth family moved to the town of Feucht, near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuremberg,_Germany" title="Nuremberg, Germany">Nuremberg, Germany</a>, which became part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Zone" title="American Zone">American Zone</a> of occupied Germany, and also the location of the high-level war-crimes trials of the surviving Nazi leaders. Oberth was allowed to leave Nuremberg to move to <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> in 1948, where he worked as an independent consultant and a writer.\n</p><p>In 1950, Oberth moved on to Italy, where he completed some of the work that he had begun at the WASAG organization for the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marina_Militare" title="Marina Militare">Italian Navy</a>. In 1953, Oberth returned to Feucht, Germany, to publish his book <i>Menschen im Weltraum</i> (<i>Man into Space</i>), in which he described his ideas for space-based <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescopes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space stations</a>, electric-powered <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spaceships</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">space suits</a>.\n</p><p>During the 1950s and 1960s, Oberth offered his opinions regarding <a href="/wiki/Unidentified_flying_object" title="Unidentified flying object">unidentified flying objects</a> (UFOs). He was a supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_hypothesis" title="Extraterrestrial hypothesis">extraterrestrial hypothesis</a> for the origin of the UFOs that were seen at the Earth. For example, in an article in <i>The American Weekly</i> magazine of 24 October 1954, Oberth stated, "It is my thesis that flying saucers are real, and that they are space ships from another solar system. I think that they possibly are manned by intelligent observers who are members of a race that may have been investigating our earth for centuries..." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He also wrote an article in the second edition of <i>Flying Saucer Review</i> titled "They Come From Outer Space". He discussed the history of reports of "strange luminous objects" in the sky, mentioning that the earliest historical case is of "Shining Shields" reported by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>. He wrote, "Having weighed all the pros and cons, I find the explanation of flying discs from outer space the most likely one. I call this the "Uraniden" hypothesis, because from our viewpoint the hypothetical beings appear to come from the sky (Greek &#8211; \'Uranos\')." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Oberth eventually came to work for his former student, <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>, who was developing space rockets for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>. (<i>See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_German_rocket_scientists_in_the_United_States" title="List of German rocket scientists in the United States">List of German rocket scientists in the United States</a></i>). Among other things, Oberth  was involved in writing the study, <i>The Development of Space Technology in the Next Ten Years</i>. In 1958, Oberth was back in Feucht, Germany, where he published his ideas on a lunar exploration vehicle, a "lunar catapult", and on "muffled" helicopters and airplanes. In 1960, back in the United States again, Oberth went to work for the <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair Corporation</a> as a technical consultant on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket)" title="Atlas (rocket)">Atlas rocket</a> program.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Oberth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_nel_1961.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="269" decoding="async" height="379" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Hermann_Oberth_nel_1961.jpg/220px-Hermann_Oberth_nel_1961.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/Hermann_Oberth_nel_1961.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hermann_Oberth_nel_1961.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hermann Oberth with <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany">Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany</a>, 1961</div></div></div>\n<p>Oberth retired in 1962 at the age of 68. From 1965 to 1967 he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party</a>, which was considered to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_right" title="Far right">far right</a>. In July 1969, Oberth returned to the United States to witness the launch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_project" title="Apollo project">Apollo project</a> <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> rocket from the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> that carried the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> crew on the first landing mission to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-centennial_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-centennial-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 petroleum crisis</a> inspired Oberth to look into alternative energy sources, including a plan for a <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power station</a> that could utilize the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>. However, his primary interest during his retirement years was to turn to more abstract philosophical questions. Most notable among his several books from this period is <i>Primer For Those Who Would Govern</i>.\n</p><p>Oberth returned to the United States to view the launch of <a href="/wiki/STS-61-A" title="STS-61-A">STS-61A</a>, the space shuttle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle_Challenger" title="Space shuttle Challenger"><i>Challenger</i></a> launched 30 October 1985.\n</p><p>Oberth died in Nuremberg, West Germany, on 28 December 1989, just shortly after <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the fall of the Iron Curtain</a> that had for so long divided Germany into two countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monde_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Monde-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT1-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
