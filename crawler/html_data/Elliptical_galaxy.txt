class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abell_S740,_cropped_to_ESO_325-G004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The giant elliptical galaxy ESO 325-G004</div></div></div>\n<p>An <b>elliptical galaxy</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> having an approximately <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoidal</a> shape and a smooth, nearly featureless image. Unlike flat <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a> with organization and structure, elliptical galaxies are more three-dimensional, without much structure, and their stars are in somewhat random orbits around the center.  They are one of the three main <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification" title="Galaxy morphological classification">classes of galaxy</a> described by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Hubble_sequence#Physical_significance" title="Hubble sequence">Hubble sequence</a> and 1936 work <i>The Realm of the Nebulae</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubble-1936_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubble-1936-1">[1]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy" title="Lenticular galaxy">lenticular</a> galaxies. \nElliptical (E) galaxies are, together with <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy" title="Lenticular galaxy">lenticular galaxies</a> (S0) with their large-scale disks, and ES galaxies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with their intermediate scale disks, a subset of the "early-type" galaxy population.\n</p><p>Elliptical galaxies range in size from tens of millions to over one hundred trillion <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. Originally, Edwin Hubble hypothesized that elliptical galaxies evolved into <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a>, which was later discovered to be false,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-author_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-author-5">[5]</a></sup> although the accretion of gas and smaller galaxies may build a disk around a pre-existing ellipsoidal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\nStars found inside of elliptical galaxies are on average much older than stars found in spiral galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-author_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-author-5">[5]</a></sup>\nMost elliptical galaxies are composed of older, <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution#Low-mass_stars" title="Stellar evolution">low-mass stars</a>, with a sparse <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> and minimal <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> activity, and they tend to be surrounded by large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular clusters</a>. Elliptical galaxies are believed to make up approximately 10%&#8211;15% of galaxies in the <a href="/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster" title="Virgo Supercluster">Virgo Supercluster</a>, and they are not the dominant type of galaxy in the universe overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They are preferentially found close to the centers of <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">galaxy clusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elliptical_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General href="/wiki/File:Elliptical_galaxy_IC_2006.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2751" data-file-width="3895" decoding="async" height="155" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elliptical_galaxy_IC_2006.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Elliptical galaxy <a href="/wiki/IC_2006" title="IC 2006">IC 2006</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Elliptical galaxies are characterized by several properties that make them distinct from other classes of galaxy. They are spherical or ovoid masses of stars, starved of star-making gases. The smallest known elliptical galaxy is about one-tenth the size of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The motion of stars in elliptical galaxies is predominantly radial<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, unlike the disks of <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a>, which are dominated by <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a>. Furthermore, there is very little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_matter" title="Interstellar matter">interstellar matter</a> (neither gas nor dust), which results in low rates of <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>, few <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">open star clusters</a>, and few young stars; rather elliptical galaxies are dominated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">old stellar populations</a>, giving them red colors. Large elliptical galaxies typically have an extensive system of <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular clusters</a>.\n</p><p>The dynamical properties of elliptical galaxies and the <a href="/wiki/Bulge_(astronomy)" title="Bulge (astronomy)">bulges</a> of <a href="/wiki/Disc_galaxy" title="Disc galaxy">disk galaxies</a> are similar, suggesting that they may be formed by the same physical processes, although this remains controversial. The <a href="/wiki/Luminosity#In_astronomy" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> profiles of both elliptical galaxies and bulges are well fit by <a href="/wiki/Sersic_profile" title="Sersic profile">Sersic\'s law</a>, and a range of scaling relations between the elliptical galaxies\' structural parameters unify the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Every massive elliptical galaxy contains a <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">supermassive black hole</a> at its center. Observations of 46 elliptical galaxies, 20 classical bulges, and 22 pseudobulges show that each contain a black hole at the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The mass of the black hole is tightly correlated with the mass of the galaxy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> evidenced through correlations such as the <a href="/wiki/M%E2%80%93sigma_relation" title="M&#8211;sigma relation">M&#8211;sigma relation</a> which relates the <a href="/wiki/Velocity_dispersion" title="Velocity dispersion">velocity dispersion</a> of the surrounding stars to the mass of the black hole at the center.\n</p><p>Elliptical galaxies are preferentially found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxy_clusters" title="Galaxy clusters">galaxy clusters</a> and in compact <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_group" title="Galaxy group">groups of galaxies</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_formation">Star formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elliptical_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Star formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">traditional portrait</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of elliptical galaxies paints them as galaxies where <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> finished after an initial burst at high-redshift, leaving them to shine with only their aging stars. Elliptical galaxies typically appear yellow-red, which is in contrast to the distinct blue tinge of most <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a>. In spirals, this blue color emanates largely from the young, hot stars in their spiral arms. Very little <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a> is thought to occur in elliptical galaxies, because of their lack of gas compared to spiral or irregular galaxies. However, in recent years, evidence has shown that a reasonable proportion (~25%) of early-type (E, ES and S0) galaxies have residual gas reservoirs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young2011-14">[14]</a></sup>\nand low level star-formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crocker2011_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crocker2011-15">[15]</a></sup> Researchers with the <a href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory" title="Herschel Space Observatory">Herschel Space Observatory</a> have speculated that the central black holes in elliptical galaxies keep the gas from cooling enough for star formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Note the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_lensing" title="Gravitational lensing">gravitational lensing</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Elliptical galaxies vary greatly in both size and mass with diameters ranging from 3000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightyear" title="Lightyear">lightyears</a> to more than 700,000 lightyears, and masses from 10<sup>5</sup> to nearly 10<sup>13</sup> solar masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This range is much broader for this galaxy type than for any other. The smallest, the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_elliptical_galaxy" title="Dwarf elliptical galaxy">dwarf elliptical galaxies</a>, may be no larger than a typical <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular cluster</a>, but contain a considerable amount of <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> not present in clusters. Most of these small galaxies may not be related to other ellipticals.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification" title="Galaxy morphological classification">Hubble classification</a> of elliptical galaxies contains an integer that describes how elongated the galaxy image is.\nThe classification is determined by the ratio of the major (<i>a</i>) to the minor (<i>b</i>) axes of the galaxy\'s <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Other_phenomena" title="Contour line">isophotes</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle 10\\times \\left(1-{\\frac {b}{a}}\\right)}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mn>10</mn>\n<mo>&#215;<!-- &#215; --></mo>\n<mrow>\n<mo>(</mo>\n<mrow>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; --></mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mi>b</mi>\n<mi>a</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n</mrow>\n<mo>)</mo>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle 10\\times \\left(1-{\\frac {b}{a}}\\right)}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="10\\times \\left(1-{\\frac  {b}{a}}\\right)" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17443047f34992a07b0a3b19ea2aa0152f7f338f" style="vertical-align: -2.505ex; width:14.655ex; height:6.176ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>Thus for a spherical galaxy with <i>a</i> equal to <i>b</i>, the number\nis 0, and the Hubble type is E0. While the limit in the literature is about E7, it has been known since 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adsabs.harvard.edu_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-2">[2]</a></sup> that the E4 to E7 galaxies are misclassified lenticular galaxies with disks inclined at different angles to our line-of-sight.  This has been confirmed through spectral observations revealing the rotation of their stellar disks. \n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  Hubble recognized that his shape classification depends both on the intrinsic shape of the galaxy, as well as the angle with which the galaxy is observed.  Hence, some galaxies with Hubble type E0 are actually elongated.\n</p><p>It is sometimes said that there are two physical types of ellipticals: the giant ellipticals with slightly "boxy"-shaped isophotes, whose shapes result from random motion which is greater in some directions than in others (anisotropic random motion); and the "disky" normal and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwarf_elliptical" title="Dwarf elliptical">dwarf ellipticals</a>, which contain disks.\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  This is, however, an abuse of the nomenclature, as there two types of early-type galaxy, those with disks and those without. Given the existence of ES galaxies with intermediate-scale disks, it is reasonable to expect that there is a continuity from E to ES, and onto the S0 galaxies with their large-scale stellar disks that dominate the light at large radii.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dwarf_spheroidal_galaxy" title="Dwarf spheroidal galaxy">Dwarf spheroidal galaxies</a> appear to be a distinct class: their properties are more similar to those of irregulars and late spiral-type galaxies.\n</p><p>At the large end of the elliptical spectrum, there is further division, beyond Hubble\'s classification. Beyond <i>gE</i> giant ellipticals, lies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type-D_galaxy" title="Type-D galaxy">D-galaxies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Type-cD_galaxy" title="Type-cD galaxy">cD-galaxies</a>. These are similar to their smaller brethren, but more diffuse, with large haloes that may as much belong to the galaxy cluster within which they reside than the centrally-located giant galaxy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a galaxies is primarily composed of the <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_merger" title="Galaxy merger">merging</a> of smaller galaxies. Many galaxies in the universe are gravitationally bound to other galaxies, which means that they will never escape the pull of the other galaxy. If the galaxies are of similar size, the resultant galaxy will appear similar to neither of the two galaxies merging, but will instead be an elliptical galaxy.\n</p><p>Such major galactic mergers are thought to have been common at early times, but may occur less frequently today. Minor galactic mergers involve two galaxies of very different masses, and are not limited to giant ellipticals. The <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy is, depending upon an unknown tangential component, <a href="/wiki/Andromeda%E2%80%93Milky_Way_collision" title="Andromeda&#8211;Milky Way collision">on a collision course in 4&#8211;5 billion years</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>   It has been theorized that an elliptical galaxy will result from a merger of the two spirals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (September 2018)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>It is believed that black holes may play an important role in limiting the growth of elliptical galaxies in the early universe by inhibiting <a href="/wiki/Star_formation" title="Star formation">star formation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
