class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Universe_expansion-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Universe_expansion-en.svg/240px-Universe_expansion-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Universe_expansion-en.svg/360px-Universe_expansion-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Universe_expansion-en.svg/480px-Universe_expansion-en.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Universe_expansion-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Bang_theory" title="Big Bang theory">Big Bang theory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> emerged from an extremely dense and hot state (<a href="/wiki/Gravitational_singularity" title="Gravitational singularity">singularity</a>). <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">Space</a> itself has been expanding ever since, carrying <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a> with it, like raisins in a rising loaf of bread. The graphic scheme above is an artist\'s conception illustrating the expansion of a portion of a flat universe.</div></div></div>\n<p>After a classical education at a <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> secondary school, the Coll&#232;ge du Sacr&#233;-Coeur, in <a href="/wiki/Charleroi" title="Charleroi">Charleroi</a>, Lema&#238;tre began studying <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_University_of_Louvain" title="Catholic University of Louvain">Catholic University of Louvain</a> at the age of 17. In 1914, he interrupted his studies to serve as an artillery officer in the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Land_Component" title="Belgian Land Component">Belgian army</a> for the duration of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. At the end of hostilities, he received the Belgian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_Cross_(Belgium)" title="War Cross (Belgium)">War Cross</a> with palms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the war, he studied <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, and began to prepare for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocesan_priest" title="Diocesan priest">diocesan</a> <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priesthood</a>, not for the Jesuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He obtained his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">doctorate</a> in 1920 with a thesis entitled <i>l\'Approximation des fonctions de plusieurs variables r&#233;elles</i> (<i>Approximation of functions of several real variables</i>), written under the direction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_de_la_Vall%C3%A9e-Poussin" title="Charles de la Vall&#233;e-Poussin">Charles de la Vall&#233;e-Poussin</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>. He was <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordained</a> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">priest</a> on 22 September 1923 by the Cardinal <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9sir%C3%A9-Joseph_Mercier" title="D&#233;sir&#233;-Joseph Mercier">Mercier</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_1997_28&#8211;53_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert_1997_28&#8211;53-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1923, he became a research associate in astronomy at Cambridge UK, spending a year at St Edmund\'s House (now <a href="/wiki/St_Edmund%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St Edmund\'s College, Cambridge">St Edmund\'s College</a>, University of Cambridge). He worked with <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a>, who introduced him to modern <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy#Stellar_astronomy" title="Astronomy">stellar astronomy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Numerical_analysis" title="Numerical analysis">numerical analysis</a>. He spent the next year at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College_Observatory" title="Harvard College Observatory">Harvard College Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Harlow_Shapley" title="Harlow Shapley">Harlow Shapley</a>, who had just gained renown for his work on <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>, and at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT), where he registered for the doctoral program in sciences.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On his return to Belgium in 1925, he became a part-time lecturer at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic_University_of_Louvain" title="Catholic University of Louvain">Catholic University of Louvain</a>. He began the report which brought him international fame when it was published in 1927 in the <i>Annales de la Soci&#233;t&#233; Scientifique de Bruxelles</i> (<i>Annals of the Scientific Society of Brussels</i>) under the title "Un Univers homog&#232;ne de masse constante et de rayon croissant rendant compte de la vitesse radiale des n&#233;buleuses extragalactiques" ("A homogeneous Universe of constant mass and growing radius accounting for the radial velocity of extragalactic nebulae").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In this report, he presented his new idea that the universe is expanding, which he derived from General Relativity; this later became known as <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a>, but Lema&#238;tre provided the first observational estimation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The initial state he proposed was taken to be Einstein\'s own model of a finitely sized static universe. The paper had little impact because the journal in which it was published was not widely read by astronomers outside Belgium; Arthur Eddington reportedly helped translate the article into English in 1931, but the part of it pertaining to the estimation of the "Hubble constant" was not included in the translation for reasons that remained unknown for a long time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sidney_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-sidney-7">[7]</a></sup> This issue was clarified in 2011 by Mario Livio; Lema&#238;tre omitted the paragraphs when himself initially translating the paper for the Royal Astronomical Society, in favour of reports of new work on the subject, since by that time Hubble\'s calculations had already improved on his earlier ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At this time, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>, while not taking exception to the mathematics of Lema&#238;tre\'s theory, refused to accept that the universe was expanding; Lema&#238;tre recalled his commenting "<i lang="fr" title="French language text">Vos calculs sont corrects, mais votre physique est abominable</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> ("Your calculations are correct, but your physics is atrocious"). In the same year, Lema&#238;tre returned to MIT to present his doctoral thesis on <i>The gravitational field in a fluid sphere of uniform invariant density according to the theory of relativity</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Upon obtaining his Ph.D., he was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_professor" title="Ordinary professor">ordinary professor</a> at the Catholic University of Louvain.\n</p><p>In 1931, Arthur Eddington published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Monthly_Notices_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society">Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society</a></i> a long commentary on Lema&#238;tre\'s 1927 article, in which he described the latter as a "brilliant solution" to the outstanding problems of cosmology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The original paper was published in an abbreviated English translation later on in 1931, along with a sequel by Lema&#238;tre responding to Eddington\'s comments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Lema&#238;tre was then invited to London to participate in a meeting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="British Association for the Advancement of Science">British Association</a> on the relation between the physical universe and spirituality. There he proposed that the universe expanded from an initial point, which he called the "Primeval Atom". He developed this idea in a report published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Lemaitre\'s theory appeared for the first time in an article for the general reader on science and technology subjects in the December 1932 issue of Popular Science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Lema&#238;tre\'s theory became better known as the "<a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> theory," a picturesque term playfully coined during a 1949 BBC radio broadcast by the astronomer <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> who was then still a proponent of the steady state universe and remained so until his death in 2001.\n</p><p>Lema&#238;tre\'s proposal met with <a href="/wiki/Skepticism" title="Skepticism">skepticism</a> from his fellow scientists. Eddington found Lema&#238;tre\'s notion unpleasant. Einstein thought it unjustifiable from a physical point of view, although he encouraged Lema&#238;tre to look into the possibility of models of non-isotropic expansion, so it is clear he was not altogether dismissive of the concept. Einstein also appreciated Lema&#238;tre\'s argument that Einstein\'s model of a static universe could not be sustained into the infinite past.\n</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Sandoval_Vallarta" title="Manuel Sandoval Vallarta">Manuel Sandoval Vallarta</a>, Lemaitre discovered that the intensity of cosmic rays varied with latitude because these charged particles are interacting with the Earth\'s magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In their calculations, Lema&#238;tre and Vallarta made use of the MIT&#8217;s differential -analyzer computer developed by <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a>. They also worked on a theory of primary cosmic radiation and applied it to their investigations of the sun\'s magnetic field and the effects of the galaxy\'s rotation.\n</p><p>Lema&#238;tre and Einstein met on four occasions: in 1927 in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, at the time of a <a href="/wiki/Solvay_Conference" title="Solvay Conference">Solvay Conference</a>; in 1932 in Belgium, at the time of a cycle of conferences in Brussels; in California in January 1933;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and in 1935 at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a>. In 1933 at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>, after Lema&#238;tre detailed his theory, Einstein stood up, applauded, and is supposed to have said, "This is the most beautiful and satisfactory explanation of creation to which I have ever listened."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> However, there is disagreement over the reporting of this quote in the newspapers of the time, and it may be that Einstein was not referring to the theory as a whole, but only to Lema&#238;tre\'s proposal that <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a> may be the leftover artifacts of the initial "explosion". \n</p><p>In 1933, when he resumed his theory of the expanding universe and published a more detailed version in the <i>Annals of the Scientific Society of Brussels</i>, Lema&#238;tre achieved his greatest public recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Newspapers around the world called him a famous Belgian scientist and described him as the leader of the new cosmological physics.\n</p><p>He was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Pontifical Academy of Sciences">Pontifical Academy of Sciences</a> in 1936, and took an active role there, serving as its president from March 1960 until his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1941, he was elected a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Arts_of_Belgium" title="Royal Academy of Sciences and Arts of Belgium">Royal Academy of Sciences and Arts of Belgium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1946, he published his book on <i>L\'Hypoth&#232;se de l\'Atome Primitif</i> (<i>The Primeval Atom Hypothesis</i>). It was translated into Spanish in the same year and into English in 1950.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>By 1951, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> declared that Lema&#238;tre\'s theory provided a scientific validation for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> However, Lema&#238;tre resented the Pope\'s proclamation, stating that the theory was neutral and there was neither a connection nor a contradiction between his religion and his theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambert_1997_28&#8211;53_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lambert_1997_28&#8211;53-16">[16]</a></sup> Lema&#238;tre and Daniel O\'Connell, the Pope\'s scientific advisor, persuaded the Pope not to mention Creationism publicly, and to stop making proclamations about cosmology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> While a devout Roman Catholic, he opposed mixing science with religion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> although he held that the two fields were not in conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1950s, he gradually gave up part of his teaching workload, ending it completely when he took <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%89m%C3%A9ritat" title="&#201;m&#233;ritat">emeritus</a> status in 1964. In 1962, strongly opposed to the expulsion of French speakers from the Catholic University of Louvain, he created the ACAPSUL movement together with <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Garitte" title="G&#233;rard Garitte">G&#233;rard Garitte</a> to fight against the split.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_II" title="Vatican II">Second Vatican Council</a> of 1962&#8211;65 he was asked by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a> to serve on the 4th session of the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Commission_on_Birth_Control" title="Pontifical Commission on Birth Control">Pontifical Commission on Birth Control</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, since his health made it impossible for him to travel to Rome &#8211; he suffered a heart attack in December 1964 &#8211; Lema&#238;tre demurred, expressing surprise that he was chosen. He told a <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> colleague, P&#232;re Henri de Riedmatten, that he thought it was dangerous for a mathematician to venture outside of his area of expertise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He was also named <a href="/wiki/Honorary_Prelate" title="Honorary Prelate">Dometisc prelate</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Monsignor" title="Monsignor">Monsignor</a></i>) in 1960 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of his life, he was increasingly devoted to problems of numerical calculation. He was a remarkable <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraicist</a> and arithmetical calculator. Since 1930, he had used the most powerful calculating machines of the time, the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Euklid" title="Mercedes-Euklid">Mercedes-Euklid</a>. In 1958 he was introduced to the University\'s <a href="/wiki/Burroughs_Corporation" title="Burroughs Corporation">Burroughs E 101</a>, its first electronic computer. Lema&#238;tre maintained a strong interest in the development of computers and, even more, in the problems of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a>.\n</p><p>He died on 20 June 1966, shortly after having learned of the discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a>, which provided further evidence for his proposal about the birth of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lema&#238;tre was a pioneer in applying <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>\'s theory of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>. In a 1927 article, which preceded <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a>\'s landmark article by two years, Lema&#238;tre derived what became known as <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a> and proposed it as a generic phenomenon in relativistic cosmology. Lema&#238;tre also estimated the numerical value of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_constant" title="Hubble constant">Hubble constant</a>. However, the data used by Lema&#238;tre did not allow him to prove that there was an actual linear relation, which Hubble did two years later.\n</p><p>Einstein was skeptical of this paper. When Lema&#238;tre approached Einstein at the 1927 <a href="/wiki/Solvay_Conference" title="Solvay Conference">Solvay Conference</a>, the latter pointed out that <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann" title="Alexander Friedmann">Alexander Friedmann</a> had proposed a similar solution to Einstein\'s equations in 1922, implying that the radius of the universe increased over time. (Einstein had also criticized Friedmann\'s calculations, but withdrew his comments.) In 1931, his <i><a href="/wiki/Annus_mirabilis" title="Annus mirabilis">annus mirabilis</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Lema&#238;tre published an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> setting out his theory of the "primeval atom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Friedmann was handicapped by living and working in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>, and died in 1925, soon after inventing the <a href="/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric" title="Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker metric">Friedmann&#8211;Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Robertson&#8211;Walker metric</a>. Because Lema&#238;tre spent his entire career in Europe, his scientific work is not as well known in the United States as that of Hubble or Einstein, both well known in the U.S. by virtue of residing there. Nevertheless, Lema&#238;tre\'s theory changed the course of cosmology. This was because Lema&#238;tre:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Was well acquainted with the work of astronomers, and designed his theory to have testable implications and to be in accord with observations of the time, in particular to explain the observed <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a> and the linear relation between distances and velocities;</li>\n<li>Proposed his theory at an opportune time, since <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> would soon publish his <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">velocity-distance relation</a> that strongly supported an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expanding universe</a> and, consequently, Lema&#238;tre\'s Big Bang theory;</li>\n<li>Had studied under <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">Arthur Eddington</a>, who made sure that Lema&#238;tre got a hearing in the scientific community.</li></ul>\n<p>Both Friedmann and Lema&#238;tre proposed relativistic cosmologies featuring an expanding universe. However, Lema&#238;tre was the first to propose that the expansion explains the <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> of galaxies. He further concluded that an initial "<a href="/wiki/Ex_nihilo" title="Ex nihilo">creation</a>-like" event must have occurred. In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Guth" title="Alan Guth">Alan Guth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Linde" title="Andrei Linde">Andrei Linde</a> modified this theory by adding to it a period of <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)" title="Inflation (cosmology)">inflation</a>.\n</p><p>Einstein at first dismissed Friedmann, and then (privately) Lema&#238;tre, out of hand, saying that not all mathematics lead to correct theories. After Hubble\'s discovery was published, Einstein quickly and publicly endorsed Lema&#238;tre\'s theory, helping both the theory and its proposer get fast recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lema&#238;tre was also an early adopter of computers for cosmological calculations. He introduced the first computer to his university (a <a href="/wiki/Burroughs_Corporation" title="Burroughs Corporation">Burroughs E 101</a>) in 1958 and was one of the inventors of the <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">Fast Fourier transform</a> algorithm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1933, Lema&#238;tre found an important inhomogeneous solution of <a href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equations" title="Einstein field equations">Einstein\'s field equations</a> describing a spherical dust cloud, the <a href="/wiki/Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Tolman_metric" title="Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Tolman metric">Lema&#238;tre&#8211;Tolman metric</a>.\n</p><p>In 1931, Lemaitre was the first scientist to propose the expansion of the universe was actually accelerating which was confirmed observationally in the 1990s through observations of very distant Type IA supernova with the Hubble Space Telescope which led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="2011 Nobel Prize in Physics">2011 Nobel Prize in Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RiessFilippenko1998_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-RiessFilippenko1998-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1948 Lema&#238;tre published a polished mathematical essay "Quaternions et espace elliptique" which clarified an obscure space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford" title="William Kingdon Clifford">William Kingdon Clifford</a> had cryptically described <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptic_space" title="Elliptic space">elliptic space</a> in 1873 at a time when <a href="/wiki/Versor" title="Versor">versors</a> were too common to mention. Lema&#238;tre developed the theory of quaternions from first principles so that his essay can stand on its own, but he recalled the <a href="/wiki/Erlangen_program" title="Erlangen program">Erlangen program</a> in geometry while developing the metric geometry of elliptic space. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._S._M._Coxeter" title="H. S. M. Coxeter">H. S. M. Coxeter</a>, another contributor to elliptic geometry, summarized<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Lema&#238;tre\'s work for <i><a href="/wiki/Mathematical_Reviews" title="Mathematical Reviews">Mathematical Reviews</a></i>.\n</p><p>Lema&#238;tre was the first theoretical cosmologist ever nominated in 1954 for the Nobel Prize in physics for his prediction of the expanding universe. Remarkably, he was also nominated for the 1956 Nobel prize in chemistry for his primeval-atom theory.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 17 March 1934, Lema&#238;tre received the <a href="/wiki/Francqui_Prize" title="Francqui Prize">Francqui Prize</a>, the highest Belgian scientific distinction, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opold_III_of_Belgium" title="L&#233;opold III of Belgium">King L&#233;opold III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup> His proposers were Albert Einstein, Charles de la Vall&#233;e-Poussin and Alexandre de Hemptinne. The members of the international jury were Eddington, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Langevin" title="Paul Langevin">Langevin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_de_Donder" title="Th&#233;ophile de Donder">Th&#233;ophile de Donder</a> and Marcel Dehalu. The same year he received the Mendel Medal of the <a href="/wiki/Villanova_University" title="Villanova University">Villanova University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1936, Lema&#238;tre received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prix_Jules_Janssen" title="Prix Jules Janssen">Prix Jules Janssen</a>, the highest award of the <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_astronomique_de_France" title="Soci&#233;t&#233; astronomique de France">Soci&#233;t&#233; astronomique de France</a>, the French astronomical society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another distinction that the Belgian government reserves for exceptional scientists was allotted to him in 1950: the decennial prize for applied sciences for the period 1933&#8211;1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1953, he was given the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Eddington_Medal" title="Eddington Medal">Eddington Medal</a> awarded by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2005, Lema&#238;tre was voted to the 61st place of <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text"><a href="/wiki/De_Grootste_Belg" title="De Grootste Belg">De Grootste Belg</a></i> ("The Greatest Belgian"), a <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flemish</a> television program on the <a href="/wiki/Vlaamse_Radio-_en_Televisieomroeporganisatie" title="Vlaamse Radio- en Televisieomroeporganisatie">VRT</a>. In the same year he was voted to the 78th place by the audience of the <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Les_plus_grands_Belges" title="Les plus grands Belges">Les plus grands Belges</a></i> ("The Greatest Belgians"), a television show of the <a href="/wiki/RTBF" title="RTBF">RTBF</a>.\n</p><p>On 17 July 2018, <a href="/wiki/Google_Doodle" title="Google Doodle">Google Doodle</a> celebrated Georges Lema&#238;tre\'s 124th birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 26 October 2018, an electronic vote among all members of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> voted 78% to recommend changing the name of the Hubble law to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre_law" title="Hubble&#8211;Lema&#238;tre law">Hubble&#8211;Lema&#238;tre law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau2018_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-iau2018-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Namesakes">Namesakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit
