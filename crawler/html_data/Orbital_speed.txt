systems, the <b>orbital speed</b> of an astronomical body or object (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">artificial satellite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>) is the <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> at which it <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbits</a> around either the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy)" title="Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)">barycenter</a> or, if the object is much less massive than the largest body in the system, its speed relative to that largest body. The speed in this latter case may be relative to the surface of the larger body or relative to its <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>.\n</p><p>The term can be used to refer to either the mean orbital speed, i.e. the average speed over an entire orbit, or its instantaneous speed at a particular point in its orbit. Maximum (instantaneous) orbital speed occurs at <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">periapsis</a> (perigee, perhelion, etc.), while minimum speed for objects in closed orbits occurs at apoapsis (aphelion, apogee, etc.). In ideal two-body systems, objects in open orbits continue to slow down forever as their distance to the barycenter increases. \n</p><p>When a system approximates a <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_two-body_problem" title="Gravitational two-body problem">two-body system</a>, instantaneous orbital speed at a given point of the orbit can be computed from its distance to the central body and the object\'s <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a>. (Specific orbital energy is constant and independent of position.)\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Radial_trajectories"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Radial trajectories</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Transverse_orbital_speed"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Transverse orbital speed</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Mean_orbital_speed"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Mean orbital speed</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Precise_orbital_speed"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Precise orbital speed</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1"><a href="#Tangential_velocities_at_altitude"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">Tangential velocities at altitude</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radial_trajectories">Radial trajectories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_speed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Radial trajectories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the following, it is assumed that the system is a two-body system and the orbiting object has a negligible mass compared to the larger (central) object. In real-world orbital mechanics, it is the system\'s barycenter, not the larger object, which is at the focus.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">Specific orbital energy</a> = K.E. + P.E. (kinetic energy + potential energy). Since kinetic energy is always non-negative (greater than or equal to zero, &#8805;0) and potential energy is always non-positive (less than or equal to zero, &#8804;0), the sign of this may be positive, zero, or negative and the sign tells us something about the type of orbit:\n</p>\n<ul><li>If the <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a> is positive the orbit is open, following a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">hyperbola</a> with the larger body the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">focus</a> of the hyperbola. Objects in open orbits do not return; once past periapsis their distance from the focus increases without bound. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_hyperbolic_trajectory" title="Radial hyperbolic trajectory">radial hyperbolic trajectory</a></li>\n<li>If the <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a> is zero, (K.E = &#8722;P.E.): the orbit is thus a <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a> with <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">focus</a> at the other body. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_parabolic_trajectory" title="Radial parabolic trajectory">radial parabolic trajectory</a>. Parabolic orbits are also open.</li>\n<li>If the energy is negative, K.E. + P.E. &lt; 0: The orbit is closed. The motion is on an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a> with one <a href="/wiki/Focus_(geometry)" title="Focus (geometry)">focus</a> at the other body. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_elliptic_trajectory" title="Radial elliptic trajectory">radial elliptic trajectory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Free-fall_time" title="Free-fall time">free-fall time</a>. Planets have closed orbits around the Sun.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transverse_orbital_speed">Transverse orbital speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_speed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transverse orbital speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The transverse orbital speed is inversely proportional to the distance to the central body because of the law of conservation of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>, or equivalently,  <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Kepler</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler\'s laws of planetary motion">second law</a>. This states that as a body moves around its orbit during a fixed amount of time, the line from the barycenter to the body sweeps a constant area of the orbital plane, regardless of which part of its orbit the body traces during that period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This law implies that the body moves slower near its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">apoapsis</a> than near its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a>, because at the smaller distance along the arc it needs to move faster to cover the same area.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_orbital_speed">Mean orbital speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
