vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">60</span></span> of one <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degree</a>. Since one degree is <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">360</span></span> of a <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">turn</a> (or complete rotation), one minute of arc is <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004216000000000000&#9824;</span>21<span style="margin-left:.25em;">600</span></span></span></span> of a turn &#8211; it is for this reason that the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_circumference" title="Earth\'s circumference">Earth\'s circumference</a> is almost exactly 21,600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautical_miles" title="Nautical miles">nautical miles</a>. A minute of arc is <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><span arc</b>, <b>arcsecond</b> (arcsec), or <b>arc second</b> is <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span For example, if the "Moon: max." circle is 10&#160;cm wide on a monitor, viewing it from 10.3&#160;m away will show true representation of the sizes.</div></div></div>\n<p>Since antiquity the arcminute and arcsecond have been used in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">ecliptic coordinate system</a>, latitude (&#946;) and longitude (&#955;); in the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system" title="Horizontal coordinate system">horizon system</a>, altitude (Alt) and <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuth</a> (Az); and in the <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system" title="Equatorial coordinate system">equatorial coordinate system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> (&#948;), are all measured in degrees, arcminutes and arcseconds. The principal exception is <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> (RA) in equatorial coordinates, which is measured in time units of hours, minutes, and seconds.\n</p><p>The arcsecond is also often used to describe small astronomical angles such as the angular diameters of planets (e.g. the angular diameter of Venus which varies between 10&#8243; and 60&#8243;), the <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">proper motion</a> of stars, the separation of components of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_star_system" title="Binary star system">binary star systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">parallax</a>, the small change of position of a star in the course of a year or of a solar system body as the Earth rotates. These small angles may also be written in milliarcseconds (mas), or thousandths of an arcsecond. The unit of distance, the <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsec</a>, named from the <i>par</i>allax of one arc <i>sec</i>ond, was developed for such parallax measurements. It is the distance at which the mean radius of the Earth\'s orbit would subtend an angle of one arcsecond.\n</p><p>The ESA <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometric</a> space probe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_mission" title="Gaia mission">Gaia</a>, launched in 2013, can approximate star positions to 7 microarcseconds (&#181;as).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Apart from the Sun, the star with the largest <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">angular diameter</a> from Earth is <a href="/wiki/R_Doradus" title="R Doradus">R Doradus</a>, a red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">supergiant</a> with a diameter of 0.05 arcsecond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup> Because of the effects of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">seeing</a>, ground-based <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a> will smear the image of a star to an angular diameter of about 0.5 arcsecond; in poor seeing conditions this increases to 1.5 arcseconds or even more. The dwarf planet <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> has proven difficult to resolve because its <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">angular diameter</a> is about 0.1 arcsecond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Space telescopes are not affected by the Earth\'s atmosphere but are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffraction_limit#Diffraction_limit_of_telescopes" title="Diffraction limit">diffraction limited</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> can reach an angular size of stars down to about 0.1&#8243;. Techniques exist for improving seeing on the ground. <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_optics" title="Adaptive optics">Adaptive optics</a>, for example, can produce images around 0.05 arcsecond on a 10&#160;m class telescope.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cartography">Cartography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minute_and_second_of_arc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cartography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Minutes (&#8242;) and seconds (&#8243;) of arc are also used in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> one minute of arc along the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> (indeed, any <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>) equals exactly one <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">geographical mile</a> along the Earth\'s equator or approximately one <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a> (1852 meters, or &#8776;1.15078 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_mile" title="Statute mile">statute miles</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> A second of arc, one sixtieth of this amount, is roughly 30 meters or 100 feet. The exact distance varies along <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">meridian arcs</a> because the <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">figure of the Earth</a> is slightly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate</a> (bulges a third of a percent at the equator).\n</p><p>Positions are traditionally given using degrees, minutes, and seconds of arcs for <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, the arc north or south of the equator, and for <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, the arc east or west of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">Prime Meridian</a>. Any position on or above the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">reference ellipsoid</a> can be precisely given with this method. However, when it is inconvenient to use  <a href="/wiki/Radix" title="Radix">base</a>-60 for minutes and seconds, positions are frequently expressed as decimal fractional degrees to an equal amount of precision. Degrees given to three decimal places (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100000000000000&#9824;</span>1000</span></span></span> of a degree) have about <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">4</span></span> the precision of degrees-minutes-seconds (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003360000000000000&#9824;</span>3600</span></span></span> of a degree) and specify locations within about 120 meters or 400 feet.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Property_cadastral_surveying">Property cadastral surveying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minute_and_second_of_arc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Property cadastral surveying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Related to cartography, property boundary <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surveying</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Metes_and_bounds" title="Metes and bounds">metes and bounds</a> system relies on fractions of a degree to describe property lines\' angles in reference to <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_direction" title="Cardinal direction">cardinal directions</a>. A boundary "mete" is described with a beginning reference point, the cardinal direction North or South followed by an angle less than 90 degrees and a second cardinal direction, and a linear distance. The boundary runs the specified linear distance from the beginning point, the direction of the distance being determined by rotating the first cardinal direction the specified angle toward the second cardinal direction. For example, <i>North&#160;65&#176;&#160;39&#8242; 18&#8243; West&#160;85.69&#160;feet</i> would describe a line running from the starting point 85.69 feet in a direction 65&#176; 39&#8242; 18&#8243; (or 65.655&#176;) away from north toward the west.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firearms">Firearms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minute_and_second_of_arc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Firearms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_(mil_and_moa).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2626" data-file-width="2078" decoding="async" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png/220px-Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png/330px-Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png/440px-Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_%28mil_and_moa%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ballistic_table_for_7.62x51_mm_NATO_(mil_and_moa).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Example ballistic table for a given <a href="/wiki/7.62%C3%9751mm_NATO" title="7.62&#215;51mm NATO">7.62&#215;51mm NATO</a> load. Bullet drop and wind drift are shown both in <a href="/wiki/Milliradian" title="Milliradian">mil</a> and minute of angle.</div></div></div>\n<p>The arcminute is commonly found in the <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a> industry and literature, particularly concerning the accuracy of <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifles</a>, though the industry refers to it as <b>minute of angle</b> (MOA). It is especially popular with shooters familiar with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_measurement_system" title="Imperial measurement system">imperial measurement system</a> because 1&#160;MOA is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtend" title="Subtend">subtended</a> by a sphere with a diameter of 1.047 <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inches</a> at 100 <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">yards</a> (2.908&#160;cm at 100&#160;m), a traditional distance on U.S. <a href="/wiki/Shooting_range" title="Shooting range">target ranges</a>. The subtension is linear with the distance, for example, at 500 yards, 1&#160;MOA is subtended by a sphere with a diameter of 5.235 inches, and at 1000 yards 1&#160;MOA is subtended by a sphere with a diameter of 10.47 inches.\nSince many modern <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">telescopic sights</a> are adjustable in half (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>), quarter (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">4</span></span>), or eighth (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">8</span></span>) MOA increments, also known as <i>clicks</i>, zeroing and adjustments are made by counting 2, 4 and 8 clicks per MOA respectively.\n</p><p>For example, if the point of impact is 3 inches high and 1.5 inches left of the point of aim at 100 yards (which for instance could be measured by using a <a href="/wiki/Spotting_scope" title="Spotting scope">spotting scope</a> with a calibrated reticle), the scope needs to be adjusted 3 MOA down, and 1.5 MOA right. Such adjustments are trivial when the scope\'s adjustment dials have a MOA scale printed on them, and even figuring the right number of clicks is relatively easy on scopes that <i>click</i> in fractions of MOA. This makes zeroing and adjustments much easier:\n</p>\n<ul><li>To adjust a &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> MOA scope 3 MOA down and 1.5 MOA right, the scope needs to be adjusted 3 &#215; 2 = 6 clicks down and 1.5 x 2 = 3 clicks right</li>\n<li>To adjust a &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> MOA scope 3 MOA down and 1.5 MOA right, the scope needs to be adjusted 3 x 4 = 12 clicks down and 1.5 &#215; 4 = 6 clicks right</li>\n<li>To adjust a &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> MOA scope 3 MOA down and 1.5 MOA right, the scope needs to be adjusted 3 x 8 = 24 clicks down and 1.5 &#215; 8 = 12 clicks right</li></ul>\n<p>Another common system of measurement in firearm scopes is the <a href="/wiki/Milliradian" title="Milliradian">milliradian</a>. Zeroing a mil based scope is easy for users familiar with <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">base ten</a> systems. The most common adjustment value in mil based scopes is <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">10</span></span> mil (which approximates &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> MOA).\n</p>\n<ul><li>To adjust a <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">10</span></span> mil scope 0.9 mil down and 0.4 mil right, the scope needs to be adjusted 9 clicks down and 4 clicks right (which equals approximately 3 and 1.5 MOA respectively).</li></ul>\n<p>One thing to be aware of is that some MOA scopes, including some higher-end models,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> are calibrated such that an adjustment of 1 MOA on the scope knobs corresponds to exactly 1 inch of impact adjustment on a target at 100 yards, rather than the mathematically correct 1.047". This is commonly known as the Shooter\'s MOA (SMOA) or Inches Per Hundred Yards (IPHY). While the difference between one true MOA and one SMOA is less than half of an inch even at 1000 yards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> this error compounds significantly on longer range shots that may require adjustment upwards of 20&#8211;30 MOA to compensate for the bullet drop. If a shot requires an adjustment of 20 MOA or more, the difference between true MOA and SMOA will add up to 1 inch or more. In competitive target shooting, this might mean the difference between a hit and a miss.\n</p><p>The physical group size equivalent to <i>m</i> minutes of arc can be calculated as follows: group size = tan(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>m</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">60</span></span>)&#160;&#215;&#160;distance. In the example previously given, for 1 minute of arc, and substituting 3,600&#160;inches for 100 yards, 3,600 tan(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">60</span></span>) &#8776; 1.047&#160;inches. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_units" title="Metric units">metric units</a> 1 MOA at 100 meters &#8776; 2.908 centimeters.\n</p><p>Sometimes, a precision firearm\'s accuracy will be measured in MOA. This simply means that under ideal conditions i.e. no wind, match-grade ammo, clean barrel, and a vise or a benchrest used to eliminate shooter error, the gun is capable of producing a <a href="/wiki/Shot_grouping" title="Shot grouping">group of shots</a> whose center points (center-to-center) fit into a circle, the average diameter of circles in several groups can be subtended by that amount of arc. For example, a <i>1 MOA rifle</i> should be capable, under ideal conditions, of shooting an average 1-inch groups at 100 yards. Most higher-end rifles are warrantied by their manufacturer to shoot under a given MOA threshold (typically 1 MOA or better) with specific ammunition and no error on the shooter\'s part. For example, Remington\'s <a href="/wiki/M24_Sniper_Weapon_System" title="M24 Sniper Weapon System">M24 Sniper Weapon System</a> is required to shoot 0.8 MOA or better, or be rejected.\n</p><p>Rifle manufacturers and gun magazines often refer to this capability as <i>sub-MOA</i>, meaning it shoots under 1 MOA. This means that a single group of 3 to 5 shots at 100 yards, or the average of several groups, will measure less than 1 MOA between the two furthest shots in the group, i.e. all shots fall within 1 MOA. If larger samples are taken (i.e., more shots per group) then group size typically increases, however this will ultimately average out. If a rifle was truly a 1 MOA rifle, it would be just as likely that two consecutive shots land exactly on top of each other as that they land 1 MOA apart. For 5 shot groups, based on 95% <a href="/wiki/Confidence_interval" title="Confidence interval">confidence</a> a rifle that normally shoots 1 MOA can be expected to shoot groups between 0.58 MOA and 1.47 MOA, although the majority of these groups will be under 1 MOA. What this means in practice is if a rifle that shoots 1-inch groups on average at 100 yards shoots a group measuring 0.7 inches followed by a group that is 1.3 inches this is not statistically abnormal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">Metric System</a> counterpart of the MOA is the <a href="/wiki/Milliradian" title="Milliradian">milliradian</a> or <b>mil</b>, being equal to one 1000th of the target range, laid out on a circle that has the observer as centre and the target range as radius. The number of mils on a full such circle therefore always is equal to 2 &#215; <span class="texhtml">&#960;</span> &#215; 1000, regardless the target range. Therefore, 1 MOA &#8776; 0.2908 mil. This means that an object which spans 1 mil on the <a href="/wiki/Reticle" title="Reticle">reticle</a> is at a range that is in meters equal to the object\'s size in millimeters (e.g. an object of 100&#160;mm @ 1 mrad is 100 meters away). So there is no conversion factor required, contrary to the MOA system.  A reticle with markings (hashes or dots) spaced with a one mil apart (or a fraction of a mil) are collectively called a mil reticle. If the markings are round they are called <b>mil-dots</b>.\n</p><p>In the table below conversions from mil to metric values are exact (e.g. 0.1&#160;mil equals exactly 1&#160;cm at 100 meters), while
