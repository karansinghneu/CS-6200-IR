950 &#8211; c. 1003), known as <b>Erik the Red</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a>: <i lang="non">Eir&#237;kr hinn rau&#240;i</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Norse</a> explorer, remembered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> and Icelandic <a href="/wiki/Saga" title="Saga">saga</a> sources as having founded the first <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">settlement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Sagas_of_Icelanders" title="Sagas of Icelanders">Icelandic sagas</a>, he was born in the <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A6ren" title="J&#230;ren">J&#230;ren</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Rogaland" title="Rogaland">Rogaland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, as the son of <a href="/wiki/Thorvald_Asvaldsson" title="Thorvald Asvaldsson">&#222;orvald &#193;svaldsson</a>. He therefore also appears, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymically</a>, as Erik Thorvaldsson (<i>Eir&#237;kr &#222;orvaldsson</i>). The appellation "the Red" most likely refers to his hair color<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the color of his beard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leif_Erikson" title="Leif Erikson">Leif Erikson</a>, the famous <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Icelandic</a> explorer, was Erik\'s son.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_the_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Exiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Erik the Red\'s father (<a href="/wiki/Thorvald_Asvaldsson" title="Thorvald Asvaldsson">Thorvald Asvaldsson</a>) was banished from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> because of some killings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He left with his son Erik to northwest Iceland, where he died before 980. According to the "Gr&#230;nlendinga Saga," &#8220;There was a man called Thorvald, who was the father of Eirik the Red. He and Eirik left their home in Jaederen, in Norway, because of some killings and went to Iceland, which had been extensively settled by then;&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> when Erik was about 10 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> He sailed west from Norway with his family and settled in <a href="/wiki/Hornstrandir" title="Hornstrandir">Hornstrandir</a> in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-7">[7]</a></sup> The Icelanders later sentenced Erik to exile for three years for killing Eyiolf the Foul around the year 982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After marrying Thjodhild (&#222;j&#243;&#240;hildr), Erik moved to Haukadalr (Hawksdale) where he built a farm called <a href="/wiki/Eir%C3%ADkssta%C3%B0ir" title="Eir&#237;kssta&#240;ir">Eir&#237;kssta&#240;ir</a>. The initial confrontation occurred when his <a href="/wiki/Thrall" title="Thrall">thralls</a> (slaves) started a <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslide</a> on the neighboring farm belonging to Valthjof (<i>Val&#254;j&#243;fr</i>). Valthjof\'s friend, Eyiolf the Foul (<i>Eyj&#243;lfr saurr</i>), killed the thralls. In retaliation, Erik killed Eyjiolf and Holmgang-Hrafn (<i>H&#243;lmg&#246;ngu-Hrafn</i>). Eyiolf\'s kinsmen demanded his banishment from Haukadal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Erik then moved to the island of &#214;xney. He asked Thorgest (&#222;&#243;rgestr) to keep his <i>setstokkr</i> &#8211; inherited ornamented beams of significant mystical value,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which his father had brought from Norway. When he had finished his new house, he went back to get them, but they "could not be obtained". Erik then went to Breidabolstad and took them. These are likely to have been Thorgest\'s <i>setstokkr</i>, although the sagas are unclear at this point.\n</p><p>Thorgest gave chase, and in the ensuing fight Erik slew both Thorgest\'s sons and "a few other men".\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>After this, each of them retained a considerable body of men with him at his home. Styr gave Erik his support, as did also Eyiolf of Sviney, Thorbjiorn, Vifil\'s son, and the sons of Thorbrand of Alptafirth; while Thorgest was backed by the sons of Thord the Yeller, and Thorgeir of <a href="/wiki/H%C3%ADtardalur" title="H&#237;tardalur">Hitardal</a>, Aslak of Langadal and his son Illugi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The dispute was resolved at an assembly, the <a href="/wiki/Thing_(assembly)" title="Thing (assembly)">Thing</a>, with the result that Erik was Nearly a century before Erik, strong winds had driven Gunnbj&#246;rn towards a land he called "Gunnbjarnarsker" ("<a href="/wiki/Gunnbj%C3%B6rn%27s_skerries" title="Gunnbj&#246;rn\'s skerries">Gunnbj&#246;rn\'s skerries</a>"). After Gunnbj&#246;rn, <a href="/wiki/Sn%C3%A6bj%C3%B6rn_Galti" title="Sn&#230;bj&#246;rn Galti">Sn&#230;bj&#246;rn Galti</a> had also visited Greenland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to records from the time, Galti headed the first Norse attempt to colonize Greenland, which ended in disaster. Erik the Red was the first <i>permanent</i> European settler.\n</p><p>In this context, about 982, Erik sailed to a somewhat mysterious and little-known land.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He rounded the southern tip of the island (later known as <a href="/wiki/Cape_Farewell,_Greenland" title="Cape Farewell, Greenland">Cape Farewell</a>) and sailed up the western coast. He eventually reached a part of the coast that, for the most part, seemed ice-free and consequently had conditions&#8212;similar to those of Iceland&#8212;that promised growth and future prosperity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> According to the <i>Saga of Erik the Red</i>, he spent his three years of exile exploring this land. The first winter he spent on the island of Eiriksey, the second winter he passed in Eiriksholmar (close to Hvarfsgnipa). In the final summer he explored as far north as Snaefell and into Hrafnsfjord.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>When Erik returned to Iceland after his exile had expired, he is said to have brought with him stories of "Greenland". Erik deliberately gave the land a more appealing name than "Iceland" in order to lure potential settlers. He explained, "people would be attracted to go there if it had a favorable name".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saga_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-saga-12">[12]</a></sup> He knew that the success of any settlement in Greenland would need the support of as many people as possible. His salesmanship proved successful, as many people (especially "those <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> living on poor land in Iceland" and those that had suffered a "recent famine") became convinced that Greenland held great opportunity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>After spending the winter in Iceland, Erik returned to Greenland in 985 with a large number of colonists. Out of 25 ships that left for Greenland only 14 arrived, 11 were lost at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The Icelanders established two colonies on the southwest coast: the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Settlement" title="Eastern Settlement">Eastern Settlement</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eystribygg%C3%B0" title="Eystribygg&#240;">Eystribygg&#240;</a>, in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Qaqortoq" title="Qaqortoq">Qaqortoq</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Settlement" title="Western Settlement">Western Settlement</a> or Vestribygg&#240;, close to present-day <a href="/wiki/Nuuk" title="Nuuk">Nuuk</a>. (Eventually, a <a href="/wiki/Ivittuut" title="Ivittuut">Middle Settlement</a> grew, but many people suggest it formed part of the Western Settlement.) The Eastern and Western Settlements, both established on the southwest coast, proved the only two areas suitable for farming. During the summers, when the weather favored travel more, each settlement would send an army of men to hunt in <a href="/wiki/Disko_Bay" title="Disko Bay">Disko Bay</a> above the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> for food and other valuable commodities such as seals (used for rope), <a href="/wiki/Ivory" title="Ivory">ivory</a> from <a href="/wiki/Walrus" title="Walrus">walrus</a> tusks, and <a href="/wiki/Cetacean_stranding" title="Cetacean stranding">beached whales</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Eystribygg.C3.B0"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Eystribygg&#240;">Eystribygg&#240;</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_the_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eystribygg&#240;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Eystribygg&#240; or Eastern Settlement, Erik built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estate_(house)" title="Estate (house)">estate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brattahl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Brattahl&#237;&#240;">Brattahl&#237;&#240;</a>, near present-day <a href="/wiki/Narsarsuaq" title="Narsarsuaq">Narsarsuaq</a>. He held the title of <a href="/wiki/Paramount_chieftain" title="Paramount chieftain">paramount chieftain</a> of Greenland and became both greatly respected and wealthy.\n</p><p>The settlement flourished, growing to 5,000 inhabitants spread over a considerable area along Eriksfjord and neighboring <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a>. Groups of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigrant" title="Immigrant">immigrants</a> escaping overcrowding in Iceland joined the original party. However, one group of immigrants which arrived in 1002 brought with it an <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> that ravaged the colony, killing many of its leading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citizen" title="Citizen">citizens</a>, including Erik himself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the colony rebounded and survived until the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> made the land marginal for European life-styles in the 15th century (shortly before <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>\'s first <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">voyage to the Americas</a> in 1492). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pirate" title="Pirate">Pirate</a> raids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> conflict with <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> moving into the Norse territories, and the colony\'s abandonment by Norway became other factors in its decline.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relations">Relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_the_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: in Haukadalur</div></div></div>\n<p>Medieval Icelandic tradition relates that Erik the Red and his wife &#222;j&#243;&#240;hildr (Thjodhildr) had four children: a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Freyd%C3%ADs_Eir%C3%ADksd%C3%B3ttir" title="Freyd&#237;s Eir&#237;ksd&#243;ttir">Freyd&#237;s</a>, and three sons, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_explorers" title="List of explorers">explorer</a> Leif Eir&#237;ksson, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorvaldr_Eir%C3%ADksson" title="&#222;orvaldr Eir&#237;ksson">&#222;orvaldr</a> (Thorvald) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%9Eorsteinn_Eir%C3%ADksson" title="&#222;orsteinn Eir&#237;ksson">&#222;orsteinn</a> (Thorstein). Erik himself remained a follower of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_paganism" title="Norse paganism">Norse paganism</a>, unlike his son Leif and Leif\'s wife, who became <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a>. After being baptized by king <a href="/wiki/Olaf_Tryggvason" title="Olaf Tryggvason">Olaf Tryggvason</a>, Leif brought the message of Christianity to Greenland, becoming something of an evangelist. While his wife took heartily to the religion, even commissioning Greenland\'s first church, Erik greatly disliked the faith and stuck to his Norse Gods &#8211; which, the sagas relate, led Thjodhild to withhold intercourse from her husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> Thjothhild was the daughter of J&#248;rundur Ulfsson and Thorbj&#248;rg Gilsdottir (from whom Gilsfj&#248;rd is named). J&#248;rund\'s mother Bj&#248;rg was granddaughter to <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Ireland" title="Monarchy of Ireland">Irish king</a> <a href="/wiki/Cerball_mac_D%C3%BAnlainge" title="Cerball mac D&#250;nlainge">Cerball mac D&#250;nlainge</a> (Kjarval) through his daughter Rafarta.\n</p><p>While not the first to sight the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> continent, Leif Erikson became the first Viking to explore the land of <a href="/wiki/Vinland" title="Vinland">Vinland</a> (part of North America, probably near modern-day <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>). Leif invited his father on the voyage, but according to legend, Erik fell off his horse on his way to the ship and took this as a bad sign, leaving his son to continue without his company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> Erik died the winter after his son\'s departure.  There is no evidence that Leif was aware of his father\'s death until he returned to Greenland.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Comparisons_to_Gr.C5.93nlendinga_Saga"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_to_Gr&#339;nlendinga_Saga">Comparisons to Gr&#339;nlendinga Saga</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_the_Red&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparisons to Gr&#339;nlendinga Saga">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are numerous parallels to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gr%C5%93nlendinga_saga" title="Gr&#339;nlendinga saga">Gr&#339;nlendinga saga</a>, including recurring characters and recounts of the same expeditions, although with a few notable differences. The saga of Erik the Red presents a number of the expeditions in the Gr&#339;nlendinga saga as just one expedition led by Thorfinnr Karlsefni, although Thorvaldr Eir&#237;ksson, Freyd&#237;s Eir&#237;kssd&#243;ttir and Karlsefni\'s wife Gu&#240;r&#237;&#240;r play key roles in the retelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Another notable difference is the location of their settlements.  In the Gr&#339;nlendinga saga, Karlsefni and the others settle in a place that is only referred to as Vinland, while in Erik the Red\'s Saga, they form two base settlements:  Straumfj&#491;r&#240;r where they spend winter and the following spring, and Hop, where they later settle and run into problems with the natives, as depicted in the Gr&#339;nlendinga saga.  Otherwise, the tales are largely similar, both with heavy focuses on the exploits of Thorfinnr Karlsefni and his wife Gu&#240;r&#237;&#240;r.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
