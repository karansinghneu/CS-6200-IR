The <b>kelvin</b> (symbol: K) is the <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">base unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI).\n</p><p>Until 2018, the kelvin was defined as the fraction &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>273.16</sub></span> of the thermodynamic temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point#Triple_point_of_water" title="Triple point">triple point of water</a> (exactly 0.01&#160;&#176;C or 32.018&#160;&#176;F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res413_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-res413-1">[1]</a></sup> In other words, it was defined such that the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of water is exactly 273.16&#160;K.\n</p><p>On 16 November 2018, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redefinition_of_SI_base_units" title="Redefinition of SI base units">new definition</a> was adopted, in terms of a fixed value of the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a>. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_metrology#Legal_metrology" title="Legal metrology">legal metrology</a> purposes, the new definition will officially come into force on 20 May 2019 (the 130th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Metre_Convention" title="Metre Convention">Metre Convention</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draft-resolution-A_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-draft-resolution-A-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Kelvin scale is named after the Belfast-born, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glasgow_University" title="Glasgow University">Glasgow University</a> engineer and physicist <a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin</a> (1824&#8211;1907), who wrote of the need for an "absolute thermometric scale". Unlike the degree <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a> and degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>, the kelvin is not referred to or written as a <a href="/wiki/Degree_(temperature)" title="Degree (temperature)">degree</a>. The kelvin is the primary unit of temperature measurement in the physical sciences, but is often used in conjunction with the degree Celsius, which has the same magnitude. The definition implies that absolute zero (0&#160;K) is equivalent to &#8722;273.15&#160;&#176;C (&#8722;459.67&#160;&#176;F).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" temperature">Thermodynamic temperature &#167;&#160;History</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" unit</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1848, William Thomson, who later was made Lord Kelvin, wrote in his paper, <i>On an Absolute Thermometric Scale</i>, of the need for a scale whereby "infinite cold" (absolute zero) was the scale\'s null point, and which used the degree Celsius for its unit increment. Kelvin calculated that absolute zero was equivalent to &#8722;273&#160;&#176;C on the air thermometers of the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lordkelvin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lordkelvin-3">[3]</a></sup> This absolute scale is known today as the Kelvin thermodynamic temperature scale. Kelvin\'s value of "&#8722;273" was the negative reciprocal of 0.00366&#8212;the accepted expansion coefficient of gas per degree Celsius relative to the ice point, giving a remarkable consistency to the currently accepted value.\n</p><p>In 1954, Resolution 3 of the 10th <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> (CGPM) gave the Kelvin scale its modern definition by designating the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_point_of_water" title="Triple point of water">triple point of water</a> as its second defining point and assigned its temperature to exactly 273.16 kelvins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res310_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-res310-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1967/1968, Resolution 3 of the 13th CGPM renamed the unit increment of thermodynamic temperature "kelvin", symbol K, replacing "degree Kelvin", symbol &#176;K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res313_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-res313-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, feeling it useful to more explicitly define the magnitude of the unit increment, the 13th CGPM also held in Resolution 4 that "The kelvin, unit of thermodynamic temperature, is equal to the fraction &#8203;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>273.16</sub></span> of the thermodynamic temperature of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple_point_of_water" title="Triple point of water">triple point of water</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res413_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-res413-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">Comit&#233; International des Poids et Mesures</a> (CIPM), a committee of the CGPM, affirmed that for the purposes of delineating the temperature of the triple point of water, the definition of the Kelvin thermodynamic temperature scale would refer to water having an isotopic composition specified as <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water" title="Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water">Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sib2115_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sib2115-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2018, Resolution A of the 26th CGPM adopted a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redefinition_of_SI_base_units" title="Redefinition of SI base units">redefinition of SI base units</a> which included redefining the Kelvin in terms of a fixed value for the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a> of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977138064900000000&#9824;</span>1.380<span style="margin-left:.25em;">649</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;23</sup>&#160;J/K</span>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_conventions">Usage conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When spelled out or spoken, the unit is pluralised using the same grammatical rules as for other <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units#Writing_the_unit_names" title="International System of Units">SI units</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">ohm</a> (e.g. "the triple point of water is exactly 273.16 kelvins"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sib211_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sib211-7">[7]</a></sup>). When reference is made to the "Kelvin <i>scale</i>", the word "kelvin"&#8212;which is normally a noun&#8212;functions <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">adjectivally</a> to modify the noun "scale" and is capitalized. As with most other SI unit symbols (angle symbols, e.g. 45&#176;&#160;3&#8242;&#160;4&#8243;, are the exception) there is a space between the numeric value and the kelvin symbol (e.g. "99.987&#160;K").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nist-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sib533_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sib533-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before the 13th CGPM in 1967&#8211;1968, the unit kelvin was called a "degree", the same as with the other temperature scales at the time. It was distinguished from the other scales with either the adjective suffix "Kelvin" ("degree Kelvin") or with "absolute" ("degree absolute") and its symbol was &#176;K. The latter term (degree absolute), which was the unit\'s official name from 1948 until 1954, was ambiguous since it could also be interpreted as referring to the <a href="/wiki/Rankine_scale" title="Rankine scale">Rankine scale</a>. Before the 13th CGPM, the plural form was "degrees absolute". The 13th CGPM changed the unit name to simply "kelvin" (symbol: K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The omission of "degree" indicates that it is not relative to an arbitrary reference point like the Celsius and Fahrenheit scales (although the Rankine scale continued to use "degree Rankine"), but rather an absolute unit of measure which can be manipulated algebraically (e.g. multiplied by two to indicate twice the amount of "mean energy" available among elementary degrees of freedom of the system).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_conjunction_with_degrees_Celsius">Use in conjunction with degrees Celsius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in conjunction with degrees Celsius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4729" data-file-width="2717" decoding="async" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg/170px-CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg/255px-CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg/340px-CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CelsiusKelvinThermometer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A thermometer calibrated in degrees Celsius (left) and kelvins (right).</div></div></div>\n<p>In science and engineering, degrees Celsius and kelvins are often used simultaneously in the same article, where absolute temperatures are given in degrees Celsius, but temperature intervals are given in kelvins.  E.g. "its measured value was <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273160280000000&#9824;</span>0.010<span style="margin-left:.25em;">28</span>&#160;&#176;C</span> with an uncertainty of 60&#160;&#181;K."\n</p><p>This practice is permissible because the degree Celsius is a special name for the kelvin for use in expressing relative temperatures, and the magnitude of the degree Celsius is exactly equal to that of the kelvin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sib222_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sib222-11">[11]</a></sup> Notwithstanding that the official endorsement provided by Resolution 3 of the 13th CGPM states "a temperature interval may also be expressed in degrees Celsius",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-res313_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-res313-5">[5]</a></sup> the practice of simultaneously using both &#176;C and K is widespread throughout the scientific world. The use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix#List_of_SI_prefixes" title="SI prefix">SI prefixed</a> forms of the degree Celsius (such as <i>&#181;&#176;C</i> or <em>microdegree Celsius</em>) to express a temperature interval has not been widely adopted.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2018_redefinition">2018 redefinition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2018 redefinition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redefinition_of_SI_base_units" title="Redefinition of SI base units">Redefinition of SI base units</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CODATA_2018" title="CODATA 2018">CODATA 2018</a></div>\n<p>In 2005 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIPM" title="CIPM">CIPM</a> embarked on a programme to redefine the kelvin (along with the other SI units) using a more experimentally rigorous methodology. In particular, the committee proposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redefinition_of_SI_base_units#Kelvin" title="Redefinition of SI base units">redefining the kelvin</a> such that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_constant" title="Boltzmann\'s constant">Boltzmann\'s constant</a> takes the exact value <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977138065049999999&#9824;</span>1.380<span style="margin-left:.25em;">6505</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;23</sup>&#160;J/K</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draft_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-draft-12">[12]</a></sup> The committee had hoped that the programme would be completed in time for its adoption by the CGPM at its 2011 meeting, but at the 2011 meeting the decision was postponed to the 2014 meeting when it would be considered as part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proposed_redefinition_of_SI_base_units" title="Proposed redefinition of SI base units">larger programme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The redefinition was further postponed in 2014, pending more accurate measurements of Boltzmann\'s constant in terms of the current definition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\nbut was finally adopted at the 26th CGPM in late 2018, with a value of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">k</span>&#160;=&#160;<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977138064900000000&#9824;</span>1.380<span style="margin-left:.25em;">649</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;23</sup>&#160;J/K</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-draft_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-draft-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-codata2017_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-codata2017-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From a scientific point of view, the main advantage is that this will allow measurements at very low and very high temperatures to be made more accurately, as the techniques used depend on the Boltzmann constant.  It also has the philosophical advantage of being independent of any particular substance.  The challenge was to avoid degrading the accuracy of measurements close to the triple point.  From a practical point of view, the redefinition will pass unnoticed; water will still freeze at 273.15 K (0 &#176;C),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the triple point of water will continue to be a commonly used laboratory reference temperature.\n</p><p>The difference is that, before the redefinition, the triple point of water was exact and the Boltzmann constant had a measured value of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977138064903000000&#9824;</span>1.380<span style="margin-left:.25em;">649</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">03</span>(51)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;23</sup>&#160;J/K</span>, with a relative standard uncertainty of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6993370000000000000&#9824;</span>3.7<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">&#215;</span>10<sup>&#8722;7</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-codata2017_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-codata2017-15">[15]</a></sup>  Afterward, the Boltzmann constant is exact and the uncertainty is transferred to the triple point of water, which is now <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273160000000000&#9824;</span>273.1600(1)&#160;K</span>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Temperature_conversion_formulas" title="Temperature conversion formulas">Comparisons among various temperature scales</a></small>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colour_temperature">Colour temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Colour temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_constant" title="Stefan&#8211;Boltzmann constant">Stefan&#8211;Boltzmann constant</a></div>\n<p>The kelvin is often used as a measure of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_temperature" title="Colour temperature">colour temperature</a> of light sources. Colour temperature is based upon the principle that a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> radiator emits light with a frequency distribution characteristic of its temperature. Black bodies at temperatures below about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003400000000000000&#9824;</span>4000&#160;K</span> appear reddish, whereas those above about <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003750000000000000&#9824;</span>7500&#160;K</span> appear bluish. Colour temperature is important in the fields of image projection and <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, where a colour temperature of approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003560000000000000&#9824;</span>5600&#160;K</span> is required to match "daylight" film emulsions. In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">stellar classification</a> of stars and their place on the <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram" title="Hertzsprung&#8211;Russell diagram">Hertzsprung&#8211;Russell diagram</a> are based, in part, upon their surface temperature, known as <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a>. The photosphere of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, for instance, has an effective temperature of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003577800000000000&#9824;</span>5778&#160;K</span>.\n</p><p>Digital cameras and photographic software often use colour temperature in K in edit and setup menus. The simple guide is that higher colour temperature produces an image with enhanced white and blue hues. The reduction in colour temperature produces an image more dominated by reddish, <a href="/wiki/Color_theory#Warm_vs._cool_colors" title="Color theory">"warmer" colours</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kelvin_as_a_unit_of_noise_temperature">Kelvin as a unit of noise temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kelvin as a unit of noise temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Noise_figure" title="Noise figure">Noise figure</a></div>\n<p>In electronics, the kelvin is used as an indicator of how <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_noise" title="Electronic noise">noisy</a> a circuit is in relation to an ultimate <a href="/wiki/Noise_floor" title="Noise floor">noise floor</a>, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Noise_temperature" title="Noise temperature">noise temperature</a>. The so-called <a href="/wiki/Johnson%E2%80%93Nyquist_noise" title="Johnson&#8211;Nyquist noise">Johnson&#8211;Nyquist noise</a> of discrete resistors and capacitors is a type of thermal noise derived from the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a> and can be used to determine the noise temperature of a circuit using the <a href="/wiki/Friis_formulas_for_noise" title="Friis formulas for noise">Friis formulas for noise</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode_character">Unicode character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Unicode character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The symbol is encoded in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> at code point <span class="nowrap"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r861301850">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced">U+212A</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">&#8490;</span> <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872446130">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">KELVIN SIGN</span>. However, this is a <a href="/wiki/Unicode_compatibility_characters" title="Unicode compatibility characters">compatibility character</a> provided for compatibility with legacy encodings. The Unicode standard recommends using <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+004B</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">K</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">LATIN CAPITAL LETTER K</span> instead; that is, a normal capital K. "Three letterlike symbols have been given canonical equivalence to regular letters: <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+2126</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">&#8486;</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">OHM SIGN</span>, <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+212A</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">&#8490;</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">KELVIN SIGN</span>, and <span class="nowrap"><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861301850" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="monospaced">U+212B</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">&#8491;</span> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r872446130" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">ANGSTROM SIGN</span>. In all three instances, the regular letter should be used."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparison_of_temperature_scales" title="Comparison of temperature scales">Comparison of temperature scales</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/International_Temperature_Scale_of_1990" title="International Temperature Scale of 1990">International Temperature Scale of 1990</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Negative_temperature" title="Negative temperature">Negative temperature</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes_and_references">Notes and references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Notes and references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-res413-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-res413_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-res413_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="https://www.bipm.org/en/CGPM/db/13/4/" rel="nofollow">"Resolution 4: Definition of the SI unit of thermodynamic temperature (kelvin)"</a>. <i>Resolutions of the 13th CGPM</i>.
