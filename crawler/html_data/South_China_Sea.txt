href="/wiki/Marginal_sea" title="Marginal sea">marginal sea</a> that is part of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, encompassing an area from the <a href="/wiki/Karimata_Strait" title="Karimata Strait">Karimata</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Malacca Straits</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Strait" title="Taiwan Strait">Strait of Taiwan</a> of around 3,500,000 square kilometres (1,400,000&#160;sq&#160;mi). The sea carries tremendous strategic importance; one-third of the world\'s shipping passes through it carrying over $3 trillion in trade each year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it contains lucrative fisheries that are crucial for the food security of millions in Southeast Asia, and huge oil and gas reserves are believed to lie beneath its seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> (IHO) <i>Limits of Oceans and Seas</i>, 3rd edition (1953), it is located<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHO_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IHO-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>south of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>;</li>\n<li>east of <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>;</li>\n<li>west of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>;</li>\n<li>east of the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strait_of_Singapore" title="Strait of Singapore">Strait of Singapore</a> in the western, and</li>\n<li>north of the <a href="/wiki/Bangka_Belitung_Islands" title="Bangka Belitung Islands">Bangka Belitung Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a></li></ul>\n<p>However, in its unapproved draft 4th edition (1986),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> IHO proposed the <a href="/wiki/Natuna_Sea" title="Natuna Sea">Natuna Sea</a>, thus the South China Sea southern boundary was shifted northward, from north of the Bangka Belitung Islands to\n</p>\n<ul><li>north and northeast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natuna_Islands" title="Natuna Islands">Natuna Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHO-1986_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IHO-1986-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The minute <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea_Islands" title="South China Sea Islands">South China Sea Islands</a>, collectively an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>, number in the hundreds. The sea and its mostly uninhabited islands are subject to competing claims of sovereignty by several countries. These claims are also reflected in the variety of names used for the islands and the sea.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" China Sea</i> is the dominant term used in English for the sea, and the name in most European languages is equivalent. This name is a result of early European interest in the sea as a route from Europe and South Asia to the trading opportunities of China. In the sixteenth century Portuguese sailors called it the China Sea (<i>Mare da China</i>); later needs to differentiate it from nearby bodies of water led to calling it the South China Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">International Hydrographic Organization</a> refers to the sea as "South China Sea (Nan Hai)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHO_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-IHO-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yizhoushu" title="Yizhoushu">Yizhoushu</a></i>, which was a chronicle of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Zhou" title="Western Zhou">Western Zhou</a> dynasty (1046&#8211;771 BCE) gives the first Chinese name for the South China Sea as <i>Nanfang Hai</i> (Chinese: <span lang="zh">&#21335;&#26041;&#28023;</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">N&#225;nf&#257;ng H&#462;i</span></i>; literally: "Southern Sea"), claiming that barbarians from that sea gave tributes of <a href="/wiki/Hawksbill_sea_turtle" title="Hawksbill sea turtle">hawksbill sea turtles</a> to the Zhou rulers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shen_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shen-9">[9]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Poetry" title="Classic of Poetry">Classic of Poetry</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuo_Zhuan" title="Zuo Zhuan">Zuo Zhuan</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Guoyu_(book)" title="Guoyu (book)">Guoyu</a></i> classics of the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (771&#8211;476 BCE) also referred to the sea, but by the name <i>Nan Hai</i> (Chinese: <span lang="zh">&#21335;&#28023;</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">N&#225;n H&#462;i</span></i>; literally: "South Sea") in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Chu_(state)" title="Chu (state)">State of Chu\'s</a> expeditions there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shen_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shen-9">[9]</a></sup> Nan Hai, the South Sea, was one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Seas" title="Four Seas">Four Seas</a> of Chinese literature. There are three other seas, one for each of the four cardinal directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Han" title="Eastern Han">Eastern Han</a> dynasty (23&#8211;220 CE), China\'s rulers called the Sea <i>Zhang Hai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#28466;&#28023;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zh&#462;ng H&#462;i</span></i>; literally: "distended sea").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shen_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shen-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Fei Hai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#27832;&#28023;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">F&#232;i H&#462;i</span></i>; literally: "boil sea") became popular during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_and_Northern_Dynasties" title="Southern and Northern Dynasties">Southern and Northern Dynasties</a> period. Usage of the current Chinese name, <i>Nan Hai</i> (South Sea), became gradually widespread during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Southeast Asia it was once called the <i>Champa Sea</i> or <i>Sea of Cham</i>, after the maritime kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Champa" title="Champa">Champa</a> that flourished there before the sixteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG-The_Cham_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NG-The_Cham-12">[12]</a></sup> The majority of the sea came under Japanese naval control during World War II following the military acquisition of many surrounding South East Asian territories in 1941. Japan calls the sea <i>Minami <a href="/wiki/Shina_(word)" title="Shina (word)">Shina</a> Kai</i> "South China Sea". This was written &#21335;&#25903;&#37027;&#28023; until 2004, when the Japanese Foreign Ministry and other departments switched the spelling to &#21335;&#12471;&#12490;&#28023;, which has become the standard usage in Japan.\n</p><p>In China, it is called the "South Sea", &#21335;&#28023; <i>N&#225;nh&#462;i</i>, and in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> the "East Sea", <i>Bi&#7875;n &#272;&#244;ng</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, it was long called the "South China Sea" (<i>Dagat Timog Tsina</i> in <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>, <i>Laut China Selatan</i> in <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>), with the part within Philippine territorial waters often called the "<a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> Sea", <i>Dagat Luzon</i>, by the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, following an escalation of the <a href="/wiki/Spratly_Islands_dispute" title="Spratly Islands dispute">Spratly Islands dispute</a> in 2011, various Philippine government agencies started using the name "West Philippine Sea". A <a href="/wiki/PAGASA" title="PAGASA">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA)</a> spokesperson said that the sea to the east of the Philippines will continue to be called the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Sea" title="Philippine Sea">Philippine Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2012, Philippine President <a href="/wiki/Benigno_Aquino_III" title="Benigno Aquino III">Benigno Aquino III</a> signed Administrative Order No. 29, mandating that all government agencies use the name "West Philippine Sea" to refer to the parts of the South China Sea within the Philippines\' <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luzon_Sea" title="Luzon Sea">Luzon Sea</a> as well as the waters around, within and adjacent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalayaan_Island_Group" title="Kalayaan Island Group">Kalayaan Island Group</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bajo_de_Masinloc" title="Bajo de Masinloc">Bajo de Masinloc</a>, and tasked the <a href="/wiki/National_Mapping_and_Resource_Information_Authority" title="National Mapping and Resource Information Authority">National Mapping and Resource Information Authority</a> (NAMRIA) to use the name in official maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2017, to assert its sovereignty, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> renamed the northern reaches of its <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zone</a> in the South China Sea as the "North Natuna Sea", which is located north of the Indonesian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natuna_Islands" title="Natuna Islands">Natuna Islands</a>, bordering the southern Vietnam exclusive economic zone, corresponding to the southern end of the South China Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The "Natuna Sea" is located south of Natuna Island within Indonesian territorial waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Therefore, Indonesia has named two seas that are portions of the South China Sea; the <a href="/wiki/Natuna_Sea" title="Natuna Sea">Natuna Sea</a> located between Natuna Islands and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lingga_Archipelago" title="Lingga Archipelago">Lingga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tambelan_Archipelago" title="Tambelan Archipelago">Tambelan Archipelagos</a>, and the North Natuna Sea located between the Natuna Islands and <a href="/wiki/Cape_C%C3%A0_Mau" title="Cape C&#224; Mau">Cape C&#224; Mau</a> on the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a> in Vietnam.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span></h2>\n<p>States and territories with during <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">recent ice ages</a> global sea level was hundreds of metres lower, and <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> was part of the Asian mainland.\n</p><p>The South China Sea opened around <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">45 million years ago</a> when the "<a href="/wiki/Dangerous_Ground_(South_China_Sea)" title="Dangerous Ground (South China Sea)">Dangerous Ground</a>" rifted away from southern China. Extension culminated in <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a> around 30 million years ago, a process that propagated to the SW resulting in the V-shaped basin we see today. Extension ceased around 17 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Arguments have continued about the role of tectonic extrusion in forming the basin. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Tapponnier" title="Paul Tapponnier">Paul Tapponnier</a> and colleagues have argued that as India collides with Asia it pushes <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> to the SE. The relative shear between Indochina and China caused the South China Sea to open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This view is disputed by geologists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> who do not consider Indochina to have moved far relative to mainland Asia. Marine geophysical studies in the Gulf of Tonkin by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Clift" title="Peter Clift">Peter Clift</a> has shown that the <a href="/wiki/Red_River_Fault" title="Red River Fault">Red River Fault</a> was active and causing basin formation at least by 37 million years ago in the NW South China Sea, consistent with extrusion playing a part in the formation of the sea. Since opening the South China Sea has been the repository of large sediment volumes delivered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_River" title="Mekong River">Mekong River</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_River_(Vietnam)" title="Red River (Vietnam)">Red River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_(China)" title="Pearl River (China)">Pearl River</a>. Several of these deltas are rich in oil and gas deposits.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Islands_and_seamounts">Islands and seamounts</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea_Islands" title="South China Sea Islands">South China Sea Islands</a></div>\n<p>The South China Sea contains over 250 small <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atoll" title="Atoll">atolls</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cays" title="Cays">cays</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoals" title="Shoals">shoals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reefs</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandbars" title="Sandbars">sandbars</a>, most of which have no indigenous people, many of which are naturally under water at high tide, and some of which are permanently submerged. It is the second most used <a href="/wiki/Sea_lane" title="Sea lane">sea lane</a> in the world, while in terms of world annual merchant fleet tonnage, over 50% passes through the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Malacca" title="Strait of Malacca">Strait of Malacca</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Strait" title="Sunda Strait">Sunda Strait</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lombok_Strait" title="Lombok Strait">Lombok Strait</a>. A 2013 report by the U.S. Energy Information Administration raised the total estimated oil reserves to 11 billion barrels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2014 China began to drill for oil in waters disputed with Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to studies made by the Department of Environment and Natural Resources, Philippines, this body of water holds one third of the entire world\'s marine biodiversity, thereby making it a very important area for the ecosystem. href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Indonesia\'s maritime waters have been breached by fishing fleets from Vietnam and the Philippines leading to said ships being seized and sunk by Indonesian Such disputes have been regarded as Asia\'s most potentially dangerous point of conflict.\nBoth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People\'s Republic of China">People\'s Republic of China</a> (PRC) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a> (ROC, commonly known as Taiwan) claim almost the entire body as their own, demarcating their claims within what is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine-dotted_line" title="Nine-dotted line">nine-dotted line</a>, which claims overlap with virtually every other country in the region. Competing claims include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Indonesia, China, and Taiwan over waters NE of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natuna_Islands" title="Natuna Islands">Natuna Islands</a></li>\n<li>The Philippines, China, and Taiwan over <a href="/wiki/Scarborough_Shoal" title="Scarborough Shoal">Scarborough Shoal</a>.</li>\n<li>Vietnam, China, and Taiwan over waters west of the <a href="/wiki/Spratly_Islands" title="Spratly Islands">Spratly Islands</a>. Some or all of the islands themselves are also disputed between Vietnam, China, Taiwan, Brunei, Malaysia, and the Philippines.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Paracel_Islands" title="Paracel Islands">Paracel Islands</a> are disputed between the China, Taiwan and Vietnam.</li>\n<li>Malaysia, Cambodia, Thailand and Vietnam over areas in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a>.</li>\n<li>Singapore and Malaysia along the Strait of Johore and the Strait of Singapore.</li></ul>\n<p>China and Vietnam have both been vigorous in prosecuting their claims. China (various governments) and South Vietnam <a href="/wiki/Paracel_Islands#Historical_perspectives" title="Paracel Islands">each controlled part of the Paracel Islands before 1974</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Paracel_Islands" title="Battle of the Paracel Islands">brief conflict in 1974</a> resulted in 18 Chinese and 53 Vietnamese deaths, and China has controlled the whole of Paracel since then. The <a href="/wiki/Spratly_Islands" title="Spratly Islands">Spratly Islands</a> have been the site of a naval clash, in which over 70 Vietnamese sailors were killed just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chigua_Reef" title="Chigua Reef">Chigua Reef</a> in March 1988. Disputing claimants regularly report clashes between naval vessels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASEAN" title="ASEAN">ASEAN</a> in general, and Malaysia in particular, have been keen to ensure that the territorial disputes within the South China Sea do not escalate into armed conflict. As such, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joint_Development_Authorities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joint Development Authorities (page does not exist)">Joint Development Authorities</a> have been set up in areas of overlapping claims to jointly develop the area and divide the profits equally without settling the issue of sovereignty over the area. This is true particularly in the Gulf of Thailand. Generally, China has preferred to resolve competing claims bilaterally,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> while some ASEAN countries prefer multilateral talks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> believing that they are disadvantaged in bilateral negotiations with the much larger China and that because many countries claim the same territory only multilateral talks could effectively resolve the competing claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The overlapping claims over <a href="/wiki/Pedra_Branca,_Singapore" title="Pedra Branca, Singapore">Pedra Branca</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pedra_Branca,_Singapore" title="Pedra Branca, Singapore">Pulau Batu Putih</a> including the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Middle_Rocks" title="Middle Rocks">Middle Rocks</a> by both Singapore and Malaysia were settled in 2008 by the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a>, awarding Pedra Branca/Pulau Batu Puteh to Singapore and the Middle Rocks to Malaysia.\n</p><p>In July 2010, US Secretary of State Hillary Clinton called for China to resolve the territorial dispute. China responded by demanding the US keep out of the issue. This came at a time when both countries had been engaging in naval exercises in a show of force to the opposing side, which increased tensions in the region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Defense" title="US Department of Defense">US Department of Defense</a> released a statement on August 18 where it opposed the use of force to resolve the dispute, and accused China of assertive behaviour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\nOn July 22, 2011, one of India\'s amphibious assault vessels, the <a href="/wiki/INS_Airavat_(L24)" title="INS Airavat (L24)">INS <i>Airavat</i></a> which was on a friendly visit to Vietnam, was reportedly contacted at a distance of 45 nautical miles from the Vietnamese coast in the disputed South China Sea on an open radio channel by a vessel identifying itself as the Chinese Navy and stating that the ship was entering Chinese waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The spokesperson for the Indian Navy clarified that as no ship or aircraft was visible from INS <i>Airavat</i> it proceeded on her onward journey as scheduled. The Indian Navy further clarified that "[t]here was no confrontation involving the INS Airavat. India supports freedom of navigation in international waters, including in the South China Sea, and the right of passage in accordance with accepted principles of international law.
