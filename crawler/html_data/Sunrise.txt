class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id=".22Rise.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'"Rise"\'>"Rise"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title=\'Edit section: "Rise"\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although the Sun appears to "rise" from the horizon, it is actually the <i>Earth\'s</i> motion that causes the Sun to appear. The illusion of a moving Sun results from Earth observers being in a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">rotating reference frame</a>; this apparent motion is so convincing that most cultures had mythologies and religions built around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geocentric" title="Geocentric">geocentric</a> model, which prevailed until astronomer <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a> first formulated the <a href="/wiki/Heliocentrism" title="Heliocentrism">heliocentric</a> model in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Architect <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a> proposed the terms "sunsight" and "sunclipse" to better represent the heliocentric model, though the terms have not entered into common language.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning_and_end">Beginning and end</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beginning and end">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Astronomically, sunrise occurs for only an instant: the moment at which the upper limb of the Sun appears tangent to the horizon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the term <i>sunrise</i> commonly refers to periods of time both before and after this point:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">Twilight</a>, the period in the morning during which the sky is <a href="/wiki/Sky_brightness" title="Sky brightness">brightening</a>, but the Sun is not yet visible. The beginning of morning twilight is called <i>astronomical <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a></i>.</li>\n<li>The period after the Sun rises during which striking colors and atmospheric effects are still seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mw_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mw-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunrise-Sunset_angle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="1017" decoding="async" height="204" href="/wiki/File:Sunrise-Sunset_angle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This diagram of the Sun at <a href="/wiki/False_sunrise" title="False sunrise">sunrise</a> (or <a href="/wiki/False_sunset" title="False sunset">sunset</a>) shows the effects of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a>.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angle">Angle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Angle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/False_sunrise" title="False sunrise">Sunrise</a> actually occurs <i>before</i> the Sun truly reaches the horizon because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">refracts</a> the Sun\'s image. At the horizon, the average amount of refraction is 34 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminute" title="Arcminute">arcminutes</a>, though this amount varies based on atmospheric conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also, unlike most other solar measurements, sunrise occurs when the Sun\'s <i>upper limb</i>, rather than its center, appears to cross the horizon. The apparent radius of the Sun at the horizon is 16 arcminutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>These two angles combine to define sunrise to occur when the Sun\'s center is 50 arcminutes below the horizon, or 90.83&#176; from the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_of_day">Time of day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Time of day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The timing of sunrise varies throughout the year and is also affected by the viewer\'s longitude and latitude, altitude, and time zone. These changes are driven by the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> of Earth, daily rotation of the Earth, the planet\'s movement in its annual <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical</a> orbit around the Sun, and the Earth and Moon\'s paired revolutions around each other. The <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">analemma</a> can be used to make approximate predictions of the time of sunrise.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunrise_-_Libreville,_Gabon_-_2008.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="263" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg/350px-Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg/525px-Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg/700px-Sunrise_-_Libreville%2C_Gabon_-_2008.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunrise_-_Libreville,_Gabon_-_2008.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Time of sunrise in 2008 for <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">Libreville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>. Near the equator, the variation of the time of sunrise is mainly governed by the variation of the <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_time" title="Equation of time">equation of time</a>. See <a href="/wiki/File:Sunrise_-_Dakar,_Senegal_-_2008.svg" title="File:Sunrise - Dakar, Senegal - 2008.svg">here</a> for the sunrise chart of a different location.</div></div></div>\n<p>In late winter and spring, sunrise as seen from temperate latitudes occurs earlier each day, reaching its earliest time near the <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">summer solstice</a>; although the exact date varies by latitude. After this point, the time of sunrise gets later each day, reaching its latest sometime around the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a>. The offset between the dates of the solstice and the earliest or latest sunrise time is caused by the eccentricity of Earth\'s orbit and the tilt of its axis, and is described by the <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">analemma</a>, which can be used to predict the dates.\n</p><p>Variations in atmospheric refraction can alter the time of sunrise by changing its apparent position. Near the poles, the time-of-day variation is exaggerated, since the Sun crosses the horizon at a very shallow angle and thus rises more slowly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Navy_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Accounting for atmospheric refraction and measuring from the leading edge slightly increases the average duration of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">day</a> relative to <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sunrise_equation" title="Sunrise equation">sunrise equation</a>, however, which is used to derive the time of sunrise and sunset, uses the Sun\'s physical center for calculation, neglecting atmospheric refraction and the non-zero angle subtended by the solar disc.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_on_the_horizon">Location on the horizon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Location on the horizon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Neglecting the effects of refraction and the Sun\'s non-zero size, whenever sunrise occurs, in temperate regions it is always in the northeast quadrant from the March equinox to the September equinox and in the southeast quadrant from the September equinox to the March equinox.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Sunrises occur approximately due east on the March and September equinoxes for all viewers on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Exact calculations of the <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">azimuths</a> of sunrise on other dates are complex, but they can be estimated with reasonable accuracy by using the <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">analemma</a>.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span id="Colors">Colors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1333" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg/220px-Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg/330px-Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg/440px-Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Before-sunrise-perse-rock.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colors 10 minutes before sunrise. Rocher Perc&#233; (Pierced Rock), Quebec, Canada.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_optics" title="Atmospheric optics">Atmospheric optics</a></div>\n<p>Air molecules and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">airborne particles</a> scatter white sunlight as it passes through the Earth\'s atmosphere. This is done by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mie_scattering" title="Mie scattering">Mie scattering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saha_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-saha-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a ray of white sunlight travels through the atmosphere to an observer, some of the colors are scattered out of the beam by air molecules and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">airborne particles</a>, changing the final color of the beam the viewer sees.\nBecause the shorter wavelength components, such as blue and green, scatter more strongly,\nthese colors are preferentially removed from the beam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saha_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-saha-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At sunrise and sunset, when the path through the atmosphere is longer, the blue and green components are removed almost completely leaving the longer wavelength orange and red <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hues</a> seen at those times. The remaining reddened sunlight can then be scattered by cloud droplets and other relatively large particles to light up the horizon red and orange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guenther_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-guenther-7">[7]</a></sup>  The removal of the shorter wavelengths of light is due to <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> by air molecules and particles much smaller than the wavelength of visible light (less than 50&#160;nm in diameter).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohren_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bohren-9">[9]</a></sup>  The scattering by cloud droplets and other particles with diameters comparable to or larger than the sunlight\'s wavelengths (more than 600&#160;nm) is due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mie_theory" title="Mie theory">Mie scattering</a> and is not strongly wavelength-dependent.  Mie scattering is responsible for the light scattered by clouds, and also for the daytime <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halo</a> of white light around the Sun (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_scattering" title="Forward scattering">forward scattering</a> of white light).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sunset colors are typically more brilliant than sunrise colors, because the evening air contains more particles than morning air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saha_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-saha-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guenther_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-guenther-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bohren_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bohren-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hecht_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-hecht-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3963" data-file-width="5936" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg/220px-Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg/330px-Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg/440px-Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unrest_Sea_During_Sunrise_In_Sydney.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peach sunrise over the sea, Sydney, Australia</div></div></div>\n<p>Ash from volcanic eruptions, trapped within the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, tends to mute sunset and sunrise colors, while volcanic ejecta that is instead lofted into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> (as thin clouds of tiny sulfuric acid droplets), can yield beautiful post-sunset colors called <a href="/wiki/Afterglow" title="Afterglow">afterglows</a> and pre-sunrise glows. A number of eruptions, including those of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> in 1991 and <a href="/wiki/Krakatoa" title="Krakatoa">Krakatoa</a> in 1883, have produced sufficiently high stratospheric sulfuric acid clouds to yield remarkable sunset afterglows (and pre-sunrise glows) around the world. The high altitude clouds serve to reflect strongly reddened sunlight still striking the stratosphere after sunset, down to the surface.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_illusions_and_other_phenomena">Optical illusions and other phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunrise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Optical illusions and other phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:False_Sunrise.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/False_Sunrise.jpg/220px-False_Sunrise.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/False_Sunrise.jpg/330px-False_Sunrise.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/False_Sunrise.jpg/440px-False_Sunrise.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:False_Sunrise.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This is a <a href="/wiki/False_sunrise" title="False sunrise"><i>False Sunrise</i></a>, a very particular kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parhelion" title="Parhelion">Parhelion</a></div></div></div>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">Atmospheric refraction</a> causes the Sun to be seen while it is still below the horizon.</li>\n<li>Light from the lower edge of the Sun\'s disk is refracted more than light from the upper edge. This reduces the apparent height of the Sun when it appears just above the horizon. The width is not affected, so the Sun appears wider than it is high.</li>\n<li>The Sun appears larger at sunrise than it does while higher in the sky, in a manner similar to the <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">moon illusion</a>.</li>\n<li>The Sun appears to rise above the horizon and circle the Earth, but it is actually the Earth that is rotating, with the Sun remaining fixed. This effect results from the fact that an observer on Earth is in a <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">rotating reference frame</a>.</li>\n<li>Occasionally a <i><a href="/wiki/False_sunrise" title="False sunrise">false sunrise</a></i> occurs, demonstrating a very particular kind of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parhelion" title="Parhelion">Parhelion</a> belonging to the optical phenomenon family of <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a>.</li>\n<li>Sometimes just before sunrise or after sunset a <a href="/wiki/Green_flash" title="Green flash">green flash</a> can be seen. This is an optical phenomenon in which a green spot is visible above the sun, usually for no more than a second or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
