Ponce de Le&#243;n</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberger2005_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberger2005-1">[1]</a></sup> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[&#712;xwam &#712;pon&#952;e &#240;e le&#712;on]</a></span>; 1474 &#8211; July 1521)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison1974_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morison1974-2">[2]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spanish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">explorer</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a></i> born in <a href="/wiki/Santerv%C3%A1s_de_Campos" title="Santerv&#225;s de Campos">Santerv&#225;s de Campos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valladolid_(province)" title="Valladolid (province)">Valladolid</a>, Spain in 1474. Though little is known about his family, he was of noble birth and served in the Spanish  military from a young age. He first came to the Americas as a "gentlemen volunteer" with <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>\'s second expedition in 1493.\n</p><p>By the early 1500s, Ponce de Le&#243;n was a top military official in the colonial government of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, where he helped crush a rebellion of the native <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Ta&#237;no">Ta&#237;no</a> people. He was authorized to explore the neighboring island of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> in 1508 and was named the first <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Governor of Puerto Rico</a> by appointment of the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Spain" title="Monarchy of Spain">Spanish crown</a> in 1509. While Ponce de Le&#243;n grew quite wealthy from his plantations and mines, he faced an ongoing legal conflict with <a href="/wiki/Diego_Columbus" title="Diego Columbus">Diego Columbus</a>, the late Christopher Columbus\'s son, over the right to govern Puerto Rico. After a long court battle, Columbus replaced Ponce de Le&#243;n as governor in 1511. Ponce de Le&#243;n decided to follow the advice of the sympathetic <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">King Ferdinand</a> and explore more of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.\n</p><p>In 1513, Ponce de Le&#243;n led the first known European expedition to <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Florida" title="Spanish Florida">La Florida</a>, which he named during his first voyage to the area. He landed somewhere along Florida\'s east coast, then charted the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast down to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> and north along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf</a> coast, perhaps as far as <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Harbor_(estuary)" title="Charlotte Harbor (estuary)">Charlotte Harbor</a>. Though in popular culture he was supposedly searching for the <a href="/wiki/Fountain_of_Youth" title="Fountain of Youth">Fountain of Youth</a>, there is no contemporary evidence to support the story, which all modern historians call a myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-history_com-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce de Le&#243;n returned to Spain in 1514 and was knighted by King Ferdinand, who also re-instated him as the governor of Puerto Rico and authorized him to settle Florida. He returned to the Caribbean in 1515, but plans to organize an expedition to Florida were delayed by the death of King Ferdinand in 1516, after which Ponce de Le&#243;n again traveled to Spain to defend his grants and titles. He would not return to Puerto Rico for two years.\n</p><p>In 1521, Ponce de Le&#243;n finally returned to <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Florida" title="Southwest Florida">southwest Florida</a> with the first large-scale attempt to establish a Spanish colony in what is now the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">continental United States</a>. However, the native <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a> people fiercely resisted the incursion, and he was seriously wounded in a skirmish. The colonization attempt was abandoned, and its leader died from his wounds soon after returning to Cuba. Ponce de Le&#243;n was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interred" title="Interred">interred</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and his tomb is located inside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Cathedral of San Juan Bautista">Cathedral of San Juan Bautista</a> in San Juan.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input title="Santerv&#225;s de Campos">Santerv&#225;s de Campos</a> in the northern part of what is now the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">province</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valladolid_(province)" title="Valladolid (province)">Valladolid</a>. Although early historians placed his birth in 1460, and this date has been used traditionally, more recent evidence shows he was likely born in 1474.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The surname Ponce de Le&#243;n dates from the 13th century. The Ponce de Le&#243;n lineage began with <a href="/wiki/Ponce_Vela_de_Cabrera" title="Ponce Vela de Cabrera">Ponce V&#233;laz de Cabrera</a>, descendant of count <a href="/wiki/Bermudo_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez" title="Bermudo N&#250;&#241;ez">Bermudo N&#250;&#241;ez</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sancha_Ponce_de_Cabrera" title="Sancha Ponce de Cabrera">Sancha Ponce de Cabrera</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999188_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999188-5">[5]</a></sup> daughter of <a href="/wiki/Ponce_Giraldo_de_Cabrera" title="Ponce Giraldo de Cabrera">Ponce Giraldo de Cabrera</a>. Before October 1235, a son of Ponce Vela de Cabrera and his wife Teresa Rodr&#237;guez Gir&#243;n named <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Ponce_de_Cabrera" title="Pedro Ponce de Cabrera">Pedro Ponce de Cabrera</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999191_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999191-6">[6]</a></sup> married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldonza_Alfonso" title="Aldonza Alfonso">Aldonza Alfonso</a>, an illegitimate daughter of King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_IX_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Alfonso IX of Le&#243;n">Alfonso IX of Le&#243;n</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999191_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETorres_Sevilla-Qui&#241;ones_de_Le&#243;n1999191-6">[6]</a></sup> The descendants of this marriage added the "de Le&#243;n" to their patronymic and were known henceforth as the Ponce de Le&#243;n.\n</p><p>The identity of his parents is still unknown, but he appears to have been a member of a distinguished and influential noble family. His relatives included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodrigo_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n,_Marquis_of_C%C3%A1diz" title="Rodrigo Ponce de Le&#243;n, Marquis of C&#225;diz">Rodrigo Ponce de Le&#243;n, Marquis of C&#225;diz</a>, a celebrated figure in the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moorish</a> wars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce de Le&#243;n was related to another notable family, the N&#250;&#241;ez de Guzm&#225;ns, and as a young man he served as squire to Pedro N&#250;&#241;ez de Guzm&#225;n, Knight Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Calatrava" title="Order of Calatrava">Order of Calatrava</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMidd2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-VMidd2008-8">[8]</a></sup> A contemporary chronicler, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Oviedo_y_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Gonzalo Fern&#225;ndez de Oviedo y Vald&#233;s">Gonzalo Fern&#225;ndez de Oviedo y Vald&#233;s</a>, states that Ponce de Le&#243;n gained his experience as a soldier fighting in the Spanish campaigns that defeated the Moors in <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Granada</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Granada" title="Battle of Granada">completed</a> the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">re-conquest</a> of Spain in 1492.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_in_the_New_World">Arrival in the New World</span></h2>\n<p>Once <a href="/wiki/Granada_War" title="Granada War">the war</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Granada" title="Battle of Granada">ended</a>, there was no apparent need for his military services at home, so, like many of his contemporaries, Ponce de Le&#243;n looked abroad for his next opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMidd2008_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-VMidd2008-8">[8]</a></sup> In September 1493, some 1,200 sailors, colonists, and soldiers joined <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> for his <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus#Second_voyage" title="Christopher Columbus">second voyage</a> to the New World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessel2003_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kessel2003-10">[10]</a></sup> Ponce de Le&#243;n was a member of this expedition, one of  200 "gentleman volunteers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fleet reached the Caribbean in November 1493. They visited several islands before arriving at their primary destination in <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In particular they anchored on the coast of a large island the natives called Borinquen but would eventually become known as <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. This was Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s first glimpse of the place that would play a major role in his future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Historians are divided on what he did during the next several years, but it is possible that he returned to Spain at some point and made his way back to Hispaniola with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol&#225;s de Ovando">Nicol&#225;s de Ovando</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</span></h2>\n<p>In 1502 the newly appointed governor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol&#225;s de Ovando">Nicol&#225;s de Ovando</a>, arrived in <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. The Spanish Crown expected Ovando to bring order to a colony in disarray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kessel2003_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kessel2003-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marley2008-15">[15]</a></sup> Ovando interpreted this as authorizing subjugation of the native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Ta&#237;no people">Ta&#237;nos</a>. Thus, Ovando authorized the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaragua_Massacre" title="Jaragua Massacre">Jaragua Massacre</a> in November 1503. In 1504, when Tainos overran a small Spanish garrison in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hig%C3%BCey_(city)" title="Hig&#252;ey (city)">Hig&#252;ey</a> on the island\'s eastern side, Ovando assigned Ponce de Le&#243;n to crush the rebellion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2008_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marley2008-15">[15]</a></sup> Ponce de Le&#243;n was actively involved in the Hig&#252;ey massacre, about which friar <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_de_las_Casas" title="Bartolom&#233; de las Casas">Bartolom&#233; de las Casas</a> attempted to notify Spanish authorities. Ovando rewarded his victorious commander by appointing him frontier governor of the newly conquered province, then named <a href="/wiki/La_Altagracia_Province" title="La Altagracia Province">Hig&#252;ey</a> also. Ponce de Le&#243;n received a substantial land grant which authorized sufficient Indian slave labor to farm his new estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce de Le&#243;n prospered in this new role. He found a ready market for his farm produce and livestock at nearby <a href="/wiki/Boca_de_Yuma" title="Boca de Yuma">Boca de Yuma</a> where Spanish ships stocked supplies before the long voyage back to Spain. In 1505 Ovando authorized Ponce de Le&#243;n to establish a new town in Hig&#252;ey, which he named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvale%C3%B3n_de_Hig%C3%BCey" title="Salvale&#243;n de Hig&#252;ey">Salvale&#243;n</a>. In 1508 King <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand</a> (Queen Isabella having opposed the exploitation of natives but dying in 1504) authorized Ponce de Le&#243;n to conquer the remaining Ta&#237;nos and exploit them in gold mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Tainos_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Tainos-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Around this time, Ponce de Le&#243;n married Leonora, an innkeeper\'s daughter. They had three daughters (Juana, Isabel and Maria) and one son (Luis). The large stone house Ponce de Le&#243;n ordered built for his growing family still stands today near the city of Salvale&#243;n de Hig&#252;ey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="768" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG/220px-Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG/330px-Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG/440px-Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ponce_de_Leon_house.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruins of Juan Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s residence at <a href="/wiki/Caparra_Archaeological_Site" title="Caparra Archaeological Site">Caparra</a></div></div></div>\n<p>As provincial governor, Ponce de Le&#243;n had occasion to meet with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADnos" title="Ta&#237;nos">Ta&#237;nos</a> who visited his province from neighboring <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. They told him stories of a fertile land with much gold to be found in the many rivers. Inspired by the possibility of riches, Ponce de Le&#243;n requested and received permission from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_de_Ovando" title="Nicol&#225;s de Ovando">Ovando</a> to explore the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>His first reconnaissance of the island is usually dated to 1508 but there is evidence that he had made a previous exploration as early as 1506. This earlier trip was done quietly because the Spanish crown had commissioned <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Y%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_Pinz%C3%B3n" title="Vicente Y&#225;&#241;ez Pinz&#243;n">Vicente Y&#225;&#241;ez Pinz&#243;n</a> to settle the island in 1505. Pinz&#243;n did not fulfill his commission and it expired in 1507, leaving the way clear for Ponce de Le&#243;n.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>His earlier exploration had confirmed the presence of gold and gave him a good understanding of the geography of the island. In 1508, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a> gave permission to Ponce de Le&#243;n for the first <i>official</i> expedition to the island, which the Spanish then called San Juan Bautista. This expedition, consisting of about 50 men in one ship, left Hispaniola on July 12, 1508 and eventually anchored in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bay" title="San Juan Bay">San Juan Bay</a>, near today\'s city of San Juan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Ponce de Le&#243;n searched inland until he found a suitable site about two miles from the bay. Here he erected a storehouse and a fortified house, creating the first settlement in Puerto Rico, <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico#History" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">Caparra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Although a few crops were planted, the settlers spent most of their time and energy searching for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. By early 1509 Ponce de Le&#243;n decided to return to Hispaniola. His expedition had collected a good quantity of the precious metal but was running low on food and supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The expedition was deemed a great success and Ovando appointed Ponce de Le&#243;n governor of San Juan Bautista. This appointment was later confirmed by Ferdinand II on August 14, 1509. He was instructed to extend the settlement of the island and continue mining for gold. The new governor returned to the island as instructed, bringing with him his wife and children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Back on his island, Ponce de Le&#243;n parceled out the native Ta&#237;nos amongst himself and other settlers using a system of forced labor known as <a href="/wiki/Encomienda" title="Encomienda">encomienda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  The Indians were put to work growing food crops and mining for gold.  Many of the <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> treated the Ta&#237;nos very harshly and newly introduced diseases like <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> took a severe toll on the local population. By June 1511 the Ta&#237;nos were pushed to a short-lived rebellion, which was forcibly put down by Ponce de Le&#243;n and a small force of troops armed with <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebuses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Floyd_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Floyd-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even as Ponce de Le&#243;n was settling the island of San Juan, significant changes were taking place in the politics and government of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_West_Indies" title="Spanish West Indies">Spanish West Indies</a>. On July 10, 1509, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diego_Col%C3%B3n" title="Diego Col&#243;n">Diego Col&#243;n</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, arrived in Hispaniola as acting <a href="/wiki/Viceroy#Spanish_Empire" title="Viceroy">Viceroy</a>, replacing Nicol&#225;s de Ovando.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> For several years Diego Col&#243;n had been waging a legal battle over his rights to inherit the titles and privileges granted to his father. The Crown regretted the sweeping powers that had been granted to Columbus and his heirs and sought to establish more direct control in the New World. In spite of the Crown\'s opposition, Col&#243;n prevailed in court and Ferdinand was required to appoint him Viceroy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\nAlthough the courts had ordered that Ponce de Le&#243;n should remain in office, Col&#243;n circumvented this directive on October 28, 1509 by appointing Juan Ceron chief justice and Miguel Diaz chief constable of the island, effectively overriding the authority of the governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMidd08_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-VMidd08-30">[30]</a></sup> This situation prevailed until March 2, 1510, when Ferdinand issued orders reaffirming Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s position as governor. Ponce de Le&#243;n then had Ceron and Diaz arrested and sent back to Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMidd08_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-VMidd08-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The political struggle between Col&#243;n and Ponce de Le&#243;n continued in this manner for the next few years. Ponce de Le&#243;n had influential supporters in Spain and Ferdinand regarded him as a loyal servant. However, Col&#243;n\'s position as Viceroy made him a powerful opponent and eventually it became clear that Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s position on San Juan was not tenable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Finally, on November 28, 1511, Ceron returned from Spain and was officially reinstated as governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage_to_Florida">First voyage to Florida</span></h2>\n<table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks" class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Template:Spanish colonization of the Americas"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Template talk:Spanish colonization of the Americas"><abbr title="Discuss this template">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Rumors of undiscovered islands to the northwest of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> had reached Spain by 1511, and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand</a> was interested in forestalling further exploration and discovery by Col&#243;n. In an effort to reward Ponce de Le&#243;n for his services, Ferdinand urged him to seek these new lands outside the authority of Col&#243;n. Ponce de Le&#243;n readily agreed to a new venture, and in February 1512 a royal contract was dispatched outlining his rights and authorities to search for "the Islands of Benimy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The contract stipulated that Ponce de Le&#243;n held exclusive rights to the discovery of Benimy and neighboring islands for the next three years. He would be governor for life of any lands he discovered, but he was expected to finance for himself all costs of exploration and settlement. In addition, the contract gave specific instructions for the distribution of gold, Native Americans, and other profits extracted from the new lands. Notably, there was no mention of a rejuvenating fountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce de Le&#243;n equipped three ships with at least 200 men at his own expense and set out from Puerto Rico on March 4, 1513. The only near contemporary description known for this expedition comes from <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Herrera_y_Tordesillas" title="Antonio de Herrera y Tordesillas">Antonio de Herrera y Tordesillas</a>, a Spanish historian who apparently had access to the original ships\' logs or related secondary sources from which he created a summary of the voyage published in 1601.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The brevity of the account and occasional gaps in the record have led historians to speculate and dispute many details of the voyage.\n</p><p>The three ships in this small fleet were the <i>Santiago</i>, the <i>San Cristobal</i> and the <i>Santa Maria de la Consolacion</i>. Anton de Alaminos was their chief pilot. He was already an experienced sailor, and would become one of the most respected pilots in the region. After leaving Puerto Rico, they sailed northwest along the great chain of Bahama Islands, known then as the Lucayos. On March 27, Easter Sunday, they sighted an island that was unfamiliar to the sailors on the expedition. Because many Spanish seamen were acquainted with the Bahamas, which had been depopulated by slaving ventures, some scholars believe that this "island" was actually Florida, as it was thought to be an island for several years after its formal discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner_2012,_p._5_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2012,_p._5-38">[38]</a></sup> Other scholars have speculated that this island was one of the northern Bahama islands, perhaps <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Abaco" title="Great Abaco">Great Abaco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the next several days the fleet crossed open water until <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Florida_History" title="Timeline of Florida History">April 2, 1513</a>, when they sighted land which Ponce de Le&#243;n believed was another island. He named it <i><a href="/wiki/Spanish_Florida" title="Spanish Florida">La Florida</a></i> in recognition of the verdant landscape and because it was the <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> season, which the Spaniards called <i><a href="/wiki/Pascua_Florida" title="Pascua Florida">Pascua Florida</a></i> (Festival of Flowers). The following day they came ashore to seek information and take possession of this new land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison_1974,_p._507_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morison_1974,_p._507-40">[40]</a></sup> The precise location of their landing on the Florida coast has been disputed for many years. Some historians believe it occurred at or near <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine,_Florida" title="St. Augustine, Florida">St. Augustine</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> others prefer a more southern landing at a small harbor now called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n_Inlet" title="Ponce de Le&#243;n Inlet">Ponce de Le&#243;n Inlet</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morison_1974,_p._507_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morison_1974,_p._507-40">[40]</a></sup> but some also believe that Ponce came ashore even farther south near the present location of <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Beach,_Florida" title="Melbourne Beach, Florida">Melbourne Beach</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBN_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBN-44">[44]</a></sup> a theory that has been criticized by some scholars in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The latitude coordinate recorded in the ship\'s log closest to the landing site, reported by Herrera (who had the original logbook) in 1601, was 30 degrees, 8 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner_2012,_p._5_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2012,_p._5-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> This sighting was recorded at noon the day before with either a <a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(instrument)" title="Quadrant (instrument)">quadrant</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Mariner%27s_astrolabe" title="Mariner\'s astrolabe">mariner\'s astrolabe</a>, and the expedition sailed north for the remainder of the day before anchoring for the night and rowing ashore the following morning. This latitude corresponds to a spot north of St. Augustine between what is now the <a href="/wiki/Guana_Tolomato_Matanzas_National_Estuarine_Research_Reserve" title="Guana Tolomato Matanzas National Estuarine Research Reserve">Guana Tolomato Matanzas National Estuarine Research Reserve</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ponte_Vedra_Beach" title="Ponte Vedra Beach">Ponte Vedra Beach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner_2012,_p._5_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_2012,_p._5-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After remaining in the area of their first landing for about five days, the ships turned south for further exploration of the coast. On April 8 they encountered a current so strong that it pushed them backwards and forced them to seek anchorage. The tiniest ship, the <i>San Cristobal</i>, was carried out of sight and lost for two days. This was the first encounter with the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> where it reaches maximum force between the Florida coast and the Bahamas. Because of the powerful boost provided by the current, it would soon become the primary route for eastbound ships leaving the Spanish Indies bound for Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weddle,_p._42_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weddle,_p._42-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p><p>They continued down the coast hugging the shore to avoid the strong head current. By May 4 the fleet reached and named <a href="/wiki/Biscayne_Bay" title="Biscayne Bay">Biscayne Bay</a> and took on water at an island they named Santa Marta (now <a href="/wiki/Key_Biscayne" title="Key Biscayne">Key Biscayne</a>) and explored the Tequesta Miami mound town at the mouth of the Miami River. The <a href="/wiki/Tequesta" title="Tequesta">Tequesta</a> did not engage the Spanish, they evacuated into the coastal woodlands. On May 15 they left Biscayne Bay and sailed along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>, looking for a passage to head north and explore the west coast of the Florida peninsula. From a distance the Keys reminded Ponce de Le&#243;n of men who were suffering, so he named them <i>Los Martires</i> (the Martyrs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weddle,_p._42_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weddle,_p._42-48">[48]</a></sup> Eventually they found a gap in the reefs and sailed "to the north and other times to the northeast" until they reached the Florida mainland on May 23, where they encountered the <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a>, who refused to trade and drove off the Spanish ships by surrounding them with warriors in sea canoes armed with long bows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PR-Statue_of_Juan_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="383" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PR-Statue_of_Juan_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Back of Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s statue in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="Old San Juan, Puerto Rico">Old San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> The statue was made in <a (1797)">English attacked San Juan in 1797</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Again, the exact site of their landfall is controversial.  The vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Harbor,_Florida" title="Charlotte Harbor, Florida">Charlotte Harbor</a> is the most commonly identified spot, while some assert a landing further north at <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay" title="Tampa Bay">Tampa Bay</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen97_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen97-52">[52]</a></sup> Other historians have argued the distances were too great to cover in the available time and the more likely location was <a href="/wiki/Cape_Romano" title="Cape Romano">Cape Romano</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cape_Sable" title="Cape Sable">Cape Sable</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen97_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allen97-52">[52]</a></sup> Here Ponce de Le&#243;n anchored for several days to take on water and repair the ships. They were approached by <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a>, who might have been initially interested in trading but relations soon turned hostile. Several skirmishes followed with casualties on both sides and the Spaniards took eight Indians captive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> including one to become a translator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2008_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marley2008-15">[15]</a></sup> On June 4, there was another encounter with natives near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanibel_Island" title="Sanibel Island">Sanibel Island</a> and the Calusa in war canoes, with the Spanish sinking a fourth of them. An unsubstantiated claim to justify Spanish retreat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On June 14 they set sail again looking for a chain of islands in the west that had been described by their captives. They reached the <a href="/wiki/Dry_Tortugas" title="Dry Tortugas">Dry Tortugas</a> on June 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2008_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Marley2008-15">[15]</a></sup> There they captured giant <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_monk_seal" title="Caribbean monk seal">Caribbean monk seals</a>, and thousands of <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a>. From these islands they sailed southwest in an apparent attempt to circle around Cuba and return home to Puerto Rico. Failing to take into account the powerful currents pushing them eastward, they struck the northeast shore of Cuba and were initially confused about their location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once they regained their bearings, the fleet retraced their route east along the Florida Keys and around the Florida peninsula, reaching <a href="/wiki/Grand_Bahama" title="Grand Bahama">Grand Bahama</a> on July 8. They were surprised to come across another Spanish ship, piloted by <a href="/wiki/Diego_Miruelo" title="Diego Miruelo">Diego Miruelo</a>, who was either on a slaving voyage or had been sent by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diego_Col%C3%B3n" title="Diego Col&#243;n">Diego Col&#243;n</a> to spy on Ponce de Le&#243;n. Shortly thereafter Miruelo\'s ship was wrecked in a storm and Ponce de Le&#243;n rescued the stranded crew.\n</p><p>From here the little fleet disbanded. Ponce de Le&#243;n tasked the <i>Santa Maria</i> with further exploration while he returned home with the rest of crew. Ponce de Le&#243;n reached Puerto Rico on October 19 after having been away for almost eight months. The other ship, after further explorations returned safely on February 20, 1514.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although Ponce de Le&#243;n is widely credited with the discovery of Florida, he almost certainly was not the first European to reach the peninsula. Spanish slave expeditions had been regularly raiding the Bahamas since 1494 and there is some evidence that one or more of these slavers made it as far as the shores of Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>\nAnother piece of evidence that others came before Ponce de Le&#243;n is the <a href="/wiki/Cantino_planisphere" title="Cantino planisphere">Cantino Map</a> from 1502, which shows a peninsula near Cuba that looks like Florida\'s and includes characteristic place names.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fountain_of_Youth">Fountain of Youth</span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fountain_of_Youth" title="Fountain of Youth">Fountain of Youth</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1689" data-file-width="2251" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG/220px-Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG/330px-Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG/440px-Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bahamian_Love_Vine.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bahamian love vine (<i><a href="/wiki/Cassytha_filiformis" title="Cassytha filiformis">Cassytha filiformis</a></i>), Bahamas</div></div></div>\n<p>According to a popular legend, Ponce de Le&#243;n discovered Florida while searching for the Fountain of Youth. Though stories of vitality-restoring waters were known on both sides of the Atlantic long before Ponce de Le&#243;n, the story of his searching for them was not attached to him until after his death. In his <i>Historia general y natural de las Indias</i> of 1535, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Oviedo_y_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Gonzalo Fern&#225;ndez de Oviedo y Vald&#233;s">Gonzalo Fern&#225;ndez de Oviedo y Vald&#233;s</a> wrote that Ponce de Le&#243;n was looking for the waters of <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a> to cure his aging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> A similar account appears in <a href="/wiki/Francisco_L%C3%B3pez_de_G%C3%B3mara" title="Francisco L&#243;pez de G&#243;mara">Francisco L&#243;pez de G&#243;mara</a>\'s <i>Historia general de las Indias</i> of 1551.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Then in 1575, <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Escalante_Fontaneda" title="Hernando de Escalante Fontaneda">Hernando de Escalante Fontaneda</a>, a shipwreck survivor who had lived with the Native Americans of Florida for 17 years, published his memoir in which he locates the waters in Florida, and says that Ponce de Le&#243;n was supposed to have looked for them there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Though Fontaneda doubted that Ponce de Le&#243;n had really gone to Florida looking for the waters, the account was included in the <i>Historia general de los hechos de los castellanos</i> of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Herrera_y_Tordesillas" title="Antonio de Herrera y Tordesillas">Antonio de Herrera y Tordesillas</a> of 1615. Most historians hold that the search for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish Empire</a> were far more imperative than any potential search for such a fountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sauer1975_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sauer1975-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is a possibility that the Fountain of Youth was an allegory for the <a href="/wiki/Cassytha_filiformis" title="Cassytha filiformis">Bahamian love vine</a>, which locals brew today as an aphrodisiac.  Ponce de Le&#243;n could have been seeking it as a potential entrepreneurial venture.  <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> believed Indian servants brewing a "brown tea" in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> may have inspired Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s search for the Fountain of Youth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>  Arne Molander has speculated that the adventurous conquistador mistook the natives\' "vid" (vine) for "vida" (life) &#8211; transforming their "fountain vine" into an imagined "fountain of life".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Between_voyages">Between voyages</span></h2>\n<p>Upon his return to Puerto Rico, Ponce de Le&#243;n found the island in turmoil. A party of <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Caribs</a> from a neighboring island had attacked the settlement of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico#History" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">Caparra</a>, killed several Spaniards and burned it to the ground.  Ponce de Le&#243;n\'s own <a href="/wiki/Caparra_Archaeological_Site" title="Caparra Archaeological Site">house</a> was destroyed and his family narrowly escaped. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diego_Col%C3%B3n" title="Diego Col&#243;n">Col&#243;n</a> used the attack as a pretext for renewing hostilities against the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Ta&#237;no people">Ta&#237;no</a> tribes. The explorer suspected that Col&#243;n was working to further undermine his position on the island and perhaps even to take his claims for the newly discovered Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ponce de Le&#243;n decided he should return to Spain and personally report the results of his recent expedition. He left Puerto Rico in April 1514 and was warmly received by Ferdinand when he arrived at court in <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">Valladolid</a>. There he was knighted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marley2008_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Marley2008-15">[15]</a></sup> and given a personal <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>, becoming the first conquistador to receive these honors. He also visited <a href="/wiki/Casa_de_Contrataci%C3%B3n" title="Casa de Contrataci&#243;n">Casa de Contrataci&#243;n</a> in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, which was the central bureaucracy and clearinghouse for all of Spain\'s activities in the New World. The Casa took detailed notes of his discoveries and added them to the <a href="/wiki/Padr%C3%B3n_Real" title="Padr&#243;n Real">Padr&#243;n Real</a>, a master map which served as the basis for official navigation charts provided to Spanish captains and pilots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During his stay in Spain, a new contract<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> was drawn up for Ponce de Le&#243;n confirming his rights to settle and govern <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a> and Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shepherd1907_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shepherd1907-67">[67]</a></sup> which was then presumed to be an island. In addition to the usual directions for sharing gold and other valuables with the king, the contract was one of the first to stipulate that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Requerimiento" title="Requerimiento">Requerimiento</a> was to be read to the inhabitants of the islands prior to their conquest.  Ponce de Le&#243;n was also ordered to organize an armada for the purpose of attacking and subduing the Caribs, who continued to attack Spanish settlements in the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg/170px-Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg/255px-Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg/340px-Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_Ponce_de_Leon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tomb of Ponce de Le&#243;n in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Cathedral of San Juan Bautista">Cathedral of San Juan Bautista</a> in San Juan, Puerto Rico</div></div></div>\n<p>Three ships were purchased for his armada and after repairs and provisioning Ponce de Le&#243;n left Spain on May 14, 1515 with his little fleet. The record of his activities against the Caribs is vague. There was one engagement in <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on his return to the area and possibly two or three other encounters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The campaign came to an abrupt end in 1516 when Ferdinand died. The king had been a strong supporter and Ponce de Le&#243;n felt it was imperative he return to Spain and defend his privileges and titles. He did receive assurances of support from Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Jim%C3%A9nez_de_Cisneros" title="Francisco Jim&#233;nez de Cisneros">Francisco Jim&#233;nez de Cisneros</a>, the regent appointed to govern Castile, but it was nearly two years before he was able to return home to Puerto Rico.\n</p><p>Meanwhile, there had been at least two unauthorized voyages to "his" Florida both ending in repulsion by the native Calusa Tequesta warriors. Ponce de Le&#243;n realized he had to act soon if he was to maintain his claim.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_voyage_to_Florida">Last voyage to Florida</span></h2>\n<p>In early 1521, Ponce de Le&#243;n organized a colonizing expedition consisting of some 200 men, including priests, farmers and artisans, 50 horses and other domestic animals, and farming implements carried on two ships. The expedition landed somewhere on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Florida" title="Southwest Florida">southwest Florida</a>, likely in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Harbor_(estuary)" title="Charlotte Harbor (estuary)">Charlotte Harbor</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Caloosahatchee_River" title="Caloosahatchee River">Caloosahatchee River</a>, areas which Ponce de Le&#243;n had visited in his earlier voyage to Florida.\n</p><p>Before the settlement could be established, the colonists were attacked by the <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Florida" title="Indigenous peoples of Florida">indigenous</a> people who dominated southern Florida and whose principal town was nearby. Ponce de Le&#243;n was mortally wounded in the skirmish when, historians believe, an arrow <a href="/wiki/Arrow_poison" title="Arrow poison">poisoned</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Sap" title="Sap">sap</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchineel_tree" title="Manchineel tree">manchineel tree</a> struck his thigh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The expedition immediately abandoned the colonization attempt and sailed to <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, where Ponce de Le&#243;n soon died of his wounds. He was buried in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, in the crypt of <a href="/wiki/San_Jos%C3%A9_Church" title="San Jos&#233; Church">San Jos&#233; Church</a> from 1559 to 1836, when his remains were exhumed and transferred to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Cathedral of San Juan Bautista">Cathedral of San Juan Bautista</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<div aria-label="Portals"
