title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/a&#618;/: \'i\' in \'tide\'">a&#618;</span><span title="/&#594;/: \'o\' in \'body\'">&#594;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span></span>, <wbr/>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#601;/: \'a\' in \'about\'">&#601;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> that has a non-zero net <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electrical charge</a>. Since the charge of the electron (considered "negative" by convention) is equal and opposite to that of the proton (considered "positive" by convention), the net charge of an ion is non-zero due to its total number of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> being unequal to its total number of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>.  A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> is a positively charged ion, with fewer electrons than protons, while an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> is negatively charged, with more electrons than protons. Because of their opposite electric currents, cations and anions attract each other and readily form <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a>.\n</p><p>Ions consisting of only a single atom are termed atomic or <a href="/wiki/Monatomic_ion" title="Monatomic ion">monatomic ions</a>, while two or more atoms form molecular ions or <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic ions</a>. In the case of physical ionization in a medium, such as a gas,  "ion pairs" are created by ion collisions, where each generated pair consists of a free electron and a positive ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knoll_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-knoll-2">[2]</a></sup> Ions are also created by chemical interactions, such as the dissolution of a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> in liquids, or by other means, such as passing a <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> through a conducting solution, dissolving an <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> via <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionization</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_discovery">History of discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i>ion</i> comes from the Greek word &#7984;&#972;&#957;, <i>ion</i>, "going", the present participle of &#7984;&#941;&#957;&#945;&#953;, <i>ienai</i>, "to go". This term was introduced by English physicist and chemist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> in 1834 for the then-unknown species that <i>goes</i> from one <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a> to the other through an aqueous medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Faraday did not know the nature of these species, but he knew that since metals dissolved into and entered a solution at one electrode and new metal came forth from a solution at the other electrode; that some kind of substance has moved through the solution in a current. This conveys matter from one place to the other.\n</p><p>Faraday also introduced the words <i>anion</i> for a negatively charged ion, and <i>cation</i> for a positively charged one. In Faraday\'s nomenclature, cations were named because they were attracted to the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> in a galvanic device and anions were named due to their attraction to the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> put forth, in his 1884 dissertation, his explanation of the fact that solid crystalline salts <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociate</a> into paired charged particles when dissolved, for which he would win the 1903 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Arrhenius\' explanation was that in forming a solution, the salt dissociates into Faraday\'s ions. Arrhenius proposed that ions formed even in the absence of an electric current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-columbia_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-columbia-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncBrit_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncBrit-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciBio_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciBio-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Ions in their gas-like state are highly reactive and will rapidly interact with ions of opposite charge to give neutral molecules or ionic salts. Ions are also produced in the liquid or solid state when salts interact with solvents (for example, water) to produce <i>solvated ions</i>, which are more stable, for reasons involving a combination of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> changes as the ions move away from each other to interact with the liquid.  These stabilized species are more commonly found in the environment at low temperatures. A common example is the ions present in seawater, which are derived from dissolved salts.\n</p><p>As charged objects, ions are attracted to opposite electric charges (positive to negative, and vice versa) and repelled by like charges. When they move, their trajectories can be deflected by a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.\n</p><p>Electrons, due to their smaller mass and thus larger space-filling properties as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matter_waves" title="Matter waves">matter waves</a>, determine the size of atoms and molecules that possess any electrons at all. Thus, anions (negatively charged ions) are larger than the parent molecule or atom, as the excess electron(s) repel each other and add to the physical size of the ion, because its size is determined by its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">electron cloud</a>. Cations are smaller than the corresponding parent atom or molecule due to the smaller size of the electron cloud. One particular cation (that of hydrogen) contains no electrons, and thus consists of a single proton - <i>very much smaller</i> than the parent hydrogen atom.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anions_and_cations">Anions and cations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anions and cations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="882" data-file-width="1576" decoding="async" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ions.svg/390px-Ions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ions.svg/585px-Ions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ions.svg/780px-Ions.svg.png 2x" width="390"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">Hydrogen atom</a> (center) contains a single <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> and a single <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>.  Removal of the electron gives a cation (left), whereas addition of an electron gives an anion (right).  The hydrogen anion, with its loosely held two-electron cloud, has a larger radius than the neutral atom, which in turn is much larger than the bare proton of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>. Hydrogen forms the only charge-+1 cation that has no electrons, but even cations that (unlike hydrogen) still retain one or more electrons are still smaller than the neutral atoms or molecules from which they are derived.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Cation" and "Anion" redirect here. For the particle physics/quantum computing concept, see <a href="/wiki/Anyon" title="Anyon">Anyon</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Ion_(disambiguation)" title="Ion (disambiguation)">Ion (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>Since the electric charge on a proton is equal in magnitude to the charge on an electron, the net electric charge on an ion is equal to the number of protons in the ion minus the number of electrons.\n</p><p>An <b><span id="anion">anion</span></b> (&#8722;) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="/&#230;/: \'a\' in \'bad\'">&#230;</span><span title="\'n\' in \'nigh\'">n</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/a&#618;/: \'i\' in \'tide\'">a&#618;</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/&#601;n/: \'on\' in \'button\'">&#601;n</span></span>/</a></span></span>), from the Greek word &#7940;&#957;&#969; (<i>&#225;n&#333;</i>), meaning "up",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> is an ion with more electrons than protons, giving it a net negative charge (since electrons are negatively charged and protons are positively charged).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A <b><span id="cation">cation</span></b> (+) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/&#712;/: primary stress follows">&#712;</span><span title="\'k\' in \'kind\'">k</span><span title="/&#230;/: \'a\' in \'bad\'">&#230;</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/a&#618;/: \'i\' in \'tide\'">a&#618;</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/&#601;n/: \'on\' in \'button\'">&#601;n</span></span>/</a></span></span>), from the Greek word &#954;&#940;&#964;&#969; (<i>k&#225;to</i>), meaning "down",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is an ion with fewer electrons than protons, giving it a positive charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are additional names used for ions with multiple charges.  For example, an ion with a &#8722;2 charge is known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dianion" title="Dianion">dianion</a> and an ion with a +2 charge is known as a <a href="/wiki/Dication" title="Dication">dication</a>.  A <a href="/wiki/Zwitterion" title="Zwitterion">zwitterion</a> is a neutral molecule with positive and negative charges at different locations within that molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cations and anions are measured by their <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radius</a> and they differ in relative size: "Cations are small, most of them less than 10<sup>&#8722;10</sup> m (10<sup>&#8722;8</sup> cm) in radius. But most anions are large, as is the most common Earth anion, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. From this fact it is apparent that most of the space of a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> is occupied by the anion and that the cations fit into the spaces between them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A cation has radius less than 0.8 &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;10</sup> m (0.8 &#197;) while an anion has radius greater than 1.3 &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;10</sup> m (1.3 &#197;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrences">Natural occurrences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Natural occurrences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_oxidation_states_of_the_elements" title="List of oxidation states of the elements">List of oxidation states of the elements</a></div>\n<p>Ions are ubiquitous in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> and are responsible for diverse phenomena from the luminescence of the Sun to the existence of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>. Atoms in their ionic state may have a different colour from neutral atoms, and thus light absorption by metal ions gives the colour of <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a>. In both inorganic and organic chemistry (including biochemistry), the interaction of water and ions is extremely important; an example is the energy that drives breakdown of adenosine triphosphate (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>). The following sections describe contexts in which ions feature prominently; these are arranged in decreasing physical length-scale, from the astronomical to the microscopic.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_technology">Related technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Ions can be non-chemically prepared using various <a href="/wiki/Ion_source" title="Ion source">ion sources</a>, usually involving high <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> or temperature.  These are used in a multitude of devices such as <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_emission_spectrometer" title="Optical emission spectrometer">optical emission spectrometers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_accelerators" title="Particle accelerators">particle accelerators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion_implantation" title="Ion implantation">ion implanters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ion_thruster" title="Ion thruster">ion engines</a>.\n</p><p>As reactive charged particles, they are also used in <a href="/wiki/Air_ioniser" title="Air ioniser">air purification</a> by disrupting microbes, and in household items such as <a href="/wiki/Smoke_detector" title="Smoke detector">smoke detectors</a>.\n</p><p>As signalling and metabolism in organisms are controlled by a precise ionic gradient across <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">membranes</a>, the disruption of this gradient contributes to cell death.  This is a common mechanism exploited by natural and artificial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocides" title="Biocides">biocides</a>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_channels" title="Ion channels">ion channels</a> <a href="/wiki/Gramicidin" title="Gramicidin">gramicidin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphotericin" title="Amphotericin">amphotericin</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicide</a>).\n</p><p>Inorganic dissolved ions are a component of <a href="/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids" title="Total dissolved solids">total dissolved solids</a>, a widely-known indicator of <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_of_ionizing_radiation">Detection of ionizing radiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Detection of ionizing radiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ion_chamber_operation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2258" data-file-width="3241" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ion_chamber_operation.gif/300px-Ion_chamber_operation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ion_chamber_operation.gif/450px-Ion_chamber_operation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ion_chamber_operation.gif/600px-Ion_chamber_operation.gif 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ion_chamber_operation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic of an ion chamber, showing drift of ions. The original ionization event in these instruments results in the formation of an "ion pair"; a positive ion and a free electron, by ion impact by the radiation on the gas molecules. The <a href="/wiki/Ionization_chamber" title="Ionization chamber">ionization chamber</a> is the simplest of these detectors, and collects all the charges created by <i>direct ionization</i> within the gas through the application of an electric field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knoll_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-knoll-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93M%C3%BCller_tube" as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Townsend_avalanche" title="Townsend avalanche">Townsend avalanche</a> to multiply the effect of the original ionizing event by means of a cascade effect whereby the free electrons are given sufficient energy by the electric field to release further electrons by ion impact.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denoting_the_charged_state">Denoting the charged state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Denoting the charged state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ions_notation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="99" data-file-width="309" decoding="async" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ions_notation.svg/220px-Ions_notation.svg.png" written in superscript immediately after the chemical structure for the molecule/atom.  The net charge is written with the magnitude <i>before</i> the sign; that is, a doubly charged cation is indicated as <b>2+</b> instead of <b>+2</b>.   However, the magnitude of the charge is omitted for singly charged molecules/atoms; for example, the <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> cation is indicated as Na<sup>+</sup> and <i>not</i> Na<sup>1+</sup>.\n</p><p>An alternative (and acceptable) way of showing a molecule/atom with multiple charges is by drawing out the signs multiple times, this is often seen with transition metals.  Chemists sometimes circle the sign; this is merely ornamental and does not alter the chemical meaning.  All three representations of <span class="chemf nowrap">Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> shown in the figure, are thus equivalent.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ions_notation2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="172" data-file-width="208" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Ions_notation2.svg/220px-Ions_notation2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Ions_notation2.svg/330px-Ions_notation2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Ions_notation2.svg/440px-Ions_notation2.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ions_notation2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mixed Roman numerals and charge notations for the uranyl ion. The oxidation state of the metal is shown as superscripted Roman numerals, whereas the charge of the entire complex is shown by the angle symbol together with the magnitude and sign of the net charge.</div></div></div>\n<p>Monatomic ions are sometimes also denoted with Roman numerals; for example, the <span class="chemf nowrap">Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> example seen above is occasionally referred to as Fe II, Fe(II) or Fe<sup>II</sup>.  The Roman numeral designates the <i>formal <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a></i> of an element, whereas the superscripted numerals denote the net charge.  The two notations are, therefore, exchangeable for monatomic ions, but the Roman numerals <i>cannot</i> be applied to polyatomic ions.  However, it is possible to mix the notations for the individual metal centre with a polyatomic complex, as shown by the uranyl ion example.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-classes">Sub-classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sub-classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>If an ion contains <a href="/wiki/Unpaired_electron" title="Unpaired electron">unpaired electrons</a>, it is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a></i> ion. Just like uncharged radicals, radical ions are very reactive.  Polyatomic ions containing oxygen, such as carbonate and sulfate, are called <i><a href="/wiki/Oxyanion" title="Oxyanion">oxyanions</a></i>.   Molecular ions that contain at least one carbon to hydrogen bond are called <i>organic ions</i>.  If the charge in an organic ion is formally centred on a carbon, it is termed a <i><a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocation</a></i> (if positively charged) or <i><a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanion</a></i> (if negatively charged).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_monatomic_ions">Formation of monatomic ions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Formation of monatomic ions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Monatomic ions are formed by the gain or loss of electrons to the <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence shell</a> (the outer-most electron shell) in an atom. The inner shells of an atom are filled with electrons that are tightly bound to the positively charged <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a>, and so do not participate in this kind of chemical interaction. The process of gaining or losing electrons from a neutral atom or molecule is called <i>ionization</i>.\n</p><p>Atoms can be ionized by bombardment with <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, but the more usual process of ionization encountered in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> is the transfer of electrons between atoms or molecules. This transfer is usually driven by the attaining of stable ("closed shell") electronic configurations. Atoms will gain or lose electrons depending on which action takes the least energy.\n</p><p>For example, a <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> atom, Na, has a single electron in its valence shell, surrounding 2 stable, filled inner shells of 2 and 8 electrons. Since these filled shells are very stable, a sodium atom tends to lose its extra electron and attain this stable configuration, becoming a sodium cation in the process\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Na &#8594; <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>\n<p>On the other hand, a <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> atom, Cl, has 7 electrons in its valence shell, which is one short of the stable, filled shell with 8 electrons. Thus, a chlorine atom tends to <i>gain</i> an extra electron and attain a stable 8-electron configuration, becoming a chloride anion in the process:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Cl + <span style="white-space:nowrap;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:right"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>e<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.0em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> &#8594; <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></dd></dl>\n<p>This driving force is what causes sodium and chlorine to undergo a chemical reaction, wherein the "extra" electron is transferred from sodium to chlorine, forming sodium cations and chloride anions. Being oppositely charged, these cations and anions form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonds</a> and combine to form <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, NaCl, more commonly known as table salt.\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> &#8594; NaCl</dd></dl>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_polyatomic_and_molecular_ions">Formation of polyatomic and molecular ions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Formation of polyatomic and molecular ions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nitrate-ion-elpot.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="714" data-file-width="751" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Nitrate-ion-elpot.png/220px-Nitrate-ion-elpot.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Nitrate-ion-elpot.png/330px-Nitrate-ion-elpot.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Nitrate-ion-elpot.png/440px-Nitrate-ion-elpot.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nitrate-ion-elpot.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electrostatic potential</a> map of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrate_ion" title="Nitrate ion">nitrate ion</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>). The 3-dimensional shell represents a single arbitrary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isopotential" title="Isopotential">isopotential</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Polyatomic and molecular ions are often formed by the gaining or losing of elemental ions such as a proton, H<sup>+</sup>, in neutral molecules. For example, when <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, NH<sub>3</sub>, accepts a proton, H<sup>+</sup>&#8212;a process called <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a>&#8212;it forms the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>. Ammonia and ammonium have the same number of electrons in essentially the same <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electronic configuration</a>, but ammonium has an extra proton that gives it a net positive charge.\n</p><p>Ammonia can also lose an electron to gain a positive charge, forming the ion <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8226;+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. However, this ion is unstable, because it has an incomplete <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence shell</a> around the nitrogen atom, making it a very reactive <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> ion.\n</p><p>Due to the instability of radical ions, polyatomic and molecular ions are usually formed by gaining or losing elemental ions such as <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, rather than gaining or losing electrons. This allows the molecule to preserve its stable electronic configuration while acquiring an electrical charge.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionization_potential">Ionization potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ionization potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionization_potential" title="Ionization potential">Ionization potential</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> required to detach an electron in its lowest energy state from an atom or molecule of a gas with less net electric charge is called the <i>ionization potential</i>, or <i>ionization energy</i>. The <i>n</i>th ionization energy of an atom is the energy required to detach its <i>n</i>th electron after the first <i>n &#8722; 1</i> electrons have already been detached.\n</p><p>Each successive ionization energy is markedly greater than the last. Particularly great increases occur after any given block of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">atomic orbitals</a> is exhausted of electrons. For this reason, ions tend to form in ways that leave them with full orbital blocks. For example, sodium has one <i><a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electron</a></i> in its outermost shell, so in ionized form it is commonly found with one lost electron, as <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>. On the other side of the periodic table, chlorine has seven valence electrons, so in ionized form it is commonly found with one gained electron, as <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>. Caesium has the lowest measured ionization energy of all the elements and helium has the greatest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenntech.com_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-lenntech.com-16">[16]</a></sup> In general, the ionization energy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">metals</a> is much lower than the ionization energy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonmetals" title="Nonmetals">nonmetals</a>, which is why, in general, metals will lose electrons to form positively charged ions and nonmetals will gain electrons to form negatively charged ions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionic_bonding">Ionic bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ionic bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">Ionic bond</a></div>\n<p><i>Ionic bonding</i> is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonding</a> that arises from the mutual attraction of oppositely charged ions. Ions of like charge repel each other, and ions of opposite charge attract each other. Therefore, ions do not usually exist on their own, but will bind with ions of opposite charge to form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">crystal lattice</a>. The resulting compound is called an <i>ionic compound</i>, and is said to be held together by <i>ionic bonding</i>. In ionic compounds there arise characteristic distances between ion neighbours from which the spatial extension and the <a href="/wiki/Ionic_radius" title="Ionic radius">ionic radius</a> of individual ions may be derived.\n</p><p>The most common type of ionic bonding is seen in compounds of metals and nonmetals (except <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a>, which rarely form chemical compounds). Metals are characterized by having a small number of electrons in excess of a stable, closed-shell electronic configuration. As such, they have the tendency to lose these extra electrons in order to attain a stable configuration. This property is known as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositivity" title="Electropositivity">electropositivity</a></i>. Non-metals, on the other hand, are characterized by having an electron configuration just a few electrons short of a stable configuration. As such, they have the tendency to gain more electrons in order to achieve a stable configuration. This tendency is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a></i>. When a highly electropositive metal is combined with a highly electronegative nonmetal, the extra electrons from the metal atoms are transferred to the electron-deficient nonmetal atoms. This reaction produces metal cations and nonmetal anions, which are attracted to each other to form a <i><a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span
