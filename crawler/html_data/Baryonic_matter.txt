They are also classified as <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>, i.e., they have half-integer <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a>. \n</p><p>The name "baryon" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> word for "heavy" (&#946;&#945;&#961;&#973;&#962;, <i>bar&#253;s</i>), because, at the time of their naming, most known elementary particles had lower masses than the baryons. Each baryon has a corresponding <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> (antibaryon) where their corresponding antiquarks replace quarks. For example, a proton is made of two <a href="/wiki/Up_quark" title="Up quark">up quarks</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Down_quark" title="Down quark">down quark</a>; and its corresponding antiparticle, the <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiproton</a>, is made of two up antiquarks and one down antiquark.\n</p><p>As quark-based particles, baryons participate in the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a>, which is <a href="/wiki/Force_carrier" title="Force carrier">mediated</a> by particles known as <a href="/wiki/Gluon" title="Gluon">gluons</a>. The most familiar baryons are <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, both of which contain three quarks, and for this reason these particles are sometimes described as <b>triquarks</b>.  These particles make up most of the mass of the visible <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>, as well as forming the components of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus of every atom</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">Electrons</a> (the other major component of the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>) are members of a different family of particles, known as <a href="/wiki/Lepton" title="Lepton">leptons</a>, which do not interact via the strong force. <a href="/wiki/Exotic_baryon" title="Exotic baryon">Exotic baryons</a> containing five quarks (known as <a href="/wiki/Pentaquark" title="Pentaquark">pentaquarks</a>) have also been discovered and studied.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Baryons are strongly interacting <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>; that is, they are acted on by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_nuclear_force" title="Strong nuclear force">strong nuclear force</a> and are described by <a class="mw-redirect" title="Pauli exclusion principle">Pauli exclusion principle</a>. This is in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a>, which do not obey the exclusion principle.\n</p><p>Baryons, along with <a href="/wiki/Meson" title="Meson">mesons</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a>, particles composed of <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>. Quarks have <a href="/wiki/Baryon_number" title="Baryon number">baryon numbers</a> of <i>B</i>&#160;=&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span> and antiquarks have baryon numbers of <i>B</i>&#160;=&#160;&#8722;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>. The term "baryon" usually refers to <i>triquarks</i>&#8212;baryons made of three quarks (<i>B</i>&#160;=&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;+&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;+&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;=&#160;1).\n</p><p>Other <a href="/wiki/Exotic_baryon" title="Exotic baryon">exotic baryons</a> have been proposed, such as <a href="/wiki/Pentaquark" title="Pentaquark">pentaquarks</a>&#8212;baryons made of four quarks and one antiquark (<i>B</i>&#160;=&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;+&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;+&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">3</span></span>&#160;+&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; The particle physics community as a whole did not view their existence as likely in 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDGPentaquarks2006_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDGPentaquarks2006-4">[4]</a></sup> and in 2008, considered evidence to be overwhelmingly against the existence of the reported pentaquarks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PDGPentaquarks2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PDGPentaquarks2008-5">[5]</a></sup> However, in July 2015, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LHCb" title="LHCb">LHCb</a> experiment observed two resonances consistent with pentaquark states in the &#923;<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">b</sub></span> &#8594; J/&#968;K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>p decay, with a combined <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">statistical significance</a> of 15&#963;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LHCb-public_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LHCb-public-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LHCb2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-LHCb2015-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In theory, heptaquarks (5 quarks, 2 antiquarks), nonaquarks (6 quarks, 3 antiquarks), etc. could also exist.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baryonic_matter">Baryonic matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baryon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Baryonic matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Nearly all matter that may be encountered or experienced in everyday life is baryonic <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> of any sort, and provides them with the property of mass. Non-baryonic matter, as implied by the name, is any sort of matter that is not composed primarily of baryons. This might include <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a> and free <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetric particles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Axion" title="Axion">axions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>.\n</p><p>The very existence of baryons is also a significant issue in cosmology because it is assumed that the Big Bang produced a state with equal amounts of baryons and antibaryons. The process by which baryons came to outnumber their <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticles</a> is called <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">baryogenesis</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baryogenesis">Baryogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baryon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Baryogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baryogenesis" title="Baryogenesis">Baryogenesis</a></div>\n<p>Experiments are consistent with the number of quarks in the universe being a constant and, to be more specific,  the number of baryons being a constant<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>; in technical language, the total <a href="/wiki/Baryon_number" title="Baryon number">baryon number</a> appears to be <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics)" title="Conservation law (physics)">conserved</a>.</i>  Within the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Standard_Model" title="Standard Model">Standard Model</a> of particle physics, the number of baryons may change in multiples of three due to the action of <a href="/wiki/Sphaleron" title="Sphaleron">sphalerons</a>, although this is rare and has not been observed under experiment.  Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unified_theory" title="Grand unified theory">grand unified theories</a> of particle physics also predict that a single <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> can decay, changing the baryon number by one; however, this has not yet been observed under experiment. The excess of baryons over antibaryons in the present universe is thought to be due to non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_baryon_number" title="Conservation of baryon number">conservation of baryon number</a> in the very early universe, though this is not well understood.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span The higher the mass, the lower the strangeness (the more s quarks). Particles could be described with isospin projections (related to charge) and strangeness (mass) (see the uds octet and decuplet figures on the right). As other quarks were discovered, new quantum numbers were made to have similar description of udc and udb octets and decuplets. Since only the u and d mass are similar, this description of particle mass and charge in terms of isospin and flavour quantum numbers works well only for octet and decuplet made of one u, one d, and one other quark, and breaks down for the other octets and decuplets (for example, ucb octet and decuplet). If the quarks all had the same mass, their behaviour would be called <i>symmetric</i>, as they would all behave in the same way to the strong interaction. Since quarks do not have the same mass, they do not interact in the same way (exactly like an electron placed in an electric field will accelerate more than a proton placed in the same field because of its lighter mass), and the symmetry is said to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_symmetry" title="Broken symmetry">broken</a>.\n</p><p>It was noted that charge (<i>Q</i>) was related to the isospin projection (<i>I</i><sub>3</sub>), the <a href="/wiki/Baryon_number" title="Baryon number">baryon number</a> (<i>B</i>) and flavour quantum numbers (<i>S</i>, <i>C</i>, <i>B</i>&#8242;, <i>T</i>) by the <a href="/wiki/Gell-Mann%E2%80%93Nishijima_formula" title="Gell-Mann&#8211;Nishijima formula">Gell-Mann&#8211;Nishijima formula</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WongA_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-WongA-11">[11]</a></sup><br/>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle Q=I_{\\mathrm {3} }+{\\frac {1}{2}}(B+S+C+B^{\\prime }+T),}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>Q</mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<msub>\n<mi>I</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>3</mn>\n</mrow>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo stretchy="false">(</mo>\n<mi>B</mi>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mi>S</mi>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mi>C</mi>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<msup>\n<mi>B</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi class="MJX-variant" mathvariant="normal">&#8242;<!-- &#8242; In some systems of <a href="/wiki/Natural_units" title="Natural units">natural units</a>, &#295; is chosen to be 1, and therefore does not appear anywhere.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">Quarks</a> are <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermionic</a> particles of spin <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span> (<i>S</i>&#160;=&#160;<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>). This concept of mirror reflection is called <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">intrinsic parity</a>" or simply "parity" (<i>P</i>). <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">Gravity</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_force" title="Electromagnetic force">electromagnetic force</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Strong_interaction" title="Strong interaction">strong interaction</a> all behave in the same way regardless of whether or not the universe is reflected in a mirror, and thus are said to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-symmetry" title="P-symmetry">conserve parity</a> (P-symmetry). However, the <a href="/wiki/Weak_interaction" title="Weak interaction">weak interaction</a> does distinguish "left" from "right", a phenomenon called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parity_violation" title="Parity violation">parity violation</a> (P-violation).\n</p><p>Based on this, if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavefunction" title="Wavefunction">wavefunction</a> for each particle (in more precise terms, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_field" title="Quantum field">quantum field</a> for each particle type) were simultaneously mirror-reversed, then the new set of wavefunctions would perfectly satisfy the laws of physics (apart from the weak interaction). It turns out that this is not quite true: for the equations to be satisfied, the wavefunctions of certain types of particles have to be multiplied by &#8722;1, in addition to being mirror-reversed.
