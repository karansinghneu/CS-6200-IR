srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ea/University_of_Otago_logo.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>University of Otago</b> (<a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="M&#257;ori language">M&#257;ori</a>: <i lang="mi">Te Whare W&#257;nanga o Ot&#257;go</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_university" title="Collegiate university">collegiate university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otago" title="Otago">Otago</a>, New Zealand. It scores highly for average research quality, and in 2006<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (July 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> was second in New Zealand only to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Auckland" title="University of Auckland">University of Auckland</a> in the number of A-rated academic researchers it employs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morst_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-morst-3">[3]</a></sup> In the past it has topped the New Zealand <a href="/wiki/Performance_Based_Research_Fund" title="Performance Based Research Fund">Performance Based Research Fund</a> evaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The university was created by a committee led by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burns_(minister)" title="Thomas Burns (minister)">Thomas Burns</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenguinHistoryKing_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PenguinHistoryKing-5">[5]</a></sup> and officially established by an ordinance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otago_Provincial_Council" title="Otago Provincial Council">Otago Provincial Council</a> in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The university accepted its first students in July 1871, making it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_universities_in_continuous_operation#Oceania" title="List of oldest universities in continuous operation">oldest university</a> in New Zealand and third-oldest in Oceania. Between 1874 and 1961 the University of Otago was a part of the federal <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Zealand" title="University of New Zealand">University of New Zealand</a>, and issued degrees in its name.\n</p><p>Otago is known for its vibrant student life, particularly its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatting" title="Flatting">flatting</a>, which is often in old houses. Otago students (Scarfies) have a long standing tradition of naming their flats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The nickname "Scarfie" comes from the habit of wearing a <a href="/wiki/Scarf" title="Scarf">scarf</a> during the cold southern winters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TeAra_Scarfie_Flats_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TeAra_Scarfie_Flats-9">[9]</a></sup> The university\'s graduation song, <i>Gaudeamus igitur, iuvenes dum sumus</i> ("Let us rejoice, while we are young"), acknowledges students will continue to live up to the challenge, if not always in the way intended.\n</p><p>The architectural grandeur and accompanying gardens of Otago University led to it being ranked as one of the world\'s most beautiful universities by the British publications <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup href="/wiki/Otago_Association" title="Otago Association">Otago Association</a>\'s plan for the European settlement of southern New Zealand, conceived under the principles of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Gibbon_Wakefield" title="Edward Gibbon Wakefield">Edward Gibbon Wakefield</a> in the 1840s, envisaged a university.\n</p><p>Dunedin leaders <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burns_(minister)" title="Thomas Burns (minister)">Thomas Burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Macandrew" title="James Macandrew">James Macandrew</a> urged the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otago_Provincial_Council" title="Otago Provincial Council">Otago Provincial Council</a> during the 1860s to set aside a land endowment for an institute of higher education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TeAra_Burns_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-TeAra_Burns-12">[12]</a></sup> An ordinance of the council established the university in 1869, giving it 100,000 acres (400&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of land and the power to grant degrees in Arts, Medicine, Law and Music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UoO_History_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-UoO_History-13">[13]</a></sup> Burns was named Chancellor but he did not live to see the university open on 5 July 1871.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenguinHistoryKing_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-PenguinHistoryKing-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TeAra_Burns_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-TeAra_Burns-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The university conferred just one degree, to <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Watt_Williamson" title="Alexander Watt Williamson">Alexander Watt Williamson</a>, before becoming an affiliate college of the federal <a href="/wiki/University_of_New_Zealand" title="University of New Zealand">University of New Zealand</a> in 1874. With the dissolution of the University of New Zealand in 1961 and the passage of the University of Otago Amendment Act 1961, the university resumed its power to confer degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UoO_History_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-UoO_History-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Originally operating from <a href="/wiki/William_Mason_(architect)" title="William Mason (architect)">William Mason</a>\'s Post Office building on <a href="/wiki/Princes_Street,_Dunedin" title="Princes Street, Dunedin">Princes Street</a>, it relocated to <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_Bury" title="Maxwell Bury">Maxwell Bury</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago_Registry_Building" title="University of Otago Registry Building">Clocktower</a> and Geology buildings in 1878 and 1879.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UoO_History_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-UoO_History-13">[13]</a></sup> This evolved into the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago_Clocktower_complex" title="University of Otago Clocktower complex">Clocktower complex</a>, a striking group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_revival" title="Gothic revival">Gothic revival</a> buildings at the heart of the campus. These buildings were inspired by then-new main building at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glasgow_University" title="Glasgow University">Glasgow University</a> in Scotland.\n</p><p>Otago was the first university in Australasia to permit women to take a law degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Benjamin" title="Ethel Benjamin">Ethel Benjamin</a> graduated LLB in 1897. Later that year she became the first woman in the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> to appear as counsel in court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MayhewAddress_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MayhewAddress-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Otago University helped train medical personnel as part of the Otago University Medical Corps. They supplied or trained most of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Army" title="New Zealand Army">New Zealand Army</a>\'s doctors and dentists during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Professor <a href="/wiki/Robert_Jack_(physicist)" title="Robert Jack (physicist)">Robert Jack</a> made the first radio broadcast in New Zealand from the physics department on 17 November 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> visited the university library with the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Duke of Edinburgh">Duke of Edinburgh</a> on 18 March 1970. This was the first time the royals completed informal &#8220;walkabouts&#8221; to meet the public, and it was the first visit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a> (then 21 years old) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Anne" title="Princess Anne">Princess Anne</a> (19 years) to this country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because it had a wide range of courses, Otago attracted more students from outside its provincial district. This led to the growth of colleges and informal accommodation in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Dunedin" title="North Dunedin">north Dunedin</a> around the faculty buildings. This development of a residential campus gave Otago a more vibrant undergraduate student life at the same time as comparable but smaller developments in Christchurch, Wellington and Auckland were eclipsed in the late 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Otago now has the most substantial residential campus of any university in New Zealand or Australia,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although this is not without its problems.\n</p><p>In May 2010 University joined the <a href="/wiki/Matariki_Network_of_Universities" title="Matariki Network of Universities">Matariki Network of Universities</a> (MNU) together with <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> (US), <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a> (UK), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen%E2%80%99s_University" title="Queen&#8217;s University">Queen&#8217;s University</a> (Canada), <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of T&#252;bingen">University of T&#252;bingen</a> (Germany), <a href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Australia" title="University of Western Australia">University of Western Australia</a> (Australia) and <a href="/wiki/Uppsala_University" title="Uppsala University">Uppsala University</a> (Sweden).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Universit%C3%A9_Otago.JPG/170px-Universit%C3%A9_Otago.JPG" Arms, Scotland is Azure, on a saltire cantoned between four mullets of six points Or, a book, gilt-edged and bound in a cover Gules charged with a mullet of six points of the second and a book-marker of the third issuance from the page-foot, and in an Escrol under the same this Motto "Sapere Aude".\n</p><p>The motto may be translated as \'dare to be wise\' or \'have courage to be wise\'. The shield is first described and it is blue (azure.) On the shield is a saltire which is an &#8220;X&#8221; shaped object. On the saltire sits a gilt edged book the cover of which is red (gules.) On the cover of the book is a star (mullet) of six points. Mullets normally only have five points. At the foot of the book is a bookmark also in red being the third colour mentioned. The saltire and the book are surrounded by four other stars each also of six points which are placed in the spaces formed by the saltire. The five stars and the saltire are all coloured gold (or) which was the second colour mentioned. An Escrol is the scroll under the shield containing the motto.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span of Sciences</li>\n<li>School of Business</li></ul>\n<p>For external and marketing purposes, the Division of Commerce is known as the School of Business, as that is the term commonly used for its equivalent in North America. Historically, there were a number of schools and faculties, which have now been grouped with stand alone departments to form these divisions.\n</p><p>In addition to the usual university disciplines, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Otago_Medical_School" title="University of Otago Medical School">University of Otago Medical School</a> (founded 1875) is one of only two medical schools in New Zealand (with component schools in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunedin_School_of_Medicine" title="Dunedin School of Medicine">Dunedin</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago,_Christchurch" title="University of Otago, Christchurch">Christchurch</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago,_Wellington" title="University of Otago, Wellington">Wellington</a>); and Otago is the only university in the country to offer training in Dentistry. Other professional schools and faculties not found in all New Zealand universities include Pharmacy, Physical Education, Physiotherapy, Medical Laboratory Science, and Surveying. It was also home to the School of Mines, until this was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Auckland" title="University of Auckland">University of Auckland</a> in 1987. Theology is also offered, traditionally in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Knox_College,_Otago" title="Knox College, Otago">School of Ministry, Knox College</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" The <i>University of Otago College of Education</i> is now based on the College site, and includes the College\'s campuses in <a href="/wiki/Invercargill" title="Invercargill">Invercargill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandra,_New_Zealand" title="Alexandra, New Zealand">Alexandra</a>. Staff of the University\'s Faculty of Education relocated to the college site. A merger had been considered before, however the present talks progressed further, and more amicably, than previously.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Otago&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:University_of_Otago_New_Library_(inside).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg/220px-University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg/330px-University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg/440px-University_of_Otago_New_Library_%28inside%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:University_of_Otago_New_Library_(inside).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior of the Central Library</div></div></div>\n<p>The University of Otago has ten libraries: seven based in Dunedin on the main university campus, the education library in Southland, plus two medical libraries in Wellington and Christchurch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> All libraries have wireless access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Central Library is part of the Information Services Building and has over 2000 study spaces, 130 computer terminals, and laptop connections at 500 desks. It has Te Aka a T&#257;whaki, a collection of M&#257;ori resources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the Special Collections consisting of about 9,000 books printed before 1801. In total, the Central Library has over 800,000 print and electronic materials relating to the arts and humanities, commerce, education, physical education, social sciences, and technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_Tour_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-University_Tour-26">[26]</a></sup> It was designed by the American architecture firm <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Hardy" title="Hugh Hardy">Hardy Holzman Pfeiffer</a> and opened in 2001, replacing what was previously a 1960s-era modernist building.\n</p><p>The Health Sciences Library is in the Sayers Building, opposite the main entrance to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunedin_Hospital" title="Dunedin Hospital">Dunedin Hospital</a>. The Health Sciences Library book collection only includes the last 10 years of content, but does have over 150,000 volumes, the vast majority of which are in storage. There is seating for over 400.\n</p><p>The Science Library is at the north end of the campus in the Science III building, with seating for approximately 500.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hocken_Collections" title="Hocken Collections">Hocken Collections</a> is a research library, archive, and art gallery of national significance which is administrated by the University of Otago. The library\'s specialist areas include items relating to the history of New Zealand and the Pacific, with specific emphasis on the <a href="/wiki/Otago" title="Otago">Otago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southland,_New_Zealand" title="Southland, New Zealand">Southland</a> regions. The Hocken Collections was established in 1910 when Dunedin philanthropist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hocken" title="Thomas Hocken">Thomas Hocken</a> donated his entire private collection to the University of Otago. It currently houses over 8,000 linear metres of archives and manuscripts. It is currently situated at the site of the former Otago Co-operative Dairy Company factory on Anzac Avenue, east of the main campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_Tour_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-University_Tour-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stout_Law_Library" title="Robert Stout Law Library">Robert Stout Law Library</a> is the university\'s law library and is based in the Richardson Building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Robertson_Library" title="Robertson Library">Robertson Library</a> is the university\'s education library and is jointly run by the University of Otago\'s <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago_College_of_Education" title="University of Otago College of Education">College of Education</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otago_Polytechnic" title="Otago Polytechnic">Otago class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Otago&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: O-Week">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Otago_clocktower_race.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Otago_clocktower_race.jpg/220px-Otago_clocktower_race.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Otago_clocktower_race.jpg/330px-Otago_clocktower_race.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Otago_clocktower_race.jpg/440px-Otago_clocktower_race.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Otago_clocktower_race.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Participants in the annual clocktower race lining up, ready to go.</div></div></div>\n<p>\'O-Week\' or <i>Orientation Week</i> is the Otago equivalent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshers%27_Week" title="Freshers\' Week">Freshers\' Week</a>. New students are most commonly known by their seniors as \'freshers\' or simply as \'first-years\'. O-week is organised by the <a href="/wiki/Otago_University_Students%27_Association" title="Otago University Students\' Association">Otago University Students\' Association</a> and involves competitions such as \'Fresher of the Year\' whereby several students volunteer to carry out a series of tasks throughout the week before being voted to win. Other competitions include that of different faculties facing off with each other. The OUSA also organises events each night including various concerts, a comedy night, hypnotist plus bigger events at <a href="/wiki/Forsyth_Barr_Stadium" title="Forsyth Barr Stadium">Forsyth Barr Stadium</a>. Typically there is a Highlanders rugby game scheduled during the week. Local bars organise events also with a range of live music and promotional deals. Historically events have included the Cookathon and a Miss O-Week competition hosted by The Outback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.seenindunedin.co.nz/events/student/oweek_2005.html_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.seenindunedin.co.nz/events/student/oweek_2005.html-35">[35]</a></sup> The Cookathon was held by a local pub (the Cook) with the premise that your first drink costs you about $20 which gives you a t-shirt, three meal vouchers and reduced price on drinks then you spend the rest of the day binge drinking and \'telephoning\' the occasional jug with mates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://www.varsity.co.nz/blog/orientation-otago.html_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://www.varsity.co.nz/blog/orientation-otago.html-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Traditions</dt></dl>\n<p>Each year the first years are encouraged to attend the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toga_parade" title="Toga parade">toga parade</a> and party dressed in white sheets wrapped as togas. Retailers called for an end of the parade after property damage and disorder during the 2009 event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, the OUSA took it upon themselves to reintroduce this tradition, with a festival like event taking place at the stadium. 2012 Toga Party saw an unofficial world record. A clocktower race also occurs, in the style of <i><a href="/wiki/Chariots_of_Fire" title="Chariots of Fire">Chariots of Fire</a></i>. Students must race round the tower and attached building, beginning on the first chime of the clock at noon and completing before the chimes cease. Unlike <i>Chariots of Fire</i>, the task is possible with a couple of students completing each year.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioural_issues">Behavioural issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Otago&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Behavioural issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Student behaviour is a major concern for both the University administration and Dunedin residents in general. Concerns over student behaviour prompted the University to introduce a Code of Conduct (CoC) which its students must abide by in 2007. The introduction of the CoC was accompanied by the establishment of the dedicated \'Campus Watch\' security force to keep tabs on crime and anti-social behaviour on campus and in the student neighbourhoods nearby. Campus Watch reports directly to the University\'s Proctor.\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Riots</dt></dl>\n<p>Riots took place in 2006, 2007, 2008 and 2009 related to events surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Undie_500" title="Undie 500">Undie 500</a> car rally organised by students from Canterbury University. Other student social events during the year such as the Toga Parade and the Hyde Street Keg Race are also notable for attracting police attention, but not to the scale of the Undie riots. In 2012 there were 80 people treated by emergency services and 15 arrests by police after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyde_Street_party" title="Hyde Street party">Hyde Street party</a> went out of control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Protest</dt></dl>\n<p>Otago students are notable for protesting over contentious political issues in nearly every decade. In the 1960s students at Otago who were involved with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Progressive_Youth_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Progressive Youth Movement (page does not exist)">Progressive Youth Movement</a> led protests against the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. In the 1970s mixed flatting (males and females were prohibited from sharing housing up to that time) was contested in various creative ways by Otago students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> On 28 September 1993 Otago students protested against a fee increase at the University Registry (Clocktower Building), which ended in a violent clash with police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In the lead up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1996" title="New Zealand general election, 1996">1996 general election</a> students trying to stop a 25% fee increase occupied the University Registry (Clocktower Building) for over a week (which was followed by similar occupations at campuses around the country), fee increases were limited to 17%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Since 2004, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otago_University_NORML" title="Otago University NORML">Otago University NORML</a> club has met weekly on the Otago campus to protest by smoking cannabis in defiance of New Zealand\'s cannabis laws. In 2008, several members were arrested and issued with trespass notices banning them from the Union Lawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
