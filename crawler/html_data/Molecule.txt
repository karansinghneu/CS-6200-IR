chemical structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>molecule</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrically" title="Electrically">electrically</a> neutral group of two or more <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> held together by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iupac-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Molecules are distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> by their lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_charge" title="Electrical charge">electrical charge</a>. However, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, the term <i>molecule</i> is often used less strictly, also being applied to <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic ions</a>.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">kinetic theory of gases</a>, the term <i>molecule</i> is often used for any gaseous <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particle</a> regardless of its composition. According to this definition, <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a> atoms are considered molecules as they are monatomic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A molecule may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homonuclear" title="Homonuclear">homonuclear</a>, that is, it consists of atoms of one <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a>, as with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>); or it may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteronuclear" title="Heteronuclear">heteronuclear</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> composed of more than one element, as with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O). Atoms and complexes connected by <a href="/wiki/Non-covalent_interactions" title="Non-covalent interactions">non-covalent interactions</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonds</a>, are typically not considered single molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Molecules as components of matter are common in organic substances (and therefore biochemistry). They also make up most of the oceans and atmosphere. However, the majority of familiar solid substances on Earth, including most of the minerals that make up the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_core" title="Earth core">core of the Earth</a>, contain many chemical bonds, but are <i>not</i> made of identifiable molecules. Also, no typical molecule can be defined for <a href="/wiki/Ionic_crystal" title="Ionic crystal">ionic crystals</a> (<a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>) and covalent crystals (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_solid" title="Network solid">network solids</a>), although these are often composed of repeating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_cell" title="Unit cell">unit cells</a> that extend either in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> (such as in <a href="/wiki/Graphene" title="Graphene">graphene</a>) or three-dimensionally (such as in <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>). The theme of repeated unit-cellular-structure also holds for most condensed phases with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">metallic bonding</a>, which means that solid metals are also not made of molecules. In <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> (solids that exist in a vitreous disordered state), atoms may also be held together by chemical bonds with no presence of any definable molecule, nor any of the regularity of repeating units that characterizes crystals.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_science">Molecular science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Molecular science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The science of molecules is called <i>molecular chemistry</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Molecular_physics" title="Molecular physics">molecular physics</a></i>, depending on whether the focus is on chemistry or physics. Molecular chemistry deals with the laws governing the interaction between molecules that results in the formation and breakage of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>, while molecular physics deals with the laws governing their structure and properties. In practice, however, this distinction is vague. In molecular sciences, a molecule consists of a stable system (<a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bound state</a>) composed of two or more <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">Polyatomic ions</a> may sometimes be usefully thought of as electrically charged molecules. The term <i>unstable molecule</i> is used for very <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactive</a> species, i.e., short-lived assemblies (<a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">resonances</a>) of electrons and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nuclei</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radicals</a>, molecular <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rydberg_molecule" title="Rydberg molecule">Rydberg molecules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transition_state" title="Transition state">transition states</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_bonding" title="Van der Waals bonding">van der Waals complexes</a>, or systems of colliding atoms as in <a href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate" title="Bose&#8211;Einstein condensate">Bose&#8211;Einstein condensate</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_etymology">History and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_molecular_theory" title="History of molecular theory">History of molecular theory</a></div>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam-Webster</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Online_Etymology_Dictionary" title="Online Etymology Dictionary">Online Etymology Dictionary</a>, the word "molecule" derives from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> "<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>" or small unit of mass.\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Molecule</b> (1794)&#160;&#8211; "extremely minute particle", from French <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mol%C3%A9cule" title="wikt:mol&#233;cule">mol&#233;cule</a></i> (1678), from <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/molecula" title="wikt:molecula">molecula</a></i>, diminutive of Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/moles" title="wikt:moles">moles</a></i> "mass, barrier". A vague meaning at first; the vogue for the word (used until the late 18th century only in Latin form) can be traced to the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="Ren&#233; Descartes">Descartes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The definition of the molecule has evolved as knowledge of the structure of molecules has increased. Earlier definitions were less precise, defining molecules as the smallest <a href="/wiki/List_of_particles#Molecules" title="List of particles">particles</a> of pure <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substances</a> that still retain their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">composition</a> and chemical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This definition often breaks down since many substances in ordinary experience, such as <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, are composed of large crystalline networks of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemically bonded</a> atoms or <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, but are not made of discrete molecules.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding">Bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Molecules are held together by either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent_bonding" title="Covalent bonding">covalent bonding</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">ionic bonding</a>. Several types of non-metal elements exist only as molecules in the environment. For example, hydrogen only exists as hydrogen molecule. A molecule of a compound is made out of two or more elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Covalent">Covalent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Covalent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent_bonding" title="Covalent bonding">Covalent bonding</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="94" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg/220px-Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg/330px-Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg/440px-Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Covalent_bond_hydrogen.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A covalent bond forming H<sub>2</sub> (right) where two <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atoms</a> share the two electrons</div></div></div>\n<p>A covalent bond is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> that involves the sharing of <a href="/wiki/Electron_pair" title="Electron pair">electron pairs</a> between <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. These electron pairs are termed <i>shared pairs</i> or <i>bonding pairs</i>, and the stable balance of attractive and repulsive forces between atoms, when they share electrons, is termed <i>covalent bonding</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ionic">Ionic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ionic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">Ionic bonding</a></div> <div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NaF.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="560" decoding="async" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/NaF.gif/220px-NaF.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/NaF.gif/330px-NaF.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/NaF.gif/440px-NaF.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NaF.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> undergoing a redox reaction to form <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a>. Sodium loses its outer <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>  to give it a stable <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configuration</a>, and this electron enters the fluorine atom <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermically</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Ionic bonding is a type of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> that involves the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatic</a> attraction between oppositely charged <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, and is the primary interaction occurring in <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a>. The ions are atoms that have lost one or more <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> (termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a>) and atoms that have gained one or more electrons (termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This transfer of electrons is termed <i>electrovalence</i> in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalence</a>. In the simplest case, the cation is a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> atom and the anion is a <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal" title="Nonmetal">nonmetal</a> atom, but these ions can be of a more complicated nature, e.g. molecular ions like NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> or SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2&#8722;</sup>. Basically, an ionic bond is the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal for both atoms to obtain a full valence shell.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_size">Molecular size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Molecular size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most molecules are far too small to be seen with the naked eye, but there are exceptions. <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecule</a>, can reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> sizes, as can molecules of many <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>. Molecules commonly used as building blocks for organic synthesis have a dimension of a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">angstroms</a> (&#197;) to several dozen &#197;, or around one billionth of a meter. Single molecules cannot usually be observed by <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> (as noted above), but small molecules and even the outlines of individual atoms may be traced in some circumstances by use of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_force_microscope" title="Atomic force microscope">atomic force microscope</a>. Some of the largest molecules are <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supermolecule" title="Supermolecule">supermolecules</a>.\n</p><p>The smallest molecule is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>), with a bond length of 0.74 &#197;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Effective molecular radius is the size a molecule displays in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Table_of_permselectivity_for_different_substances" title="Table of permselectivity for different substances">table of permselectivity for different substances</a> contains examples.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_formulas">Molecular formulas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Molecular formulas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_formula_types">Chemical formula types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemical formula types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">Chemical formula</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> for a molecule uses one line of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> symbols, numbers, and sometimes also other symbols, such as parentheses, dashes, brackets, and <i>plus</i> (+) and <i>minus</i> (&#8722;) signs. These are limited to one typographic line of symbols, which may include subscripts and superscripts.\n</p><p>A compound\'s <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> is a very simple type of chemical formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is the simplest <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integer</a> <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> that constitute it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> For example, water is always composed of a 2:1 ratio of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms, and ethyl <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> is always composed of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in a 2:6:1 ratio. However, this does not determine the kind of molecule uniquely&#160;&#8211; <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a> has the same ratios as ethanol, for instance. Molecules with the same <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> in different arrangements are called <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a>. Also carbohydrates, for example, have the same ratio (carbon:hydrogen:oxygen= 1:2:1) (and thus the same empirical formula) but different total numbers of atoms in the molecule.\n</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> reflects the exact number of atoms that compose the molecule and so characterizes different molecules. However different isomers can have the same atomic composition while being different molecules.\n</p><p>The empirical formula is often the same as the molecular formula but not always. For example, the molecule <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a> has molecular formula C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>, but the simplest integer ratio of elements is CH.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> can be calculated from the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> and is expressed in conventional <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass units</a> equal to 1/12 of the mass of a neutral carbon-12 (<sup>12</sup><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>) atom. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Network_solid" title="Network solid">network solids</a>, the term <a href="/wiki/Formula_unit" title="Formula unit">formula unit</a> is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> calculations.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" involving atoms bonded to four different substituents, a simple molecular formula or even semi-structural <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> may not be enough to completely specify the molecule. about which they continuously oscillate through vibrational and rotational motions. A pure substance is composed of molecules with the same average geometrical structure. The chemical formula and the structure of a molecule are the two important factors that determine its properties, particularly its <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">Isomers</a> share a chemical formula but normally have very different properties because of their different structures. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" title="Stereoisomer">Stereoisomers</a>, a particular type of isomer, may have very similar physico-chemical properties and at the same time Molecules have quantized energy levels that can be analyzed by detecting the molecule\'s energy exchange through <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorbance</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Emission (electromagnetic radiation)">emission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-iupac2-24">[24]</a></sup>\nSpectroscopy does not generally refer to <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> studies where particles such as <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, or high energy <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> interact with a regular arrangement of molecules (as in a crystal).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy" title="Microwave spectroscopy">Microwave spectroscopy</a> commonly measures changes in the rotation of molecules, and can be used to identify molecules in outer space. <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">Infrared spectroscopy</a> measures changes in vibration of molecules, including stretching, bending or twisting motions. Nuclear resonance spectroscopy actually measures the environment of particular nuclei in the molecule, and can be used to characterise the numbers of atoms in different positions in a molecule.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_aspects">Theoretical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molecule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theoretical aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The study of molecules by <a href="/wiki/Molecular_physics" title="Molecular physics">molecular physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_chemistry" title="Theoretical chemistry">theoretical chemistry</a> is largely based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanic" title="Quantum mechanic">quantum mechanics</a> and is essential for the understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a>. The simplest of molecules is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_molecule-ion" title="Hydrogen molecule-ion">hydrogen molecule-ion</a>, H<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, and the simplest of all the chemical bonds is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One-electron_bond" title="One-electron bond">one-electron bond</a>. H<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> is composed of two positively charged <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and one negatively charged <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, which means that the <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schr&#246;dinger equation">Schr&#246;dinger equation</a> for the system can be solved more easily due to the lack of electron&#8211;electron repulsion. With the development of fast digital computers, approximate solutions for more complicated molecules became possible and are one of the main aspects of <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">computational chemistry</a>.\n</p><p>When trying to define rigorously whether an arrangement of atoms is <i>sufficiently stable</i> to be considered a molecule, IUPAC suggests that it "must correspond to a depression on the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy_surface" title="Potential energy surface">potential energy surface</a> that is deep enough to confine at least one vibrational state".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-iupac-4">[4]</a></sup> This definition does not depend on the nature of the interaction between the atoms, but only on the strength of the interaction. In fact, it includes weakly bound species that would not traditionally be considered molecules, such as the <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium_dimer" title="Helium dimer">He<sub>2</sub></a>, which has one vibrational <a href="/wiki/Bound_state" title="Bound state">bound state</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and is so loosely bound that it is only likely to be observed at very low temperatures.\n</p><p>Whether or not an arrangement of atoms is <i>sufficiently stable</i> to be considered a molecule is inherently an operational definition. Philosophically, therefore, a molecule is not a fundamental entity (in contrast, for instance, to an <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particle</a>); rather, the concept of a molecule is the chemist\'s way of making a useful statement about the strengths of atomic-scale interactions in the world that we observe.\n</p>\n<div
