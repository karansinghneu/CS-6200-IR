The lobes only emit in the radio frequency range, and so appear red. Gas and dust in the galaxy emits <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>. Thermal X-ray radiation from hot gas and non-thermal emission from relativistic electrons can be seen in the blue \'shells\' around the lobes, particularly to the south (bottom).</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Radio galaxies</b> and their relatives, radio-loud <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">blazars</a>, are types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galaxy_nuclei" title="Active galaxy nuclei">active galaxy nuclei</a> that are very luminous at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio wavelengths</a>, with luminosities up to 10<sup>39</sup> <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a> between 10&#160;MHz and 100&#160;GHz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The radio emission is due to the <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron process</a>. The observed structure in radio emission is determined by the interaction between twin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_jet" title="Relativistic jet">jets</a> and the external medium, modified by the effects of <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_beaming" title="Relativistic beaming">relativistic beaming</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Host_galaxy" title="Host galaxy">host galaxies</a> are almost exclusively large <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a>. <i>Radio-loud</i> active galaxies can be detected at large distances, making them valuable tools for <a href="/wiki/Observational_cosmology" title="Observational cosmology">observational cosmology</a>. Recently, much work has been done on the effects of these objects on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intergalactic_medium" title="Intergalactic medium">intergalactic medium</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters" title="Galaxy groups and clusters">galaxy groups and clusters</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_processes">Emission processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Emission processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio emission</a> from radio-loud active galaxies is <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">synchrotron emission</a>, as inferred from its very smooth, broad-band nature and strong <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a>. This implies that the radio-emitting <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> contains, at least, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> with <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">relativistic</a> speeds (<a href="/wiki/Lorentz_factor" title="Lorentz factor">Lorentz factors</a> of ~10<sup>4</sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>. Since the plasma must be neutral, it must also contain either <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>. There is no way of determining the particle content directly from observations of synchrotron radiation. Moreover, there is no way to determine the energy densities in particles and magnetic fields from observation: the same synchrotron emissivity may be a result of a few electrons and a strong field, or a weak field and many electrons, or something in between. It is possible to determine a minimum energy condition which is the minimum energy density that a region with a given emissivity can have, but for many years there was no particular reason to believe that the true energies were anywhere near the minimum energies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A sister process to the synchrotron radiation is the <a href="/wiki/Compton_scattering" title="Compton scattering">inverse-Compton</a> process, in which the relativistic electrons interact with ambient photons and <a href="/wiki/Thomson_scattering" title="Thomson scattering">Thomson scatter</a> them to high energies. Inverse-Compton emission from radio-loud sources turns out to be particularly important in X-rays,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and, because it depends only on the density of electrons, a detection of inverse-Compton scattering allows a somewhat model-dependent estimate of the energy densities in the particles and magnetic fields. This has been used to argue that many powerful sources are actually quite near the minimum-energy condition.\n</p><p>Synchrotron radiation is not confined to radio wavelengths: if the radio source can accelerate particles to high enough energies, features that are detected in the radio wavelengths may also be seen in the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">optical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> or even <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a>. In the latter case the responsible electrons must have energies in excess of 1 <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">TeV</a> in typical magnetic field strengths. Again, polarization and continuum spectrum are used to distinguish the synchrotron radiation from other emission processes. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jets" title="Astrophysical jets">Jets</a> and hotspots are the usual sources of high-frequency synchrotron emission. It is hard to distinguish observationally between the synchrotron and inverse-Compton radiation, making them a subject of ongoing research.\n</p><p>Processes, collectively known as particle acceleration, produce populations of relativistic and non-thermal particles that give rise to synchrotron and inverse-Compton radiation. <a href="/wiki/Fermi_acceleration" title="Fermi acceleration">Fermi acceleration</a> is one plausible particle acceleration process in radio-loud active galaxies.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_structures">Radio structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radio structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radio_galaxy_3C98.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="222" decoding="async" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Radio_galaxy_3C98.png/200px-Radio_galaxy_3C98.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/Radio_galaxy_3C98.png 1.5x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radio_galaxy_3C98.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-colour" title="Pseudo-colour">Pseudo-colour</a> image of the large-scale radio structure of the FRII radio galaxy 3C98. Lobes, jet and hotspot are labelled.</div></div></div>\n<p>Radio galaxies, and to a lesser extent, radio-loud quasars display a wide range of structures in radio maps. The most common large-scale structures are called <i>lobes</i>: these are double, often fairly symmetrical, roughly ellipsoidal structures placed on either side of the active nucleus. A significant minority of low-luminosity sources exhibit structures usually known as <i>plumes</i> which are much more elongated. Some radio galaxies show one or two long narrow features known as <i>jets</i> (the most famous example being the giant galaxy <a href="/wiki/Messier_87" title="Messier 87">M87</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgo_cluster" title="Virgo cluster">Virgo cluster</a>) coming directly from the nucleus and going to the lobes. Since the 1970s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scheuer74_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scheuer74-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the most widely accepted model has been that the lobes or plumes are powered by <i>beams</i> of high-energy particles and magnetic field coming from close to the active nucleus. The jets are believed to be the visible manifestations of the beams, and often the term <i>jet</i> is used to refer both to the observable feature and to the underlying flow.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:195px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radio_galaxy_3C31.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="193" decoding="async" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Radio_galaxy_3C31.png" width="193"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radio_galaxy_3C31.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-colour" title="Pseudo-colour">Pseudo-colour</a> image of the large-scale radio structure of the FRI radio galaxy <a href="/wiki/NGC_383" title="NGC 383">3C31</a>. Jets and plumes are labelled.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1974, radio sources were divided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernard_Fanaroff" title="Bernard Fanaroff">Fanaroff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julia_Riley" title="Julia Riley">Riley</a> into two classes, now known as <a href="/wiki/Fanaroff-Riley_classification" title="Fanaroff-Riley classification">Fanaroff and Riley Class I (FRI), and Class II (FRII)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fanaroff74_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fanaroff74-6">[6]</a></sup> The distinction was originally made based on the morphology of the large-scale radio emission (the type was determined by the distance between the brightest points in the radio emission): FRI sources were brightest towards the centre, while FRII sources were brightest at the edges. Fanaroff and Riley observed that there was a reasonably sharp divide in <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">luminosity</a> between the two classes: FRIs were low-luminosity, FRIIs were high luminosity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fanaroff74_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fanaroff74-6">[6]</a></sup> With more detailed radio observations, the morphology turns out to reflect the method of energy transport in the radio source. FRI objects typically have bright jets in the centre, while FRIIs have faint jets but bright <i>hotspots</i> at the ends of the lobes. FRIIs appear to be able to transport energy efficiently to the ends of the lobes, while FRI beams are inefficient in the sense that they radiate a significant amount of their energy away as they travel.\n</p><p>In more detail, the FRI/FRII division depends on host-galaxy environment in the sense that the FRI/FRII transition appears at higher luminosities in more massive galaxies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> FRI jets are known to be decelerating in the regions in which their radio emission is brightest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and so it seems that the FRI/FRII transition reflects whether a jet/beam can propagate through the host galaxy without being decelerated to sub-relativistic speeds by interaction with the intergalactic medium. From analysis of relativistic beaming effects, the jets of FRII sources are known to remain relativistic (with speeds of at least 0.5c) out to the ends of the lobes. The hotspots that are usually seen in FRII sources are interpreted as being the visible manifestations of <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shocks</a> formed when the fast, and therefore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a>, jet (the speed of sound cannot exceed c/&#8730;3) abruptly terminates at the end of the source, and their spectral energy distributions are consistent with this picture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Often multiple hotspots are seen, reflecting either continued outflow after the shock or movement of the jet termination point: the overall hotspot region is sometimes called the hotspot complex.\n</p><p>Names are given to several particular types of radio source based on their radio structure:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Classical double</i> refers to an FRII source with clear hotspots.</li>\n<li><i>Wide-angle tail</i> normally refers to a source intermediate between standard FRI and FRII structure, with efficient jets and sometimes hotspots, but with plumes rather than lobes, found at or near the centres of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groups_and_clusters_of_galaxies" title="Groups and clusters of galaxies">clusters</a>.</li>\n<li><i>Narrow-angle tail</i> or <i>Head-tail source</i> describes an FRI that appears to be bent by <a href="/wiki/Ram_pressure" title="Ram pressure">ram pressure</a> as it moves through a cluster.</li>\n<li><i>Fat doubles</i> are sources with diffuse lobes but neither jets nor hotspots. Some such sources may be <i>relics</i> whose energy supply has been permanently or temporarily turned off.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycles_and_dynamics">Life cycles and dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life cycles and dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The largest radio galaxies have lobes or plumes extending to <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">megaparsec</a> scales (more in the case of giant radio galaxies like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3C236" title="3C236">3C236</a>), implying a timescale for growth of the order of tens to hundreds of millions of years. This means that, except in the case of very small, very young sources, we cannot observe radio source dynamics directly, and so must resort to theory and inferences from large numbers of objects. Clearly radio sources must start small and grow larger. In the case of sources with lobes, the dynamics are fairly simple:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scheuer74_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scheuer74-4">[4]</a></sup> the jets feed the lobes, the pressure of the lobes increases, and the lobes expand. How fast they expand depends on the density and pressure of the external medium. The highest-pressure phase of the external medium, and thus the most important phase from the point of view of the dynamics, is the X-ray emitting diffuse hot gas. For a long time it was assumed that powerful sources would expand supersonically, pushing a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock</a> through the external medium. However, X-ray observations show that the internal lobe pressures of powerful FRII sources are often close to the external thermal pressures and not much higher than the external pressures, as would be required for supersonic expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The only unambiguously supersonically expanding system known consists of the inner lobes of the low-power radio galaxy <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a>  which are probably a result of a comparatively recent outburst of the active nucleus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Host_galaxies_and_environments">Host galaxies and environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Host galaxies and environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>These radio sources are almost universally found <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Host_galaxy" title="Host galaxy">hosted</a> by <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy" title="Elliptical galaxy">elliptical galaxies</a>, though there is one well-documented exception, namely <a href="/wiki/NGC_4151" title="NGC 4151">NGC 4151</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seyfert_galaxies" title="Seyfert galaxies">Seyfert galaxies</a> show weak, small radio jets, but they are not radio-luminous enough to be classified as radio-loud. Such information as there is about the host galaxies of radio-loud <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blazar" title="Blazar">blazars</a> suggests that they are also hosted by elliptical galaxies.\n</p><p>There are several possible reasons for this very strong preference for ellipticals. One is that ellipticals generally contain the most massive <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">black holes</a>, and so are capable of powering the most luminous active galaxies (see <a href="/wiki/Eddington_luminosity" title="Eddington luminosity">Eddington luminosity</a>). Another is that ellipticals generally inhabit richer environments, providing a large-scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intergalactic_medium" title="Intergalactic medium">intergalactic medium</a> to confine the radio source. It may also be that the larger amounts of cold gas in <a href="/wiki/Spiral_galaxy" title="Spiral galaxy">spiral galaxies</a> in some way disrupts or stifles a forming jet. To date there is no compelling single explanation for the observations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unified_models">Unified models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unified models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The different types of radio-loud active galaxies are linked by unified models. The key observation that led to the adoption of unified models for powerful radio galaxies and radio-loud quasars was that all quasars appear to be beamed towards us, showing <a href="/wiki/Superluminal_motion" title="Superluminal motion">superluminal motion</a> in the cores<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and bright jets on the side of the source nearest to us (the <i>Laing-Garrington effect</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>). If this is the case, there must be a population of objects not beamed towards us, and, since we know the lobes are not affected by beaming, they would appear as radio galaxies, provided that the quasar nucleus is obscured when the source is seen side-on. It is now accepted that at least some powerful radio galaxies have \'hidden\' quasars, though it is not clear whether all such radio galaxies would be quasars if viewed from the right angle. In a similar way, low-power radio galaxies are a plausible parent population for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BL_Lac_object" title="BL Lac object">BL Lac objects</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_radio_galaxies">Uses of radio galaxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses of radio galaxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distant_sources">Distant sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distant sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Radio galaxies and radio-loud quasars have been widely used, particularly in the 80s and 90s, to find distant galaxies: by selecting based on radio spectrum and then observing the host galaxy it was possible to find objects at high <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> at modest cost in telescope time. The problem with this method is that hosts of active galaxies may not be typical of galaxies at their redshift. Similarly, radio galaxies have in the past been used to find distant X-ray emitting clusters, but unbiased selection methods are now preferred.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_rulers">Standard rulers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Standard rulers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some work has been done attempting to use radio galaxies as <a href="/wiki/Standard_ruler" title="Standard ruler">standard rulers</a> to determine <a href="/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model" title="Lambda-CDM model">cosmological parameters</a>. This method is fraught with difficulty because a radio galaxy\'s size depends on both its age and its environment. When a model of the radio source is used, though, methods based on radio galaxies can give good agreement with other cosmological observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_environment">Effects on environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effects on environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Whether or not a radio source is expanding supersonically, it must do work against the external medium in expanding, and so it puts energy into heating and lifting the external plasma. The minimum energy stored in the lobes of a powerful radio source might be 10<sup>53</sup> <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>. The lower limit on the work done on the external medium by such a source is several times this. A good deal of the current interest in radio sources focuses on the effect they must have at the centres of clusters at the present day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Equally interesting is their likely effect on structure formation over cosmological time: it is thought that they may provide a feedback mechanism to slow the formation of the most massive objects.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Widely used terminology is awkward now that it is generally accepted that quasars and radio galaxies are the same objects (see <a href="#Unified_models">above</a>). The acronym <i>DRAGN</i> (for \'Double Radiosource Associated with Galactic Nucleus\') has been coined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\nbut has not yet taken off. <i>Extragalactic radio source</i> is common but can lead to confusion, since many other extragalactic objects are detected in radio surveys, notably <a href="/wiki/Starburst_galaxy" title="Starburst galaxy">starburst galaxies</a>. <i>Radio-loud active galaxy</i> is unambiguous, and so is often used in this article.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#Relativistic_jet" title="Astrophysical jet">Relativistic jet</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/X-shaped_radio_galaxy" title="X-shaped radio galaxy">X-shaped radio galaxy</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/M%E2%80%93sigma_relation" title="M&#8211;sigma relation">M&#8211;sigma relation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/3C_321" title="3C 321">Death Star Galaxy</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_galaxy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
