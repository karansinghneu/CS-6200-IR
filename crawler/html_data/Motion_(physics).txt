class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Motion_(disambiguation)" title="Motion (disambiguation)">Motion (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Change in position of an object over time</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>motion</b> is a change in <a href="/wiki/Position_(vector)" title="Position (vector)">position</a> of an object over time. Motion is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematically" title="Mathematically">mathematically</a>  described in terms of <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(vector)" title="Displacement (vector)">displacement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>. Motion of a body is observed by attaching a <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> to an observer and measuring the change in position of the body relative to that frame.\n</p><p>If the position of a body is not changing with respect to a given frame of reference (reference point), the body is said to be <i>at rest</i>, <i>motionless</i>, <i>immobile</i>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stationary" title="wikt:stationary">stationary</a></i>, or to have constant (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time-invariant" title="Time-invariant">time-invariant</a>) position. An object\'s motion cannot change unless it is acted upon by a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a>, as described. Momentum is a quantity which is used for measuring the motion of an object. An object\'s <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> is directly related to the object\'s <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and velocity, and the total momentum of all objects in an isolated system (one not affected by external forces) does not change with time, as described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_conservation_of_momentum" title="Law of conservation of momentum">law of conservation of momentum</a>.\n</p><p>As there is no absolute frame of reference, <i><a href="/wiki/Absolute_space_and_time" title="Absolute space and time">absolute motion</a></i> cannot be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus, everything in the universe can be considered to be moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyson-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>20&#8211;21</span></sup>\n</p><p>Motion applies to objects, bodies, and matter particles, to radiation, radiation fields and radiation particles, and to space, its curvature and space-time. One can also speak of motion of shapes and boundaries. So, the term motion, in general, signifies a continuous change in the configuration of a physical system. For example, one can talk about motion of a wave or about motion of a quantum particle, where the configuration consists of probabilities of occupying specific positions.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">Mechanics</a></div>\n<p>In physics, motion is described through two sets of apparently contradictory <a href="/wiki/Scientific_law" title="Scientific law">laws</a> of mechanics. Motions of all large-scale and familiar objects in the universe (such as <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>) are described by <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>. Whereas the motion of very small <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atomic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">sub-atomic</a> objects is described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanic" title="Quantum mechanic">quantum mechanics</a>.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td><b>First law</b>:\n</td>\n<td>In an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_reference_frame" title="Inertial reference frame">inertial reference frame</a>, an object either remains at rest or continues to move at a constant <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, unless acted upon by a <a href="/wiki/Net_force" title="Net force">net force</a>.\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><b>Second law</b>:\n</td>\n<td>In an inertial reference frame, the vector <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_sum" title="Vector sum">sum</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forces" title="Forces">forces</a> <b>F</b> on an object is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> <i>m</i> of that object multiplied by the <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> <b>a</b> of the object: <b>F</b> = <i>m</i><b>a</b>.\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><b>Third law</b>:\n</td>\n<td>When one body exerts a force on a second body, the second body simultaneously exerts a force equal in is used for describing the motion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> objects, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projectiles" title="Projectiles">projectiles</a> to parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinery" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_objects" title="Astronomical objects">astronomical objects</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>. It produces very accurate results within these domains, and is one of the oldest and largest in <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>.\n</p><p>Classical mechanics is fundamentally based on <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton\'s laws of motion">Newton\'s laws of motion</a>. These laws describe the relationship between the forces acting on a body and the motion of that body. They were first compiled by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Sir Isaac Newton</a> in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophi&#230; Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophi&#230; Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i>, first published on July 5, 1687. Newton\'s three laws are:\n</p>\n<ol><li>A <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">body</a> either is at rest or moves with constant velocity, until and unless an outer force is applied to it.</li>\n<li>An object will travel in one direction only until an outer force changes its direction.</li>\n<li>Whenever one body exerts a force <b>F</b> onto a second body, (in some cases, which is standing still) the second body exerts the force &#8722;<b>F</b> on the first body. <b>F</b> and &#8722;<b>F</b> are equal in magnitude and opposite in sense. So, the body which exerts <b>F</b> will go backwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>Newton\'s three laws of motion were the first to accurately provide a mathematical model for understanding <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbiting</a> bodies in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. This explanation unified the motion of celestial bodies and motion of objects on earth.\n</p><p>Classical mechanics was further enhanced by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>. Special relativity is concerned with the motion of objects with a high <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, approaching the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>; general relativity is employed to handle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitational</a> motion at a deeper level.\n</p><p><b>Uniform Motion:</b>\n</p>\n<table class="box-Missing_information plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w.svg"><img alt="Wiki letter w.svg" data-file-height="44" data-file-width="44" decoding="async" height="44" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/44px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/66px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/88px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png 2x" width="44"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section \'<i>is missing information about the information in the last sentence of this section <b>does not provide sufficient and complete information\'</b></i>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please expand the section to include this information. These descriptions include the simultaneous wave-like and particle-like behavior of both <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> energy as described in the <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave&#8211;particle duality">wave&#8211;particle duality</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In classical mechanics, accurate <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurements</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">predictions</a> of the state of objects can be calculated, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_location" title="Absolute location">location</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>. In the quantum mechanics, due to the <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">Heisenberg uncertainty principle</a>, the complete state of a subatomic particle, such as its location and velocity, cannot be simultaneously determined.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In addition to describing the motion of atomic level phenomena, quantum mechanics is useful in understanding some large-scale phenomenon such as <a href="/wiki/Superfluidity" title="Superfluidity">superfluidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological systems</a>, including the function of <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">smell receptors</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Protein_structure" title="Protein structure">structures of proteins</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="List_of_.22imperceptible.22_human_motions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'List_of_"imperceptible"_human_motions\'>List of "imperceptible" human motions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title=\'Edit section: List of "imperceptible" human motions\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Humans, like all known things in the universe, are in constant motion;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyson_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tyson-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8&#8211;9</span></sup> however, aside from obvious movements of the various external <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">body</a> parts and <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a>, humans are in motion in a variety of ways which are more difficult to <a href="/wiki/Motion_perception" title="Motion perception">perceive</a>.  Many of these "imperceptible motions" are only perceivable with the help of special tools and careful observation. The larger scales of imperceptible motions are difficult for humans to perceive for two reasons: <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton\'s laws of motion">Newton\'s laws of motion</a> (particularly the third) which prevents the feeling of motion on a mass to which the observer is connected, and the lack of an obvious <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a> which would allow individuals to easily see that they are moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The smaller scales of these motions are too small to be detected conventionally with human <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universe">Universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">Spacetime</a> (the fabric of the universe) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">expanding</a> meaning everything in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is stretching like a <a href="/wiki/Rubber_band" title="Rubber band">rubber band</a>. This motion is the most obscure as it is not physical motion as such, but rather a change in the very nature of the universe. The primary source of verification of this expansion was provided by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a> who demonstrated that all galaxies and distant astronomical objects were moving away from Earth, known as <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble\'s law">Hubble\'s law</a>, predicted by a universal expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxy">Galaxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Galaxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy" title="Milky Way Galaxy">Milky Way Galaxy</a> is moving through <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a> and many astronomers believe the velocity of this motion to be approximately 600 kilometres per second (1,340,000&#160;mph) relative to the observed locations of other nearby galaxies. Another reference frame is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background" title="Cosmic microwave background">Cosmic microwave background</a>. This frame of reference indicates that the Milky Way is moving at around 582 kilometres per second (1,300,000&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dipole_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dipole-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (August 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sun_and_solar_system">Sun and solar system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sun and solar system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Milky Way is <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotating</a> around its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">dense</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_center" title="Galactic center">galactic center</a>, thus the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> is moving in a circle within the <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>. Away from the central bulge, or outer rim, the typical stellar <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> is between 210 and 240 kilometres per second (470,000 and 540,000&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fn4_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fn4-7">[7]</a></sup> All planets and their moons move with the sun. Thus, the solar system is moving.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Earth is <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotating</a> or spinning around its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">axis</a>. This is evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a>, at the equator the earth has an eastward velocity of 0.4651 kilometres per second (1,040&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_goodard_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_goodard-8">[8]</a></sup> The Earth is also <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbiting</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_revolution" title="Orbital revolution">orbital revolution</a>. A complete orbit around the sun takes one <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>, or about 365 days; it averages a speed of about 30 kilometres per second (67,000&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earth_fact_sheet_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-earth_fact_sheet-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continents">Continents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Continents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_tectonic" title="Plate tectonic">Plate tectonics</a> tells us that the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> are drifting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_current" title="Convection current">convection currents</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> causing them to move across the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> at the slow speed of approximately 2.54 centimetres (1&#160;in) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the velocities of plates range widely. The fastest-moving plates are the oceanic plates, with the <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Plate" title="Cocos Plate">Cocos Plate</a> advancing at a rate of 75 millimetres (3.0&#160;in) per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> moving 52&#8211;69 millimetres (2.0&#8211;2.7&#160;in) per year. At the other extreme, the slowest-moving plate is the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a>, progressing at a typical rate of about 21 millimetres (0.83&#160;in) per year.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_body">Internal body</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Internal body">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The human <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> is constantly contracting to move <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> throughout the body. Through larger veins and arteries in the body, blood has been found to travel at approximately 0.33&#160;m/s. Though considerable variation exists, and peak flows in the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a> have been found between 0.1 and 0.45 metres per second (0.33 and 1.48&#160;ft/s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscles</a> of hollow internal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscera" title="Viscera">organs</a> are moving. The most familiar would be the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a> which is where digested <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> is forced throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_tract" title="Digestive tract">digestive tract</a>. Though different foods travel through the body at different rates, an average speed through the human <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> is 3.48 kilometres per hour (2.16&#160;mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The human <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic system</a> is also constantly causing movements of excess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluids" title="Fluids">fluids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a>, and immune system related products around the body. The lymph fluid has been found to move through a lymph capillary of the <a href="/wiki/Human_skin" title="Human skin">skin</a> at approximately 0.0000097&#160;m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cells">Cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> have many structures which move throughout them. <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasmic_streaming" title="Cytoplasmic streaming">Cytoplasmic streaming</a> is a way which cells move molecular substances throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_proteins" title="Motor proteins">motor proteins</a> work as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_motors" title="Molecular motors">molecular motors</a> within a cell and move along the surface of various cellular substrates such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microtubules" title="Microtubules">microtubules</a>, and motor proteins are typically powered by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a approximately 0.00000152&#160;m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particles">Particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">laws of thermodynamics</a>, all <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">particles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> are in constant random motion as long as the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> is above <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>. Thus the <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> which make up the human body are vibrating, colliding, and moving. This motion can be detected as temperature; higher temperatures, which represent greater <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> in the particles, feel warm to humans who sense the thermal energy transferring from the object being touched to their nerves. Similarly, when lower temperature objects are touched, the senses perceive the transfer of heat away from the body as feeling cold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subatomic_particles">Subatomic particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Subatomic particles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Within each atom, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> exist in an area around the nucleus.  This area is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_cloud" title="Electron cloud">electron cloud</a>.  According to <a href="/wiki/Bohr_model" title="Bohr model">Bohr\'s model</a> of the atom, electrons have a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_velocity" title="Electron velocity">velocity</a>, and the larger the nucleus they are orbiting the faster they would need to move.  If electrons \'move\' about the electron cloud in strict paths the same way planets orbit the sun, then electrons would be required to do so at speeds which far exceed the speed of light.  However, there is no reason that one must confine one\'s self to this strict conceptualization, that electrons move in paths the same way macroscopic objects do.  Rather one can conceptualize electrons to be \'particles\' that capriciously exist within the bounds of the electron cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Inside the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> are also probably moving around due to the electrical repulsion of the protons and the presence of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> of both particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light">Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">Speed of light</a></div>\n<p>Light propagates at 299,792,458&#160;m/s, often approximated as 299,792 kilometres per second (186,282&#160;mi/s) in a vacuum. The speed of light (or <i>c</i>) is also the speed of all <a href="/wiki/Massless_particle" title="Massless particle">massless particles</a> and associated <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">fields</a> in a vacuum, and it is the upper limit on the speed at which energy, matter, <a href="/wiki/Physical_information" title="Physical information">information</a> or <a href="/wiki/Causality_(physics)" title="Causality (physics)">causation</a> can travel; the speed of light is the limit of speed for all physical systems.\n</p><p>In addition, the speed of light is an invariant quantity: it has the same value, irrespective of the position or speed of the observer.
