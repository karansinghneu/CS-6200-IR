The <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a> may be an illustration of touch or an allusion to the opposite (the tortoise isolating in its shell).</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>sense</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiological" title="Physiological">physiological</a> capacity of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> that provides data for <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a>. The senses and their operation, classification, and theory are overlapping topics studied by a variety of fields, most notably <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_science" title="Cognitive science">cognitive science</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_perception" title="Philosophy of perception">philosophy of perception</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> has a specific <a href="/wiki/Sensory_nervous_system" title="Sensory nervous system">sensory nervous system</a>, and a <b>sense organ</b>, or <b>sensor</b>, dedicated to each sense.\n</p><p>Humans have a multitude of sensors. Sight (<a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a>), hearing (<a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">audition</a>), taste (<a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">gustation</a>), smell (<a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">olfaction</a>), and touch (<a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensation</a>) are the five traditionally recognized senses. The ability to detect other stimuli beyond those governed by these most broadly recognized senses also exists, and these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_modalities" title="Sensory modalities">sensory modalities</a> include temperature (<a href="/wiki/Thermoception" title="Thermoception">thermoception</a>), kinesthetic sense (<a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">proprioception</a>), pain (<a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">nociception</a>), balance (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">equilibrioception</a>), vibration (<a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">mechanoreception</a>), and various internal stimuli (e.g. the different <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor" title="Chemoreceptor">chemoreceptors</a> for detecting <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> concentrations in the blood, or sense of <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hunger</a> and sense of <a href="/wiki/Thirst" title="Thirst">thirst</a>). However, what constitutes a sense is a matter of some debate, leading to difficulties in defining what exactly a distinct sense is, and where the borders lie between responses to related stimuli.\n</p><p>Other animals also have receptors to sense the world around them, with degrees of capability varying greatly between species. Humans have a comparatively weak sense of smell and a stronger sense of sight relative to many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a> while some animals may lack one or more of the traditional five senses. Some animals may also intake and interpret sensory <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">stimuli</a> in very different ways. Some species of animals are able to sense the world in a way that humans cannot, with some species able to sense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_field" title="Electrical field">electrical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic fields</a>, and detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pressure" title="Water pressure">water pressure</a> and currents.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peacock_served_in_full_plumage_(detail_of_BRUEGHEL_Taste,_Hearing_and_Touch).jpg" 1618</div></div></div>\n<p>A broadly acceptable definition of a sense would be "A system that consists of a group of sensory cell types that responds to a specific physical phenomenon, and that corresponds to a particular group of regions within the brain where the signals are received and interpreted." There is no firm agreement as to the number of senses because of differing definitions of what constitutes a sense.\n</p><p>The senses are frequently divided into exteroceptive and interoceptive:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Exteroceptive senses</i> are senses that perceive the body\'s own position, motion, and state, known as <i>proprioceptive senses</i>. External senses include the traditional five: sight, hearing, touch, smell and taste, as well as <a href="/wiki/Thermoception" title="Thermoception">thermoception</a> (temperature differences) and possibly an additional weak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetoception" title="Magnetoception">magnetoception</a> (direction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voustianiouk_2000_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voustianiouk_2000-1">[1]</a></sup> Proprioceptive senses include <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">nociception</a> (pain); <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">equilibrioception</a> (balance); <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">proprioception</a> (a sense of the position and movement of the parts of one\'s own body).</li>\n<li><i>Interoceptive senses</i> are senses that perceive <a href="#Other_internal_senses">sensations in internal organs</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Non-human animals may possess senses that are absent in humans, such as <a href="/wiki/Electroreception" title="Electroreception">electroreception</a> and detection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarized_light" title="Polarized light">polarized light</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_philosophy" title="Buddhist philosophy">Buddhist philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayatana" title="Ayatana">Ayatana</a> or "sense-base" includes the mind as a sense organ, in addition to the traditional five. This addition to the commonly acknowledged senses may arise from the psychological orientation involved in Buddhist thought and practice. The mind considered by itself is seen as the principal gateway to a different spectrum of phenomena that differ from the physical sense data. This way of viewing the human sense system indicates the importance of internal sources of sensation and perception that complements our experience of the external world.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Five_.22traditional.22_senses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=\'Five_"traditional"_senses\'>Five "traditional" senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title=\'Edit section: Five "traditional" senses\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href=\'/wiki/Five_wits#The_"outward"_wits\' title="Five wits">Five wits &#167;&#160;The "outward" wits</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sight">Sight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1271" data-file-width="1799" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg/220px-Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg/330px-Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg/440px-Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pietro_Paolini_-_Allegory_of_the_Five_Senses_-_Walters_372768.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In this painting by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Paolini" title="Pietro Paolini">Pietro Paolini</a>, each individual represents one of the five senses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">Sight</a></b> or <b>vision</b> (adjectival form: visual/optical) is the capability of the eye(s) to focus and detect images of visible <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> on <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">photoreceptors</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> of each eye that generates electrical <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">nerve impulses</a> for varying colors, hues, and brightness.  There are two types of photoreceptors: <a href="/wiki/Rod_cell" title="Rod cell">rods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cones</a>. Rods are very sensitive to light but do not distinguish colors.  Cones distinguish colors but are less sensitive to dim light. There is some disagreement as to whether this constitutes one, two or three senses. Neuroanatomists generally regard it as two senses, given that different receptors are responsible for the perception of color and brightness. Some argue<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that <a href="/wiki/Stereopsis" title="Stereopsis">stereopsis</a>, the perception of depth using both eyes, also constitutes a sense, but it is generally regarded as a cognitive (that is, post-sensory) function of the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> of the brain where patterns and objects in <a href="/wiki/Real_image" title="Real image">images</a> are recognized and interpreted based on previously learned information. This is called visual memory.\n</p><p>The inability to see is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a>. Blindness may result from damage to the eyeball, especially to the retina, damage to the optic nerve that connects each eye to the brain, and/or from <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infarct" title="Infarct">infarcts</a> in the brain).  Temporary or permanent blindness can be caused by poisons or medications.\n</p><p>People who are blind from degradation or damage to the visual cortex, but still have functional eyes, are actually capable of some level of vision and reaction to visual stimuli but not a conscious perception; this is known as <a href="/wiki/Blindsight" title="Blindsight">blindsight</a>. People with blindsight are usually not aware that they are reacting to visual sources, and instead just unconsciously adapt their behavior to the stimulus.\n</p><p>On February 14, 2013 researchers developed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_implant" title="Neural implant">neural implant</a> that gives <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> the ability to sense <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light which for the first time provides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_creatures" title="Living creatures">living creatures</a> with new abilities, instead of simply replacing or augmenting existing abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hearing">Hearing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hearing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">Hearing</a></b> or <b>audition</b> (adjectival form: auditory) is the sense of <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> perception. Hearing is all about vibration. Mechanoreceptors turn motion into electrical nerve pulses, which are located in the inner ear. Since sound is vibration, propagating through a medium such as air, the detection of these vibrations, that is the sense of the hearing, is a mechanical sense because these vibrations are mechanically conducted from the eardrum through a series of tiny bones to hair-like fibers in the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a>, which detect mechanical motion of the fibers within a range of about 20 to 20,000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hertz" title="Hertz">hertz</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with substantial variation between individuals. Hearing at high frequencies declines with an increase in age. Inability to hear is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafness" title="Deafness">deafness</a> or hearing impairment. Sound can also be detected as <a href="/wiki/Seismic_communication" title="Seismic communication">vibrations conducted through the body</a> by tactition. Lower frequencies that can be heard are detected this way. Some deaf people are able to determine the direction and location of vibrations picked up through the feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taste">Taste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">Taste</a></b> or <b>gustation</b> (adjectival form: gustatory) is one of the traditional five senses. It refers to the capability to detect the taste of substances such as food, certain minerals, and poisons, etc. The sense of taste is often confused with the "sense" of flavor, which is a combination of taste and smell perception. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4711" data-file-width="5459" decoding="async" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Philippe_Mercier_-_The_Sense_of_Taste_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Philippe Mercier - The Sense of Taste - Google Art Project</div></div></div><p> Flavor depends on odor, texture, and temperature as well as on taste. Humans receive tastes through sensory organs called taste buds, or gustatory calyculi, concentrated on the upper surface of the tongue. There are five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_taste" title="Basic taste">basic tastes</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweet" title="Sweet">sweet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_(taste)#Bitterness" title="Bitter (taste)">bitter</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sour" title="Sour">sour</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltiness" title="Saltiness">salty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umami" title="Umami">umami</a>. Other tastes such as calcium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tordoff_2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tordoff_2008-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sciencedaily_2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sciencedaily_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_fatty_acids" title="Free fatty acids">free fatty acids</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattes_2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mattes_2009-8">[8]</a></sup> may also be basic tastes but have yet to receive widespread acceptance. The inability to taste is called <a href="/wiki/Ageusia" title="Ageusia">ageusia</a>.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smell">Smell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Smell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">Smell</a></b> or <b>olfaction</b> (adjectival form: olfactory) is the other "chemical" sense. Unlike taste, there are hundreds of olfactory receptors (388 according to one source<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moonwalk_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moonwalk-9">[9]</a></sup>), each binding to a particular molecular feature. Odor molecules possess a variety of features and, thus, excite specific receptors more or less strongly. This combination of excitatory signals from different receptors makes up what we perceive as the molecule\'s smell. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5070" data-file-width="3900" decoding="async" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg/220px-The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg/330px-The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg/440px-The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_sense_of_smell_Philippe_Mercier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sense of smell <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Mercier" title="Philippe Mercier">Philippe Mercier</a></div></div></div><p> In the brain, olfaction is processed by the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a>. <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor_neuron" title="Olfactory receptor neuron">Olfactory receptor neurons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Human_nose" title="Human nose">nose</a> differ from most other neurons in that they die and regenerate on a regular basis. The inability to smell is called <a href="/wiki/Anosmia" title="Anosmia">anosmia</a>. Some neurons in the nose are specialized to detect <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiveScience_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Touch">Touch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Touch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">Touch</a></b> or <b>somatosensation</b> (adjectival form: somatic), also called <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tactition" title="Tactition">tactition</a></b>  (adjectival form: tactile) or <b><a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">mechanoreception</a></b>, is a perception resulting from activation of neural <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">receptors</a>, generally in the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> including <a href="/wiki/Hair_follicle" title="Hair follicle">hair follicles</a>, but also in the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throat" title="Throat">throat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucosa</a>. A variety of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> receptors respond to variations in pressure (firm, brushing, sustained, etc.). The touch sense of <a href="/wiki/Itch" title="Itch">itching</a> caused by insect bites or allergies involves special itch-specific neurons in the skin and spinal cord.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sun_Zhao_2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sun_Zhao_2009-11">[11]</a></sup> The loss or impairment of the ability to feel anything touched is called tactile <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthesia</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Paresthesia" title="Paresthesia">Paresthesia</a> is a sensation of tingling, pricking, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numbness" title="Numbness">numbness</a> of the skin that may result from nerve damage and may be permanent or temporary.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_senses">Other senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Balance_and_acceleration">Balance and acceleration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Balance and acceleration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_system" title="Vestibular system">Vestibular system</a></div>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Balance_(ability)" title="Balance (ability)">Balance</a></b>, <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrioception" title="Equilibrioception">equilibrioception</a></b>, or <b>vestibular sense</b> is the sense that allows an organism to sense body movement, direction, and acceleration, and to attain and maintain postural equilibrium and balance. The organ of equilibrioception is the vestibular labyrinthine system found in both of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ears</a>. In technical terms, this organ is responsible for two senses of <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> acceleration and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_acceleration" title="Linear acceleration">linear acceleration</a> (which also senses <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>), but they are known together as equilibrioception.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Vestibular_nerve" title="Vestibular nerve">vestibular nerve</a> conducts information from sensory receptors in three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osseous_ampullae" title="Osseous ampullae">ampulla</a> that sense motion of fluid in three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semicircular_canal" title="Semicircular canal">semicircular canals</a> caused by three-dimensional rotation of the head.  The vestibular nerve also conducts information from the <a href="/wiki/Utricle_(ear)" title="Utricle (ear)">utricle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Saccule" title="Saccule">saccule</a>, which contain hair-like sensory receptors that bend under the weight of <a href="/wiki/Otolith" title="Otolith">otoliths</a> (which are small crystals of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>) that provide the inertia needed to detect head rotation, linear acceleration, and the direction of gravitational force.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Thermoception" title="Thermoception">Thermoception</a></b> is the sense of <b>heat</b> and the absence of heat (<b>cold</b>) by the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> and internal skin passages, or, rather, the <b><a href="/wiki/Heat_flux" title="Heat flux">heat flux</a></b> (the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_flow" title="Heat flow">heat flow</a>) in these areas. There are specialized receptors for cold (declining temperature) and for heat (increasing temperature). The cold receptors play an important part in the animal\'s sense of smell, telling wind direction.  The heat receptors are sensitive to infrared radiation and can occur in specialized organs, for instance in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pit_vipers" title="Pit vipers">pit vipers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Thermoreceptor" title="Thermoreceptor">thermoceptors</a> in the skin are quite different from the <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostatic</a> thermoceptors in the brain (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POAH" title="POAH">hypothalamus</a>), which provide feedback on internal body temperature.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proprioception">Proprioception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Proprioception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">Proprioception</a></b>, the <b>kinesthetic sense</b>, provides the <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal cortex</a> of the brain with information on the movement and relative positions of the parts of the body. Neurologists test this sense by telling patients to close their eyes and touch their own nose with the tip of a finger. Assuming proper proprioceptive function, at no time will the person lose awareness of where the hand actually is, even though it is not being detected by any of the other senses. Proprioception and touch are related in subtle ways, and their impairment results in surprising and deep deficits in perception and action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roblesdelatorre_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-roblesdelatorre-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pain">Pain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">Nociception</a></b> (physiological <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>) signals nerve-damage or damage to tissue. The three types of pain receptors are cutaneous (skin), somatic (joints and bones), and visceral (body organs). It was previously believed that pain was simply the overloading of pressure receptors, but research in the first half of the 20th century indicated that pain is a distinct phenomenon that intertwines with all of the other senses, including touch. Pain was once considered an entirely subjective experience, but recent studies show that pain is registered in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_gyrus" title="Anterior cingulate gyrus">anterior cingulate gyrus</a> of the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulbright_2001_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fulbright_2001-13">[13]</a></sup> The main function of pain is to attract our <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a> to dangers and motivate us to avoid them. For example, humans avoid touching a sharp needle, or hot object, or extending an arm beyond a safe limit because it is dangerous, and thus hurts. Without pain, people could do many dangerous things without being aware of the dangers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_stimulation">Sexual stimulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sexual stimulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_stimulation" title="Sexual stimulation">Sexual stimulation</a></div>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Sexual_stimulation" title="Sexual stimulation">Sexual stimulation</a></b> is any <a href="/wiki/Stimulation" title="Stimulation">stimulus</a> (including bodily contact) that leads to, enhances and maintains <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">sexual arousal</a>, and may lead to <a href="/wiki/Orgasm" title="Orgasm">orgasm</a>. Distinct from the general sense of touch, sexual stimulation is strongly tied to hormonal activity and chemical triggers in the body. Although sexual arousal may arise without <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_stimulation" title="Physical stimulation">physical stimulation</a>, achieving orgasm usually requires physical sexual stimulation (stimulation of the Krause-Finger <a href="/wiki/Bulboid_corpuscle" title="Bulboid corpuscle">corpuscles</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> found in erogenous zones of the body).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_internal_senses">Other internal senses<span id="Interoception"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other internal senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An <b>internal sense</b> also known as <a href="/wiki/Interoception" title="Interoception">interoception</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Craig_2003_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Craig_2003-15">[15]</a></sup> is "any sense that is normally stimulated from within the body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunn_Galton_2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dunn_Galton_2010-16">[16]</a></sup>  These involve numerous sensory receptors in internal organs, such as stretch receptors that are neurologically linked to the brain. Interoception is thought to be atypical in clinical conditions such as alexithymia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> \nSome examples of specific receptors class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Opsin" title="Opsin">Opsins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Direct_DNA_damage" title="Direct DNA damage">direct DNA damage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melanocyte" title="Melanocyte">melanocytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keratinocyte" title="Keratinocyte">keratinocytes</a> can sense <a class="mw-headline" id="Perception_not_based_on_a_specific_sensory_organ">Perception not based on a specific sensory organ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Perception not based on a specific sensory organ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time">Time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Time_perception" title="Time perception">time perception</a></div>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Time_perception" title="Time perception">Chronoception</a></b> refers to how the passage of time is perceived and experienced. Although the <a href="/wiki/Time_perception" title="Time perception">sense of time</a> is not associated with a specific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_system" title="Sensory system">sensory system</a>, the work of <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientists</a> indicates that human brains do have a system governing the <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> of <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao_2001_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rao_2001-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> composed of a highly distributed system involving the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basal_ganglia" title="Basal ganglia">basal ganglia</a>. One particular component, the <a href="/wiki/Suprachiasmatic_nucleus" title="Suprachiasmatic nucleus">suprachiasmatic nucleus</a>, is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian (or daily) rhythm</a>, while other cell clusters appear to be capable of shorter-range (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultradian" title="Ultradian">ultradian</a>) timekeeping.\n</p><p>One or more <a href="/wiki/Dopaminergic_pathways" title="Dopaminergic pathways">dopaminergic pathways</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> appear to have a strong modulatory influence on <a href="/wiki/Mental_chronometry" title="Mental chronometry">mental chronometry</a>, particularly interval timing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amph-DA_reaction_time_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amph-DA_reaction_time-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agency">Agency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Agency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sense_of_agency" title="Sense of agency">Sense of agency</a></div>\n<p>The sense of agency refers to the subjective feeling of having chosen a particular action. Some conditions, such as schizophrenia, can lead to a loss of this sense, causing a person to feel like a machine or even leading to delusions of being controlled from some outside source. The opposite extreme occurs too, with some people experiencing everything in their environment as if they had decided that it would happen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even in non-pathological cases, there is a measurable difference between making a decision and the feeling of agency. Through methods such as <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience_of_free_will#Libet_experiment" title="Neuroscience of free will">the Libet experiment</a>, a gap of half a second or more can be detected from the time when there are detectable neurological signs of a decision having been made to the time when the subject actually becomes conscious of the decision.\n</p><p>There are also experiments in which an illusion of agency is induced in psychologically normal subjects. In Wegner and Wheatley 1999, subjects were given instructions to move a mouse around a scene and point to an image about once every thirty seconds. However, a second person&#8212;acting as a test subject but actually a confederate&#8212;had their hand on the mouse at the same time, and controlled some of the movement. Experimenters were able to arrange for subjects to perceive certain "forced stops" as if they were their own choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Familiarity">Familiarity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Familiarity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Recognition_memory" title="Recognition memory">Recognition memory</a> is sometimes divided into two functions by neuroscientists: familiarity and recollection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A strong sense of familiarity can occur without any recollection, for example in cases of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deja_vu" title="Deja vu">deja vu</a>. The temporal lobe, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Perirhinal_cortex" title="Perirhinal cortex">perirhinal cortex</a>, responds differently to stimuli which feel novel than to things which feel familiar. Firing rates in the perirhinal cortex are connected with the sense of familiarity in humans and other mammals. In tests, stimulating this area at 10&#8211;15&#160;Hz caused animals to treat even novel images as familiar, and stimulation at 30&#8211;40&#160;Hz caused novel images to be partially treated as familiar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Specifically, stimulation at 30&#8211;40&#160;Hz led to animals looking at a familiar image for longer periods, as they would for an unfamiliar one; but it did not lead to the same exploration behavior normally associated with novelty. Recent studies on lesions in the area concluded that rats with a damaged perirhinal cortex were still more interested in exploring when novel objects were present, but seemed unable to tell novel objects from familiar ones&#8212;they examined both equally. Thus, other brain regions are involved with noticing unfamiliarity, but the perirhinal cortex is needed to associate the feeling with a specific source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_senses">Non-human senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Non-human senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analogous_to_human_senses">Analogous to human senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Analogous to human senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Other living organisms have receptors to sense the world around them, including many of the senses listed above for humans. However, the mechanisms and capabilities vary widely.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Smell_2">Smell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Smell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Most non-human mammals have a much keener sense of smell than humans, although the mechanism is similar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Any non-anectodal sources? (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An example of smell in non-mammals is that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharks" title="Sharks">sharks</a>, which combine their keen sense of smell with timing to determine the direction of a smell. They follow the nostril that first detected the smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardiner_Atema_2010_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner_Atema_2010-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">Insects</a> have olfactory receptors on their <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vomeronasal_organ">Vomeronasal organ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Vomeronasal organ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Many animals (<a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>) have a <a href="/wiki/Vomeronasal_organ" title="Vomeronasal organ">vomeronasal organ</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Takami_2002_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Takami_2002-34">[34]</a></sup> that is connected with the mouth cavity. In mammals it is mainly used to detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pheromones" title="Pheromones">pheromones</a> of marked territory, trails, and sexual state. Reptiles like <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monitor_lizard" title="Monitor lizard">monitor lizards</a> make extensive use of it as a smelling organ by transferring scent molecules to the vomeronasal organ with the tips of the forked tongue. In reptiles the vomeronasal organ is commonly referred to as Jacobsons organ. In mammals, it is often associated with a special behavior called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flehmen" title="Flehmen">flehmen</a> characterized by uplifting of the lips. The organ is <a href="/wiki/Human_vestigiality" title="Human vestigiality">vestigial in humans</a>, because associated neurons have not been found that give any sensory input in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frasnelli_2011_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frasnelli_2011-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Taste_2">Taste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Taste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flies" title="Flies">Flies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butterflies" title="Butterflies">butterflies</a> have taste organs on their feet, allowing them to taste anything they land on. <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">Catfish</a> have taste organs across their entire bodies, and can taste anything they touch, including chemicals in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision_and_light_sensing">Vision and light sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Vision and light sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">Cats</a> have the ability to see in low light, which is due to muscles surrounding their <a href="/wiki/Iris_(anatomy)" title="Iris (anatomy)">irides</a>&#8211;which contract and expand their pupils&#8211;as well as to the <a href="/wiki/Tapetum_lucidum" title="Tapetum lucidum">tapetum lucidum</a>, a reflective membrane that optimizes the image.\n<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crotalinae" title="Crotalinae">Pit vipers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pythonidae" title="Pythonidae">pythons</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Boidae" title="Boidae">boas</a> have organs that allow them to detect <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> light, such that these snakes are able to sense the body heat of their prey. The <a href="/wiki/Common_vampire_bat" title="Common vampire bat">common vampire bat</a> may also have an infrared sensor on its nose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It has been found that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a> and some other animals are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrachromats" title="Tetrachromats">tetrachromats</a> and have the ability to see in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> down to 300 nanometers. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bees" title="Bees">Bees</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragonflies" title="Dragonflies">dragonflies</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_Kleef_Berry_2008_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_Kleef_Berry_2008-38">[38]</a></sup> are also able to see in the ultraviolet. <a href="/wiki/Mantis_shrimp" title="Mantis shrimp">Mantis shrimps</a> can perceive both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarized_light" title="Polarized light">polarized light</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multispectral" title="Multispectral">multispectral</a> images and have twelve distinct kinds of color receptors, unlike humans which have three kinds and most mammals which have two kinds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">Cephalopods</a> have the ability to change color using <a href="/wiki/Chromatophore" title="Chromatophore">chromatophores</a> in their skin. Researchers believe that <a href="/wiki/Opsin" title="Opsin">opsins</a> in the skin can sense different wavelengths of light and help the creatures choose a coloration that camouflages them, in addition to light input from the eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  Other researchers hypothesize that <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod_eye" title="Cephalopod eye">cephalopod eyes</a> in species which only have a single <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_protein" title="Photoreceptor protein">photoreceptor protein</a> may use <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_aberration" title="Chromatic aberration">chromatic aberration</a> to turn monochromatic vision into color vision,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> explaining pupils shaped like the letter U, the letter W, or a <a href="/wiki/Dumbbell" title="Dumbbell">dumbbell</a>, as well as explaining the need for colorful mating displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  Some cephalopods can distinguish the polarization of light.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Balance">Balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Many invertebrates have a <a href="/wiki/Statocyst" title="Statocyst">statocyst</a>, which is a sensor for acceleration and orientation that works very differently from the mammalian\'s semi-circular canals.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensing_gravity">Sensing gravity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Sensing gravity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Some plants (such as mustard) have genes that are necessary for the plant to sense the direction of gravity.  If these genes are disabled by a mutation, a plant cannot grow upright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Not_analogous_to_human_senses">Not analogous to human senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Not analogous to human senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In addition, some animals have senses that humans do not, including the following:\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Echolocation">Echolocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Echolocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Animal_echolocation" title="Animal echolocation">Animal echolocation</a></div>\n<p>Certain animals, including <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">cetaceans</a>, have the ability to determine orientation to other objects through interpretation of reflected sound (like <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a>). They most often use this to navigate through poor lighting conditions or to identify and track prey. There is currently an uncertainty whether this is simply an extremely developed post-sensory interpretation of auditory perceptions or it actually constitutes a separate sense. Resolution of the issue will require brain scans of animals while they actually perform echolocation, a task that has proven difficult in practice.\n</p><p>Blind people report they are able to navigate and in some cases identify an object by interpreting reflected sounds (especially their own footsteps), a phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/Human_echolocation" title="Human echolocation">human echolocation</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electroreception">Electroreception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Electroreception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Electroreception" title="Electroreception">Electroreception</a></b> (or <b>electroception</b>) is the ability to detect <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric fields</a>. Several species of fish, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, and rays have the capacity to sense changes in electric fields in their immediate vicinity. For cartilaginous fish this occurs through a specialized organ called the <a href="/wiki/Ampullae_of_Lorenzini" title="Ampullae of Lorenzini">Ampullae of Lorenzini</a>. Some fish passively sense changing nearby electric fields; some generate their own weak electric fields, and sense the pattern of field potentials over their body surface; and some use these electric field generating and sensing capacities for social <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a>. The mechanisms by which electroceptive fish construct a spatial representation from very small differences in field potentials involve comparisons of spike latencies from different parts of the fish\'s body.\n</p><p>The only orders of mammals that are known to demonstrate electroception are the <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monotreme" title="Monotreme">monotreme</a> orders. Among these mammals, the <a href="/wiki/Platypus" title="Platypus">platypus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Electroreceptive_Mechanisms_in_the_Platypus_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Electroreceptive_Mechanisms_in_the_Platypus-44">[44]</a></sup> has the most acute sense of electroception.\n</p><p>A dolphin can detect electric fields in water using electroreceptors in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whisker" title="Whisker">vibrissal crypts</a> arrayed in pairs on its snout and which evolved from whisker motion sensors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drake_2011_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drake_2011-45">[45]</a></sup> These electroreceptors can detect electric fields as weak as 4.6 microvolts per centimeter, such as those generated by contracting muscles and pumping gills of potential prey. This permits the dolphin to locate prey from the seafloor where sediment limits visibility and echolocation.\n</p><p>Spiders have been shown to detect electric fields to determine a suitable time to extend web for \'ballooning\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Body_modification" title="Body modification">Body modification</a> enthusiasts have experimented with magnetic implants to attempt to replicate this sense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> However, in general humans (and it is presumed other mammals) can detect electric fields only indirectly by detecting the effect they have on hairs. An electrically charged balloon, for instance, will exert a force on human arm hairs, which can be felt through tactition and identified as coming from a static charge (and not from wind or the like). This is not electroreception, as it is a post-sensory cognitive action.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetoception">Magnetoception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Magnetoception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetoception" title="Magnetoception">Magnetoception</a></b> (or <b>magnetoreception</b>) is the ability to detect the direction one is facing based on the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>. Directional awareness is most commonly observed in <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, which rely on their magnetic sense to navigate during migration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theoretical_and_Computational_Biophysics_Group_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theoretical_and_Computational_Biophysics_Group-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theoretical_and_Computational_Biophysics_Group_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Theoretical_and_Computational_Biophysics_Group-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baylor_College_of_Medicine_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baylor_College_of_Medicine-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wu_Dickman_2012_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wu_Dickman_2012-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cressey2012_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cressey2012-51">[51]</a></sup> It has also been observed in insects such as <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a>. Cattle make use of magnetoception to align themselves in a north-south direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_NEWS_-_Science/Nature_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_NEWS_-_Science/Nature-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Magnetotactic_bacteria" title="Magnetotactic bacteria">Magnetotactic bacteria</a> build miniature magnets inside themselves and use them to determine their orientation relative to the Earth\'s magnetic field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blakemore_1975_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blakemore_1975-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urban_2000_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Urban_2000-54">[54]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hygroreception">Hygroreception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Hygroreception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Hygroreception" title="Hygroreception">Hygroreception</a></b> is the ability to detect changes in the moisture content of the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_sensing">Infrared sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Infrared sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infrared_sensing_in_snakes" title="Infrared sensing in snakes">Infrared sensing in snakes</a></div>\n<p>The ability to sense <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> thermal radiation evolved independently in various families of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snakes" title="Snakes">snakes</a>. Essentially, it allows these reptiles to "see" radiant heat at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavelengths" title="Wavelengths">wavelengths</a> between 5 and 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="&#924;m">&#956;m</a> to a degree of accuracy such that a blind <a href="/wiki/Rattlesnake" title="Rattlesnake">rattlesnake</a> can target vulnerable body parts of the prey at which it strikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> It was previously thought that the organs evolved primarily as prey detectors, but it is now believed that it may also be used in thermoregulatory decision making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The facial pit underwent <a href="/wiki/Parallel_evolution" title="Parallel evolution">parallel evolution</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitvipers" title="Pitvipers">pitvipers</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Boidae" title="Boidae">boas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pythons" title="Pythons">pythons</a>, having evolved once in pitvipers and multiple times in boas and pythons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Electrophysiology" title="Electrophysiology">electrophysiology</a> of the structure is similar between the two lineages, but they differ in gross structural <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>. Most superficially, pitvipers possess one large pit organ on either side of the head, between the eye and the nostril (<a href="/wiki/Loreal_pit" title="Loreal pit">Loreal pit</a>), while boas and pythons have three or more comparatively smaller pits lining the upper and sometimes the lower lip, in or between the scales. Those of the pitvipers are the more advanced, having a suspended sensory membrane as opposed to a simple pit structure. Within the family <a href="/wiki/Viperidae" title="Viperidae">Viperidae</a>, the pit organ is seen only in the subfamily Crotalinae: the pitvipers. The organ is used extensively to detect and target <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> prey such as rodents and birds, and it was previously assumed that the organ evolved specifically for that purpose. However, recent evidence shows that the pit organ may also be used for thermoregulation. According to Krochmal et al., pitvipers can use their pits for thermoregulatory decision making while true vipers (vipers who do not contain heat-sensing pits) cannot.\n</p><p>In spite of its detection of IR light, the pits\' IR detection mechanism is not similar to photoreceptors &#8211; while photoreceptors detect light via photochemical reactions, the protein in the pits of snakes is in fact a temperature sensitive ion channel. It senses infrared signals through a mechanism involving warming of the pit organ, rather than chemical reaction to light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gracheva_et_al._2010_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gracheva_et_al._2010-60">[60]</a></sup> This is consistent with the thin pit membrane, which allows incoming IR radiation to quickly and precisely warm a given ion channel and trigger a nerve impulse, as well as vascularize the pit membrane in order to rapidly cool the ion channel back to its original "resting" or "inactive" temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gracheva_et_al._2010_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gracheva_et_al._2010-60">[60]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li><b>Pressure detection</b> uses the organ of Weber, a system consisting of three appendages of vertebrae transferring changes in shape of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_bladder" title="Gas bladder">gas bladder</a> to the middle ear. It can be used to regulate the buoyancy of the fish. Fish like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fish" title="Weather fish">weather fish</a> and other loaches are also known to respond to low pressure areas but they lack a swim bladder.</li>\n<li><b>Current detection</b> is a detection system of water currents, consisting mostly of <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a>, found in the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> of fish and aquatic forms of amphibians. The lateral line is also sensitive to low-frequency vibrations. The mechanoreceptors are <a href="/wiki/Hair_cell" title="Hair cell">hair cells</a>, the same mechanoreceptors for vestibular sense and hearing. It is used primarily for navigation, hunting, and schooling. The receptors of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electroception" title="Electroception">electrical sense</a> are modified hair cells of the lateral line system.</li>\n<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarized_light" title="Polarized light">Polarized light</a> direction/detection</b> is used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bees" title="Bees">bees</a> to orient themselves, especially on cloudy days. <a href="/wiki/Cuttlefish" title="Cuttlefish">Cuttlefish</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beetles" title="Beetles">beetles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mantis_shrimp" title="Mantis shrimp">mantis shrimp</a> can also perceive the polarization of light.  Most sighted humans can in fact learn to roughly detect large areas of polarization by an effect called <a href="/wiki/Haidinger%27s_brush" title="Haidinger\'s brush">Haidinger\'s brush</a>, however this is considered an <a href="/wiki/Entoptic_phenomenon" title="Entoptic phenomenon">entoptic phenomenon</a> rather than a separate sense.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Slit_sensilla" title="Slit sensilla">Slit sensillae</a> of spiders</b> detect mechanical strain in the exoskeleton, providing information on force and vibrations.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_senses">Plant senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Plant senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By using a variety of sense receptors, plants sense light, gravity, temperature, humidity, chemical substances, chemical gradients, reorientation, magnetic fields, infections, tissue damage and mechanical pressure. The absence of a nervous system notwithstanding, plants interpret and respond to these stimuli by a variety of hormonal and cell-to-cell communication pathways that result in movement, morphological changes and physiological state alterations at the organism level, that is, result in plant behavior. Such physiological and cognitive functions are generally not believed to give rise to mental phenomena or qualia, however, as these are typically considered the product of nervous system activity. The emergence of mental phenomena from the activity of systems  functionally or computationally analogous to that of nervous systems is, however, a hypothetical possibility explored by some schools of thought in the philosophy of mind field, such as <a href="/wiki/Functionalism_(philosophy_of_mind)" title="Functionalism (philosophy of mind)">functionalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computational_theory_of_mind" title="Computational theory of mind">computationalism</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
