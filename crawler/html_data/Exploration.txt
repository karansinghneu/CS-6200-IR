Exploration occurs in all <a href="/wiki/Sessility_(motility)" title="Sessility (motility)">non-sessile</a> animal species, including humans. In human <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, its most dramatic rise was during the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a> when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_peoples" title="European peoples">European</a> explorers sailed and charted much of the rest of the world for a variety of reasons. Since then, <a href="/wiki/Major_explorations_after_the_Age_of_Discovery" title="Major explorations after the Age of Discovery">major explorations after the Age of Discovery</a> have occurred for reasons mostly aimed at information discovery.\n</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_research" title="Scientific research">scientific research</a>, exploration is one of three purposes of empirical research (the other two being description and <a href="/wiki/Explanation" title="Explanation">explanation</a>). The term is often used metaphorically. href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a> though many of their routes are still unknown today. The presence of tin in some Phoenician artifacts suggests that they may have traveled to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>. According to Virgil\'s <a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a> and other ancient sources, the legendary Queen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dido_(Queen_of_Carthage)" title="Dido (Queen from <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a> (380 &#8211; <i>c.</i> 310 BC) was the first to circumnavigate <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, explore <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and reach <i><a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">Thule</a></i> (most class="mw-headline" id="Roman_explorations">Roman explorations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Exploration</dt></dl>\n<p>The Romans organized expeditions to cross the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert with five different routes:\n</p>\n<ul><li>through the western Sahara, toward the <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">Niger</a> river and actual <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>.</li>\n<li>through the Tibesti mountains, toward Lake Chad and actual <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a></li>\n<li>through the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> river, toward actual <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.</li>\n<li>though the western coast of Africa, toward the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands.</li>\n<li>through the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, toward actual <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>All these expeditions were supported by legionaries and had mainly a commercial purpose. Only the one done by emperor <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">Nero</a> seemed to be a preparative for the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>: in 62 AD two legionaries explored the sources of the Nile river.\n</p><p>One of the main reasons of the explorations was to get gold using the camel to transport it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The explorations near the African western and eastern coasts were supported by Roman ships and deeply related to the naval commerce (mainly toward the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>).\nRomans organized several explorations also in Northern Europe, and as far as Asia up to China .\n</p>\n<dl><dt>30 BC-640 AD</dt>\n<dd>With the acquisition of Ptolemaic Egypt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_trade_with_India" title="Roman trade with India">The Romans begin trading with India</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">Chinese historical texts</a> describe Roman embassies, from a land they called <i><a href="/wiki/Daqin" title="Daqin">Daqin</a></i>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>2nd century</dt>\n<dd>Roman traders reach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siam" title="Siam">Siam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>161</dt>\n<dd>An embassy from Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a> or his successor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a> reaches Chinese <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huan_of_Han" title="Emperor Huan of Han">Emperor Huan of Han</a> at <a href="/wiki/Luoyang" title="Luoyang">Luoyang</a>.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dt>226</dt>\n<dd>A Roman diplomat or merchant lands in northern Vietnam and visits <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, China and the court of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a>, ruler of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wu" title="Eastern Wu">Eastern Wu</a></dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_exploration_of_Central_Asia">Chinese exploration of Central Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chinese exploration of Central Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the 2nd century BC, the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> explored much of the Eastern Northern Hemisphere. Starting in 139 BC, the Han diplomat <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> traveled west in an unsuccessful attempt to secure an alliance with the Da <a href="/wiki/Yuezhi" title="Yuezhi">Yuezhi</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> (the Yuezhi had been evicted from <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> by the Xiongnu in 177 BC); however, Zhang\'s travels discovered entire countries which the Chinese were unaware of, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic For example, it is known that the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> explorer, <a href="/wiki/Erik_the_Red" central and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">south Pacific</a> for around 5,000 years, up to about 1280 when they discovered <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. The key invention to their exploration was the <a href="/wiki/Outrigger_canoe" title="Outrigger canoe">outrigger canoe</a>, which provided a swift and stable platform for carrying goods and people. Based on limited evidence, it is thought that the voyage to New Zealand was deliberate. It is unknown if one or more boats went to New Zealand, or the type of boat, or the names of those who migrated. 2011 studies at <a href="/wiki/Wairau_Bar" title="Wairau Bar">Wairau Bar</a> in New Zealand show a high probability that one origin was Ruahine Island in the <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">Society Islands</a>. Polynesians may have used the prevailing north easterly <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> to reach New Zealand in about three weeks. The Cook Islands are in direct line along the migration path and may have been an intermediate stopping point. There are cultural and language similarities between <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islanders" title="Cook Islanders">Cook Islanders</a> and New Zealand <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="M&#257;ori people">Maori</a>. Early Maori had different legends of their origins, but the stories were misunderstood and reinterpreted in confused written accounts by early European historians in New Zealand trying to present a coherent pattern of Maori settlement in New Zealand.\n</p><p>Mathematical modelling based on DNA <a href="/wiki/Identity_by_descent" title="Identity by descent">genome studies</a>, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State-of-the-art" title="State-of-the-art">state-of-the-art</a> techniques, have shown that a large number of Polynesian migrants (100&#8211;200), including women, arrived in New Zealand around the same time, in about 1280.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otago_University" title="Otago University">Otago University</a> studies have tried to link distinctive DNA teeth patterns, which show special dietary influence, with places in or nearby the <a href="/wiki/Society_Islands" title="Society Islands">Society Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_exploration_of_the_Indian_Ocean">Chinese exploration of the Indian Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chinese exploration of the Indian Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Chinese explorer, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Dayuan" title="Wang Dayuan">Wang Dayuan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 1311&#8211;1350) made two major trips by ship to the Indian Ocean. During 1328&#8211;1333, he sailed along the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> and visited many places in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and reached as far as <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, landing in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. Then in 1334&#8211;1339, he visited <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>. Later, the Chinese admiral <a href="/wiki/Zheng_He" title="Zheng He">Zheng He</a> (1371&#8211;1433) made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treasure_voyages" title="Treasure voyages">seven voyages</a> href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Age_of_Discovery">European Age of It started in the early 15th century and lasted until the 17th century. In that period, Europeans discovered and/or explored vast areas of the Americas, Africa, Asia and Oceania. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_empire" title="Portuguese empire">Portugal</a> America</a>. After the discovery of America by Columbus, a number of important expeditions were sent out to explore the Western Hemisphere. This included <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le&#243;n">Juan Ponce de Le&#243;n</a> (1474&#8211;1521), who discovered and mapped the coast of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>; <a href="/wiki/Aleixo_Garcia" title="Aleixo Garcia">Aleixo Garcia</a> (14?&#8211;1527), who explored the territories of present-day southern Brazil, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Chaco" title="Gran Chaco">Chaco</a> and reaching the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Sucre" title="Sucre">Sucre</a>). \n</p><p><a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvar_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_Cabeza_de_Vaca" title="&#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca">&#193;lvar N&#250;&#241;ez Cabeza de Vaca</a> (1490&#8211;1558) discovered the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> and was the first European to sail the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and cross <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier" title="Jacques Cartier">Jacques Cartier</a> (1491&#8211;1557) drew the first maps of part of central and maritime Canada; <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira" title="&#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira">&#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a</a> (1542&#8211;1595), who discovered the <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a> archipelago, the <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">Marquesas</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wake_Island" title="Wake Island">Wake Island</a>.\n</p><p>Explorers of Australia included  <a href="/wiki/Willem_Janszoon" title="Willem Janszoon">Willem Janszoon</a> (1570&#8211;1630), who made the first recorded European landing in Australia; <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%B1igo_Ortiz_de_Retez" title="Y&#241;igo Ortiz de Retez">Y&#241;igo Ortiz de Retez</a>, who discovered and reached eastern and northern <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_V%C3%A1ez_de_Torres" title="Luis V&#225;ez de Torres">Luis V&#225;ez de Torres</a> (1565&#8211;1613), who discovered the <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait" title="Torres Strait">Torres Strait</a> between <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>; <a href="/wiki/Abel_Tasman" title="Abel Tasman">Abel Tasman</a> (1603&#8211;1659), who explored North Australia, discovered <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.\n</p><p>In North America, major explorers included <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hudson" title="Henry Hudson">Henry Hudson</a> (156?&#8211;1611), who explored the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> in Canada; <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a> (1574&#8211;1635), who explored  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> (in Canada and northern United States); and <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Robert_Cavelier,_Sieur_de_La_Salle" title="Ren&#233;-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle">Ren&#233;-Robert Cavelier, Sieur de La Salle</a> (1643&#8211;1687), who explored the Great Lakes region of the United States and Canada, and the entire length of the Mississippi River.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Modern_Age">The Modern Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Modern Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Long after the golden age of discovery, other explorers <i>completed</i> the world map, such as various <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russians</a> explorers, reaching the <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> Pacific coast and the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, at the extreme edge of Asia and Alaska (North America); <a href="/wiki/Vitus_Bering" title="Vitus Bering">Vitus Bering</a> (1681&#8211;1741) who in the service of the Russian Navy, explored the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>, the North American coast of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and some other northern areas of the Pacific Ocean; and <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>, who explored the east coast of Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, and circumnavigated the Antarctic continent.\n</p><p>There were still significant explorations which occurred well into the modern age. This includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark_expedition" title="Lewis and Clark expedition">Lewis and Clark expedition</a> (1804-1806), an overland expedition dispatched by President <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a> to explore the newly-acquired Louisiana Purchase and to find an interior aquatic route to the Pacific Ocean, along with other objectives to examine the flora and fauna of the continent. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Exploring_Expedition" title="United States Exploring Expedition">United States Exploring Expedition</a> (1838-1842) was an expedition sent by President <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, in order to survey the Pacific Ocean and surrounding lands.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_exploration">Space exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Space exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Humanity is continuing to follow the impulse to explore, moving beyond <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">Space exploration</a> started in the 20th century with the invention of exo-atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>. This has given humans the opportunity to travel to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, and to send robotic explorers to other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> and far beyond.\n</p><p>Both of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voyager_probes" title="Voyager probes">Voyager probes</a> have left the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, bearing imprinted gold discs with multiple data types.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioral_trait">Behavioral trait</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Behavioral trait">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Individual_mobility" title="Individual mobility">Individual mobility</a></div>\n<p>A 2015 study, performed on mobile phone data  and on GPS tracks of private vehicles in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, demonstrated that  individuals naturally split into two well-defined categories according to their mobility habits, dubbed "returners" and "explorers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> \n"Explorers" showed a star-like  mobility pattern: they have a central core of locations (composed by home and work places) around which distant core of locations gravitates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
