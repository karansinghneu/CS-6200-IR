class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Goddess_Nut_2.JPG/440px-Goddess_Nut_2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goddess_Nut_2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nut swallows the sun, which travels through her body at night to be reborn at dawn.</div></div></div>\n<p>The pronunciation of <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">ancient Egyptian</a> is uncertain because vowels were long omitted from its writing, although her name often includes the unpronounced <a href="/wiki/Determinative" title="Determinative">determinative</a> <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs" title="Egyptian hieroglyphs">hieroglyph</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Sky_(hieroglyph)" title="Sky (hieroglyph)">sky</a>". Her name <i><i lang="egy" title="Ancient Egyptian language text">Nwt</i></i>, itself also meaning "Sky",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is usually transcribed as "Nut" but also sometimes appears in older sources as <b>Nunut</b>, <b>Nenet</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-budgie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-budgie-3">[3]</a></sup> <b>Naunet</b>, <b>Nuit</b>\n</p><p>She also appears in the hieroglyphic record by a number of <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithets</a>, not all of which are understood.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goddess_of_the_sky">Goddess of the sky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Goddess of the sky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Nut is a daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shu_(Egyptian_deity)" title="Shu (Egyptian deity)">Shu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tefnut" title="Tefnut">Tefnut</a>. Her brother and husband is <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a>. She had four or, in some sources, five children: <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nephthys" title="Nephthys">Nephthys</a>, and in some sources <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She is considered one of the oldest deities among the Egyptian pantheon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancient_Egypt_2001_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ancient_Egypt_2001-5">[5]</a></sup> with her origin being found on the creation story of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">Heliopolis</a>. She was originally the goddess of the <a href="/wiki/Night_(hieroglyph)" title="Night (hieroglyph)">nighttime</a> <a href="/wiki/Sky_(hieroglyph)" title="Sky (hieroglyph)">sky</a>, but eventually became referred to as simply the sky goddess. Her headdress was the hieroglyphic of part of her name, a <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pot</a>, which may also symbolize the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a>. Mostly depicted in nude human form, Nut was also sometimes depicted in the form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a> whose great body formed the sky and heavens, a <a href="/wiki/Ficus_sycomorus" title="Ficus sycomorus">sycamore tree</a>, or as a giant <a href="/wiki/Suidae" title="Suidae">sow</a>, suckling many piglets (representing the stars).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NutMAM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2232" data-file-width="3346" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/NutMAM.jpg/250px-NutMAM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/NutMAM.jpg/375px-NutMAM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/NutMAM.jpg/500px-NutMAM.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NutMAM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Great goddess Nut with her wings stretched across a coffin</div></div></div>\n<p>A sacred <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> of Nut was the ladder used by <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> to enter her heavenly skies. This ladder-symbol was called <i>maqet</i> and was placed in <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tombs</a> to protect the deceased, and to invoke the aid of the deity of the dead. Nut and her brother, Geb, may be considered enigmas in the world of mythology. In direct contrast to most other mythologies which usually develop a <a href="/wiki/Sky_father" title="Sky father">sky father</a> associated with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_mother" title="Earth mother">Earth mother</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Mother_Nature" title="Mother Nature">Mother Nature</a>), she personified the sky and he the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nut appears in the creation myth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">Heliopolis</a> which involves several goddesses who play important roles: <a href="/wiki/Tefnut" title="Tefnut">Tefnut</a> (Tefenet) is a personification of moisture, who mated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shu_(Egyptian_deity)" title="Shu (Egyptian deity)">Shu</a> (Air) and then gave birth to Sky as the goddess Nut, who mated with her brother Earth, as <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a>. From the union of Geb and Nut came, among others, the most popular of Egyptian goddesses, <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, the mother of <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>, whose story is central to that of her brother-husband, the resurrection god Osiris. Osiris is killed by his brother Set and scattered over the Earth in 14 pieces, which Isis gathers up and puts back together. Osiris then climbs a ladder into his mother Nut for safety and eventually becomes king of the dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Leeming_2004_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-David_Leeming_2004-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A huge cult developed about Osiris that lasted well into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_times" title="Roman times">Roman times</a>. Isis was her husband\'s queen in the underworld and the theological basis for the role of the queen on earth. It can be said that she was a version of the great goddess Hathor. Like <a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a>, she not only had death and rebirth associations, but was the protector of children and the goddess of childbirth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-David_Leeming_2004_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-David_Leeming_2004-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth_of_Nut_and_Ra">Myth of Nut and Ra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Myth of Nut and Ra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nut1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="774" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Nut1.JPG/220px-Nut1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Nut1.JPG/330px-Nut1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Nut1.JPG/440px-Nut1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nut1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sky goddess Nut depicted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">cow</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_god" title="Sun god">sun god</a>, was the second to rule the world, according to the reign of the gods. Ra was a strong ruler but he feared anyone taking his throne. When he discovered that Nut was to have children, he was furious. He decreed, "Nut shall not give birth any day of the year." At that time, the year was only 360 days. Nut spoke to <a href="/wiki/Thoth" title="Thoth">Thoth</a>, god of <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a>, and he had a plan. Thoth gambled with <a href="/wiki/Khonsu" title="Khonsu">Khonsu</a>, god of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_moon" title="The moon">the moon</a>, whose light rivaled that of Ra\'s. Every time Khonsu lost, he had to give Thoth some of his moonlight. Khonsu lost so many times that Thoth had enough moonlight to make five extra days. Since these days were not part of the year, Nut could have her children. She had five children: <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>, later ruler of the gods and then god of the dead; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horus_the_Elder" title="Horus the Elder">Horus the Elder</a>, god of war; <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, god of chaos and the desert; <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, goddess of magic; and <a href="/wiki/Nephthys" title="Nephthys">Nephthys</a>, goddess of water. When Ra found out, he was furious. He separated Nut from her husband <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a> for eternity. Her father, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shu_(Egyptian_deity)" title="Shu (Egyptian deity)">Shu</a>, was to keep them apart. Nevertheless, Nut did not regret her decision.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Some of the titles of Nut were:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Coverer of the Sky</i>: Nut was said to be covered in <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> touching the different points of her body.</li>\n<li><i>She Who Protects</i>: Among her jobs was to envelop and protect <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a>, the sun god.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancient_Egypt_2001_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ancient_Egypt_2001-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Mistress of All</i> or "She who Bore the Gods": Originally, Nut was said to be lying on top of <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a> (Earth) and continually having <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">intercourse</a>. During this time she birthed four children: <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Set_(deity)" title="Set (deity)">Set</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nephthys" title="Nephthys">Nephthys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A fifth child named Arueris is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He was the Egyptian counterpart to the Greek god <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, who was made syncretic with Horus in the Hellenistic era as \'Horus the Elder\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">Ptolemaic</a> temple of <a href="/wiki/Edfu" title="Edfu">Edfu</a> is dedicated to Horus the Elder and there he is called the son of Nut and <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a>, brother of Osiris, and the eldest son of Geb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>She Who Holds a Thousand Souls</i>: Because of her role in the re-birthing of <a href="/wiki/Ra" title="Ra">Ra</a> every morning and in her son Osiris\' resurrection, Nut became a key god in many of the myths about the afterlife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ancient_Egypt_2001_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ancient_Egypt_2001-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geb,_Nut,_Shu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1140" data-file-width="1691" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg/220px-Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg/330px-Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg/440px-Geb%2C_Nut%2C_Shu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geb,_Nut,_Shu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nut, goddess of sky supported by Shu the god of air, and the ram-headed Heh deities, while the earth god <a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a> reclines beneath.</div></div></div>\n<p>Nut was the goddess of the sky and all <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">heavenly bodies</a>, a symbol of protecting the dead when they enter the afterlife. According to the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>, during the day, the heavenly bodies&#8212;such as the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>&#8212;would make their way across her body. Then, at dusk, they would be swallowed, pass through her belly during the night, and be reborn at dawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nut is also the barrier separating the forces of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a> from the ordered <a href="/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">cosmos</a> in the world. She was pictured as a woman arched on her toes and fingertips over the earth; her body portrayed as a star-filled sky. Nut\'s fingers and toes were believed to touch the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_points" title="Cardinal points">cardinal points</a> or directions of north, south, east, and west.\n</p><p>Because of her role in saving Osiris, Nut was seen as a friend and protector of the dead, who appealed to her as a child appeals to its mother: "O my Mother Nut, stretch Yourself over me, that I may be placed among the imperishable stars which are in You, and that I may not die." Nut was thought to draw the dead into her star-filled sky, and refresh them with <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>: "I am Nut, and I have come so that I may enfold and protect you from all things evil."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ani_page_57_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ani_page_57-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p><p>She was often painted on the inside lid of the <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a>, protecting the deceased. The <a href="/wiki/Burial_vault_(tomb)" title="Burial vault (tomb)">vaults</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tombs" title="Tombs">tombs</a> were often painted dark <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a> with many stars as a representation of Nut.\nThe <a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Dead" title="Book of the Dead">Book of the Dead</a> says, "Hail, thou Sycamore Tree of the Goddess Nut! Give me of the <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> which is in thee. I embrace that throne which is in Unu, and I keep guard over the Egg of Nekek-ur. It flourisheth, and I flourish; it liveth, and I live; it snuffeth the air, and I snuff the air, I the Osiris Ani, whose word is truth, in peace.\'\'\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_of_Nut">Book of Nut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nut_(goddess)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Book of Nut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>The <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Nut" title="Book of Nut">Book of Nut</a></i> is a modern title of what was known in ancient times as <i>The Fundamentals of the Course of the Stars</i>. This is an important collection of ancient Egyptian astronomical texts, perhaps the earliest of several other such texts, going back at least to 2,000 BC. Nut, being the sky goddess, plays the big role in the <i>Book of Nut</i>. The text also tells about various other sky and earth deities, such as the star deities and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decans" title="Decans">decans</a> deities. The cycles of the stars and the planets, and the time keeping are covered in the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
