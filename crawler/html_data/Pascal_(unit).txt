unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> used to quantify <a href="/wiki/Internal_pressure" title="Internal pressure">internal pressure</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young\'s modulus">Young\'s modulus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultimate_tensile_strength" title="Ultimate tensile strength">ultimate tensile strength</a>. It is defined as one <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> per <a href="/wiki/Square_metre" title="Square metre">square metre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is named after the French <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a> <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>.\n</p><p>Common multiple units of the pascal are the hectopascal (1&#160;hPa = 100&#160;Pa) which is equal to one <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)#Usage" title="Bar (unit)">millibar</a>, and the kilopascal (1&#160;kPa = 1000&#160;Pa) which is equal to one centibar.\n</p><p>The unit of measurement called <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmosphere (atm)</a> is defined as <span style="white-space:nowrap">101<span style="margin-left:0.25em">325</span>&#160;Pa</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorological</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">reports</a> in the United States typically state <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)#Usage" title="Bar (unit)">millibars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In Canada these reports are given in terms of kilopascals.  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div href="/w/index.php?title=Pascal_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The unit is named after <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>, noted for his contributions to hydrodynamics and hydrostatics, and experiments with a <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a>. pascal (Pa) or kilopascal (kPa) as a unit of pressure measurement is widely used throughout the world and has largely replaced the <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">pounds per square inch</a> (psi) unit, except in some countries that still use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_measurement_system" title="Imperial measurement system">imperial measurement system</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_system" title="US customary system">US customary system</a>, including the United States.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysicists</a> use the gigapascal (GPa) in measuring or calculating tectonic stresses and pressures within the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.\n</p><p>Medical <a href="/wiki/Elastography" title="Elastography">elastography</a> measures tissue stiffness non-invasively with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">ultrasound</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a>, and often displays the <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young\'s modulus">Young\'s modulus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Shear_modulus" title="Shear modulus">shear modulus</a> of tissue in kilopascals.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, the pascal measures the <a href="/wiki/Young%27s_modulus" title="Young\'s modulus">stiffness</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compressive_strength" title="Compressive strength">compressive strength</a> of materials. In engineering use, because the pascal represents a very small quantity, the megapascal (MPa) is the preferred unit for these uses.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<caption>Approximate Young\'s modulus for common substances <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</caption>\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Material</th>\n<th>Young\'s modulus\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon 6</a></td>\n<td>2&#8211;4&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> fibre</td>\n<td>35&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a></td>\n<td>69&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">tooth enamel</a></td>\n<td>83&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a></td>\n<td>117&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">structural steel</a></td>\n<td>200&#160;GPa\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a></td>\n<td>1220&#160;GPa\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The pascal is also equivalent to the SI unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>, J/m<sup>3</sup>. This applies not only to the thermodynamics of pressurised gases, but also to the energy density of <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential_energy" title="Electric potential energy">electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field#Energy_stored_in_magnetic_fields" title="Magnetic field">magnetic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_energy" title="Gravitational energy">gravitational</a> fields.\n</p><p>In measurements of <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure" title="Sound pressure">sound pressure</a> or loudness of sound, one pascal is equal to 94 decibels <a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure#Sound_pressure_level" title="Sound pressure">SPL</a>.  The quietest sound a human can hear, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Threshold_of_hearing" title="Threshold of hearing">threshold of hearing</a>, is 0&#160;dB&#160;<a href="/wiki/Sound_pressure#Sound_pressure_level" title="Sound pressure">SPL</a>, or 20&#160;&#181;Pa.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Blower_door" title="Blower door">The airtightness of buildings</a> is measured at 50&#160;Pa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hectopascal_and_millibar_units">Hectopascal and millibar units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pascal_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hectopascal and millibar units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">Bar (unit)</a></div>\n<p>The units of atmospheric pressure commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> were formerly the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>, which was close to the average air pressure on Earth, and the millibar. Since the introduction of <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI units</a>, meteorologists generally measure pressures in hectopascals (hPa) unit, equal to 100 pascals or 1 millibar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Exceptions include Canada, which use kilopascals (kPa). In many other fields of science, the SI is preferred, which means Pa with a prefix (in multiples of 1000) is preferred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many countries also use the millibars. In practically all other fields, the kilopascal (1000 pascals) is used instead.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
