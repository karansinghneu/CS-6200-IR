<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Yu&#232;zh&#299;</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Y&#252;eh<sup>4</sup>-chih<sup>1</sup></span></i>, <span class="IPA" style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Mandarin" title="Help:IPA/Mandarin">[&#613;e&#770;&#160;&#648;&#642;&#635;&#809;&#769;]</a></span>) were an ancient <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Europeans" title="Proto-Indo-Europeans">Indo-European</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB_Zhang_Qian_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB_Zhang_Qian-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAO_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAO-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> people first described in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> histories as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic_pastoralists" title="Nomadic pastoralists">nomadic pastoralists</a> living in an arid <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> area in the western part of the modern Chinese province of <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>, during the 1st millennium BC. After a major defeat by the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> in 176 BC, the Yuezhi split into two groups migrating in different directions: the <b>Greater Yuezhi</b> (<i>D&#224; Yu&#232;zh&#299;</i> &#22823;&#26376;&#27663;) and <b>Lesser Yuezhi</b> (<i>Xi&#462;o Yu&#232;zh&#299;</i> &#23567;&#26376;&#27663;).\n</p><p>The Greater Yuezhi initially migrated northwest into the <a href="/wiki/Ili_River" title="Ili River">Ili Valley</a> (on the modern borders of China and Kazakhstan), where they reportedly displaced elements of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakas" title="Sakas">Sakas</a>. They were driven from the Ili Valley by the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a> and migrated southward to <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdia</a> and later settled in <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, where they then defeated the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a>. The Greater Yuezhi have consequently often been identified with Bactrian peoples mentioned in classical European sources, like the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tukhara" title="Tukhara">T&#243;kharioi</a></i> (Greek <i>&#932;&#959;&#967;&#940;&#961;&#953;&#959;&#953;</i>; Sanskrit <i>Tukh&#257;ra</i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Asii" title="Asii">Asii</a></i> (or <i>Asioi</i>). During the 1st century BC, one of the five major Greater Yuezhi tribes in Bactria, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushanas" title="Kushanas">Kushanas</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#36020;&#38684;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Gu&#236;shu&#257;ng</span></i>), began to subsume the other tribes and neighbouring peoples. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a>, at its peak in the 3rd century CE, stretched from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turfan" title="Turfan">Turfan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>, in the north to <a href="/wiki/Pataliputra" title="Pataliputra">Pataliputra</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gangetic_plain" title="Gangetic plain">Gangetic plain</a> of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in the south. The Kushanas played an important role in the development of trade on the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism" title="Silk Road transmission of Buddhism">introduction of Buddhism to China</a>.\n</p><p>The Lesser Yuezhi migrated southward to the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a>. Some are reported to have settled among the <a href="/wiki/Qiang_(historical_people)" title="Qiang (historical people)">Qiang people</a> in <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, and to have been involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liangzhou_Rebellion" title="Liangzhou Rebellion">Liangzhou Rebellion</a> (184&#8211;221 CE). Others are said to have founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_state" title="City state">city state</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumu%E1%B8%8Da" title="Cumu&#7693;a">Cumu&#7693;a</a> (now known as <i>Kumul</i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hami_City" title="Hami City">Hami</a>) in the eastern Tarim. A fourth group of Lesser Yuezhi may have become part of the <a href="/wiki/Jie_people" title="Jie people">Jie people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Shanxi" title="Shanxi">Shanxi</a>, who established the 4th century AD <a href="/wiki/Later_Zhao" title="Later Zhao">Later Zhao</a> state (although this remains controversial).\n</p><p>Although some scholars have associated the Yuezhi with artifacts of extinct cultures in the Tarim Basin, such as the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_mummies" title="Tarim mummies">Tarim mummies</a> and texts recording the <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian languages</a>, the evidence for any such link is purely circumstantial.\n</p>\n<table class="infobox collapsible uncollapsed plainlist" style="width:22em">\n<tbody><tr><th class="navbox-title" colspan="2" style="padding: 0.2em;">&#160;&#160;Timeline of the Yuezhi <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></th></tr>\n<tr style="vertical-align:top"><th style="text-align:center; font-weight:normal; border-bottom:#dcdcdc 1px solid;">\nBefore<br/><b>221&#160;BCE</b>&#160;</th><td style="border-bottom:#dcdcdc 1px solid">\nThe Yuezhi are powerful near Dunhuang, near the western end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexi_corridor" title="Hexi corridor">Hexi corridor</a>, and control the jade trade from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarim_basin" title="Tarim basin">Tarim basin</a>. id="Earliest_references_in_Chinese_texts">Earliest references in Chinese texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Earliest references in Chinese texts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1097" decoding="async" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg/220px-QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg/330px-QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg/440px-QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:QinEmpireWithOrdos.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>By the 3rd century BC, the Yuezhi resided to the northwest of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_empire" title="Qin empire">Qin China</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Three pre-Han texts mention peoples who appear to be the Yuezhi, albeit under slightly different names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>The philosophical tract <i><a href="/wiki/Guanzi_(text)" title="Guanzi (text)">Guanzi</a></i> (73, 78, 80 and 81) mentions <a href="/wiki/Nomadic_pastoralism" title="Nomadic pastoralism">nomadic pastoralists</a> known as the <i>Y&#250;zh&#299;</i> &#31162;&#27663; (<a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a>: *&#331;&#695;jo-kje) or <i>Ni&#250;zh&#299;</i> &#29275;&#27663; (<a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">OC</a>: *&#331;&#695;j&#601;-kje), who supplied <a href="/wiki/Jade" title="Jade">jade</a> to the Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup> (The <i>Guanzi</i> is now generally believed to have been compiled around 26 BC, based on older texts, including some from the <a href="/wiki/Qi_(state)" title="Qi (state)">Qi state era</a> of the 11th to 3rd centuries BC. Most scholars no longer attribute its primary authorship to <a href="/wiki/Guan_Zhong" title="Guan Zhong">Guan Zhong</a>, a Qi official in the 7th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>)  The export of jade from the Tarim Basin, since at least the late 2nd millennium BC, is well-documented archaeologically. For example, hundreds of jade pieces found in the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_Fu_Hao" title="Tomb of Fu Hao">Tomb of Fu Hao</a> (c. 1200 BC) originated from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khotan" title="Khotan">Khotan</a> area, on the southern rim of the Tarim Basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu2001a265_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2001a265-14">[14]</a></sup> According to the <i>Guanzi</i>, the Y&#250;zh&#299;/Ni&#250;zh&#299;, unlike the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>, did not engage in conflict with nearby Chinese states.</li>\n<li>The epic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Tale_of_King_Mu,_Son_of_Heaven" title="Tale of King Mu, Son of Heaven">Tale of King Mu, Son of Heaven</a></i> (early 4th century BC) also mentions a plain of <i>Y&#250;zh&#299;</i> &#31162;&#30693; (OC: *&#331;&#695;jo-kje) to the northwest of the Zhou lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Chapter 59 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Yi_Zhou_Shu" title="Yi Zhou Shu">Yi Zhou Shu</a></i> (probably dating from the 4th to 1st century BC) refers to a <i>Y&#250;zh&#299;</i> &#31162;&#27663; (OC: *&#331;&#695;jo-kje) people living to the northwest of the Zhou domain and offering horses as tribute. A late supplement contains the name <i>Yu&#232;d&#299;</i> &#26376;&#27664; (OC: *&#331;&#695;jat-tij), which may be a misspelling of the name <i>Yu&#232;zh&#299;</i> &#26376;&#27663; (OC: *&#331;&#695;jat-kje) found in later texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>In the 1st century BC, <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a> &#8211; widely regarded as the founder of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_historiography" title="Chinese historiography">Chinese historiography</a> &#8211; describes how the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a> (221&#8211;206 BC) bought jade and highly valued military <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> from a people that Sima Qian called the <i>W&#363;zh&#299;</i> &#28879;&#27663; (OC: *&#660;a-kje), led by a man named as <i>Luo</i>. The <i>W&#363;zh&#299;</i> traded these goods for Chinese <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, which they then sold on to other neighbours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBenjamin200732_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBenjamin200732-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu20103&#8211;4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu20103&#8211;4-16">[16]</a></sup> This is probably the first reference to the Yuezhi as a lynchpin in trade on the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu2001a273_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2001a273-17">[17]</a></sup> which in the 3rd century BC began to link Chinese states to Central Asia and, eventually, the Middle East, the Mediterranean and Europe.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Account_of_Zhang_Qian">Account of Zhang Qian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Account of Zhang Qian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest detailed account of the Yuezhi is found in chapter 123 of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Great_Historian" title="Records of the Great Historian">Records of the Great Historian</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>, describing a mission of <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> in the late 2nd century BC. Essentially the same text appears in chapter 61 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i>, though Sima Qian has added occasional words and phrases to clarify the meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both texts use the name <i>Yu&#232;zh&#299;</i> &#26376;&#27663; (OC: *&#331;&#695;jat-kje), composed of characters meaning "moon" and "clan" respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup> Several different <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanizations</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese-language</a> name have appeared in print. The Iranologist <a href="/wiki/Harold_Walter_Bailey" title="Harold Walter Bailey">H. W. Bailey</a> preferred <i>&#220;e-&#7789;&#351;i</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-19">[19]</a></sup> Another modern Chinese pronunciation of the name is <i>R&#242;uzh&#299;</i>, based on the theory that the character <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9C%88" title="wikt:&#26376;">&#26376;</a> in the name is a scribal error for <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%82%89" title="wikt:&#32905;">&#32905;</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThierry2005_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThierry2005-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yuezhi_and_Xiongnu">Yuezhi and Xiongnu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Yuezhi and Xiongnu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The account begins with the Yuezhi occupying the grasslands to the northwest of China at the beginning of the 2nd century BC:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Great Yuezhi was a nomadic horde. They moved about following their cattle, and had the same customs as those of the Xiongnu. As their soldiers numbered more than hundred thousand, they were strong and despised the Xiongnu. In the past, they lived in the region between Dunhuang and Qilian.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i>, 61</cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The area between the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">Dunhuang</a> lies in the western part of the modern Chinese province of <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>, but no archaeological remains of the Yuezhi have yet been found in this area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000283&#8211;284_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000283&#8211;284-20">[20]</a></sup>\nSome scholars have argued that "Dunhuang" should be <a href="/wiki/Dunhong" title="Dunhong">Dunhong</a>, a mountain in the <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a>, and that Qilian should be interpreted as a name for the Tian Shan. They have thus placed the original homeland of the Yuezhi 1,000&#160;km further northwest in the grasslands to the north of the Tian Shan (in the northern part of modern <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000283&#8211;284_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000283&#8211;284-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu2001a267&#8211;268_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2001a267&#8211;268-21">[21]</a></sup>\nOther authors suggest that the area identified by Sima Qian was merely the core area of an empire encompassing the western part of the Mongolian plain, the upper reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a> and possibly much of central Asia, including the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai Mountains</a>, the site of the <a href="/wiki/Pazyryk_burials" title="Pazyryk burials">Pazyryk burials</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ukok_Plateau" title="Ukok Plateau">Ukok Plateau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994169&#8211;172_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994169&#8211;172-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the late 3rd century the Yuezhi were so powerful that the Xiongnu monarch <a href="/wiki/Touman" title="Touman">Touman</a> even sent his eldest son <a href="/wiki/Modu_Chanyu" title="Modu Chanyu">Modu</a> as a hostage to the Yuezhi. The Yuezhi often attacked their neighbour the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a> to acquire slaves and pasture lands. Wusun originally lived together with the Yuezhi in the region between Dunhuang and Qilian Mountain. The Yuezhi attacked the Wusuns, killed their monarch Nandoumi and took his territory. The son of Nandoumi, Kunmo fled to the Xiongnu and was brought up by the Xiongnu monarch.\n</p><p>Gradually the Xiongnu grew stronger and war broke out between them and the Yuezhi. There were at least four wars between the Yuezhi and Xiongnu according to the Chinese accounts. The first war broke out during the reign of the Xiongnu monarch Touman (who died in 209 B.C) who suddenly attacked the Yuezhi. The Yuezhi wanted to kill <a href="/wiki/Modu_Chanyu" title="Modu Chanyu">Modu</a>, the son of the Xiongnu king <a href="/wiki/Touman" title="Touman">Touman</a> kept as a hostage to them, but Modu stole a good horse from them and managed to escape to his country. He subsequently killed his father and became ruler of the Xiongnu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200094_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200094-23">[23]</a></sup> It appears that the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a> did not defeat the Yuezhi in this first war. The second war took place in the 7th year of Modu era (203 B.C.). From this war, a large area of the territory originally belonging to the Yuezhi was seized by the Xiongnu and the hegemony of the Yuezhi started to shake. The third war probably was at 176 BC (or shortly earlier) and the Yuezhi were badly defeated.\n</p><p>Shortly before 176 BC, led by one of Modu\'s tribal chiefs, the Xiongnu invaded Yuezhi territory in the <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> region and achieved a crushing victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_Marshak_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin_Marshak-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith2009380&#8211;383_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith2009380&#8211;383-25">[25]</a></sup> Modu boasted in a letter (174 BC) to the Han emperor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> that due to "the excellence of his fighting men, and the strength of his horses, he has succeeded in wiping out the Yuezhi, slaughtering or forcing to submission every number of the tribe." The son of Modu, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laoshang_Chanyu" title="Laoshang Chanyu">Laoshang Chanyu</a> (ruled 174&#8211;166 BC), subsequently killed the king of the Yuezhi and, in accordance with nomadic traditions, "made a <a href="/wiki/Skull_cup" title="Skull cup">drinking cup</a> out of his skull." (<i>Shiji</i> 123.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993234_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993234-1">[1]</a></sup>)\n</p><p>Nevertheless, in about 173&#160;BC, the Wusun were apparently defeated by the Yuezhi, who killed a Wusun king (<i>kunmi</i> <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#26118;&#24396;</span> or <i>kunmo</i> <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#26118;&#33707;</span>) known as Nandoumi (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#38627;&#20828;&#38753;</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_Marshak_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin_Marshak-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exodus_of_the_Great_Yuezhi">Exodus of the Great Yuezhi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exodus of the Great Yuezhi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Western_Regions_1st_century_BC(en).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="680" decoding="async" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png/280px-Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png/420px-Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png/560px-Western_Regions_1st_century_BC%28en%29.png 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Western_Regions_1st_century_BC(en).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Central Asia in the 1st century BCE</div></div></div>\n<p>After their defeat by the Xiongnu, the Yuezhi split into two groups. The Lesser or Little Yuezhi (<i>Xiao Yuezhi</i>) moved to the "southern mountains", believed to be the <a href="/wiki/Qilian_Mountains" title="Qilian Mountains">Qilian Mountains</a> on the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a>, to live with the <a href="/wiki/Qiang_(historical_people)" title="Qiang (historical people)">Qiang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994170_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994170-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The so-called Greater (or Great) Yuezhi (<i>Da Yuezhi</i>) began migrating north-west in about 165 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> first settling in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ili_valley" title="Ili valley">Ili valley</a>, immediately north of the <a href="/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> mountains, where they defeated the <a href="/wiki/Saka" title="Saka">Sai</a> (Sakas): "The Yuezhi attacked the king of the Sai who moved a considerable distance to the south and the Yuezhi then occupied his lands" (<i>Book of Han</i> 61 4B). This was "the first historically recorded movement of peoples originating in the high plateaus of Asia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rene_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rene-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 132 BC the <a href="/wiki/Wusun" title="Wusun">Wusun</a>, in alliance with the Xiongnu and out of revenge from an earlier conflict, again managed to dislodge the Yuezhi from the Ili Valley, forcing them to move south-west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benjamin_Marshak_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin_Marshak-24">[24]</a></sup> The Yuezhi passed through the neighbouring urban civilization of <a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferghana" title="Ferghana">Ferghana</a>) and settled on the northern bank of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>, in the region of northern <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visit_of_Zhang_Qian">Visit of Zhang Qian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Visit of Zhang Qian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ZhangQianTravels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="528" data-file-width="725" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ZhangQianTravels.jpg/225px-ZhangQianTravels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ZhangQianTravels.jpg/338px-ZhangQianTravels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/ZhangQianTravels.jpg/450px-ZhangQianTravels.jpg 2x" width="225"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ZhangQianTravels.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A later mural (c. 618&#8211;712 CE) from the <a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">Mogao Caves</a>, depicting the Chinese mission of <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> to the Yuezhi in 126 BC.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Yuezhi were visited in <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a> by a Chinese mission, led by <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Qian" title="Zhang Qian">Zhang Qian</a> in 126 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> which sought an offensive alliance with the Yuezhi against the Xiongnu. Zhang Qian, who spent a year in Transoxiana and <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, wrote a detailed account in the <i>Shiji</i>, which gives considerable insight into the situation in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993233&#8211;236_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993233&#8211;236-32">[32]</a></sup> The request for an alliance was denied by the son of the slain Yuezhi king, who preferred to maintain peace in Transoxiana rather than seek revenge.\n</p><p>Zhang Qian also reported:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>the Great Yuezhi live 2,000 or 3,000 <i>li</i> [832&#8211;1,247 kilometers] west of <i><a href="/wiki/Dayuan" title="Dayuan">Dayuan</a></i>, north of the <i>Gui</i> [<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a> ] river. They are bordered on the south by <i><a href="/wiki/Daxia" title="Daxia">Daxia</a></i> [<a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>], on the west by <i>Anxi</i> [<a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>], and on the north by <i><a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a></i> [beyond the middle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaxartes" title="Jaxartes">Jaxartes</a>/Syr Darya]. They are a nation of <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">nomads</a>, moving from place to place with their herds, and their customs are like those of the Xiongnu. They have some 100,000 or 200,000 archer warriors.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><i>Shiji</i>, 123<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993234_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993234-1">[1]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>In a sweeping analysis of the physical types and cultures of Central Asia, Zhang Qian reports:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Although the states from Dayuan west to Anxi (Parthia), speak rather different languages, their customs are generally similar and their languages mutually intelligible. The men have deep-set eyes and profuse beards and whiskers. They are skilful at commerce and will haggle over a fraction of a cent. Women are held in great respect, and the men make decisions on the advice of their women.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><i>Shiji</i>, 123<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993245_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993245-33">[33]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amudaryamap.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="936" decoding="async" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Amudaryamap.jpg/220px-Amudaryamap.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Amudaryamap.jpg/330px-Amudaryamap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Amudaryamap.jpg/440px-Amudaryamap.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amudaryamap.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Watershed of the Oxus River (modern Amu Darya)</div></div></div>\n<p>Zhang Qian also described the remnants of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a> on the other side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus_River" title="Oxus River">Oxus River</a> (Chinese <i>Gui</i>) as a number of autonomous city-states under Yuezhi suzerainty:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994175_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994175-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Daxia is located over 2,000 li southwest of Dayuan, south of the Gui river. Its people cultivate the land and have cities and houses. Their customs are like those of Ta-Yuan. It has no great ruler but only a number of petty chiefs ruling the various cities. The people are poor in the use of arms and afraid of battle, but they are clever at commerce. After the Great Yuezhi moved west and attacked the lands, the entire country came under their sway. The population of the country is large, numbering some 1,000,000 or more persons. The capital is called the city of Lanshi and has a market where all sorts of goods are bought and sold.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><i>Shiji</i>, 123<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson1993235_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson1993235-35">[35]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Chinese_accounts">Later Chinese accounts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later Chinese accounts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The next mention of the Yuezhi in Chinese sources is found in chapter 96A of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Han" title="Book of Han">Book of Han</a></i> (completed in 111 CE), relating to the early 1st century BC.  At this time, the Yuezhi are described as occupying the whole of Bactria, organized into five major tribes or <i>x&#299;h&#243;u</i> (Ch:&#32726;&#20399;, "Allied Prince").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENarain1990158_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENarain1990158-36">[36]</a></sup>\nThese tribes were known to the Chinese as:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Xi&#363;m&#236;</i> (&#20241;&#23494;) in Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wakh%C4%81n" title="Wakh&#257;n">Wakh&#257;n</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zibak" title="Zibak">Zibak</a>;</li>\n<li><i>Gu&#236;shu&#257;ng</i> (&#36020;&#38684;) in <a href="/wiki/Badakhshan" title="Badakhshan">Badakhshan</a> and adjoining territories north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxus" title="Oxus">Oxus</a>;</li>\n<li><i>Shu&#257;ngm&#237;</i> (&#38617;&#38753;) in the region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shughnan" title="Shughnan">Shughnan</a>;</li>\n<li><i>X&#299;d&#249;n</i> (&#32952;&#38931;) in the region of <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">Balkh</a>, and;</li>\n<li><i>D&#363;m&#236;</i> (&#37117;&#23494;) in the region of <a href="/wiki/Termez" title="Termez">Termez</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Later_Han" title="Book of the Later Han">Book of the Later Han</a></i> (5th century CE) also records the visit of Yuezhi envoys to the Chinese capital in 2 BC, who gave oral teachings on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> sutras to a student, suggesting that some Yuezhi already followed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> faith during the 1st century BC (Baldev Kumar (1973)).\n</p><p>Chapter 88 of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Later_Han" title="Book of the Later Han">Book of the Later Han</a></i> relies on a report of <a href="/wiki/Ban_Yong" title="Ban Yong">Ban Yong</a>, based on the campaigns of his father <a href="/wiki/Ban_Chao" title="Ban Chao">Ban Chao</a> in the late 1st century CE.  It reports that one of the five tribes of the Yuezhi, the <i>Guishuang</i>, had managed to take control of the tribal confederation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENarain1990159_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENarain1990159-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>More than a hundred years later, the <i>xihou</i> of Guishuang, named Qiujiu Que (Ch: &#19992;&#23601;&#21371;, Kujula Kadphises) attacked and exterminated the four other <i>xihou</i>. He set himself up as king of a kingdom called Guishuang (Kushan). He invaded Anxi (<a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthia</a>) and took the Gaofu (Ch:&#39640;&#38468;, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>) region. He also defeated the whole of the kingdoms of Puda (Ch: &#28654;&#36948;) and Jibin (Ch: &#32637;&#36051;, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kapi%C5%9Ba" title="Kapi&#347;a">Kapi&#347;a</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gandh%C4%81ra" title="Gandh&#257;ra">Gandh&#257;ra</a>). Qiujiu Que (Kujula Kadphises) was more than eighty years old when he died.\nHis son, <i>Yan Gaozhen</i> (Ch:&#38331;&#39640;&#29645;) (<a href="/wiki/Vima_Takto" title="Vima Takto">Vima Takto</a>), became king in his place. He returned and defeated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Names_of_India#Tianzhu" title="Names of India">Tianzhu</a> (Northwestern India) and installed a General to supervise and lead it. The Yuezhi then became extremely rich. All the kingdoms call [their king] the Guishuang (Kushan) king, but the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a> call them by their original name, Da Yuezhi.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><i>Book of the Later Han</i>, trans. John Hill<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHill200928&#8211;29_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHill200928&#8211;29-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>A later Chinese annotation in Zhang Shoujie\'s  <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i> (quoting Wan Zhen &#33836;&#38663; in <i>N&#225;nzh&#333;uzh&#236;</i> &#21335;&#24030;&#24535; ["Strange Things from the Southern Region"], a now-lost 3rd-century text from the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wu" title="Eastern Wu">Wu kingdom</a>), describes the Kushans as living in the same general area north of India, in cities of Greco-Roman style, and with sophisticated handicraft. The quotes are dubious, as Wan Zhen probably never visited the Yuezhi kingdom through the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, though he might have gathered his information from the trading ports in the coastal south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taishan_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-taishan-41">[41]</a></sup> Chinese sources continued to use the name Yuezhi and seldom used the Kushan (or <i>Guishuang</i>) as a generic term:\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Great Yuezhi are located about seven thousand <i>li</i> [2,910 km] north of India. Their land is at a high altitude; the climate is dry; the region is remote. The king of the state calls himself "son of heaven". There are so many riding horses in that country that the number often reaches several hundred thousand. City layouts and palaces are quite similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Daqin" title="Daqin">Daqin</a> [the Roman Empire]. The skin of the people there is reddish white. People are skilful at horse archery. Local products, rarities, treasures, clothing, and upholstery are very good, and even India cannot compare with it.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>Wan Zhen (3rd century CE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kushana">Kushana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span struck in India as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushans" title="Kushans">Kushans</a> (1st&#8211;3rd century CE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>\nThey spoke <a href="/wiki/Bactrian_language" title="Bactrian language">Bactrian</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_language" title="Eastern Iranian language">Eastern Iranian language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tocharian_Online_Introduction_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tocharian_Online_Introduction-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bactria">Bactria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bactria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 3rd century BC, Bactria had been conquered by the Greeks under <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a> and since settled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">Hellenistic civilization</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucids" title="Seleucids">Seleucids</a>.\n</p><p>\nThe resulting <a href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian_Kingdom" title="Greco-Bactrian Kingdom">Greco-Bactrian Kingdom</a> lasted until the 2nd century BC. The area came under pressure from various nomadic peoples and the Greek city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandria_on_the_Oxus" title="Alexandria on the Oxus">Alexandria on the Oxus</a> was apparently burnt to the ground in about 145 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBernard1994100_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBernard1994100-52">[52]</a></sup> The last Greco-Bactrian king, <a href="/wiki/Heliocles_I" title="Heliocles I">Heliocles I</a>, retreated and moved his capital to the Kabul Valley. In about 140&#8211;130 BC, the Greco-Bactrian state was conquered by the nomads and dissolved. The Greek geographer <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> mentions this event in his account of the central Asian tribes he called "<a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a>":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174-53">[53]</a></sup> <link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>All, or the greatest part of them, are nomads. The best known tribes are those who deprived the Greeks of <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactriana</a>: the Asii, Pasiani, Tochari, and Sacarauli, who came from the country on the other side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaxartes" title="Jaxartes">Jaxartes</a> [Syr Darya], opposite the Sacae and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdians" title="Sogdians">Sogdiani</a>.</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote><p> The Roman historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompeius_Trogus" title="Pompeius Trogus">Pompeius Trogus</a> (1st century BC) attributes destruction of the Greco-Bactrian state to the Sacaraucae and the Asiani "kings of the Tochari".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174-53">[53]</a></sup> Both Pompeius and <a href="/wiki/Justin_(historian)" title="Justin (historian)">Justin</a> (2nd century CE) record that the Parthian king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artabanus_II_of_Parthia" title="Artabanus II of Parthia">Artabanus II</a> was mortally wounded in a war against the Tokhari in 124 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several relationships between these tribes and those named in Chinese sources have been proposed, but remain contentious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994174-53">[53]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yuezhi_copy_of_Heliocles_I_coin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="800" reverse</div></div></div>\n<p>After they settled in Bactria, the Yuezhi became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenisation" title="Hellenisation">Hellenized</a> to some degree &#8211; as shown by their adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> and by some remaining coins, minted in the style of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> kings, with the text in Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENarain1990161_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENarain1990161-56">[56]</a></sup> The area of Bactria they settled came to be known as Tokharistan and the Yuezhi were known as <i>T&#243;kharoi</i> by the Greeks.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Hindu_Kush">In the Hindu Kush</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In the Hindu Kush">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The area of the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> (<a href="/wiki/Paropamisadae" title="Paropamisadae">Paropamisadae</a>) was ruled by the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Greek" title="Indo-Greek">Indo-Greek</a> king until the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Hermaeus" title="King Hermaeus">Hermaeus</a> (reigned c. 90 BC&#8211;70 BC). After that date, no Indo-Greek kings are known in the area. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bopearachchi" title="Bopearachchi">Bopearachchi</a>, no trace of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Scythian" title="Indo-Scythian">Indo-Scythian</a> occupation (nor coins of major Indo-Scythian rulers such as <a href="/wiki/Maues" title="Maues">Maues</a> or <a href="/wiki/Azes_I" title="Azes I">Azes I</a>) have been found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paropamisade" title="Paropamisade">Paropamisade</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhara</a>. The Hindu Kush may have been subsumed by the Yuezhi,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (August 2008)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> who by then had been dominated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactria" title="Greco-Bactria">Greco-Bactria</a> for almost two centuries.\n</p><p>As they had done in Bactria with their copying of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> coinage, the Yuezhi copied the coinage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Hermaeus" title="King Hermaeus">Hermeaus</a> on a vast scale, up to around 40 CE, when the design blends into the coinage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kushan" title="Kushan">Kushan</a> king <a href="/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises" title="Kujula Kadphises">Kujula Kadphises</a>. Such coins may provide the earliest known names of Yuezhi <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yabgu" title="Yabgu">yabgu</a></i> (a minor royal title, similar to prince), namely <a href="/wiki/Sapadbizes" title="Sapadbizes">Sapadbizes</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (August 2008)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> and/or <a href="/wiki/Agesiles" title="Agesiles">Agesiles</a>, who both lived in or about 20 BC.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kushan_Empire">Kushan Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kushan Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Asia_001ad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1181" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Asia_001ad.jpg/260px-Asia_001ad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Asia_001ad.jpg/390px-Asia_001ad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Asia_001ad.jpg/520px-Asia_001ad.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Asia_001ad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Asia circa 1 AD &#8211; the Yuezhi ("Tocharians") are located near the centre of the map.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coin_of_Heraios.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="504" decoding="async" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Coin_of_Heraios.jpg/220px-Coin_of_Heraios.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Coin_of_Heraios.jpg/330px-Coin_of_Heraios.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Coin_of_Heraios.jpg/440px-Coin_of_Heraios.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coin_of_Heraios.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first self-declared Kushan ruler <a href="/wiki/Heraios" title="Heraios">Heraios</a> (1&#8211;30 AD) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Bactrian" title="Greco-Bactrian">Greco-Bactrian</a> style<br/><b>Obv:</b> Bust of Heraios, with Greek royal headband.<br/><b>Rev:</b> Horse-mounted King, crowned with a wreath by the <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> goddess of victory <a href="/wiki/Nike_(mythology)" title="Nike (mythology)">Nike</a>. Greek legend: TVPANNOVOTO&#931; H&#923;OV &#8211; &#931;&#923;N&#923;B &#8211; KO&#222;&#222;ANOY "The Tyrant Heraios, Sanav (meaning unknown), of the Kushans"</div></div></div>\n<p>After that point, they extended their control over the northwestern area of the Indian subcontinent, founding the <a href="/wiki/Kushan_Empire" title="Kushan Empire">Kushan Empire</a>, which was to rule the region for several centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiu2001b156_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2001b156-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200984&#8211;85_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith200984&#8211;85-59">[59]</a></sup> Despite their change of name, most Chinese authors continued to refer to the Kushanas as the Yuezhi.\n</p><p>The Kushanas expanded to the east during the 1st century CE. The first Kushan emperor, <a href="/wiki/Kujula_Kadphises" title="Kujula Kadphises">Kujula Kadphises</a>, ostensibly associated himself with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Hermaeus" title="King Hermaeus">King Hermaeus</a> on his coins, suggesting that he may<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have been one of the king\'s descendants by alliance, or at least wanted to claim his legacy.\n</p><p>The Kushanas integrated <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> into a pantheon of many deities and became great promoters of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahayana_Buddhism" title="Mahayana Buddhism">Mahayana Buddhism</a>, and their interactions with Greek civilization helped the <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandharan</a> culture and <a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhism" title="Greco-Buddhism">Greco-Buddhism</a> flourish.\n</p><p>During the 1st and 2nd centuries, the Kushan Empire expanded militarily to the north and occupied parts of the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>, putting them at the center of the lucrative Central Asian commerce with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. The Kushanas collaborated militarily with the Chinese against their mutual enemies. This included a campaign with the Chinese general <a href="/wiki/Ban_Chao" title="Ban Chao">Ban Chao</a> against the Sogdians in 84 CE, when the latter were trying to support a revolt by the king of <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a>. In around 85 CE,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the Kushanas also assisted the Chinese in an attack on <a href="/wiki/Turpan" title="Turpan">Turpan</a>, east of the Tarim Basin.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1126" data-file-width="1189" decoding="async" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg/220px-YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg/330px-YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg/440px-YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:YuezhiKingAndAttendants.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Possible Yuezhi king and attendants, <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhara</a> <a href="/wiki/Stone_palette" title="Stone palette">stone palette</a>, 1st century CE</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the military support provided to the Han, the Kushan emperor requested a marriage alliance with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han</a> princess and sent gifts to the Chinese court in expectation that this would occur. After the Han court refused, a Kushan army 70,000 strong marched on Ban Chao in 86 CE. The army was apparently exhausted by the time it reached its objective and was defeated by the Chinese force. The Kushanas retreated and later paid tribute to the Chinese emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_He_of_Han_China" title="Emperor He of Han China">Han He</a> (89&#8211;106).\n</p><p>In about 120 CE, Kushan troops installed Chenpan &#8211; a prince who had been sent as a hostage to them and had become a favorite of the Kushan Emperor &#8212; on the throne of <a href="/wiki/Kashgar" title="Kashgar">Kashgar</a>, thus expanding their power and influence in the <a href="/wiki/Tarim_Basin" title="Tarim Basin">Tarim Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHill200914,_43_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHill200914,_43-60">[60]</a></sup> There they introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hm%C4%AB_script" title="Br&#257;hm&#299; script">Brahmi</a> script, the Indian <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a> language for administration, and <a href="/wiki/Greco-Buddhist_art" title="Greco-Buddhist art">Greco-Buddhist art</a>, which developed into <a href="/wiki/Serindian_art" title="Serindian art">Serindian art</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:287px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BuddhistTriad.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="970" data-file-width="1587" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/BuddhistTriad.JPG/285px-BuddhistTriad.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/BuddhistTriad.JPG/428px-BuddhistTriad.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/BuddhistTriad.JPG/570px-BuddhistTriad.JPG 2x" width="285"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BuddhistTriad.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Buddhist art c. 300 CE, depicting (left to right) a Kushan lay Buddhist, <a href="/wiki/Maitreya" title="Maitreya">Maitreya</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddha" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalokitesvara" title="Avalokitesvara">Avalokitesvara</a>, and a Kushan Buddhist monk.</div></div></div>\n<p>Following this territorial expansion, the Kushanas introduced <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> to northern and northeastern Asia, by both direct missionary efforts and the translation of Buddhist scriptures into Chinese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Major Kushan missionaries and translators included <a href="/wiki/Lokaksema_(Buddhist_monk)" title="Lokaksema (Buddhist monk)">Lokaksema</a> (born c. 147 CE) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dharmaraksa" title="Dharmaraksa">Dharmaraksa</a> (c. 233 &#8211; c. 311), both of whom were influential translators of the <a href="/wiki/Mahayana_sutras" title="Mahayana sutras">Mahayana sutras</a> into Chinese. They who went to China and established translation bureaus, thereby being at the center of the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism" title="Silk Road transmission of Buddhism">Silk Road transmission of Buddhism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Records of the Three Kingdoms">Records of the Three Kingdoms</a></i> (chap. 3), it was recorded that in 229 CE, "The king of the Da Yuezhi [Kushanas], Bodiao &#27874;&#35519; (<a href="/wiki/Vasudeva_I" title="Vasudeva I">Vasudeva I</a>), sent his envoy to present tribute, and His Majesty (Emperor <a href="/wiki/Cao_Rui" title="Cao Rui">Cao Rui</a>) granted him the title of King of the Da Yuezhi Intimate with the <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Wei</a> (Ch: &#35242;&#39759;&#22823;&#26376;&#27663;&#29579;, <i>Q&#299;n W&#232;i D&#224; Yu&#232;zh&#299; W&#225;ng</i>)."\n</p><p>Soon afterwards, the military power of the Kushanas began to decline. The rival <a href="/wiki/Sasanian_Empire" title="Sasanian Empire">Sasanian Empire</a> of Persia extended its dominion into Bactria during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Ardashir_I" title="Ardashir I">Ardashir I</a> around 230 CE. The Sasanians also occupied neighboring <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdia</a> by 260 AD and made it into a <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrapy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the course of the 3rd and 4th centuries CE, the Kushan Empire was divided and conquered by the Sasanians, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hephthalite" title="Hephthalite">Hephthalite</a> tribes from the north,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcwb2_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-lcwb2-63">[63]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Gupta_Empire" title="Gupta Empire">Gupta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yaudheya" title="Yaudheya">Yaudheya</a> empires from India.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" The term is used of peoples in locations as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanxi" title="Shanxi">Shanxi</a> and the Tarim Basin.\n</p><p>Some of the Lesser Yuezhi settled among the <a href="/wiki/Qiang_(historical_people)" title="Qiang (historical people)">Qiang people</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huangzhong" title="Huangzhong">Huangzhong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, according to archaeologist Sophia-Katrin Psarras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Yuezhi and Qiang were said to be among members of the Auxiliary of Loyal Barbarians From Huangzhong that mutinied against the Han dynasty, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liangzhou_Rebellion" title="Liangzhou Rebellion">Liangzhou Rebellion</a> (184&#8211;221 CE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Elements of the Lesser Yuezhi are said to have been a component of the <a href="/wiki/Jie_people" title="Jie people">Jie people</a>, who originated from <a href="/wiki/Yushe_County" title="Yushe County">Yushe County</a> in Shanxi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other theories link the Jie more strongly to the Xiongnu, Kangju, or the Tocharian-speaking peoples of the Tarim. Led by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Shi_Le" title="Shi Le">Shi Le</a> the Jie established the <a href="/wiki/Later_Zhao" title="Later Zhao">Later Zhao</a> state (319&#8211;351 CE) and were massacred by King <a href="/wiki/Ran_Min" title="Ran Min">Ran Min</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ran_Wei" title="Ran Wei">Ran Wei</a> state, during the <a href="/wiki/Wei%E2%80%93Jie_war" title="Wei&#8211;Jie war">Wei&#8211;Jie war</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, the Lesser Yuezhi constituted the <i>Gar</i> or <i>mGar</i>  &#8211; a clan name associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blacksmiths" title="Blacksmiths">blacksmiths</a>. The <i>Gar</i> became influential during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Empire" title="Tibetan Empire">Tibetan Empire</a> &#8211; until the end of the 7th century, when 2,000 of them were massacred by the Tibetan emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tridu_Songts%C3%A4n" title="Tridu Songts&#228;n">Tridu Songts&#228;n</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Some of the Lesser Yuezhi are said to have founded the city state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumu%E1%B8%8Da" title="Cumu&#7693;a">Cumu&#7693;a</a> (also <i>Cimuda</i> or <i>Cunuda</i>), near the <a href="/wiki/Lop_Nur" title="Lop Nur">Lop Nur</a> lake system, in the eastern Tarim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-19">[19]</a></sup> Cumu&#7693;a and its people were known to the Han as &#20210;&#38642; <i>Zhongyun</i> (pinyin; Wade&#8211;Giles <i>Tchong-yun</i>). In the 10th century, a Chinese monk named Gao Juhui, who had traveled to the Tarim Basin, stated that the Zhongyun were descendants of the <i>Xiao Yuezhi</i>, and that the king of Zhongyun resided near Lop Nur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> (Following the subsequent settlement of <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_language" title="Uyghur language">Uyghur</a>-speaking people in the area, Cumu&#7693;a became known as <i>&#268;ungul</i>,  <i>Xungul</i> and <i>Kumul</i>. Under subsequent Han Chinese influence, it became known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hami_City" title="Hami City">Hami</a> &#21704;&#23494;.)\n</p><p>Whatever their fate may have been, the <i>Xiao Yuezhi</i> ceased to be identifiable by that name and appear to have been subsumed by other ethnicities, including <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uyghur_people" title="Uyghur people">Uyghurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_links_to_other_groups">Proposed links to other groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuezhi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Proposed links to other groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heraios_profile.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="732" data-file-width="645" decoding="async" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Heraios_profile.jpg/220px-Heraios_profile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Heraios_profile.jpg/330px-Heraios_profile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Heraios_profile.jpg/440px-Heraios_profile.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Heraios_profile.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of the Kushan ruler <a href="/wiki/Heraios" title="Heraios">Heraios</a>, who reigned 1&#8211;30 CE.</div></div></div>\n<p>The relationship between the Yuezhi and other Central Asian peoples is unclear. Based on claimed similarities of names, different scholars have linked them to several groups, but none of these identifications is widely accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200098&#8211;99,_281&#8211;283_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200098&#8211;99,_281&#8211;283-67">[67]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mallory and Mair suggest that the Yuezhi and Wusun were among the nomadic peoples, at least some of whom spoke <a href="/wiki/Iranian_languages" title="Iranian languages">Iranian languages</a>, who moved into northern Xinjiang from the Central Asian steppe in the 2nd millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000318_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair2000318-68">[68]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scholars such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Pulleyblank" title="Edwin Pulleyblank">Edwin Pulleyblank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Josef_Markwart" title="Josef Markwart">Josef Markwart</a> (a.k.a. Joseph Marquart) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Torday&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L&#225;szl&#243; Torday (page does not exist)">L&#225;szl&#243; Torday</a>, suggest that the name  <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Iatioi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Iatioi (page does not exist)">Iatioi</a></i> &#8211; a Central Asian people mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i> (150 CE) &#8211; may also be an attempt to render Yuezhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jhutti_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jhutti-69">[69]</a></sup>\n</p><p>There has been only limited scholarly support for a theory developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W._B._Henning" title="W. B. Henning">W. B. Henning</a>, who proposed that the Yuezhi were descended from the <a href="/wiki/Gutian_people" title="Gutian people">Guti</a> (or Gutians) and an associated, but little known tribe known as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tukri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tukri (page does not exist)">Tukri</a>, who were native to the <a href="/wiki/Zagros_Mountains" title="Zagros Mountains">Zagros Mountains</a> (modern Iran/Iraq), during the mid-3rd millennium BCE. In addition to phonological similarities between these names and <i>*&#331;&#695;jat-kje</i> and Tukh&#257;ra, Henning pointed out that the Guti could have migrated from the Zagros to Gansu,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> by the time that the Yuezhi entered the historical record in China, during the 1st millennium BC. However, the only material evidence presented by Henning, namely similar ceramic ware, is generally considered to be far from conclusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Proposed links with the <a href="/wiki/Aorsi" title="Aorsi">Aorsi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asii" title="Asii">Asii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Getae" title="Getae">Getae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gushi_culture" title="Gushi culture">Gushi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jatts" title="Jatts">Jatts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massagetae" title="Massagetae">Massagetae</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994171_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEnokiKoshelenkoHaidary1994171-74">[74]</a></sup> and other groups have also gathered little support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200098&#8211;99,_281&#8211;283_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryMair200098&#8211;99,_281&#8211;283-67">[67]</a></sup>\n</p><p>When manuscripts dating from the 6th to 8th centuries AD written in two hitherto-unknown languages were discovered in the northern Tarim Basin, the early 20th-century linguist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W._K._M%C3%BCller" title="Friedrich W. K. M&#252;ller">Friedrich W. K. M&#252;ller</a> assumed that the authors were <i>T&#243;kharoi</i> and referred to the newly discovered languages as "<a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">Tocharian</a>". This became the common name for both the languages of the Tarim manuscripts and the people who produced them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tocharian_Online_Introduction_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tocharian_Online_Introduction-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Most historians now reject the identification of the Tocharians of the Tarim with the <i>T&#243;kharoi</i> of Bactria, who are not known to have spoken any languages other than Bactrian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_p72_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen_p72-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997590_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalloryAdams1997590-76">[76]</a></sup> Other scholars suggest that the Kushanas may previously have spoken Tocharian before shifting to Bactrian on their arrival in Bactria, an example of an invading or colonising elite <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">adopting a local language</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>\nHowever, while Tocharian contains some loanwords from Bactrian, there are no traces of Tocharian in Bactrian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tocharian_Online_Introduction_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tocharian_Online_Introduction-51">[51]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endonym" title="Endonym">endonym</a> of the Yuezhi was put forward by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._W._Bailey" title="H. W. Bailey">H. W. Bailey</a>, claimed that they were referred to, in 9th and 10th century <a href="/wiki/Saka_language" title="Saka language">Khotan Saka</a> Iranian texts, as the <i>Gara</i>. According to Bailey, the <i>Tu&#160;Gara</i> ("Great Gara") were the Great Yuezhi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-19">[19]</a></sup> This is consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> &#932;&#972;&#967;&#945;&#961;&#959;&#953; <i>Tokharoi</i> (Latinised <i>Tochari</i>) in reference to the faction of the Kushans that conquered Bactria, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Tibetan language</a> name <i>Gar</i> (or <i>mGar</i>), for the members of the Lesser Yuezhi who settled in the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Empire" title="Tibetan Empire">Tibetan Empire</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
