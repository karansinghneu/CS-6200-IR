href="/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer" title="Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer">WISE</a>&#160;&#8594;</div></div>&#160;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Interstellar Boundary Explorer</b> (<b>IBEX</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> satellite in Earth orbit that uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energetic_Neutral_Atoms" title="Energetic Neutral Atoms">Energetic Neutral Atoms</a> (ENAs) to image the interaction region between the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstellar_space" title="Interstellar space">interstellar space</a>. The mission is part of NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Small_Explorer_program" title="Small Explorer program">Small Explorer program</a> and was launched with a <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(rocket)" title="Pegasus (rocket)">Pegasus-XL</a> rocket on October 19, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-MSC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBEX-MSC-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mission is led by Dr. <a href="/wiki/David_J._McComas" title="David J. McComas">David J. McComas</a> (IBEX Principal Investigator), formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Research_Institute" title="Southwest Research Institute">Southwest Research Institute</a> and now with <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> Advanced Technology Center built the IBEX-Hi and IBEX-Lo sensors respectively. The <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a> manufactured the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacecraft_bus" title="Spacecraft bus">spacecraft bus</a> and was the location for spacecraft environmental testing. The nominal mission baseline duration was two years after commissioning, and the prime ended in early 2011. The spacecraft and sensors are still healthy and the mission is continuing in its extended mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibex_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibex-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>IBEX is in a Sun-oriented spin-stabilized orbit around the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-facts-5">[5]</a></sup> In June 2011, IBEX was shifted to a new more efficient, much more stable orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexnews201111_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibexnews201111-6">[6]</a></sup> It does not come as close to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> in the new orbit, and expends less fuel to maintain its position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexnews201111_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibexnews201111-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science Goal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Interstellar Boundary Explorer (IBEX) mission science goal is to discover the nature of the interactions between the solar wind and the interstellar medium at the edge of our solar system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_IBEX_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_IBEX-7">[7]</a></sup>  IBEX has achieved this goal by generating full sky maps of the intensity (integrated over the line-of-sight) of ENAs in a range of energies every six months.  Most of these ENAs are generated in the <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere#Heliosheath" title="Heliosphere">heliosheath</a>, which is the region of interaction.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The IBEX satellite was mated to its <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(rocket)" title="Pegasus (rocket)">Pegasus</a> XL rocket at <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_Air_Force_Base" title="Vandenberg Air Force Base">Vandenberg Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and the combined vehicle was then suspended below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-1011" title="Lockheed L-1011">Lockheed L-1011</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Stargazer_(aircraft)" title="Stargazer (aircraft)">Stargazer</a></i> mother airplane and flown to <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein_Atoll" title="Kwajalein Atoll">Kwajalein Atoll</a> in the Central <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>Stargazer</i> arrived at Kwajalein on Sunday, October 12, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_IBEX_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_IBEX-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The IBEX satellite was carried into space on October 19, 2008, by the Pegasus XL rocket. The rocket was released from <i>Stargazer</i>, which took off from Kwajalein, at 17:47:23&#160;<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBEX-MSC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-IBEX-MSC-3">[3]</a></sup> By launching from this site close to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, the Pegasus rocket lifted as much as 16&#160;kg (35&#160;lb) more mass to orbit than it would have with a launch from the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_profile">Mission profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The IBEX satellite, initially launched into a highly-elliptical transfer orbit with a low perigee, used a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_fuel_rocket_motor" title="Solid fuel rocket motor">solid fuel rocket motor</a> as its final boost stage at apogee, in order to raise its perigee greatly and to achieve its desired high-altitude elliptical orbit.\n</p><p>IBEX is in a highly-eccentric elliptical terrestrial orbit, which ranges from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> of about 86,000&#160;km (53,000&#160;mi) to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">apogee</a> of about 260,000&#160;km (160,000&#160;mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2yo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-n2yo-2">[2]</a></sup> Its original orbit was about 7,000 by 320,000&#160;km (4,300 by 198,800&#160;mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facts_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-facts-5">[5]</a></sup>&#8212;that is, about 80% of the distance to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>&#8212;which has changed primarily due to an intentional adjustment to prolong the spacecraft\'s useful life (see <a href="#Orbit_adjusted">Orbit adjusted</a> below).\n</p><p>This very high orbit allows the IBEX satellite to move out of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> when making scientific observations. This extreme altitude is critical due to the amount of charged-particle interference that would occur while taking measurements within the magnetosphere. When within the magnetosphere of the Earth (70,000&#160;km or 43,000&#160;mi), the satellite also performs other functions, including <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> downlinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexfaq_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibexfaq-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_adjusted">Orbit adjusted</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orbit adjusted">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In June 2011 IBEX shifted to a new orbit that raised its perigee to more than 30,000 kilometres (19,000&#160;mi). The new orbit has a period of one third of a lunar month, which, with the correct phasing, avoids taking the spacecraft too close to the Moon, whose gravity can negatively affect IBEX\'s orbit. Now the spacecraft uses less fuel to maintain a stable orbit, increasing its useful lifespan to more than 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexnews201111_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ibexnews201111-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg/170px-IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg/255px-IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg/340px-IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IBEX_Lo_sensor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>IBEX Lo sensor</div></div></div>\n<p>The heliospheric boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is being imaged by measuring the location and magnitude of charge-exchange collisions occurring in all directions. The satellite\'s payload consists of two <a href="/wiki/Energetic_neutral_atom" title="Energetic neutral atom">energetic neutral atom</a> (ENA) imagers, IBEX-Hi and IBEX-Lo. Each consists of a <a href="/wiki/Collimator" title="Collimator">collimator</a> that limits their fields-of-view, a conversion surface to convert neutral <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_analyzer" title="Electrostatic analyzer">electrostatic analyzer</a> (ESA) to suppress <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light and to select ions of a specific energy range, and a detector to count particles and identify the type of each ion. Both of these sensors are a single-pixel camera with a field-of-view of roughly 7&#176; x 7&#176;.  The IBEX-Hi instrument is recording particle counts in a higher energy band (300 eV to 6 keV) than the IBEX-Lo energy band (10 eV to 2 keV). The scientific payload also includes a Combined Electronics Unit (CEU) that controls the voltages on the collimator and the ESA, and it reads and records data from the particle detectors of each sensor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexnssdc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibexnssdc-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication">Communication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Communication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Compared to other space observatories, IBEX has a low data transfer rate due to the limited requirements of the mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexqanda_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibexqanda-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... IBEX data transfer rates are slow compared with other telescopes due to the nature of the data it collects. IBEX does not need a "high speed" connection, since it only has the opportunity to collect up to a few particles per minute. Communication from the satellite to the ground is 20 times slower than a typical home cable modem (320,000 bits per second,) and from the ground to the satellite only 2,000 bits per second, which is 250 times slower! Once the signal is collected by the receivers on Earth it is carried over the internet to Mission Control Center in Dulles, VA and to the IBEX Science Operation Center in San Antonio, TX."</p><div class="templatequotecite">&#8212;&#8201;<cite>NASA\'s IBEX Q and A<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibexqanda_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibexqanda-12">[12]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_collection">Data collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Fluff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It has also observed ENAs from the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibex_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibex-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2010, significant changes were detected in the ribbon after six months, based on the second set of IBEX observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-change-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>It went on to detect neutral atoms from outside the Solar System, which were found to differ in composition from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bow_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-bow-19">[19]</a></sup> Surprisingly, IBEX discovered that the heliosphere has no <a href="/wiki/Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics" title="Bow shocks in astrophysics">bow shock</a>, and it measured its speed relative to the local <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (LISM) as 23.2&#160;km/s (52,000&#160;mph), improving on the previous measurement of 26.3&#160;km/s (59,000&#160;mph) by <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">Ulysses</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astrobites20120514_20-0"><a
