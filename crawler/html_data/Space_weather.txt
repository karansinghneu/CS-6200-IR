href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery"><i>Discovery</i></a>, May 1991</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Space weather</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">space physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeronomy" title="Aeronomy">aeronomy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Heliophysics" title="Heliophysics">heliophysics</a>, concerned with the time varying conditions within the Solar System, including the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, emphasizing the space surrounding the Earth, including conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>, terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a> (<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>). The term <i>space weather</i> was first used in the 1950s and came into common usage in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-origin-2">[2]</a></sup> Similar to terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, the long term patterns of space weather comprise <a href="/wiki/Space_climate" title="Space climate">space climate</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For many centuries, the effects of space weather were noticed but not understood. Displays of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroral</a> light have long been observed at high latitudes.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genesis">Genesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1724, <a href="/wiki/George_Graham_(clockmaker)" title="George Graham (clockmaker)">George Graham</a> reported that the needle of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_compass" title="Magnetic compass">magnetic compass</a> was regularly deflected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_north" title="Magnetic north">magnetic north</a> over the course of each day. This effect was eventually attributed to overhead electric currents flowing in the ionosphere and magnetosphere by <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Stewart" title="Balfour Stewart">Balfour Stewart</a> in 1882, and confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schuster" title="Arthur Schuster">Arthur Schuster</a> in 1889 from analysis of magnetic observatory data.\n</p><p>In 1852, astronomer and British major general <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sabine" title="Edward Sabine">Edward Sabine</a> showed that the probability of the occurrence of magnetic storms on Earth was correlated with the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a>, thus demonstrating a novel solar-terrestrial interaction. In 1859, a great <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">magnetic storm</a> caused brilliant auroral displays and disrupted global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> operations. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">Richard Carrington</a> correctly connected the storm with a <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flare</a> that he had observed the day before in the vicinity of a large sunspot group&#8212;thus demonstrating that specific solar events could affect the Earth.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">Kristian Birkeland</a> explained the physics of aurora by creating artificial aurora in his laboratory and predicted the solar wind. The introduction of radio revealed that periods of extreme static or noise occurred. Severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_jamming" title="Radar jamming">radar jamming</a> during a large solar event in 1942 led to the discovery of solar radio bursts (radio waves which cover a broad frequency range created by a solar flare), another aspect of space weather.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twentieth_century">Twentieth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Twentieth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 20th century the interest in space weather expanded as military and commercial systems came to depend on systems affected by space weather. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communications_satellites" title="Communications satellites">Communications satellites</a> are a vital part of global commerce. <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Weather satellite</a> systems provide information about terrestrial weather. The signals from satellites of the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> (GPS) are used in a wide variety of applications. Space weather phenomena can interfere with or damage these satellites or interfere with the radio signals with which they operate. Space weather phenomena can cause damaging surges in long distance <a href="/wiki/Overhead_power_line" title="Overhead power line">transmission lines</a> and expose passengers and crew of aircraft travel to <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> especially on polar routes.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a> (IGY) increased research into space weather. Ground-based data obtained during IGY demonstrated that the aurora occurred in an <i>auroral oval</i>, a permanent region of luminescence 15 to 25 degrees in latitude from the magnetic poles and 5 to 20 degrees wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1958, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer_I" title="Explorer I">Explorer I</a> satellite discovered the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen belts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> regions of radiation particles trapped by the Earth\'s magnetic field. In January 1959, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a> first directly observed the solar wind and measured its strength. A smaller <a href="/wiki/International_Heliophysical_Year" title="International Heliophysical Year">International Heliophysical Year</a> (IHY) occurred in 2007-2008.\n</p><p>In 1969, INJUN-5 (a.k.a. Explorer 40<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>) made the first direct observation of the electric field impressed on the Earth\'s high latitude ionosphere by the solar wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the early 1970s, Triad data demonstrated that permanent electric currents flowed between the auroral oval and the magnetosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term space weather came into usage in the late 1950s as the space age began and satellites began to measure the space environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-origin-2">[2]</a></sup> The term regained popularity in the 1990s along with the belief that space\'s impact on human systems demanded a more coordinated research and application framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nas1997_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nas1997-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_National_Space_Weather_Program">US National Space Weather Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: US National Space Weather Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The purpose of the US National Space Weather Program is to focus research on the needs of the affected commercial and military communities, to connect the research and user communities, to create coordination between operational data centers and to better define user community needs.\n</p><p>The concept was turned into an action plan in 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> an implementation plan in 2002, an assessment in 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and a revised strategic plan in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A revised action plan was scheduled to be released in 2011 followed by a revised implementation plan in 2012.\n</p><p>One part of the National Space Weather Program is to show users that space weather affects their business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nap.edu_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nap.edu-14">[14]</a></sup> Private companies now acknowledge space weather "is a real risk for today\'s businesses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phenomena">Phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Within the solar system, space weather is influenced by the solar wind and the <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">interplanetary magnetic field</a> (IMF) carried by the solar wind <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>. A variety of physical phenomena are associated with space weather, including <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">geomagnetic storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Substorm" title="Substorm">substorms</a>, energization of the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belts</a>, ionospheric disturbances and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(astronomy)" title="Scintillation (astronomy)">scintillation</a> of satellite-to-ground radio signals and long-range radar signals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurora</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current" title="Geomagnetically induced current">geomagnetically induced currents</a> at Earth\'s surface. <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">Coronal mass ejections</a> (CMEs), their associated <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coronal_cloud" title="Coronal cloud">coronal clouds</a> are also important drivers of space weather as they can compress the magnetosphere and trigger geomagnetic storms. <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">Solar energetic particles</a> (SEP) accelerated by coronal mass ejections or solar flares can trigger <a href="/wiki/Solar_particle_event" title="Solar particle event">solar particle events</a> (SPEs), a critical driver of human impact space weather as they can damage electronics onboard spacecraft (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_15" title="Galaxy 15">Galaxy 15</a> failure), and threaten the lives of <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> as well as increase radiation hazards to high-altitude, high-latitude aviation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_electronics">Spacecraft electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spacecraft electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1658" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg/480px-ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg/640px-ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ExtremeEvent_20031026-00h_20031106-24h.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>GOES-11 and GOES-12 monitored space weather conditions during the October 2003 solar activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Extreme_Space_Weather_Events_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Extreme_Space_Weather_Events-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Some spacecraft failures can be directly attributed to space weather; many more are thought to have a space weather component. For example, 46 of the 70 failures reported in 2003 occurred during the October 2003 geomagnetic storm. The two most common adverse space weather effects on spacecraft are <a href="/wiki/Radiation_damage" title="Radiation damage">radiation damage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_charging" title="Spacecraft charging">spacecraft charging</a>.\n</p><p>Radiation (high energy particles) passes through the skin of the spacecraft and into the electronic components. In most cases the radiation causes an erroneous signal or changes one bit in memory of a spacecraft\'s electronics (<a href="/wiki/Single_event_upset" title="Single event upset">single event upsets</a>). In a few cases, the radiation destroys a section of the electronics (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latchup" title="Latchup">single-event latchup</a>).\n</p><p>Spacecraft charging is the accumulation of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_charge" title="Electrostatic charge">electrostatic charge</a> on a non-conducting material on the spacecraft\'s surface by low energy particles. If enough charge is built up, a discharge (spark) occurs. This can cause an erroneous signal to be detected and acted on by the spacecraft computer. A recent study indicates that spacecraft charging is the predominant space weather effect on spacecraft in <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_orbit_changes">Spacecraft orbit changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spacecraft orbit changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The orbits of spacecraft in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO) decay to lower and lower altitudes due to the resistance from the friction between the spacecraft\'s surface (<i>i.e. </i>, drag) and the outer layer of the Earth\'s atmosphere (a.k.a. the thermosphere and exosphere). Eventually, a LEO spacecraft falls out of orbit and towards the Earth\'s surface. Many spacecraft launched in the past couple of decades have the ability to fire a small rocket to manage their orbits. The rocket can increase altitude to extend lifetime, to direct the reentry towards a particular (marine) site, or route the satellite to avoid collision with other spacecraft. Such maneuvers require precise information about the orbit. A geomagnetic storm can cause an orbit change over a couple of days that otherwise would occur over a year or more. The geomagnetic storm adds heat to the thermosphere, causing the thermosphere to expand and rise, increasing the drag on spacecraft. The <a href="/wiki/2009_satellite_collision" title="2009 satellite collision">2009 satellite collision</a> between the Iridium 33 and Cosmos 2251 demonstrated the importance of having precise knowledge of all objects in orbit. Iridium 33 had the capability to maneuver out of the path of Cosmos 2251 and could have evaded the crash, if a credible collision prediction had been available,\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans_in_space">Humans in space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Humans in space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_spaceflight_on_the_human_body" title="Effect of spaceflight on the human body">Effect of spaceflight on the human body</a></div>\n<p>The exposure of a human body to <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a> has the same <a href="/wiki/Acute_radiation_syndrome" title="Acute radiation syndrome">harmful effects</a> whether the source of the radiation is a medical <a href="/wiki/X-ray_machine" title="X-ray machine">X-ray machine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a> or radiation in space. The degree of the harmful effect depends on the length of exposure and the radiation\'s <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>. The ever-present <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_belt" title="Radiation belt">radiation belts</a> extend down to the altitude of manned spacecraft such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station#Radiation" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS) and the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, but the amount of exposure is within the <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight_radiation_carcinogenesis#Current_Permissible_Exposure_Limits" title="Spaceflight radiation carcinogenesis">acceptable lifetime exposure limit</a> under normal conditions. During a major space weather event that includes an SEP burst, the <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> can increase by orders of magnitude. Areas within ISS provide shielding that can keep the total dose within safe limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, such an event would have required immediate mission termination.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_systems">Ground systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ground systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SpaceWeather-artl.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2160" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/SpaceWeather-artl.jpg/220px-SpaceWeather-artl.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/SpaceWeather-artl.jpg/330px-SpaceWeather-artl.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/SpaceWeather-artl.jpg/440px-SpaceWeather-artl.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SpaceWeather-artl.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Art inspired from the concept of space weather</div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft_signals">Spacecraft signals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Spacecraft signals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The ionosphere bends radio waves in the same manner that water in a swimming pool bends visible light. When the medium through which such waves travel is disturbed, the light image or radio information is distorted and can become unrecognizable. The degree of distortion (scintillation) of a radio wave by the ionosphere depends on the signal frequency. Radio signals in the <a href="/wiki/Very_high_frequency" title="Very high frequency">VHF</a> band (30 to 300&#160;MHz) can be distorted beyond recognition by a disturbed ionosphere. Radio signals in the <a href="/wiki/Ultra_high_frequency" title="Ultra high frequency">UHF</a> band (300&#160;MHz to 3&#160;GHz) transit a disturbed ionosphere, but a receiver may not be able to keep locked to the carrier frequency. GPS uses signals at 1575.42&#160;MHz (L1) and 1227.6&#160;MHz (L2) that can be distorted by a disturbed ionosphere. Space weather events that corrupt GPS signals can significantly impact society. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Wide_Area_Augmentation_System" title="Wide Area Augmentation System">Wide Area Augmentation System</a> (WAAS) operated by the US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> (FAA) is used as a navigation tool for North American commercial aviation. It is disabled by every major space weather event. Outages can range from minutes to days. Major space weather events can push the disturbed polar ionosphere 10&#176; to 30&#176; of latitude toward the equator and can cause large ionospheric gradients (changes in density over distance of hundreds of km) at mid and low latitude. Both of these factors can distort GPS signals.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-distance_radio_signals">Long-distance radio signals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Long-distance radio signals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Radio wave in the <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">HF</a> band (3 to 30&#160;MHz) (also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> band) are reflected by the ionosphere. Since the ground also reflects HF waves, a signal can be transmitted around the curvature of the Earth beyond the line of sight. During the 20th century, HF communications was the only method for a ship or aircraft far from land or a base station to communicate. The advent of systems such as <a href="/wiki/Iridium_satellite_constellation" title="Iridium satellite constellation">Iridium</a> brought other methods of communications, but HF remains critical for vessels that do not carry the newer equipment and as a critical backup system for others. Space weather events can create irregularities in the ionosphere that scatter HF signals instead of reflecting them, preventing HF communications. At auroral and polar latitudes, small space weather events that occur frequently disrupt HF communications. At mid-latitudes, HF communications are disrupted by solar radio bursts, by X-rays from solar flares (which enhance and disturb the ionospheric D-layer) and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Electron_Content" title="Total Electron Content">TEC</a> enhancements and irregularities during major geomagnetic storms.\n</p><p>Trans<a href="/wiki/Polar_route" title="Polar route">polar airline routes</a> are particularly sensitive to space weather, in part because <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Regulations" title="Federal Aviation Regulations">Federal Aviation Regulations</a> require reliable communication over the entire flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Diverting such a flight is estimated to cost about $100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nap.edu_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nap.edu-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aviation_radiation_environment.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1943" data-file-width="897" decoding="async" height="477" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Aviation_radiation_environment.png/220px-Aviation_radiation_environment.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Aviation_radiation_environment.png/330px-Aviation_radiation_environment.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Aviation_radiation_environment.png/440px-Aviation_radiation_environment.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aviation_radiation_environment.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>All passengers in commercial aircraft flying above 26,000 feet will typically experience some exposure in this aviation radiation environment.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans_in_commercial_aviation">Humans in commercial aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Humans in commercial aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The magnetosphere guides cosmic ray and solar energetic particles to polar latitudes, while high energy charged particles enter the mesosphere, stratosphere, and troposphere. These energetic particles at the top of the atmosphere shatter atmospheric atoms and molecules, creating harmful lower energy particles that penetrate deep into the atmosphere and create measurable radiation. All aircraft flying above 8&#160;km (26,200 feet) altitude are exposed to these particles. The dose exposure is greater in polar regions than at mid-latitude and equatorial regions. Many commercial aircraft fly over the polar region. When a space weather event causes radiation exposure to exceed the safe level set by aviation authorities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the aircraft\'s flight path is diverted.\n</p><p>While the most significant, but highly unlikely, health consequences to atmospheric radiation exposure include death from cancer due to long-term exposure, many lifestyle-degrading and career-impacting cancer forms can also occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A cancer diagnosis can have significant career impact for a commercial pilot. A cancer diagnosis can ground a pilot temporarily or permanently. International guidelines from the International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) have been developed to mitigate this statistical risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The ICRP recommends effective dose limits of a 5-year average of 20 <a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">mSv</a> per year with no more than 50&#160;mSv in a single year for non-pregnant, occupationally exposed persons, and 1&#160;mSv per year for the general public. Radiation dose limits are not engineering limits. In the U.S., they are treated as an upper limit of acceptability and not a regulatory limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Measurements of the radiation environment at commercial aircraft altitudes above 8&#160;km (26,000&#160;ft) have historically been done by instruments that record the data on board where the data are then processed later on the ground. However, a system of real-time radiation measurements on-board aircraft has been developed through the NASA Automated Radiation Measurements for Aerospace Safety (ARMAS) program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="http://sol.spacenvironment.net/~ARMAS/" rel="nofollow">ARMAS</a> has flown hundreds of flights since 2013, mostly on research aircraft, and sent the data to the ground through Iridium satellite links. The eventual goal of these types of measurements is to data assimilate them into physics-based global radiation models, e.g., NASA\'s Nowcast of Atmospheric Ionizing Radiation System (<a class="external text" href="http://sol.spacenvironment.net/~nairas/index.html" rel="nofollow">NAIRAS</a>), so as to provide the weather of the radiation environment rather than the climatology.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground-induced_electric_fields">Ground-induced electric fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ground-induced electric fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">Magnetic storm activity</a> can induce geoelectric fields in the Earth\'s conducting <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Corresponding voltage differentials can <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current" title="Geomagnetically induced current">find their way into electric power grids through ground connections</a>, driving uncontrolled electric currents that interfere with grid operation, damage transformers, trip protective relays and sometimes cause blackouts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This complicated chain of causes and effects was demonstrated during <a href="/wiki/March_1989_geomagnetic_storm" title="March 1989 geomagnetic storm">the magnetic storm of March 1989</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which caused the complete collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Qu&#233;bec">Hydro-Qu&#233;bec</a> electric-power grid in Canada, temporarily leaving nine million people without electricity. The possible occurrence of an even more intense storm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> led to operational standards intended to mitigate induction-hazard risks, while <a href="/wiki/Reinsurance" title="Reinsurance">reinsurance</a> companies commissioned revised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk_assessments" title="Risk assessments">risk assessments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysical_exploration">Geophysical exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Geophysical exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Air- and ship-borne <a href="/wiki/Aeromagnetic_survey" title="Aeromagnetic survey">magnetic surveys</a> can be affected by rapid magnetic field variations during geomagnetic storms. Such storms cause data interpretation problems because the space-weather-related magnetic field changes are similar in magnitude to those of the sub-surface crustal magnetic field in the survey area. Accurate geomagnetic storm warnings, including an assessment of storm magnitude and duration allows for an economic use of survey equipment.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysics_and_hydrocarbon_production">Geophysics and hydrocarbon production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geophysics and hydrocarbon production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For economic and other reasons, oil and gas production often involves <a href="/wiki/Directional_drilling" title="Directional drilling">horizontal drilling</a> of well paths many kilometers from a single wellhead. Accuracy requirements are strict, due to target size &#8211; reservoirs may only be a few tens to hundreds of meters across &#8211; and safety, because of the proximity of other boreholes. The most accurate gyroscopic method is expensive, since it can stop drilling for hours. An alternative is to use a magnetic survey, which enables <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MWD_(measurement_while_drilling)" title="MWD (measurement while drilling)">measurement while drilling (MWD)</a>. Near real-time magnetic data can be used to correct drilling direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Magnetic data and space weather forecasts can help to clarify unknown sources of drilling error.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_weather">Terrestrial weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Terrestrial weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The amount of energy entering the troposphere and stratosphere from space weather phenomena is trivial compared to the solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> in the visible and infra-red portions of the solar electromagnetic spectrum. However, some linkage between the 11-year sunspot cycle and the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> has been claimed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maunder_minimum" title="Maunder minimum">Maunder minimum</a>, a 70-year period almost devoid of sunspots, allegedly correlated to a cooler climate. One suggestion for the linkage between space and terrestrial weather is that changes in cosmic ray flux cause changes in the amount of cloud formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Another suggestion is that variations in the EUV flux subtly influence existing drivers of the climate and tip the balance between <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni&#241;o">El Ni&#241;o</a>/<a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Ni&#241;a">La Ni&#241;a</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, a linkage between space weather and the climate has not been demonstrated conclusively.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation">Observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Observation of space weather is done both for scientific research and for applications. Scientific observation has evolved with the state of knowledge, while application-related observation expanded with the ability to exploit such data.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground-based">Ground-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ground-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Space weather is monitored at ground level by observing changes in the Earth\'s magnetic field over periods of seconds to days, by observing the surface of the Sun and by observing radio noise created in the Sun\'s atmosphere.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">Sunspot Number</a> (SSN) is the number of <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a> on the Sun\'s photosphere in visible light on the side of the Sun visible to an Earth observer. The number and total area of sunspots are related to the brightness of the Sun in the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a> (EUV) and X-ray portions of the <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">solar spectrum</a> and to solar activity such as solar flares and coronal mass ejections (CMEs).\n</p><p>10.7&#160;cm radio flux (F10.7) is a measurement of RF emissions from the Sun and is approximately correlated with the solar EUV flux. Since this RF emission is easily obtained from the ground and EUV flux is not, this value has been measured and disseminated continuously since 1947. The world standard measurements are made by the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Radio_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Dominion Radio Astrophysical Observatory">Dominion Radio Astrophysical Observatory</a> at Penticton, B.C., Canada and reported once a day at local noon<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> in solar flux units (10<sup>&#8722;22</sup>J&#183;m<sup>&#8722;2</sup>). F10.7 is archived by the National Geophysical Data Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Fundamental space weather monitoring data are provided by ground-based magnetometers and magnetic observatories. Magnetic storms were first discovered by ground-based measurement of occasional magnetic disturbance. Ground magnetometer data provide real-time situational awareness for post-event analysis. Magnetic observatories have been in continuous operations for decades to centuries, providing data to inform studies of long-term changes in space climatology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Disturbance_storm_time_index" title="Disturbance storm time index">Dst index</a> is an estimate of the magnetic field change at the Earth\'s magnetic equator due to a ring of electric current at and just earthward of the <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The index is based on data from four ground-based magnetic observatories between 21&#176; and 33&#176; <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_latitude" title="Geomagnetic latitude">magnetic latitude</a> during a one-hour period. Stations closer to the magnetic equator are not used due to ionospheric effects. The Dst index is compiled and archived by the World Data Center for Geomagnetism, Kyoto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kyoto_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-kyoto-43">[43]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/K-index" title="K-index">Kp/ap</a> Index: \'a\' is an index created from the geomagnetic disturbance at one mid-latitude (40&#176; to 50&#176; latitude) geomagnetic observatory during a 3-hour period. \'K\' is the quasi-logarithmic counterpart of the \'a\' index. Kp and ap are the average of K and an over 13 geomagnetic observatories to represent planetary-wide geomagnetic disturbances. The Kp/ap index<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> indicates both geomagnetic storms and substorms (auroral disturbance). Kp/ap is available from 1932 onward.\n</p><p>AE index is compiled from geomagnetic disturbances at 12 geomagnetic observatories in and near the auroral zones and is recorded at 1-minute intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kyoto_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-kyoto-43">[43]</a></sup> The public AE index is available with a lag of two to three days that limits its utility for space weather applications. The AE index indicates the intensity of geomagnetic substorms except during a major geomagnetic storm when the auroral zones expand equatorward from the observatories.\n</p><p>Radio noise bursts are reported by the Radio Solar Telescope Network to the U.S. Air Force and to NOAA. The radio bursts are associated with solar flare plasma that interacts with the ambient solar atmosphere.\n</p><p>The Sun\'s photosphere is observed continuously<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> for activity that can be the precursors to solar flares and CMEs. The Global Oscillation Network Group (GONG)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> project monitors both the surface and the interior of the Sun by using <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">helioseismology</a>, the study of sound waves propagating through the Sun and observed as ripples on the solar surface. GONG can detect sunspot groups on the far side of the Sun. This ability has recently been verified by visual observations from the <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a> spacecraft.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Neutron_monitor" title="Neutron monitor">Neutron monitors</a> on the ground indirectly monitor cosmic rays from the Sun and galactic sources. When cosmic rays interact with the atmosphere, atomic interactions occur that cause a shower of lower energy particles to descend into the atmosphere and to ground level. The presence of cosmic rays in the near-Earth space environment can be detected by monitoring high energy neutrons at ground level. Small fluxes of cosmic rays are present continuously. Large fluxes are produced by the Sun during events related to energetic solar flares.\n</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Electron_Content" title="Total Electron Content">Total Electron Content</a> (TEC) is a measure of the ionosphere over a given location. TEC is the number of electrons in a column one meter square from the base of the ionosphere (approximately 90&#160;km altitude) to the top of the ionosphere (approximately 1000&#160;km altitude). Many TEC measurements are made by monitoring the two frequencies transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> spacecraft. Presently GPS TEC is monitored and distributed in real time from more than 360 stations maintained by agencies in many countries.\n</p><p>Geoeffectiveness is a measure of how strongly space weather magnetic fields, such as coronal mass ejections, couple with the Earth\'s magnetic field. This is determined by the direction of the magnetic field held within the plasma that originates from the Sun. New techniques measuring <a href="/wiki/Faraday_effect" title="Faraday effect">Faraday Rotation</a> in radio waves are in development to measure field direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite-based">Satellite-based</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Satellite-based">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A host of research spacecraft have explored space weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PfaffBorovsky1998_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-PfaffBorovsky1998-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Orbiting_Geophysical_Observatory" title="Orbiting Geophysical Observatory">Orbiting Geophysical Observatory</a> series were among the first spacecraft with the mission of analyzing the space environment. Recent spacecraft include the NASA-ESA Solar-Terrestrial Relations Observatory (STEREO) pair of spacecraft launched in 2006 into solar orbit and the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes" title="Van Allen Probes">Van Allen Probes</a>, launched in 2012 into a highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical" title="Elliptical">elliptical</a> Earth-orbit. The two STEREO spacecraft drift away from the Earth by about 22&#176; per year, one leading and the other trailing the Earth in its orbit. Together they compile information about the solar surface and atmosphere in three dimensions. The Van Allen probes record detailed information about the radiation belts, geomagnetic storms and the relationship between the two.\n</p><p>Some spacecraft with other primary missions have carried auxiliary instruments for solar observation. Among the earliest such spacecraft were the <a href="/wiki/Applications_Technology_Satellite" title="Applications Technology Satellite">Applications Technology Satellite</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> (ATS) series at GEO that were precursors to the modern <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Operational_Environmental_Satellite" title="Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite">Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite</a> (GOES) weather satellite and many communication satellites. The ATS spacecraft carried environmental particle sensors as auxiliary payloads and had their navigational magnetic field sensor used for sensing the environment.\n</p><p>Many of the early instruments were research spacecraft that were re-purposed for space weather applications. One of the first of these was the IMP-8 (Interplanetary Monitoring Platform)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It orbited the Earth at 35 Earth Radii and observed the solar wind for two-thirds of its 12-day orbits from 1973 to 2006. Since the solar wind carries disturbances that affect the magnetosphere and ionosphere, IMP-8 demonstrated the utility of continuous solar wind monitoring. IMP-8 was followed by <a href="/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer" title="International Cometary Explorer">ISEE-3</a>, which was placed near the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>-<a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">Lagrangian point</a>, 235 Earth radii above the surface (about 1.5 million km, or 924,000 miles) and continuously monitored the solar wind from 1978 to 1982. The next spacecraft to monitor the solar wind at the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> point was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">WIND</a> from 1994 to 1998. After April 1998, the WIND spacecraft orbit was changed to circle the Earth and occasionally pass the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> point. The NASA <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">Advanced Composition Explorer</a> (ACE) has monitored the solar wind at the <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">L<sub>1</sub></a> point from 1997 to present.\n</p><p>In addition to monitoring the solar wind, monitoring the Sun is important to space weather. Because the solar EUV cannot be monitored from the ground, the joint <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">Solar and Heliospheric Observatory</a> (SOHO) spacecraft was launched and has provided solar EUV images beginning in 1995. SOHO is a main source of near-real time solar data for both research and space weather prediction and inspired the <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a> mission. The <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">Yohkoh</a> spacecraft at LEO observed the Sun from 1991 to 2001 in the X-ray portion of the solar spectrum and was useful for both research and space weather prediction. Data from Yohkoh inspired the <a href="/wiki/Solar_X-ray_Imager" title="Solar X-ray Imager">Solar X-ray Imager</a> on GOES.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ExtremeEvent_19891018-00h_19891031-24h.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1658" data-file-width="2144" decoding="async" applications include the <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Operational_Environmental_Satellite" title="Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite">Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite</a> (GOES) series of spacecraft, the <a href="/wiki/Polar_Operational_Environmental_Satellites" title="Polar Operational Environmental Satellites">POES</a> series, the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Meteorological_Satellite_Program" title="Defense Meteorological Satellite Program">DMSP</a> series, and the <a href="/wiki/Meteosat" title="Meteosat">Meteosat</a> series. The GOES spacecraft have carried an X-ray sensor (XRS) which measures the flux from the whole solar disk in two bands &#8211; 0.05 to 0.4&#160;nm and 0.1 to 0.8&#160;nm &#8211; since 1974, an X-ray imager (SXI) since 2004, a magnetometer which measures the distortions of the Earth\'s magnetic field due to space weather, a whole disk <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">EUV</a> sensor since 2004, and particle sensors (EPS/HEPAD) which measure ions and electrons in the energy range of 50 keV to 500 MeV. Starting sometime after 2015, the GOES-R generation of GOES spacecraft will replace the SXI with a solar EUV image (SUVI) similar to the one on <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a> and <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">STEREO</a> and the particle sensor will be augmented with a component to extend the energy range down to 30&#160;eV.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory" title="Deep Space Climate Observatory">Deep Space Climate Observatory</a> (DSCOVR) satellite is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Earth observation and space weather satellite that launched in February 2015. Among its features is advance warning of coronal mass ejections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Space weather models are simulations of the space weather environment. Models use sets of mathematical equations to describe physical processes.\n</p><p>These models take a limited data set and attempt to describe all or part of the space weather environment in or to predict how weather evolves over time. Early models were heuristic; i.e<i>., </i> they did not directly employ physics. These models take less resources than their more sophisticated descendants.\n</p><p>Later models use physics to account for as many phenomena as possible. No model can yet reliably predict the environment from the surface of the Sun to the bottom of the Earth\'s ionosphere. Space weather models differ from meteorological models in that the amount of input is vastly smaller.\n</p><p>A significant portion of space weather model research and development in the past two decades has been done as part of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_space#Geospace" title="Outer space">Geospace</a> Environmental Model (GEM) program of the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>. The two major modeling centers are the Center for Space Environment Modeling (CSEM)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and the Center for Integrated Space weather Modeling (CISM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Community_Coordinated_Modeling_Center" title="Community Coordinated Modeling Center">Community Coordinated Modeling Center</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> (CCMC) at the NASA <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> is a facility for coordinating the development and testing of research models, for improving and preparing models for use in space weather prediction and application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Modeling techniques include (a) <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">magnetohydrodynamics</a>, in which the environment is treated as a fluid, (b) particle in cell, in which non-fluid interactions are handled within a cell and then cells are connected to describe the environment, (c) first principles, in which physical processes are in balance (or equilibrium) with one another, (d) semi-static modeling, in which a statistical or empirical relationship is described, or a combination of multiple methods.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_space_weather_development">Commercial space weather development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Commercial space weather development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the first decade of the 21st Century, a commercial sector emerged that engaged in space weather, serving agency, academia, commercial and consumer sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Space weather providers are typically smaller companies, or small divisions within a larger company, that provide space weather data, models, derivative products and service distribution.\n</p><p>The commercial sector includes scientific and engineering researchers as well as users. Activities are primarily directed toward the impacts of space weather upon technology. These include, for example: \n</p>\n<ul><li>Atmospheric drag on LEO satellites caused by energy inputs into the thermosphere from solar UV, FUV, <a href="/wiki/Lyman_series" title="Lyman series">Lyman-alpha</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EUV" title="EUV">EUV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">XUV</a>, X-ray, and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> photons as well as by charged particle precipitation and <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">Joule heating</a> at high latitudes;</li>\n<li>Surface and internal charging from increased energetic particle fluxes, leading to effects such as discharges, single event upsets and latch-up, on LEO to GEO satellites;</li>\n<li>Disrupted GPS signals caused by ionospheric scintillation leading to increased uncertainty in navigation systems such as aviation\'s <a href="/wiki/Wide_Area_Augmentation_System" title="Wide Area Augmentation System">Wide Area Augmentation System</a> (WAAS);</li>\n<li>Lost HF, UHF and L-band radio communications due to ionosphere scintillation, solar flares and geomagnetic storms;</li>\n<li>Increased radiation to human tissue and avionics from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_cosmic_rays" title="Galactic cosmic rays">galactic cosmic rays</a> SEP, especially during large solar flares, and possibly bremsstrahlung gamma-rays produced by precipitating radiation belt energetic electrons at altitudes above 8&#160;km;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Increased inaccuracy in surveying and oil/gas exploration that uses the Earth\'s main magnetic field when it is disturbed by geomagnetic storms;</li>\n<li>Loss of power transmission from GIC surges in the electrical power grid and transformer shutdowns during large geomagnetic storms.</li></ul>\n<p>Many of these disturbances result in societal impacts that account for a significant part of the national GDP.\n</p><p>The concept of incentivizing commercial space weather was first suggested by the idea of a Space Weather Economic Innovation Zone discussed by the American Commercial Space Weather Association (ACSWA) in 2015. The establishment of this economic innovation zone would encourage expanded economic activity developing applications to manage the risks space weather and would encourage broader research activities related to space weather by universities. It could encourage U.S. business investment in space weather services and products. It promoted the support of U.S. business innovation in space weather services and products by requiring U.S. government purchases of U.S. built commercial hardware, software, and associated products and services where no suitable government capability pre-exists. It also promoted U.S. built commercial hardware, software, and associated products and services sales to international partners. designate U.S. built commercial hardware, services, and products as &#8220;Space Weather Economic Innovation Zone&#8221; activities; Finally, it recommended that U.S. built commercial hardware, services, and products be tracked as Space Weather Economic Innovation Zone contributions within agency reports. In 2015 the U.S. Congress bill HR1561 provided groundwork where social and environmental impacts from a Space Weather Economic Innovation Zone could be far-reaching. In 2016, the Space Weather Research and Forecasting Act (S. 2817) was introduced to build on that legacy. Later, in 2017-2018 the HR3086 Bill took these concepts, included the breadth of material from parallel agency studies as part of the OSTP-sponsored Space Weather Action Program (SWAP)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>, and with bicameral and bipartisan support the 116th Congress (2019) is considering passage of the Space Weather Coordination Act (S141, 115th Congress).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Commercial_Space_Weather_Association">American Commercial Space Weather Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_weather&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: American Commercial Space Weather Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On April 29, 2010, the commercial space weather community created the American Commercial Space Weather Association (<a class="external text" href="http://acswa.us" rel="nofollow">ACSWA</a>) an industry association. ACSWA promotes space weather risk mitigation for national infrastructure, economic strength and national security. It seeks to:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>provide quality space weather data and services to help mitigate risks to technology;</li>\n<li>provide advisory services to government agencies;</li>\n<li>provide guidance on the best task division between commercial providers and government agencies;</li>\n<li>represent the interests of commercial providers;</li>\n<li>represent commercial capabilities in the national and international arena;</li>\n<li>develop best-practices.</li></ul>\n<p>A summary of the broad technical capabilities in space weather that are available from the association can be found on their web site <a class="external free" href="http://www.acswa.us" rel="nofollow">http://www.acswa.us</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_events">Notable events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
