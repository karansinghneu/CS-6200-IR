title="Enlarge"></a></div>White <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> is <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersed</a> by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangular_prism_(optics)" title="Triangular prism (optics)">prism</a> into the colors of the visible spectrum.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>visible spectrum</b> is the portion of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visible</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">Electromagnetic radiation</a> in this range of <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> is called <b>visible light</b> or simply <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>. A typical <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a> will respond to wavelengths from about 380 to 740 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometers" title="Nanometers">nanometers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In terms of frequency, this corresponds to a band in the vicinity of 430&#8211;770 <a href="/wiki/Hertz#SI_prefixed_forms_of_hertz" title="Hertz">THz</a>.\n</p><p>The spectrum does not contain all the <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">colors</a> that the human eyes and brain can distinguish. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excitation_purity" title="Excitation purity">Unsaturated colors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a> variations like <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">magenta</a>, for example, are absent because they can only be made from a mix of multiple wavelengths. Colors containing only one wavelength are also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excitation_purity" title="Excitation purity">pure colors</a> or spectral colors.\n</p><p>Visible wavelengths pass largely unattenuated through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> via the "<a href="/wiki/Optical_window" title="Optical window">optical window</a>" region of the electromagnetic spectrum. An example of this phenomenon is when clean air <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scatters</a> blue light more than red light, and so the midday sky appears blue. The optical window is also referred to as the "visible window" because it overlaps the human visible response spectrum. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_infrared" title="Near infrared">near infrared</a> (NIR) window lies just out of the human vision, as well as the medium wavelength infrared (MWIR) window, and the long wavelength or far infrared (LWIR or FIR) window, although other animals may experience them.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newton%27s_color_circle.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="810" data-file-width="788" decoding="async" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Newton%27s_color_circle.png/220px-Newton%27s_color_circle.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Newton%27s_color_circle.png/330px-Newton%27s_color_circle.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Newton%27s_color_circle.png/440px-Newton%27s_color_circle.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Newton%27s_color_circle.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Newton\'s color circle, from <i>Opticks</i> of 1704, showing the colors he associated with <a href="/wiki/Musical_note" title="Musical note">musical notes</a>. The spectral colors from red to violet are divided by the notes of the musical scale, starting at D. The circle completes a full <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octave</a>, from D to D. Newton\'s circle places red, at one end of the spectrum, next to violet, at the other. This reflects the fact that non-spectral <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a> colors are observed when red and violet light are mixed.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a> theorized that <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbows</a> were produced by a similar process to the passage of light through glass or crystal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> discovered that prisms could disassemble and reassemble white light, and described the phenomenon in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Opticks" title="Opticks">Opticks</a></i>. He was the first to use the word <i>spectrum</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "appearance" or "apparition") in this sense in print in 1671 in describing his <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>. Newton observed that, when a narrow beam of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> strikes the face of a glass <a href="/wiki/Dispersive_prism" title="Dispersive prism">prism</a> at an angle, some is <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected</a> and some of the beam passes into and through the glass, emerging as different-colored bands. Newton hypothesized light to be made up of "corpuscles" (particles) of different colors, with the different colors of light moving at different speeds in transparent matter, red light moving more quickly than violet in glass. The result is that red light is bent (<a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a>) less sharply than violet as it passes through the prism, creating a spectrum of colors.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="701" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG/350px-Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG/525px-Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG/700px-Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Newton_prismatic_colours.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Newton\'s observation of prismatic colors (<a href="/wiki/David_Brewster" title="David Brewster">David Brewster</a> 1855)</div></div></div><p> Newton originally divided the spectrum into six named colors: <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_(color)" title="Orange (color)">orange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Violet_(color)" title="Violet (color)">violet</a>. He later added <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> as the seventh color since he believed that seven was a perfect number as derived from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greek</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophism" title="Sophism">sophists</a>, of there being a connection between the colors, the musical notes, the known objects in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, and the days of the week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isacoff2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isacoff2009-3">[3]</a></sup> The human eye is relatively insensitive to indigo\'s frequencies, and some people who have otherwise-good vision cannot distinguish indigo from blue and violet. For this reason, some later commentators, including <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> have suggested that indigo should not be regarded as a color in its own right but merely as a shade of blue or violet. However, the evidence indicates that what Newton meant by "indigo" and "blue" does not correspond to the modern meanings of those color words. Comparing Newton\'s observation of prismatic colors to a color image of the visible light spectrum shows that "indigo" corresponds to what is today called blue, whereas "blue" corresponds to <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 18th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang Goethe">Goethe</a> wrote about optical spectra in his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_Colours_(book)" title="Theory of Colours (book)">Theory of Colours</a></i>. Goethe used the word <i>spectrum</i> (<i>Spektrum</i>) to designate a ghostly optical <a href="/wiki/Afterimage" title="Afterimage">afterimage</a>, as did <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Schopenhauer</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/On_Vision_and_Colors" title="On Vision and Colors">On Vision and Colors</a></i>. Goethe argued that the continuous spectrum was a compound phenomenon. Where Newton narrowed the beam of light to isolate the phenomenon, Goethe observed that a wider aperture produces not a spectrum but rather reddish-yellow and blue-cyan edges with <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a> between them. The spectrum appears only when these edges are close enough to overlap.\n</p><p>In the early 19th century, the concept of the visible spectrum became more definite, as light outside the visible range was discovered and characterized by <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm_Ritter" title="Johann Wilhelm Ritter">Johann Wilhelm Ritter</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>), <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist)" title="Thomas Young (scientist)">Thomas Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Johann_Seebeck" title="Thomas Johann Seebeck">Thomas Johann Seebeck</a>, and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\nYoung was the first to measure the wavelengths of different colors of light, in 1802.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The connection between the visible spectrum and <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">color vision</a> was explored by Thomas Young and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">Hermann von Helmholtz</a> in the early 19th century. Their <a href="/wiki/Young%E2%80%93Helmholtz_theory" title="Young&#8211;Helmholtz theory">theory of color vision</a> correctly proposed that the eye uses three distinct receptors to perceive color.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_color_vision">Animal color vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visible_spectrum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Animal color vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Color_vision#Physiology_of_color_perception" title="Color vision">Color vision &#167;&#160;Physiology of color perception</a></div>\n<p>Many species can see light within frequencies outside the human "visible spectrum". <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">Bees</a> and many other insects can detect ultraviolet light, which helps them find <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nectar_(plant)" title="Nectar (plant)">nectar</a> in flowers. Plant species that depend on insect pollination may owe reproductive success to their appearance in ultraviolet light rather than how colorful they appear to humans. Birds, too, can see into the ultraviolet (300&#8211;400&#160;nm), and some have sex-dependent markings on their plumage that are visible only in the ultraviolet range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Many animals that can see into the ultraviolet range, however, cannot see red light or any other reddish wavelengths. Bees\' visible spectrum ends at about 590&#160;nm, just before the orange wavelengths start.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Birds, however, can see some red wavelengths, although not as far into the light spectrum as humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Varela_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Varela-13">[13]</a></sup> The popular belief that the common goldfish is the only animal that can see both infrared and ultraviolet light <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> is incorrect, because goldfish cannot see infrared light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Similarly, dogs are often thought to be color blind but they have been shown to be sensitive to colors, though not as many as humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Some snakes can "see"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a Spectroscopy is an important investigative tool in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, where scientists use it to analyze the properties of distant objects. Typically, <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">astronomical spectroscopy</a> uses high-dispersion <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">diffraction gratings</a> to observe spectra at very high spectral resolutions. <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">Helium</a> was first detected by analysis of the spectrum of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">Chemical elements</a> can be detected in astronomical objects by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_line" title="Absorption line">absorption lines</a>.\n</p><p>The shifting of spectral lines can be used to measure the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">Doppler shift</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_shift" title="Red shift">red shift</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_shift" title="Blue shift">blue shift</a>) of distant objects.\n</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
