This occurs when the magnetic field of an astrophysical object interacts with the nearby flowing ambient <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>. For example, when the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, flowing with a relative speed of order 400&#160;km/s, encounters the magnetic field of Earth, a bow shape boundary forms. For Earth and other magnetized planets, it is the boundary at which the speed of the stellar wind abruptly drops as a result of its approach to the <a href="/wiki/Magnetopause" title="Magnetopause">magnetopause</a>. For stars, this boundary is typically the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrosphere" title="Astrosphere">astrosphere</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar wind</a> meets the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a For instance, the charged particles making up the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> follow spiral paths along magnetic field lines. The velocity of each particle as it gyrates around a field line can be treated similarly to a thermal velocity in an ordinary gas, and in an ordinary gas, the mean thermal velocity is roughly the speed of sound. At the bow shock, the bulk forward velocity of the wind (which is the component of the velocity parallel to the field lines about which the particles gyrate) drops below the speed at which the particles are gyrating.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_Earth">Around the Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Around the Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The best-studied example of a bow shock is that occurring where the Sun\'s wind encounters <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s magnetopause, although bow shocks occur around all planets, both unmagnetized, such as \n<a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and magnetized, such as <a href="/wiki/Jupiter#Magnetosphere" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Saturn#Magnetosphere" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Earth\'s bow shock is about 17 kilometres (11&#160;mi) thick<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and located about 90,000 kilometres (56,000&#160;mi) from the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_comets">At comets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: At comets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bow shocks form at <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> as a result of the interaction between the solar wind and the cometary ionosphere. Far away from the Sun, a comet is an icy boulder without an atmosphere. As it approaches the Sun, the heat of the sunlight causes gas to be released from the cometary nucleus, creating an atmosphere called a <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a>. The coma is partially ionized by the sunlight, and when the solar wind passes through this ion coma, the bow shock appears.\n</p><p>The first observations were made in the 1980s and 90s as several spacecraft flew by comets <a href="/wiki/21P/Giacobini%E2%80%93Zinner" title="21P/Giacobini&#8211;Zinner">21P/Giacobini&#8211;Zinner</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley\'s Comet">1P/Halley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/26P/Grigg%E2%80%93Skjellerup" title="26P/Grigg&#8211;Skjellerup">26P/Grigg&#8211;Skjellerup</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was then found that the bow shocks at comets are wider and more gradual than the sharp planetary bow shocks seen at for example Earth. These observations were all made near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Perihelion and aphelion">perihelion</a> when the bow shocks already were fully developed.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)">Rosetta</a> spacecraft followed comet <a href="/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko" title="67P/Churyumov&#8211;Gerasimenko">67P/Churyumov&#8211;Gerasimenko</a> from far out in the solar system, at a heliocentric distance of 3.6 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a>, in toward perihelion at 1.24 AU, and back out again. This allowed Rosetta to observe the bow shock as it formed when the outgassing increased during the comet\'s journey toward the Sun. In this early state of development the shock was called the "infant bow shock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The infant bow shock is asymmetric and, relative to the distance to the nucleus, wider than fully developed bow shocks.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_Sun">Around the Sun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_shocks_in_astrophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Around the Sun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere#Bow_shock" title="Heliosphere">Heliosphere &#167;&#160;Bow shock</a></div>\n<p>For several decades, the solar wind has been thought to form a bow shock at the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">heliosphere</a>, where it collides with the surrounding interstellar medium. Moving away from the Sun, the point where the solar wind flow becomes subsonic is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Termination_shock" title="Termination shock">termination shock</a>, the point where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">heliopause</a>, and the point where the flow of the interstellar medium becomes subsonic would be the bow shock. This solar bow shock was thought to lie at a distance around 230 AU<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> from the Sun &#8211; more than twice the distance of the termination shock as encountered by the Voyager spacecraft.\n</p><p>However, data obtained in 2012 from NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer" title="Interstellar Boundary Explorer">Interstellar Boundary Explorer</a> (IBEX) indicates the lack of any solar bow shock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bow_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-bow-13">[13]</a></sup> Along with corroborating results from the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager spacecraft</a>, these findings have motivated some theoretical refinements; current thinking is that happens when the solar wind reaches the ionosphere of Venus:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the flow deflects around the object draping the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> along the wake flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The condition for the flow to be super-Alfvenic means that the relative velocity between the flow and object, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: For unmagnetized and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrically_conductive" title="Electrically conductive">electrically conductive</a> objects, the ambient field creates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_currents" title="Electric currents">electric currents</a> inside the object, and into the surrounding plasma, such that the flow is deflected and slowed down as the time scale of magnetic <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> is much longer than the time scale of magnetic field <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>. The induced currents in turn generate magnetic fields that deflect the flow creating a bow shock. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionospheres</a> of Mars and Venus provide the conductive environments for the interaction with the solar wind. The latter occurs, for example, when the solar wind interacts with <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> which has no ionosphere. In magnetic draping, the field lines are wrapped and draped around the leading side of the object creating a narrow sheath which is similar to the bow shocks in the planetary magnetospheres.
