15">Apollo 15</a> lunar mission in 1971.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>sample-return mission</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> mission with the goal of collecting and returning samples from an extraterrestrial location to Earth for analysis. Sample-return missions may bring back merely atoms and molecules or a deposit of complex compounds such as loose material ("soil") and rocks. These samples may be obtained in a number of ways, such as soil and rock excavation or a collector array used for capturing particles of solar wind or cometary debris.\n</p><p>To date, samples of <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">Moon rock</a> from Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> have been collected by robotic and crewed missions, the comet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wild_2" title="Wild 2">Wild 2</a> and the asteroid <a href="/wiki/25143_Itokawa" title="25143 Itokawa">25143 Itokawa</a> have  been visited by a robotic spacecraft which returned samples to Earth, and samples of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> have been returned by a robotic mission.\n</p><p>In addition to sample-return missions, samples from three identified non-terrestrial bodies have been collected by means other than sample-return missions: samples from the Moon in the form of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_meteorite" title="Lunar meteorite">Lunar meteorites</a>, samples from <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">Martian meteorites</a>, and samples from <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">Vesta</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/HED_meteorite" title="HED meteorite">HED meteorites</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="Scientific_use">Scientific use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bilanga_meteorite,_4.3g_slice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3200" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/220px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/330px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg/440px-Bilanga_meteorite%2C_4.3g_slice.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bilanga_meteorite,_4.3g_slice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A meteorite from Vesta that fell on Africa</div></div></div>\n<p>Samples available on Earth can be analyzed in <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratories</a>, so we can further our understanding and knowledge as part of the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_and_exploration_of_the_Solar_System" title="Discovery and exploration of the Solar System">discovery and exploration of the Solar System</a>. Until now many important scientific discoveries about the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> were made remotely with <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a>, and some Solar System bodies were visited by orbiting or even landing spacecraft with instruments capable of <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> or sample analysis. While such an investigation of the Solar System is technically easier than a sample-return mission, the scientific tools available on Earth to study such samples are far more advanced and diverse than those that can go on spacecraft. Analysis of samples on Earth allows to follow up any findings with different tools, including tools that have yet to be developed; in contrast, a spacecraft can carry only a limited set of analytic tools, and these have to be chosen and built long before launch.\n</p><p>Samples analyzed on Earth can be matched against findings of remote sensing, for more insight into the <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">processes that formed the Solar System</a>. This was done, for example, with findings by the <a href="/wiki/Dawn_(spacecraft)" title="Dawn (spacecraft)"><i>Dawn</i> spacecraft</a>, which visited the asteroid Vesta from 2011 to 2012 for imaging, and samples from <a href="/wiki/HED_meteorite" title="HED meteorite">HED meteorites</a> (collected on Earth until then), which were compared to data gathered by Dawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These meteorites could then be identified as material ejected from the large impact crater <a href="/wiki/Rheasilvia" title="Rheasilvia">Rheasilvia</a> on Vesta. This allowed deducing the composition of crust, <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and core of Vesta. Similarly some <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_spectral_types" title="Asteroid spectral types">differences in composition of asteroids</a> (and, to a lesser extent, different compositions of <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>) can be discerned by imaging alone. However, for a more precise inventory of the material on these different bodies, more samples will be collected and returned in the future, to match their compositions with the data gathered through telescopes and <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">astronomical spectroscopy</a>.\n</p><p>One further focus of such investigation&#8212;besides the basic composition and <a href="/wiki/Historical_geology" title="Historical geology">geologic history</a> of the various Solar System bodies&#8212;is the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">building blocks of life</a> on comets, asteroids, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> or the moons of the <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>. Several sample-return missions to asteroids and comets are currently in the works. More samples from asteroids and comets will help determine whether life formed in space and was carried to Earth by meteorites. Another question under investigation is whether <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">extraterrestrial life</a> formed on other Solar System bodies like <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> or on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability" title="Natural satellite habitability">the moons of the gas giants</a>, and whether life might even exist there. The result of NASA\'s last "Decadal Survey" was to prioritize a Mars sample-return mission, as Mars has a special importance: it is comparatively "nearby", might have harbored life in the past, and might even continue to sustain life. <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter\'s</a> moon <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> is another important focus in the search for life in the Solar System. However, due to the distance and other constraints, Europa might not be the target of a sample-return mission in the foreseeable future.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_protection">Planetary protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planetary protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">Planetary protection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_sample_curation" title="Extraterrestrial sample curation">Extraterrestrial sample curation</a></div>\n<p>Planetary protection aims to prevent biological contamination of both the target <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_(planet)" title="Earth (planet)">Earth</a>&#8212;in the case of sample-return missions. No sample has yet been returned with alien life in it. A sample-return from Mars or other location with potential to host life, is a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_protection" title="Planetary protection">category V mission under COSPAR</a> which directs to containment of any unsterilized sample returned to Earth. This is because it is unknown the effects of such hypothetical life would be on humans or on the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> of Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lederberg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lederberg-2">[2]</a></sup> For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Lederberg" title="Joshua Lederberg">Joshua Lederberg</a> argued in the <a href="/wiki/1970s" title="1970s">1970s</a> that we should do sample-return missions classified as category V missions with extreme caution, and later studies by the NRC and ESF agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lederberg_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lederberg-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrc2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrc2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iMars_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iMars-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esf2010_PP_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-esf2010_PP-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAS-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">lunar rock</a>s and <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">lunar \'soil\'</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Receiving_Laboratory" title="Lunar Receiving Laboratory">Lunar Receiving Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orloff-EVA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orloff-EVA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Today, 75% of the samples are stored at the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Sample_Laboratory_Facility" title="Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility">Lunar Sample Laboratory Facility</a> built in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In July 1969, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> achieved the first successful sample return from another Solar System body. It returned approximately 22 kilograms (49&#160;lb) of Lunar surface material. This was followed by 34 kilograms (75&#160;lb) of material from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">Apollo 12</a>, 94.35 pounds (42.80&#160;kg) of material from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">Apollo 14</a>, 169.10 pounds (76.70&#160;kg) of material from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a>, 207.89 pounds (94.30&#160;kg) of material from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">Apollo 16</a>, and 243.40 pounds (110.40&#160;kg)  of material from <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a>.\n</p><p>One of the most significant advances in sample-return missions occurred in 1970 when the robotic <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet</a> mission known as <a href="/wiki/Luna_16" title="Luna 16">Luna 16</a> successfully returned 101 grams (3.6&#160;oz) of lunar soil. Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Luna_20" title="Luna 20">Luna 20</a> returned 55 grams (1.9&#160;oz) in 1974, and <a href="/wiki/Luna_24" title="Luna 24">Luna 24</a> returned 170 grams (6.0&#160;oz) in 1976. Although they recovered far less than the Apollo missions, they did this fully automatically. Apart from these three successes, other attempts under the <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna programme</a> failed. The first two missions were intended to outstrip Apollo 11 and were undertaken shortly before them in June and July 1969: <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5_No._402" title="Luna E-8-5 No. 402">Luna E-8-5 No. 402</a> failed at start, and <a href="/wiki/Luna_15" title="Luna 15">Luna 15</a> crashed on the Moon. Later, other sample-return missions failed: <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_300" title="Kosmos 300">Kosmos 300</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_305" title="Kosmos 305">Kosmos 305</a> in 1969, <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5_No._405" title="Luna E-8-5 No. 405">Luna E-8-5 No. 405</a> in 1970, <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-8-5M_No._412" title="Luna E-8-5M No. 412">Luna E-8-5M No. 412</a> in 1975 at unsuccessful launches, and <a href="/wiki/Luna_18" title="Luna 18">Luna 18</a> in 1971 and <a href="/wiki/Luna_23" title="Luna 23">Luna 23</a> in 1974 at unsuccessful landings on the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-E8_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA-E8-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1970, the Soviet Union planned for a 1975 first Martian sample-return mission in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_5NM" title="Mars 5NM">Mars 5NM</a> project. This mission was planned to use an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N1_rocket" title="N1 rocket">N1 rocket</a>, but as this rocket never flew successfully, the mission evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Mars_5M" title="Mars 5M">Mars 5M</a> project, which would use a double launch with the smaller <a href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket_family)" title="Proton (rocket family)">Proton</a> rocket and an assembly at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut" title="Salyut">Salyut</a> space station. This Mars 5M mission was planned for 1979, but got canceled in 1977 due to technical problems and complexity; all hardware was ordered destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_missions_after_a_20-year_hiatus">New missions after a 20-year hiatus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New missions after a 20-year hiatus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="box-Disputed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="System-search.svg" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="45" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/45px-System-search.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/68px-System-search.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/90px-System-search.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section\'s <b>factual accuracy is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Sample-return_mission#Dispute" title="Talk:Sample-return mission">Talk:Sample-return mission</a>. Please help to ensure that disputed statements are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">reliably sourced</a>.</span> <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2016</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>NASA has a long-term stratospheric flight program to collect cosmic dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1984 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDEF" title="LDEF">LDEF</a> mission returned <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">chondritic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_iron" title="Meteoric iron">iron-nickel</a> impactors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> as did the 1992 <a href="/wiki/European_Retrievable_Carrier" title="European Retrievable Carrier">EURECA</a> mission.\n</p><p>The Earth-Orbital Debris Collection (ODC) experiment was deployed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mir_space_station" title="Mir space station">Mir space station</a> for 18 months during 1996&#8211;97 and used <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">aerogel</a> to capture particles from low Earth orbit, consisting of interplanetary dust and man-made particles. Far from being "the last sample-return mission... in... twenty years", ODC was a portable version of an LDEF collector, decreasing collection time significantly, and effective area by orders of magnitude.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="850" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/250px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/375px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg/500px-GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GenSC_collection2_150dpi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An artist\'s rendering of <i>Genesis</i> collecting <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The next mission to return extraterrestrial samples was the <i><a href="/wiki/Genesis_(spacecraft)" title="Genesis (spacecraft)">Genesis</a></i> mission, which returned solar wind samples to Earth from beyond Earth orbit in 2004. Unfortunately, the <i>Genesis</i> capsule failed to open its parachute while re-entering the Earth\'s atmosphere and crash-landed in the Utah desert. There were fears of severe contamination or even total mission loss, but scientists managed to save many of the samples. They were the first to be collected from beyond lunar orbit. <i>Genesis</i> used a collector array made of wafers of ultra-pure <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>. Each different wafer was used to collect a different part of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="1664" decoding="async" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/250px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/375px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg/500px-Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stardust_Capsule_on_Ground.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample-return capsule from the <i><a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">Stardust</a></i> mission</div></div></div>\n<p><i>Genesis</i> was followed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">Stardust</a></i> spacecraft, which returned comet samples to Earth on January 15, 2006. It safely passed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Wild_2" title="Comet Wild 2">Comet Wild 2</a> and collected dust samples from the comet\'s <a href="/wiki/Coma_(cometary)" title="Coma (cometary)">coma</a> while imaging the comet\'s nucleus. <i>Stardust</i> used a collector array made of low-density aerogel (99% of which is empty space), which has about 1/1000 of the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of glass. This permits the ability to collect the cometary particles without damaging them due to high impact velocities. Particle collisions with even slightly porous solid collectors would result in destruction of those particles and damage to the collection apparatus. During cruise, the second side of the array collected at least seven interstellar dust particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2010 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency</a> (JAXA) <a href="/wiki/Hayabusa" title="Hayabusa">Hayabusa</a> probe returned asteroid samples to Earth after a rendezvous with (and a landing on) <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">S-type asteroid</a> <a href="/wiki/25143_Itokawa" title="25143 Itokawa">25143 Itokawa</a>. In November 2010, scientists at the agency confirmed that, despite failure of the sampling device, the probe retrieved micrograms of dust from the asteroid, the first brought back to Earth in pristine condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Russian <a href="/wiki/Fobos-Grunt" title="Fobos-Grunt">Fobos-Grunt</a> was a failed sample-return mission designed to return samples from <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a>, one of the moons of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. It was launched on November 8, 2011, but failed to leave Earth orbit and crashed after several weeks into the southern Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2250" data-file-width="2700" decoding="async" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/250px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/375px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg/500px-OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OSIRIS-REx_artist_rendetion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of OSIRIS-REx collecting a sample from asteroid <span class="nowrap"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999_RQ36" title="1999 RQ36">1999 RQ<span style="position: relative; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (October 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx" title="OSIRIS-REx">OSIRIS-REx</a> mission was launched in September 2016 on a mission to return samples from the asteroid <a href="/wiki/101955_Bennu" title="101955 Bennu">101955 Bennu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a of organic compounds that led to the formation of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSIRIS-REx_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSIRIS-REx-25">[25]</a></sup> The sample will be collected with the <a diamond</div></div></div>\n<p>Sample-return methods include, but are not restricted to the following:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collector_array">Collector array</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Collector array">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A collector array may be used to collect millions or billions of atoms, molecules, and fine particulates by using a number of wafers made of different elements. The molecular structure of these wafers allows the collection of various sizes of particles. Collector arrays, such as those flown on <i>Genesis</i>, are ultra-pure in order to ensure maximal collection efficiency, durability, and analytical distinguishability.\n</p><p>Collector arrays are useful for collecting tiny, fast-moving atoms such as those expelled by the Sun through the solar wind, but can also be used for collection of larger particles such as those found in the coma of a comet. The NASA spacecraft known as <i>Stardust</i> implemented this technique. However, due to the high speeds and size of the particles that make up the coma and the area nearby, a dense solid-state collector array was not viable. As a result, another means for collecting samples had to be designed as to preserve the safety of the spacecraft and the samples themselves.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerogel">Aerogel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aerogel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">Aerogel</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="690" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/200px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/300px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg/400px-Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meteoroid_track_through_aerogel_from_EURECA_mission.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A particle captured in aerogel</div></div></div>\n<p>Aerogel is a <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silica</a>-based porous solid with a sponge-like structure, 99.8% of whose volume is empty space. Aerogel has about 1/1000 of the density of glass. An aerogel was used in the <i>Stardust</i> spacecraft because the dust particles the spacecraft was to collect would have an impact speed of about 6&#160;km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A collision with a dense solid at that speed could alter their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a> or perhaps vaporize them completely.\n</p><p>Since the aerogel is mostly transparent, and the particles leave a carrot-shaped path once they penetrate the surface, scientists can easily find and retrieve them. Since its pores are on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometer</a> scale, particles, even ones smaller than a grain of sand, do not merely pass through the aerogel completely. Instead, they slow to a stop and then are embedded within it.\n</p><p>The <i>Stardust</i> spacecraft has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tennis-racket" title="Tennis-racket">tennis-racket</a>-shaped collector with aerogel fitted to it. The collector is retracted into its capsule for safe storage and delivery back to Earth. Aerogel is quite strong and easily survives both launching and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer-space" title="Outer-space">outer-space</a> environments.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robotic_excavation_and_return">Robotic excavation and return</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sample-return_mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Robotic excavation and return">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some of the most risky and difficult types of sample-return missions are those that require landing on an extraterrestrial body such as an asteroid, moon, or planet. It takes a great deal of time, money, and technical ability in order to even initiate such plans. It is a difficult feat that requires that everything from launch to landing to retrieval and launch back to Earth is planned out with high precision and accuracy.\n</p><p>This type of sample return, although having the most risks, is the most rewarding for planetary science. Furthermore, such missions carry a great deal of public outreach potential, which is an important attribute for <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> when it comes to public support. The only successful robotic sample-return missions of this type have been the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna landers</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_missions">List of missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
