title="Star">stars</a> that forms an imaginary outline or pattern on the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>, typically representing an animal, mythological person or creature, a god, or an inanimate object.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxforddic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxforddic-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The origins of the earliest constellations likely go back to <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a>. People used them to relate stories of their beliefs, experiences, <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>. Different cultures and countries adopted their own constellations, some of which lasted into the early <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a> before today\'s constellations were internationally recognized. Adoption of constellations has changed significantly over time. Many have changed in size or shape. Some became popular, only to drop into obscurity. Others were limited to single cultures or nations.\n</p><p>The 48 traditional Western constellations are Greek. They are given in <a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a>\' work <i>Phenomena</i> and <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i>, though their origin probably predates these works by several centuries. Constellations in the far <a href="/wiki/Southern_celestial_hemisphere" title="Southern celestial hemisphere">southern sky</a> were added from the <a href="/wiki/15th_century" title="15th century">15th century</a> until the mid-<a href="/wiki/18th_century" title="18th century">18th century</a> when European explorers began traveling to the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. Twelve ancient constellations belong to the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a> (straddling the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> all traverse). The origins of the zodiac remain historically uncertain; its <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrological</a> divisions became prominent <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">c.</a> 400 BC in <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">Babylonian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chaldea" title="Chaldea">Chaldean</a> astronomy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> probably dates back to prehistory.\n</p><p>In 1928, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) formally accepted <a href="/wiki/IAU_designated_constellations" title="IAU designated constellations">88 modern constellations</a>, with contiguous boundaries that together cover the entire celestial sphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU1928_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAU1928-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Any given point in a <a href="/wiki/Celestial_coordinate_system" title="Celestial coordinate system">celestial coordinate system</a> lies in one of the modern constellations. Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_designation" title="Stellar designation">astronomical naming systems</a> include the constellation where a given celestial object is found to convey its approximate location in the sky. The <a href="/wiki/Flamsteed_designation" title="Flamsteed designation">Flamsteed designation</a> of a star, for example, consists of a number and the genitive form of the constellation name.\n</p><p>Other star patterns or groups called <a href="/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy)" title="Asterism (astronomy)">asterisms</a> are not constellations per se but are used by observers to navigate the night sky. Asterisms often refer to several stars within a constellation or may share stars with several constellations. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">Hyades</a> within the constellation <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">Taurus</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_Cross" title="False Cross">False Cross</a> split between the southern constellations <a href="/wiki/Carina_(constellation)" title="Carina (constellation)">Carina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vela_(constellation)" title="Vela (constellation)">Vela</a>, or Venus\' Mirror in the constellation of Orion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOCdb2018_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOCdb2018-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SoftBisque2018_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SoftBisque2018-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word "constellation" comes from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">Late Latin</a> term <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">c&#333;nstell&#257;ti&#333;</i>, which can be translated as "set of stars"; it came into use in <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">English</a> during the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> word for constellation is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CF%83%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD" title="wikt:&#7940;&#963;&#964;&#961;&#959;&#957;">&#7940;&#963;&#964;&#961;&#959;&#957;.</a> A more modern astronomical sense of the term "constellation" is simply as a recognisable pattern of stars whose appearance is associated with mythological characters or creatures, or  earthbound animals, or objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxforddic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxforddic-1">[1]</a></sup> It can also specifically denote the officially recognized 88 named constellations used today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Colloquial usage does not draw a sharp distinction between "constellations" and smaller "asterisms" (pattern of stars), yet the modern accepted astronomical constellations employ such a distinction. E.g., the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">Hyades</a> are both asterisms, and each lies within the boundaries of the constellation of Taurus. Another example is the popular northern asterism known as the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper" title="Big Dipper">Big Dipper</a> (US) or the Plough (UK), composed of the seven brightest stars within the area of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">IAU</a>-defined constellation of <a href="/wiki/Ursa_Major" title="Ursa Major">Ursa Major</a>. The southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_Cross" title="False Cross">False Cross</a> asterism includes portions of the constellations <a href="/wiki/Carina_(constellation)" title="Carina (constellation)">Carina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vela_(constellation)" title="Vela (constellation)">Vela</a>.\n</p><p>A constellation (or star), viewed from a particular latitude on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, that never sets below the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> is termed <a href="/wiki/Circumpolar_star" title="Circumpolar star">circumpolar</a>. From the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> or <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, all constellations south or north of the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a> are <a href="/wiki/Circumpolar_constellation" title="Circumpolar constellation">circumpolar</a>. Depending on the definition, equatorial constellations may include those that lie between declinations 45&#176; north and 45&#176; south,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harbord_goodwin1897_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-harbord_goodwin1897-9">[9]</a></sup> or those that pass through the declination range of the ecliptic or <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a> ranging between 23&#189;&#176; north, the celestial equator, and 23&#189;&#176; south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton14th_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton14th-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steele1884_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-steele1884-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although stars in constellations appear near each other in the sky, they usually lie at a variety of distances away from the Earth. Since stars have their own independent motions, all constellations will change slowly over time. After tens to hundreds of thousands of years, familiar outlines will generally become unrecognizable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-marc-12">[12]</a></sup> Astronomers can predict the past or future constellation outlines by measuring individual stars\' <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">common proper motions</a> or cpm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Koupelis2007_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Koupelis2007-13">[13]</a></sup> by accurate <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometry</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-c1-15">[15]</a></sup> and their <a href="/wiki/Radial_velocity" title="Radial velocity">radial velocities</a> by <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">astronomical spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUInfBull91_c1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAUInfBull91_c1-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_early_constellations">History of the early constellations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the early constellations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest evidence for the humankind\'s identification of constellations comes from Mesopotamian (within modern Iraq) inscribed stones and clay writing tablets that date back to 3000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogers_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers-17">[17]</a></sup> It seems that the bulk of the Mesopotamian constellations were created within a relatively short interval from around 1300 to 1000 BC. Mesopotamian constellations appeared later in many of the classical Greek constellations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greek_Constellations_2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greek_Constellations_2006-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Babylonian_astronomy" title="Old around 1000 BC. However, the numerous <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian names</a> in these catalogues suggest that they built on older, but otherwise unattested, <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> traditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">Early Bronze Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The classical Zodiac is a revision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Babylonian" title="Neo-Babylonian">Neo-Babylonian</a> constellations from the 6th century BC. The Greeks adopted the Babylonian constellations in the 4th century BC. Twenty Ptolemaic constellations are from the Ancient Near East. Another ten have the same stars but different names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greek_Constellations_2006_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greek_Constellations_2006-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Biblical scholar, <a href="/wiki/E._W._Bullinger" title="E. W. Bullinger">E. W. Bullinger</a> interpreted some of the creatures mentioned in the books of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel" title="Book of Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Revelation</a> as the middle signs of the four quarters of the Zodiac,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> with the Lion as <a href="/wiki/Leo_(astrology)" title="Leo (astrology)">Leo</a>, the Bull as <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(astrology)" title="Taurus (astrology)">Taurus</a>, the Man representing Aquarius and the Eagle standing in for Scorpio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen1899_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen1899-22">[22]</a></sup> The biblical <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">Book of Job</a> also makes reference to a number of constellations, including <span dir="rtl" lang="he" title="Hebrew language text">&#1506;&#1497;&#1513;</span>&#8206; <i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew language text">&#8216;Ayish</i> "bier", <span dir="rtl" lang="he" title="Hebrew language text">&#1499;&#1505;&#1497;&#1500;</span>&#8206; <i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew language text">chesil</i> "fool" and <span dir="rtl" lang="he" title="Hebrew language text">&#1499;&#1497;&#1502;&#1492;</span>&#8206; <i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew language text">chimah</i> "heap" (Job 9:9, 38:31-32), rendered as "Arcturus, Orion and Pleiades" by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KJV" title="KJV">KJV</a>, but <i>&#8216;Ayish</i> "the bier" actually corresponding to Ursa Major.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The term <i><a href="/wiki/Mazzaroth" title="Mazzaroth">Mazzaroth</a></i> <span dir="rtl" lang="he" title="Hebrew language text">&#1502;&#1463;&#1494;&#1464;&#1468;&#1512;&#1493;&#1465;&#1514;</span>&#8206;, translated as <i>a garland of crowns</i>, is a <i><a href="/wiki/Hapax_legomenon" title="Hapax legomenon">hapax legomenon</a></i> in Job 38:32, and it might refer to the zodiacal constellations.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_antiquity">Classical antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classical antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_astronomy" title="Egyptian astronomy">Egyptian astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_astronomy" title="Ancient Greek astronomy">Ancient Greek astronomy</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Senenmut.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="759" data-file-width="617" decoding="async" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Senenmut.jpg/170px-Senenmut.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Senenmut.jpg/255px-Senenmut.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Senenmut.jpg/340px-Senenmut.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Senenmut.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ancient Egyptian star chart and decanal clock on the ceiling from the tomb of Senenmut</div></div></div>\n<p>There is only limited information on ancient Greek constellations, with some fragmentary evidence being found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Works_and_Days" title="Works and Days">Works and Days</a></i> of the Greek poet <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, who mentioned the "heavenly bodies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Greek astronomy essentially adopted the older Babylonian system in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_era" title="Hellenistic era">Hellenistic era</a>, first introduced to Greece by <a href="/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus" title="Eudoxus of Cnidus">Eudoxus of Cnidus</a> in the 4th century BC. The original work of Eudoxus is lost, but it survives as a versification by <a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a>, dating to the 3rd century BC. The most complete existing works dealing with the mythical origins of the constellations are by the Hellenistic writer termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-Eratosthenes" title="Pseudo-Eratosthenes">pseudo-Eratosthenes</a> and an early Roman writer styled pseudo-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyginus" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a>. The basis of Western astronomy as taught during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> and until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" title="Early Modern period">Early Modern period</a> is the <i><a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, written in the 2nd century.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">Ptolemaic Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_astronomy" title="Egyptian astronomy">native Egyptian</a> tradition of anthropomorphic figures representing the planets, stars, and various constellations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall_Clagett_1995,_p._111_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall_Clagett_1995,_p._111-25">[25]</a></sup> Some of these were combined with Greek and Babylonian astronomical systems culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Dendera_zodiac" title="Dendera zodiac">Zodiac of Dendera</a>, but it remains unclear when this occurred, but most were placed during the Roman period between 2nd to 4th centuries AD. The oldest known depiction of the zodiac showing all the now familiar constellations, along with some original Egyptian constellations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decans" title="Decans">decans</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">planets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Ptolemy\'s <i>Almagest</i> remained the standard definition of constellations in the medieval period both in Europe and in <a href="/wiki/Astronomy_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world" title="Astronomy in the medieval Islamic world">Islamic astronomy</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_China">Ancient China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ancient China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_constellations" title="Chinese constellations">Chinese constellations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese astronomy</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="984" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG/220px-Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG/330px-Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG/440px-Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Su_Song_Star_Map_1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_star_map" title="Chinese star map">Chinese star map</a> with a cylindrical projection (<a href="/wiki/Su_Song" title="Su Song">Su Song</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/History_of_China#Ancient_China" title="History of China">Ancient China</a> had a long tradition of observing celestial phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Nonspecific <a href="/wiki/Chinese_star_names" title="Chinese star names">Chinese star names</a>, later categorized in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twenty-eight_mansions" title="Twenty-eight mansions">twenty-eight mansions</a>, have been found on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oracle_bones" title="Oracle bones">oracle bones</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anyang" title="Anyang">Anyang</a>, dating back to the middle <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Chinese_constellations" title="Chinese constellations">constellations</a> are some of the most important observations of Chinese sky, attested from the 5th century BC. Parallels to the earliest Babylonian (Sumerian) star catalogues suggest that the ancient Chinese system did not arise independently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-sun-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Three schools of classical <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese astronomy</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_period" title="Han period">Han period</a> are attributed to astronomers of the earlier <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a>. The constellations of the three schools were conflated into a single system by <a href="/wiki/Chen_Zhuo" title="Chen Zhuo">Chen Zhuo</a>, an astronomer of the 3rd century (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_period" title="Three Kingdoms period">Three Kingdoms period</a>). Chen Zhuo\'s work has been lost, but information on his system of constellations survives in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_period" title="Tang period">Tang period</a> records, notably by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qutan_Xida" title="Qutan Xida">Qutan Xida</a>. The oldest extant Chinese star chart dates to that period and was preserved as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunhuang_Manuscripts" title="Dunhuang Manuscripts">Dunhuang Manuscripts</a>. Native Chinese astronomy flourished during the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>, and during the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> became increasingly influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Islamic_astronomy" title="Medieval Islamic astronomy">medieval Islamic astronomy</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_Astrology_of_the_Kaiyuan_Era" title="Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era">Treatise on Astrology of the Kaiyuan Era</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sun_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-sun-29">[29]</a></sup> As maps were prepared during this period on more scientific lines, they were considered as more reliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selin2008_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Selin2008-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A well known map from the Song period is the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_star_maps" title="Chinese star maps">Suzhou Astronomical Chart</a>, which was prepared with carvings of stars on the <a href="/wiki/Planisphere" title="Planisphere">planisphere</a> of the Chinese sky on a stone plate; it is done accurately based on observations, and it shows the supernova of the year of 1054 in Taurus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selin2008_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Selin2008-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Influenced by European astronomy during the late <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>, more stars were depicted on the charts but retaining the traditional constellations; new stars observed were incorporated as supplementary stars in old constellations in the southern sky which did not depict any of the traditional stars recorded by ancient Chinese astronomers. Further improvements were made during the later part of the Ming dynasty by <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Xu Guangqi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Adam_Schall_von_Bell" title="Johann Adam Schall von Bell">Johann Adam Schall von Bell</a>, the German Jesuit and was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Chongzhen_calendar" title="Chongzhen calendar">Chongzhen Lishu</a> (Calendrical Treatise of <a href="/wiki/Chongzhen_Emperor" title="Chongzhen Emperor">Chongzhen period</a>, 1628).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Traditional Chinese star maps incorporated 23 new constellations with 125 stars of the southern hemisphere of the sky based on the knowledge of Western star charts; with this improvement, the Chinese Sky was integrated with the World astronomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selin2008_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Selin2008-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_astronomy">Early modern astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early modern astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically, the origins of the constellations of the northern and southern skies are distinctly different. Most northern constellations date to antiquity, with names based mostly on Classical Greek legends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton14th_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norton14th-10">[10]</a></sup> Evidence of these constellations has survived in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_charts" title="Star charts">star charts</a>, whose oldest representation appears on the statue known as the <a href="/wiki/Farnese_Atlas" title="Farnese Atlas">Farnese Atlas</a>, based perhaps on the star catalogue of the Greek astronomer <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley2005_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley2005-32">[32]</a></sup> Southern constellations are more modern inventions, sometimes as substitutes for ancient constellations (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Argo_Navis" title="Argo Navis">Argo Navis</a>). Some southern constellations had long names that were shortened to more usable forms; e.g. Musca Australis became simply Musca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton14th_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Norton14th-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some of the early constellations were never universally adopted. Stars were often grouped into constellations differently by different observers, and the arbitrary constellation boundaries often led to confusion as to which constellation a celestial object belonged. Before astronomers delineated precise boundaries (starting in the 19th century), constellations generally appeared as ill-defined regions of the sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norton2nd_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norton2nd-33">[33]</a></sup> Today they now follow officially accepted designated lines of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_Ascension" title="Right Ascension">Right Ascension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">Declination</a> based on those defined by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Apthorp_Gould" title="Benjamin Apthorp Gould">Benjamin Gould</a> in <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epoch</a> 1875.0 in his star catalogue <i>Uranometria Argentina</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 1603 star atlas "<a href="/wiki/Uranometria" title="Uranometria">Uranometria</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bayer" title="Johann Bayer">Johann Bayer</a> assigned stars to individual constellations and formalized the division by assigning a series of Greek and Latin letters to the stars within each constellation.  These are known today as <a href="/wiki/Bayer_designation" title="Bayer designation">Bayer designations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swerdlow1986_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swerdlow1986-35">[35]</a></sup> Subsequent star atlases led to the development of today\'s accepted modern constellations.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Netherlandish_cartography" title="Golden Age of Netherlandish cartography">golden age of Netherlandish cartography</a>, by the Dutch cartographer <a href="/wiki/Frederik_de_Wit" title="Frederik de Wit">Frederik de Wit</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The southern sky, below about &#8722;65&#176; <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a>, was only partially catalogued by ancient Babylonians, Egyptian, Greeks, Chinese, and Persian astronomers of the north. Knowledge that northern and southern star patterns differed goes back to Classical writers, who describe, for example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Africa" title="Exploration of Africa">African circumnavigation</a> expedition commissioned by Egyptian Pharaoh Necho II in c. 600 BC and those of <a href="/wiki/Hanno_the_Navigator" title="Hanno the Navigator">Hanno the Navigator</a> in c. 500 BC. However, much of this history was lost with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destruction_of_the_Library_of_Alexandria" title="Destruction of the Library of Alexandria">Destruction of the Library of Alexandria</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The history of southern constellations is not straightforward.  Different groupings and different names were proposed by various observers, some reflecting national traditions or designed to promote various sponsors. Southern constellations were important from the 14th to 16th centuries, when sailors used the stars for <a href="/wiki/Celestial_navigation" title="Celestial navigation">celestial navigation</a>. Italian explorers who recorded new southern constellations include <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Corsali" title="Andrea Corsali">Andrea Corsali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Pigafetta" title="Antonio Pigafetta">Antonio Pigafetta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">Amerigo Vespucci</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen1899_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allen1899-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/88_modern_constellations" title="88 modern constellations">88 IAU-recognized constellations</a> in this region first appeared on celestial globes developed in the late 16th century by <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Plancius" title="Petrus Plancius">Petrus Plancius</a>, based mainly on observations of the Dutch navigators <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Dirkszoon_Keyser" title="Pieter Dirkszoon Keyser">Pieter Dirkszoon Keyser</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_de_Houtman" title="Frederick de Houtman">Frederick de Houtman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> These became widely known through <a href="/wiki/Johann_Bayer" title="Johann Bayer">Johann Bayer</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_atlas" title="Star atlas">star atlas</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Uranometria" title="Uranometria">Uranometria</a></i> of 1603.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Category:Constellations_listed_by_Lacaille" title="Category:Constellations listed by Lacaille">Seventeen</a> more were created in 1763 by the French astronomer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicolas_Louis_de_Lacaille" title="Nicolas Louis de Lacaille">Nicolas Louis de Lacaille</a> appearing in his star catalogue, published in 1756.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Several modern proposals have not survived. The French astronomers <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Lemonnier" title="Pierre Lemonnier">Pierre Lemonnier</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Lalande" title="Joseph Lalande">Joseph Lalande</a>, for example, proposed constellations that were once popular but have since been dropped. The northern constellation <a href="/wiki/Quadrans_Muralis" title="Quadrans Muralis">Quadrans Muralis</a> survived into the 19th century (when its name was attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadrantid" title="Quadrantid">Quadrantid</a> meteor shower), but is now divided between <a href="/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes" title="Bo&#246;tes">Bo&#246;tes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Draco_(constellation)" title="Draco (constellation)">Draco</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="88_modern_constellations">88 modern constellations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 88 modern constellations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/IAU_designated_constellations" title="IAU designated constellations">IAU designated constellations</a></div>\n<p>A general list of 88 constellations was produced for the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> in 1922.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> It is roughly based on the traditional Greek constellations listed by Ptolemy in his <i>Almagest</i> in the 2nd century and <a href="/wiki/Aratus" title="Aratus">Aratus</a>\' work <i>Phenomena</i>, with early modern modifications and additions (most importantly introducing constellations covering the parts of the southern sky unknown to Ptolemy) by Petrus Plancius (1592, 1597/98 and 1613), <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">Johannes Hevelius</a> (1690) and Nicolas Louis de Lacaille (1763),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau-const_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-iau-const-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> who named fourteen constellations and renamed a fifteenth one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wisconsin-Madison_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-Madison-47">[47]</a></sup> De Lacaille studied the stars of the southern hemisphere from 1750 until 1754 from <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>, when he was said to have observed more than 10,000 stars using a 0.5 inches (13&#160;mm) <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">refracting telescope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wisconsin-Madison_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-Madison-47">[47]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1922, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell" title="Henry Norris Russell">Henry Norris Russell</a> produced a general list of 88 constellations and some useful abbreviations for them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> However, these constellations did not have clear borders between them. In 1928, the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) formally accepted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/88_modern_constellations" title="88 modern constellations">88 modern constellations</a>, with contiguous boundaries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> along vertical and horizontal lines of <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Joseph_Delporte" title="Eug&#232;ne Joseph Delporte">Eugene Delporte</a> that, together, cover the entire celestial sphere;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lachi&#232;ze-ReyLuminet2001_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lachi&#232;ze-ReyLuminet2001-51">[51]</a></sup> this list was finally published in 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU1928_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-IAU1928-3">[3]</a></sup> Where possible, these modern constellations usually share the names of their Graeco-Roman predecessors, such as <a href="/wiki/Orion_(constellation)" title="Orion (constellation)">Orion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leo_(constellation)" title="Leo (constellation)">Leo</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scorpius" title="Scorpius">Scorpius</a>. The aim of this system is area-mapping, i.e. the division of the celestial sphere into contiguous fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iau-const_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-iau-const-44">[44]</a></sup> Out of the 88 modern constellations, 36 lie predominantly in the northern sky, and the other 52 predominantly in the southern.\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 492px"><div style="width: 492px">\n<div class="thumb" style="width: 490px;"><div style="margin:0px auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="272" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg/735px-Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg/1102px-Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg/1469px-Hipparcos_Catalogue_equirectangular_plot.svg.png 2x" width="490"/></a></div></div>\n<div class="gallerytext">\n<p>Equirectangular plot of declination vs right ascension of stars brighter than apparent magnitude 5 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hipparcos_Catalogue" title="Hipparcos Catalogue">Hipparcos Catalogue</a>, coded by spectral type and apparent magnitude, relative to the modern constellations and the ecliptic.\n</p>\n</div>\n</div></li>\n</ul>\n<p>The boundaries developed by Delporte used data that originated back to <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epoch</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B1875.0" title="B1875.0">B1875.0</a>, which was when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_A._Gould" title="Benjamin A. Gould">Benjamin A. Gould</a> first made his proposal to designate boundaries for the celestial sphere, a suggestion upon which Delporte would base his work. class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constellation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dark cloud constellations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Great_Rift_(astronomy)" title="Great Rift (astronomy)">Great Rift (astronomy)</a></div>\n<p>The Great Rift, a series of dark patches in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, is more visible and striking in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a> than in the northern. It vividly stands out when conditions are otherwise so dark that the Milky Way\'s central region casts shadows on the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Some cultures have discerned shapes in these patches and have given names to these "dark cloud constellations". Members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inca" title="Inca">Inca</a> civilization identified various dark areas or <a href="/wiki/Dark_nebula" title="Dark nebula">dark nebulae</a> in the Milky Way as animals and associated their appearance with the seasonal rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_astronomy" title="Australian Aboriginal astronomy">Australian Aboriginal astronomy</a> also describes dark cloud constellations, the most famous being the "emu in the sky" whose head is formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coalsack" title="Coalsack">Coalsack</a>, a dark nebula, instead of the stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bordeleau2013_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bordeleau2013-57">[57]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-packed">\n<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 586.66666666667px"><div style="width: 586.66666666667px">\n<div class="thumb" style="width: 584.66666666667px;"><div style="margin:0px auto;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emu_public.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="89" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/Emu_public.jpg" width="585"/></a></div></div>\n<div class="gallerytext">\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">Emu</a> in the sky</i>&#8212;a constellation defined by dark clouds rather than by stars.
