Note that the zenith is opposite the nadir.</div></div></div>\n<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zenith" title="wiktionary:zenith">zenith</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>zenith</b> is an imaginary point directly "above" a particular location, on the imaginary <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>. "Above" means in the vertical direction opposite to the apparent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_force" title="Gravitational force">gravitational force</a> at that location. The opposite direction, i.e. the direction in which <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> pulls, is toward the <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>.  The zenith is the "highest" point on the celestial sphere.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Origin"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Origin</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Relevance_and_use"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Relevance and use</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word "zenith" derives from an inaccurate reading of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> expression <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" title="Arabic language text">&#1587;&#1605;&#1578; &#1575;&#1604;&#1585;&#1571;&#1587;</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="DIN 31635 Arabic (Arabic language) transliteration">samt ar-ra\'s</i>), meaning "direction of the head" or "path above the head", by <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> scribes in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (during the 14th century), possibly through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">Old Spanish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was reduced to \'samt\' ("direction") and miswritten as \'senit\'/\'cenit\', the "m" being misread as "ni". Through the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> \'cenith\', \'zenith\' first appeared in the 17th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relevance_and_use">Relevance and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relevance and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Azimuth-Altitude_schematic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="436" decoding="async" class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Azimuth-Altitude_schematic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Angles and planes of a celestial sphere</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tropical-area-mactan-philippines.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="876" data-file-width="1280" decoding="async" height="164" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tropical-area-mactan-philippines.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The shadows of trees are the shortest on Earth when the Sun is directly overhead (at the zenith). This happens only at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_noon" title="Solar noon">solar noon</a> on certain days in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, where the trees\' <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and the Sun\'s <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> equal on certain days.</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">Culmination</a></div>\n<p>The term <i>zenith</i> is sometimes used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Culmination" title="Culmination">highest point</a>, way, or level reached by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">celestial body</a> on its daily apparent path around a given point of observation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This sense of the word is often used to describe the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sun%27s_position&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sun\'s position (page does not exist)">position</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> ("The sun reached its zenith..."), but to an astronomer, the Sun does not have its own zenith and is at the zenith only if it is directly overhead.\n</p><p>In a scientific context, the zenith is the direction of reference for measuring the <b>zenith angle</b>, the angle between a direction of interest (e.g. a star) and the local zenith.\n</p><p>The Sun reaches the observer\'s zenith when it is 90&#176; above the horizon, and this only happens between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>.\n</p><p>At a given location during the course of a day, the Sun reaches not only its zenith but also its <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">antipode</a> of that location 12 hours from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_noon" title="Solar noon">solar noon</a>.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the altitude in the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system" title="Horizontal coordinate system">horizontal coordinate system</a> and the zenith angle are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complementary_angles" title="Complementary angles">complementary angles</a>, with the horizon perpendicular to the zenith. The astronomical <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">meridian</a> is also determined by the zenith, and is defined as a circle on the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a> that passes through the zenith, <a href="/wiki/Nadir" title="Nadir">nadir</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">celestial poles</a>.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Zenith_telescope" title="Zenith telescope">zenith telescope</a> is a type of telescope designed to point straight up at or near the zenith, and used for precision measurement of star positions, to simplify telescope construction, or both. The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Orbital_Debris_Observatory" title="NASA Orbital Debris Observatory">NASA Orbital Debris Observatory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Large_Zenith_Telescope" title="Large Zenith Telescope">Large Zenith Telescope</a> are both zenith telescopes since the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_mirror_telescopes" title="Liquid mirror telescopes">liquid mirrors</a> meant these telescopes could only point straight up.\n</p><p>On the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, <i>zenith</i> and <i>nadir</i> are used instead of <i>up</i> and <i>down</i>, referring to directions within and around the station, relative to the earth.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
