International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[di&#712;o&#611;u &#712;k&#592;&#771;w&#771;]</a></span>), anglicised as <b>Diogo Cam</b> and also known as <b>Diego Cam</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">explorer</a> and one of the most notable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navigators" title="Navigators">navigators</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">Age of Discovery</a>. He made two voyages sailing along the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in the 1480s, exploring the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a> and the coasts of the present-day <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911_2-0"><a tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Armas_Diogo_C%C3%A3o.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="370" data-file-width="267" He married and had four children: Pedro C&#227;o, Manuel C&#227;o, Andr&#233; Afonso C&#227;o, and Isabel C&#227;o.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explorer">Explorer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_C%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explorer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caopillar.JPG"><img alt="" Diogo C&#227;o</div></div></div>\n<p>He was the first European known to sight and enter the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo River</a> and to explore the West African coast between Cape St. Catherine in <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cross" title="Cape Cross">Cape Cross</a>, almost from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> to <a href="/wiki/Walvis_Bay" title="Walvis Bay">Walvis Bay</a> in Namibia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage">First voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_C%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When King <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">John II of Portugal</a> restarted the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_the_Navigator" title="Henry the Navigator">Henry the Navigator</a>, he sent out C&#227;o (probably around midsummer 1482) to explore the African coast south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. He discovered the mouth and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> of the Congo, probably in August 1482 and marked it with a <a href="/wiki/Padr%C3%A3o" title="Padr&#227;o">padr&#227;o</a>, or stone pillar erected on Shark Point, attesting to the sovereignty of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>. This padr&#227;o still stands to this day, albeit in ruins. He also ascended the great river for a short distance, and commenced modest commerce with the natives of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kongo" title="Kingdom of Kongo">Bakongo</a> kingdom. C&#227;o then coasted down along the present <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> (Portuguese West Africa), and erected a second padr&#227;o, probably marking the termination of this voyage, at Cape Saint Mary (the Monte Negro of these first visitors). He returned to Lisbon by 8 April 1484, where John II ennobled him, promoting him from esquire to a <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a> of his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" title="Court (royal)"> household</a>, and granted him an annuity and a coat of arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> On his return he discovered the Island of <a href="/wiki/Annob%C3%B3n" title="Annob&#243;n">Annob&#243;n</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_voyage">Second voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_C%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="315" decoding="async" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg/190px-Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg/285px-Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portuguese_discoveries_diogo_cao.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diogo C&#227;o</div></div></div>\n<p>That C&#227;o, on his second voyage of 1484&#8211;1486, was accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Behaim" title="Martin Behaim">Martin Behaim</a> (as alleged on the latter\'s Nuremberg globe of 1492) is very doubtful. But it is known that the explorer revisited the Congo and erected two more padr&#227;o on land beyond his previous voyage. The first was at Cabo Negro, Angola, the second at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cross" title="Cape Cross">Cape Cross</a>. The Cape Cross pillar probably marked the end of his progress southward, some 1,400 kilometers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> C&#227;o ascended the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Kongo River</a> (which he thought led towards the realm of <a href="/wiki/Prester_John" title="Prester John">Prester John</a>), up to the neighborhood of the site of <a href="/wiki/Matadi" title="Matadi">Matadi</a>. There, in October or November 1485, near the falls of <a href="/wiki/Yellala_Falls" title="Yellala Falls">Ielala</a>, he left an inscription engraved on the stone which testifies of its passage and that of his men: <i>Aqui chegaram os navios do esclarecido rei D.Jo&#227;o II de Portugal - Diogo C&#227;o, Pero Anes, Pero da Costa.</i> ("Here reached the ships of the enlightned king <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Portugal" title="John II of Portugal">John II of Portugal</a> &#8211; Diogo C&#227;o, Pero Anes, Pero da Costa").\n</p><p>According to one authority (a legend on the 1489 map of <a href="/wiki/Henricus_Martellus_Germanus" title="Henricus Martellus Germanus">Henricus Martellus Germanus</a>), C&#227;o died off <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cross" title="Cape Cross">Cape Cross</a>; but <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_de_Barros" title="Jo&#227;o de Barros">Jo&#227;o de Barros</a> and others wrote of his return to the Kongo, and subsequent taking of a native envoy to Portugal. The four pillars set up by C&#227;o on his two voyages have all been discovered still on their original site, and the inscriptions on two of them from Cape Santa Maria and Cape Cross, dated 1482 and 1485 respectively, are still to be read and have been printed. The Cape Cross padr&#227;o is now at <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> (replaced on the spot by a granite facsimile); those from the Kongo estuary and the more southerly Cape Santa Maria and Cabo Negro are in the Museum of the Lisbon Geographical Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diogo_C%C3%A3o&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Diogo C&#227;o is the subject of <i>Padr&#227;o</i>, one of the best-known poems in <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a>\'s book <i>Mensagem</i>, the only one published during the author\'s lifetime.
