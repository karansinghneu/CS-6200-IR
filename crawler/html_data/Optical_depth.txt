navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth_(astrophysics)" title="Optical depth (astrophysics)">Optical depth (astrophysics)</a>.</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>optical depth</b> or <b>optical thickness</b>, is the <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_logarithm" title="Natural logarithm">natural logarithm</a></i> of the ratio of incident to <i>transmitted</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiant_power" title="Radiant power">radiant power</a> through a material, and <b>spectral optical depth</b> or <b>spectral optical thickness</b> is the natural logarithm of the ratio of incident to <i>transmitted</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiant_power" title="Radiant power">spectral radiant power</a> through a material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoldBook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GoldBook-1">[1]</a></sup> Optical depth is <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless</a>, and in particular is not a length, though it is a <a href="/wiki/Monotonic_function" title="Monotonic function"> monotonically</a> increasing function of path length, and approaches zero as the path length approaches zero. The use of the term "optical density" for optical depth is discouraged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GoldBook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GoldBook-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, a closely related quantity called "<a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorbance</a>" or "decadic absorbance" is used instead of optical depth: the <i><a href="/wiki/Common_logarithm" title="Common logarithm">common logarithm</a></i> of the ratio of incident to <i>transmitted</i> radiant power through a material, that is the optical depth divided by ln 10.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle"
