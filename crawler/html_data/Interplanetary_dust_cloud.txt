consists of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">cosmic dust</a> (small particles floating in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>) that pervades the space between <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> within <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">planetary systems</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. This system of particles has been studied for many years in order to understand its nature, origin, and relationship to larger bodies.\n</p><p>In our <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the interplanetary dust particles have a role in <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles" title="Light scattering by particles">scattering</a> <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> and in emitting <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a>, which is the most prominent feature of the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a>\'s radiation with wavelengths ranging 5&#8211;50 <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">&#956;m</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Particle_size" title="Particle size">particle sizes</a> of grains characterizing the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> emission near <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth\'s orbit">Earth\'s orbit</a> typically range 10&#8211;100 &#956;m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The total mass of the interplanetary dust cloud is approximately the mass of an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> of radius 15 km (with density of about 2.5 g/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Straddling the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, this dust cloud is visible as the <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light" title="Zodiacal light">zodiacal light</a> in a moonless and naturally dark  sky and is best seen toward the Sun\'s direction during astronomical <a href="/wiki/Twilight" title="Twilight">twilight</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer</a> spacecraft observations in the 1970s linked the Zodiacal light with the interplanetary dust cloud in Earth\'s solar system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The sources of interplanetary dust particles (IDPs) include at least: asteroid collisions, <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">cometary</a> activity and collisions in the inner Solar System, <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> collisions, and <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> grains (Backman, D., 1997). Indeed, one of the longest-standing controversies debated in the interplanetary dust community revolves around the relative contributions to the interplanetary dust cloud from asteroid collisions and cometary activity.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_of_a_particle">Life cycle of a particle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle of a particle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The main physical processes "affecting" (destruction or expulsion mechanisms) interplanetary dust particles are: expulsion by <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">radiation pressure</a>, inward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poynting-Robertson_drag" title="Poynting-Robertson drag">Poynting-Robertson (PR) radiation drag</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> pressure (with significant electromagnetic effects), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a>, mutual collisions, and the dynamical effects of planets (Backman, D., 1997).\n</p><p>The lifetimes of these dust particles are very short compared to the lifetime of the Solar System. If one finds grains around a star that is older than about 10,000,000 years, then the grains must have been from recently released fragments of larger objects, i.e. they cannot be leftover grains from the <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">protoplanetary disk</a> (Backman, private communication).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="We can\'t use private communications. Technically I ought to remove this claim. But I don\'t want to, because it\'s very interesting and seems likely to be supported &#8212; just someone needs to find an RS. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Therefore, the grains would be "later-generation" dust. The zodiacal dust in the Solar System is 99.9% later-generation dust and 0.1% intruding <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> dust. All primordial grains from the Solar System\'s formation were removed long ago.\n</p><p>Particles which are affected primarily by radiation pressure are known as "beta meteoroids". They are generally less than 1.4 &#215; 10<sup>&#8722;12</sup>&#160;g and are pushed outward from the Sun into interstellar space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_structures">Cloud structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cloud structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The interplanetary dust cloud has a complex structure (Reach, W., 1997). Apart from a background density, this includes:\n</p>\n<ul><li>At least 8 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dust_trail" title="Dust trail">dust trails</a>&#8212;their source is thought to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short-period_comet" title="Short-period comet">short-period comets</a>.</li>\n<li>A number of dust bands, the sources of which are thought to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_families" title="Asteroid families">asteroid families</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_asteroid_belt" title="Main asteroid belt">main asteroid belt</a>. The three strongest bands arise from the <a href="/wiki/Themis_family" title="Themis family">Themis family</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Koronis_family" title="Koronis family">Koronis family</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Eos_family" title="Eos family">Eos family</a>. Other source families include the <a href="/wiki/Maria_family" title="Maria family">Maria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eunomia_family" title="Eunomia family">Eunomia</a>, and possibly the <a href="/wiki/Vesta_family" title="Vesta family">Vesta</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Hygiea_family" title="Hygiea family">Hygiea</a> families (Reach et al. 1996).</li>\n<li>At least 2 resonant dust rings are known (for example, the Earth-resonant dust ring, although every planet in the Solar System is thought to have a resonant ring with a "wake") (Jackson and Zook, 1988, 1992) (Dermott, S.F. et al., 1994, 1997)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dust_collection_on_Earth">Dust collection on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dust collection on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1951, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fred_Whipple" title="Fred Whipple">Fred Whipple</a> predicted that micrometeorites smaller than 100 micrometers in diameter might be decelerated on impact with the Earth\'s upper atmosphere without melting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whipple-1950_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whipple-1950-6">[6]</a></sup> The modern era of laboratory study of these particles began with the stratospheric collection flights of D. E. Brownlee and collaborators in the 1970s using balloons and then <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a> aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownlee-1977_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brownlee-1977-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although some of the particles found were similar to the material in present-day meteorite collections, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoporous" title="Nanoporous">nanoporous</a> nature and unequilibrated cosmic-average composition of other particles suggested that they began as fine-grained aggregates of nonvolatile building blocks and cometary ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker-1986_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-1986-9">[9]</a></sup> The interplanetary nature of these particles was later verified by <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudson-1981_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hudson-1981-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flare</a> track<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley-1984_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley-1984-11">[11]</a></sup> observations.\n</p><p>In that context a program for atmospheric collection and curation of these particles was developed at <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center" title="Johnson Space Center">Johnson Space Center</a> in Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cosmic-Dust-Sample-Collection_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cosmic-Dust-Sample-Collection-12">[12]</a></sup> This stratospheric micrometeorite collection, along with <a href="/wiki/Presolar_grains" title="Presolar grains">presolar grains</a> from meteorites, are unique sources of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_material" title="Extraterrestrial material">extraterrestrial material</a> (not to mention being small astronomical objects in their own right) available for study in laboratories today.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments">Experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Spacecraft that have carried dust detectors include  <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">Pioneer 11</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">Ulysses</a></i> (heliocentric orbit out to the distance of Jupiter), <i><a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo</a></i> (Jupiter Orbiter), <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini&#8211;Huygens">Cassini</a></i> Counter">Venetia Burney Student Dust Counter</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
