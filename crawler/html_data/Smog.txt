href="/wiki/Science_History_Institute" title="Science History Institute">Science History Institute</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Smog</b> is a type of severe air pollution. The word "smog" was coined in the early 20th century as a blending of the words <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> to refer to smoky fog, its opacity, and odor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The word was then intended to refer to what was sometimes known as <a href="/wiki/Pea_soup_fog" title="Pea soup fog">pea soup fog</a>, a familiar and serious problem in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> from the 19th century to the mid-20th century. This kind of visible air pollution is composed of nitrogen oxides, sulphur oxides, ozone, smoke and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulates</a>. Man-made smog is derived from coal combustion emissions, vehicular emissions, industrial emissions, forest and agricultural fires and photochemical reactions of these emissions.\n</p><p>Smog is often categorized as being either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summer_smog" title="Summer smog">summer smog</a> or winter smog. Summer smog is primarily associated with the photochemical formation of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>. During the summer season when the temperatures are warmer and there is more sunlight present, photochemical smog is the dominant type of smog formation. During the winter months when the temperatures are colder, and <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">atmospheric inversions</a> are common, there is an increase in coal and other fossil fuel usage to heat homes and buildings. These combustion emissions, together with the lack of pollutant <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling" title="Atmospheric dispersion modeling">dispersion</a> under inversions, characterize winter smog formation. While photochemical smog is the main smog formation mechanism during summer months, winter smog episodes are still common. Smog formation in general relies on both primary and secondary pollutants. Primary pollutants are emitted directly from a source, such as emissions of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> from coal combustion. Secondary pollutants, such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, are formed when primary pollutants undergo chemical reactions in the atmosphere.\n</p><p>Photochemical smog, as found for example in Los Angeles, is a type of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vehicular_emission" title="Vehicular emission">vehicular emission</a> from <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a> and industrial fumes. These pollutants react in the atmosphere with sunlight to form secondary pollutants that also combine with the primary emissions to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_smog" title="Photochemical smog">photochemical smog</a>. In certain other cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, smog severity is often aggravated by <a href="/wiki/Stubble_burning" title="Stubble burning">stubble burning</a> in neighboring agricultural areas. The atmospheric pollution levels of Los Angeles, Beijing, Delhi, Lahore, Mexico City, Tehran and other cities are often increased by an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> that traps pollution close to the ground. The developing smog is usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> to humans and can cause severe sickness, a shortened life span, or premature death.\n</p>\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Coinage of the term "smog" is generally attributed to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Antoine_Des_Voeux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Henry Antoine Des Voeux (page does not exist)">Dr. Henry Antoine Des Voeux</a> in his 1905 paper, "Fog and Smoke" for a meeting of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Health_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Public Health Congress (page does not exist)">Public Health Congress</a>. The 26 July 1905 edition of the London newspaper <i>Daily Graphic</i> quoted Des Voeux, "He said it required no science to see that there was something produced in great cities which was not found in the country, and that was smoky fog, or what was known as \'smog\'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The following day the newspaper stated that "Dr. Des Voeux did a public service in coining a new word for the London fog."  However, this is predated by a <i>Los Angeles Times</i> article of January 19, 1893, in which the word is attributed to "a witty English writer".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Coal fires can emit significant clouds of smoke that contribute to the formation of winter smog. Coal fires can be used to heat individual buildings or to provide energy in a power-producing plant. Air pollution from this source has been reported in England since the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-middleages3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-middleages3-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-middleages22_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-middleages22-4">[4]</a></sup>London, in particular, was notorious up through the mid-20th century for its coal-caused smogs, which were nicknamed \'<a href="/wiki/Pea_soup_fog" title="Pea soup fog">pea-soupers</a>.\' Air pollution of this type is still a problem in areas that generate significant smoke from burning coal. The emissions from coal combustion are one of the main causes of air pollution in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Especially during autumn and winter when coal-fired heating ramps up, the amount of produced smoke at times forces some Chinese cities to close down roads, schools or airports. One prominent example for this was China\'s Northeastern city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Harbin_smog" title="2013 Harbin smog">Harbin in 2013</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_emissions">Transportation emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transportation emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Traffic emissions &#8211; such as from <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>&#8211; also contribute to the formation of smog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Airborne <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a> from vehicle <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_system" title="Exhaust system">exhaust systems</a> cause <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and are a major ingredient in the creation of smog in some large cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The major culprits from transportation sources are carbon monoxide (CO),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queensland2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-queensland2-12">[12]</a></sup>nitrogen oxides (NO and NO<sub>x</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa-no22_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa-no22-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_NOx2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_NOx2-15">[15]</a></sup>volatile organic compounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queensland2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-queensland2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa-no22_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa-no22-13">[13]</a></sup> and hydrocarbons (hydrocarbons are the main component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleum_fuels" title="Petroleum fuels">petroleum fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queensland2_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-queensland2-12">[12]</a></sup> Transportation emissions also include sulfur dioxides and particulate matter but in much smaller quantities than the pollutants mentioned previously. The nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds can undergo a series of chemical reactions with sunlight, heat, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, moisture, and other compounds to form the noxious vapors, ground level <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, and particles that comprise smog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queensland2_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-queensland2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa-no22_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-epa-no22-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photochemical_smog">Photochemical smog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photochemical smog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photochemical_smog_formation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="1297" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Photochemical_smog_formation.png/220px-Photochemical_smog_formation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Photochemical_smog_formation.png/330px-Photochemical_smog_formation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Photochemical_smog_formation.png/440px-Photochemical_smog_formation.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photochemical_smog_formation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The photochemical smog formation diagram. (Based on U 6.3.3 in mrgsciences.com<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>)</div></div></div>\n<p>Photochemical smog, often referred to as <b>summer smog</b>, is the chemical reaction of sunlight, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> in the atmosphere, which leaves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">airborne particles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">ground-level ozone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Photochemical smog depends on primary pollutants as well as the formation of secondary pollutants. These primary pollutants include <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (NO) and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> (NO<sub>2</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a>. The relevant secondary pollutants include <a href="/wiki/Peroxyacyl_nitrates" title="Peroxyacyl nitrates">peroxylacyl nitrates</a> (PAN), <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">tropospheric ozone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a>. An important secondary pollutant for photochemical smog is ozone, which is formed when hydrocarbons (HC) and nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>) combine in the presence of sunlight; nitrogen dioxide (NO<sub>2</sub>), which is formed as nitric oxide (NO) combines with oxygen in the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In addition, when SO<sub>2</sub> and NO<sub>x</sub> are emitted they eventually are oxidized in the troposphere to <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, which, when mixed with water, form the main components of acid rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> All of these harsh chemicals are usually highly reactive and oxidizing. Photochemical smog is therefore considered to be a problem of modern industrialization. It is present in all modern cities, but it is more common in cities with sunny, warm, dry climates and a large number of motor vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Because it travels with the wind, it can affect sparsely populated areas as well.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PLANE_(SUPPLIED_BY_NASA)_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2036" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg/220px-PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg/330px-PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg/440px-PLANE_%28SUPPLIED_BY_NASA%29_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PLANE_(SUPPLIED_BY_NASA)_USES_SPACE_TECHNIQUES_IN_SMOG_RESEARCH._SCIENTISTS_FROM_STATEWIDE_AIR_-_NARA_-_542670.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airplane used to collect airborne hydrocarbons, May 1972</div></div></div>\n<p>The composition and chemical reactions involved in photochemical smog were not understood until the 1950s. In 1948, flavor chemist <a href="/wiki/Arie_Jan_Haagen-Smit" title="Arie Jan Haagen-Smit">Arie Haagen-Smit</a> adapted some of his equipment to collect chemicals from polluted air, and identified ozone as a component of Los Angeles smog. Haagen-Smit went on to discover that nitrogen oxides from automotive exhausts and gaseous hydrocarbons from cars and oil refineries, exposed to sunlight, were key ingredients in the formation of ozone and photochemical smog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hundred2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-hundred2-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>219&#8211;224</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner2-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kean2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kean2-23">[23]</a></sup> Haagen-Smit worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnold_Beckman" title="Arnold Beckman">Arnold Beckman</a>, who developed various equipment for detecting smog, ranging from an "Apparatus for recording gas concentrations in the atmosphere" patented on October 7, 1952, to "air quality monitoring vans" for use by government and industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hundred2_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-hundred2-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>224&#8211;226</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_Reactions">Formation and Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation and Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>During the morning rush hour, a high concentration of nitric oxide and hydrocarbons are emitted to the atmosphere, mostly via on-road traffic but also from industrial sources. By replacing Reaction 6 with Reaction 2 and Reaction 3, the O<sub>3</sub> molecule is no longer destroyed. Therefore, the concentration of ozone keeps increasing throughout the day. This mechanism can escalate the formation of ozone in smog. Other reactions such as the photooxidation of formaldehyde (HCHO), a common secondary pollutant, can also contribute to the increased concentration of ozone and NO<sub>2</sub>. Photochemical smog is more prevalent during summer days since incident solar radiation fluxes are high, which favors the formation of ozone (reactions 4 and 5). The presence of a temperature inversion layer is another important factor. That is because it prevents the vertical convective mixing of the air and thus allows the pollutants, including ozone, to accumulate near the ground level, which again favors the formation of photochemical smog.\n</p><p>There are certain reactions that can limit the formation of O<sub>3</sub> in smog. The main limiting reaction in polluted areas is:\n</p><p>(7) <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\ce {NO2 + OH{.}+ M -&gt; HNO3 + M}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<msubsup>\n<mtext>NO</mtext>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mrow>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mspace depth=".2em" height="0pt" width="0pt"></mspace>\n</mrow>\n</msubsup>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mtext>OH</mtext>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mo>&#8901;<!-- &#8901; --></mo>\n</mrow>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mtext>M</mtext>\n<mo stretchy="false">&#10230;<!-- &#10230; --></mo>\n<msubsup>\n<mtext>HNO</mtext>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mn>3</mn>\n</mrow>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mspace depth=".2em" height="0pt" width="0pt"></mspace>\n</mrow>\n</msubsup>\n<mo>+</mo>\n<mtext>M</mtext>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle {\\ce {NO2 + OH{.}+ M -&gt; HNO3 + M}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\displaystyle {\\ce {NO2 + OH{.}+ M -&gt; HNO3 + M}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d3ada20c812a9c8bcf242659f8f2c0c9357b3b2" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:33.031ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>\n</p><p>This reaction removes NO<sub>2</sub> which limits the amount of O<sub>3</sub> that can be produced from its photolysis (reaction 4). HNO<sub>3</sub> is a sticky compound that can easily be removed onto surfaces (dry deposition) or dissolved in water and be rained out (wet deposition). Both ways are common in the atmosphere and can efficiently remove the radicals and nitrogen dioxide.\n</p>\n<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="239" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg/600px-Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg 1.5x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lightmatter_Golden_gate_bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The presence of smog in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> is shown near the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a>. The brown coloration is due to the NO<sub>2</sub> formed from photochemical smog reactions.</div></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_causes">Natural causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An erupting volcano can emit high levels of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> along with a large quantity of particulate matter; two key components to the creation of smog. However, the smog created as a result of a volcanic eruption is often known as <a href="/wiki/Vog" title="Vog">vog</a> to distinguish it as a natural occurrence. The chemical reactions that form smog following a volcanic eruption are different than the reactions that form photochemical smog. The term smog encompasses the effect when a large amount of gas phase molecules and particulate matter are emitted to the atmosphere, creating a visible haze. The event causing a large amount of emissions can vary but still result in the formation of smog.\n</p><p>Plants are another natural source of hydrocarbons that could undergo reactions in the atmosphere and produce smog. Globally both plants and soil contribute a substantial amount to the production of hydrocarbons, mainly by producing <a href="/wiki/Isoprene" title="Isoprene">isoprene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Hydrocarbons released by plants can often be more reactive than man-made hydrocarbons. For example when plants release isoprene, the isoprene reacts very quickly in the atmosphere with hydroxyl radicals. These reactions produce hydroperoxides which increase ozone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/File:LA_smog_masks.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Highland Park <a href="/wiki/Optimist_International" title="Optimist International">Optimist Club</a> wearing smog-gas masks at banquet, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, circa 1954</div></div></div>\n<p>Smog is a serious problem in many cities and continues to harm human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">Ground-level ozone</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> are especially harmful for senior citizens, children, and people with heart and lung conditions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airnow-smog_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-airnow-smog-28">[28]</a></sup> It can inflame breathing passages, decrease the lungs\' working capacity, cause shortness of breath, pain when inhaling deeply, wheezing, and coughing. It can cause eye and nose irritation and it dries out the protective membranes of the nose and throat and interferes with the body\'s ability to fight infection, increasing susceptibility to illness. Hospital admissions and respiratory deaths often increase during periods when ozone levels are high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> There is a lack of knowledge on the long-term effects of air pollution exposure and the origin of asthma. An experiment was carried out using intense air pollution similar to that of the 1952 Great Smog of London. The results from this  experiment concluded that there is a link between early-life pollution exposure that leads to the development of asthma, proposing the ongoing effect of the Great Smog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\nModern studies continue to find links between mortality and the presence of smog. One study, published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_magazine" title="Nature magazine">Nature magazine</a>, found that smog episodes in the city of Jinan, a large city in eastern China, during 2011&#8211;15, were associated with a 5.87% (95% CI 0.16&#8211;11.58%) increase in the rate of overall mortality. This study highlights the effect of exposure to air pollution on the rate of mortality in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Levels_of_unhealthy_exposure">Levels of unhealthy exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Levels of unhealthy exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. EPA</a> has developed an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Quality_Index" title="Air Quality Index">Air Quality Index</a> to help explain air pollution levels to the general public. 8 hour average ozone concentrations of 85 to 104 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_notation" title="Parts per notation">ppbv</a> are described as "Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups", 105 ppbv to 124 ppbv as "unhealthy" and 125 ppb to 404 ppb as "very unhealthy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airnow-smog_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-airnow-smog-28">[28]</a></sup> The "very unhealthy" range for some other pollutants are: 355 &#956;g m<sup>&#8722;3</sup> &#8211; 424 &#956;g m<sup>&#8722;3</sup> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PM10" title="PM10">PM10</a>; 15.5 ppm &#8211; 30.4ppm for CO and 0.65 ppm &#8211; 1.24 ppm for NO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premature_deaths_due_to_cancer_and_respiratory_disease">Premature deaths due to cancer and respiratory disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Premature deaths due to cancer and respiratory disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Medical_Association" title="Ontario Medical Association">Ontario Medical Association</a> announced that smog is responsible for an estimated 9,500 premature deaths in the province each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>A 20-year <a href="/wiki/American_Cancer_Society" title="American Cancer Society">American Cancer Society</a> study found that cumulative exposure also increases the likelihood of premature death from a respiratory disease, implying the 8-hour standard may be insufficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alzheimer_risk">Alzheimer risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alzheimer risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tiny magnetic particles from air pollution have for the first time been discovered to be lodged in human brains&#8211; and researchers think they could be a possible cause of Alzheimer&#8217;s disease.\nResearchers at Lancaster University found abundant <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> nanoparticles in the brain tissue from 37 individuals aged three to 92-years-old who lived in Mexico City and Manchester. This strongly magnetic mineral is toxic and has been implicated in the production of reactive oxygen species (free radicals) in the human brain, which are associated with neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer&#8217;s disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a Because of the smokeless zones, reduced levels of sooty particulates eliminated the intense and persistent London smog.\n</p><p>It was after this that the great clean-up of London began. One by one, historical buildings which, during the previous two centuries had gradually completely blackened externally, had their stone facades cleaned and restored to their original appearance. Victorian buildings whose appearance changed dramatically after cleaning included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="British Museum of Natural History">British Museum of Natural History</a>. The over-reliance on vehicles for transportation in these regions, combined with the additional effects of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> and Los Angeles/<a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a> port complexes, frequently contribute to further air pollution.\n</p><p>Los Angeles in particular is strongly predisposed to accumulation of smog, because of peculiarities of its geography and weather patterns. Los Angeles is situated in a flat basin with ocean on one side and mountain ranges on three sides. A nearby cold ocean current depresses surface air temperatures in the area, resulting in an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion layer</a>: a phenomenon where air temperature increases, instead of decreasing, with altitude, suppressing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermals" title="Thermals">thermals</a> and restricting vertical convection. All taken together, this results in a relatively thin, enclosed layer of air above the city that cannot easily escape out of the basin and tends to accumulate pollution.\n</p><p>Los Angeles was one of the best known cities suffering from transportation smog for much of the 20th century, so much so that it was sometimes said that <i>Los Angeles</i> was a synonym for <i>smog.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In 1970, when the Clean Air Act was passed, Los Angeles was the most polluted basin in the country, and California was unable to create a State Implementation Plan that would enable it to meet the new air quality standards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>. However, ensuing strict regulations by state and federal government agencies overseeing this problem (such as the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>), including tight restrictions on allowed emissions levels for all new cars sold in California and mandatory regular emission tests of older vehicles, resulted in significant improvements in air quality. For example, air concentrations of volatile organic compounds declined by a factor of 50 between 1962 and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Concentrations of air pollutants such as nitrous oxides and ozone declined by 70% to 80% over the same period of time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_incidents_in_the_U.S.">Major incidents in the U.S.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Major incidents in the U.S.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li>1943, July 26, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>: A smog so sudden and severe that "Los Angeles residents believe the Japanese are attacking them with chemical warfare."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/1948_Donora_smog" title="1948 Donora smog">1948, October 30&#8211;31</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donora,_Pennsylvania" title="Donora, Pennsylvania">Donora, Pennsylvania</a>: 20 died, 600 hospitalized, thousands more stricken. An estimated 150,000 households, mainly living in traditional Mongolian <a href="/wiki/Yurt" title="Yurt">gers</a> on the outskirts of Ulaanbaatar, burn wood and coal (some poor families burn even car tires and trash) to heat themselves during the harsh winter, which lasts from October to April, since these outskirts are not connected to the city\'s central heating system. A temporary solution to decrease smog was proposed in the form of stoves with improved efficiency, although with no visible results. \nCoal-fired ger stoves release high levels of ash and other particulate matter (PM). When inhaled, these particles can settle in the lungs and respiratory tract and cause health problems. At two to 10 times above Mongolian and international air quality standards, Ulaanbaatar\'s PM rates are among the worst in the world, according to a December 2009 World Bank report. The Asian Development Bank (ADB) estimates that health costs related to this air pollution account for as much as 4 percent of Mongolia\'s GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">Southeast Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Asia">Southeast Asia</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">land and forest fires</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" Farmers and plantation owners are usually responsible for the fires, which they use to clear tracts of land for further plantings. Those fires mainly affect <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> The economic losses of the fires in 1997 have been estimated at more than US$9 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> This includes damages in agriculture production, destruction of forest lands, health, transportation, tourism, and other economic endeavours. Not included are social, environmental, and psychological problems and long-term health effects. The <a href="/wiki/2006_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="2006 Southeast Asian haze">second-latest bout of haze</a> to occur in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malacca_Straits" title="Malacca Straits">Malacca Straits</a> is in October 2006, and was caused by smoke from fires in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> being blown across the Straits of Malacca by south-westerly winds. A similar haze has occurred in June 2013, with the PSI setting a <a href="/wiki/Pollutant_Standards_Index#PSI_in_Singapore" title="Pollutant Standards Index">new record</a> in Singapore on June 21 at 12pm with a reading of 401, which is in the "Hazardous" range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Southeast_Asian_Nations" title="Association of Southeast Asian Nations">Association of Southeast Asian Nations</a> (ASEAN) reacted. In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/ASEAN_Agreement_on_Transboundary_Haze_Pollution" title="ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution">Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution</a> was signed between all ASEAN nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> ASEAN formed a Regional Haze Action Plan (RHAP) and established a co-ordination and support unit (CSU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> RHAP, with the help of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, established a monitoring and warning system for forest/vegetation fires and implemented a Fire Danger Rating System (FDRS). The Malaysian Meteorological Department (MMD) has issued a daily rating of fire danger since September 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Indonesia has been ineffective at enforcing legal policies on errant farmers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pollution_over_S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(Jardins).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="1024" Haze however can also be an indication of poor air quality though this is often better reflected using accurate purpose built air indexes such as the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Quality_Index" title="Air Quality Index">Air Quality Index</a>, the Malaysian API (Air Pollution Index) and the Singaporean <a href="/wiki/Pollutant_Standards_Index" title="Pollutant Standards Index">Pollutant Standards Index</a>.\n</p><p>In hazy conditions, it is likely that the index will report the suspended particulate level. The disclosure of the responsible pollutant is mandated in some jurisdictions.\n</p><p>The Malaysian API does not have a capped value; hence its most hazardous readings can go above 500. Above 500, a state of emergency is declared in the affected area. Usually, this means that non-essential government services are suspended, and all ports in the affected area are closed. There may also be prohibitions on private sector commercial and industrial activities in the affected area excluding the food sector. So far, state of emergency rulings due to hazardous API levels were applied to the Malaysian towns of Port Klang, Kuala Selangor and the state of Sarawak during the <a href="/wiki/2005_Malaysian_haze" title="2005 Malaysian haze">2005 Malaysian haze</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1997_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="1997 Southeast Asian haze">1997 Southeast Asian haze</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Smog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Claude_Monet_015.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="889" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Claude_Monet_015.jpg/220px-Claude_Monet_015.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Claude_Monet_015.jpg/330px-Claude_Monet_015.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Claude_Monet_015.jpg/440px-Claude_Monet_015.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Claude_Monet_015.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a> made several trips to London between 1899 and 1901, during which he painted views of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">Thames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a> which show the sun struggling to shine through London\'s smog-laden atmosphere.</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>The London "<a href="/wiki/Pea_soup_fog" title="Pea soup fog">pea-soupers</a>" earned the capital the nickname of "The Smoke".
