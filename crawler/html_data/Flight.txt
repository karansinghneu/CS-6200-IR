(physics)">object</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">moves</a> through an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> (or beyond it, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a>) without contact with the surface. This can be achieved by generating <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">aerodynamic lift</a> associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_propulsion" title="Air propulsion">propulsive thrust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lighter_than_air" title="Lighter than air">aerostatically</a> using <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, or by <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistic</a> movement.\n</p><p>Many things can fly, from natural aviators such as <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, to human inventions like <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> which may carry <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>.\n</p><p>The engineering aspects of flight are the purview of <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a> which is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>, the study of vehicles that travel through the air, and <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>, the study of vehicles that travel through space, and in <a href="/wiki/Ballistics" title="Ballistics">ballistics</a>, the study of the flight of projectiles.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buoyant_flight">Buoyant flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buoyant flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">Aerostat</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goodyear-blimp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="182" data-file-width="458" decoding="async" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Goodyear-blimp.jpg/220px-Goodyear-blimp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Goodyear-blimp.jpg/330px-Goodyear-blimp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Goodyear-blimp.jpg/440px-Goodyear-blimp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goodyear-blimp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An airship flies because the upward force, from air displacement, is equal to or greater than the force of gravity</div></div></div>\n<p>Humans have managed to construct lighter than air vehicles that raise off the ground and fly, due to their <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> in air.\n</p><p>An <b>aerostat</b> is a system that remains aloft primarily through the use of <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> to give an aircraft the same overall density as air. Aerostats include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balloon_(aircraft)" title="Balloon (aircraft)">free balloons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moored_balloon" title="Moored balloon">moored balloons</a>. An aerostat\'s main structural component is its <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/envelope" title="wikt:envelope">envelope</a>, a lightweight <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> that encloses a volume of <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lifting gas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to provide <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, to which other components are attached.\n</p><p>Aerostats are so named because they use "aerostatic" lift, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoyant" title="Buoyant">buoyant</a> force that does not require lateral movement through the surrounding air mass to effect a lifting force. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft#Heavier_than_air_.E2.80.93_aerodynes" title="Aircraft">aerodynes</a> primarily use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">aerodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>, which requires the lateral movement of at least some part of the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> through the surrounding air mass.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerodynamic_flight">Aerodynamic flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aerodynamic flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Unpowered_flight_versus_powered_flight">Unpowered flight versus powered flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unpowered flight versus powered flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unpowered_flight" title="Unpowered flight">Unpowered flight</a></div>\n<p>Some things that fly do not generate propulsive thrust through the air, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Flying_squirrel" title="Flying squirrel">flying squirrel</a>. This is termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gliding_(flight)" title="Gliding (flight)">gliding</a>. Some other things can exploit rising air to climb such as <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> (when gliding) and <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">man-made sailplane gliders</a>. This is termed <a href="/wiki/Lift_(soaring)" title="Lift (soaring)">soaring</a>. However most other birds and all <a href="/wiki/Powered_aircraft" title="Powered aircraft">powered aircraft</a> need a source of <a href="/wiki/Propulsion" title="Propulsion">propulsion</a> to climb. This is termed powered flight.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_flight">Animal flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a title="Bat">bats</a>, while many groups have evolved gliding. The extinct <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">Pterosaurs</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> of reptiles contemporaneous with the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>, were also very successful flying animals. Each of these groups\' <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wings</a> <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">independently</a>. The wings of the flying vertebrate groups are all based on the forelimbs, but differ significantly in structure; those of insects are hypothesized to be highly modified versions of structures that form gills in most other groups of <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">Bats</a> are the only <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> capable of sustaining level flight (see <i><a href="/wiki/Bat_flight" title="Bat flight">bat flight</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, there are several <a href="/wiki/Flying_squirrel" title="Flying squirrel">gliding mammals</a> which are able to glide from tree to tree using fleshy membranes between their limbs; some can travel hundreds of meters in this way with very little loss in height. <a href="/wiki/Flying_frog" title="Flying frog">Flying frogs</a> use greatly enlarged webbed feet for a similar purpose, and there are <a href="/wiki/Draco_blanfordii" title="Draco blanfordii">flying lizards</a> which fold out their mobile ribs into a pair of flat gliding surfaces. <a href="/wiki/Chrysopelea" title="Chrysopelea">"Flying" snakes</a> also use mobile ribs to flatten their body into an aerodynamic shape, with a back and forth motion much the same as they use on the ground.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Flying_fish" title="Flying fish">Flying fish</a> can glide using enlarged wing-like fins, and have been observed soaring for hundreds of meters. It is thought that this ability was chosen by <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> because it was an effective means of escape from underwater predators. The longest recorded flight of a flying fish was 45 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-aa-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> fly (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Bird_flight" title="Bird flight">bird flight</a></i>), with some exceptions. The largest birds, the <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Emu" title="Emu">emu</a>, are earthbound, as were the now-extinct <a href="/wiki/Dodo" title="Dodo">dodos</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phorusrhacids" title="Phorusrhacids">Phorusrhacids</a>, which were the dominant predators of South America in the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> era. The non-flying <a href="/wiki/Penguin" title="Penguin">penguins</a> have wings adapted for use under water and use the same wing movements for swimming that most other birds use for flight.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most small flightless birds are native to small islands, and lead a lifestyle where flight would offer little advantage.\n</p><p>Among living animals that fly, the <a href="/wiki/Wandering_albatross" title="Wandering albatross">wandering albatross</a> has the greatest wingspan, up to 3.5 meters (11 feet); the <a href="/wiki/Great_bustard" title="Great bustard">great bustard</a> has the greatest weight, topping at 21 kilograms (46 pounds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most species of <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> can fly as adults. <a href="/wiki/Insect_flight" title="Insect flight">Insect flight</a> makes use of either of two basic aerodynamic models: creating a leading edge vortex, found in most insects, and using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clap_and_fling" title="Clap and fling">clap and fling</a>, found in very small insects such as <a href="/wiki/Thrips" title="Thrips">thrips</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical">Mechanical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Supersonic flight is associated with the formation of <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a> that form a <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a> that can be heard from the ground,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and is frequently startling. This shockwave takes quite a lot of energy to create and this makes supersonic flight generally less efficient than subsonic flight at about 85% of the speed of sound.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypersonic">Hypersonic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hypersonic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic_speed" title="Hypersonic speed">Hypersonic speed</a></div>\n<p>Hypersonic flight is very high speed flight where the heat generated by the compression of the air due to the motion through the air causes chemical changes to the air. no lift and move only or mostly under the action of momentum, gravity, air drag and in some cases thrust. This is termed <i>ballistic flight</i>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Ball" title="Ball">balls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">arrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firework" title="Firework">fireworks</a> etc.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceflight">Spaceflight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Spaceflight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">Spaceflight</a></div>\n<p>Essentially an extreme form of ballistic flight, <b>spaceflight</b> is the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_technology" title="Space technology">space technology</a> to achieve the flight of <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> into and through <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital spaceflight</a> etc.\n</p><p>Spaceflight is used in <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, and also in commercial activities like <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications_satellite" title="Telecommunications satellite">satellite telecommunications</a>. Additional non-commercial uses of spaceflight include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_observatory" title="Space observatory">space observatories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellites</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">earth observation satellites</a>.\n</p><p>A spaceflight typically begins with a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket_launch" title="Rocket launch">launch</a>, which provides the initial thrust to overcome the force of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and propels the spacecraft from the surface of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Once in space, the motion of a spacecraft&#8212;both when unpropelled and when under propulsion&#8212;is covered by the area of study called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">astrodynamics</a>. Some spacecraft remain in space indefinitely, some disintegrate during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric reentry</a>, and others reach a planetary or lunar surface for landing or impact.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a devices that fly, from the earliest projectiles such as stones and spears,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the\n<a href="/wiki/Boomerang" title="Boomerang">boomerang</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the hot air <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kongming_lantern" title="Kongming lantern">Kongming lantern</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kite" title="Kite">kites</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">Aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviation_history" title="Aviation history">Aviation history</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> studied flight scientifically in the first half of the 19th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctie_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctie-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in the second half of the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lilienthal" title="Otto Lilienthal">Otto Lilienthal</a> made over 200 gliding flights and was also one of the first to understand flight scientifically. His work was replicated and extended by the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> who made gliding flights and finally the first controlled and extended, manned powered flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaceflight_2">Spaceflight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Spaceflight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_spaceflight" title="History of spaceflight">History of spaceflight</a></div>\n<p>Spaceflight, particularly <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> became a reality in the 20th century following theoretical and practical breakthroughs by <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">first orbital spaceflight</a> was in 1957<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> was carried aboard the first manned orbital spaceflight in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">Physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luftschiff_small.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="138" data-file-width="320" decoding="async" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Luftschiff_small.jpg/220px-Luftschiff_small.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Luftschiff_small.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luftschiff_small.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lighter-than-air <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a> are able to fly without any major input of energy</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</a></div>\n<p>There are different approaches to flight. If an object has a lower <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> than air, then it is <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyant</a> and is able to <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">float in the air</a> without expending energy. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavier_than_air" title="Heavier than air">heavier than air</a> craft, known as an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft#Heavier_than_air_.80.93_aerodynes" title="Aircraft">aerodyne</a>, includes flighted animals and insects, <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a>. Because the craft is heavier than air, it must generate <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> to overcome its <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>. The wind resistance caused by the craft moving through the air is called <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> and is overcome by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_propulsion" title="Air propulsion">propulsive thrust</a> except in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gliding_(flight)" title="Gliding (flight)">gliding</a>.\n</p><p>Some vehicles also use thrust for flight, for example <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrier_Jump_Jet" title="Harrier Jump Jet">Harrier Jump Jets</a>.\n</p><p>Finally, <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> dominates the flight of ballistic flying objects.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forces">Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Forces">edit</a><span <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forces2.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main forces on a heavier-than-air aircraft</div></div></div>\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">Thrust</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aeroforces.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="417" data-file-width="909" decoding="async" 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aeroforces.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Forces on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerofoil" title="Aerofoil">aerofoil</a> cross section</div></div></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a> generates forward thrust when air is pushed in the direction opposite to flight.  This can be done in several ways including by the spinning blades of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propeller</a>, or a rotating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_fan" title="Mechanical fan">fan</a> pushing air out from the back of a <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engine</a>, or by ejecting hot gases from a <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  The forward thrust is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of the airstream multiplied by the difference in <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the airstream. Reverse thrust can be generated to aid braking after landing by reversing the pitch of variable-pitch propeller blades, or using a <a href="/wiki/Thrust_reversal" title="Thrust reversal">thrust reverser</a> on a jet engine. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_wing_aircraft" title="Rotary wing aircraft">Rotary wing aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrust_vectoring" title="Thrust vectoring">thrust vectoring</a> <a href="/wiki/V/STOL" title="V/STOL">V/STOL</a> aircraft use engine thrust to support the weight of the aircraft, and vector sum of this thrust fore and aft to control forward speed.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift">Lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift (force)</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2484" data-file-width="4504" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/300px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/450px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/600px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lift is defined as the component of the total aerodynamic force perpendicular to the flow direction, and drag is the component parallel to the flow direction</div></div></div>\n<p>In the context of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_flow" title="Fluid flow">air flow</a> relative to a flying body, the <b>lift</b> force is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometric)#Vector_components" title="Vector (geometric)">component</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_force" title="Aerodynamic force">aerodynamic force</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to the flow direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Aerodynamic lift results when the wing causes the surrounding air to be deflected - the air then causes a force on the wing in the opposite direction, in accordance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_third_law_of_motion" title="Newton\'s third law of motion">Newton\'s third law of motion</a>.\n</p><p>Lift is commonly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">aircraft</a>, although lift is also generated by <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">rotors</a> on <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a> (which are effectively rotating wings, performing the same function without requiring that the aircraft move forward through the air). While common meanings of the word "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lift#English" title="wikt:lift">lift</a>" suggest that lift opposes gravity, aerodynamic lift can be in any direction. When an aircraft is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_(flight)" title="Cruise (flight)">cruising</a> for example, lift does oppose gravity, but lift occurs at an angle when climbing, descending or banking. On high-speed cars, the lift force is directed downwards (called "down-force") to keep the car stable on the road.\n</p><p>Lift can also occur in a different way if the air is not still, especially if there is an updraft due to heat ("thermals") or wind blowing along sloping terrain or other meteorological conditions. This form of lift permits <a href="/wiki/Lift_(soaring)" title="Lift (soaring)">soaring</a> and is particularly important for gliding. It is used by birds and gliders to stay in the air for long periods with little effort.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Drag">Drag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Drag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">Drag (physics)</a></div>\n<p>For a solid object moving through a fluid, the drag is the component of the <a href="/wiki/Net_force" title="Net force">net</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_force" title="Aerodynamic force">aerodynamic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamics" title="Hydrodynamics">hydrodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> acting opposite to the direction of the movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAdrag_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdrag-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Therefore, drag opposes the motion of the object, and in a powered vehicle it must be overcome by <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a>. The process which creates lift also causes some drag.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift-to-drag_ratio">Lift-to-drag ratio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Lift-to-drag ratio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="707" decoding="async" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/220px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/330px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg/440px-Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drag_curves_for_aircraft_in_flight.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Speed and drag relationships for a typical aircraft</div></div></div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lift-to-drag_ratio" title="Lift-to-drag ratio">Lift-to-drag ratio</a></div>\n<p>Aerodynamic lift is created by the motion of an aerodynamic object (wing) through the air, which due to its shape and angle deflects the air. For sustained straight and level flight, lift must be equal and opposite to weight. In general, long narrow wings are able deflect a large amount of air at a slow speed, whereas smaller wings need a higher forward speed to deflect an equivalent amount of air and thus generate an equivalent amount of lift. Large cargo aircraft tend to use longer wings with higher angles of attack, whereas supersonic aircraft tend to have short wings and rely heavily on high forward speed to generate lift.\n</p><p>However, this lift (deflection) process inevitably causes a retarding force called drag. Because lift and drag are both aerodynamic forces, the ratio of lift to drag is an indication of the aerodynamic efficiency of the airplane. The lift to drag ratio is the L/D ratio, pronounced "L over D ratio." An airplane has a high L/D ratio if it produces a large amount of lift or a small amount of drag. The lift/drag ratio is determined by dividing the lift coefficient by the drag coefficient, CL/CD. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The lift coefficient Cl is equal to the lift L divided by the (density r times half the velocity V squared times the wing area A). [Cl = L / (A * .5 * r * V^2)] The lift coefficient is also affected by the compressibility of the air, which is much greater at higher speeds, so velocity V is not a linear function. Compressibility is also affected by the shape of the aircraft surfaces.\n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The drag coefficient Cd is equal to the drag D divided by the (density r times half the velocity V squared times the reference area A). [Cd = D / (A * .5 * r * V^2)] \n<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lift-to-drag ratios for practical aircraft vary from about 4:1 for vehicles and birds with relatively short wings, up to 60:1 or more for vehicles with very long wings, such as gliders. A greater angle of attack relative to the forward movement also increases the extent of deflection, and thus generates extra lift. However a greater angle of attack also generates extra drag. \n</p><p>Lift/drag ratio also determines the glide ratio and gliding range. Since the glide ratio is based only on the relationship of the aerodynamics forces acting on the aircraft, aircraft weight will not affect it. The only effect weight has is to vary the time that the aircraft will glide for &#8211; a heavier aircraft gliding at a higher airspeed will arrive at the same touchdown point in a shorter time. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buoyancy">Buoyancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Buoyancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">Buoyancy</a></div>\n<p>Air pressure acting up against an object in air is greater than the pressure above pushing down. The buoyancy, in both cases, is equal to the weight of fluid displaced - <a href="/wiki/Archimedes%27_principle" title="Archimedes\' principle">Archimedes\' principle</a> holds for air just as it does for water.\n</p><p>A cubic meter of air at ordinary <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> and room temperature has a mass of about 1.2 kilograms, so its weight is about 12 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newtons</a>. Therefore, any 1-cubic-meter object in air is buoyed up with a force of 12 newtons. If the mass of the 1-cubic-meter object is greater than 1.2 kilograms (so that its weight is greater than 12 newtons), it falls to the ground when released. If an object of this size has a mass less than 1.2 kilograms, it rises in the air. Any object that has a mass that is less than the mass of an equal volume of air will rise in air - in other words, any object less dense than air will rise.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> to <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> (where weight means weight at the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s standard Different objects and creatures capable of flight vary in the efficiency of their muscles, motors and how well this translates into forward thrust.\n</p><p>Propulsive efficiency determines how much energy vehicles generate from a unit of fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range">Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_(aircraft)" title="Range (aircraft)">range (aircraft)</a></div>\n<p>The range that powered flight articles can achieve is ultimately limited by their drag, as well as how much energy they can store on board and how efficiently they can turn that energy into propulsion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For powered aircraft the useful energy is determined by their <a href="/wiki/Fuel_fraction" title="Fuel fraction">fuel fraction</a>- what percentage of the takeoff weight is fuel, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> of the fuel used.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power-to-weight_ratio">Power-to-weight ratio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Power-to-weight ratio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">power-to-weight ratio</a></div>\n<p>All animals and devices capable of sustained flight need relatively high power-to-weight ratios to be able to generate enough lift and/or thrust to achieve take off.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Takeoff_and_landing">Takeoff and landing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Takeoff and landing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Takeoff_and_landing" title="Takeoff and landing">takeoff and landing</a></div>\n<p>Vehicles that can fly can have different ways to <b>takeoff and land</b>. Conventional aircraft accelerate along the ground until sufficient lift is generated for <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a>, and reverse the process for <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a>. Some aircraft can take off at low speed; this is called a short takeoff. Some aircraft such as helicopters and Harrier jump jets can take off and land vertically. Rockets also usually take off and land vertically, but some designs can land horizontally.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Guidance.2C_navigation_and_control"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidance,_navigation_and_control">Guidance, navigation and control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Guidance, navigation and control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guidance,_navigation_and_control" title="Guidance, navigation and control">Guidance, navigation and control</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navigation">Navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">Navigation</a> is the systems necessary to calculate current position (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/LORAN" title="LORAN">LORAN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star_tracker" title="Star tracker">star tracker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inertial_measurement_unit" title="Inertial measurement unit">inertial measurement unit</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a>).\n</p><p>In aircraft, successful <a href="/wiki/Air_navigation" title="Air navigation">air navigation</a> involves piloting an aircraft from place to place without getting lost, breaking the laws applying to aircraft, or endangering the safety of those on board or on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">ground</a>.\n</p><p>The techniques used for navigation in the air will depend on whether the aircraft is flying under the <a href="/wiki/Visual_flight_rules" title="Visual flight rules">visual flight rules</a> (VFR) or the <a href="/wiki/Instrument_flight_rules" title="Instrument flight rules">instrument flight rules</a> (IFR). In the latter case, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a> will navigate exclusively using <a href="/wiki/Flight_instruments" title="Flight instruments">instruments</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_navigation_aid" title="Radio navigation aid">radio navigation aids</a> such as beacons, or as directed under <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> control by <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a>. In the VFR case, a pilot will largely navigate using <a href="/wiki/Dead_reckoning" title="Dead reckoning">dead reckoning</a> combined with visual observations (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilotage" title="Pilotage">pilotage</a>), with reference to appropriate maps. This may be supplemented using radio navigation aids.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidance">Guidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Guidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Guidance_system" title="Guidance system">Guidance system</a></div>\n<p>A <b>guidance system</b> is a device or group of devices used in the <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, or other moving object. Typically, guidance is responsible for the calculation of the vector (i.e., direction, velocity) toward an objective.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_control_system" title="Flight control system">Flight control system</a></div>\n<p>A conventional fixed-wing <b>aircraft flight control system</b> consists of <a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces" title="Flight control surfaces">flight control surfaces</a>, the respective cockpit controls, connecting linkages, and the necessary operating mechanisms to control an aircraft\'s direction in flight. <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine_controls" title="Aircraft engine controls">Aircraft engine controls</a> are also considered as flight controls as they change speed.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the case of aircraft, air traffic is controlled by <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> systems.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Collision_avoidance_(spacecraft)" title="Collision avoidance (spacecraft)">Collision avoidance</a> is the process of controlling spacecraft to try to prevent collisions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_safety">Flight safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Flight safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_safety" title="Aviation safety">aviation safety</a></div>\n<p><b>Air safety</b> is a term encompassing the theory, investigation and categorization of <a href="/wiki/Aviation_accidents_and_incidents" title="Aviation accidents and incidents">flight failures</a>, and the prevention of such failures through regulation, education and training. It can also be applied in the context of campaigns that inform the public as to the safety of <a href="/wiki/Air_travel" title="Air travel">air travel</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="1376" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/30px-Commons-logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/45px-Commons-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Commons-logo.svg/59px-Commons-logo.svg.png 2x" width="30"/></td>\n<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Wikimedia Commons has media related to <i><b><a class="extiw" href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Flight" title="commons:Category:Flight">Flight</a></b></i>.</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">Aerodynamics</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Levitation" title="Levitation">Levitation</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transvection_(flying)" title="Transvection (flying)">Transvection (flying)</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<dl><dt>Notes</dt></dl>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 33em; Cayley described many of the concepts and elements of the modern aeroplane and was the first to understand and explain in engineering terms the concepts of lift and thrust.</q></cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=U.S.+Centennial+of+Flight+Commission+%E2%80%93+Sir+George+Cayley&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.centennialofflight.gov%2Fessay%2FPrehistory%2FCayley%2FPH2.htm&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3AFlight"></span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r879151008" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-16"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-16">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://www.shapell.org/btl.aspx?2972360" rel="nofollow">"Orville Wright\'s Personal Letters on Aviation."</a> <i>Shapell Manuscript Foundation</i>, (Chicago), 2012.</span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external free" href="https://history.nasa.gov/sputnik/sputorig.html" rel="nofollow">https://history.nasa.gov/sputnik/sputorig.html</a></span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-18"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-18">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="external text" href="http://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/shuttle/sts1/gagarin_anniversary.html" rel="nofollow">"Gagarin anniversary."</a> <i>NASA</i>.
