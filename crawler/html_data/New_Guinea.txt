href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Nieuw-Guinea</i>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Neuguinea</i>; <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a>: <i lang="id">Papua</i>, is a large island separated by a shallow sea from the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australian continent</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area" title="List of islands by area">world\'s second-largest</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, covering a land area of 785,753&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (303,381&#160;sq&#160;mi), and the largest wholly or partly within the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>.\n</p><p>The eastern half of the island is the major land mass of the independent state of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>. The western half, referred to as either <a href="/wiki/Western_New_Guinea" title="Western New Guinea">Western New Guinea</a> or West Papua, has been administered by <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> since 1962 and comprises the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Papua_(province)" title="Papua (province)">Papua</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Papua_(province)" title="West Papua (province)">West Papua</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" means "not united" or, "territory that geographically is far away (and thus not united)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geop_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-geop-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ploeg reports that the word <i>papua</i> is often said to derive from the Malay word <i>papua</i> or <i>pua-pua</i>, meaning "frizzly-haired", referring to the highly curly hair of the inhabitants of these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Another possibility, put forward by Sollewijn Gelpke in 1993, is that it comes from the <a href="/wiki/Biak_language" title="Biak language">Biak</a> phrase <i>sup i papwa</i> which means \'the land below [the sunset]\' and refers to the islands west of the <a href="/wiki/Bird%27s_Head_Peninsula" title="Bird\'s Head Peninsula">Bird\'s Head</a>, as far as <a href="/wiki/Halmahera" title="Halmahera">Halmahera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Whatever its origin, the name <i>Papua</i> came to be associated with this area, and more especially with Halmahera, which was known to the Portuguese by this name during the era of their colonization in this part of the world.\n</p><p>When the Portuguese and Spanish explorers arrived in the island via the <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Spice Islands</a>, they also referred to the island as <i>Papua</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geop_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-geop-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the name <i>New Guinea</i> was later used by Westerners starting with the Spanish explorer <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%B1igo_Ortiz_de_Retez" title="Y&#241;igo Ortiz de Retez">Y&#241;igo Ortiz de Retez</a> in 1545, referring to the similarities of the indigenous people\'s appearance with the natives of the <a href="/wiki/Guinea_(region)" title="Guinea (region)">Guinea region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geop_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-geop-2">[2]</a></sup> The name is one of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toponyms" title="Toponyms">toponyms</a> sharing similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placename_etymology" title="Placename etymology">etymologies</a>, ultimately meaning "land of the blacks" or similar meanings, in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Dark_skin" title="Dark skin">dark skin</a> of the inhabitants.\n</p><p>The Dutch, who arrived later under <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Le_Maire" title="Jacob Le Maire">Jacob Le Maire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willem_Schouten" title="Willem Schouten">Willem Schouten</a>, called it <i>Schouten island</i>, but later this name was used only to refer to islands off the north coast of Papua proper, the <a href="/wiki/Schouten_Islands" title="Schouten Islands">Schouten Islands</a> or Biak Island. When the Dutch colonized it as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Netherlands_East_Indies" title="Netherlands East Indies">Netherlands East Indies</a>, they called it <i>Nieuw Guinea</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geop_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-geop-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The name <i>Irian</i> was used in the Indonesian language to refer to the island and Indonesian province, as "Irian Jaya Province". The name was promoted in 1945 by Marcus Kaisiepo,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tides_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tides-1">[1]</a></sup> brother of the future governor <a href="/wiki/Frans_Kaisiepo" title="Frans Kaisiepo">Frans Kaisiepo</a>. It is taken from the <a href="/wiki/Biak_language" title="Biak language">Biak language</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biak_Island" title="Biak Island">Biak Island</a>, and means "to rise", or "rising spirit". <i>Irian</i> is the name used in the Biak language and other languages such as Serui, Merauke and Waropen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geop_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-geop-2">[2]</a></sup> The name was used until 2001, when the name <i>Papua</i> was again used for the island and the province. The name <i>Irian</i>, which was originally favored by natives, is now considered to be a name imposed by the authority of <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tides_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tides-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg/170px-Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg/255px-Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg/340px-Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oceania_UN_Geoscheme_-_Map_of_Melanesia.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regions of <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>: <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>. Physiographically, Australasia includes the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Australia_(landmass)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Australia (landmass) (page does not exist)">Australian landmass</a> (including Tasmania), New Zealand, and New Guinea.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" Guinea map of K&#246;ppen climate classification</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newguinea_topo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1409" data-file-width="2500" decoding="async" height="186" href="/wiki/File:Newguinea_topo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Topographical map of New Guinea</div></div></div>\n<p>New Guinea is an island to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Mainland_Australia" title="Mainland Australia">Australian mainland</a>, but south of the equator. It is isolated by the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Sea</a> to the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait" title="Torres Strait">Torres Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coral_Sea" title="Coral Sea">Coral Sea</a> to the east. Sometimes considered to be the easternmost island of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_archipelago" title="Indonesian archipelago">Indonesian archipelago</a>, it lies north of Australia\'s <a href="/wiki/Top_End" title="Top End">Top End</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_York_peninsula" title="Cape York peninsula">Cape York peninsula</a>, and west of the <a href="/wiki/Bismarck_Archipelago" title="Bismarck Archipelago">Bismarck Archipelago</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands_(archipelago)" title="Solomon Islands (archipelago)">Solomon Islands Archipelago</a>.\n</p><p>Politically, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Papua_(region)" title="West Papua (region)">western half of the island</a> comprises two <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Indonesia" title="Provinces of Indonesia">provinces of Indonesia</a>: <a href="/wiki/Papua_(province)" title="Papua (province)">Papua</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Papua_(province)" title="West Papua (province)">West Papua</a>. The eastern half forms the <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">mainland</a> of the country of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> (PNG).\n</p><p>The shape of New Guinea is often compared to that of a <a href="/wiki/Paradisaea" title="Paradisaea">bird-of-paradise</a> (indigenous to the island), and this results in the usual names for the two extremes of the island: the <a href="/wiki/Bird%27s_Head_Peninsula" title="Bird\'s Head Peninsula">Bird\'s Head Peninsula</a> in the northwest (<i>Vogelkop</i> in Dutch, <i>Kepala Burung</i> in Indonesian; also known as the Doberai Peninsula), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird%27s_Tail_Peninsula" title="Bird\'s Tail Peninsula">Bird\'s Tail Peninsula</a> in the southeast (also known as the Papuan Peninsula).\n</p><p>A spine of east&#8211;west mountains, the <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_Highlands" title="New Guinea Highlands">New Guinea Highlands</a>, dominates the geography of New Guinea, stretching over 1,600&#160;km (1,000&#160;mi) from the \'head\' to the \'tail\' of the island, with many high mountains over 4,000&#160;m (13,100&#160;ft). The <a href="/wiki/Western_New_Guinea" title="Western New Guinea">western-half</a> of the island of New Guinea contains the highest mountains in <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>, rising up to 4,884&#160;m (16,024&#160;ft) high, which is even higher than <a href="/wiki/Mont_Blanc" title="Mont Blanc">Mont Blanc</a> in Europe, ensuring a steady supply of rain from the equatorial atmosphere. The <a href="/wiki/Tree_line" title="Tree line">tree line</a> is around 4,000&#160;m (13,100&#160;ft) elevation and the tallest peaks contain permanent equatorial <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>&#8212;which have been retreating since at least 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Various other smaller mountain ranges occur both north and west of the central ranges. Except in high elevations, most areas possess a warm humid climate throughout the year, with some seasonal variation associated with the northeast monsoon season.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_of_New_Guinea" title="List of highest mountains of New Guinea">highest peaks on the island of New Guinea</a> are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Puncak_Jaya" title="Puncak Jaya">Puncak Jaya</a>, sometimes known by its former Dutch name <i>Carstensz Pyramid</i>, is a mist-covered limestone mountain peak on the Indonesian side of the border. At 4,884 metres (16,024&#160;ft), Puncak Jaya makes New Guinea the world\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_highest_point" title="List of islands by highest point">fourth-highest landmass</a> after <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Puncak_Mandala" title="Puncak Mandala">Puncak Mandala</a>, also located in Papua, is the second-highest peak on the island at 4,760 metres (15,617&#160;ft).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Puncak_Trikora" title="Puncak Trikora">Puncak Trikora</a>, also in Papua, is 4,750 metres (15,584&#160;ft).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilhelm" title="Mount Wilhelm">Mount Wilhelm</a> is the highest peak on the PNG side of the border at 4,509 metres (14,793&#160;ft). Its granite peak is the highest point of the Bismarck Range.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mount_Giluwe" title="Mount Giluwe">Mount Giluwe</a> 4,368 metres (14,331&#160;ft) is the second-highest summit in PNG. It is also the highest volcanic peak in Oceania.</li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg/220px-Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg/330px-Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg/440px-Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Bosavi_-rim_of_crater-14Oct2008.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mount_Bosavi" title="Mount Bosavi">Mount Bosavi</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Another major habitat feature is the vast southern and northern lowlands. Stretching for hundreds of kilometres, these include lowland <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>, extensive wetlands, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> grasslands, and some of the largest expanses of <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forest in the world. The southern lowlands are the site of <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_National_Park" title="Lorentz National Park">Lorentz National Park</a>, also a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. The northern lowlands are drained principally by the <a href="/wiki/Mamberamo_River" title="Mamberamo River">Mamberamo River</a> and its tributaries on the Indonesian side, and by the <a href="/wiki/Sepik" title="Sepik">Sepik</a> on the PNG side; the more extensive southern lowlands by a larger number of rivers, principally the <a href="/wiki/Digul" title="Digul">Digul</a> on the Indonesian side and the <a href="/wiki/Fly_River" title="Fly River">Fly</a> on the PNG side. These are the island\'s major river systems, draining roughly northwest, northeast, southwest, and southeast, respectively. Many have broad areas of meander and result in large areas of <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and freshwater swamps. The largest island offshore, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolak" title="Dolak">Dolak</a> (Frederik Hendrik, Yos Sudarso), lies near the Digul estuary, separated by a strait so narrow it has been named a "creek".\n</p><p>New Guinea contains many of the world\'s ecosystem types: glacial, <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine tundra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montane" title="Montane">montane</a> and lowland rainforest, <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, lake and <a href="/wiki/River_ecosystem" title="River ecosystem">river ecosystems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seagrass" title="Seagrass">seagrasses</a>, and some of the richest <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> on the planet.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_surroundings">Relation to surroundings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to surroundings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island of New Guinea lies to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>, with which it is sometimes included as part of a greater Indo-Australian Archipelago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wallace_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wallace-9">[9]</a></sup> Geologically it is a part of the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> as Australia. When world sea levels were low, the two shared shorelines (which now lie 100 to 140 metres below sea level),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aims_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-aims-10">[10]</a></sup> and combined with lands now inundated into the tectonic continent of <a href="/wiki/Sahul_Shelf" title="Sahul Shelf">Sahul</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ballard-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_1977_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen_1977-12">[12]</a></sup> also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Australia" title="Greater Australia">Greater Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The two landmasses became separated when the area now known as the Torres Strait flooded after the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>.\n</p><p>Anthropologically, New Guinea is considered part of <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>New Guinea is differentiated from its drier, flatter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and less fertile<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> southern counterpart, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, by its much higher rainfall and its active volcanic geology, with its highest point, <a href="/wiki/Puncak_Jaya" title="Puncak Jaya">Puncak Jaya</a>, reaching an elevation of 4,884 m (16,023 ft). Yet the two land masses share a similar animal fauna, with marsupials, including <a href="/wiki/Wallaby" title="Wallaby">wallabies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phalangeriformes" title="Phalangeriformes">possums</a>, and the egg-laying monotreme, the <a href="/wiki/Echidna" title="Echidna">echidna</a>. Other than bats and some two dozen indigenous rodent genera,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> there are no pre-human indigenous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placental_mammal" title="Placental mammal">placental mammals</a>. Pigs, several additional species of rats, and the ancestor of the <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_singing_dog" title="New Guinea singing dog">New Guinea singing dog</a> were introduced with human <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a>.\n</p><p>Prior to the 1970s, archaeologists called the single <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> landmass by the name <i>Australasia</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ballard-11">[11]</a></sup> although this word is most often used for a wider region that includes lands, such as <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, which are not on the same continental shelf. In the early 1970s, they introduced the term <i>Greater Australia</i> for the Pleistocene continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-ballard-11">[11]</a></sup> Then, at a 1975 conference and consequent publication,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_1977_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allen_1977-12">[12]</a></sup> they extended the name <i>Sahul</i> from its previous use for just the <a href="/wiki/Sahul_Shelf" title="Sahul Shelf">Sahul Shelf</a> to cover the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballard_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-ballard-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_presence">Human presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Human presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The human presence on the island dates back at least 40,000 years, to the oldest homo sapiens migrations out of Africa. Research indicates that the highlands were an early and independent center of agriculture, with evidence of irrigation going back at least 10,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Because of the time depth of its inhabitation and its highly fractured landscape, an unusually high number of languages are spoken on the island, with some 1,000 languages (a figure higher than that of most continents) having been catalogued out of an estimated worldwide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> total of more than 7,000 currently spoken human languages according to <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a>. Most are classified as <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">Papuan languages</a>, a generally accepted geographical term. A number of <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian languages</a> are spoken on the coast and on offshore islands.\n</p><p>In the 16th century, Spanish explorers arrived at the island and called it <i>Nueva Guinea</i>. In recent history, western New Guinea was included in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> colony. The Germans annexed the northern coast of the eastern half of the island as <a href="/wiki/German_New_Guinea" title="German New Guinea">German New Guinea</a> in their pre&#8211;World War I effort to establish themselves as a colonial power, whilst the southeastern portion was reluctantly claimed by Britain. Following the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a>, the German portion was awarded to Australia (which was already governing the British claim, named the <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Papua" title="Territory of Papua">Territory of Papua</a>) as a <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> mandate. The eastern half of the island was granted independence from Australia in 1975, as Papua New Guinea. The western half gained independence from the Dutch in 1961, but became part of Indonesia soon afterwards in <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Free_Choice" title="Act of Free Choice">controversial circumstances</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_divisions">Political divisions</span><span People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Papua_New_Guinea" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Yali_man_Baliem_Valley_Papua.jpg/400px-Yali_man_Baliem_Valley_Papua.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a title="Baliem Valley">Baliem Valley</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The current population of the island of New Guinea is about eleven million. Many believe human habitation on the island dates to as early as 50,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">BC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and first settlement possibly dating back to 60,000 years ago has been proposed. The island is presently populated by almost a thousand different tribal groups and a near-equivalent number of separate languages, which makes New Guinea the most linguistically diverse area in the world. <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a>\'s 14th edition lists 826 languages of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> and 257 languages of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irian_Jaya" title="Irian Jaya">Irian Jaya</a>, total 1073 languages, with 12 languages overlapping. They fall into one of two groups, the <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">Papuan languages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian languages</a>.\n</p><p>The separation was not merely linguistic; <a href="/wiki/Endemic_warfare" title="Endemic warfare">warfare</a> among societies was a factor in the evolution of the <i>men\'s house</i>: separate housing of groups of adult men, from the single-family houses of the women and children, for mutual protection from other tribal groups. <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">Pig</a>-based trade between the groups and pig-based feasts are a common theme with the other peoples of southeast Asia and Oceania. Most societies practice <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, supplemented by <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunting and gathering</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="740" decoding="async" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg/230px-Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg/345px-Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg/460px-Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kurulu_Village_War_Chief.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kurulu Village War Chief at Baliem Valley</div></div></div>\n<p>The great variety of the island\'s indigenous populations is frequently assigned to one of two main ethnological divisions, based on archaeological, linguistic and genetic evidence: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papuan_peoples" title="Papuan peoples">Papuan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austronesian_people" title="Austronesian people">Austronesian</a> groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Current evidence indicates that the Papuans (who constitute the majority of the island\'s peoples) are descended from the earliest human inhabitants of New Guinea. These original inhabitants first arrived in New Guinea at a time (either side of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, approx 21,000 years ago) when the island was connected to the Australian continent via a <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridge</a>, forming the landmass known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahul_continent" title="Sahul continent">Sahul</a>. These peoples had made the (shortened) sea-crossing from the islands of <a href="/wiki/Wallacea" title="Wallacea">Wallacea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sundaland" title="Sundaland">Sundaland</a> (the present <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">Malay Archipelago</a>) by at least 40,000 years ago, subsequent to the dispersal of peoples from Africa (circa) 50,000 &#8211; 70,000 years ago.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KorowaiHombre01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/KorowaiHombre01.jpg/220px-KorowaiHombre01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/KorowaiHombre01.jpg/330px-KorowaiHombre01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/KorowaiHombre01.jpg/440px-KorowaiHombre01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KorowaiHombre01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Korowai_people" title="Korowai people">Korowai</a> tribesman</div></div></div>\n<p>The ancestral Austronesian peoples are believed to have arrived considerably later, approximately 3,500 years ago, as part of a gradual seafaring migration from <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, possibly originating in Taiwan. Austronesian-speaking peoples colonized many of the offshore islands to the north and east of New Guinea, such as <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_(island)" title="New Ireland (island)">New Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Britain" title="New Britain">New Britain</a>, with settlements also on the coastal fringes of the main island in places. Human habitation of New Guinea over tens of thousands of years has led to a great deal of diversity, which was further increased by the later arrival of the Austronesians and the more recent history of European and Asian settlement through events like <a href="/wiki/Transmigration_program" title="Transmigration program">transmigration</a>. About half of the 2.4&#160;million inhabitants of Indonesian Papua are <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Javanese</a> migrants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celerier_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-celerier-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Large areas of New Guinea are yet to be explored by scientists and anthropologists. The Indonesian province of <a href="/wiki/West_Papua_(province)" title="West Papua (province)">West Papua</a> is home to an estimated 44 <a href="/wiki/Uncontacted_peoples" title="Uncontacted peoples">uncontacted tribal groups</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_and_ecology">Biodiversity and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biodiversity and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_New_Guinea" title="Fauna of New Guinea">Fauna of New Guinea</a></div>\n<p>With some 786,000 km<sup>2</sup> of tropical land&#8212;less than one-half of one percent (0.5%) of the Earth\'s surface&#8212;New Guinea has an immense <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, containing between 5 and 10 percent of the total species on the planet. This percentage is about the same amount as that found in the United States or Australia. A high percentage of New Guinea\'s species are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endemic_(ecology)" title="Endemic (ecology)">endemic</a>, and thousands are still unknown to science: probably well over 200,000 species of insect, between 11,000 and 20,000 plant species, and over 650 resident bird species. Most of these species are shared, at least in their origin, with the continent of Australia, which was until fairly recent geological times part of the same landmass (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australia-New_Guinea" title="Australia-New Guinea">Australia-New Guinea</a> for an overview). The island is so large that it is considered \'nearly a continent\' in terms of its biological distinctiveness.\n</p><p>In the period from 1998 to 2008, conservationists identified 1,060 new species in New Guinea, including 218 plants, 43 reptiles, 12 mammals, 580 invertebrates, 134 amphibians, 2 birds and 71 fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a Botanically, New Guinea is considered part of <a href="/wiki/Malesia" title="Malesia">Malesia</a>, a floristic region that extends from the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a> across Indonesia to New Guinea and the <a href="/wiki/East_Melanesian_Islands" title="East Melanesian Islands">East Melanesian Islands</a>. The flora of New Guinea is a mixture of many <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a> species with origins in Asia, together with typically Australasian flora. Typical Southern Hemisphere flora include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">conifers</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Podocarpus" title="Podocarpus">Podocarpus</a></i> and the rainforest emergents <i><a href="/wiki/Araucaria" title="Araucaria">Araucaria</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Agathis" title="Agathis">Agathis</a>,</i> as well as <a href="/wiki/Tree_fern" title="Tree fern">tree ferns</a> and several species of <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">Eucalyptus</a></i>.\n</p><p>New Guinea has 284 species and six orders of mammals: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monotremes" title="Monotremes">monotremes</a>, three orders of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupials" title="Marsupials">marsupials</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>; 195 of the mammal species (69%) are endemic. New Guinea has 578 species of breeding birds, of which 324 species are endemic. The island\'s frogs are one of the most poorly known vertebrate groups, totalling 282 species, but this number is expected to double or even triple when all species have been documented. New Guinea has a rich diversity of coral life and 1,200 species of fish have been found. Also about 600 species of reef-building coral&#8212;the latter equal to 75 percent of the world\'s known total. The entire coral area covers 18 million hectares off a peninsula in northwest New Guinea.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" Guinea">History of Papua New Guinea</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Western_New_Guinea" title="History of Western New Guinea">History of Western New Guinea</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1007" data-file-width="1111" decoding="async" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png/200px-Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png/300px-Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png/400px-Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Sunda_and_Sahul.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The continent of Sahul before the rising ocean sundered Australia and New Guinea after the last ice age.</div></div></div>\n<p>The first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inhabitant" title="Inhabitant">inhabitants</a> of New Guinea arrived at least 50,000 years ago, having travelled through the south-east Asian peninsula. These first inhabitants, from whom the Papuan people are probably descended, adapted to the range of ecologies and, in time, developed one of the earliest known agricultures. Remains of this agricultural system, in the form of ancient irrigation systems in the highlands of Papua New Guinea, are being studied by archaeologists. This work is still in its early stages, so there is still uncertainty as to precisely what crop was being grown, or when/where agriculture arose. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">Sugar cane</a> was cultivated for the first time in New Guinea around 6000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The gardens of the <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_Highlands" title="New Guinea Highlands">New Guinea Highlands</a> are ancient, intensive <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permacultures</a>, adapted to high population densities, very high rainfalls (as high as 10,000 mm/yr (400 in/yr)), earthquakes, hilly land, and occasional frost. Complex mulches, crop rotations and tillages are used in rotation on terraces with complex irrigation systems. Western agronomists still do not understand all of the practices, and it has been noted that native gardeners are as, or even more, successful than most scientific farmers in raising certain crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> There is evidence that New Guinea gardeners invented crop rotation well before western Europeans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A unique feature of New Guinea permaculture is the <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silviculture</a> of <i>Casuarina oligodon</i>, a tall, sturdy native <a href="/wiki/Casuarinaceae" title="Casuarinaceae">ironwood</a> tree, suited to use for timber and fuel, with root nodules that fix nitrogen. <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Pollen studies</a> show that it was adopted during an ancient period of extreme deforestation.\n</p><p>In more recent millennia, another wave of people arrived on the shores of New Guinea. These were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austronesian_people" title="Austronesian people">Austronesian people</a>, who had spread down from <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Southeast_Asia" title="Maritime Southeast Asia">South-east Asian archipelago</a>, colonising many of the islands on the way. The Austronesian people had technology and skills extremely well adapted to ocean voyaging and Austronesian language speaking people are present along much of the coastal areas and islands of New Guinea. These Austronesian migrants are considered the ancestors of most people in insular Southeast Asia, from <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> to <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>, as well as coastal new Guinea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precolonial_history">Precolonial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Precolonial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House,_Mairy_Pass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2001" data-file-width="2915" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg/220px-Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg/330px-Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg/440px-Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House%2C_Mairy_Pass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Picturesque_New_Guinea_Plate_XXXIX_-_Group_and_Native_House,_Mairy_Pass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Group of natives at Mairy Pass. Mainland of British New Guinea in 1885.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg/220px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg/330px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg/440px-COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:COLLECTIE_TROPENMUSEUM_Papoea%27_s_op_de_Lorentzrivier_TMnr_10009529.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Papuans on the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_River" title="Lorentz River">Lorentz River</a>, photographed during the third South New Guinea expedition in 1912&#8211;13.</div></div></div>\n<p>The western part of the island was in contact with kingdoms in other parts of modern-day Indonesia. The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negarakertagama" title="Negarakertagama">Negarakertagama</a></i> mentioned the region of Wanin in eastern <a href="/wiki/Nusantara" title="Nusantara">Nusantara</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/Majapahit" title="Majapahit">Majapahit</a>\'s tributary. This has been identified with the Onin Peninsula, part of the <a href="/wiki/Bomberai_Peninsula" title="Bomberai Peninsula">Bomberai Peninsula</a> near the city of <a href="/wiki/Fakfak" title="Fakfak">Fakfak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The sultans of <a href="/wiki/Tidore" title="Tidore">Tidore</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Maluku Islands</a>, claimed sovereignty over various coastal parts of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crocombe_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-crocombe-35">[35]</a></sup> During Tidore\'s rule, the main exports of the island during this period were resins, spices, slaves and the highly priced feathers of the <a href="/wiki/Bird-of-paradise" title="Bird-of-paradise">bird-of-paradise</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crocombe_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-crocombe-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nuku_Muhammad_Amiruddin" title="Nuku Muhammad Amiruddin">Sultan Nuku</a>, one of the most famous Tidore sultans who rebelled against Dutch colonization, called himself "Sultan of Tidore and Papua",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuku_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-nuku-36">[36]</a></sup> during his revolt in 1780s. He commanded loyalty from both Moluccan and Papuan chiefs, especially those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raja_Ampat" title="Raja Ampat">Raja Ampat</a> Islands. Following Tidore\'s defeat, much of the territory it claimed in western part of New Guinea came under Dutch rule as part of Dutch East Indies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nuku_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-nuku-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_contact">European contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: European contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first European contact with New Guinea was by <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> sailors in the 16th century. In 1526&#8211;27, the Portuguese explorer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_de_Meneses" title="Jorge de Meneses">Jorge de Meneses</a> saw the western tip of New Guinea and named it <i>ilhas dos Papuas</i>. In 1528, the Spanish navigator <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Saavedra_Cer%C3%B3n" title="&#193;lvaro de Saavedra Cer&#243;n">&#193;lvaro de Saavedra</a> also recorded its sighting when trying to return from <a href="/wiki/Tidore" title="Tidore">Tidore</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>. In 1545, the Spaniard <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%B1igo_Ortiz_de_Retez" title="Y&#241;igo Ortiz de Retez">&#205;&#241;igo Ort&#237;z de Retes</a> sailed along the north coast of New Guinea as far as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mamberamo" title="Mamberamo">Mamberamo River</a>, near which he landed on 20 June, naming the island \'Nueva Guinea\'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The first map showing the whole island (as an island) was published in 1600 and shows it as \'Nova Guinea\'. In 1606, <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres" title="Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres">Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres</a> explored the southern coast of New Guinea from <a href="/wiki/Milne_Bay" title="Milne Bay">Milne Bay</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Papua" title="Gulf of Papua">Gulf of Papua</a> including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Orangerie_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Orangerie Bay (page does not exist)">Orangerie Bay</a>, which he named <i>Bah&#237;a de San Lorenzo</i>. His expedition also discovered <a href="/wiki/Basilaki_Island" title="Basilaki Island">Basilaki Island</a> naming it <i>Tierra de San Buenaventura</i>, which he claimed for <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in July 1606.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-38">[38]</a></sup> On 18 October, his expedition reached the western part of the island in present-day <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and also claimed the territory for the King of Spain.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_Guinea_(1884-1919).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="618" decoding="async" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png/250px-New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png/375px-New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png/500px-New_Guinea_%281884-1919%29.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_Guinea_(1884-1919).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Guinea from 1884 to 1919. <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">The Netherlands</a> controlled the western half of New Guinea, <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> the north-eastern part, and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">Britain</a> the south-eastern part.</div></div></div>\n<p>A successive European claim occurred in 1828, when the Netherlands formally claimed the western half of the island as <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_New_Guinea" title="Netherlands New Guinea">Netherlands New Guinea</a>. In 1883, following a short-lived French annexation of <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_(island)" title="New Ireland (island)">New Ireland</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_colony" title="British colony">British colony</a> of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> annexed south-eastern New Guinea. However, the Queensland government\'s superiors in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> revoked the claim, and (formally) assumed direct responsibility in 1884, when <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a> claimed north-eastern New Guinea as the protectorate of <a href="/wiki/German_New_Guinea" title="German New Guinea">German New Guinea</a> (also called <a href="/wiki/Kaiser-Wilhelmsland" title="Kaiser-Wilhelmsland">Kaiser-Wilhelmsland</a>).\n</p><p>The first Dutch government posts were established in 1898 and in 1902: Manokwari on the north coast, Fak-Fak in the west and Merauke in the south at the border with <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Papua" title="Territory of Papua">British New Guinea</a>. The German, Dutch and British colonial administrators each attempted to suppress the still-widespread practices of inter-village warfare and <a href="/wiki/Headhunting" title="Headhunting">headhunting</a> within their respective territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1905, the British government transferred some administrative responsibility over southeast New Guinea to Australia (which renamed the area "<a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Papua" title="Territory of Papua">Territory of Papua</a>"); and, in 1906, transferred all remaining responsibility to Australia. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Australian forces seized German New Guinea, which in 1920 became the <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_New_Guinea" title="Territory of New Guinea">Territory of New Guinea</a>, to be administered by Australia under a <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations_mandate" title="League of Nations mandate">mandate</a>. The territories under Australian administration became collectively known as The Territories of Papua and New Guinea (until February 1942).\n</p><p>Before about 1930, European maps showed the highlands as uninhabited forests.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> When first flown over by aircraft, numerous settlements with agricultural terraces and stockades were observed. The most startling discovery took place on 4 August 1938, when <a href="/wiki/Richard_Archbold" title="Richard Archbold">Richard Archbold</a> discovered the <a href="/wiki/Baliem_Valley" title="Baliem Valley">Grand Valley</a> of the Baliem River, which had 50,000 yet-undiscovered Stone Age farmers living in orderly villages. The people, known as the <a href="/wiki/Dani_people" title="Dani people">Dani</a>, were the last society of its size to make first contact with the rest of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_campaign" title="New Guinea campaign">New Guinea campaign</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="479" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg/220px-Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg/330px-Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg/440px-Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aust_soldiers_Finisterres.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Australian soldiers resting in the <a href="/wiki/Finisterre_Range" title="Finisterre Range">Finisterre Ranges</a> of New Guinea while en route to the front line</div></div></div>\n<p>Netherlands New Guinea and the Australian territories were invaded in 1942 by the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Japanese</a>. The Australian territories were put under military administration and were known simply as New Guinea. The highlands, northern and eastern parts of the island became key battlefields in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South West Pacific theatre of World War II">South West Pacific Theatre</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Papuans often gave vital assistance to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>, fighting alongside Australian troops, and carrying equipment and injured men across New Guinea. Approximately 216,000 Japanese, Australian and U.S. soldiers, sailors and airmen died during the New Guinea Campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_World_War_II">Since World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Since World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Papua_conflict" title="Papua conflict">Papua conflict</a></div>\n<p>Following the return to civil administration after World War II, the Australian section was known as the Territory of Papua-New Guinea from 1945 to 1949 and then as <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Papua_and_New_Guinea" title="Territory of Papua and New Guinea">Territory of Papua and New Guinea</a>. Although the rest of the Dutch East Indies achieved independence as Indonesia on 27 December 1949, the Netherlands regained control of western New Guinea.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_Guinea.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1533" data-file-width="2310" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/New_Guinea.png/220px-New_Guinea.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/New_Guinea.png/330px-New_Guinea.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/New_Guinea.png/440px-New_Guinea.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_Guinea.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of New Guinea, with place names as used in English in the 1940s</div></div></div>\n<p>During the 1950s, the Dutch government began to prepare <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_New_Guinea" title="Netherlands New Guinea">Netherlands New Guinea</a> for full independence and allowed elections in 1959; the elected <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_Council" title="New Guinea Council">New Guinea Council</a> took office on 5 April 1961. The Council decided on the name of West Papua for the territory, along with an emblem, <a href="/wiki/Morning_Star_flag" title="Morning Star flag">flag</a>, and anthem to complement those of the Netherlands. On 1 October 1962, the Dutch handed over the territory to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Temporary_Executive_Authority" title="United Nations Temporary Executive Authority">United Nations Temporary Executive Authority</a>, until 1 May 1963, when <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> took control. The territory was renamed West Irian and then Irian Jaya. In 1969, Indonesia, under the 1962 <a href="/wiki/New_York_Agreement" title="New York Agreement">New York Agreement</a>, organised a referendum named the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Free_Choice" title="Act of Free Choice">Act of Free Choice</a>, in which hand picked Papuan tribal elders reached a consensus to continue the union with Indonesia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>There has been resistance to Indonesian integration and occupation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celerier_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-celerier-23">[23]</a></sup> both through civil disobedience (such as <a href="/wiki/Morning_Star_flag" title="Morning Star flag">Morning Star flag</a> raising ceremonies) and via the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisasi_Papua_Merdeka" title="Organisasi Papua Merdeka">Organisasi Papua Merdeka</a> (OPM, or Free Papua Movement) in 1965. <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> has estimated more than 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">Papuans</a>, one-sixth of the population, have died as a result of government-sponsored violence against West Papuans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Westpapua.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="408" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Westpapua.png/240px-Westpapua.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Westpapua.png/360px-Westpapua.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Westpapua.png 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Westpapua.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Western_New_Guinea" title="Western New Guinea">Western New Guinea</a> was formally annexed by Indonesia in 1969</div></div></div>\n<p>From 1971, the name Papua New Guinea was used for the Australian territory. On 16 September 1975, Australia granted full independence to <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.\n</p><p>In 2000, Irian Jaya was formally renamed "The Province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papua_(Indonesian_province)" title="Papua (Indonesian province)">Papua</a>" and a Law on Special Autonomy was passed in 2001. The Law established a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Papuan_People%27s_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Papuan People\'s Assembly (page does not exist)">Papuan People\'s Assembly</a> (MRP) with representatives of the different indigenous cultures of Papua. The MRP was empowered to protect the rights of Papuans, raise the status of women in Papua, and to ease religious tensions in Papua; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_grants" title="Block grants">block grants</a> were given for the implementation of the Law as much as $266&#160;million in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_system_of_Indonesia" title="Court system of Indonesia">Indonesian courts</a>\' enforcement of the Law on Special Autonomy blocked further creation of subdivisions of Papua: although President <a href="/wiki/Megawati_Sukarnoputri" title="Megawati Sukarnoputri">Megawati Sukarnoputri</a> was able to create a separate <a href="/wiki/West_Papua_(province)" title="West Papua (province)">West Papua</a> province in 2003 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fait_accompli" title="Fait accompli">fait accompli</a>, plans for a third province on western New Guinea were blocked by the courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Critics argue that the Indonesian government has been reluctant to establish or issue various government implementing regulations so that the legal provisions of special autonomy could be put into practice, and as a result special autonomy in Papua has failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religions">Religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>See <a
