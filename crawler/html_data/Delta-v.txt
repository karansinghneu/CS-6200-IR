It is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics)" title="Scalar (mathematics)">scalar</a> that has the units of <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>. As used in this context, it is <i>not</i> the same as the <a href="/wiki/Delta-v_(physics)" title="Delta-v (physics)">physical change in velocity</a> of the vehicle.\n</p><p>As a simple example, take a conventional rocket which achieves thrust by burning fuel.  Delta-v is the change in velocity that can be achieved by burning that rocket\'s entire fuel load.\n</p><p>Delta-<i>v</i> is produced by <a href="/wiki/Reaction_engine" title="Reaction engine">reaction engines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a>, and is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> per unit mass and the burn time.  It is used to determine the mass of <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">propellant</a> required for the given maneuver through the <a href="/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_rocket_equation" title="Tsiolkovsky rocket equation">Tsiolkovsky rocket equation</a>.\n</p><p>For multiple maneuvers, delta-<i>v</i> sums linearly.\n</p><p>For interplanetary missions delta-<i>v</i> is often plotted on a <a href="/wiki/Porkchop_plot" title="Porkchop plot">porkchop plot</a>, which displays the required mission delta-<i>v</i> as a function of launch date.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input Propellant usage is an exponential function of delta-<i>v</i> in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_equation" title="Rocket equation">rocket equation</a>, it will also depend on the exhaust velocity.\n</p><p>It is not possible to determine delta-<i>v</i> requirements from <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conservation of energy</a> by considering only the total energy of the vehicle in the initial and final orbits since energy is carried away in the exhaust (see also below). For example, most spacecraft are launched in an orbit with inclination fairly near to the latitude at the launch site, to take advantage of the Earth\'s rotational surface speed. If it is necessary, for mission-based reasons, to put the spacecraft in an orbit of different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a>, a substantial delta-<i>v</i> is required, though the <a href="/wiki/Specific_kinetic_energy" title="Specific kinetic energy">specific kinetic</a> and potential energies in the final orbit and the initial orbit are equal.\n</p><p>When rocket thrust is applied in short bursts the other sources of acceleration may be negligible, and the magnitude of the velocity change of one burst may be simply approximated by the delta-<i>v</i>. The total delta-<i>v</i> to be applied can then simply be found by addition of each of the delta-<i>v\'</i>s needed at the discrete burns, even though between bursts the magnitude and direction of the velocity changes due to gravity, e.g. in an <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a>.\n</p><p>For examples of calculating delta-<i>v</i>, see <a href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit" title="Hohmann transfer orbit">Hohmann transfer orbit</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_slingshot" title="Gravitational slingshot">gravitational slingshot</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Transport_Network" title="Interplanetary Transport Network">Interplanetary Transport Network</a>. It is also notable that large thrust can reduce <a href="/wiki/Gravity_drag" title="Gravity drag">gravity drag</a>.\n</p><p>Delta-<i>v</i> is also required to keep satellites in orbit and is expended in propulsive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_stationkeeping" title="Orbital stationkeeping">orbital stationkeeping</a> maneuvers. Since the propellant load on most satellites cannot be replenished, the amount of propellant initially loaded on a satellite may well determine its useful lifetime.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oberth_effect">Oberth effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta-v&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oberth effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a></div>\n<p>From power considerations, it turns out that when applying delta-<i>v</i> in the direction of the velocity the <a href="/wiki/Specific_orbital_energy" title="Specific orbital energy">specific orbital energy</a> gained per unit delta-<i>v</i> is equal to the instantaneous speed. This is called the Oberth effect.\n</p><p>For example, a satellite in an elliptical orbit is boosted more efficiently at high speed (that is, small altitude) than at low speed (that is, high altitude).\n</p><p>Another example is that when a vehicle is making a pass of a planet, burning the propellant at closest approach rather than further out gives significantly higher final speed, and this is even more so when the planet is a large one with a deep gravity field, such as Jupiter.\n</p><p>See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_slingshot#Powered_slingshots" title="Gravitational slingshot">powered slingshots</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Porkchop_plot">Porkchop plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Delta-v&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Porkchop plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Porkchop_plot" title="Porkchop plot">Porkchop plot</a></div>\n<p>Due to the relative positions of planets changing over time, different delta-vs are required at different launch dates. A diagram that shows the required delta-<i>v</i> plotted against time is sometimes called a <i>porkchop plot</i>.
