V&#225;zquez de Coronado y Luj&#225;n</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[f&#638;an&#712;&#952;isko &#712;&#946;a&#952;ke&#952; &#240;e ko&#638;o&#712;na&#240;o]</a></span>; 1510 &#8211; 22 September 1554) was a <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a> and explorer who led a large expedition from <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> to present-day <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> through parts of the southwestern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> between 1540 and 1542. V&#225;zquez de Coronado had hoped to reach the Cities of C&#237;bola, often referred to now as the mythical <a href="/wiki/Seven_Cities_of_Gold" title="Seven Cities of Gold">Seven Cities of Gold</a>, which is a term not invented until American gold-rush days in the 1800s. His expedition marked the first European sightings of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>, among other landmarks. His name is often Anglicized as "Vasquez de Coronado".\n</p>\n<table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks" style="float:right;clear:right;width:22.0em;margin:0 0 1.0em href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado was born into a noble family in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salamanca,_Spain" title="Salamanca, Spain">Salamanca, Spain</a>, in 1510 as the second son of Juan V&#225;zquez de Coronado y Sosa de Ulloa and Isabel de Luj&#225;n. Juan V&#225;zquez held various positions in the administration of the recently captured <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">Emirate of Granada</a> under I&#241;igo L&#243;pez de Mendoza, its first Spanish governor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flint_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flint-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Francisco V&#225;zquez de Coronado went to <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>) in 1535 at about age 25, in the entourage of its first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Viceroys_of_New_Spain" title="List of Viceroys of New Spain">Viceroy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Mendoza" title="Antonio de Mendoza">Antonio de Mendoza</a>, the son of his father\'s patron and V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s personal friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flint_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flint-1">[1]</a></sup> In New Spain, he married twelve-year-old Beatriz de Estrada, called "the Saint" (<i>la Santa</i>), sister of Leonor de Estrada, ancestor of the de Alvarado family and daughter of <a href="/wiki/Treasurer" title="Treasurer">Treasurer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Governor" title="Governor">Governor</a> Alonso de Estrada y Hidalgo, Lord of <a href="/wiki/Pic%C3%B3n" title="Pic&#243;n">Pic&#243;n</a>, and wife Marina Flores Guti&#233;rrez <a href="/wiki/De_la_Caballeria" title="De la Caballeria">de la Caballer&#237;a</a>, from a <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">converso</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sefardi_Jews" title="Sefardi Jews">Jewish</a> family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> V&#225;zquez de Coronado inherited a large portion of a Mexican encomendero estate through Beatriz and had eight children by her. \n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition">Expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado was the Governor of the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Nueva_Galicia" title="Nueva Galicia">Nueva Galicia</a> (New Galicia), a province of New Spain located northwest of Mexico and comprising the contemporary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_state" title="Mexican state">Mexican states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa" title="Sinaloa">Sinaloa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nayarit" title="Nayarit">Nayarit</a>. In 1539, he dispatched Friar <a href="/wiki/Marcos_de_Niza" title="Marcos de Niza">Marcos de Niza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estevanico" title="Estevanico">Estevanico</a> (more properly known as Estevan), a survivor of the <a href="/wiki/Narv%C3%A1ez_expedition" title="Narv&#225;ez expedition">Narv&#225;ez expedition</a>, on an expedition north from <a href="/wiki/Compostela,_Nayarit" title="Compostela, Nayarit">Compostela</a> toward present-day <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>. When de Niza returned, he told of a city of vast wealth, a golden city called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quivira_and_C%C3%ADbola" title="Quivira and C&#237;bola">C&#237;bola</a>, whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuni_people" title="Zuni people">Zuni</a> residents were assumed to have killed Estevan. Though he did not claim to have entered the city of C&#237;bola, he mentioned that it stood on a high hill and that it appeared wealthy and as large as Mexico City.\n</p><p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado assembled an expedition with two components. One component carried the bulk of the expedition\'s supplies, traveling via the Guadalupe River under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Alarc%C3%B3n" title="Hernando de Alarc&#243;n">Hernando de Alarc&#243;n</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The other component traveled by land, along the trail on which Friar Marcos de Niza had followed Esteban. V&#225;zquez de Coronado and Viceroy <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Mendoza" title="Antonio de Mendoza">Antonio de Mendoza</a> invested large sums of their own money in the venture. Mendoza appointed V&#225;zquez de Coronado the commander of the expedition, with the mission to find the mythical <a href="/wiki/Seven_Cities_of_Gold" title="Seven Cities of Gold">Seven Cities of Gold</a>. This is the reason he pawned his wife\'s estates and was lent 70,000 pesos.\n</p><p>In the autumn of 1539, Mendoza ordered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melchior_D%C3%ADaz" title="Melchior D&#237;az">Melchior D&#237;az</a>, commander of the Spanish outpost at <a href="/wiki/Culiac%C3%A1n" title="Culiac&#225;n">San Miguel de Culiac&#225;n</a>, to investigate Friar de Niza\'s findings, and on November 17, 1539, D&#237;az departed for C&#237;bola with fifteen horsemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup> At the ruins of Chichilticalli, he turned around because of "snows and fierce winds from across the wilderness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup> D&#237;az had encountered V&#225;zquez de Coronado before he had departed San Miguel de Culiac&#225;n, and reported that initial investigations into Friar de Niza\'s report disproved the existence of the bountiful land he had described. D&#237;az\'s report was delivered to Viceroy Mendoza on March 20, 1540.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition_2">Expedition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expedition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coronado_expedition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="1230" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Coronado_expedition.jpg/350px-Coronado_expedition.jpg" tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coronado-Remington.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="768" href="/wiki/File:Coronado-Remington.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Coronado Sets Out to the North,</i> by <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Remington" title="Frederic Remington">Frederic Remington</a>, 1861&#8211;1909</div></div></div>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado" title="V&#225;zquez de Coronado">V&#225;zquez de Coronado</a> set out from Compostela on February 23, 1540, at the head of a much larger expedition composed of about 400 <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> men-at-arms (mostly <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spaniards</a>), 1,300 to 2,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_Indian" title="Mexican Indian">Mexican Indian</a> allies, four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friars (the most notable of whom were <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Padilla" title="Juan de Padilla">Juan de Padilla</a> and the newly appointed <a href="/wiki/Provincial_superior" title="Provincial superior">provincial superior</a> of the Franciscan order in the New World, <a href="/wiki/Marcos_de_Niza" title="Marcos de Niza">Marcos de Niza</a>), and several slaves, both natives and Africans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Many other family members and servants also joined the party.\n</p><p>He followed the Sinaloan coast northward, keeping the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> on his left to the west until he reached the northernmost Spanish settlement in Mexico, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Miguel_de_Culiac%C3%A1n" title="San Miguel de Culiac&#225;n">San Miguel de Culiac&#225;n</a>, about March 28, 1540, whereupon he rested his expedition before they began trekking the inland trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Aside from his mission to verify Friar de Niza\'s report, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melchior_D%C3%ADaz" title="Melchior D&#237;az">Melchior D&#237;az</a> had also taken notice of the forage and food situation along the trail, and reported that the land along the route would not be able to support a large concentrated body of soldiers and animals. V&#225;zquez de Coronado, therefore, decided to divide his expedition into small groups and time their departures so that grazing lands and water holes along the trail could recover. At intervals along the trail, V&#225;zquez de Coronado established camps and stationed garrisons of soldiers to keep the supply route open. For example, in September 1540, Melchior D&#237;az, along with "seventy or eighty of the weakest and least reliable men" in V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s army, remained at the town of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Hieronimo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Hieronimo (page does not exist)">San Hieronimo</a>, in the valley of <a href="/wiki/Corazones" title="Corazones">Corazones</a>, or Hearts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Once the scouting and planning was done, V&#225;zquez de Coronado led the first group of soldiers up the trail. They were horsemen and foot soldiers who were able to travel quickly, while the main bulk of the expedition would set out later.\n</p><p>After leaving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culiacan" title="Culiacan">Culiacan</a> on April 22, 1540, V&#225;zquez de Coronado followed the coast, "bearing off to the left", as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mota_Padilla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mota Padilla (page does not exist)">Mota Padilla</a> says, by an extremely rough way, to the <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa_River" title="Sinaloa River">Sinaloa River</a>. The configuration of the country made it necessary to follow the river valley until he could find a passage across the mountains to the course of the <a href="/wiki/Yaqui_River" title="Yaqui River">Yaqui River</a>. He traveled alongside this stream for some distance, then crossed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_Sonora" title="Rio Sonora">Rio Sonora</a>, which he followed nearly to its source before a pass was discovered. On the southern side of the mountains he found a stream he called the <i>Nexpa</i>, which may have been either the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_River_(Arizona)" title="Santa Cruz River (Arizona)">Santa Cruz</a> or the <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro_River_(Arizona)" title="San Pedro River (Arizona)">San Pedro</a> in modern <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> of modern maps, most likely the northward-flowing San Pedro River. The party followed this river valley until they reached the edge of the wilderness, where, as Friar Marcos had described it to them, they found Chichilticalli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chichilticalli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chichilticalli (page does not exist)">Chichilticalli</a> is in southern <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_Springs_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sulfur Springs Valley (page does not exist)">Sulfur Springs Valley</a>, within the bend of the <a href="/wiki/Dos_Cabezas_Mountains" title="Dos Cabezas Mountains">Dos Cabezas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiricahua_Mountains" title="Chiricahua Mountains">Chiricahua Mountains</a>. This fits the chronicle of Laus Deo description, which reports that "at Chichilticalli the country changes its character again and the spiky vegetation ceases. The reason is that ... the mountain chain changes its direction at the same time that the coast does. Here they had to cross and pass the mountains in order to get into the level country."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There V&#225;zquez de Coronado met a crushing disappointment: C&#237;bola was nothing like the great golden city that de Niza had described. Instead, it was just a village of simple <a href="/wiki/Pueblo" title="Pueblo">pueblos</a> constructed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuni_people" title="Zuni people">Zuni</a> Native Americans. The soldiers were upset with de Niza for his mendacious imagination, so V&#225;zquez de Coronado sent him back south to <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> in disgrace.\n</p><p>Despite what is shown in the accompanying map, on-the-ground research by Nugent Brasher beginning in 2005 revealed evidence that V&#225;zquez de Coronado traveled north between Chichilticalli and Zuni primarily on the future New Mexico side of the state line, not the Arizona side as has been thought by historians since the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Also, most scholars believe Quivira was about thirty miles east of the great bend of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River" title="Arkansas River">Arkansas River</a>, ending about twenty miles west-southwest of the location depicted on the map, with Quivira being mostly on tributaries of the Arkansas River instead of directly on the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_River" title="Kansas River">Kansas River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For details, see the heading below, "Location of Quivira...."\n</p>\n<h4><span <small>(<a href="/wiki/DjVu" title="DjVu">DjVu</a> format)</small></div></div></div>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado traveled north on one side or the other of today\'s Arizona&#8211;New Mexico state line, and from the headwaters of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Colorado_River" title="Little Colorado River">Little Colorado River</a>, he continued on until he came to the <a href="/wiki/Zuni_River" title="Zuni River">Zuni River</a>. He followed the Zuni until he found the region inhabited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuni_(tribe)" title="Zuni (tribe)">Zuni people</a>. The members of the expedition were almost starving and demanded entrance into the village of <a href="/wiki/Hawikuh_Ruins" title="Hawikuh Ruins">Hawikuh</a> (of which the preferred Zuni word is Hawikku). The natives refused, denying the expedition entrance to the village. V&#225;zquez de Coronado and his expeditionaries attacked the Zunis. The ensuing skirmish constituted the extent of what can be called the Spanish "Conquest of C&#237;bola". During the battle, V&#225;zquez de Coronado was injured. He never personally led his men-at-arms in any subsequent battles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the weeks that the expedition stayed at Zuni, he sent out several scouting expeditions.\n</p><p>The first scouting expedition was led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pedro_de_Tovar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pedro de Tovar (page does not exist)">Pedro de Tovar</a>. This expedition headed northwest to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hopi_people" title="Hopi people">Hopi</a> villages, which they recorded as <i>Tusayan</i>. Upon arrival, the Spanish were also denied entrance to the village that they came across and, once again, resorted to using force to enter. Materially, the Hopi region was just as poor as the Zuni in precious metals, but the Spaniards did learn that a large river (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorado_River_(U.S.)" title="Colorado River (U.S.)">Colorado</a>) lay to the west.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration_of_the_Colorado_River">Exploration of the Colorado River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exploration of the Colorado River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Three leaders affiliated with the V&#225;zquez de Coronado expedition were able to reach the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a>. The first was <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Alarc%C3%B3n" title="Hernando de Alarc&#243;n">Hernando de Alarc&#243;n</a>, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melchior_D%C3%ADaz" title="Melchior D&#237;az">Melchior D&#237;az</a> and lastly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garcia_Lopez_de_C%C3%A1rdenas" title="Garcia Lopez de C&#225;rdenas">Garcia Lopez de C&#225;rdenas</a>. Alarc&#243;n\'s fleet was tasked to carry supplies and to establish contact with the main body of V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s expedition but was unable to do so because of the extreme distance to Cibola. He traveled up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_of_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Sea of Cort&#233;s">Sea of Cort&#233;s</a> and then the Colorado River. In this exploration, he hauled some supplies for V&#225;zquez de Coronado, but eventually, he buried them with a note in a bottle. Melchior D&#237;az was sent down from C&#237;bola by V&#225;zquez de Coronado to take charge of the camp of Corazones and to establish contact with the fleet. Soon after arriving at the camp he set out from the valley of Corazones in Sonora and traveled overland in a north/northwesterly direction until he arrived at the junction of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gila_River" title="Gila River">Gila River</a>. There the local natives, probably the CocoMaricopa (see Seymour 2007b), told him that Alarc&#243;n\'s sailors had buried supplies and left a note in a bottle. The supplies were retrieved, and the note stated that Alarc&#243;n\'s men had rowed up the river as far as they could, searching in vain for the V&#225;zquez de Coronado expedition. They had given up and decided to return to their departure point because worms were eating holes in their boats. D&#237;az named the river the "Firebrand (Tiz&#243;n) River" because the natives in the area used firebrands to keep their bodies warm in the winter. D&#237;az died on the trip back to the camp in the valley of the Corazones.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1494" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg/220px-La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg/330px-La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg/440px-La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_conquista_del_Colorado.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>La conquista del Colorado</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Ferrer-Dalmau" title="Augusto Ferrer-Dalmau">Augusto Ferrer-Dalmau</a>, depicts Francisco V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s 1540&#8211;1542 expedition</div></div></div>\n<p>While at Hawikuh, V&#225;zquez de Coronado sent another scouting expedition overland to find the Colorado River, led by Don Garcia L&#243;pez de C&#225;rdenas. The expedition returned to Hopi territory to acquire scouts and supplies. Members of C&#225;rdenas\'s party eventually reached the South Rim of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a>, where they could see the Colorado River thousands of feet below, becoming the first Europeans to do so. After trying and failing to climb down into the canyon to reach the river, the expedition reported that they would not be able to use the Colorado River to link up with Hernando de Alarc&#243;n\'s fleet. After this, the main body of the expedition began its journey to the next populated center of pueblos, along another large river to the east, the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tiguex_War">Tiguex War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tiguex War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hernando_de_Alvarado&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hernando de Alvarado (page does not exist)">Hernando de Alvarado</a> was sent to the east, and found several villages around the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a>. V&#225;zquez de Coronado had one commandeered for his winter quarters, Coofor, which is across the river from present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernalillo" title="Bernalillo">Bernalillo</a> near <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>. During the winter of 1540&#8211;41, his army found themselves in conflict with the Rio Grande natives, which led to the brutal <a href="/wiki/Tiguex_War" title="Tiguex War">Tiguex War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This war resulted in the destruction of the Tiguex pueblos and the deaths of hundreds of Native Americans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_for_Quivira">Search for Quivira</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Search for Quivira">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>From an Indian the Spanish called "the Turk" (<i>el turco</i>), V&#225;zquez de Coronado heard of a wealthy civilization called <a href="/wiki/Quivira" title="Quivira">Quivira</a> far to the east. In spring 1541 he led his army and priests and Indian allies onto the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> to search for Quivira. The Turk was probably either a <a href="/wiki/Wichita_people" title="Wichita people">Wichita</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Pawnee_people" title="Pawnee people">Pawnee</a> and his intention seems to have been to lead V&#225;zquez de Coronado astray and hope that he got lost in the wilderness.\n</p><p>With the Turk guiding him, V&#225;zquez de Coronado and his army might have crossed the flat and featureless steppe called the <a href="/wiki/Llano_Estacado" title="Llano Estacado">Llano Estacado</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_New_Mexico" title="Eastern New Mexico">Eastern New Mexico</a>, passing through the present-day communities of <a href="/wiki/Hereford,_Texas" title="Hereford, Texas">Hereford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian,_Texas" title="Canadian, Texas">Canadian</a>. The Spanish were awed by the Llano. "The country they [the buffalo] traveled over was so smooth that if one looked at them the sky could be seen between their legs." Men and horses became lost in the featureless plain and V&#225;zquez de Coronado felt like he had been swallowed up by the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On the Llano, V&#225;zquez de Coronado encountered vast herds of <a href="/wiki/Bison" title="Bison">bison</a>&#8212;the American buffalo. "I found such a quantity of cows ... that it is impossible to number them, for while I was journeying through these plains ... there was not a day that I lost sight of them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Querechos_and_Teyas">Querechos and Teyas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Querechos and Teyas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado found a settlement of people he called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Querechos" title="Querechos">Querechos</a>. The Querechos were not awed or impressed by the Spanish, their weapons, and their "big dogs" (horses). "They did nothing unusual when they saw our army, except to come out of their tents to look at us, after which they came to talk to the advance guard, and asked who we were."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> As V&#225;zquez de Coronado described them, the Querechos were nomads, following the buffalo herds on the plains. The Querechos were numerous. Chroniclers mentioned one settlement of two hundred tipis&#8212;which implies a population of more than one thousand people living together for at least part of the year. Authorities agree that the Querechos (Becquerel\'s) were <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">Apache</a> Indians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2923" data-file-width="3991" decoding="async" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG/220px-Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG/330px-Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG/440px-Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Francisco_Coronado_picture_IMG_4884.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>V&#225;zquez de Coronado as depicted at the <a href="/wiki/Deaf_Smith_County_Historical_Museum" title="Deaf Smith County Historical Museum">Deaf Smith County Historical Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hereford,_Texas" title="Hereford, Texas">Hereford</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a></div></div></div>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado left the Querechos behind and continued southeast in the direction in which the Turk told him that Quivira was located. He and his army descended off the tabletop of the Llano Estacado into the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caprock_Escarptment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Caprock Escarptment (page does not exist)">caprock</a> canyon country. He soon met with another group of Indians, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teyas" title="Teyas">Teyas</a>, enemies of the Querechos.\n</p><p>The Teyas, like the Querechos, were numerous and buffalo hunters, although they had additional resources. The canyons they inhabited had trees and flowing streams and they grew or foraged for beans, but not corn. The Spanish, however, did note the presence of mulberries, roses, grapes, walnuts, and plums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>\n</p><p>An intriguing event was V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s meeting among the Teyas an old blind bearded man who said that he had met many days before "four others like us". He was probably talking about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabeza_de_Vaca" title="Cabeza de Vaca">Cabeza de Vaca</a>, who with Esteban and two other Spanish survivors of the Narv&#225;ez expedition to Florida made his way across southern Texas six years before V&#225;zquez de Coronado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scholars differ in their opinions as to which historical Indian group were the Teyas. A plurality believe they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caddoan" title="Caddoan">Caddoan</a> speakers and related to the Wichita.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The place where V&#225;zquez de Coronado found the Teyas has also been debated. The mystery may have been cleared up&#8212;to the satisfaction of some&#8212;by the discovery of a likely V&#225;zquez de Coronado campsite. While V&#225;zquez de Coronado was in the canyon country, his army suffered one of the violent climatic events so common on the plains. "A tempest came up one afternoon with a very high wind and hail ... The hail broke many tents and tattered many helmets, and wounded many of the horses, and broke all the crockery of the army, and the gourds which was no small loss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1993, Jimmy Owens found crossbow points in <a href="/wiki/Blanco_Canyon" title="Blanco Canyon">Blanco Canyon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crosby_County,_Texas" title="Crosby County, Texas">Crosby County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, near the town of <a href="/wiki/Floydada,_Texas" title="Floydada, Texas">Floydada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Floyd_County,_Texas" title="Floyd County, Texas">Floyd County</a>. Archaeologists subsequently searched the site and found pottery sherds, more than forty crossbow points, and dozens of horseshoe nails of Spanish manufacture, plus a Mexican-style stone blade. This find strengthens the evidence that V&#225;zquez de Coronado found the Teyas in Blanco Canyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quivira">Quivira</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quivira">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Another guide, probably Pawnee and named Ysopete, and probably Teyas as well told V&#225;zquez de Coronado that he was going in the wrong direction, saying <a href="/wiki/Quivira" title="Quivira">Quivira</a> lay to the north. By this time, V&#225;zquez de Coronado seems to have lost his confidence that fortune awaited him. He sent most of his expedition back to New Mexico and continued with only forty Spanish soldiers and priests and an unknown number of Indian soldiers, servants, and guides. V&#225;zquez de Coronado, thus, dedicated himself to a reconnaissance rather than a mission of conquest.\n</p><p>After more than thirty days journey, V&#225;zquez de Coronado found a river larger than any he had seen before. This was the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_River" title="Arkansas River">Arkansas</a>, probably a few miles east of present-day <a href="/wiki/Dodge_City,_Kansas" title="Dodge City, Kansas">Dodge City, Kansas</a>. The Spaniards and their Indian allies followed the Arkansas northeast for three days and found Quivirans hunting buffalo. The Indians greeted the Spanish with wonderment and fear but calmed down when one of V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s guides addressed them in their own language.\n</p><p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado reached Quivira itself after a few more days of traveling. He found Quivira "well settled ... along good river bottoms, although without much water, and good streams which flow into another". V&#225;zquez de Coronado believed that there were twenty-five settlements in Quivira. Both men and women Quivirans were nearly naked. V&#225;zquez de Coronado was impressed with the size of the Quivirans and all the other Indians he met. They were "large people of very good build".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> V&#225;zquez de Coronado spent twenty-five days among the Quivirans trying to learn of richer kingdoms just over the horizon. He found nothing but straw-thatched villages of up to two hundred houses and fields containing corn, beans, and squash. A copper pendant was the only evidence of wealth he discovered. The Quivirans were almost certainly the ancestors of the Wichita people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:West_indies.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1105" data-file-width="1983" decoding="async" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/West_indies.jpg/220px-West_indies.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/West_indies.jpg/330px-West_indies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/West_indies.jpg/440px-West_indies.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:West_indies.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Episode from the Conquest of America" by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mostaert" title="Jan Mostaert">Jan Mostaert</a> (c. 1545), probably V&#225;zquez de Coronado in New Mexico</div></div></div>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado was escorted to the further edge of Quivira, called Tabas, where the neighboring land of Harahey began. He summoned the "Lord of Harahey" who, with two hundred followers, came to meet with the Spanish. He was disappointed. The Harahey Indians were "all naked -- with bows, and some sort of things on their heads, and their privy parts slightly covered".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> They were not the wealthy people V&#225;zquez de Coronado sought. Disappointed, he returned to New Mexico. Before leaving Quivira, V&#225;zquez de Coronado ordered the Turk <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garrot" title="Garrot">garroted</a>. The Turk is regarded as an Indian hero in a display at Albuquerque\'s <a href="/wiki/Indian_Pueblo_Cultural_Center" title="Indian Pueblo Cultural Center">Indian Pueblo Cultural Center</a> because he led V&#225;zquez de Coronado onto the Great Plains and thus relieved the beleaguered pueblos of Spanish depredations for at least a few months.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Location_of_Quivira.2C_Tabas.2C_and_Harahey"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_of_Quivira,_Tabas,_and_Harahey">Location of Quivira, Tabas, and Harahey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Location of Quivira, Tabas, and Harahey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Archaeological evidence suggests that Quivira was in central <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> with the western-most village near the small town of <a href="/wiki/Lyons,_Kansas" title="Lyons, Kansas">Lyons</a> on Cow Creek, extending twenty miles east to the <a href="/wiki/Little_Arkansas_River" title="Little Arkansas River">Little Arkansas River</a>, and north another twenty miles to the town of <a href="/wiki/Lindsborg,_Kansas" title="Lindsborg, Kansas">Lindsborg</a> on a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Smoky_Hill_River" title="Smoky Hill River">Smoky Hill River</a>. Tabas was likely on the Smoky Hill River. Archaeologists have found numerous 16th-century sites in these areas that probably include some of the settlements visited by V&#225;zquez de Coronado.\n</p><p>At Harahey "was a river, with more water and more inhabitants than the other". This sounds as if V&#225;zquez de Coronado may have reached the Smoky Hill River near <a href="/wiki/Salina,_Kansas" title="Salina, Kansas">Salina</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abilene,_Kansas" title="Abilene, Kansas">Abilene</a>. It is a larger river than either Cow Creek or the Little Arkansas and is located at roughly the 25 league distance from Lyons that V&#225;zquez de Coronado said he traveled in Quivira. The people of Harahey seem Caddoan, because "it was the same sort of a place, with settlements like these, and of about the same size" as Quivira. They were probably the ancestors of the Pawnee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition_end">Expedition end</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Expedition end">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado returned to the Tiguex Province in New Mexico from Quivira and was badly injured in a fall from his horse "after the winter was over", according to the chronicler Casta&#241;eda&#8212;probably in March 1542. During a long convalescence, he and his expeditionaries decided to return to New Spain (Mexico). V&#225;zquez de Coronado and his expedition departed New Mexico in early April 1542, leaving behind two friars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> His expedition had been a failure. Although he remained governor of <a href="/wiki/Nueva_Galicia" title="Nueva Galicia">Nueva Galicia</a> until 1544, the expedition forced him into bankruptcy and resulted in charges of war crimes being brought against him and his field master, C&#225;rdenas. V&#225;zquez de Coronado was cleared by his friends on the Audiencia, but C&#225;rdenas was convicted in Spain of basically the same charges by the Council of the Indies. V&#225;zquez de Coronado remained in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, where he died of an infectious disease on September 22, 1554.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He was buried under the altar of the <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo_(Mexico_City)" title="Santo Domingo (Mexico City)">Church of Santo Domingo</a> in Mexico City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>\n</p><p>V&#225;zquez de Coronado caused a large loss of life among the Puebloans, both from the battles he fought with them in the <a href="/wiki/Tiguex_War" title="Tiguex War">Tiguex War</a> and from the demands for food and clothing that he levied on their fragile economies. However, thirty-nine years later when the Spanish again visited the Southwestern United States, they found little evidence that V&#225;zquez de Coronado had any lasting cultural influences on the Indians except for their surprise at seeing several light-skinned and light-haired Puebloans. See: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Chamuscado_and_Rodriguez_Expedition" title="The Chamuscado and Rodriguez Expedition">The Chamuscado and Rodriguez Expedition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Espejo" title="Antonio de Espejo">Antonio de Espejo</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Coronado_(disambiguation)" title="Coronado (disambiguation)">Coronado (disambiguation)</a></div>\n<p>In 1952, the United States established <a href="/wiki/Coronado_National_Memorial" title="Coronado National Memorial">Coronado National Memorial</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Vista,_Arizona" title="Sierra Vista, Arizona">Sierra Vista, Arizona</a> to commemorate his expedition.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Last_Crusade" title="Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade">Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade</a></i> references the "Cross of Coronado". According to the film, this gold cross, discovered in a Utah cave system, was given to V&#225;zquez de Coronado by <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hern&#225;n Cort&#233;s">Hern&#225;n Cort&#233;s</a> in 1521. Such an event never happened because V&#225;zquez de Coronado would have been 11 or 12 years old in 1521 and still living in Spain. In addition, when Indy captures the cross from robbers aboard a ship off the coast of Portugal, the ship can be seen to be named \'The Coronado\'.\n</p><p>In 1992, underground found-footage filmmaker Craig Baldwin made the film "O No Coronado!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> detailing the expedition of V&#225;zquez de Coronado through the use of recycled images from Westerns, conquest films, and <i>The Lone Ranger</i> television series.\n</p><p>The song <i>Hitchin\' to Quivira</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> from independent singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Tyler_Jakes" title="Tyler Jakes">Tyler Jakes</a>\'s 2016 album <i>Mojo Suicide</i> is based on the story of V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s expedition.\n</p><p>The song <i>Coronado And The Turk</i> from singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Steve_Tilston" title="Steve Tilston">Steve Tilston</a>\'s 1992 album <i>Of Moor And Mesa</i> is based on the story of V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s expedition.\n</p><p>There is a large hill just northwest of Lindsborg, Kansas, that is called <a href="/wiki/Coronado_Heights" title="Coronado Heights">Coronado Heights</a>. The former owner of the land built a small castle atop the hill to commemorate V&#225;zquez de Coronado\'s 1541 visit to the area. The castle and the area around it is now a public camping and recreation area. The soft sandstone rocks at the peak of the hill are covered in the names of past visitors to the area.\n</p><p>Coronado High Schools in <a href="/wiki/Coronado_High_School_(Lubbock,_Texas)" title="Coronado High School (Lubbock, Texas)">Lubbock</a>, Texas, <a href="/wiki/Coronado_High_School_(El_Paso,_Texas)" title="Coronado High School (El Paso, Texas)">El Paso</a>, Texas, <a href="/wiki/Coronado_High_School_(Colorado)" title="Coronado High School (Colorado)">Colorado Springs</a>, Colorado, and <a href="/wiki/Coronado_High_School_(Arizona)" title="Coronado High School (Arizona)">Scottsdale</a>, Arizona, were named for V&#225;zquez de Coronado. Because a don is a name for a Spanish nobleman, the Coronado Don became the school mascot in Scottsdale.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bernalillo,_New_Mexico" title="Bernalillo, New Mexico">Bernalillo, New Mexico</a>, calls itself the "City of Coronado" because he stayed there for two winters.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Coronado_Center" title="Coronado Center">Coronado Center</a>, a two-story indoor shopping mall in <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a> is named after V&#225;zquez de Coronado.\n</p><p>Coronado Road in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>, was named after V&#225;zquez de Coronado. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_40_in_New_Mexico#Albuquerque_Metro_Area" title="Interstate 40 in New Mexico">Interstate 40 through Albuquerque</a> has been named the Coronado Freeway.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Coronado,_California" title="Coronado, California">Coronado, California</a> is not named after Francisco V&#225;zquez de Coronado, but is named after <a href="/wiki/Coronado_Islands" title="Coronado Islands">Coronado Islands</a>, which were named in 1602 by <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Vizca%C3%ADno" title="Sebasti&#225;n Vizca&#237;no">Sebasti&#225;n Vizca&#237;no</a> who called them <i>Los Cuatro Coronados</i> (the four crowned ones) to honor four martyrs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestors">Ancestors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ancestors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r872447739">.mw-parser-output table.ahnentafel{border-collapse:separate;border-spacing:0;line-height:130%}.mw-parser-output .ahnentafel tr{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .ahnentafel-t{border-top:#000 solid 1px;border-left:#000 solid 1px}.mw-parser-output .ahnentafel-b{border-bottom:#000 solid 1px;border-left:#000 solid 1px}</style><div class="noresize"><table class="collapsible collapsed" style="margin:0.3em auto;clear:none;min-width:60em;width:auto;font-size:88%;border:1px solid #aaa"><tbody><tr><th style="padding:0.2em 0.3em 0.2em 4.3em;background:none;width:auto">Ancestors of Francisco V&#225;zquez de Coronado y Luj&#225;n</th></tr><tr><td style="text-align:center"><table class="ahnentafel" style="margin-left:auto;
