style="display:none">Identical nucleotide sequence due to inheritance without recombination from a common ancestor</div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> segment is <a href="/wiki/Identity_by_type" title="Identity by type">identical by state</a> (IBS) in two or more individuals if they have identical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleotide_sequences" title="Nucleotide sequences">nucleotide sequences</a> in this segment. An IBS segment is <b>identical by descent</b> (<b>IBD</b>) (note: the acronym IBD is also used for another concept in population genetics, <a href="/wiki/Isolation_by_distance" title="Isolation by distance">isolation by distance</a>) in two or more individuals if they have inherited it from a common ancestor without <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombination</a>, that is, the segment has the same ancestral origin in these individuals. DNA segments that are IBD are IBS per definition, but segments that are not IBD can still be IBS due to the same <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> in different individuals or recombinations that do not alter the segment.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_by_descent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>All individuals in a finite population are related if traced back long enough and will, therefore, share segments of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genomes" title="Genomes">genomes</a> IBD. During <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a> segments of IBD are broken up by recombination. Therefore, the expected length of an IBD segment depends on the number of generations since the <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> at the locus of the segment. The length of IBD segments that result from a common ancestor <i>n</i> generations in the past (therefore involving 2<i>n</i> meiosis) is exponentially distributed with mean 1/(2<i>n</i>) <a href="/wiki/Centimorgan" title="Centimorgan">Morgans</a> (M).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning.2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning.2008-1">[1]</a></sup> The expected number of IBD segments decreases with the number of generations since the common ancestor at this locus. For a specific DNA segment, the probability of being IBD decreases as 2<sup>&#8722;2<i>n</i></sup> since in each meiosis the probability of transmitting this segment is 1/2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson.2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2008-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_by_descent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Identified IBD segments can be used for a wide range of purposes. As noted above the amount (length and number) of IBD sharing depends on the familial relationships between the tested individuals. Therefore, one application of IBD segment detection is to quantify relatedness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrechtsen.2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albrechtsen.2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning.2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning.2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purcell.2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell.2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Measurement of relatedness can be used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic_genetics" title="Forensic genetics">forensic genetics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evett.1998_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evett.1998-7">[7]</a></sup> but can also increase information in <a href="/wiki/Genetic_linkage" title="Genetic linkage">genetic linkage</a> mapping<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrechtsen.2009_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Albrechtsen.2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leutenegger.2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leutenegger.2003-8">[8]</a></sup> and help to decrease <a href="/wiki/Bias_(statistics)" title="Bias (statistics)">bias</a> by undocumented relationships in standard <a href="/wiki/Genetic_association" title="Genetic association">association studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purcell.2007_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell.2007-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voight.2005_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voight.2005-9">[9]</a></sup>\nAnother application of IBD is <a href="/wiki/Imputation_(genetics)" title="Imputation (genetics)">genotype imputation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> <a href="/wiki/Gametic_phase" title="Gametic phase">phase</a> inference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kong.2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kong.2008-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2012_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2012-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning.2009_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning.2009-12">[12]</a></sup> Long shared segments of IBD, which are broken up by short regions may be indicative for phasing errors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2009_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hochreiter.2013_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hochreiter.2013-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>SI</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IBD_mapping">IBD mapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_by_descent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: IBD mapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>IBD mapping<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrechtsen.2009_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Albrechtsen.2009-3">[3]</a></sup> is similar to linkage analysis, but can be performed without a known pedigree on a cohort of unrelated individuals. IBD mapping can be seen as a new form of association analysis that increases the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statistical_power" title="Statistical power">power</a> to map genes or genomic regions containing multiple rare disease susceptibility variants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purcell.2007_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell.2007-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning.2012b_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning.2012b-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Using simulated data, Browning and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_A._Thompson" title="Elizabeth A. Thompson">Thompson</a> showed that IBD mapping has higher power than association testing when multiple rare variants within a gene contribute to disease susceptibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning.2012b_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Browning.2012b-14">[14]</a></sup> Via IBD mapping, genome-wide <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">significant</a> regions in isolated populations as well as outbred populations were found while standard association tests failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2012_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2012-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2011_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2011-15">[15]</a></sup> Houwen et al. used IBD sharing to identify the chromosomal location of a gene responsible for benign recurrent intrahepatic <a href="/wiki/Cholestasis" title="Cholestasis">cholestasis</a> in an isolated fishing population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houwen.1994_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houwen.1994-16">[16]</a></sup> Kenny et al. also used an isolated population to fine-map a signal found by a <a href="/wiki/Genome-wide_association_study" title="Genome-wide association study">genome-wide association study</a> (GWAS) of plasma <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sterol" title="Plant sterol">plant sterol</a> (PPS) levels, a surrogate measure of cholesterol absorption from the intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenny.2009_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenny.2009-17">[17]</a></sup> Francks et al. was able to identify a potential susceptibility locus for <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_disorder" title="Bipolar disorder">bipolar disorder</a> with genotype data of case-control samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Francks.2008_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Francks.2008-18">[18]</a></sup> Lin et al. found a genome-wide significant linkage signal in a dataset of <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a> patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin.2013_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lin.2013-19">[19]</a></sup> Letouz&#233; et al. used IBD mapping to look for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founder_mutation" title="Founder mutation">founder mutations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letouze.2012_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letouze.2012-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" Below the turquoise bar, the IBD segment in ancient genomes is displayed.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IBD_in_population_genetics">IBD in population genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identity_by_descent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: IBD in population genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Detection of <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a> in the human genome is also possible via detected IBD segments. Selection will usually tend to increase the number of IBD segments among individuals in a population. By scanning for regions with excess IBD sharing, regions in the human genome that have been under strong, very recent selection can be identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrechtsen.2010_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albrechtsen.2010-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Han.2013_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Han.2013-22">[22]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to that, IBD segments can be useful for measuring and identifying other influences on population structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purcell.2007_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell.2007-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cockerham.1983_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cockerham.1983-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2012b_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2012b-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palamara.2012_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palamara.2012-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palamara.2013_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palamara.2013-26">[26]</a></sup> Gusev et al. showed that IBD segments can be used with additional modeling to estimate demographic history including <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">bottlenecks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_admixture" title="Genetic admixture">admixture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2012b_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2012b-24">[24]</a></sup> Using similar models Palamara et al. and Carmi et al. reconstructed the <a href="/wiki/Historical_demography" title="Historical demography">demographic history</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jewish" title="Ashkenazi Jewish">Ashkenazi Jewish</a> and Kenyan <a href="/wiki/Maasai_people" title="Maasai people">Maasai</a> individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palamara.2012_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Palamara.2012-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palamara.2013_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Palamara.2013-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carmi.2013_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carmi.2013-27">[27]</a></sup> Botigu&#233; et al. investigated differences in African ancestry among European populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Botigue.2013_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Botigue.2013-28">[28]</a></sup> Ralph and Coop used IBD detection to quantify the common ancestry of different European populations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ralph.2013_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ralph.2013-29">[29]</a></sup> and Gravel et al. similarly tried to draw conclusions of the genetic history of populations in the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gravel.2013_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gravel.2013-30">[30]</a></sup> Ringbauer et al. utilized geographic structure of IBD segments to estimate dispersal within Eastern Europe during the last centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Using the <a href="/wiki/1000_Genomes_Project" title="1000 Genomes Project">1000 Genomes</a> data Hochreiter found differences in IBD sharing between African, Asian and European populations as well as IBD segments that are shared with ancient genomes like the <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal" title="Neanderthal">Neanderthal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denisova_hominin" title="Denisova hominin">Denisova</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hochreiter.2013_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hochreiter.2013-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_and_software">Methods and software</span><span href="https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2017/01/26/103325" rel="nofollow">RAPID</a>: Ultra-fast Identity by Descent Detection in Biobank-Scale Cohorts using Positional Burrows-Wheeler Transform <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naseri_2017_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naseri_2017-32">[32]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://parente.stanford.edu/" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2009_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2009-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://www1.cs.columbia.edu/~gusev/dash/" rel="nofollow">DASH</a>: builds upon pairwise IBD segments to infer clusters of individuals likely to be sharing a single haplotype<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gusev.2011_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gusev.2011-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://pngu.mgh.harvard.edu/~purcell/plink/index.shtml" rel="nofollow">PLINK</a>: is a tool set for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whole_genome_association" title="Whole genome association">whole genome association</a> and population-based linkage analyses including a method for pairwise IBD segment detection<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Purcell.2007_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell.2007-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.popgen.dk/software/index.php/Relate" rel="nofollow">Relate</a>: estimates the probability of IBD between pairs of individuals at a specific locus using SNPs<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albrechtsen.2009_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Albrechtsen.2009-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://people.binf.ku.dk/ida/Software/MCMC_IBDfinder/" rel="nofollow">MCMC_IBDfinder</a>: is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Markov_Chain_Monte_Carlo" title="Markov Chain Monte Carlo">Markov Chain Monte Carlo</a> (MCMC) for finding IBD segments in multiple individuals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moltke.2011_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moltke.2011-37">[37]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.cs.ucla.edu/~danhe/Software/IBDGroupon.html" rel="nofollow">IBD-Groupon</a>: detects group-wise IBD segments based on pairwise IBD relationships<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-He.2013_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-He.2013-38">[38]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.bioinf.jku.at/research/short-IBD/" rel="nofollow">HapFABIA</a>: identifies very short IBD segments characterized by rare variants in large <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">sequencing</a> data simultaneously in multiple individuals<sup class="reference"
