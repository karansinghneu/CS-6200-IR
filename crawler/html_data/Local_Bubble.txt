title="Category:Lists of nebulae">Lists of nebulae</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Local Bubble</b>, or <b>Local Cavity</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abt2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abt2015-3">[3]</a></sup> is a relative cavity in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (ISM) of  the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Arm" title="Orion Arm">Orion Arm</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. It contains among others, the <a href="/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud" title="Local Interstellar Cloud">Local Interstellar Cloud</a>, which contains the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/G-Cloud" title="G-Cloud">G-Cloud</a>. It is at least 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_years" title="Light years">light years</a> across and is defined by its neutral-<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> density of about 0.05 <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>/cm<sup>3</sup>, or approximately one tenth of the average for the ISM in the Milky Way (0.5 atoms/cm<sup>3</sup>), and one sixth that of the Local Interstellar Cloud (0.3 atoms/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The exceptionally sparse matter, namely gas, of the Local Bubble is the result of <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> that exploded within the past ten to twenty million years and remains in an excited state, emitting in the <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solstation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-solstation-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was once thought that the most likely candidate for the remains of this supernova was <a href="/wiki/Geminga" title="Geminga">Geminga</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_(constellation)" title="Gemini (constellation)">constellation Gemini</a>. Later, however, it has been suggested that multiple supernovae in subgroup B1 of the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a> moving group were more likely responsible,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T._W._Berghoefer,_D._Breitschwerdt_2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-T._W._Berghoefer,_D._Breitschwerdt_2002-7">[7]</a></sup> becoming a remnant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supershell" title="Supershell">supershell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" &amp; <a href="/wiki/Loop_I_Bubble" title="Loop I Bubble">Loop I Bubble</a> (cyan).</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> has been traveling through the region currently occupied by the Local Bubble for the last five to ten million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solstation_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-solstation-5">[5]</a></sup> Its current location lies in the <a href="/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud" title="Local Interstellar Cloud">Local Interstellar Cloud</a> (LIC), a minor region of denser material within the Bubble. The LIC formed where the Local Bubble and the <a href="/wiki/Loop_I_Bubble" title="Loop I Bubble">Loop I Bubble</a> met. The gas within the LIC has a density of approximately 0.3 atoms per cubic centimeter.\n</p><p>The Local Bubble is not spherical, but seems to be narrower in the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_plane" title="Galactic plane">galactic plane</a>, becoming somewhat egg-shaped or elliptical, and may widen above and below the galactic plane, becoming shaped like an hourglass. It abuts other bubbles of less dense interstellar medium (ISM), including, in particular, the Loop I Bubble. The Loop I Bubble was cleared, heated and maintained by supernovae and <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">stellar winds</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scorpius%E2%80%93Centaurus_Association" title="Scorpius&#8211;Centaurus Association">Scorpius&#8211;Centaurus Association</a>, some 500 light years from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The Loop I Bubble contains the star <a href="/wiki/Antares" title="Antares">Antares</a> (also known as Alpha Scorpii), as shown on the diagram above right. Several tunnels connect the cavities of the Local Bubble with the Loop I Bubble, called the "Lupus Tunnel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other bubbles which are adjacent to the Local Bubble are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loop_II_Bubble" title="Loop II Bubble">Loop II Bubble</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loop_III_Bubble" title="Loop III Bubble">Loop III Bubble</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation">Observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_Bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Launched in February 2003 and active until April 2008, a small space observatory called <a href="/wiki/CHIPSat" title="CHIPSat">Cosmic Hot Interstellar Plasma Spectrometer</a> (CHIPS or CHIPSat) examined the hot gas within the Local Bubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  The Local Bubble was also the region of interest for the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_Ultraviolet_Explorer" title="Extreme Ultraviolet Explorer">Extreme Ultraviolet Explorer</a> mission (1992&#8211;2001), which examined hot EUV sources within the bubble.
