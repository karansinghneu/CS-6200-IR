This behavior is codified in Heisenberg\'s <a href="/wiki/Uncertainty_principle#Energy&#8211;time_uncertainty_principle" title="Uncertainty principle">energy&#8211;time uncertainty principle</a>. Still, the exact effect of such fleeting bits of energy is difficult to quantify. The vacuum energy is a special case of <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a> that relates to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_vacuum" title="Quantum vacuum">quantum vacuum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table style="float:right; width:30%; margin:0em 1em 1em 1em; background:#eee; border:#ccc solid">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_dropshade.png"><img alt="Question dropshade.png" data-file-height="50" data-file-width="50" decoding="async" height="20" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Question_dropshade.png/20px-Question_dropshade.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Question_dropshade.png/30px-Question_dropshade.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Question_dropshade.png/40px-Question_dropshade.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td>\n<td><b>Unsolved problem in physics</b>:<br/><div style="padding:5px; font-style:italic;">Why does the <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a> of the vacuum not cause a large <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>? What cancels it out?</div><small><a href="/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_physics" title="List of unsolved problems in physics">(more unsolved problems in physics)</a></small>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The effects of vacuum energy can be experimentally observed in various phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_emission" title="Spontaneous emission">spontaneous emission</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Casimir_effect" title="Casimir effect">Casimir effect</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">Lamb shift</a>, and are thought to influence the behavior of the Universe on <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological scales</a>. Using the upper limit of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, the vacuum energy of free space has been estimated to be 10<sup>&#8722;9</sup> <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> (10<sup>&#8722;2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Erg" title="Erg">ergs</a>) per cubic meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, in both <a href="/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics" title="Quantum electrodynamics">quantum electrodynamics</a> (QED) and <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_electrodynamics" title="Stochastic electrodynamics">stochastic electrodynamics</a> (SED), consistency with the principle of <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_covariance" title="Lorentz covariance">Lorentz covariance</a> and with the magnitude of the <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">Planck constant</a> suggest a much larger value of 10<sup>113</sup> joules per cubic meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This huge discrepancy is known as the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant_problem" title="Cosmological constant problem">cosmological constant problem</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label references</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vacuum_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">Quantum field theory</a> states that all fundamental <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">fields</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_field" title="Electromagnetic field">electromagnetic field</a>, must be <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(physics)" title="Quantization (physics)">quantized</a> at each and every point in space<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. A field in physics may be envisioned as if space were filled with interconnected vibrating balls and springs, and the strength of the field is like the displacement of a ball from its rest position. The theory requires "vibrations" in, or more accurately changes in the strength of, such a field to propagate as per the appropriate <a href="/wiki/Wave_equation" title="Wave equation">wave equation</a> for the particular field in question. The <a href="/wiki/Second_quantization" title="Second quantization">second quantization</a> of quantum field theory requires that each such ball&#8211;spring combination be quantized, that is, that the strength of the field be quantized at each point in space. Canonically, if the field at each point in space is a <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator" title="Harmonic oscillator">simple harmonic oscillator</a>, its quantization places a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator" title="Quantum harmonic oscillator">quantum harmonic oscillator</a> at each point. Excitations of the field correspond to the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>. Thus, according to the theory, even the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> has a vastly complex structure and all calculations of quantum field theory must be made in relation to this model of the vacuum.\n</p><p>The theory considers vacuum to implicitly have the same properties as a particle, such as <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a> in the case of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, energy, and so on. According to the theory, most of these properties cancel out on average leaving the vacuum empty in the literal sense of the word. One important exception, however, is the vacuum energy or the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_expectation_value" title="Vacuum expectation value">vacuum expectation value</a> of the energy. The quantization of a simple harmonic oscillator requires the lowest possible energy, or <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a> of such an oscillator to be:\n</p><p><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {E}={\\frac {1}{2}}h\\nu .}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>E</mi>\n</mrow>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mn>1</mn>\n<mn>2</mn>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mi>h</mi>\n<mi>&#957;<!-- &#957; --></mi>\n<mo>.</mo>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle {E}={\\frac {1}{2}}h\\nu .}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{E}={\\frac  {1}{2}}h\\nu ." aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c04fe1192d08209e5e1777a7340fad7c24bf1e8e" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:10.091ex; height:5.176ex;"/></span>\n</p><p>Summing over all possible oscillators at all points in space gives an infinite quantity.  To remove this infinity, one may argue that only differences in energy are physically measurable, much as the concept of <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> has been treated in <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a> for centuries. This argument is the underpinning of the theory of <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">renormalization</a>. In all practical calculations, this is how the infinity is handled.\n</p><p>Vacuum energy can also be thought of in terms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virtual_particles" title="Virtual particles">virtual particles</a> (also known as vacuum fluctuations) which are created and destroyed out of the vacuum. These particles are always created out of the vacuum in particle&#8211;<a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> pairs, which in most cases shortly annihilate each other and disappear. However, these particles and antiparticles may interact with others before disappearing, a process which can be mapped using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feynman_diagrams" title="Feynman diagrams">Feynman diagrams</a>. Note that this method of computing vacuum energy is mathematically equivalent to having a <a href="/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator" title="Quantum harmonic oscillator">quantum harmonic oscillator</a> at each point and, therefore, suffers the same renormalization problems.\n</p><p>Additional contributions to the vacuum energy come from <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking" title="Spontaneous symmetry breaking">spontaneous symmetry breaking</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum field theory</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications">Implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vacuum_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Vacuum energy has a number of consequences. In 1948, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Casimir" title="Hendrik Casimir">Hendrik B. G. Casimir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Polder" title="Dirk Polder">Dirk Polder</a> predicted the existence of a tiny attractive force between closely placed metal plates due to <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonances</a> in the vacuum energy in the space between them. This is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Casimir_effect" title="Casimir effect">Casimir effect</a> and has since been extensively experimentally verified. It is therefore believed that the vacuum energy is "real" in the same sense that more familiar conceptual objects such as electrons, magnetic fields, etc., are real. However, alternative explanations for the Casimir effect have since been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other predictions are harder to verify. Vacuum fluctuations are always created as particle&#8211;antiparticle pairs. The creation of these virtual particles near the <a href="/wiki/Event_horizon" title="Event horizon">event horizon</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a> has been hypothesized by physicist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> to be a mechanism for the eventual <a href="/wiki/Hawking_radiation" title="Hawking radiation">"evaporation" of black holes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Page-6">[6]</a></sup>  If one of the pair is pulled into the black hole before this, then the other particle becomes "real" and energy/mass is essentially radiated into space from the black hole. This loss is cumulative and could result in the black hole\'s disappearance over time. The time required is dependent on the mass of the black hole (the equations indicate that the smaller the black hole, the more rapidly it evaporates) but could be on the order of <a href="/wiki/Googol" title="Googol">10<sup>100</sup></a> years for large solar-mass black holes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Page-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The vacuum energy also has important consequences for <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">physical cosmology</a>. <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">General relativity</a> predicts that energy is equivalent to mass, and therefore, if the vacuum energy is "really there", it should exert a <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravitational</a> force. Essentially, a non-zero vacuum energy is expected to contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>, which affects the <a href="/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe" title="Expansion of the universe">expansion of the universe</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  In the special case of vacuum energy, <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> stipulates that the gravitational field is proportional to <span class="nowrap"><i>&#961;</i> + 3<i>p</i></span> (where <i>&#961;</i> is the mass&#8211;energy density, and <i>p</i> is the pressure). Quantum theory of the vacuum further stipulates that the pressure of the zero-state vacuum energy is always negative and equal in magnitude to <i>&#961;</i>.  Thus, the total is <span class="nowrap"><i>&#961;</i> + 3<i>p</i> = <i>&#961;</i> &#8722; 3<i>&#961;</i> = &#8722;2<i>&#961;</i></span>, a negative value. If indeed the vacuum ground state has non-zero energy, the calculation implies a repulsive gravitational field, giving rise to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerating_universe" title="Accelerating universe">acceleration of the expansion of the universe</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. However, the vacuum energy is mathematically infinite without <a href="/wiki/Renormalization" title="Renormalization">renormalization</a>, which is based on the assumption that we can only measure energy in a relative sense, which is not true if we can observe it indirectly via the <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">cosmological constant</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The existence of vacuum energy is also sometimes used as theoretical justification for the possibility of free-energy machines. It has been argued that due to the broken symmetry (in QED), free energy does not violate conservation of energy, since the laws of thermodynamics only apply to equilibrium systems.  However, consensus amongst physicists is that this is unknown as the nature of vacuum energy remains an unsolved problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  In particular, the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> is unaffected by the existence of vacuum energy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  However, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stochastic_Electrodynamics" title="Stochastic Electrodynamics">Stochastic Electrodynamics</a>, the energy density is taken to be a classical random noise wave field which consists of real electromagnetic noise waves propagating isotropically in all directions.  The energy in such a wave field would seem to be accessible, e.g., with nothing more complicated than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directional_coupler" title="Directional coupler">directional coupler</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  The most obvious difficulty appears to be the spectral distribution of the energy, which compatibility with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentz_invariance" title="Lorentz invariance">Lorentz invariance</a> requires to take the form <i>Kf</i><span style="padding-left:0.12em;"><sup>3</sup></span>, where <i>K</i> is a constant and <i>f</i> denotes frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  It follows that the energy and momentum flux in this wave field only becomes significant at extremely short wavelengths where directional coupler technology is currently lacking.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vacuum_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1934, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lema&#238;tre">Georges Lema&#238;tre</a> used an unusual <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">perfect-fluid</a> <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">equation of state</a> to interpret the cosmological constant as due to vacuum energy. In 1948, the <a href="/wiki/Casimir_effect" title="Casimir effect">Casimir effect</a> provided an experimental method for a verification of the existence of vacuum energy; in 1955, however, <a href="/wiki/Evgeny_Lifshitz" title="Evgeny Lifshitz">Evgeny Lifshitz</a> offered a different origin for the Casimir effect. In 1957, <a href="/wiki/Tsung-Dao_Lee" title="Tsung-Dao Lee">Lee</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chen_Ning_Yang" title="Chen Ning Yang">Yang</a> proved the concepts of broken symmetry and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parity_violation" title="Parity violation">parity violation</a>, for which they won the Nobel prize. In 1973, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Tryon" title="Edward Tryon">Edward Tryon</a> proposed the <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_universe" title="Zero-energy universe">zero-energy universe</a> hypothesis: that the Universe may be a large-scale quantum-mechanical vacuum fluctuation where positive <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>&#8211;energy is balanced by negative gravitational <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a>. During the 1980s, there were many attempts to relate the fields that generate the vacuum energy to specific fields that were predicted by attempts at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_unification_theory" title="Grand unification theory">Grand unification theory</a> and to use observations of the Universe to confirm one or another version. However, the exact nature of the particles (or fields) that generate vacuum energy, with a density such as that required by inflation theory, remains a mystery.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
