For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Stargazing_(disambiguation)" title="Stargazing (disambiguation)">Stargazing (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1010" data-file-width="1000" decoding="async" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg/250px-Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg/375px-Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg/500px-Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Astronomy_Amateur_3_V2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Amateur astronomers watch the night sky during the <a href="/wiki/Perseids" title="Perseids">Perseid</a> meteor shower.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Amateur astronomy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobby</a> where participants enjoy observing or imaging celestial objects in the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">unaided eye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescopes</a>. Even though scientific <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> may not be their primary goal, some amateur astronomers make contributions in doing <a href="/wiki/Citizen_science" title="Citizen science">citizen science</a>, such as by monitoring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variable_stars" title="Variable stars">variable stars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAVSO_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAVSO-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_stars" title="Double stars">double stars</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heintz1978_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heintz1978-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspots</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iota2017_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iota2017-4">[4]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iota2017_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iota2017-4">[4]</a></sup> or by discovering <a href="/wiki/Transient_astronomical_event" title="Transient astronomical event">transient astronomical event</a>s, such as <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clay1981_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clay1981-5">[5]</a></sup> galactic <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">novae</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsden1988_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsden1988-6">[6]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a> in other <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuckerman1996_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zuckerman1996-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Amateur astronomers do not use the field of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> as their primary source of <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> or support, and usually have no <a href="/wiki/Professional_degree" title="Professional degree">professional degree</a> in astrophysics or advanced academic training in the subject.  Most amateurs are beginners or <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">hobbyists</a>, while others have a high degree of experience in astronomy and may often assist and work alongside professional astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skytelescope_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skytelescope-8">[8]</a></sup> Many astronomers have studied the sky throughout history in an amateur framework; however, since the beginning of the twentieth century, professional astronomy has become an activity clearly distinguished from amateur astronomy and associated activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows1988_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows1988-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Amateur astronomers typically view the <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">sky</a> at night, when most celestial objects and astronomical events are visible, but others observe during the daytime by viewing the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipses</a>.  Some just look at the sky using nothing more than their eyes or <a href="/wiki/Binoculars" title="Binoculars">binoculars</a>, but more dedicated amateurs often use portable <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">telescopes</a> or telescopes situated in their private or club <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatories</a>. Amateurs can also join as members of amateur <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_society" title="Astronomical society">astronomical societies</a>, which can advise, educate or guide them towards ways of finding and observing celestial objects; or even promoting the science of astronomy among the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motta2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Motta2006-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div The image is the combination of the 2.2-metre MPG/ESO telescope of the La Silla Observatory in Chile and a 0.4-meter amateur telescope.</div></div></div>\n<p>Collectively, amateur astronomers observe a variety of celestial objects and <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomena</a>.  Common targets of amateur astronomers include the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor showers</a>, and a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sky_object" title="Deep sky object">deep sky objects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">nebulae</a>.  Many amateurs like to specialise in observing particular objects, types of objects, or types of events which interest them. One branch of amateur astronomy, amateur <a href="/wiki/Astrophotography" title="Astrophotography">astrophotography</a>, involves the taking of photos of the night sky.  Astrophotography has become more popular with the introduction of far easier to use equipment including, digital cameras, DSLR cameras and relatively sophisticated purpose built high quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCD_camera" title="CCD camera">CCD cameras</a>.\n</p><p>Most amateur astronomers work at visible <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">wavelengths</a>, but a small minority experiment with wavelengths outside the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>.  An early pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> was <a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">Grote Reber</a>, an  amateur astronomer who constructed the first purpose built <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> in the late 1930s to follow up on the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio wavelength</a> emissions from space by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Guthe_Jansky" title="Karl Guthe Jansky">Karl Jansky</a>.  Non-visual amateur astronomy includes the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_filter" title="Infrared filter">infrared filters</a> on conventional telescopes, and also the use of <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescopes</a>.  Some amateur astronomers use home-made radio telescopes, while others use radio telescopes that were originally built for astronomical research but have since been made available for use by amateurs. (September 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_hopping">Star hopping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Star hopping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Star_hopping" title="Star hopping">Star hopping</a></div>\n<p><b>Star hopping</b> is a method often used by amateur astronomers with low-tech equipment such as binoculars or a manually driven telescope.  It involves the use of maps (or memory) to locate known landmark stars, and "hopping" between them, often with the aid of a <a href="/wiki/Finderscope" title="Finderscope">finderscope</a>.  Because of its simplicity, star hopping is a very common method for finding objects that are close to naked-eye stars.\n</p><p>More advanced methods of locating objects in the sky include <a href="/wiki/Telescope_mount" title="Telescope mount">telescope mounts</a> with <i>setting circles</i>, which assist with pointing telescopes to positions in the sky that are known to contain objects of interest, and <i>GOTO telescopes</i>, which are fully automated telescopes that are capable of locating objects on demand (having first been <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">calibrated</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_apps">Mobile apps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mobile apps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The advent of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile applications</a> for use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smartphones" title="Smartphones">smartphones</a> has led to the creation of many dedicated apps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These apps allow any user to easily locate celestial objects of interest by simply pointing the smartphone device in that direction in the sky. These apps make use of the inbuilt hardware in the phone, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a> location and <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">gyroscope</a>. Useful information about the pointed object like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_coordinates" title="Celestial coordinates">celestial coordinates</a>, the name of the object, its constellation, etc. are provided for a quick reference. Some paid versions give more information. These apps are gradually getting into regular use during observing, for the alignment process of telescopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting_circles">Setting circles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Setting circles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Setting_circles" title="Setting circles">Setting circles</a></div>\n<p><b>Setting circles</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_measurement" title="Angular measurement">angular measurement</a> scales that can be placed on the two main <a href="/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis" title="Rotation around a fixed axis">rotation axes</a> of some telescopes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Since the widespread adoption of digital setting circles, any classical engraved setting circle is now specifically identified as an "analog setting circle" (ASC).  By knowing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate" title="Coordinate">coordinates</a> of an object (usually given in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_coordinates" title="Equatorial coordinates">equatorial coordinates</a>), the telescope user can use the setting circle to align (i.e., point) the telescope in the appropriate direction before looking through its <a href="/wiki/Eyepiece" title="Eyepiece">eyepiece</a>.  A computerized setting circle is called a "digital setting circle" (DSC).  Although digital setting circles can be used to display a telescope\'s <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">RA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">Dec</a> coordinates, they are not simply a digital read-out of what can be seen on the telescope\'s analog setting circles.  As with go-to telescopes, digital setting circle computers (commercial names include Argo Navis, Sky Commander, and NGC Max) contain databases of tens of thousands of celestial objects and projections of planet positions.\n</p><p>To find a celestial object in a telescope equipped with a DSC computer, one does not need to look up the specific RA and Dec coordinates in a book or other resource, and then adjust the telescope to those numerical readings. Rather, the object is chosen from the electronic database, which causes distance values and arrow markers to appear in the display that indicate the distance and direction to move the telescope. The telescope is moved until the two angular distance values reach zero, indicating that the telescope is properly aligned. When both the RA and Dec axes are thus "zeroed out", the object should be in the eyepiece. Many DSCs, like go-to systems, can also work in conjunction with laptop sky programs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Computerized systems provide the further advantage of computing coordinate precession. Traditional printed sources are subtitled by the <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)"><i>epoch</i> year</a>, which refers to the positions of celestial objects at a given time to the nearest year (e.g., J2005, J2007).  Most such printed sources have been updated for intervals of only about every fifty years (e.g., J1900, J1950, J2000).  Computerized sources, on the other hand, are able to calculate the right ascension and declination of the "epoch of date" to the exact instant of observation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argo2008_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Argo2008-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GoTo_telescopes">GoTo telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: GoTo telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/GoTo_(telescopes)" title="GoTo (telescopes)">GoTo (telescopes)</a></div>\n<p><b>GOTO telescopes</b> have become more popular since the 1980s as technology has improved and prices have been reduced.  With these computer-driven telescopes, the user typically enters the name of the item of interest and the mechanics of the telescope point the telescope towards that item automatically.  They have several notable advantages for amateur astronomers intent on <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>.  For example, GOTO telescopes tend to be faster for locating items of interest than star hopping, allowing more time for studying of the object.  GOTO also allows manufacturers to add equatorial tracking to mechanically simpler alt-azimuth telescope mounts, allowing them to produce an overall less expensive product. GOTO telescopes usually have to be calibrated using alignment stars in order to provide accurate tracking and positioning. However, several telescope manufacturers have recently developed telescope systems that are calibrated with the use of built-in GPS, decreasing the time it takes to set up a telescope at the start of an observing session.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote-controlled_telescopes">Remote-controlled telescopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Remote-controlled telescopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With the development of fast Internet in the last part of the 20th century along with advances in computer controlled telescope mounts and CCD cameras "Remote Telescope" astronomy is now a viable means for amateur astronomers not aligned with major telescope facilities to partake in research and deep sky imaging. This enables anyone to control a telescope a great distance away in a dark location. The observer can image through the telescope using CCD cameras. The digital data collected by the telescope is then transmitted and displayed to the user by means of the Internet. An example of a digital remote telescope operation for public use via the Internet is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bareket_observatory" title="Bareket observatory">Bareket observatory</a>, and there are telescope farms in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert#Astronomical_observatories" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama</a> in Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imaging_techniques">Imaging techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Work of scientific merit is possible, however, and many amateurs successfully contribute to the knowledge base of professional astronomers.  Astronomy is sometimes promoted as one of the few remaining sciences for which amateurs can still contribute useful data.  To recognize this, the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Society_of_the_Pacific" title="Astronomical Society of the Pacific">Astronomical Society of the Pacific</a> annually gives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateur_Achievement_Award_of_Astronomical_Society_of_the_Pacific" title="Amateur Achievement Award of Astronomical Society of the Pacific">Amateur Achievement Awards</a> for significant contributions to astronomy by amateurs.\n</p><p>The majority of scientific contributions by amateur astronomers are in the area of data collection.  In particular, this applies where large numbers of amateur astronomers with small telescopes are more effective than the relatively small number of large telescopes that are available to professional astronomers.  Several organizations, such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Variable_Star_Observers" title="American Association of Variable Star Observers">American Association of Variable Star Observers</a>, exist to help coordinate these contributions.\n</p><p>Amateur astronomers often contribute toward activities such as monitoring the changes in brightness of <a href="/wiki/Variable_star" title="Variable star">variable stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernovae</a>, helping to track <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, and observing <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a> to determine both the shape of asteroids and the shape of the terrain on the apparent edge of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> as seen from Earth. With more advanced equipment, but still cheap in comparison to professional setups, amateur astronomers can measure the light spectrum emitted from astronomical objects, which can yield high-quality scientific data if the measurements are performed with due care.  A relatively recent role for amateur astronomers is searching for overlooked phenomena (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kreutz_Sungrazers" title="Kreutz Sungrazers">Kreutz Sungrazers</a>) in the vast libraries of digital images and other data captured by Earth and space based observatories, much of which is available over the Internet.\n</p><p>In the past and present, amateur astronomers have played a major role in discovering new <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>.  Recently however, funding of projects such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LINEAR" title="LINEAR">Lincoln Near-Earth Asteroid Research</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Earth_Asteroid_Tracking" title="Near Earth Asteroid Tracking">Near Earth Asteroid Tracking</a> projects has meant that most comets are now discovered by automated systems long before it is possible for amateurs to see them.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Societies">Societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_astronomical_societies" title="List of astronomical societies">List of astronomical societies</a></div>\n<p>There are a large number of amateur <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_society" title="Astronomical society">astronomical societies</a> around the world, that serve as a meeting point for those interested in amateur astronomy. Members range from active observers with their own equipment to "armchair astronomers" who are simply interested in the topic. Societies range widely in their goals and activities, which may depend on a variety of factors such as geographic spread, local circumstances, size, and membership. For example, a small local society located in dark countryside may focus on practical observing and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_parties" title="Star parties">star parties</a>, whereas a large one based in a major city might have numerous members but be limited by <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a> and thus hold regular indoor meetings with guest speakers instead. Major national or international societies generally publish their own <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">journal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Newsletter" title="Newsletter">newsletter</a>, and some hold large multi-day meetings akin to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_conference" title="Scientific conference">scientific conference</a> or <a href="/wiki/Convention_(meeting)" title="Convention (meeting)">convention</a>. They may also have sections devoted to particular topics, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observing_the_Moon" title="Observing the Moon">observing the Moon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Amateur_telescope_making" title="Amateur telescope making">amateur telescope making</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_amateur_astronomers">Notable amateur astronomers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amateur_astronomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notable amateur astronomers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main page: <a href="/wiki/Category:Amateur_astronomers" title="Category:Amateur astronomers">Category:Amateur astronomers</a></div>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/George_Alcock" title="George Alcock">George Alcock</a>, discovered several comets and novae.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bopp" title="Thomas Bopp">Thomas Bopp</a>, shared the discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_Hale-Bopp" title="Comet Hale-Bopp">Comet Hale-Bopp</a> in 1995 with unemployed PhD physicist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Hale_(astronomer)" title="Alan Hale (astronomer)">Alan Hale</a>.</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Burnham,_Jr." title="Robert Burnham, Jr.">Robert Burnham, Jr.</a> (1931&#8211;1993), author of the <i>Celestial Handbook</i>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ainslie_Common" title="Andrew Ainslie Common">Andrew Ainslie Common</a> (1841&#8211;1903), built his own very large reflecting telescopes and demonstrated that photography could record astronomical features invisible to the human eye.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Cox" title="Robert E.
