(E-1A series) or <b>Lunik 2</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">&#1051;&#1091;&#1085;&#1072; 2 or &#1051;&#1091;&#1085;&#1080;&#1082; 2</span>) was the second of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luna programme</a> spacecraft launched to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>. It was the first spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">to reach the surface of the Moon</a>, and the first man-made object to land on another celestial body. On September 14, 1959, it hit the Moon\'s surface east of <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">Mare Imbrium</a> near the craters Aristides, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes_(crater)" title="Archimedes (crater)">Archimedes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Autolycus_(crater)" title="Autolycus (crater)">Autolycus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_profile">Mission profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Launch was scheduled for September 9, but the Blok I core stage was shut down after it failed to reach full thrust at ignition. The booster was removed from the pad and replaced by a different vehicle, delaying the flight by three days. <i>Luna 2</i>, like <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a></i>, took a <a href="/wiki/Direct_ascent" title="Direct ascent">direct path</a> to the Moon, with a velocity high enough to result in a travel time of around 36 hours. <i>Luna 2</i> hit the Moon about 800 kilometers (500&#160;mi) from the centre of the visible disk on 1959 September 13 at 21:02:24 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Coordinated_Time" title="Universal Coordinated Time">UTC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya.info_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya.info-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_map_Luna_17_Luna_2_Apollo_15_Surveyor_6_Surveyor_4_Luna_7_Luna_8_Luna_11.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="924" data-file-width="1278" decoding="async" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Luna 2</i> site is near the right of the image, close to the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">Apollo 15</a> landing site</div></div></div>\n<p><i>Luna 2</i> was similar in design to <i><a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a></i>, a spherical spacecraft with protruding antennas and <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation" title="Instrumentation">instrumentation</a>. The instrumentation was also similar to Luna 1, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_counters" title="Scintillation counters">scintillation counters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counters</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation" title="Cherenkov radiation">Cherenkov</a> detectors, and <a href="/wiki/Micrometeorite" title="Micrometeorite">micrometeorite</a> detectors. There were no propulsion systems on <i>Luna 2</i> itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya.info_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-zarya.info-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once <i>Luna 2</i> was split from its upper stage it started transmitting information back to Earth using three different transmitters. These transmitters provided precise information on its course, allowing scientists to calculate that <i>Luna 2</i> would hit its mark on the Moon around 00:05 on September 14 (<a href="/wiki/Moscow_Time" title="Moscow Time">Moscow Time</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey,_B._2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey,_B._2007-3">[3]</a></sup>\nIn order to be able to provide a visual from Earth on September 13, the spacecraft released a vapor cloud that would expand to 650-kilometer (400&#160;mi) diameter that would be seen by observatories in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alma_Ata" title="Alma Ata">Alma Ata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byurakan" title="Byurakan">Byurakan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abastumani" title="Abastumani">Abastumani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi" title="Tbilisi">Tbilisi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalinabad" title="Stalinabad">Stalinabad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey,_B._2007_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey,_B._2007-3">[3]</a></sup> This vapor cloud also acted as an experiment to see how the sodium gas would act in a vacuum and zero gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The last stage of the rocket that carried <i>Luna 2</i> did not carry any type of tracking device so there was uncertainty as to where it landed, but it did hit the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> surface about 30 minutes after <i>Luna 2</i> hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For a time, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">Americans</a> were starting to believe that they were making progress in the space race and that although the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviets</a> might have had larger rockets, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> had better guidance systems. This myth was busted after the Soviets were able to crash <i>Luna 2</i> onto the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox,_D._W._1962_pg._47,_118_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cox,_D._W._1962_pg._47,_118-5">[5]</a></sup> At that time the closest Americans had come to the Moon was about 37,000 miles (60,000&#160;km) with <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_4" title="Pioneer 4">Pioneer 4</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Khrushchev, on his only visit to the U.S., gave president Eisenhower a replica of the Soviet pennants that <i>Luna 2</i> had just placed onto the lunar surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>\n<i>Luna 2</i> and its predecessors all came to be used throughout the USSR and around the world as pro-communist propaganda. Donald W. Cox explains, in his book <i>The Space Race</i>, &#8220;Although the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputniks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luna_programme" title="Luna programme">Luniks</a> did not themselves provide better cars, refrigerators, color TV sets, and homes for the peasants and laborers of the Soviet Union and her satellite states, they did evoke added inspiration for the earthbound followers of the <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communist</a> way of life helping to take their minds off shortages of consumer goods. The people were spurred on to work just a little harder for the glorious motherland and to outstrip the west in the less dramatic and more basic things of life, like coal and steel production.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cox,_D._W._1962_pg._47,_118_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cox,_D._W._1962_pg._47,_118-5">[5]</a></sup>\nAlthough <i>Luna 2</i> was a success for the Soviets, it also helped the U.S. by starting a trend of crash landing. It would eventually lead to the U.S.-made <a href="/wiki/Ranger_program" title="Ranger program">Rangers</a> which would go on to also crash land on the Moon in exactly the same way. Crash landing proved useful even after soft landing was mastered. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> used crash landings to test whether Moon craters contained ice by crashing space probes into craters and testing the debris that got thrown out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The satellite\'s impact made it the first man-made object to land on another celestial body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiments">Experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Luna 2</i> carried 5 different instruments to conduct various tests while it was on its way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moon" title="The Moon">the Moon</a>. The scintillation counters would be used to measure any ionizing radiation; the <a href="/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation" title="Cherenkov radiation">Cherenkov radiation</a> detectors would be measuring for electromagnetic radiation caused by charged particles. &#8220;The Geiger Counter carried on <i>Luna 2</i> had the primary scientific objective of determining the electron spectrum of the outer radiation belt. The instrument consisted of three STS-5 gas-discharge counters mounted on the outside of the hermetically sealed container, and would be powered by a 360 V battery&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The last instrument on Luna 2 was, &#8220;a three component fluxgate magnetometer similar to that used on Luna 1 but with the dynamic range reduced by a factor of 4 to &#8722;750 to +750 gammas so that the quantization uncertainty was &#8722;12 to +12 gammas.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> Together the devices were searching for lunar magnetic and radiation fields similar to the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a> around Earth, sending information about once every minute<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> until its last transmission which came about 55&#160;km away from the lunar surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-12">[12]</a></sup> Although it did prove previous measurements of the Van Allen radiation belts that were taken from Luna 1 around the Earth, it was not able to detect any type of radiation belts around the Moon. Because of claims that information received from Luna 1 was fake, as soon as the scientists of <i>Luna 2</i> started receiving transmissions they sent out the intended time of impact, and the transmission and trajectory details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-12">[12]</a></sup> Even though Soviet scientists gave all this information, the Americans were still skeptical until <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lovell" title="Bernard Lovell">Bernard Lovell</a> was able to prove that the radio signal was coming from <i>Luna 2</i> by showing the Doppler shift from its transmissions. After this no one seemed to question the veracity of the Soviets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Van_Allen_radiation_belt">Van Allen radiation belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Van Allen radiation belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>Luna 2</i> showed time variations in the <a href="/wiki/Electric_flux" title="Electric flux">electron flux</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_spectrum" title="Energy spectrum">energy spectrum</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="USSR_pennants">USSR pennants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luna_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: USSR pennants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Kansas_Cosmosphere_Luna_2_Pennant_2013.JPG/330px-Kansas_Cosmosphere_Luna_2_Pennant_2013.JPG 1.5x, class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kansas_Cosmosphere_Luna_2_Pennant_2013.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The copy of the Soviet pennant sent on the <i>Luna 2</i> probe to the Moon, at the Kansas Cosmosphere</div></div></div>\n<p>The spacecraft also carried Soviet <a href="/wiki/Pennant_(commissioning)" title="Pennant (commissioning)">pennants</a>. Two of them, located in the spacecraft, were sphere-shaped, with the surface covered by identical <a href="/wiki/Pentagon" title="Pentagon">pentagonal</a> elements. In the center was an explosive charge designed to shatter the sphere, sending the pentagonal shields in all directions.  This was a low-tech method that would blow back a few elements in the direction opposite of its velocity vector to greatly reduce the energy upon reaching the surface (in the way that <a href="/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft)" title="Lander (spacecraft)">landers</a> are designed to reduce velocity) which would improve the chance that some part of the sphere might survive the impact.  Each pentagonal element was made of <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> and had the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR_Coat_of_Arms" title="USSR Coat of Arms">USSR Coat of Arms</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrillic" title="Cyrillic">Cyrillic</a> letters <i>&#1057;&#1057;&#1057;&#1056;</i> ("USSR") engraved on one side, and the words <i>&#1057;&#1057;&#1057;&#1056; &#1103;&#1085;&#1074;&#1072;&#1088;&#1100; 1959</i> ("USSR January 1959") on the other side. They most likely vaporized on impact, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The third pennant was located in the last stage of the <i>Luna 2</i> rocket, which collided with the Moon\'s surface 30 minutes after the spacecraft did. It was a capsule filled with liquid, with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> strips placed into it. On each of these strips the USSR Coat of Arms, the words <i>1959 &#1103;&#1085;&#1074;&#1072;&#1088;&#1100;</i> ("1959 January"), and the words <i>&#1057;&#1054;&#1070;&#1047; &#1057;&#1054;&#1042;&#1045;&#1058;&#1057;&#1050;&#1048;&#1061; &#1057;&#1054;&#1062;&#1048;&#1040;&#1051;&#1048;&#1057;&#1058;&#1048;&#1063;&#1045;&#1057;&#1050;&#1048;&#1061; &#1056;&#1045;&#1057;&#1055;&#1059;&#1041;&#1051;&#1048;&#1050;</i> (English: "Union of Soviet Socialist Republics") were engraved.\n</p><p>On September 15, 1959, the premier of the Soviet Union, <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>, presented the American president <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> with a copy of the spherical pennant as a gift. That sphere is kept at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eisenhower_Presidential_Center" title="Eisenhower Presidential Center">Eisenhower Presidential Library and Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Abilene,_Kansas" title="Abilene, Kansas">Abilene, Kansas</a>. The only other known copy of the spherical pennant is located at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansas_Cosmosphere_and_Space_Center" title="Kansas Cosmosphere and Space Center">Kansas Cosmosphere</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hutchinson,_Kansas" title="Hutchinson, Kansas">Hutchinson, Kansas</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
