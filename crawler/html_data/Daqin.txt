(<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#22823;&#31206;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">D&#224;q&#237;n</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Ta<sup>4</sup>-ch\'in<sup>2</sup></span></i>; alternative transliterations include <b>Tachin</b>, <b>Tai-Ch\'in</b>) is the ancient Chinese name for the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> or, depending on context, the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syria_(Roman_province)" title="Syria (Roman province)">Syria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lhc-1">[1]</a></sup> It literally means the "great China", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" title="Qin Dynasty">Qin</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#31206;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Q&#237;n</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Ch\'in<sup>2</sup></span></i>) being the name of the founding dynasty of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Imperial_China" title="Early Imperial China">Chinese Empire</a>. Historian John Foster defined it as "<a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="History of the Roman Empire">the Roman Empire</a>, or rather that part of it which alone was <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">known to the Chinese</a>, Syria".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In various texts its capitals were given as <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, with no clear descriptions of the city of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. Its basic facets such as <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">laws</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_culture" title="Roman culture">customs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clothing_in_ancient_Rome" title="Clothing in ancient Rome">dress</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">currency</a> were explained in Chinese sources. Its medieval incarnation was described <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">in histories</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> (618&#8211;907 AD) onwards as <i>Fulin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#25282;&#33787;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">F&#250;l&#464;n</span></i>)&#27827;=Putlen, which <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hirth" title="Friedrich Hirth">Friedrich Hirth</a> and other scholars have identified as the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> Daqin was also commonly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac-speaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorian Christians</a> who lived in China during the Tang dynasty.\n</p><p>Chinese sources describe several <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Roman</a> embassies <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">arriving in China</a>, beginning in 166 AD and lasting into the 3rd century. These early embassies were said to arrive <a href="/wiki/Indo-Roman_trade_relations" title="Indo-Roman trade relations">by a maritime route</a> via the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> in the Chinese province of <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhi" title="Jiaozhi">Jiaozhi</a> (now northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>). Archaeological evidence such as <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">Roman coins</a> points to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Roman_commerce" title="Roman commerce">Roman commercial</a> activity in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. Later recorded embassies arriving from <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Byzantine_Empire" title="History of the Byzantine Empire">the Byzantine Empire</a>, lasting from the 7th to 11th centuries, ostensibly took an overland route following the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, alongside other <a href="/wiki/Europeans_in_Medieval_China" title="Europeans in Medieval China">Europeans in Medieval China</a>. Byzantine Greeks are recorded as being present in the court of <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a> (1260&#8211;1294), <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">the Mongol</a> ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Khanbaliq" title="Khanbaliq">Khanbaliq</a> (<a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>), while the <a href="/wiki/Hongwu_Emperor" title="Hongwu Emperor">Hongwu Emperor</a> (r. 1368&#8211;1398), founder of the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>, sent a letter of correspondence to Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/John_V_Palaiologos" title="John V Palaiologos">John V Palaiologos</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daqin">Daqin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Daqin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term <i>Daqin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">&#22823;&#31206;</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">D&#224; q&#237;n</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade&#8211;Giles">Wade&#8211;Giles</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-wadegile">Ta<sup>4</sup>-ch\'in<sup>2</sup></span></i>), meaning "Great Qin," is derived from <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">the dynasty</a> founded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Shihuangdi" title="Qin Shihuangdi">Qin Shihuangdi</a>, ruler of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Qin" title="State of Qin">State of Qin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">China\'s first emperor</a> who unified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warring_States_Period" title="Warring States Period">China\'s Warring States</a> by 221 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lieu_2013_p_126_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lieu_2013_p_126-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefix</a> "da" (&#22823;) or "great" signified that the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> was on par with the might of the Qin Empire and was viewed as a <a href="/wiki/Utopia" title="Utopia">utopian</a> land located to the northwest of the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lieu_2013_p_126_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-lieu_2013_p_126-4">[4]</a></sup> The title "Daqin" does not seem to have any phonetic derivation from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Roma</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>Romaik&#275;</i>, although it is possible that the Latin term used for China, <i><a href="/wiki/Serica" title="Serica">Serica</a></i> The Romans occupied one extreme position on the trade route, with the Chinese located on the other.\n</p><p>China never managed to reach the Roman Empire directly in antiquity, although general <a href="/wiki/Ban_Chao" title="Ban Chao">Ban Chao</a> sent <a href="/wiki/Gan_Ying" title="Gan Ying">Gan Ying</a> as an envoy to "Daqin" in 97 AD. Gan Ying did not reach Daqin: he stopped at the coast of a large sea, because "sailor(s) of the <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthian</a> west border" told him that the voyage to cross the sea might take a long time and be dangerous. certain states that were temporarily conquered during the <a href="/wiki/Roman%E2%80%93Parthian_Wars" title="Roman&#8211;Parthian Wars">Roman&#8211;Parthian Wars</a> (66 BC &#8211; 217 AD) when, for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Later_Han" title="Book of Later Han">Book of Later Han</a></i> even mentions the <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_bridge" title="Pontoon bridge">pontoon bridge</a> ("flying bridge") across the <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> at the Roman city of <a href="/wiki/Zeugma,_Commagene" title="Zeugma, Commagene">Zeugma, Commagene</a> (in modern-day Turkey).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hirth and Arkenberg identified Si-fu (Chinese: &#27740;&#24489;) with Emesa. However, John E. Hill provides evidence that it was most likely <a href="/wiki/Petra" title="Petra">Petra</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Nabataean_Kingdom" title="Nabataean Kingdom">Nabataean Kingdom</a>), given the directions and distance from "Yuluo" (i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Karak" title="Al Karak">Al Karak</a>) and the fact that it fell under Roman dominion in 106 AD when it was annexed by Trajan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hill_2004_web_notes_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-hill_2004_web_notes-12">[12]</a></sup> Even more convincing for Hill is the fact that Si-fu in Chinese means "an arm of a river which rejoins the main stream" or more aptly "rejoined water courses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hill_2004_web_notes_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-hill_2004_web_notes-12">[12]</a></sup> He believes this is directly related to the <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cistern" title="Cistern">cistern</a> flood-control system harnessing the many streams running through the settlement and nearby canyons, or wadis, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wadi_Musa" title="Wadi Musa">Wadi Musa</a> ("Valley of Moses").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hill_2004_web_notes_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-hill_2004_web_notes-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" later eras, starting in 550 AD, as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syriac_Christians" title="Syriac Christians">Syriac Christians</a> settled along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman church</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lhc-1">[1]</a></sup>  So, for example, when the Taoist Tang Emperor Wuzong closed Christian monasteries in the mid-9th century, the imperial edict commanded:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p> As for the Tai-Ch\'in (Syrian Christian) and Muh-hu (Zoroastrian) forms of worship, name "Daqin" for Rome was used on Chinese maps as late as the 16th century, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sihai_Huayi_Zongtu" title="Sihai Huayi Zongtu">Sihai Huayi Zongtu</a>. The identification of "Daqin" with the Western Roman Empire, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_East" title="Church of the East">Church of the East</a> varies with the era and context of the document. The <a href="/wiki/Nestorian_Stele" title="Nestorian Stele">Nestorian Stele</a> erected in 781 in <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">the Tang</a> capital <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang\'an">Chang\'an</a> contains an inscription that briefly summarizes the knowledge about Daqin in the Chinese histories written up to that point and notes how only the "luminous" religion (i.e. Christianity) was practiced there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_cities">Capital cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Capital cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To the Chinese, the capital of Daqin was "An-tu", or <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, the first great Christian city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, the <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Book_of_Tang" title="Old Book of Tang">Old Book of Tang</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Book_of_Tang" title="New Book of Tang">New Book of Tang</a></i>, which identified Daqin and "Fulin" (&#25282;&#33787;; i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>) as the same countries, noted a different capital city (<a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>), one that had <a href="/wiki/Walls_of_Constantinople" title="Walls of Constantinople">walls of "enormous height"</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(674-678)" title="Siege of Constantinople (674-678)">was eventually besieged</a> by the commander "Mo-yi" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: &#25705;&#25341;&#20240;&#20043;; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a>: <i>M&#243; zhu&#257;i f&#225; zh&#299;</i>) of the <i>Da shi</i> (&#22823;&#39135;; i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_conquests" title="Muslim conquests">the Arabs</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hirth" title="Friedrich Hirth">Friedrich Hirth</a> identifies this commander as <a href="/wiki/Muawiyah_I" title="Muawiyah I">Muawiyah I</a>, who was first governor of Syria before becoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">caliph</a> and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/History_of_Rome" title="History of Rome">city of Rome</a> itself does not appear to have been described.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embassies">Embassies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a>, Roman histories offer only vague accounts of China and the silk-producing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">Seres</a></i> of the distant east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostrovsky_2007_p44_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ostrovsky_2007_p44-16">[16]</a></sup> The 2nd-century historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florus" title="Florus">Florus</a> seems to have conflated the Seres with peoples of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, or at least noted that their skin complexions proved that they both lived "beneath another sky" than the Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostrovsky_2007_p44_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ostrovsky_2007_p44-16">[16]</a></sup> The 1st-century geographer <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">Pomponius Mela</a> noted that their lands formed the center of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">an eastern ocean</a>, flanked by India to the south and the <a href="/wiki/Scythians" title="Scythians">Scythians</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Steppe" title="Eurasian Steppe">northern steppe</a>, while the historian <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a> (c. 330 &#8211; c. 400) wrote that the land of the Seres was enclosed by great natural walls around a river called Bautis, perhaps the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ostrovsky_2007_p44_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-ostrovsky_2007_p44-16">[16]</a></sup> In his <i><a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">Geography</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a> also provided a rough sketch of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>, with a port city called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattigara" title="Cattigara">Cattigara</a> lying beyond the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Chersonese" title="Golden Chersonese">Golden Chersonese</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>) visited by a Greek sailor named Alexander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_2001_p_29_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-young_2001_p_29-17">[17]</a></sup> Among the proposed sites for Ptolemy\'s Cattigara are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oc_Eo" title="Oc Eo">Oc Eo</a>, Vietnam, where Roman artefacts have been found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twenty-four_Histories" title="Twenty-four Histories">Chinese histories</a> offer an abundance of source material about their interactions with alleged Roman embassies and descriptions of their country. The first of these embassies is recorded in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Later_Han" title="Book of Later Han">Book of Later Han</a></i> as having arrived by sea in 166 AD and came by way of <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhou_(region)" title="Jiaozhou (region)">Jiaozhou</a>, later known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annam_(Chinese_province)" title="Annam (Chinese province)">Annam</a> (<a href="/wiki/Northern_Vietnam" title="Northern Vietnam">northern Vietnam</a>), as would later embassies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> Its members claimed to be representatives of the Daqin ruler "Andun" (&#23433;&#25958;), either <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a> or his co-emperor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius Antoninus</a>, and offered gifts to the court of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huan_of_Han" title="Emperor Huan of Han">Emperor Huan of Han</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_crespigny_2007_600_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_crespigny_2007_600-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Other embassies arrived sporadically afterwards. The <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Liang" title="Book of Liang">Book of Liang</a></i> mentions a Daqin embassy to <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wu" title="Eastern Wu">Eastern Wu</a> in 226, while the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jin" title="Book of Jin">Book of Jin</a></i> records a Daqin embassy to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Wu of Jin">Emperor Wu</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(265%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (265&#8211;420)">Jin</a> in 284.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yang_of_Sui" title="Emperor Yang of Sui">Emperor Yang</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sui_dynasty" title="Sui dynasty">Sui</a> (r. 604&#8211;618) had desired to send an embassy to Daqin, this never came to fruition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Instead, an embassy from a country that was now called <i>Fulin</i> (&#25282;&#33787;, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>), which the <i><a href="/wiki/Old_Book_of_Tang" title="Old Book of Tang">Old Book of Tang</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/New_Book_of_Tang" title="New Book of Tang">New Book of Tang</a></i> identified as being the same as Daqin, arrived in 643 at the court of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taizong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Taizong of Tang">Emperor Taizong</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang</a> and claimed to represent their king <i>Bo duoli</i> (&#27874;&#22810;&#21147;; i.e. K&#333;nstantinos Pogonatos, "Constantine the Bearded", the nickname of <a href="/wiki/Constans_II" title="Constans II">Constans II</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> Several other <i>Fulin</i> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>) embassies during the Tang dynasty are mentioned for the years 667, 701, and 719.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Wenxian_Tongkao" title="Wenxian Tongkao">Wenxian Tongkao</a></i> written by <a href="/wiki/Ma_Duanlin" title="Ma Duanlin">Ma Duanlin</a> (1245&#8211;1322) and the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Song" title="History of Song">History of Song</a></i> record that the Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/Michael_VII_Doukas" title="Michael VII Doukas">Michael VII Parapinak&#275;s Caesar</a> (<i>Mie li sha ling kai sa</i> &#28357;&#21147;&#27801;&#38728;&#25913;&#25746;) of <i>Fulin</i> (i.e. Byzantium) sent an embassy to China that arrived in 1081, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Shenzong_of_Song" title="Emperor Shenzong of Song">Emperor Shenzong of Song</a> (r. 1067&#8211;1085).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-13"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SezginEhrig-Eggert1996_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SezginEhrig-Eggert1996-22">[22]</a></sup> During the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> (1271&#8211;1368), an unprecedented number of <a href="/wiki/Europeans_in_Medieval_China" title="Europeans in Medieval China">Europeans started to visit and live in China</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">Marco Polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Katarina_Vilioni" title="Katarina Vilioni">Katarina Vilioni</a>, and papal missionaries such as <a href="/wiki/John_of_Montecorvino" title="John of Montecorvino">John of Montecorvino</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_de_Marignolli" title="Giovanni de Marignolli">Giovanni de Marignolli</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Yuan" title="History of Yuan">History of Yuan</a></i> recounts how a man of <i>Fulin</i> named Ai-sie (transliteration of either Joshua or Joseph), initially in the service of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCy%C3%BCk_Khan" title="G&#252;y&#252;k Khan">G&#252;y&#252;k Khan</a>, was well-versed in Western languages and had expertise in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_medicine" title="Byzantine medicine">medicine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_astronomy" title="Byzantine astronomy">astronomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bretschneider_1888p144_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-bretschneider_1888p144-26">[26]</a></sup> This convinced <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a>, founder of the Yuan dynasty, to offer him a position as the director of medical and astronomical boards, eventually honoring him with the title of Prince of Fulin (Chinese: &#25282;&#33787;&#29579;; <i>F&#250; l&#464;n w&#225;ng</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bretschneider_1888p144_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-bretschneider_1888p144-26">[26]</a></sup> His biography in the <i>History of Yuan</i> lists his children by their <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a>, which are similar to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian names</a> Elias (<i>Ye-li-ah</i>), Luke (<i>Lu-ko</i>), and Antony (<i>An-tun</i>), with a daughter named <i>A-na-si-sz</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bretschneider_1888p144_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-bretschneider_1888p144-26">[26]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ming" title="History of Ming">History of Ming</a> explains how the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> (1368&#8211;1644), the <a href="/wiki/Hongwu_Emperor" title="Hongwu Emperor">Hongwu Emperor</a>, sent a merchant of <i>Fulin</i> named "Nieh-ku-lun" (&#25423;&#21476;&#20523;) back to his home country with a letter announcing the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Ming_dynasty" title="History of the Ming dynasty">founding of a new dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-14"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grant2005_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grant2005-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hirth_1939_1885_p_66_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hirth_1939_1885_p_66-28">[28]</a></sup> It is speculated that this "merchant" was actually a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Beijing" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Beijing">former bishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Khanbaliq" title="Khanbaliq">Khanbaliq</a> named Nicolaus de Bentra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luttwak2009_2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luttwak2009_2-29">[29]</a></sup> The <i>History of Ming</i> goes on to explain that contacts between China and <i>Fulin</i> ceased thereafter, whereas an envoy of the great western sea (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>) did not arrive again until the 16th century, with the Italian Jesuit missionary <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Ricci" title="Matteo Ricci">Matteo Ricci</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-15"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Currency_and_coinage">Currency and coinage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Currency and coinage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Roman_currency" title="Roman currency">Roman currency</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_currency" title="Byzantine currency">Byzantine currency</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_coin_of_Contantius_II_337_361_found_in_Karghalik.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1162" data-file-width="1161" decoding="async" height="220" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Bronze_coin_of_Contantius_II_337_361_found_in_Karghalik.jpg/440px-Bronze_coin_of_Contantius_II_337_361_found_in_Karghalik.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_coin_of_Contantius_II_337_361_found_in_Karghalik.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bronze coin of <a href="/wiki/Constantius_II" title="Constantius II">Constantius II</a> (337&#8211;361), found in <a href="/wiki/Kargilik_Town" title="Kargilik Town">Karghalik</a>, modern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a> imported Han Chinese silk while the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_Han_dynasty" title="Economy of the Han dynasty">Han-dynasty Chinese</a> imported <a href="/wiki/Roman_glass" title="Roman glass">Roman glasswares</a> as discovered in their tombs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Hansen" title="Valerie Hansen">Valerie Hansen</a> (2012) claimed that no Roman coins from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> (507&#8211;27 BC) or the <a href="/wiki/Principate" title="Principate">Principate</a> (27 BC&#8211;284 AD) era of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> have been found in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_p97_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_p97-32">[32]</a></sup> Yet this assumption has been overturned; <a href="/wiki/Warwick_Ball" title="Warwick Ball">Warwick Ball</a> (2016) notes the discovery of sixteen Roman coins found at <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi\'an">Xi\'an</a>, China (site of the Han capital <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang\'an">Chang\'an</a>) minted during the reign of various emperors from <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> (14&#8211;37 AD) to <a href="/wiki/Aurelian" title="Aurelian">Aurelian</a> (270&#8211;275 AD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ball_2016_p154_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ball_2016_p154-33">[33]</a></sup> The earliest gold <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)"><i>solidus</i> coins</a> from the Eastern Roman Empire found in China date to the reign of Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_II" title="Theodosius II">Theodosius II</a> (r. 408&#8211;450) and altogether only forty-eight of them have been found (compared to thirteen hundred silver coins) in <a href="/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a> and the rest of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_2012_p97_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen_2012_p97-32">[32]</a></sup> However, Roman golden <a href="/wiki/Medal" title="Medal">medallions</a> from the reign of <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a>, and possibly his successor <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>, have been discovered at <a href="/wiki/%C3%93c_Eo" title="&#211;c Eo">&#211;c Eo</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, which was then part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Funan" title="Kingdom of Funan">Kingdom of Funan</a> bordering the Chinese province of <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhi" title="Jiaozhi">Jiaozhi</a> in northern Vietnam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_2001_p_29_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-young_2001_p_29-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This was the same region where Chinese historical texts claim the Romans first landed before venturing further into China to conduct diplomacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-young_2001_p_29_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-young_2001_p_29-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-16"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Chinese histories offer descriptions of Byzantine coins. In discussing trade <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sino-Indian_relations" title="Sino-Indian relations">with India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian Empire</a> and the Roman Empire, the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jin" title="Book of Jin">Book of Jin</a></i>, as well as the later <i><a href="/wiki/Wenxian_Tongkao" title="Wenxian Tongkao">Wenxian Tongkao</a></i>, noted how ten ancient Roman silver coins were worth one Roman gold coin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-17"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> With fluctuations, the Roman golden <i><a href="/wiki/Aureus" title="Aureus">aureus</a></i> was worth about twenty-five silver <i><a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denarii</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Song" title="History of Song">History of Song</a></i> notes how the Byzantines made coins of either silver or gold, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Chinese_coinage#early_round_coins" title="Ancient Chinese coinage">without holes in the middle</a> yet with an inscription of the king\'s name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-18"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_and_order">Law and order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Law and order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_law" title="Byzantine law">Byzantine law</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roman_Lictor_Clothes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="326" decoding="async" height="389" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Roman_Lictor_Clothes.png/220px-Roman_Lictor_Clothes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Roman_Lictor_Clothes.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roman_Lictor_Clothes.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An 1860 sketch depicting a Roman <a href="/wiki/Lictor" title="Lictor">lictor</a>, a bodyguard for <a href="/wiki/Roman_magistrate" title="Roman magistrate">Roman magistrates</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_Song" title="History of Song">History of Song</a></i> described forms of punishment in criminal law as they were carried out in <i>Daqin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>) and <i>Fulin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>). It states that they made a distinction between minor and major offenses, with 200 strikes from a bamboo rod being reserved for major crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-19"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> It described their form of <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> as having the guilty person being stuffed into a "feather bag" and thrown into the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-20"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> This seems to correspond with the Romano-Byzantine punishment of <i><a href="/wiki/Poena_cullei" title="Poena cullei">poena cullei</a></i> (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> "punishment of the sack"), where those who committed parricide (i.e. murder of a father or mother) were sewn up into a sack, sometimes with wild animals, and thrown into either a river or sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The <i>History of Song</i> also mentioned how it was forbidden by law to <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit" title="Counterfeit">counterfeit</a> the coins minted by <i>Fulin</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-21"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup> These descriptions from the <i>History of Song</i> are also found in the <i><a href="/wiki/Wenxian_Tongkao" title="Wenxian Tongkao">Wenxian Tongkao</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halsall_2000_3-22"><a href="#cite_note-halsall_2000-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Daqin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">Chinese surname</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman naming conventions</a></div>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">Chinese histories</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">names of Romans and Byzantines</a> were often <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterated</a> into <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Chinese_language" title="History of the Chinese language">Chinese</a> as they were heard, yet occasionally the <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> stemmed from their country of origin, Daqin (&#22823;&#31206;). For instance, the Roman merchant Qin Lun (&#31206;&#35542;), who visited the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Wu" title="Eastern Wu">Eastern Wu</a> court of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a> in 226 AD, bears the surname derived from the name for his homeland, while having a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> that is perhaps derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">Greek name</a> <a href="/wiki/Leon_(given_name)" title="Leon (given name)">Leon</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Leon_of_Sparta" title="Leon of Sparta">Leon of Sparta</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hill_2004_webpage_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-hill_2004_webpage-37">[37]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Chinese_phonology" title="Historical Chinese phonology">Han-era intermediate spoken language</a> between <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a>, the pronunciation for his given name "Lun" (&#35542;) would have sounded quite different than <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">modern spoken Mandarin</a>: K. 470b *li&#815;w&#601;n / li&#815;u&#277;n or *lw&#601;n / lu&#601;n; <a href="/wiki/Historical_Chinese_phonology#Periodization_of_Chinese" title="Historical Chinese phonology">EMC</a> lw&#601;n or lw&#601;nh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hill_2004_webpage_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-hill_2004_webpage-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Granting Roman individuals the surname "Qin" followed a common Chinese naming convention for foreign peoples. For instance, people from the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">Parthian Empire</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Persia" title="Ancient Persia">ancient Persia</a> such as <a href="/wiki/An_Shigao" title="An Shigao">An Shigao</a> were often given the <a href="/wiki/An_(surname)" title="An (surname)">surname "An"</a> (&#23433;) derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Anxi</a></i> (&#23433;&#24687;), the <a href="/wiki/Arsaces_I_of_Parthia" title="Arsaces I of Parthia">Arsacid dynasty</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sogdia" title="Sogdia">Sogdians</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_people" title="Eastern Iranian people">Eastern Iranian people</a> from Central Asia, were also frequently given the surname "An" (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Li_Baoyu" title="Li Baoyu">An Chongzhang</a>), especially those from <a href="/wiki/Bukhara" title="Bukhara">Bukhara</a>, while Sogdians from <a href="/wiki/Samarkand" title="Samarkand">Samarkand</a> were surnamed <a href="/wiki/Kang_(Chinese_surname)" title="Kang (Chinese surname)">"Kang"</a> (&#24247;; e.g. <a href="/wiki/Kang_Senghui" title="Kang Senghui">Kang Senghui</a>), derived from <i><a href="/wiki/Kangju" title="Kangju">Kangju</a></i>, the Chinese term for <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The name given for Antoninus Pius/Marcus Aurelius Antoninus in the Chinese histories was "An Dun" (&#23433;&#25958;).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_crespigny_2007_600_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-de_crespigny_2007_600-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[note 1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
