other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Extinction_(disambiguation)" title="Extinction (disambiguation)">Extinction (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <b>extinction</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">scattering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> by dust and gas between an emitting <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical object</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observer</a>. Interstellar extinction was first documented as such in 1930 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Julius_Trumpler" title="Robert Julius Trumpler">Robert Julius Trumpler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karttunen2003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karttunen2003-2">[2]</a></sup> However, its effects had been noted in 1847 by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_von_Struve" title="Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve">Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and its effect on the colors of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> had been observed by a number of individuals who did not connect it with the general presence of galactic dust. For stars that lie near the plane of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> and are within a few thousand <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">parsecs</a> of the Earth, extinction in the visual band of frequencies (<a href="/wiki/Photometric_system" title="Photometric system">photometric system</a>) is <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">on the order</a> of 1.8&#160;<a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">magnitudes</a> per kiloparsec.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>-bound observers, extinction arises both from the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a> (ISM) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a>; it may also arise from <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_dust" title="Circumstellar dust">circumstellar dust</a> around an observed object. Strong extinction in earth\'s atmosphere of some <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> regions (such as <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>) is overcome by the use of space-based observatories. Since <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a> light is much more strongly <a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">attenuated</a> than <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> light, extinction causes objects to appear redder than expected, a phenomenon referred to as <b>interstellar reddening</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basicastronomy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-basicastronomy-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctext">Further reading</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstellar_reddening">Interstellar reddening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interstellar reddening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, interstellar reddening is a phenomenon associated with interstellar extinction where the <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">spectrum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">radiation source</a> changes characteristics from that which the object originally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Emission (electromagnetic radiation)">emitted</a>. Reddening occurs due to the <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">scattering</a> off <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>. Interstellar reddening is a different phenomenon from <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a>, which is the proportional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">frequency shifts</a> of spectra without distortion. Reddening preferentially removes shorter <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a> from a radiated spectrum while leaving behind the longer wavelength photons (in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">optical</a>, light that is <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">redder</a>), leaving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_spectral_line" title="Atomic spectral line">spectroscopic lines</a> unchanged.\n</p><p>In most <a href="/wiki/Photometric_system" title="Photometric system">photometric systems</a> filters (passbands) are used from which readings of magnitude of light may take account of latitude and humidity among terrestrial factors. Interstellar reddening equates to the "color excess", defined as the difference between an object\'s observed color index and its intrinsic color index (sometimes referred to as its normal color index). The latter is the theoretical value which it would have if unaffected by extinction. In the first system, the <a href="/wiki/UBV_photometric_system" title="UBV photometric system">UBV photometric system</a> devised in the 1950s and its most closely related successors, the object\'s color excess <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle E_{B-V}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>E</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>B</mi>\n<mo>&#8722;<!-- &#8722; exactly 0.02 depending on which spectral point, i.e. precise passband within the abbreviated color name is in question, see <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">color index</a>). At least two and up to five measured passbands in magnitude are then compared by subtraction: U,B,V,I or R during which the color excess from extinction is calculated and deducted. The name of the four sub-indices (R minus I etc.) and order of the subtraction of recalibrated magnitudes is from right to immediate left within this sequence.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_characteristics">General characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Interstellar reddening occurs because <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar dust</a> absorbs and scatters blue light waves more than red light waves, making stars appear redder than they are. This is similar to the effect seen when dust particles in the atmosphere of Earth contribute to red sunsets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Broadly speaking, interstellar extinction is strongest at short wavelengths, generally observed by using techniques from <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>. Extinction results in a change in the shape of an observed spectrum. Superimposed on this general shape are absorption features (wavelength bands where the intensity is lowered) that have a variety of origins and can give clues as to the chemical composition of the interstellar material, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust grains</a>. Known absorption features include the 2175 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">&#197;</a> bump, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band" title="Diffuse interstellar band">diffuse interstellar bands</a>, the 3.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="&#924;m">&#956;m</a> water ice feature, and the 10 and 18 &#956;m <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> features.\n</p><p>In the solar neighborhood, the rate of interstellar extinction in the <a href="/wiki/UBV_photometric_system" title="UBV photometric system">Johnson-Cousins V-band (visual filter)</a> averaged at a wavelength of 540 nm is usually taken to be 0.7-1.0 mag/kpc&#8722;simply an average due to the <i>clumpiness</i> of interstellar dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In general, however, this means that a star will have its brightness reduced by about a factor of 2 in the V-band viewed from a good night sky vantage point on earth for every <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">kiloparsec</a> (3,260 light years) it is farther away from us.\n</p><p>The amount of extinction can be significantly higher than this in specific directions. For example, some regions of the <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Center" title="Galactic Center">Galactic Center</a> are awash with obvious intervening dark dust from our spiral arm (and perhaps others) and themselves in a bulge of dense matter, causing as much as more than 30 magnitudes of extinction in the optical, meaning that less than 1 optical photon in 10<sup>12</sup> passes through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This results in the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zone_of_avoidance" title="Zone of avoidance">zone of avoidance</a>, where our view of the extra-galactic sky is severely hampered, and background galaxies, such as <a href="/wiki/Dwingeloo_1" title="Dwingeloo 1">Dwingeloo 1</a>, were only discovered recently through observations in <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">infrared</a>.\n</p><p>The general shape of the ultraviolet through near-infrared (0.125 to 3.5 &#956;m) extinction curve (plotting extinction in magnitude against wavelength, often inverted) looking from our vantage point at other objects in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, is fairly well characterized by the stand-alone parameter of relative visiblity (of such visible light) R(V) (which is different along different lines of sight),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ca89_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ca89-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> but there are known deviations from this characterization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Extending the extinction law into the mid-infrared wavelength range is difficult due to the lack of suitable targets and various contributions by absorption features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>R(V) compares aggregate and particular extinctions. It is <span class="texhtml">A(V)/E(B-V)</span>. Restated, it is the total extinction, A(V) divided by the selective total extinction (A(B)-A(V)) of those two wavelengths (bands). A(B) and A(V) are the <b>total extinction</b> at the <a href="/wiki/UBV_photometric_system" title="UBV photometric system">B and V</a> filter bands. Another measure used in the literature is the <b>absolute extinction</b> A(&#955;)/A(V) at wavelength &#955;, comparing the total extinction at that wavelength to that at the V band.\n</p><p>R(V) is known to be correlated with the average size of the dust grains causing the extinction. For our own galaxy, the Milky Way, the typical value for R(V) is 3.1,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> but is found to vary considerably across different lines of sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ma16_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ma16-16">[16]</a></sup> As a result, when computing cosmic distances it can be advantageous to move to star data from the near-infared (of which the filter or passband Ks is quite standard) where the variations and amount of extinction are significantly less, and similar ratios as to R(Ks):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <b>0.49&#177;0.02</b> and <b>0.528&#177;0.015</b> were found respectively by independent groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ma16_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ma16-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ni09_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ni09-18">[18]</a></sup>  Those two more modern findings differ substantially relative to the commonly referenced historical value &#8776;0.7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ca89_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ca89-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The relationship between the total extinction, A(V) (measured in <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">magnitudes</a>), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Column_density" title="Column density">column density</a> of neutral <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms column, N<sub>H</sub> (usually measured in cm<sup>&#8722;2</sup>), shows how the gas and dust in the interstellar medium are related. From studies using ultraviolet spectroscopy of reddened stars and X-ray scattering halos in the Milky Way, Predehl and Schmitt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> found the relationship between N<sub>H</sub> and A(V) to be approximately:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle {\\frac {N_{H}}{A(V)}}\\approx 1.8\\times 10^{21}~{\\mbox{atoms}}~{\\mbox{cm}}^{-2}~{\\mbox{mag}}^{-1}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<msub>\n<mi>N</mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>H</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n<mrow>\n<mi>A</mi>\n<mo stretchy="false">(</mo>\n<mi>V</mi>\n<mo stretchy="false">)</mo>\n</mrow>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n<mo>&#8776;<!-- &#8776; In the case of <a href="/wiki/Emission_nebula" title="Emission nebula">emission nebulae</a>, it is common to look at the ratio of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">emission lines</a> which should not be affected by the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> in the nebula.  For example, the ratio of <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">hydrogen alpha</a> to <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">hydrogen beta</a> emission is always around 2.85 under a wide range of conditions prevailing in nebulae.  A ratio other than 2.85 must therefore be due to extinction, and the amount of extinction can thus be calculated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_2175-angstrom_feature">The 2175-angstrom feature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The 2175-angstrom feature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One prominent feature in measured extinction curves of many objects within the Milky Way is a broad \'bump\' at about 2175 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">&#197;</a>, well into the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>. This feature was first observed in the 1960s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> but its origin is still not well understood. Several models have been presented to account for this bump which include <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphitic</a> grains with a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">PAH</a> molecules. Investigations of interstellar grains embedded in interplanetary dust particles (IDP) observed this feature and identified the carrier with organic carbon and amorphous silicates present in the grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction_curves_of_other_galaxies">Extinction curves of other galaxies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extinction curves of other galaxies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Interstellar_extinction_ave_curves_local_group.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1050" decoding="async" height="157" for the MW, LMC2, LMC, and SMC Bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mw_lmc_smc_comp_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-mw_lmc_smc_comp-29">[29]</a></sup> The curves are plotted versus 1/wavelength to emphasize the UV.</div></div></div>\n<p>The form of the standard extinction curve depends on the composition of the ISM, which varies from <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">galaxy</a> to galaxy.  In the <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">Local Group</a>, the best-determined extinction curves are those of the Milky Way, the <a href="/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Small Magellanic Cloud">Small Magellanic Cloud</a> (SMC) and the <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">Large Magellanic Cloud</a> (LMC).\n</p><p>In the LMC, there is significant variation in the characteristics of the ultraviolet extinction with a weaker 2175 &#197; bump and stronger far-UV extinction in the region associated with the LMC2 supershell (near the 30 Doradus starbursting region) than seen elsewhere in the LMC and in the Milky Way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>\nIn the SMC, more extreme variation is seen with no 2175 &#197; and very strong far-UV extinction in the star forming Bar and fairly normal ultraviolet extinction seen in the more quiescent Wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This gives clues as to the composition of the ISM in the various galaxies.  Previously, the different average extinction curves in the Milky Way, LMC, and SMC were thought to be the result of the different <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">metallicities</a> of the three galaxies: the LMC\'s metallicity is about 40% of that of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>, while the SMC\'s is about 10%.  Finding extinction curves in both the LMC and SMC which are similar to those found in the Milky Way<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mw_lmc_smc_comp_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-mw_lmc_smc_comp-29">[29]</a></sup> and finding extinction curves in the Milky Way that look more like those found in the LMC2 supershell of the LMC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and in the SMC Bar<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> has given rise to a new interpretation.  The variations in the curves seen in the Magellanic Clouds and Milky Way may instead be caused by processing of the dust grains by nearby star formation.  This interpretation is supported by work in starburst galaxies (which are undergoing intense star formation episodes) that their dust lacks the 2175 &#197; bump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_extinction">Atmospheric extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Atmospheric extinction gives the <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">rising</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">setting</a> Sun an orange hue and varies with location and <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>. Astronomical <a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatories</a> generally are able to characterise the local extinction curve very accurately, to allow observations to be corrected for the effect. Nevertheless, the atmosphere is completely opaque to many wavelengths requiring the use of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> to make observations.\n</p><p>This extinction has three main components: <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> by air molecules, <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles" title="Light scattering by particles">scattering</a> by <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulates</a>, and molecular <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a>. Molecular absorption is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Telluric_contamination" title="Telluric contamination">telluric absorption</a>, as it is caused by the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> (<i>telluric</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a> for <i>terrestrial</i>). The most important sources of telluric absorption are <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">molecular oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, which strongly absorb radiation near <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, which strongly absorbs <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>.\n</p><p>The amount of such extinction is lowest at the observer\'s <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> and highest near the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>. A given star reaches its greatest <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system" title="Horizontal coordinate system">celestial altitude</a> and optimal time for observation when the star is near the local <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy)" title="Meridian (astronomy)">meridian</a> around solar <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">midnight</a> and if the star has a favorable <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">declination</a> (i.e. similar to the observer\'s <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>); thus, the seasonal time due to <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> is key. Extinction is approximated by multiplying the standard atmospheric extinction curve (plotted against each wavelength) by the mean <a href="/wiki/Air_mass_(astronomy)" title="Air mass (astronomy)">air mass</a> calculated over the duration of the observation. A dry atmosphere reduces infrared extinction significantly.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">\n<cite class="citation journal">Trumpler, R.
