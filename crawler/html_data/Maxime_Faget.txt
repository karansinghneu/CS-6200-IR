Maxime Faget, April 1, 1969</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Maxime Allen "Max" Faget</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (pronounced <i>fah-ZHAY</i>; August 26, 1921&#160;&#8211; October 9, 2004) was a <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belizean</a>-born <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_Engineering" title="Mechanical Engineering">mechanical engineer</a>. Faget was the designer of the <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_capsule" title="Space capsule">spacecraft</a>, and contributed to the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemini_program" title="Gemini program">Gemini</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> spacecraft as well as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maxime_Faget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Faget was the son of American doctor <a href="/wiki/Guy_Henry_Faget" title="Guy Henry Faget">Guy Henry Faget</a>, and great-grandson of another prominent physician, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Faget" title="Jean Charles Faget">Jean Charles Faget</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Dangriga" title="Dangriga">Stann Creek Town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> (then known as <i><a href="/wiki/British_Honduras" title="British Honduras">British Honduras</a></i>), Faget attended <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_San_Francisco" title="City College of San Francisco">City College of San Francisco</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>, and he received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_Engineering" title="Mechanical Engineering">Mechanical Engineering</a> from <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">Louisiana State University</a> in 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After three years as a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submariner</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/USS_Guavina" title="USS Guavina">USS&#160;<i>Guavina</i></a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a>, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Langley_Research_Center" title="Langley Research Center">Langley Research Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_Virginia" title="Hampton, Virginia">Hampton, Virginia</a> as a research scientist. While working for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NACA" title="NACA">NACA</a> at Langley, Faget worked on the design of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">X-15</a> hypersonic spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1958, Faget became one of the 35 engineers who formed the <a href="/wiki/Space_Task_Group" title="Space Task Group">Space Task Group</a>, creating the Mercury spacecraft. Faget based his designs on the aerodynamic work of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Julian_Allen" title="Harry Julian Allen">Harvey Allen</a> from the mid-1950s, and was instrumental in selecting the blunt-body shape that won the Mercury competition over numerous contenders. Faget also led the development of the escape tower system used on Mercury, which was used in various forms on almost all following manned spacecraft. Faget also worked on the <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" title="Project Apollo">Apollo</a> vehicles, which shared many design points with the Mercury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Faget filed a patent for a space shuttle vehicle design in 1972. His design, which he named "<a href="/wiki/North_American_DC-3" title="North American DC-3">DC-3</a>" in homage to the famed <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a> airliner, was a small two-stage fully reusable shuttle with a payload capacity around 15,000 pounds (6,800&#160;kg). DC-3 was officially studied by <a href="/wiki/North_American_Aviation" title="North American Aviation">North American Aviation</a> and shown in the press as a baseline contender for the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Transportation System</a> (STS). North American also studied a version of the same basic system with a much larger 50,000 pounds (23,000&#160;kg) payload. However, the DC-3\'s nose-high re-entry profile was controversial, and eventually doomed it when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> joined the Shuttle program and demanded cross-range performance that the DC-3 simply couldn\'t meet. In the end, its most lasting contribution was to clearly identify the trade-offs inherent in any reusable design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1962 he became the Director of Engineering and Development at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">Manned Spacecraft Center</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> and continued to work for NASA until his retirement in 1981, shortly after the second Space Shuttle flight (<a href="/wiki/STS-2" title="STS-2">STS-2</a>). After his retirement, Faget was among the founders of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Industries_Inc." title="Space Industries Inc.">Space Industries Inc.</a>, established in 1982. One of the projects of the company was the <a href="/wiki/Wake_Shield_Facility" title="Wake Shield Facility">Wake Shield Facility</a>, a device to create near-perfect <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a>. The WSF flew three times with a Space Shuttle in 1994&#8211;96 (<a href="/wiki/STS-60" title="STS-60">STS-60</a>, <a href="/wiki/STS-69" title="STS-69">STS-69</a>, <a href="/wiki/STS-80" title="STS-80">STS-80</a>).\n</p><p>Faget was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Space_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Space Hall of Fame">International Space Hall of Fame</a> in 1969, the National Inventors Hall of Fame, and received the <a href="/wiki/NASA_Outstanding_Leadership_Medal" title="NASA Outstanding Leadership Medal">NASA Outstanding Leadership Medal</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_J._Montgomery_Award" title="John J. Montgomery Award">John J. Montgomery Award</a>.\n</p><p>Faget died of <a href="/wiki/Bladder_cancer" title="Bladder cancer">bladder cancer</a> on October 9, 2004, aged 83.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patents">Patents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maxime_Faget&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Max Faget was a co-inventor on five United States patents issued to Space Industries, Inc. between 1988 and 1992.
