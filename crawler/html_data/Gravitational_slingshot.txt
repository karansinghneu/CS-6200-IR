The "assist" is provided by the motion of the gravitating body as it pulls on the spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basics-traject_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-basics-traject-1">[1]</a></sup> probes from <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a> onwards, including the two <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager</a> probes\' notable <a and flyby position of the incoming spacecraft</div></div></div>\n<p>A gravity assist around a planet changes a spacecraft\'s <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> (relative to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>) by entering and leaving the gravitational sphere of influence of a planet. The spacecraft\'s speed increases as it approaches the planet and decreases while escaping its gravitational pull (which is approximately the same), but because the planet orbits the Sun the spacecraft is affected by this motion during the maneuver. To increase speed, the spacecraft flies with the movement of the planet (taking a small amount of the planet\'s orbital energy); to decrease speed, the spacecraft flies against the movement of the planet. The sum of the kinetic energies of both bodies remains constant (see <a href="/wiki/Elastic_collision" title="Elastic collision">elastic collision</a>). A slingshot maneuver can therefore be used to change the spaceship\'s trajectory and speed relative to the Sun.\n</p><p>A close terrestrial analogy is provided by a tennis ball bouncing off the front of a moving train.  Imagine standing on a train platform, and throwing a ball at 30&#160;km/h toward a train approaching at 50&#160;km/h.  The driver of the train sees the ball approaching at 80&#160;km/h and then departing at 80&#160;km/h after the ball bounces elastically off the front of the train.  Because of the train\'s motion, however, that departure is at 130&#160;km/h relative to the train platform; the ball has added twice the train\'s velocity to its own.\n</p><p>Translating this analogy into space: in the planet <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">reference frame</a>, the spaceship has a vertical velocity of <i>v</i> relative to the planet. After the slingshot occurs the spaceship is leaving on a course 90 degrees to that which it arrived on. It will still have a velocity of <i>v</i>, but in the horizontal direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> In the Sun reference frame, the planet has a horizontal velocity of v, and by using the Pythagorean Theorem, the spaceship initially has a total velocity of <i><span class="nowrap">&#8730;<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;"></span></span></i>2<i>v</i>. After the spaceship leaves the planet, it will have a velocity of <i>v + v =</i> 2<i>v</i>, gaining around 0.6<i>v</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>This oversimplified example is impossible to refine without additional details regarding the orbit, but if the spaceship travels in a path which forms a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">hyperbola</a>, it can leave the planet in the opposite direction without firing its engine. This example is also one of many trajectories and gains of speed the spaceship can have.\n</p><p>This explanation might seem to violate the conservation of energy and momentum, apparently adding velocity to the spacecraft out of nothing, but the spacecraft\'s effects on the planet must also be taken into consideration to provide a complete picture of the mechanics involved.  The linear momentum gained by the spaceship is equal in magnitude to that lost by the planet, so the spacecraft gains velocity and the planet loses velocity. However, the planet\'s enormous mass compared to the spacecraft makes the resulting change in its speed negligibly small.  These effects on the planet are so slight (because planets are so much more massive than spacecraft) that they can be ignored in the calculation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Realistic portrayals of encounters in space require the consideration of three dimensions. Both <a href="/wiki/Mariner_10" title="Mariner 10">Mariner 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">MESSENGER</a> performed this maneuver to reach <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>.\n</p><p>If even more speed is needed than available from gravity assist alone, the most economical way to utilize a rocket burn is to do it near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a> (closest approach). A given rocket burn always provides the same change in velocity (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%94v" title="&#916;v">&#916;v</a>), but the change in kinetic energy is proportional to the vehicle\'s velocity at the time of the burn. So to get the most kinetic energy from the burn, the burn must occur at the vehicle\'s maximum velocity, at periapsis. <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a> describes this technique in more detail.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_origins">Historical origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravity_assist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In his paper <i>&#1058;&#1077;&#1084; &#1082;&#1090;&#1086; &#1073;&#1091;&#1076;&#1077;&#1090; &#1095;&#1080;&#1090;&#1072;&#1090;&#1100;, &#1095;&#1090;&#1086;&#1073;&#1099; &#1089;&#1090;&#1088;&#1086;&#1080;&#1090;&#1100;</i> (To those who will be reading [this paper] in order to build [an interplanetary rocket]),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> published in 1938 but dated <i>1918&#8211;1919</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Kondratyuk" title="Yuri Kondratyuk">Yuri Kondratyuk</a> suggested that a spacecraft traveling between two planets could be accelerated at the beginning and end of its trajectory by using the gravity of the two planets\' moons.  In his 1925 paper "&#1055;&#1088;&#1086;&#1073;&#1083;&#1077;&#1084;&#1072; &#1087;&#1086;&#1083;&#1077;&#1090;&#1072; &#1087;&#1088;&#1080; &#1087;&#1086;&#1084;&#1086;&#1097;&#1080; &#1088;&#1077;&#1072;&#1082;&#1090;&#1080;&#1074;&#1085;&#1099;&#1093; &#1072;&#1087;&#1087;&#1072;&#1088;&#1072;&#1090;&#1086;&#1074;: &#1084;&#1077;&#1078;&#1087;&#1083;&#1072;&#1085;&#1077;&#1090;&#1085;&#1099;&#1077; &#1087;&#1086;&#1083;&#1077;&#1090;&#1099;" [Problems of flight by jet propulsion:  interplanetary flights],<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Zander" title="Friedrich Zander">Friedrich Zander</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Guido_von_Pirquet" title="Guido von Pirquet">Guido von Pirquet</a> made a similar argument.\n</p><p>The gravity assist maneuver was first used in 1959 when the Soviet probe <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">Luna 3</a> photographed the far side of Earth\'s Moon.  The maneuver relied on research performed under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Mstislav_Keldysh" title="Mstislav Keldysh">Mstislav Keldysh</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Steklov_Institute_of_Mathematics" title="Steklov Institute of Mathematics">Steklov Institute of Mathematics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>inter alia</i> by Vsevolod Alexandrovich Egorov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravity_assist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/220px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/330px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/440px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plot of Voyager 2\'s heliocentric velocity against its distance from the Sun, illustrating the use of gravity assist to accelerate the spacecraft by Jupiter, Saturn and Uranus. To observe <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a>, Voyager 2 passed over Neptune\'s north pole resulting in an acceleration out of the plane of the ecliptic and reduced velocity away from the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basics-traject_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-basics-traject-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>A spacecraft traveling from Earth to an inner planet will increase its relative speed because it is falling toward the Sun, and a spacecraft traveling from Earth to an outer planet will decrease its speed because it is leaving the vicinity of the Sun.\n</p><p>Although the orbital speed of an inner planet is greater than that of the Earth, a spacecraft traveling to an inner planet, even at the minimum speed needed to reach it, is still accelerated by the Sun\'s gravity to a speed notably greater than the orbital speed of that destination planet. If the spacecraft\'s purpose is only to fly by the inner planet, then there is typically no need to slow the spacecraft. However, if the spacecraft is to be inserted into <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> about that inner planet, then there must be some way to slow it down.\n</p><p>Similarly, while the orbital speed of an outer planet is less than that of the Earth, a spacecraft leaving the Earth at the minimum speed needed to travel to some outer planet is slowed by the Sun\'s gravity to a speed far less than the orbital speed of that outer planet. Thus, there must be some way to accelerate the spacecraft when it reaches that outer planet if it is to enter orbit about it. However, if the spacecraft is accelerated to more than the minimum required, less total propellant will be needed to enter orbit about the target planet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2018)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Gravity_assist#Dubious" title="Talk:Gravity assist">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> In addition, accelerating the spacecraft early in the flight reduces the travel time.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">Rocket engines</a> can certainly be used to increase and decrease the speed of the spacecraft. However, rocket thrust takes propellant, propellant has mass, and even a small change in velocity (known as &#916;<i>v</i>, or "delta-<i>v</i>", the <a href="/wiki/Delta_(letter)" title="Delta (letter)">delta symbol</a> being used to represent a change and "v" signifying <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>) translates to a far larger requirement for propellant needed to escape Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Gravity_well" title="Gravity well">gravity well</a>. This is because not only must the primary-stage engines lift the extra propellant, they must also lift the extra propellant beyond that, which is needed to lift <i>that</i> additional propellant. Thus the liftoff mass requirement <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_equation" title="Rocket equation">increases exponentially</a> with an increase in the required delta-<i>v</i> of the spacecraft.\n</p><p>Because additional fuel is needed to lift fuel into space, space missions are designed with a tight propellant "budget", known as the "<a href="/wiki/Delta-v_budget" title="Delta-v budget">delta-v budget</a>". The delta-v budget is in effect the total propellant that will be available after leaving the earth, for speeding up, slowing down, stabilization against external buffeting (by particles or other external effects), or direction changes, if it cannot acquire more propellant. The entire mission must be planned within that capability. Therefore, methods of speed and direction change that do not require fuel to be burned are advantageous, because they allow extra maneuvering capability and course enhancement, without spending fuel from the limited amount which has been carried into space. Gravity assist maneuvers can greatly change the speed of a spacecraft without expending propellant, and can save significant amounts of propellant, so they are a very common technique to save fuel.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits">Limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravity_assist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_Path.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="794" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Voyager_Path.svg/220px-Voyager_Path.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Voyager_Path.svg/330px-Voyager_Path.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Voyager_Path.svg/440px-Voyager_Path.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_Path.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The trajectories that enabled NASA\'s twin Voyager spacecraft to tour the four giant planets and achieve velocity to escape the Solar System</div></div></div>\n<p>The main practical limit to the use of a gravity assist maneuver is that planets and other large masses are seldom in the right places to enable a voyage to a particular destination. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager</a> missions which started in the late 1970s were made possible by the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_Grand_Tour" title="Planetary Grand Tour">Grand Tour</a>" alignment of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. A similar alignment will not occur again until the middle of the 22nd century. That is an extreme case, but even for less ambitious missions there are years when the planets are scattered in unsuitable parts of their orbits.\n</p><p>Another limitation is the atmosphere, if any, of the available planet. The closer the spacecraft can approach, the faster its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a> speed as gravity accelerates the spacecraft, allowing for more kinetic energy to be gained from a rocket burn. However, if a spacecraft gets too deep into the atmosphere, the energy lost to drag can exceed that gained from the planet\'s gravity. On the other hand, the atmosphere can be used to accomplish <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">aerobraking</a>.  There have also been theoretical proposals to use <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">aerodynamic lift</a> as the spacecraft flies through the atmosphere. This maneuver, called an <a href="/wiki/Aerogravity_assist" title="Aerogravity assist">aerogravity assist</a>, could bend the trajectory through a larger angle than gravity alone, and hence increase the gain in energy.\n</p><p>Even in the case of an airless body, there is a limit to how close a spacecraft may approach. The magnitude of the achievable change in velocity depends on the spacecraft\'s approach velocity and the planet\'s escape velocity at the point of closest approach (limited by either the surface or the atmosphere.)\n</p><p>Interplanetary slingshots using the Sun itself are not possible because the Sun is at rest relative to the Solar System as a whole. However, thrusting when near the Sun has the same effect as the powered slingshot described as the <a href="/wiki/Oberth_effect" title="Oberth effect">Oberth effect</a>. This has the potential to magnify a spacecraft\'s thrusting power enormously, but is limited by the spacecraft\'s ability to resist the heat.\n</p><p>An interstellar slingshot using the Sun is conceivable, involving for example an object coming from elsewhere in our galaxy and swinging past the Sun to boost its galactic travel. The energy and angular momentum would then come from the Sun\'s orbit around the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>.  This concept features prominently in <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">Arthur C. Clarke</a>\'s 1972 award-winning novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rendezvous_With_Rama" title="Rendezvous With Rama">Rendezvous With Rama</a></i>; his story concerns an interstellar spacecraft that uses the Sun to perform this sort of maneuver, and in the process alarms many nervous humans.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Rotating_black_hole" title="Rotating black hole">rotating black hole</a> might provide additional assistance, if its spin axis is aligned the right way. <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">General relativity</a> predicts that a large spinning mass produces <a href="/wiki/Frame-dragging" title="Frame-dragging">frame-dragging</a>&#8212;close to the object, space itself is dragged around in the direction of the spin. Any ordinary rotating object produces this effect. Although attempts to measure frame dragging about the Sun have produced no clear evidence, experiments performed by <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Probe_B" title="Gravity Probe B">Gravity Probe B</a> have detected frame-dragging effects caused by Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRL_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRL-10">[10]</a></sup> General relativity predicts that a spinning black hole is surrounded by a region of space, called the <a href="/wiki/Ergosphere" title="Ergosphere">ergosphere</a>, within which standing still (with respect to the black hole\'s spin) is impossible, because space itself is dragged at the speed of light in the same direction as the black hole\'s spin. The <a href="/wiki/Penrose_process" title="Penrose process">Penrose process</a> may offer a way to gain energy from the ergosphere, although it would require the spaceship to dump some "ballast" into the black hole, and the spaceship would have had to expend energy to carry the "ballast" to the black hole.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Tisserand_parameter_and_gravity_assists">The Tisserand parameter and gravity assists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravity_assist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Tisserand parameter and gravity assists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The use of gravity assists is constrained by a conserved quantity called the <a href="/wiki/Tisserand%27s_parameter" title="Tisserand\'s parameter">Tisserand parameter</a> (or invariant). This is an approximation to the <a href="/wiki/Jacobi_integral" title="Jacobi integral">Jacobi constant</a> of the restricted <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem" title="Three-body problem">three-body problem</a>. All the planets orbit approximately in a plane aligned with the equator of the Sun. Thus, to enter an orbit passing over the poles of the Sun, the spacecraft would have to eliminate the 30&#160;km/s speed it inherited from the Earth\'s orbit around the Sun and gain the speed needed to orbit the Sun in the pole-to-pole plane &#8212; tasks that are impossible with current <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">spacecraft propulsion</a> systems alone, making gravity assist maneuvers essential.\n</p><p>Accordingly, <i>Ulysses</i> was first sent toward Jupiter, aimed to arrive at a point in space just ahead and south of the planet. As it passed Jupiter, the probe fell through the planet\'s gravity field, exchanging momentum with the planet. This gravity assist maneuver bent the probe\'s trajectory northward relative to the Ecliptic Plane onto an orbit which passes over the poles of the Sun. The MESSENGER mission included\none flyby of Earth, two flybys of Venus, and three flybys of Mercury before finally arriving at Mercury in March 2011 with a velocity low enough to permit orbit insertion with available fuel. Although the flybys were primarily orbital maneuvers, each provided an opportunity for significant scientific observations.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cassini"><i>Cassini</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravity_assist&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cassini">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini&#8211;Huygens"><i>Cassini&#8211;Huygens</i> spacecraft</a> passed by Venus twice, then Earth, and finally Jupiter on the way to Saturn. The 6.7-year transit was slightly longer than the six years needed for a Hohmann transfer, but cut the extra velocity (delta-<i>v</i>) needed to about 2&#160;km/s, so that the large and heavy <i>Cassini</i> probe was able to reach Saturn, which would not have been possible in a direct transfer even with the <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a>, the largest launch vehicle available at the time. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hohmann_transfer" title="Hohmann transfer">Hohmann transfer</a> to Saturn would require a total of 15.7&#160;km/s delta-<i>v</i> (disregarding Earth\'s and Saturn\'s own gravity wells, and disregarding <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">aerobraking</a>), which is not within the capabilities of current launch vehicles and spacecraft propulsion systems.\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="thumb tmulti tnone center"><div class="thumbinner"
