<i>Valentin Petrovich Glushko</i>; <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">&#1042;&#1072;&#1083;&#1077;&#1085;&#1090;&#1080;&#1085; &#1055;&#1077;&#1090;&#1088;&#1086;&#1074;&#1080;&#1095; &#1043;&#1083;&#1091;&#1096;&#1082;&#1086;</span>, <i>Valentyn Petrovych Hlushko</i>; born 2 September 1908 &#8211; 10 January 1989), was a <a tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Valentin_Glushko&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At the age of fourteen he became interested in <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a> after reading novels by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>. He is known to have written a letter to <a href="/wiki/Konstantin_Tsiolkovsky" title="Konstantin Tsiolkovsky">Konstantin Tsiolkovsky</a> in 1923. He studied at an <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a> trade school, where he learned to be a <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet metal</a> worker. After graduation he apprenticed at a hydraulics fitting plant. He was first trained as a fitter, then moved to lathe operator.\n</p><p>During his time in Odessa, Glushko performed experiments with explosives. These were recovered from unexploded artillery shells that had been left behind by the White Guards during their retreat. From 1924-25 he wrote articles concerning the exploration of the Moon, as well as the use of Tsiolkovsky\'s proposed engines for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_flight" title="Space flight">space flight</a>.\n</p><p>He attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leningrad_State_University" title="Leningrad State University">Leningrad State University</a> where he studied physics and mathematics, but found the specialty programs were not to his interest. He reportedly left without graduating in April, 1929. From 1929-1930 he pursued rocket research at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gas Dynamics Laboratory (page does not exist)">Gas Dynamics Laboratory</a>. A new research section was apparently set up for the study of liquid-propellant and electric engines. He became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Group_for_the_Study_of_Reactive_Motion" title="Group for the Study of Reactive Motion">GIRD</a> (Group for the study of Rocket Propulsion Systems), founded in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Leningrad</a> in 1931.\n</p><p>On 23 March 1938 he became caught up in <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Great_Purge" title="Great Purge">Great Purge</a> and was rounded up by the <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a>, to be placed in the <a href="/wiki/Butyrka_prison" title="Butyrka prison">Butyrka prison</a>. By 15 August 1939 he was sentenced to eight years imprisonment; however, Glushko was put to work on various aircraft projects with other arrested scientists. In 1941 he was placed in charge of a design bureau for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket" title="Liquid rocket">liquid-fueled rocket engines</a>. He was finally released in 1944. In 1944, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a> and Glushko designed the RD-1&#160;KhZ [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] auxiliary rocket motor tested in a fast-climb <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin" title="Lavochkin">Lavochkin</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La-7" title="La-7">La-7R</a> for protection of the capital from high-altitude <i>Luftwaffe</i> attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Glushko was sent to Germany and Eastern Europe to study the German rocket program. In 1946, he became the chief designer of his own bureau, the OKB 456, and remained at this position until 1974. This bureau would play a prominent role in the development of rocket engines within the Soviet Union.\n</p><p>His OKB 456 (later <a href="/wiki/NPO_Energomash" title="NPO Energomash">NPO Energomash</a>) would design the 35-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton-force" title="Metric ton-force">metric ton</a> (340 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilonewton" title="Kilonewton">kN</a>) thrust RD-101 engine used in the R-2, the 120-ton (1,180&#160;kN) thrust RD-110 employed in the R-3, and the 44-ton (430&#160;kN) thrust RD-103 used in the <a href="/wiki/R-5_Pobeda" title="R-5 Pobeda">R-5 Pobeda</a> (SS-3 Shyster). The <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 ("Semyorka")</a> would include four of Glushko\'s <a href="/wiki/RD-107" title="RD-107">RD-107</a> engines and one RD-108. In 1954, he began to design engines for the <a href="/wiki/R-12_Dvina" title="R-12 Dvina">R-12 Dvina</a> (SS-4 Sandal), which had been designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mikhail_Kuzmich_Yangel" title="Mikhail Kuzmich Yangel">Mikhail Yangel</a>. He also became responsible for supplying rocket engines for <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>, the designer of the <a href="/wiki/R-9_Desna" title="R-9 Desna">R-9 Desna</a> (SS-8 Sasin). Among his designs was the powerful <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a> liquid propellant engine.\n</p><p>In 1974, following the successful American moon landings, premier <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> decided to cancel the troubled Soviet program to send a man to the Moon. He consolidated the Soviet space program, moving <a href="/wiki/Vasily_Mishin" title="Vasily Mishin">Vasily Mishin</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Energia_(corporation)#History" title="Energia (corporation)">OKB-1</a> (Korolev\'s former design bureau), as well as other bureaus, into a single bureau headed by Glushko, later named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Energia" title="NPO Energia">NPO Energia</a>. Glushko\'s first act, after firing Mishin altogether, was to cancel the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-1_rocket" title="N-1 rocket">N-1 rocket</a>, a program he had long criticized, despite the fact that one of the reasons for its difficulties was his own refusal to design the high power engines Korolev needed because of friction between the two men and ostensibly a disagreement over the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> fuel.\n</p><p>In 1965, after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UR-500" title="UR-500">UR-500</a> booster began flying, the Chelomei Bureau offered a counterproposal to Korolev\'s N-1 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UR-700" title="UR-700">UR-700</a>, a Saturn V-class booster with nine F-1 sized engines powered by <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a>. Korolev was an outspoken opponent of hypergolic propellants due to their toxicity, often citing the 1960 <a href="/wiki/Nedelin_catastrophe" title="Nedelin catastrophe">Nedelin catastrophe</a> as evidence of the danger posed by them, and had also objected to the UR-500 for the same reason.\n</p><p>Glushko meanwhile was an advocate of Chelomei\'s UR-700 as well as an even more powerful UR-900 with a nuclear-powered upper stage. When Korolev continued protesting about the safety risk posed by hypergolic propellants, Glushko responded with the counterargument that the US was launching the manned Gemini spacecraft atop a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_II" title="Titan II">Titan II</a> rocket with very similar propellants and it was not apparently a safety issue for them. He also argued that the N-1 was not a workable solution because they could not develop RP-1/LOX engines on the scale of the Saturn F-1. When Korolev also suggested developing a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> engine for the N-1, Glushko said that LH2 was completely impractical as a rocket fuel.\n</p><p>The UR-700, Glushko said, could enable a direct-ascent trajectory to the Moon which he considered safer and more reliable than the rendezvous-and-dock approach used by the Apollo program and Korolev\'s N-1 proposals. He also imagined the UR-700 and 900 in all sorts of applications from lunar bases to manned Mars missions to outer planet probes to orbiting battle stations.\n</p><p>When Korolev died in January 1966, his deputy Vasily Mishin took over the OKB-1 design bureau. Mishin succeeded in getting the Kremlin to terminate the UR-700/900 project as well as the RD-270 engine Glushko planned for the launch vehicle family. His main arguments were the tremendous safety risk posed by a low-altitude launch failure of the UR-700 in addition to the waste of money by developing two <a href="/wiki/Heavy-lift_launch_vehicle" title="Heavy-lift launch vehicle">HLV</a> families at once.\n</p><p>After the complete failure of the Soviet manned lunar effort, unmanned Mars missions, and the deaths of four cosmonauts, Mishin was fired in 1973 and the Kremlin decided to consolidate the entire Soviet space program into one organization headed by Glushko.\n</p><p>One of Glushko\'s first acts was to suspend the N-1 program, which however was not formally terminated until 1976. He then began work on a completely new HLV. During this time, the US was developing the Space Shuttle.\n</p><p>Glushko decided that the new HLV would use entirely liquid-fueled engines, with an LH2 core stage taking the place of the Shuttle main engines and strap-on boosters with LOX/RP-1 engines.\n</p><p>While the RD-120 engine used for the Energia core stage was developed quickly and with little difficulty, the RD-170 proved harder to work out. Glushko instead decided to use an engine with four combustion chambers fed from a single propellant feed line. The RD-170 powered strap-on boosters designed for Energia became the basis for the Zenit booster family which began flying in 1985. Since the Buran space shuttle was not ready for operations, Energia\'s maiden flight in May 1987 carried aloft a prototype space station module called <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a>. Ultimately, Buran did fly the following summer, a few months before Glushko\'s death.\n</p><p>While Energia and Buran fell victim to loss of funding after the collapse of the USSR, the RD-170 engines and its derivatives are still flying today and the experience in LH2 engines made during the Energia project would be used in later upper stages such as Briz.\n</p><p>Glushko\'s team was part of the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_General_Machine_Building" title="Ministry of General Machine Building">Ministry of General Machine Building</a> headed by Minister <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Afanasyev_(engineer)" title="Sergey Afanasyev (engineer)">Sergey Afanasyev</a>. Before his death, he appointed <a class="external text" href="http://www.sciencephoto.com/media/225617/view" rel="nofollow">Boris Gubanov</a> to become his successor.\n</p><p>Glushko died in January 1989. His obituary was signed by multiple <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> leaders, including <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>.\n</p><p>Perhaps his most significant engineering failure, as noted by division chief Yuri Demyanko, was his insistence that <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel" title="Hydrogen fuel">hydrogen fuel</a> was unsuitable for use as a rocket fuel. As a result, the Soviet space program was still discussing the use of hydrogen-fueled engines while the Americans were assembling the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launcher. Also, Glushko\'s design bureau consistently failed at building a rocket engine powered by LOX/Kerosene with a large combustion chamber to rival the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-1_(rocket_engine)" title="F-1 (rocket engine)">F-1</a> used on the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>; instead, his solution was the <a href="/wiki/RD-270" title="RD-270">RD-270</a>, a single large combustion-chamber engine powered by hypergolic fuels which had almost the same thrust and better <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> when compared to the F-1 engine. In addition, the RD-270 used the very advanced full-flow, staged, closed-cycle combustion concept as opposed to the simple open-cycle gas generator design used by the F-1 rocket engine. This was a primary reason for the failure of the N-1, which was forced to rely on a multitude of smaller engines for propulsion since Sergei Korolev, its chief designer, insisted on using the LOX/Kerosene combination, which Glushko felt would take much more time and money to design. Glushko never did overcome the combustion instability problems of large rocket motors using kerosene propellants; his eventual solution for this is seen on the <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a> which is basically four smaller combustion chamber/nozzle assemblies sharing common fuel delivery systems. This solution and engine gave the Soviets the large thrust propulsion needed to build the <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">Energia</a> <a href="/wiki/Super_heavy-lift_launch_vehicle" title="Super heavy-lift launch vehicle">super heavy-lift launch vehicle</a>, and is probably the finest example of Glushko\'s technical abilities when he was at his best.\n</p>\n<h2><span
