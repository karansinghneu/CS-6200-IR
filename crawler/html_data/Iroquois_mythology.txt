Recorded in wampum as recitations, written down later, the spellings of names differed as transliteration varies and spellings even in European languages were not entirely regularized. Different versions of some stories exist, reflecting different localities and different times. It is possible that the written versions were influenced by <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.\n</p><p>Each village had its own storyteller who was responsible for learning all the stories by heart. No stories were ever told during the summer months. Violations would be punished by the Jo-ga-oh, and if the violator ignored the warning he would suffer greater evils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>This version of the creation story is taken from Converse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup></i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The Earth was a thought in the mind of the ruler of a great island floating above the clouds. This ruler was called by various names, among them <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hahgwehdiyu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hahgwehdiyu (page does not exist)">Ha-wen-ni-yu</a></b>, meaning <i>He who governs</i> or <i>The Ruler</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-2">[2]</a></sup> The island is a place of calm where all needs are provided and there is no pain or death. On this island grew a great apple<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[note 1]</a></sup> tree where the inhabitants held council. The Ruler said "let us make a new place where another people can grow. Under our council tree is a great sea of clouds which calls out for light." He ordered the council tree to be uprooted and he looked down into the depths. He had <b><a href="/wiki/Atahensic" title="Atahensic">Ata-en-sic</a></b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[note 2]</a></sup> <i>Sky Woman</i>, look down. He heard the voice of the sea calling; he told Ata-en-sic, who was pregnant, to bring it life. He wrapped her in light and dropped her down through the hole.\n</p><p>All the birds and animals who lived in the great cloud sea were panicked. The Duck asked "where can it rest?" "Only the earth can hold it," replied the Beaver&#8212;the oeh-dah from the bottom of our great sea&#8212;"I will get some." The Beaver dove down, but never came up. Then the Duck tried, but its dead body floated to the surface. Many of the other birds and animals tried and failed. Finally the Muskrat returned with some earth in his paws. "It\'s heavy", he said, "who can support it?" The Turtle volunteered, and the earth was placed on top of his shell. When the earth was ready the birds flew up and carried Ata-en-sic on their wings to the Turtle\'s back.\n</p><p>This is how Hah-nu-nah, the Turtle, came to be the earth bearer. When he moves the sea gets rough and the earth shakes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Do-yo-da-no">The Do-yo-da-no</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Do-yo-da-no">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Once brought to the surface the oeh-dah grew and became an island. Ata-en-sic heard two voices under her heart and knew her time had come. One voice was calm and quiet, but the other was loud and angry. These were the <b>Do-yo-da-no</b>, <i>The Twins</i>. The good twin, <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hahgwehdiyu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hahgwehdiyu (page does not exist)">Hah-gweh-di-yu</a></b> or Teharonhiakwako,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[3]</a></sup> was born in the normal way. The evil twin, <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hahgwehdaetgan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hahgwehdaetgan (page does not exist)">H&#228;-qweh-da-&#283;t-g&#462;h</a></b> or Sawiskera,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[3]</a></sup> forced his way out from under his mother\'s arm, killing her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[note 3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the death of Sky Woman the island was shrouded in gloom. Hah-gweh-di-yu shaped the sky and created the sun from his mother\'s face saying "you shall rule here where your face will shine forever." H&#228;-qweh-da-&#283;t-g&#462;h, however, set the great darkness in the west to drive down the sun. Hah-gweh-di-yu then took the Moon and Stars from his mother\'s breast, and placed them, his sisters, to guard the night sky. He gave his mother\'s body to the earth, the Great Mother from whom all life came.\n</p><p>Ga-gaah, the Crow, came from the sun land carrying a grain of corn in his ear. Hah-gweh-di-yu planted the corn above his mother\'s body, and it became the first grain. Ga-gaah hovers over the corn fields, guarding them from harm but also claiming his share.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Hah-gweh-di-yu, corresponding to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huron_mythology" title="Huron mythology">Huron</a> spirit <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iosheka" title="Iosheka">Ioskeha</a></i>, created\nthe first people. He healed disease, defeated <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demons</a>, and gave many of the Iroquois magical and ceremonial rituals. Another of his gifts was <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, which has been used as a central part of the Iroquois religion.\n</p><p>Hah-gweh-di-yu is aided by a number of assistant or subordinate spirits.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="H.C3.A9-no"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="H&#233;-no">H&#233;-no</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: H&#233;-no">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H%C3%A9-no,_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="1500" decoding="async" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff/lossy-page1-220px-H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff/lossy-page1-330px-H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff/lossy-page1-440px-H%C3%A9-no%2C_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:H%C3%A9-no,_the_Iroquois_thunder_spirit.tiff" title="Enlarge"></a></div>H&#233;-no by Jesse Cornplanter</div></div></div>\n<p><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9-no&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="H&#233;-no (page does not exist)">H&#233;-no</a></b> is the spirit of thunder. He is represented as a man dressed as a warrior, wearing on his head a magic feather that makes him invulnerable to the attacks of Hah-gweh-di-yu. On his back he carries a basket filled with pieces of <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> which he launches at evil spirits and witches. It is the responsibility of H&#233;-no to bring rain to nourish the crops. The Iroquois address H&#233;-no as <i>Tisote</i> (Grandfather).\n</p><p>He once lived in a cave under <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a>. At that time a young girl living above the falls was engaged to marry a disagreeable old man. Rather than marry him she climbed into a canoe and headed down the river. The girl and the canoe were carried over the falls; the canoe was seen falling to destruction, but the girl disappeared. H&#233;-no and his two (nameless) assistants caught her in a blanket and brought her back to his cave. One of the assistants, taken with her beauty, married her.\n</p><p>Later H&#233;-no rescued her village from a huge serpent which was devastating it with disease. He lured the serpent to a spot on <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_River_(New_York)" title="Buffalo River (New York)">Buffalo Creek</a> where he struck it with a thunderbolt. Fatally wounded, the serpent tried to escape to the safety of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a>, but died before he could get away. His body floated downstream and stuck at the head of Niagara Falls, stretching nearly across the river and arching backward. The dammed up water broke the rocks, and the whole verge of the Falls along with the snake\'s body fell onto the rocks below. The break formed <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Falls" title="Horseshoe Falls">Horseshoe Falls</a>, but in the process destroyed H&#233;-no\'s home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="The_De-oh-h.C3.A1-ko"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_De-oh-h&#225;-ko">The De-oh-h&#225;-ko</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The De-oh-h&#225;-ko">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name means <i>Our Life</i> or <i>Our Supporters</i>. Often called "The Three Sisters" they are the spirits of corn, beans, and squash. They have the form of beautiful maidens who are fond of each other and like to live near each other. This is an analogy to the plants which grow up together, sometimes from the same hill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>One day while <b>O-na-tah</b>, the spirit of the corn, was wandering alone she was captured by H&#228;-qweh-da-&#283;t-g&#462;h. H&#228;-qweh-da-&#283;t-g&#462;h sent one of his monsters to devastate the fields, and the other sisters ran away. H&#228;-qweh-da-&#283;t-g&#462;h held O-na-tah captive in darkness under the earth until a searching ray of sunlight found her and guided her back to the surface. Here she wept over the devastation of her fields and her abandonment by her two sisters. She vowed that she would never again leave her fields, and now she guards them alone, without the presence of her sisters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Jo-ga-oh">The Jo-ga-oh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Jo-ga-oh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Jogah" title="Jogah">Jo-g&#228;-oh</a></b>, the "Little People", are invisible nature spirits, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairies</a> of European myth. The Jo-ga-oh protect and guide all the natural world and protect the people from unseen hidden enemies.\n</p><p>There are three tribes of Jo-g&#228;-oh.\n</p><p>The <b>Ga-hon-ga</b> inhabit the rivers and rocks. They live in rocky caves beside the streams and have great strength despite their small stature. The enjoy feats of strength, and have a fondness for inviting people to their habitations to compete in contests. They enjoy playing ball with rocks, tossing them high out of sight in the air, and so they are often called "Stone Throwers".\n</p><p>The <b>Gan-da-yah</b> protect and advise the fruits and grains. Their special gift is the strawberry plant; in the spring they loosen the earth so that the plant can grow. They turn the leaves toward the sun and guide the runners. The ripening of strawberries marks the spring festival. The Ho-non-di-ont, the "Company of Faith Keepers", hold dances of thanksgiving at night, with special thanks to the Jo-g&#228;-oh for the strawberries.\n</p><p>Throughout the growing season the Gan-da-yah guard the crops against disease and other pests.\n</p><p>\nThey sometimes visit the people in the form of birds: a robin for good news, an owl for a warning, a bat bringing news of an immanent life-and-death struggle. <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The most minute harmless insect or worm may be the bearer of important "talk" from the "Little People" and is not destroyed for the "trail is broad enough for all".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>The <b>Oh-do-was</b> inhabit the shadowy places under the earth. In this underworld there are forests and animals, including a white buffalo. The Oh-do-was guard against poisonous snakes and creatures of death that try to escape from the underworld. From time to time the Oh-do-was emerge from the underworld at night into the world above. There they hold festivals and dance in rings around trees in the forest where the grass afterwards doesn\'t grow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="G.C7.8E-oh_and_the_winds"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="G&#462;-oh_and_the_winds">G&#462;-oh and the winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: G&#462;-oh and the winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaol_(god)" title="Gaol (god)">G&#462;-oh</a></b> is the personification of the wind. A giant, he is an "instrumentality through whom the <a href="/wiki/Great_Spirit" title="Great Spirit">Great Spirit</a> moves the elements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-2">[2]</a></sup> His home is in the far northern sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[note 4]</a></sup> Here he controls the four winds: north wind (Bear), west wind (Panther), east wind (Moose), and south wind (Fawn).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Converse_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Converse-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (January 2016)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>The North Wind is personified by a <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bear</a> spirit named <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ya-o-gah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ya-o-gah (page does not exist)">Ya-o-gah</a></b>. Ya-o-gah could destroy the world with his fiercely cold breath, but is kept in check by G&#462;-oh. Ne-o-ga, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fawn" title="Fawn">fawn</a>, is the south wind, "gentle, and kind as the sunbeam". The West Wind, the <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">panther</a> Da-jo-ji, "can climb the high mountains, and tear down the forests... carry the whirlwind on [his] back, and toss the great sea waves high in the air, and snarl at the tempests" O-yan-do-ne, the <a href="/wiki/Moose" title="Moose">moose</a>, is the East Wind, who blows his breath "to chill the young clouds as they float through the sky".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="So-son-do-wah">So-son-do-wah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: So-son-do-wah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Sosondowah" title="Sosondowah">So-son-do-wah</a></b> was a great <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter" title="Hunter">hunter</a>, known for stalking a supernatural <a href="/wiki/Elk" title="Elk">elk</a>. He was captured by Dawn, a goddess who needed him as a watchman. He fell in love with <i><a href="/wiki/Gendenwitha" title="Gendenwitha">Gendenwitha</a></i> ("she who brings the day"; alternate spelling: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gendewitha" title="Gendewitha">Gendewitha</a>), a human woman. He tried to woo her with a song. In spring, he sang as a <a href="/wiki/Bluebird" title="Bluebird">bluebird</a>, in summer as a <a href="/wiki/New_World_blackbird" title="New World blackbird">blackbird</a> and in autumn as a <a href="/wiki/Hawk" title="Hawk">hawk</a>, who then tried to take Gendenwitha with him to the sky. Dawn tied him to her doorpost. She changed Gendenwitha into the <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Morning Star</a>, so the hunter could watch her all night but never be with her.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flying_Head">Flying Head</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flying Head">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Flying_Head" title="Flying Head">Flying Head</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Mohawk_language" title="Mohawk language">moh</a>:Kanontsist&#243;ntie\'s) is a monster in the form of a giant disembodied head as tall as a man. It is covered with thick hair, has long black wings and long sharp claws. It comes at night to the homes of widows and orphans, beats its wings on the walls of the houses, and issues terrifying cries in an unknown language. A few days after its visit death claims one of the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Seneca_people" title="Seneca people">Seneca</a> name <i>Dagwanoenyent</i> means "whirlwind".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuscarora_legend">Tuscarora legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tuscarora legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Virginia surveyor <a href="/wiki/William_Byrd_II" title="William Byrd II">William Byrd II</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_the_Dividing_Line" title="The History of the Dividing Line">History of the Dividing Line Betwixt North Carolina and Virginia</a></i> (1728), recorded a tradition of a former religious leader, which had been current among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuscarora_(tribe)" title="Tuscarora (tribe)">Tuscarora</a> tribe. They were an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquoian" title="Iroquoian">Iroquoian</a>-speaking tribe historically settled in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> that, because of warfare, migrated to join the rest of the Iroquois Confederacy in New York. In this account, many centuries before their tribe had become so dishonest that no man\'s property nor wife was considered inviolate,\n</p>\n<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[H]owever, their God, being unwilling to root them out for their crimes, did them the honour to send a Messenger from <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a> to instruct them, and set Them a perfect Example of Integrity and kind Behavior towards one another. But this holy Person, with all his Eloquence and Sanctity of Life, was able to make very little Reformation amongst them. Some few Old Men did listen a little to his Wholesome Advice, but all the Young fellows were quite incorrigible. They not only Neglected his Precepts, but derided and Evil Entreated his Person. At last, taking upon him to reprove some Young Rakes of the Conechta Clan very sharply for their impiety, they were so provok\'d at the Freedom of his Rebukes, that they tied him to a Tree, and shot him with Arrows through the Heart. But their God took instant vengeance on all who had a hand in that Monstrous Act, by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> from Heaven, &amp; has ever since visited their Nation with a continued Train of Calamities, nor will he ever leave off punishing, and wasting their people, till he shall have blotted every living Soul of them out of the World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[5]</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h2><span id="The_Three_Brothers_Who_Followed_the_Sun_under_the_Sky.27s_Rim"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Three_Brothers_Who_Followed_the_Sun_under_the_Sky\'s_Rim">The Three Brothers Who Followed the Sun under the Sky\'s Rim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Three Brothers Who Followed the Sun under the Sky\'s Rim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This is an Iroquois sun myth based on three brothers who tire of being on earth and decide to chase the sun and end up in the sky. Two of the three brothers succeed with the third succeeding in spirit only. The Sun Spirit remakes and tests the two brothers, who end up staying in the realm of the sky for many years. They miss their home and end up returning, only to find that many years have passed. With mostly everything they knew either changed or gone, they long to return to the realm of the sky, which happened when they were both struck by lightning as earthly perils could not harm them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[6]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Great_Peacemaker" title="Great Peacemaker">The Great Peacemaker</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Seneca_mythology" title="Seneca mythology">Seneca mythology</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Turtle_Island_(North_America)" title="Turtle Island (North America)">Turtle Island (North America)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gaasyendietha" title="Gaasyendietha">Gaasyendietha</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iroquois_mythology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Parker says "The central tree in the heaven world was the apple."
