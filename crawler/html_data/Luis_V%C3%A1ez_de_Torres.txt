href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamp</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Lu&#237;s Vaz de Torres</b> (<a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>), or <b>Luis V&#225;ez de Torres</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> spelling (born c. 1565; fl. 1607), was a 16th- and 17th-century <a href="/wiki/List_of_maritime_explorers" title="List of maritime explorers">maritime</a> <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Torres Strait">Torres Strait</a>).\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_early_life">Origins and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Captain Luis V&#225;ez de Torres was recorded as being called a "<a href="/wiki/Britonia" title="Britonia">Breton</a>" by crewmen in reports of the 1606&#8211;1608 voyage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which points to an origin in the northwest province of Spain, i. e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galicia,_Spain" title="Galicia, Spain">Galicia</a>. Most contemporary historians accept this as evidence of his origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The year and exact place of his birth are unknown; assuming him to have been in his late thirties or forties in 1606, a birth year of around 1565 is considered likely.\n</p><p>Torres has been presented by some writers as Portuguese, without any evidence other than his name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>, Galician spelling at the time being undistinguishable from Portuguese. Records never call Torres Portuguese but note remarks made by crew members of the Portuguese origins of <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s">Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</a>.\n</p><p>Torres entered the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy" title="Spanish Navy">navy of the Spanish Crown</a> at some point and found his way to its <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> colonies. By late 160, he first entered the historical record as the nominated commander of the second ship in an expedition to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> proposed by the Portuguese born navigator Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s, searching for <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>. There is no known contemporary depiction of his face or person.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="The_Queir.C3.B3s_voyage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Queir&#243;s_voyage">The Queir&#243;s voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Queir&#243;s voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s">Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</a> was a Portuguese-born navigator who commanded a party of three Spanish ships, <i>San Pedro y San Pablo</i> (60 tons), <i>San Pedrico</i> (40 tons) and the tender <i>Los Tres Reyes Magos</i>. The three ships left <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a> in Spanish <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, on 21 December 1605, with Torres in command of the <i>San Pedrico</i>.\n</p><p>In May 1606, they reached a group of islands that would later be known as the New Hebrides and  <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>. Queir&#243;s named the group <i>La Austrialia del Espiritu Santo</i>: "Austrialia  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] of the Holy Spirit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  A <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">morphological derivation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>,  Queir&#243;s\'s <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> <i>Austrialia</i> was a reference to the Austrian origins of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> &#8211; to which the Spanish royal family belonged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The largest island in Vanuatu is still known officially by the abbreviated form, <a href="/wiki/Espiritu_Santo" title="Espiritu Santo">Espiritu Santo</a>. Along with the ancient Latin name <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a></i>, Queir&#243;s\'s word <i>Austrialia</i> has often been regarded as one of the bases of the <a href="/wiki/Name_of_Australia" title="Name of Australia">name of Australia</a>.\n</p><p>After six weeks, Queir&#243;s&#8217; ships put to sea again to explore the coastline. On the night of 11 June 1606 Queir&#243;s in the <i>San Pedro y San Pablo</i> became separated from the other ships in bad weather and was unable (or so he later said) to return to safe anchorage at Espiritu Santo. He then sailed to <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, where he arrived in November 1606. In the account by Prado, which is highly critical of Queir&#243;s, mutiny and poor leadership are given as the reason for Queir&#243;s&#8217; disappearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prado_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-prado-11">[11]</a></sup> Torres remained silent on the subject other than to write that his  "condition was different to that of Captain Queir&#243;s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Torres_assumes_command">Torres assumes command</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Torres assumes command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Torres remained at Espiritu Santo for 15 days before opening sealed orders he had been given by the Viceroy of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. These contained instructions on what course to follow if the ships became separated and who would be in command in the event of the loss of Queir&#243;s. The orders appear to have listed Prado as successor to Queir&#243;s, as he was <i>capitan-entretenido</i> (spare captain) on the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, there is overwhelming evidence Torres remained in command, including Prado&#8217;s own account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_south_coast_of_New_Guinea_and_Torres_Strait">The south coast of New Guinea and Torres Strait</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The south coast of New Guinea and Torres Strait">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torres_journey.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/Torres_journey.PNG" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Torres_journey.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Torres&#8217; expedition</div></div></div>\n<p>On 26 June 1606 the <i>San Pedrico</i> and <i>Los Tres Reyes Magos</i> under Torres&#8217; command set sail for <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>. Contrary winds prevented the ships taking the more direct route along the north coast of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. Prado&#8217;s account notes that they sighted land on 14 July 1606, which was probably the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanatinai" title="Vanatinai">Tagula</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Louisiade_Archipelago" title="Louisiade Archipelago">Louisiade Archipelago</a>, south east of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. The voyage continued over the next two months along the southeastern coast, and a number of landfalls were made to replenish the ships&#8217; food and water. The expedition discovered <a href="/wiki/Milne_Bay" title="Milne Bay">Milne Bay</a> including <a href="/wiki/Basilaki_Island" title="Basilaki Island">Basilaki Island</a> which they named <i>Tierra de San Buenaventura</i>, taking possession of the land for <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in July 1606.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-12">[12]</a></sup> This brought the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> in close and sometimes violent contact with local indigenous people. Prado and Torres both record the capture of twenty people, including a woman who gave birth several weeks later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prado_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-prado-11">[11]</a></sup> From these islands, Torres sailed along the southern coast of New Guinea reaching Orangerie Bay, which he named <i>Bah&#237;a de San Lorenzo</i> because he landed on 10 August, the feast of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence" title="Saint Lawrence">Saint Lawrence</a> or <i>San Lorenzo.</i> The expedition then sailed to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Papua" title="Gulf of Papua">Gulf of Papua</a>, exploring and charting the coastline. Prado drew a number of sketch charts of anchorages in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Papua" title="Gulf of Papua">Gulf of Papua</a>, several of which survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Torres then took a route close to the New Guinea coast to navigate the 150 kilometre <a href="/wiki/Torres_Strait" title="Torres Strait">strait that now bears his name</a>. In 1980 the Queensland master mariner Captain Brett Hilder proposed that it was more likely that Torres took a southerly route through the nearby channel now called <a href="/wiki/Endeavour_Strait" title="Endeavour Strait">Endeavour Strait</a>, on 2&#8211;3 October 1606.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> From this position, he would certainly have seen <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York</a>, the northernmost extremity of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. According to 19th-century Australian writer <a href="/wiki/George_Collingridge" title="George Collingridge">George Collingridge</a>, Torres "had discovered Australia without being aware of the fact".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Willem_Janszoon" title="Willem Janszoon">Willem Janszoon</a> had made several landfalls on the west coast of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a> 7 to 8 months prior, while Torres never claimed that he had sighted the southern continent. "Here there are very large islands, and more to the south" he wrote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torres-map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="306" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Torres-map.jpg/300px-Torres-map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Torres-map.jpg/450px-Torres-map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Torres-map.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Torres-map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Torres route near Australia</div></div></div>\n<p>Torres followed the coastline of New Guinea, and claimed possession of the island in the name of the King of Spain on 18 October 1606. On 27 October he reached the western extremity of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> and then made his way north of <a href="/wiki/Seram_Island" title="Seram Island">Ceram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Misool" title="Misool">Misool</a> toward the <a href="/wiki/Halmahera_Sea" title="Halmahera Sea">Halmahera Sea</a>. At the beginning of January 1607 he reached <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">Ternate</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Maluku_Islands" title="Maluku Islands">Spice Islands</a>. He sailed on 1 May for <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> arriving on 22 May. The expedition proved that <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> was not part of the sought-after continent.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_of_the_voyage">Results of the voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Results of the voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Torres intended to personally present the captives, weapons and a detailed account to the king on his return to Spain. His short written account of the voyage indicates this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-12">[12]</a></sup>  However, it appears there was no interest in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> in outfitting his voyage back to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, and he was told his ships and men were required locally for the King&#8217;s service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 June 1607, two ships arrived in Manila from South America, one being Queir&#243;s former flagship <i>San Pedro y San Pablo</i>, now under another name, but with some of her former crewmen still aboard. Learning that Queir&#243;s had survived, Torres immediately wrote a report of his voyage to Queir&#243;s. Although that account no longer survives, Queir&#243;s himself referred to it in some of his many memorials to the king, agitating for another voyage.\n</p><p>The official account was written by Diego de Prado and signed by Torres and other officials in Manila 6 June 1608.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prado_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-prado-11">[11]</a></sup> Torres, his crew and his captives disappear entirely from the historical record at this point, and their subsequent fate is unknown. Prado returned to <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, possibly taking one of the captive New Guineans with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Most documents of Torres\'s discoveries were not published, but on reaching <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, filed away in Spanish archives, including Prado&#8217;s lengthy account and the accompanying charts.\n</p><p>Some time between 1762 and 1765, written accounts of the Torres expedition were seen by British Admiralty Hydrographer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dalrymple" title="Alexander Dalrymple">Alexander Dalrymple</a>. Dalrymple provided a sketch map which included the Queir&#243;s - Torres voyages to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Banks" title="Joseph Banks">Joseph Banks</a>, who undoubtedly passed this information to <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The original official manuscript account reappeared in the collections of Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Phillips" title="Thomas Phillips">Thomas Phillips</a> during the 19th century. At the sale of some of Phillipps\' manuscripts by Sotheby\'s, London, on 26 June 1919 it was purchased by booksellers Henry Stevens, Son and Stiles who sold it to English collector Sir <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Harmsworth" title="Leicester Harmsworth">Leicester Harmsworth</a>. The <a href="/wiki/State_Library_of_New_South_Wales" title="State Library of New South Wales">State Library of New South Wales</a> in Australia acquired it from Harmsworth\'s collection in 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prado_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-prado-11">[11]</a></sup>  It went on public display for the first time in August 1997.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounts_of_the_voyage">Accounts of the voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lu%C3%ADs_Vaz_de_Torres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accounts of the voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are a number of documents describing the Queir&#243;s &#8211; Torres voyages still in existence. Most significant are\n</p>\n<ul><li>Queir&#243;s&#8217; many subsequent Memorials to the King <a href="/wiki/Philip_III_of_Spain" title="Philip III of Spain">Philip III</a> regarding the voyage and further exploration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-24">[24]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Torres brief account to the king (written July 1607),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Torres_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Torres-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Prado&#8217;s narrative <i>Relacion Sumaria</i> (first written in 1608) and 4 charts of New Guinea<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Juan Luis Arias de Loyola&#8217;s memorial to King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a>  (written about 1630 and based on discussions between Queir&#243;s and Loyola)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>1617 may be the date of the first English translation of one of Queir&#243;s&#8217; memorials, as <i>Terra Australis Incognita</i>, or <i>A New Southerne Discoverie</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\nA short account of Queir&#243;s&#8217; voyage and discoveries was published in English by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Purchas" title="Samuel Purchas">Samuel Purchas</a> in 1625 in <i>Haklvytvs posthumus</i>, or, <i>Pvrchas his Pilgrimes</i>, vol. iv, p. 1422-1432.
