Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a> (ITU)\'S <a href="/wiki/ITU_Radio_Regulations" title="ITU Radio Regulations">ITU Radio Regulations</a> (RR) as a <i><a href="/wiki/Broadcasting-satellite_service" title="Broadcasting-satellite service">broadcasting-satellite service</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The satellite\'s signals are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast" title="Broadcast">broadcast</a> nationwide, across a much wider geographical area than terrestrial radio stations, and the service is primarily intended for the occupants of <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eandrews_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-eandrews-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbelsie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbelsie-3">[3]</a></sup> It is available by subscription, mostly commercial free, and offers subscribers more stations and a wider variety of programming options than terrestrial radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Satellite radio technology was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Space_Foundation" title="Space Foundation">Space Foundation</a> Space Technology Hall of Fame in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacefoundation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-spacefoundation-5">[5]</a></sup> Satellite radio uses the 2.3&#160;GHz <a href="/wiki/S_band" title="S band">S band</a> in North America for nationwide digital radio broadcasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csgnetwork.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-csgnetwork.com-6">[6]</a></sup> In other parts of the world, satellite radio uses the 1.4&#160;GHz <a href="/wiki/L_band" title="L band">L band</a> allocated for DAB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kbonsor_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-kbonsor-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first satellite radio broadcasts occurred in Africa and the Middle East in 1999. The first US broadcasts were in 2001 followed by Japan in 2004 and Canada in 2005.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa_and_Eurasia">Africa and Eurasia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Africa and Eurasia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WorldSpace" title="WorldSpace">WorldSpace</a> was founded by Ethiopia-born lawyer Noah Samara in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, in 1990,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> with the goal of making satellite radio programming available to the developing world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhilzenrath_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-dhilzenrath-9">[9]</a></sup> On June 22, 1991, the FCC gave WorldSpace permission to launch a satellite to provide digital programming to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eandrews_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-eandrews-2">[2]</a></sup> WorldSpace first began broadcasting satellite radio on October 1, 1999, in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> would ultimately account for over 90% of WorldSpace&#8217;s subscriber base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2008, WorldSpace announced plans to enter <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, but those plans were set aside when the company filed for <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy in November 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In March 2010, the company announced it would be de-commissioning its two satellites (one served <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, the other served Africa). <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a>, which owns 50% of Sirius XM Radio, had considered purchasing WorldSpace&#8217;s assets, but talks between the companies collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhilzenrath_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-dhilzenrath-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The satellites are now transmitting educational data and operate under the name of Yazmi USA, LLC.\n</p><p>Ondas Media was a Spanish company which had proposed to launch a subscription-based satellite radio system to serve Spain and much of Western Europe, but failed to acquire licenses throughout Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p>Onde Num&#233;rique was a French company which had proposed to launch a subscription-based satellite radio system to serve France and several other countries in Western Europe but has suspended its plans indefinitely, effective December, 2016.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sirius_Satellite_Radio" title="Sirius Satellite Radio">Sirius Satellite Radio</a> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Martine_Rothblatt" title="Martine Rothblatt">Martine Rothblatt</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Margolese" title="David Margolese">David Margolese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Briskman" title="Robert Briskman">Robert Briskman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mherper_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mherper-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swarren_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-swarren-15">[15]</a></sup> In June 1990, Rothblatt\'s shell company, Satellite CD Radio, Inc., petitioned the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) to assign new frequencies for satellites to broadcast digital sound to homes and cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eandrews_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-eandrews-2">[2]</a></sup> The company identified and argued in favor of the use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-band" title="S-band">S-band</a> frequencies that the FCC subsequently decided to allocate to digital audio broadcasting. The <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Broadcasters" title="National Association of Broadcasters">National Association of Broadcasters</a> contended that satellite radio would harm local radio stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbelsie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-lbelsie-3">[3]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 1992, Rothblatt resigned as CEO of Satellite CD Radio;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mherper_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-mherper-14">[14]</a></sup> former NASA engineer Robert Briskman, who designed the company\'s satellite technology, was then appointed chairman and CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmclean_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bmclean-17">[17]</a></sup> Six months later, <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Wireless" title="Rogers Wireless">Rogers Wireless</a> co-founder David Margolese, who had provided financial backing for the venture, acquired control of the company and succeeded Briskman. Margolese renamed the company CD Radio, and spent the next five years lobbying the FCC to allow satellite radio to be deployed, and the following five years raising $1.6 billion, which was used to build and launch three satellites into elliptical orbit from <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> in July 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmclean_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-bmclean-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1997, after Margolese had obtained regulatory clearance and "effectively created the industry," the FCC also sold a license to the American Mobile Radio Corporation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> which changed its name to <a href="/wiki/XM_Satellite_Radio" title="XM Satellite Radio">XM Satellite Radio</a> in October 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> XM was founded by Lon Levin and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Parsons" title="Gary Parsons">Gary Parsons</a>, who served as chairman until November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>\n</p><p>CD Radio purchased their license for $83.3 million, and American Mobile Radio Corporation bought theirs for $89.9 million. Digital Satellite Broadcasting Corporation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primosphere" title="Primosphere">Primosphere</a> were unsuccessful in their bids for licenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Sky Highway Radio Corporation had also expressed interest in creating a satellite radio network, before being bought out by CD Radio in 1993 for $2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In November 1999, Margolese changed the name of CD Radio to Sirius Satellite Radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swarren_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-swarren-15">[15]</a></sup> In November 2001, Margolese stepped down as CEO, remaining as chairman until November 2003, with Sirius issuing a statement thanking him "for his great vision, leadership and dedication in creating both Sirius and the satellite radio industry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>\n</p><p>XM&#8217;s first satellite was launched on March 18, 2001 and its second on May 8, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kbonsor_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-kbonsor-7">[7]</a></sup> Its first broadcast occurred on September 25, 2001, nearly four months before Sirius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sparker_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-sparker-28">[28]</a></sup> Sirius launched the initial phase of its service in four cities on February 14, 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> expanding to the rest of the contiguous United States on July 1, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sparker_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-sparker-28">[28]</a></sup> The two companies spent over $3 billion combined to develop satellite radio technology, build and launch the satellites, and for various other business expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacefoundation_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-spacefoundation-5">[5]</a></sup> Stating that it was the only way satellite radio could survive, Sirius and XM announced their merger on February 19, 2007, becoming <a href="/wiki/Sirius_XM_Satellite_Radio" title="Sirius XM Satellite Radio">Sirius XM Satellite Radio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The FCC approved the merger on July 25, 2008, concluding that it was not a monopoly, primarily due to Internet audio-streaming competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/MobaHo!" title="MobaHo!">MobaHo!</a> was a mobile satellite digital audio/video broadcasting service in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> whose services began on October 20, 2004, and ended on March 31, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>XM satellite radio was launched in Canada on November 29, 2005. Sirius followed two days later on December 1, 2005. <a href="/wiki/Sirius_Canada" title="Sirius Canada">Sirius Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/XM_Radio_Canada" title="XM Radio Canada">XM Radio Canada</a> announced their merger into <a href="/wiki/SiriusXM_Canada" title="SiriusXM Canada">SiriusXM Canada</a> on November 24, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It was approved by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Radio-television_and_Telecommunications_Commission" title="Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission">Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission</a> on April 12, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="System_design">System design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: System design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Satellite radio uses the 2.3&#160;GHz <a href="/wiki/S_band" title="S band">S band</a> in North America for nationwide digital radio broadcasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csgnetwork.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-csgnetwork.com-6">[6]</a></sup> MobaHO! operated at 2.6&#160;GHz. In other parts of the world, satellite radio uses part of the 1.4&#160;GHz <a href="/wiki/L_band" title="L band">L band</a> allocated for DAB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kbonsor_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-kbonsor-7">[7]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Satellite radio subscribers purchase a receiver and pay a monthly subscription fee to listen to programming. They can listen through built-in or portable receivers in automobiles; in the home and office with a portable or tabletop receiver equipped to connect the receiver to a stereo system; or on the Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shop.siriusxm.com_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-shop.siriusxm.com-36">[36]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ground stations transmit signals to the satellites which are 35,786 kilometers (22,236 miles) above the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">Clarke belt</a> orbits. The satellites send the signals back down to radio receivers in cars and homes. This signal contains scrambled broadcasts, along with meta data about each specific broadcast. The signals are unscrambled by the radio receiver modules, which display the broadcast information. In urban areas, ground repeaters enable signals to be available even if the satellite signal is blocked. The technology allows for nationwide broadcasting, so that, for instance US listeners can hear the same stations anywhere in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kbonsor_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-kbonsor-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edition.cnn.com_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-edition.cnn.com-37">[37]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Content.2C_availability_and_market_penetration"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Content,_availability_and_market_penetration">Content, availability and market penetration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Content, availability and market penetration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Satellite radio in the US offers commercial-free music stations, as well as news, sports, and talk, some of which include commercials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In 2004, satellite radio companies in the United States began providing background music to hotels, retail chains, restaurants, airlines and other businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfeder_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-bfeder-40">[40]</a></sup> On April 30, 2013, Sirius XM CEO Jim Meyer stated that the company would be pursuing opportunities over the next few years to provide in-car services through their existing satellites, including telematics (automated security and safety, such as stolen <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_tracking_system" title="Vehicle tracking system">vehicle tracking</a> and roadside assistance) and entertainment (such as weather and gas prices).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbaker_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbaker-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p><p>As of Q3 2016, SiriusXM had 31 million subscribers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> This was primarily due to the company&#8217;s partnerships with automakers and car dealers. Roughly 60% of new cars sold come equipped with Sirius XM, and just under half of those units gain paid subscriptions. The company has long-term deals with <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kia_Motors" title="Kia Motors">Kia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bentley" title="Bentley">Bentley</a>, <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan">Nissan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyundai" title="Hyundai">Hyundai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi" title="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trefisteam_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-trefisteam-43">[43]</a></sup> The presence of <a href="/wiki/Howard_Stern" title="Howard Stern">Howard Stern</a>, whose show attracts over 12 million listeners per week, has also been a factor in the company&#8217;s steady growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trefisteam_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-trefisteam-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> As of 2013, the main competition to satellite radio is streaming Internet services, such as <a href="/wiki/Pandora_Radio" title="Pandora Radio">Pandora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> and <a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM broadcasting">AM</a> Radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbaker_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-lbaker-41">[41]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_radio_vs._other_formats">Satellite radio vs. other formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_radio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Satellite radio vs. other formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Satellite radio differs from <a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM broadcasting">AM</a>, <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> radio, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_television_radio" title="Digital television radio">digital television radio</a> (DTR) in the following ways (the table applies primarily to the United States):\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Radio format</th>\n<th>Satellite radio</th>\n<th><a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM television radio</a> (DTR)\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td><b>Monthly fees</b></td>\n<td>US$10.99 and up</td>\n<td>Free</td>\n<td>Free for terrestrial. For cable and satellite, low to none <sup>5</sup>\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>&#178; The sound quality with both satellite radio providers and DTR providers varies with each channel. Some channels have near CD-quality audio, and others use low-bandwidth audio suitable only for speech. Since only a certain amount of bandwidth is available within the licenses available, adding more channels means that the quality on some channels must be reduced. Both the frequency response and the dynamic range of satellite channels can be superior to most, but not all AM or FM radio stations, as most AM and FM stations clip the audio peaks to sound louder; even the worst channels are still superior to most AM radios, but a very few AM tuners are equal to or better than the best FM or satellite broadcasts when tuned to a local station, even if not capable of stereo. The use of <a href="/wiki/HD_Radio" title="HD Radio">HD Radio</a> technology can allow AM and FM broadcasts to exceed the quality of satellite. AM does not suffer from multipath distortion or flutter in a moving vehicle like FM, nor does it become silent as you go behind a big hill like satellite radio.\n</p><p>&#179; Some satellite radio services and DTR services act as <i>in situ</i> repeaters for local AM/FM stations and thus feature a high frequency of interruption.\n</p><p><sup>4</sup> Nonprofit stations and public radio networks such as <a href="/wiki/Public_Radio_International" title="Public Radio International">PRI-affiliated</a> stations and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio" title="BBC Radio">BBC</a> are commercial-free. In the US, all stations are required to have periodic station identifications and public service announcements.\n</p><p><sup>5</sup> In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a> regulates technical broadcast spectrum only. Program content is unregulated. However, the FCC has tried in the past to expand its reach to regulate content to satellite radio and cable television, and its options are still open to attempt such in the future. The FCC does issue licenses to SiriusXM, the satellite radio provider, and controls who holds these licenses to broadcast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Many of their channels, including the pop music ones, are self-regulated.\n</p><p><sup>6</sup> Degree of content regulation varies by country; however, the majority of industrialized nations have regulations regarding obscene and/or objectionable content.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
