contrary to a <a href="/wiki/Wind_gust" title="Wind gust">wind gust</a> lasting seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup> They are usually associated with active weather, such as rain showers, thunderstorms, or heavy snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Squalls refer to the increase to the sustained winds over that time interval, as there may be higher gusts during a squall event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">[3]</a></sup> They usually occur in a region of strong sinking air or cooling in the mid-atmosphere. These force strong localized upward motions at the leading edge of the region of cooling, which then enhances local downward motions just in its wake.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are different versions of the word\'s origins:\n</p>\n<ul><li>By one version, the word appears to be Nordic in origin, but its <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is considered obscure. It probably has its roots in the word <i>skvala</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> an <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> word meaning literally, <i>to squeal</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li>By another version, it is an alteration of <i>squeal</i> influenced by <i>bawl</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character_of_the_wind">Character of the wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Character of the wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "squall" is used to refer to a sudden wind-speed increase lasting minutes. In 1962 the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) defined that to be classified as a "squall", the wind must increase at least 8&#160;m/s and must attain a top speed of at least 11&#160;m/s, lasting at least one minute in duration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, a squall is defined to last for several minutes before the wind returns to the long term mean value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-3">[3]</a></sup> In either case, a squall is defined to last about half as long as the definition of sustained wind in its respective country. Usually, this sudden violent wind is associated with briefly heavy precipitation as <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_terms">Regional terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Regional terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Known locally as <i>pamperos</i>, these are characterized as strong downsloped winds that move across the pampas, eventually making it to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (December 2014)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_America">Central America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Central America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Offshore <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, a <i>gully squall</i> is characterized by strong increases of the wind forced through sharp mountain valleys on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> side of the isthmus.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba">Cuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <i>bayamo</i> is a squall emanating from tropical thunderstorms near the <a href="/wiki/Bight_(geography)" title="Bight (geography)">Bight</a> of Bayamo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Indies">East Indies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: East Indies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/East_Indies" title="East Indies">East Indies</a>, <i>brubu</i> is a name for a squall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Pacific_Northwest_.28North_America.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pacific_Northwest_(North_America)">Pacific Northwest (North America)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pacific Northwest (North America)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, a <i>squall</i> is a short but furious rainstorm with strong winds, often small in area and moving at high speed, especially as a maritime term. Such a strong outflow occurring in fjords and inlets is referred to by mariners as a <i><a href="/wiki/Squamish_(wind)" title="Squamish (wind)">squamish</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Bull\'s Eye Squall</i> is a term used offshore South Africa for a squall forming in fair weather. It is named for the appearance of the small isolated cloud marking the top of the squall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ggweather_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ggweather-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Philippines_.28West_Pacific.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines_(West_Pacific)">Philippines (West Pacific)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Philippines (West Pacific)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In most parts of the country, squalls are called <i>subasko</i> and are characterized by heavy rains driven by blustery winds. Local fishermen at sea are often on the lookout for signs of impending squalls on the open water and rush to shore at its early signs.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South-East_Asia">South-East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: South-East Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>"Barat" is a term for a northwest squall in Manado Bay in <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">Sulawesi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ggweather_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ggweather-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p><p>"<a href="/wiki/Sumatras" title="Sumatras">Sumatra</a>" squall is a term used in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and Peninsular <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> for <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> that form over the island of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> and move east across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Malacca" title="Straits of Malacca">Straits of Malacca</a>. Gusts can reach up to 28&#160;m/s (100&#160;km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sumatras_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-sumatras-12">[12]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_weather">Severe weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Severe weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:301px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DangerousShelfCloud.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="183" data-file-width="346" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/DangerousShelfCloud.jpg/299px-DangerousShelfCloud.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/DangerousShelfCloud.jpg 1.5x" width="299"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DangerousShelfCloud.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a> such as this one can be a sign that a squall is imminent</div></div></div>\n<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a></i> is an organized line of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>. It is classified as a multi-cell cluster, meaning a thunderstorm complex comprising many individual updrafts. They are also called multi-cell lines. Squalls are sometimes associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, but they can also occur independently. Most commonly, independent squalls occur along <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">front lines</a>, and may contain heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, frequent <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, dangerous straight line winds, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel clouds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Squall lines require significant low-level warmth and humidity, a nearby frontal zone, and vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from an angle behind the frontal boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The strong winds at the surface are usually a reflection of dry air intruding into the line of storms, which when saturated, falls quickly to ground level due to its much higher density before it spreads out downwind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Significant squall lines with multiple bow echoes are known as <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Squall_line_life_cycle">Squall line life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Squall line life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are several forms of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale meteorology</a>, including simplistic isolated thunderstorms unrelated to advancing cold fronts, to the more complex daytime/nocturnal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" title="Mesoscale Convective System">Mesoscale Convective System</a> (MCS) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_Complex" title="Mesoscale Convective Complex">Mesoscale Convective Complex</a> (MCC), to <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a> thunderstorms.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The main driving force behind squall line creation is attributed to the process of in-filling of multiple thunderstorms and/or a single area of thunderstorms expanding outward within the leading space of an advancing <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_perturbations">Pressure perturbations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pressure perturbations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>Pressure perturbations within an extent of a thunderstorm are noteworthy. With <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> rapid within the lower and mid-levels of a mature thunderstorm, one might believe that low pressure dominates in the mesoscale environment. However, this is not the case. With downdrafts ushering colder air from mid-levels, hitting ground and propagating away in all directions, high pressure is to be found widely at surface levels, usually indicative of strong (potentially damaging) winds.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_shear">Wind shear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Wind shear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg/220px-Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg/330px-Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg/440px-Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Squall_line_arthur_ontario_2003.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A summer squall line in Southern Ontario, producing lightning and distant heavy rains.</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Wind shear</a> is an important aspect to measuring the potential of squall line severity and duration. In low to medium shear environments, mature thunderstorms will contribute modest amounts of downdrafts, enough to turn will aid in create a leading edge lifting mechanism &#8211; the gust front. In high shear environments created by opposing low level jet winds and synoptic winds, updrafts and consequential downdrafts can be much more intense (common in supercell mesocyclones). The cold air <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> leaves the trailing area of the squall line to the mid-level jet, which aids in downdraft processes.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Updrafts">Updrafts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Updrafts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The leading area of a squall line is composed primarily of multiple updrafts, or singular regions of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a>, rising from ground level to the highest extensions of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, condensing water and building a dark, ominous cloud to one with a noticeable overshooting top and anvil (thanks to <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> winds). Because of the chaotic nature of updrafts and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdrafts" title="Downdrafts">downdrafts</a>, pressure perturbations are important.\n</p><p>As thunderstorms fill into a distinct line, strong leading-edge updrafts &#8211; occasionally visible to a ground observer in the form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a> &#8211; may appear as an ominous sign of potential severe weather.\n</p><p>Beyond the strong winds because of updraft/downdraft behavior, heavy rain (and <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>) is another sign of a squall line. In the winter, squall lines can occur albeit less frequently &#8211; bringing heavy <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> and lightning &#8211; usually over inland <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> region).\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Bow_echoes">Bow echoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Bow echoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>Following the initial passage of a squall line, light to moderate <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a> precipitation is also common. A <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">Bow echo</a> is frequently seen on the northern and southern most reaches of squall line thunderstorms (via satellite imagery). This is where the northern and southern ends curl backwards towards the middle portions of the <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>, making a "bow" shape. Bow echoes are frequently featured within <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> mesoscale systems.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesolow">Mesolow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mesolow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wakelowimage_zoom.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="442" data-file-width="409" decoding="async" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Wakelowimage_zoom.png/140px-Wakelowimage_zoom.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Wakelowimage_zoom.png/210px-Wakelowimage_zoom.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Wakelowimage_zoom.png/280px-Wakelowimage_zoom.png 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wakelowimage_zoom.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a> is a mesolow</div></div></div>\n<p>The northern end of the squall line is commonly referred to as the cyclonic end, with the southern side rotating anticyclonically. Because of the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a>, the northern end may evolve further, creating a "comma shaped" mesolow, or may continue in a squall-like pattern.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Wake_low" title="Wake low">wake low</a> is another kind of mesoscale low-pressure area to the rear of a squall line near the back edge of the stratiform rain area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Due to the subsiding warm air associated with the systems formation, clearing skies are associated with the wake low. Severe weather, in the form of high winds, can be generated by the wake low when the pressure difference between the mesohigh preceding it and the wake low is intense enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaffin_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gaffin-18">[18]</a></sup> When the squall line is in the process of decay, <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">heat bursts</a> can be generated near the wake low. Once new thunderstorm activity along the squall line concludes, the wake low associated with it weakens in tandem.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissipation">Dissipation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Dissipation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As supercells and multi-cell thunderstorms dissipate due to a weak shear force or poor lifting mechanisms, (e.g. considerable <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> or lack of daytime heating) the <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a> associated with them may outrun the squall line itself and the synoptic scale area of low pressure may then infill, leading to a weakening of the cold front; essentially, the thunderstorm has exhausted its updrafts, becoming purely a downdraft dominated system. The areas of dissipating squall line thunderstorms may be regions of low <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">CAPE</a>, low <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, insufficient wind shear, or poor synoptic dynamics (e.g. an upper level low filling) leading to <a href="/wiki/Frontolysis" title="Frontolysis">frontolysis</a>.\n</p><p>From here, a general thinning of a squall line will occur: with winds decaying over time, outflow boundaries weakening updrafts substantially and clouds losing their thickness.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_in_the_sky">Signs in the sky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Signs in the sky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Shelf clouds and roll clouds are usually seen above the leading edge of a squall, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>\'s gust front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> From the time these low cloud features appear in the sky, one can expect a sudden increase in the wind in less than 15 minutes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> normally have squalls coincident with spiral bands of greater curvature than many mid-latitude systems due to their smaller size. These squalls can harbor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterspouts" title="Waterspouts">waterspouts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadoes" title="Tornadoes">tornadoes</a> due to the significant vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> which exists in the vicinity of a tropical cyclone\'s outer bands.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_weather">Winter weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Squall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Winter weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_squalls" title="Snow squalls">Snow squalls</a> can be spawned by an intrusion of cold air aloft over a relatively warm surface layer. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" title="Lake effect snow">Lake effect snows</a> can be in the form of a snow squall.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
