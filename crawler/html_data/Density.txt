id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about mass density. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Density_(disambiguation)" title="Density (disambiguation)">Density (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;font-style:italic;">Density</th></tr><tr><th scope="row"><div style="padding:0.1em 0;line-height:1.2em;">Common symbols</div></th><td><i><a href="/wiki/Rho" title="Rho">&#961;</a></i><br/> <i>D</i></td></tr><tr><th scope="row"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">SI&#160;unit</a></th><td>kg/m<sup>3</sup></td></tr></tbody></table><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Artsy_density_column.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4000" data-file-width="1080" decoding="async" height="556" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Artsy_density_column.png/150px-Artsy_density_column.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Artsy_density_column.png/225px-Artsy_density_column.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Artsy_density_column.png/300px-Artsy_density_column.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Artsy_density_column.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Graduated_cylinder" title="Graduated cylinder">graduated cylinder</a> containing various coloured liquids with different densities</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>density</b>, or more precisely, the <b>volumetric mass density</b>, of a substance is its <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> per unit <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>. The symbol most often used for density is <i>&#961;</i> (the lower case Greek letter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rho_(letter)" title="Rho (letter)">rho</a>), although the Latin letter <i>D</i> can also be used. Mathematically, density is defined as mass divided by volume:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\\displaystyle \\rho ={\\frac {m}{V}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>&#961;<!-- &#961; --></mi>\n<mo>=</mo>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mfrac>\n<mi>m</mi>\n<mi>V</mi>\n</mfrac>\n</mrow>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\rho ={\\frac {m}{V}}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="{\\displaystyle \\rho ={\\frac {m}{V}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f63465553e3f944d6ef79f90f992a02cf29c7f38" style="vertical-align: -2.005ex; width:7.177ex; height:4.843ex;"/></span></dd></dl>\n<p>where <i>&#961;</i> is the density, <i>m</i> is the mass, and <i>V</i> is the volume. In some cases (for instance, in the United States oil and gas industry), density is loosely defined as its <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> per unit <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> although this is scientifically inaccurate &#8211; this quantity is more specifically called <a href="/wiki/Specific_weight" title="Specific weight">specific weight</a>.\n</p><p>For a pure substance the density has the same numerical value as its <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(chemistry)" title="Mass concentration (chemistry)">mass concentration</a>.\nDifferent materials usually have different densities, and density may be relevant to <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, purity and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packaging" title="Packaging">packaging</a>. <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">Osmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> are the densest known elements at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard conditions for temperature and pressure</a> but certain chemical compounds may be denser.\n</p><p>To simplify comparisons of density across different systems of units, it is sometimes replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless" title="Dimensionless">dimensionless</a> quantity "<a href="/wiki/Relative_density" title="Relative density">relative density</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a>", i.e. the ratio of the density of the material to that of a standard material, usually water. Thus a relative density less than one means that the substance floats in water.\n</p><p>The density of a material varies with temperature and pressure. This variation is typically small for solids and liquids but much greater for gases. Increasing the pressure on an object decreases the volume of the object and thus increases its density. Increasing the temperature of a substance (with a few exceptions) decreases its density by increasing its volume. In most materials, heating the bottom of a fluid results in <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> of the heat from the bottom to the top, due to the decrease in the density of the heated fluid. This causes it to rise relative to more dense unheated material.\n</p><p>The reciprocal of the density of a substance is occasionally called its <a href="/wiki/Specific_volume" title="Specific volume">specific volume</a>, a term sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>. Density is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive_property" title="Intensive property">intensive property</a> in that increasing the amount of a substance does not increase its density; rather it increases its mass.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" id="History">History</span></h2>\n<p>In a well-known but probably apocryphal tale, <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> was given the task of determining whether <a href="/wiki/Hiero_II_of_Syracuse" title="Hiero II of Syracuse">King Hiero</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">goldsmith</a> was embezzling <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> during the manufacture of a golden <a href="/wiki/Wreath" title="Wreath">wreath</a> dedicated to the gods and replacing it with another, cheaper <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Archimedes knew that the irregularly shaped wreath could be crushed into a cube whose volume could be calculated easily and compared with the mass; but the king did not approve of this. Baffled, Archimedes is said to have taken an immersion bath and observed from the rise of the water upon entering that he could calculate the volume of the gold wreath through the <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(fluid)" title="Displacement (fluid)">displacement</a> of the water. Upon this discovery, he leapt from his bath and ran naked through the streets shouting, "Eureka! Eureka!" (&#917;&#973;&#961;&#951;&#954;&#945;! Greek "I have found it"). As a result, the term "<a href="/wiki/Eureka_(word)" title="Eureka (word)">eureka</a>" entered common parlance and is used today to indicate a moment of enlightenment.\n</p><p>The story first appeared in written form in <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/De_architectura" title="De architectura">books of architecture</a></i>, two centuries after it supposedly took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Some scholars have doubted the accuracy of this tale, saying among other things that the method would have required precise measurements that would have been difficult to make at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the equation for density (<i>&#961;</i> = <i>m</i>/<i>V</i>), mass density has units of mass divided by volume. As there are many units of mass and volume covering many different magnitudes there are a large number of units for mass density in use. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> per <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">cubic metre</a> (kg/m<sup>3</sup>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cgs" title="Cgs">cgs</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> per <a href="/wiki/Cubic_centimetre" title="Cubic centimetre">cubic centimetre</a> (g/cm<sup>3</sup>) are probably the most commonly used units for density. One g/cm<sup>3</sup> is equal to one thousand kg/m<sup>3</sup>.  One cubic centimetre (abbreviation cc) is equal to one millilitre. In industry, other larger or smaller units of mass and or volume are often more practical and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a> may be used. See below for a list of some of the most common units of density.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_of_density">Measurement of density</span></h2>\n<p>A number of techniques as well as standards exist for the measurement of density of materials. Such techniques include the use of a hydrometer (a buoyancy method for liquids), Hydrostatic balance (a buoyancy method for liquids and solids), immersed body method (a buoyancy method for liquids), pycnometer (liquids and solids), air comparison pycnometer (solids), oscillating densitometer (liquids), as well as pour and tap (solids).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, each individual method or technique measures different types of density (e.g. bulk density, skeletal density, etc.), and therefore it is necessary to have an understanding of the type of density  being measured as well as the type of material in question. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homogeneous_materials">Homogeneous materials</span></h3>\n<p>The density at all points of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homogeneous_(chemistry)" title="Homogeneous (chemistry)">homogeneous</a> object equals its total <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> divided by its total volume. The mass is normally measured with a <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">scale or balance</a>; the volume may be measured directly (from the geometry of the object) or by the displacement of a fluid. To determine the density of a liquid or a gas, a <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">hydrometer</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dasymeter" title="Dasymeter">dasymeter</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_flow_meter" title="Coriolis flow meter">Coriolis flow meter</a> may be used, respectively. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_weighing" title="Hydrostatic weighing">hydrostatic weighing</a> uses the displacement of water due to a submerged object to determine the density of the object.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heterogeneous_materials">Heterogeneous materials</span></h3>\n<p>If the body is not homogeneous, then its density varies between different regions of the object. In that case the density around any given location is determined by calculating the density of a small volume around that location. Many materials exist in nature as flakes, pellets, or granules.\n</p><p>Voids are regions which contain something other than the considered material.  Commonly the void is air, but it could also be vacuum,  liquid, solid, or a different gas or gaseous mixture.\n</p><p>The bulk volume of a material&#8212;inclusive of the void fraction&#8212;is often obtained by a simple measurement (e.g. with a calibrated measuring cup) or geometrically from known dimensions.\n</p><p>Mass divided by <i>bulk</i> volume determines <a href="/wiki/Bulk_density" title="Bulk density">bulk density</a>.  This is not the same thing as volumetric mass density.\n</p><p>To determine volumetric mass density, one must first discount the volume of the void fraction. Sometimes this can be determined by geometrical reasoning.  For the <a href="/wiki/Close-packing_of_equal_spheres" title="Close-packing of equal spheres">close-packing of equal spheres</a> the non-void fraction can be at most about 74%.  It can also be determined empirically.  Some bulk materials, however, such as sand, have a <i>variable</i> void fraction which depends on how the material is agitated or poured. It might be loose or compact, with more or less air space depending on handling.\n</p><p>In practice, the void fraction is not necessarily air, or even gaseous.  In the case of sand, it could be water, which can be advantageous for measurement as the void fraction for sand saturated in water&#8212;once any air bubbles are thoroughly driven out&#8212;is potentially more consistent than dry sand measured with an air void.\n</p><p>In the case of non-compact materials, one must also take care in determining the mass of the material sample. If the material is under pressure (commonly ambient air pressure at the earth\'s surface) the determination of mass from a measured sample weight might need to account for buoyancy effects due to the density of the void constituent, depending on how the measurement was conducted. In the case of dry sand, sand is so much denser than air that the buoyancy effect is commonly neglected (less than one part in one thousand).\n</p><p>Mass change upon displacing one void material with another while maintaining constant volume can be used to estimate the void fraction, if the difference in density of the two voids materials is reliably known.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_of_density">Changes of density</span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">Compressibility</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_expansivity" title="Thermal expansivity">Thermal expansivity</a></div>\n<p>In general, density can be changed by changing either the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. Increasing the pressure always increases the density of a material. Increasing the temperature generally decreases the density, but there are notable exceptions to this generalization. For example, the density of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> increases between its melting point at 0&#160;&#176;C and 4&#160;&#176;C; similar behavior is observed in <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> at low temperatures.\n</p><p>The effect of pressure and temperature on the densities of liquids and solids is small. The <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">compressibility</a> for a typical liquid or solid is 10<sup>&#8722;6</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a><sup>&#8722;1</sup> (1&#160;bar = 0.1&#160;MPa) and a typical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_expansivity" title="Thermal expansivity">thermal expansivity</a> is 10<sup>&#8722;5</sup>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a><sup>&#8722;1</sup>. This roughly translates into needing around ten thousand times atmospheric pressure to reduce the volume of a substance by one percent. (Although the pressures needed may be around a thousand times smaller for sandy soil and some clays.) A one percent expansion of volume typically requires a temperature increase on the order of thousands of degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>.\n</p><p>In contrast, the density of gases is strongly affected by pressure. (kg/m<sup>3</sup>)</li></ul>\n<p>The litre and metric tons are not part of the SI, but are acceptable for use with it, leading to the following units:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> per <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a> (kg/L)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">millilitre</a> (g/mL)</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">metric ton</a> per cubic metre (t/m<sup>3</sup>)</li></ul>\n<p>Densities using the following metric units all have exactly the same numerical value, one thousandth of the value in (kg/m<sup>3</sup>). Liquid <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> has a density of about 1&#160;kg/dm<sup>3</sup>, making any of these SI units numerically convenient to use as most <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a> have densities between 0.1 and 20&#160;kg/dm<sup>3</sup>.\n</p>\n<ul><li>kilogram per cubic decimetre (kg/dm<sup>3</sup>)</li>\n<li>gram per cubic centimetre (g/cm<sup>3</sup>)\n<ul><li>1 g/cm<sup>3</sup> = 1000&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup></li></ul></li>\n<li>megagram (metric ton) per cubic metre (Mg/m<sup>3</sup>)</li></ul>\n<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a> density can be stated in:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois_ounce" title="Avoirdupois ounce">Avoirdupois ounce</a> per <a href="/wiki/Cubic_inch" title="Cubic inch">cubic inch</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 0.578036672 oz/cu&#160;in)</li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois_ounce" title="Avoirdupois ounce">Avoirdupois ounce</a> per <a href="/wiki/Fluid_ounce" title="Fluid ounce">fluid ounce</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 1.04317556 oz/fl. oz = 1.04317556 lbs/pint)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">Avoirdupois pound</a> per cubic inch (1 g/cc &#8776; 0.036127292 lb/cu&#160;in)</li>\n<li>pound per <a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic foot</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 62.427961 lb/cu&#160;ft)</li>\n<li>pound per <a href="/wiki/Cubic_yard" title="Cubic yard">cubic yard</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 1685.5549 lb/cu&#160;yd)</li>\n<li>pound per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_liquid_gallon" title="US liquid gallon">US liquid gallon</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 8.34540445 lb/gal)</li>\n<li>pound per US <a href="/wiki/Bushel" title="Bushel">bushel</a> (1 g/cc &#8776; 77.6888513 lb/bu)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Slug_(unit)" title="Slug (unit)">slug</a> per cubic foot</li></ul>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">Imperial units</a> differing from the above (as the Imperial gallon and bushel differ from the US units) in practice are rarely used, though found in older documents. The Imperial gallon was based on the concept that an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_fluid_ounce" title="Imperial fluid ounce">Imperial fluid ounce</a> of water would have a mass of one Avoirdupois ounce, and indeed 1&#160;g/cc &#8776; 1.00224129 ounces per Imperial fluid ounce = 10.0224129 pounds per Imperial gallon. The density of <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a> could conceivably be based on <a href="/wiki/Troy_weight" title="Troy weight">Troy</a> ounces and pounds, a possible cause of confusion.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 22em; -webkit-column-width:
