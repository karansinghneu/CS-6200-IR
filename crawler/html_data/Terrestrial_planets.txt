scale</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>terrestrial planet</b>, <b>telluric planet</b>, or <b>rocky planet</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> that is composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>. Within the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, the terrestrial planets are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_planets" title="Inner planets">inner planets</a> closest to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. The terms "terrestrial planet" and "telluric planet" are derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words for <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> (<i>Terra</i> and <i>Tellus</i>), as these planets are, in terms of structure, "Earth-like". These planets are located between the Sun and the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">Asteroid Belt.</a>\n</p><p>Terrestrial planets have a solid <a href="/wiki/Planetary_surface" title="Planetary surface">planetary surface</a>, making them substantially different from the larger <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a>, which are composed mostly of some combination of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> existing in various <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">physical states</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>All terrestrial planets in the Solar System have the same basic type of structure, such as a central metallic <a href="/wiki/Planetary_core" title="Planetary core">core</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, with a surrounding silicate <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is similar, but has a much smaller iron core. <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a> are also satellites that have internal structures similar to that of terrestrial planets. Terrestrial planets can have <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">canyons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">craters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, and other surface structures, depending on the presence of water and tectonic activity. Terrestrial planets have <a href="/wiki/Secondary_atmosphere" title="Secondary atmosphere">secondary atmospheres</a>, generated through volcanism or comet impacts, in contrast to the giant planets, whose atmospheres are <a href="/wiki/Primary_atmosphere" title="Primary atmosphere">primary</a>, captured directly from the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schombert-lec14_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schombert-lec14-1">[1]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span Only one terrestrial planet, Earth, is known to have an active <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>.\n</p><p>During the formation of the Solar System, there were probably many more terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a>, but most merged with or were ejected by the four terrestrial planets.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">Dwarf planets</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body" title="Small Solar System body">small Solar System bodies</a> are similar to terrestrial planets in the fact that they do have a solid surface, but are, on average, composed of more icy materials (Ceres, Pluto and Eris have densities of 2.17, 1.87 and 2.52 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>, respectively, and <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a>\'s density is similar to <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">Pallas</a>\'s 2.8 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>). The Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> has a density of 3.4 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup> and Jupiter\'s satellites, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Io_(Moon)" title="Io (Moon)">Io</a>, 3.528 and <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, 3.013 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>; other satellites typically have densities less than 2 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>\n</p><p><br/>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density_trends">Density trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Density trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The uncompressed density of a terrestrial planet is the average density its materials would have at zero <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. A greater uncompressed density indicates greater metal content. Uncompressed density differs from the true average density (also often called "bulk" density) because compression within planet cores increases their density; the average density depends on planet size, temperature distribution and material stiffness as well as composition.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable" style="text-align: center;">\n<caption>Densities of the terrestrial planets\n</caption>\n<tbody><tr>\n<th rowspan="2" scope="col">Object\n</th>\n<th colspan="2" scope="col">Density (g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>)\n</th>\n<th rowspan="2" scope="col">Semi-major axis (AU)\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<th scope="col">Mean\n</th>\n<th scope="col">Uncompressed\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>\n</td>\n<td>5.4\n</td>\n<td>5.3\n</td>\n<td>0.39\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>\n</td>\n<td>5.2\n</td>\n<td>4.4\n</td>\n<td>0.72\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\n</td>\n<td>5.5\n</td>\n<td>4.4\n</td>\n<td>1.0\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>\n</td>\n<td>3.9\n</td>\n<td>3.8\n</td>\n<td>1.5\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The uncompressed density of terrestrial planets trends towards lower values as the distance from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> increases. The rocky minor planet <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">Vesta</a>  orbiting outside of Mars is less dense than Mars still at, 3.4 g&#183;cm<sup>&#8722;3</sup>.\n</p><p>Calculations to estimate uncompressed density inherently require a model of the planet\'s structure. Where there have been landers or multiple orbiting spacecraft, these models are constrained by seismological data and also moment of inertia data derived from the spacecraft orbits. Where such data is not available, uncertainties are inevitably higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  It is unknown, whether extrasolar terrestrial planets in general will show to follow this trend.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extrasolar_terrestrial_planets">Extrasolar terrestrial planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extrasolar terrestrial planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Super-Earth" title="Super-Earth">Super-Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mega-Earth" title="Mega-Earth">Mega-Earth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_nearest_terrestrial_exoplanet_candidates" title="List of nearest terrestrial exoplanet candidates">List of nearest terrestrial exoplanet candidates</a></div>\n<p>Most of the planets discovered outside the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> are <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">giant planets</a>, because they are more easily detectable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> But since 2005, hundreds of potentially terrestrial extrasolar planets have been found, with several being confirmed as terrestrial. Most of these are <a href="/wiki/Super-Earth" title="Super-Earth">super-Earths</a>, i.e. planets with masses between Earth\'s and Neptune\'s; super-Earths may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_planet" title="Gas planet">gas planets</a> or terrestrial, depending on their mass and other parameters.\n</p><p>During the early 1990s, the first extrasolar planets were discovered orbiting the <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a> <a href="/wiki/PSR_B1257%2B12" title="PSR B1257+12">PSR B1257+12</a>, with masses of 0.02, 4.3, and 3.9 times that of Earth\'s, by <a href="/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_exoplanets#Pulsar_Timing" title="Methods of detecting exoplanets">pulsar timing</a>.\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/51_Pegasi_b" title="51 Pegasi b">51 Pegasi b</a>, the first planet found around a star still undergoing <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">fusion</a>, was discovered, many astronomers assumed it to be a gigantic terrestrial,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> because it was assumed no gas giant could exist as close to its star (0.052&#160;AU) as 51&#160;Pegasi&#160;b did. It was later found to be a gas giant.\n</p><p>In 2005, the first planets around main-sequence stars that may be terrestrial were found: <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876_d" title="Gliese 876 d">Gliese 876 d</a>, has a mass 7 to 9 times that of Earth and an orbital period of just two Earth days. It orbits the red dwarf <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876" title="Gliese 876">Gliese 876</a>, 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">light years</a> from Earth. <a href="/wiki/OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb" title="OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb">OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb</a>, about 5.5&#160;times the mass of Earth, orbits a star about 21,000 light years away in the constellation Scorpius.\nFrom 2007 to 2010, three (possibly four) potential terrestrial planets were found orbiting within the <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581_planetary_system" title="Gliese 581 planetary system">Gliese 581 planetary system</a>. The smallest, <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581e" title="Gliese 581e">Gliese 581e</a>, is only about 1.9 Earth mass,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESO0904_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESO0904-6">[6]</a></sup> but orbits very close to the star. An ideal terrestrial planet would be 2 Earth masses with a 25-day orbital period around a red dwarf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mayor_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mayor-7">[7]</a></sup> Two others, <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581c" title="Gliese 581c">Gliese 581c</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581d" title="Gliese 581d">Gliese&#160;581d</a>, as well as a disputed planet, <a href="/wiki/Gliese_581g" title="Gliese 581g">Gliese 581g</a>, are more-massive super-Earths orbiting in or close to the habitable zone of the star, so they could  potentially be habitable, with Earth-like temperatures.\n</p><p>Another possibly terrestrial planet, <a href="/wiki/HD_85512_b" title="HD 85512 b">HD 85512 b</a>, was discovered in 2011; it has at least 3.6 times the mass of Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\nThe radius and composition of all these planets are unknown.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" candidates orbiting 2,036 stars as of November 4, 2013 (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>).</div></div></div>\n<p>The first confirmed terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kepler-10b" title="Kepler-10b">Kepler-10b</a>, was found in 2011 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_Mission" title="Kepler Mission">Kepler Mission</a>, specifically designed to discover Earth-size planets around other stars using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">transit</a> method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">Kepler Space Observatory Mission team</a> released a <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)#Extrasolar_planets_detected" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">list of 1235 extrasolar planet candidates</a>, including six that are "Earth-size" or "super-Earth-size" (i.e. they have a radius less than 2 Earth radii)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> of their star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borucki_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-borucki-11">[11]</a></sup>\nSince then, Kepler has <a href="/wiki/List_of_exoplanets_discovered_using_the_Kepler_spacecraft" title="List of exoplanets discovered using the Kepler spacecraft">discovered hundreds of planets</a> ranging from Moon-sized to super-Earths, with many more candidates in this size range (see image).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_terrestrial_exoplanets">List of terrestrial class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2013, astronomers reported, based on <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)"><i>Kepler</i> space mission</a> data, that there could be as many as 40 billion Earth- and super-Earth-sized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planets" title="Extrasolar planets">planets</a> orbiting in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Solar_analog" title="Solar analog">Sun-like stars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarfs</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20131104_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131104-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS-20131031_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-20131031-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space-20130107_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Space-20130107-14">[14]</a></sup> 11 billion of these estimated planets may be orbiting Sun-like stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes-20131104_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-20131104-15">[15]</a></sup> The nearest such planet may be 12 light-years away, according to the scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20131104_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131104-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNAS-20131031_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-20131031-13">[13]</a></sup> However, this does not give estimates for the number of extrasolar terrestrial planets, because there are planets as small as Earth that have been shown to be gas planets (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KOI-314" title="KOI-314">KOI-314c</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Planet.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="659" data-file-width="660" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Carbon_Planet.JPG/220px-Carbon_Planet.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Carbon_Planet.JPG/330px-Carbon_Planet.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Carbon_Planet.JPG/440px-Carbon_Planet.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_Planet.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist\'s impression of a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_planet" title="Carbon planet">carbon planet</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Several possible classifications for terrestrial planets have been proposed:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Silicate planet</dt>\n<dd>The standard type of terrestrial planet seen in the Solar System, made primarily of silicon-based rocky <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> with a metallic (iron) core.</dd>\n<dt><a href="/wiki/Carbon_planet" title="Carbon planet">Carbon planet</a> (also called "diamond planet")</dt>\n<dd>A theoretical class of planets, composed of a metal core surrounded by primarily carbon-based minerals. They may be considered a type of terrestrial planet if the metal content dominates. The Solar System contains no carbon planets, but does have <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">carbonaceous asteroids</a>.</dd>\n<dt><a href="/wiki/Iron_planet" title="Iron planet">Iron planet</a></dt>\n<dd>A theoretical type of terrestrial planet that consists almost entirely of iron and therefore has a greater density and a smaller radius than other terrestrial planets of comparable mass. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a> in the Solar System has a metallic core equal to 60&#8211;70% of its planetary mass. Iron planets are thought to form in the high-temperature regions close to a star, like Mercury, and if the protoplanetary disk is rich in iron.</dd>\n<dt><a href="/wiki/Coreless_planet" title="Coreless planet">Coreless planet</a></dt>\n<dd>A theoretical type of terrestrial planet that consists of silicate rock but has no metallic core, i.e. the opposite of an iron planet. Although the Solar System contains no coreless planets, <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondrite</a> asteroids and meteorites are common in the Solar System. Coreless planets are thought to form farther from the star where volatile oxidizing material is more common.</dd></dl>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chthonian_planet" title="Chthonian planet">Chthonian planet</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_analog" title="Earth analog">Earth analog</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_potentially_habitable_exoplanets" title="List of potentially habitable exoplanets">List of potentially habitable exoplanets</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">Planetary habitability</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venus_zone" title="Venus zone">Venus zone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_gravitationally_rounded_objects_of_the_Solar_System" title="List of gravitationally rounded objects of the Solar System">List of gravitationally rounded objects of the Solar System</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terrestrial_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
