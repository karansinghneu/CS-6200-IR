<a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the <b>plutinos</b> are a <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor-planet_groups" title="List of minor-planet groups">dynamical group</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian objects</a> in the outermost region of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> that orbit in 2:3 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">mean-motion resonance</a> with <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. This means that for every two orbits a plutino makes, Neptune orbits three times. The dwarf planet <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> is the largest member and namesake of this group. Plutinos <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Minor_planets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">are named after</a> mythological creatures associated with the underworld.\n</p><p>Plutinos form the inner part of the <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> and represent about a quarter of the known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt_object" title="Kuiper belt object">Kuiper belt objects</a>. They are the largest class of the <a href="/wiki/Resonant_trans-Neptunian_object" title="Resonant trans-Neptunian object">resonant trans-Neptunian objects</a> <i>(also see adjunct box with hierarchical listing)</i>. Aside from Pluto itself, the first plutino, <a href="/wiki/(385185)_1993_RO" title="(385185) 1993 RO">(385185) 1993 RO</a>, was discovered on September 16, 1993.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plutino&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>It is thought that objects that are currently in mean orbital resonances with Neptune initially followed independent heliocentric paths. As Neptune migrated outward early in the Solar System\'s history (see <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt#Origins" title="Kuiper belt">origins of the Kuiper belt</a>), the bodies it approached would have been scattered; during this process, some of them would have been captured into resonances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra1995_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malhotra1995-1">[1]</a></sup> The 3:2 resonance is a low-order resonance and the strongest and most stable among all resonances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
