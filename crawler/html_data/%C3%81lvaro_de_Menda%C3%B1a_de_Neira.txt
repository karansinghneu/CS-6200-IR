Barreto">Isabel Barreto</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>&#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a y Neira</b> (or <b>Neyra</b>) (October 1, 1542 &#8211; October 18, 1595) was a <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Navigator" title="Navigator">navigator</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Congosto" title="Congosto">Congosto</a>, in <a href="/wiki/El_Bierzo" title="El Bierzo"> El Bierzo Region</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n_(province)" title="Le&#243;n (province)">Le&#243;n</a>), he was the nephew of <a href="/wiki/Lope_Garc%C3%ADa_de_Castro" title="Lope Garc&#237;a de Castro">Lope Garc&#237;a de Castro</a>, viceroy of <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. He is best known for the two voyages of discovery he led into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> in 1567 and 1595 in search of <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_for_Terra_Australis">Search for Terra Australis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Search for Terra Australis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brett_Hilder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brett Hilder (page does not exist)">Brett Hilder</a> has written of &#8220;ardent spirits in <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Peru</a>, inspiring three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_People" title="Spanish People">Spanish</a>  voyages to the south west <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a> in the forty years from 1565 to 1605.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> One of these ardent spirits was certainly Spanish soldier <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Sarmiento_de_Gamboa" title="Pedro Sarmiento de Gamboa">Pedro Sarmiento de Gamboa</a> who arrived in Peru in 1557. <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Sarmiento_de_Gamboa" title="Pedro Sarmiento de Gamboa">Sarmiento de Gamboa</a> developed an interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inca" title="Inca">Inca</a> stories of gold and riches being collected from lands further to the west. Sarmiento\'s proposal for an expedition to find land in the Pacific was put to Governor <a href="/wiki/Lope_Garc%C3%ADa_de_Castro" title="Lope Garc&#237;a de Castro">Lope Garc&#237;a de Castro</a>, finding favour as it matched common Spanish belief in the existence of a great South Land. Historian Miriam Estensen argues that Governor Castro also agreed as a way of maintaining peace and order. &#8220;Restless and disruptive&#8221; elements in the Spanish Americas were encouraged to join such journeys of exploration to remove them from colonial society. The lure of possible wealth made these expeditions attractive to such men, often drawn from the poorest levels of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, Sarmiento de Gamboa was bitterly disappointed not to be made Captain-General of the expedition. Instead, command was given to the Governor Castro\'s nephew, the younger and relatively inexperienced &#193;lvaro de Menda&#241;a de Neira. Sarmiento was to be &#8220;Cosmographer&#8221;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Sarmiento\'s written account, he was Captain of the flagship and at least on the same level as chief pilot and navigator Hernando Gallego.  While Sarmiento\'s interest was the riches to be gained, Menda&#241;a\'s priority was conversion of "the heathen" to Christianity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Deep divisions had been created within the Spanish command, even before the expedition departed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_voyage_of_1567-1569">The first voyage of 1567-1569</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The first voyage of 1567-1569">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The two ships, the 200-ton <i>Los Reyes</i> (the Capitana or flagship) and the 140-ton <i>Todos Santos</i> (the Almiranta or secondary ship) sailed from <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> on 20 November 1567, with about 150 sailors, soldiers, priests and slaves on board.\n</p><p>After sighting a small island in mid January (probably <a href="/wiki/Nui_(atoll)" title="Nui (atoll)">Nui</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>), a significant body of land was sighted on February 7, 1568. It was <a href="/wiki/Santa_Isabel_Island" title="Santa Isabel Island">Santa Isabel Island</a>, where they landed several days later. The expedition had discovered the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> which they named <i>Islas Salomon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Spanish immediately came into contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solomon_Island" title="Solomon Island">Solomon Islanders</a> and at first the relationship was cordial. However, the Spanish expedition\'s need for fresh food and water quickly led to tension and conflict, the Solomon Islanders&#8217; subsistence economy being unable to provide continuous supplies to the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spanish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The real prizes were pigs, desperately needed by the Spanish, while vital to the local people\'s economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The cultural gulf was wide; in one famous account, the Spanish were horrified to be offered &#8220;a quarter of a boy with the arm and hand&#8221; which the islanders urged Menda&#241;a to eat. The islanders were offended when the Spanish refused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>\n</p><p>After building a small brigantine, the surrounding islands of <a href="/wiki/Malaita" title="Malaita">Malaita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadalcanal" title="Guadalcanal">Guadalcanal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makira" title="Makira">Makira</a> (which was named San Cristobal) and <a href="/wiki/Choiseul_Island" title="Choiseul Island">Choiseul Island</a> were explored. However, the attempts to barter for food led to the same cycle of friendly welcome, misunderstandings, sullen retreats, occasional reconciliations, robberies and violent retaliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Finally, at a council meeting of captains, pilots, soldiers and sailors on August 7, 1568, the decision was made to return to Peru. Menda&#241;a had wanted to sail further south, while Sarmiento de Gamboa and several soldiers unsuccessfully urged the establishment of a colony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>\n</p><p>The two ships sailed north then east, passing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshall_islands" title="Marshall islands">Marshall islands</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wake_island" title="Wake island">Wake island</a>, before reaching the Mexican coast in late January 1569. It was a long and difficult voyage, with numerous deaths from <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_of_the_first_voyage">Results of the first voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Results of the first voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The main result of the expedition was the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>. The navigators also gained valuable sailing experience for Spain, especially in crossing the vast South Pacific from <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Peru</a>. These discoveries led to successive expeditions in search for <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">Terra Australis</a>, both by Menda&#241;a himself and by <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s">Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</a>.\n</p><p>However, the Great South Land had not yet been discovered by Europeans. The islands found, by now popularly called the Solomon Islands, had not revealed more than a few indications of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. There were no <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> and the people had not been converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1595-1596_voyage">The 1595-1596 voyage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The 1595-1596 voyage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A much larger and costlier expedition had been planned by the early 1590s, after Menda&#241;a had spent years courting favour in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a>. Four ships and 378 men, women and children were to establish a colony in the Solomon Islands. Again, the leaders of this voyage had &#8220;widely divergent personalities.&#8221;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Menda&#241;a was again in command, accompanied by his wife Do&#241;a <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Barreto" title="Isabel Barreto">Isabel Barreto</a>, her three brothers and a sister. Chief pilot was to be a young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_People" title="Portuguese People">Portuguese</a> navigator in Spanish service, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fernandes_de_Queir%C3%B3s" title="Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s">Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s</a>. An argumentative old soldier, Pedro Merino Manrique was chosen as camp master. Manrique caused disputes before the fleet had even departed.\n</p><p>The four ships, <i>San Ger&#243;nimo</i> (the Capitana), the <i>Santa Ysabel</i> (the Almiranta), the smaller frigate <i>Santa Catalina</i> and the galiot <i>San Felipe</i> left <a href="/wiki/Callao" title="Callao">Callao</a> on 9 April 1595. Spirits were high in the first month, fifteen marriages being celebrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Menda&#241;a had Queir&#243;s prepare charts for his Captains that only showed <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and the Solomon Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 21 July 1595 the ships reached the <a href="/wiki/Marquesas_Islands" title="Marquesas Islands">Marquesas Islands</a>, (named for the wife of the then viceroy of Peru, <a href="/wiki/Garc%C3%ADa_Hurtado_de_Mendoza,_5th_Marquis_of_Ca%C3%B1ete" title="Garc&#237;a Hurtado de Mendoza, 5th Marquis of Ca&#241;ete">Garc&#237;a Hurtado de Mendoza, 5th Marquis of Ca&#241;ete</a>) to be met by four hundred people in canoes. Although the Spaniards admired their &#8220;graceful shape&#8221; and &#8220;almost white&#8221; complexion, the relationship with the natives later deteriorated. When the expedition left two weeks later, Queir&#243;s estimated 200 Marquesans had been killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite Menda&#241;a\'s confidence that the Solomon Islands were nearby, it was not until 8 September that they sighted land again, this time the island of <a href="/wiki/Nendo_Island" title="Nendo Island">Nendo</a>, which they named "Santa Cruz".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <i>Santa Ysabel</i> had disappeared, however, and despite searches by the two smaller vessels, it could not be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Arriving at what is now Graciosa Bay, a settlement was commenced. Relations with local islanders and their chief Malope started well, with food provided and assistance in constructing buildings. However, morale amongst the Spanish was low and sickness (almost certainly <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>) was rife. Manrique was murdered at the orders of, and in front of Menda&#241;a, and shortly afterwards, Malope was killed by soldiers.  Relations with the Islanders soon worsened.\n</p><p>Wracked by internal divisions and an increasing death toll, the settlement began to fall apart. Menda&#241;a himself died on 18 October 1595, leaving his wife as heir and governor, her brother Lorenzo as captain-general. On 30 October, the decision was made to abandon the settlement. When the three ships departed on 18 November 1595, forty-seven people had died in the space of one month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>\n</p><p>Pedro Fernandes de Queir&#243;s is credited with bringing the <i>San Ger&#243;nimo</i> safely into the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">Philippines</a> without the aid of charts, arriving in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> Bay on 11 February 1596. Over fifty people died on the twelve-week voyage from Santa Cruz, in part due to the lack of food supplies and allegedly Do&#241;a <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Barreto" title="Isabel Barreto">Isabel</a>&#8217;s refusal to share out her private store of food and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The frigate (carrying Menda&#241;a\'s body) disappeared during the voyage, while the galiot <i>San Felipe</i> arrived in the southern end of <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a> several days later.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lvaro_de_Menda%C3%B1a_de_Neira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Of the 378 who sailed from <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">Peru</a>, about 100 survived, but ten more died shortly after arriving in Manila. <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Barreto" title="Isabel Barreto">Do&#241;a Isabel Barreto</a> was honoured in Manila and Queir&#243;s was commended for his service and absolved of any responsibility for the killings on Santa Cruz. Three months later Do&#241;a Isabel married the governor\'s cousin. She continued to agitate for a return to the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>. She died in 1612.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="3000" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg/220px-Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg/330px-Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg/440px-Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bust_of_Spanish_explorer_Mendana.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bust at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solomon_Island" title="Solomon Island">Solomon Island</a> museum at <a href="/wiki/Point_Cruz" title="Point Cruz">Point Cruz</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Back in Peru in June 1597, Queir&#243;s began his campaign to return to the Solomon Islands, leading the next Spanish venture in 1605. This expedition was also unsuccessful, but it led to the discovery of the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> which were explored, and briefly settled in the case of <a href="/wiki/Espiritu_Santo" title="Espiritu Santo">Espiritu Santo</a>. Queir&#243;s visited the Solomons in 1606, but was unable to establish a settlement. The Solomons were not again visited by outsiders until 1767, when <a href="/wiki/Philip_Carteret" title="Philip Carteret">Philip Carteret</a> sighted <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Islands" title="Santa Cruz Islands">Santa Cruz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaita" title="Malaita">Malaita</a> islands.\n</p><p>The story of the voyage is told in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Islands_of_Unwisdom" title="The Islands of Unwisdom">The Islands of Unwisdom</a></i>, a historical novel by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Graves" title="Robert Graves">Robert Graves</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
